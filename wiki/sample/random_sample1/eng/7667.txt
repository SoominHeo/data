<p>The <b>World Food Programme</b> (<b>WFP</b>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i><b>Programme Alimentaire Mondial</b></i>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i><b>Programma Alimentare Mondiale</b></i>; <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i><b>Programa Mundial de Alimentos</b></i>; <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>: <b>برنامج الأغذية العالمي</b>, <i>barnamaj al'aghdhiat alealami</i>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <i><b>Всемирная продовольственная программа</b></i>, <i>Vsemirnaya prodovol'stvennaya programmaa</i>; Chinese: <b>联合国世界粮食计划署</b>, <i>Liánhéguó shìjiè liángshí jìhuà shǔ</i>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_aid" title="Food aid">food assistance</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and the world's largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanitarian" title="Humanitarian">humanitarian</a> organization addressing <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a> and promoting <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the WFP, it provides food assistance to an average of 80 million people in 75 countries each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> From its headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and more than 80 country offices around the world, the WFP works to help people who are unable to produce or obtain enough food for themselves and their families. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Group" title="United Nations Development Group">United Nations Development Group</a> and part of its Executive Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>WFP was first established in 1961<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> after the 1960 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Agricultural_Organization" title="Food and Agricultural Organization">Food and Agricultural Organization</a> (FAO) Conference, when <a href="/wiki/George_McGovern" title="George McGovern">George McGovern</a>, director of the US <a href="/wiki/Food_for_Peace" title="Food for Peace">Food for Peace</a> Programmes, proposed establishing a multilateral food aid programme. The WFP was formally established in 1963 by the FAO and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> on a three-year experimental basis. In 1965, the programme was extended to a continuing basis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The WFP is governed by an Executive Board which consists of representatives from 36 member states. <a href="/wiki/Ertharin_Cousin" title="Ertharin Cousin">Ertharin Cousin</a> is the current Executive Director, appointed jointly by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Secretary_General" title="UN Secretary General">UN Secretary General</a> and the Director-General of the FAO for a five-year term. She heads the Secretariat of the WFP. The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> is a permanent observer in the WFP and, as a major donor, participates in the work of its Executive Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its vision is a "world in which every man, woman and child has access at all times to the food needed for an active and healthy life."</p>
<p>The WFP has a staff of about 11,500 people, the majority of whom work in remote areas.</p>

<p>The WFP strives to eradicate <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>, with the ultimate goal in mind of eliminating the need for food aid itself.</p>
<p>The objectives that the WFP hopes to achieve are to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>"Save lives and protect livelihoods in emergencies"</li>
<li>"Support food security and nutrition and (re)build livelihoods in fragile settings and following emergencies"</li>
<li>"Reduce risk and enable people, communities and countries to meet their own food and nutrition needs"</li>
<li>"Reduce undernutrition and break the intergenerational cycle of hunger"</li>
</ol>
<p>WFP food aid is also directed to fight <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrient</a> deficiencies, reduce <a href="/wiki/Child_mortality" title="Child mortality">child mortality</a>, improve maternal health, and combat disease, including <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>. Food-for-work programmes help promote environmental and economic stability and agricultural production.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, the WFP reached 80.9 million people in 75 countries and provided 3.1 million tonnes of food,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> including nutritionally improved products such as <a href="/wiki/Plumpy%27nut" title="Plumpy'nut">Plumpy'sup</a>, Plumpy'doz, and Supercereal Plus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> 7.8 million malnourished children received special nutritional support in 2013, and 18.6 million children received school meals or take-home rations.</p>
<p>The WFP has scaled up its use of cash and vouchers as food assistance tools. 7.9 million people received assistance through cash or voucher programmes in 2013. In the same year, the WFP purchased food in 91 countries; 86 percent of that food came from developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among its other activities, the WFP has coordinated the five-year Purchase for Progress (P4P) pilot project. Launched in September 2008, P4P assists smallholder farmers by offering them opportunities to access agricultural markets and to become competitive players in the market place. The project spanned across 20 countries in Africa, Asia and Latin America and trained 800,000 farmers in improved agricultural production, post-harvest handling, quality assurance, group marketing, agricultural finance and contracting with the WFP. The project resulted in 366,000 metric tons of food produced and generated more than $148 million in income for its smallholder farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The WFP focuses its food assistance on those who are most vulnerable to hunger, which most frequently means women, children, the sick and the elderly. In fact, part of the response to the 2010 Haiti earthquake consisted of distributing food aid only to women as experience built up over almost 5 decades of working in emergency situations has demonstrated that giving food only to women helps to ensure that it is spread evenly among all household members. School-feeding and/or take-home ration programmes in 71 countries help students focus on their studies and encourage parents to send their children, especially girls, to school.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WFP operations are funded by voluntary donations from world governments, corporations and private donors. The organization's administrative costs are only seven percent—one of the lowest and best among <a href="/wiki/Aid_agency" title="Aid agency">aid agencies</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2011, the WFP's total revenue was $3.73 billion. From 2008-2012, private donors donated around $500 million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="FITTEST">FITTEST</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: FITTEST">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The Fast Information Technology and Telecommunications Emergency and Support Team (FITTEST) is a small, elite, technical team of emergency responders within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IT" title="IT">IT</a> division of the World Food Programme (WFP). Covering both emergency preparedness and response, FITTEST provides a range of IT, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> and power generation infrastructures and solutions to support <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_aid" title="Humanitarian aid">humanitarian aid</a> operations during emergencies and in steady-state situations.</p>
<p>Humanitarian emergencies demand rapid interventions that are efficient, coordinated and effective. FITTEST is trained to work in demanding, hazardous and hostile environments and ensures staff are on the ground and ready to operate within 48 hours. To date, FITTEST has completed more than 1,000 missions in over 130 countries, including West Africa, the Philippines, Haiti, Central African Republic, South Sudan, Iraq, Syria and, more recently, Nepal and Yemen. From natural disasters such as earthquakes and tropical cyclones, public health emergencies, such as the Ebola outbreak, to conflict and civil unrest, FITTEST provides lifesaving IT, telecommunications and power services to governments and the humanitarian community. With a team comprising just 15 to 25 responders at any given time, FITTEST team members hail from more than 10 nationalities from all six continents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_team">The team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>FITTEST’s key areas of expertise include telecommunications (radio, wireless &amp; microwave, satellite), IT (hardware / infrastructure, software, Internet, email) and electricity (power generation, wiring, and renewable and hybrid energy systems, such as solar power and wind turbines). The team also offers training and consultancy services. In preparation for future emergencies, the team holds virtual stock of equipment allowing pre-determined items to be available at a moment’s notice. FITTEST offices and warehouses are located in Dubai’s <a href="/wiki/International_Humanitarian_City" title="International Humanitarian City">International Humanitarian City</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>. From here the team develops technological solutions and delivers its services across the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FITTEST developed from the African Great Lakes humanitarian emergency in 1995. In 1998, it became an official tool for WFP to respond to emergencies. With the establishment of the United Nations Cluster system by the <a href="/wiki/Inter-Agency_Standing_Committee" title="Inter-Agency Standing Committee">Inter-Agency Standing Committee</a>, and especially the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Telecommunications_Cluster_(ETC)" title="Emergency Telecommunications Cluster (ETC)">Emergency Telecommunications Cluster</a>, FITTEST became a critical tool for WFP to fulfil its mandate of providing IT services to the entire humanitarian community. FITTEST is a unique cell within the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> system as it operates on a cost-recovery basis. Receiving no direct contribution from Governments or other humanitarian donors, FITTEST ensures its sustainability by using best practices of the private sector. The team operates on a limited margin (7%) used to cover running costs. Such a method ensures the delivery of high service quality, as FITTEST only survives if its 'clients' are satisfied and continue to utilise its services.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_partners">Official partners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Official partners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WFP coordinates and cooperates with a number of official partners in emergencies and development projects. These partners include national government agencies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DFID" title="DFID">DFID</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECHO_(European_Commission)" title="ECHO (European Commission)">ECHO</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EuropeAid_Development_and_Cooperation" title="EuropeAid Development and Cooperation">EuropeAid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAID" title="USAID">USAID</a>; UN agencies such as the Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO) and the International Fund for Agricultural Development (IFAD); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" title="Non-governmental organizations">non-governmental organizations</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Save_the_Children" title="Save the Children">Save the Children</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Relief_Services" title="Catholic Relief Services">Catholic Relief Services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Refugee_Council" title="Norwegian Refugee Council">Norwegian Refugee Council</a>; as well as corporate partners such as <a href="/wiki/Boston_Consulting_Group" title="Boston Consulting Group">Boston Consulting Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yum!_Brands" title="Yum! Brands">Yum! Brands</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.dsm.com" rel="nofollow">DSM N.V.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cargill" title="Cargill">Cargill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>WFP is also a partner in <a class="external text" href="http://www.compact2025.org" rel="nofollow">Compact2025</a>, a partnership that develops and disseminates evidence-based advice to politicians and other decision-makers aimed at ending hunger and undernutrition in the coming 10 years. WFP is represented on the Leadership Council of Compact2025 by its Executive Director <a href="/wiki/Ertharin_Cousin" title="Ertharin Cousin">Ertharin Cousin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Actor Drew Barrymore is Ambassador to the World Food Program and donated $1 million in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrymore_Ambassador_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barrymore_Ambassador-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grassroots level partners include <a class="external text" href="http://hungrykids.org" rel="nofollow">hungrykids.org</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Hunger_Relief_Week">World Hunger Relief Week</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World Hunger Relief Week">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, the WFP joined with <a href="/wiki/Yum!_Brands" title="Yum! Brands">Yum! Brands</a>, the world’s largest restaurant company, to launch the first annual World Hunger Relief Week, a global campaign to increase awareness about hunger, engage volunteers, and raise critically needed funds to help the WFP serve the world's areas of greatest need. World Hunger Relief Week 2007 leveraged the power of nearly 35,000 restaurants around the world, sparking a global movement to end hunger and generating an overwhelming outpouring of support from millions of customers, employees, franchisees and their families. Nearly one million Yum!, KFC, Pizza Hut, Taco Bell, Long John Silver's and A&amp;W All American Food employees, franchisees and their families volunteered close to 4 million hours to aid hunger relief efforts in communities worldwide, while helping to raise $16 million throughout the World Hunger Relief Week initiative for the World Food Programme and other hunger relief agencies around the world. The initiative has been repeated every year since.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grassroots_efforts">Grassroots efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Grassroots efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004, the WFP tasked <a href="/wiki/Auburn_University" title="Auburn University">Auburn University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_Alabama" title="Auburn, Alabama">Auburn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> with heading the first student-led War on Hunger effort, after a 2002 <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a> pilot. Auburn founded the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_19" title="Committee of 19">Committee of 19</a>, which has not only led campus and community hunger awareness events but also developed a War on Hunger model for use on campuses across the country.</p>
<p>The WFP has launched a global advocacy and fundraising event called <a href="/wiki/Walk_the_World" title="Walk the World">Walk the World</a>. On one single day each year, hundreds of thousands of people in every time zone all over the world walk to call for the end of child hunger. In 2005, more than 200,000 people walked in 296 locations. In 2006, there were 760,000 participants in 118 countries all over the world. This event is part of the campaign to achieve the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>, specifically to halve the number of people who suffer from hunger and poverty by 2015.</p>
<p>A growing number of grassroots global events and celebrations such as <a href="/wiki/International_Day_of_Peace" title="International Day of Peace">International Day of Peace</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=World_Party_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="World Party Day (page does not exist)">World Party Day</a> participants, and <a href="/wiki/Peace_One_Day" title="Peace One Day">Peace One Day</a> recommend the WFP on radio broadcasts as an immediate reach out action, putting help within reach of anyone with the information that a quarter feeds a child for a day. <a href="/wiki/Fill_the_Cup" title="Fill the Cup">Fill the Cup</a> campaign takes just 25 US cents to fill one of the "red cups" that the World Food Programme uses to give hungry children a regular school meal of porridge, rice or beans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">Christina Aguilera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drew_Barrymore" title="Drew Barrymore">Drew Barrymore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sean_Penn" title="Sean Penn">Sean Penn</a> are among notable celebrities who endorse the WFP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The British singer <a href="/wiki/Sami_Yusuf" title="Sami Yusuf">Sami Yusuf</a> joined with the WFP to support the drought-stricken in Horn of Africa<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> through his personal campaign, LiveFeedAfrica<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and music video, <i>Forgotten Promises</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Food_Program_USA">World Food Program USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: World Food Program USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.wfpusa.org" rel="nofollow">World Food Program USA</a>, formerly Friends of WFP, works to solve global hunger, building a world where everyone has the food and nutrition needed to lead healthy, productive lives. WFP USA raises support for these efforts in the United States by engaging individuals, organizations and businesses, shaping public policy and generating resources for WFP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Food_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenyan</a> economist <a href="/wiki/James_Shikwati" title="James Shikwati">James Shikwati</a> says in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Spiegel" title="Der Spiegel">Der Spiegel</a></i>: "aid to Africa does more harm than good".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DS_Interview_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-DS_Interview-23">[23]</a></sup> According to him, the food aid increases corruption as local politicians have the opportunity to steal some of the aid to bribe voters or to sell the aid in the black markets killing the local agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_Sell_Aid_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP_Sell_Aid-24">[24]</a></sup> He claims that the WFP people as an organisation "are in the absurd situation of, on the one hand, being dedicated to the fight against hunger while, on the other hand, being faced with unemployment were hunger actually eliminated". He suggests that WFP answers too easily to the calls of the corrupted governments, and supplies too much of food aid leading to reduction of the production of local farmers as "no one can compete with the UN's World Food Program".</p>
<p>The World Food Program was also criticized by Jose Ciro Martinez and Brent Eng in their essay "The Unintended Consequences of Emergency Food Aid: Neutrality, Sovereignty and Politics in the Syrian Civil War, 2012-15." due to an interview conducted with an employee of the organization who stated: “‘Most aid is still subject to strict control measures by the government, who also requests that it be distributed through state approved bodies such as SARC. I believe the government closely oversees if not completely controls these organizations.”” This statement was expanded upon by the authors as they noted that food aid given in Syria would go to the military and its men first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
