<p><b>Bacterial vaginosis</b> (<b>BV</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a> caused by excessive growth of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClarkTal2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClarkTal2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Common symptoms include increased <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_discharge" title="Vaginal discharge">vaginal discharge</a> that often smells like fish. The discharge is usually white or gray in color. <a href="/wiki/Dysuria" title="Dysuria">Burning with urination</a> may occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013S_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013S-4">[4]</a></sup> Itching is uncommon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013S_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013S-4">[4]</a></sup> Occasionally, there may be no symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013S_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013S-4">[4]</a></sup> Having BV approximately doubles the risk of infection by a number of other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infections" title="Sexually transmitted infections">sexually transmitted infections</a>, including <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradshaw2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradshaw2015-6">[6]</a></sup> It also increases the risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preterm" title="Preterm">early delivery</a> among pregnant women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Queenan2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Queenan2012-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>BV is caused by an imbalance of the naturally occurring bacteria in the vagina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett2015_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2015-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013O_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013O-10">[10]</a></sup> There is a change in the most common type of bacteria and a hundred to thousandfold increase in total numbers of bacteria present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Typically, bacteria other than <i>Lactobacilli</i> become more common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nardis2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nardis2013-11">[11]</a></sup> Risk factors include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douching" title="Douching">douching</a>, new or <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sex_partners" title="Multiple sex partners">multiple sex partners</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>, and using an <a href="/wiki/Intrauterine_device" title="Intrauterine device">intrauterine device</a>, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013O_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013O-10">[10]</a></sup> However, it is not considered a sexually transmitted infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc.gov_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdc.gov-12">[12]</a></sup> Diagnosis is suspected based on the symptoms, and may be verified by testing the vaginal discharge and finding a higher than normal vaginal <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, and large numbers of bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup> BV is often confused with a <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_yeast_infection" title="Vaginal yeast infection">vaginal yeast infection</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infection_with_Trichomonas" title="Infection with Trichomonas">infection with Trichomonas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Usually treatment is with an antibiotic, such as <a href="/wiki/Clindamycin" title="Clindamycin">clindamycin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metronidazole" title="Metronidazole">metronidazole</a>. These medications may also be used in the second or third trimesters of <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>. However, the condition often recurs following treatment. <a href="/wiki/Probiotic" title="Probiotic">Probiotics</a> may help prevent re-occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup> It is unclear if the use of probiotics or antibiotics affects pregnancy outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>BV is the most common vaginal infection in women of reproductive age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013O_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013O-10">[10]</a></sup> The percentage of women affected at any given time varies between 5% and 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup> BV is most common in parts of Africa and least common in Asia and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup> In the United States about 30% of women between the ages of 14 and 49 are affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC2010Stats_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC2010Stats-15">[15]</a></sup> Rates vary considerably between <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic groups</a> within a country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup> While BV like symptoms have been described for much of recorded history, the first clearly documented case occurred in 1894.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History1997_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-History1997-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common symptoms include increased <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_discharge" title="Vaginal discharge">vaginal discharge</a> that usually smells like fish. The discharge is often white or gray in color. There may be <a href="/wiki/Dysuria" title="Dysuria">burning with urination</a>. Occasionally, there may be no symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013S_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013S-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The discharge coats the walls of the vagina, and is usually without significant irritation, pain, or <a href="/wiki/Erythema" title="Erythema">erythema</a> (redness), although mild <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pruritus" title="Pruritus">itching</a> can sometimes occur. By contrast, the normal vaginal discharge will vary in consistency and amount throughout the <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">menstrual cycle</a> and is at its clearest at <a href="/wiki/Ovulation" title="Ovulation">ovulation</a>—about two weeks before the <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">period</a> starts. Some practitioners claim that BV can be asymptomatic in almost half of affected women,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11120507_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11120507-16">[16]</a></sup> though others argue that this is often a misdiagnosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_bacterial_vaginosis_microbiota" title="List of bacterial vaginosis microbiota">List of bacterial vaginosis microbiota</a></div>
<p>Healthy vaginal microbiota consists of species which neither cause symptoms or infections, nor negatively affect pregnancy. It is dominated mainly by Lactobacillus species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nardis2013_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nardis2013-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PetrovaLievens2015_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-PetrovaLievens2015-18">[18]</a></sup> BV is defined by the disequilibrium in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_microbiota_species_of_the_lower_reproductive_tract_of_women" title="List of microbiota species of the lower reproductive tract of women">vaginal microbiota</a>, with decline in the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lactobacilli" title="Lactobacilli">lactobacilli</a>. While the infection involves a number of bacteria, it is believed that most infections start with <a href="/wiki/Gardnerella_vaginalis" title="Gardnerella vaginalis">Gardnerella vaginalis</a> creating a <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilm</a>, which allows other <a href="/wiki/Opportunistic_infection" title="Opportunistic infection">opportunistic bacteria</a> to thrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClarkTal2014_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ClarkTal2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the main risks for developing BV is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douching" title="Douching">douching</a>, which alters the vaginal flora and predisposes women to developing BV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottrell_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cottrell-20">[20]</a></sup> Douching is strongly discouraged by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="U.S. Department of Health and Human Services">U.S. Department of Health and Human Services</a> and various medical authorities, for this and other reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cottrell_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cottrell-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>BV is a risk factor for <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_inflammatory_disease" title="Pelvic inflammatory disease">pelvic inflammatory disease</a>, HIV, sexually transmitted infections (STIs), and reproductive and obstetric disorders or negative outcomes. It is possible for sexually inactive persons to develop bacterial vaginosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClarkTal2014_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ClarkTal2014-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacterial vaginosis may sometimes affect women after <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">menopause</a>. Also, subclinical iron deficiency may correlate with bacterial vaginosis in early pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A longitudinal study published in February 2006, in the <i>American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology</i>, showed a link between psychosocial stress and bacterial vaginosis persisted even when other risk factors were taken into account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Exposure to the spermicide <a href="/wiki/Nonoxynol-9" title="Nonoxynol-9">nonoxynol-9</a> does not affect the risk of developing bacterial vaginosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having a <a href="/wiki/Women_who_have_sex_with_women" title="Women who have sex with women">female partner</a> increases the risk of BV by 60%. The bacteria associated with BV have been isolated from <a href="/wiki/Male_reproductive_system" title="Male reproductive system">male genitalia</a>. BV microbiota has been found in the <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_sulcus" title="Coronal sulcus">coronal sulcus</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male_urethra" title="Male urethra">male urethra</a>, in the male partners of infected females. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uncircumcised" title="Uncircumcised">Uncircumcised</a> partners may act as a ‘<a href="/wiki/Natural_reservoir" title="Natural reservoir">reservoir</a>’ increasing the likelihood of acquiring an infection after sexual intercourse. Another mode of transmission of the BV-associated microbiota is to a female sexual partner via skin-to-skin transfer. BV may be transmitted via the perineal enteric bacteria from the microbiota of the female and male genitalia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1007" data-file-width="1241" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg/220px-Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg/330px-Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg/440px-Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bacterial_vaginosis_workup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">pH indicator</a> to detect vaginal alkalinization (here showing approximately pH 8), and a <a href="/wiki/Microscope_slide" title="Microscope slide">microscope slide</a> to microscopically detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clue_cells" title="Clue cells">clue cells</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>To make a diagnosis of bacterial vaginosis, a swab from inside the vagina should be obtained. These swabs should be tested for:</p>
<ul>
<li>A characteristic "fishy" odor on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wet_mount#Wet_mount_or_temporary_mount" title="Wet mount">wet mount</a>. This test, called the <i>whiff test</i>, is performed by adding a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic_slide" title="Microscopic slide">microscopic slide</a> containing the vaginal discharge. A characteristic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishy_odor" title="Fishy odor">fishy odor</a> is considered a positive whiff test and is suggestive of bacterial vaginosis.</li>
<li>Loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a>. To control bacterial growth, the vagina is normally slightly acidic with a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 3.8–4.2. A swab of the discharge is put onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litmus_paper" title="Litmus paper">litmus paper</a> to check its acidity. A pH greater than 4.5 is considered alkaline and is suggestive of bacterial vaginosis.</li>
<li>The presence of <i><a href="/wiki/Clue_cell" title="Clue cell">clue cells</a></i> on wet mount. Similar to the whiff test, the test for clue cells is performed by placing a drop of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> solution on a slide containing vaginal discharge. If present, clue cells can be visualized under a microscope. They are so-named because they give a clue to the reason behind the discharge. These are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epithelial" title="Epithelial">epithelial</a> cells that are coated with bacteria.</li>
</ul>
<p>Two positive results in addition to the discharge itself are enough to diagnose BV. If there is no discharge, then all three criteria are needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amsel_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amsel-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2011)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Differential diagnosis for bacterial vaginosis includes the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diagnosis_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-diagnosis-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vaginal_discharge" title="Vaginal discharge">Normal vaginal discharge</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Candidiasis" title="Candidiasis">Candidiasis</a> (thrush, or a yeast infection).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trichomoniasis" title="Trichomoniasis">Trichomoniasis</a>, an infection caused by <i><a href="/wiki/Trichomonas_vaginalis" title="Trichomonas vaginalis">Trichomonas vaginalis</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aerobic_vaginitis" title="Aerobic vaginitis">Aerobic vaginitis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The Center For Disease Control (CDC) defines STIs as "a variety of clinical syndromes and infections caused by pathogens that can be acquired and transmitted through sexual activity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> But the CDC does not specifically identify BV as sexually transmitted infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc.gov_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdc.gov-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amsel_criteria">Amsel criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Amsel criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In clinical practice BV can be diagnosed using the Amsel criteria:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amsel_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amsel-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Thin, white, yellow, homogeneous discharge</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clue_cell" title="Clue cell">Clue cells</a> on <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of vaginal fluid &gt;4.5</li>
<li>Release of a fishy odor on adding <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a>—10% <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> (KOH) solution.</li>
</ol>
<p>At least three of the four criteria should be present for a confirmed diagnosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCH_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCH-29">[29]</a></sup> A modification of the Amsel criteria accepts the presence of two instead of three factors and is considered equally diagnostic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gram_stain">Gram stain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gram stain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An alternative is to use a Gram-stained vaginal smear, with the Hay/Ison<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HayIson_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-HayIson-30">[30]</a></sup> criteria or the Nugent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nugent1991_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nugent1991-31">[31]</a></sup> criteria. The Hay/Ison criteria are defined as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCH_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-GCH-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Grade 1 (Normal): <a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus" title="Lactobacillus">Lactobacillus</a> morphotypes predominate.</li>
<li>Grade 2 (Intermediate): Mixed flora with some Lactobacilli present, but Gardnerella or Mobiluncus morphotypes also present.</li>
<li>Grade 3 (Bacterial Vaginosis): Predominantly Gardnerella and/or Mobiluncus morphotypes. Few or absent Lactobacilli. (Hay et al., 1994)</li>
</ul>
<p>Gardnerella vaginalis is the main culprit in BV. Gardnerella vaginalis is a short rod (coccobacillus). Hence, the presence of clue cells and gram variable coccobacilli are indicative or diagnostic of bacterial vaginosis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nugent_score">Nugent score</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nugent score">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nugent Score is now rarely used by physicians due to the time it takes to read the slides and requires the use of a trained microscopist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett2015_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2015-9">[9]</a></sup> A score of 0-10 is generated from combining three other scores. The scores are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>0–3 is considered negative for BV</li>
<li>4–6 is considered intermediate</li>
<li>7+ is considered indicative of BV.</li>
</ul>
<p>At least 10–20 high power (1000× oil immersion) fields are counted and an average determined.</p>

</td>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_hybridization" title="DNA hybridization">DNA hybridization</a> testing with Affirm VPIII was compared to the Gram stain using the Nugent criteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17078859_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17078859-32">[32]</a></sup> The Affirm VPIII test may be used for the rapid diagnosis of BV in symptomatic women but uses expensive proprietary equipment to read results, and does not detect other pathogens that cause BV, including Prevotella spp, Bacteroides spp, &amp; Mobiluncus spp.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some steps suggested to lower the risk include: not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douching" title="Douching">douching</a>, avoiding sex, or limiting the number of sex partners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDCbv2014_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDCbv2014-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>One review concluded that probiotics may help prevent re-occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Don2014_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Don2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Another review found that while there is tentative evidence it is not strong enough to recommend their use for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mastro2013_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mastro2013-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early evidence suggested that antibiotic treatment of male partners could re-establish the normal microbiota of the male urogenital tract and prevent the recurrence of infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup> However a 2016 Cochrane review found high quality evidence that treating the sexual partners of women with bacterial vaginosis had no effect on symptoms, clinical outcomes, or recurrence in the affected women. It also found that such treatment may lead treated sexual partners to report increased adverse events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antibiotics">Antibiotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Antibiotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treatment is typically with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> <a href="/wiki/Metronidazole" title="Metronidazole">metronidazole</a> or <a href="/wiki/Clindamycin" title="Clindamycin">clindamycin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Od2009_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Od2009-36">[36]</a></sup> They can be either given by mouth or applied inside the vagina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Od2009_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Od2009-36">[36]</a></sup> About 10% to 15% of people; however, do not improve with the first course of antibiotics and recurrence rates of up to 80% have been documented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SenokVerstraelen2009_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-SenokVerstraelen2009-37">[37]</a></sup> Recurrence rates are increased with sexual activity with the same pre-/posttreatment partner and inconsistent condom use although estrogen-containing contraceptives decrease recurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> When clindamycin is given to pregnant women symptomatic with BV before 22 weeks of gestation the risk of pre-term birth before 37 weeks of gestation is lower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LamontNhan-Chang_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-LamontNhan-Chang-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other antibiotics that may work include macrolides, lincosamides, nitroimidazoles, and penicillins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacterial vaginosis is not considered a <a href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infection" title="Sexually transmitted infection">sexually transmitted infection</a>, and treatment of a male sexual partner of a woman with bacterial vaginosis is not recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10818662_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10818662-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Probiotics">Probiotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Probiotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2009 Cochrane review found tentative but insufficient evidence for probiotics as a treatment for BV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A 2014 review reached the same conclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A 2013 review found some evidence supporting the use of probiotics during pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although previously considered a mere nuisance infection, untreated bacterial vaginosis may cause complications, such as increased susceptibility to sexually transmitted infections including HIV and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pregnancy_complication" title="Pregnancy complication">pregnancy complications</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been shown that HIV-infected women with bacterial vaginosis (BV) are more likely to transmit HIV to their sexual partners than those without BV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradshaw2015_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradshaw2015-6">[6]</a></sup> Diagnostic criteria for BV have also been associated with a female genital tract factor that induces expression of HIV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is evidence of an association between BV and increased rates of sexually transmitted infections such as HIV/AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup> BV is associated with up to a six-fold increase in HIV shedding. There is also a correlation between the absence of vaginal lactobacilli and infection by <a href="/wiki/Neisseria_gonorrhoeae" title="Neisseria gonorrhoeae">Neisseria gonorrhoeae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlamydia_trachomatis" title="Chlamydia trachomatis">Chlamydia trachomatis</a>. BV is a risk factor for viral shedding and <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex_virus" title="Herpes simplex virus">herpes simplex virus</a> type 2 infection. BV may increase the risk infection or reactivation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_papillomavirus" title="Human papillomavirus">human papillomavirus</a> (HPV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SenokVerstraelen2009_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-SenokVerstraelen2009-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, bacterial vaginosis an <a href="/wiki/Intercurrent_disease_in_pregnancy" title="Intercurrent disease in pregnancy">intercurrent disease in pregnancy</a> may increase the risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pregnancy_complications" title="Pregnancy complications">pregnancy complications</a>, most notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premature_birth" title="Premature birth">premature birth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HillierNugent1995_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-HillierNugent1995-47">[47]</a></sup> Pregnant women with BV have a higher risk of chorioamnionitis, miscarriage, preterm birth, premature rupture of membranes, and postpartum endometritis. BV is associated with gynaecological and obstetric complications. Data suggest an association between BV, tubal factor infertility, and pelvic inflammatory disease. Women with BV who are treated with vitro fertilization have a lower implantation rate and higher rates of early pregnancy loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amaya2015_24-7"><a href="#cite_note-Amaya2015-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SenokVerstraelen2009_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-SenokVerstraelen2009-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacterial_vaginosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>BV is the most common infection of the vagina in women of reproductive age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013O_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013O-10">[10]</a></sup> The percentage of women affected at any given time varies between 5% and 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup> BV is most common in parts of Africa, and least common in Asia and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup> In the United States, about 30% of those between the ages of 14 and 49 are affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC2010Stats_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC2010Stats-15">[15]</a></sup> Rates vary considerably between ethnic groups within a country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ken2013_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ken2013-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
