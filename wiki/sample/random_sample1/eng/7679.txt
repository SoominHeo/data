<p>The <b>Segway PT</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dicycle_(vehicle)" title="Dicycle (vehicle)">two-wheeled</a>, self-balancing, <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicle" title="Battery electric vehicle">battery-powered electric vehicle</a> invented by <a href="/wiki/Dean_Kamen" title="Dean Kamen">Dean Kamen</a>. It is produced by <a href="/wiki/Segway_Inc." title="Segway Inc.">Segway Inc.</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>. The name <i>Segway</i> is derived from the word <i><a href="/wiki/Segue" title="Segue">segue</a></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/-/ affix">-</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>), meaning <i>smooth transition</i>. <i>PT</i> is an abbreviation for <i>personal transporter</i> (the old suffix <i>HT</i> was an initialism for <i>human transporter</i>).</p>
<p>Computers, sensors, and electric motors in the base of the Segway PT keep the device upright when powered on with balancing enabled. The rider commands the PT to go forward or backward by shifting their weight forward or backward on the platform. The PT uses <a href="/wiki/Vibrating_structure_gyroscope" title="Vibrating structure gyroscope">gyroscopic sensors</a> and accelerometer-based leveling sensors to detect the resulting changes in its pitch angle and, to maintain balance, it drives its wheels forward or backward as needed to return its pitch to upright. In the process, the rider establishes and then maintains a desired speed by modulating the extent and duration of their fore/aft weight shifts. To turn and steer, the rider shifts the handlebar to the left or right. The PT responds by adjusting the speeds of the wheels in opposite directions causing the PT to <a href="/wiki/Yaw_(rotation)" title="Yaw (rotation)">yaw</a> and, if not traveling forward or backward, turn in place. At speed, the amount of shift of the handlebar corresponds to the amount of left or right lean required by the rider to balance themselves on the platform during a turn.</p>
<p>The maximum speed of the Segway PT is 12.5 miles per hour (20.1 km/h). The product is capable of covering 24 mi (39 km) on a fully charged <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion battery</a>, depending on terrain, riding style, and the condition of the batteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_and_pre-release_marketing">Early history and pre-release marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history and pre-release marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Segway PT was known by the names <i>Ginger</i> and <i>IT</i> before it was unveiled. <i>Ginger</i> came out of the first product that used Kamen's balancing technology, the <a href="/wiki/IBOT" title="IBOT">iBOT</a> wheelchair. During development at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Plymouth" title="University of Plymouth">University of Plymouth</a>, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Precision_Products" title="Sumitomo Precision Products">Sumitomo Precision Products</a>, the iBot was nicknamed <i>Fred Upstairs</i> (after <a href="/wiki/Fred_Astaire" title="Fred Astaire">Fred Astaire</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) because it can climb stairs: hence the name <i>Ginger</i>, after Astaire's regular film partner, <a href="/wiki/Ginger_Rogers" title="Ginger Rogers">Ginger Rogers</a>, for a successor product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The invention, development, and financing of the Segway was the subject of a narrative nonfiction book, <i>Code Name Ginger</i> (in paperback as <i>Reinventing the Wheel</i>), by journalist Steve Kemper. The leak of information from that book led to speculation about the "IT" device prior to release. The speculation created an unexpected advance buzz about the product that was, at times, hyperbolic. <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> was quoted as saying that it was "as big a deal as the PC",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-time2001-4">[4]</a></sup> though later sources quoted him as saying when first introduced to the product that its design "sucked".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Doerr" title="John Doerr">John Doerr</a> speculated that it would be more important than the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time2001_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-time2001-4">[4]</a></sup> Articles were written in major publications speculating on it being a <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">Stirling engine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> devoted an <a href="/wiki/The_Entity_(South_Park)" title="The Entity (South Park)">episode</a> to making fun of the hype before the product was released.</p>
<p>A patent for the Segway was submitted in June 1999 and granted in October 2001 (US PTO #6302230).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The product was unveiled 3 December 2001, in <a href="/wiki/Bryant_Park" title="Bryant Park">Bryant Park</a> in New York City on the <a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC News</a> morning program <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Morning_America" title="Good Morning America">Good Morning America</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It was first sold to the public in 2002. The second generation Segway product line was introduced in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, British entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Heselden" title="Jimi Heselden">Jimi Heselden</a> bought Segway Inc., but soon afterwards Heselden died after falling from a cliff while riding a Segway PT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2015, Segway was acquired by Ninebot Inc., a Beijing-based transportation robotics startup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Segway introduced its third generation models, i2 SE and x2 SE, which use its LeanSteer frame and powerbase designs, and have integrated lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PTinfokey.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/PTinfokey.jpg/220px-PTinfokey.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/PTinfokey.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PTinfokey.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Segway PT's detachable wireless InfoKey</div>

<p>The dynamics of the Segway PT are similar to a classic control problem, the <a href="/wiki/Inverted_pendulum" title="Inverted pendulum">inverted pendulum</a>. The Segway PT has <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a> powered by <a href="/wiki/Valence_Technology" title="Valence Technology">Valence Technology</a> phosphate-based lithium-ion <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>, which can be charged from household current. It balances with the help of dual computers that run proprietary software, two <a href="/wiki/Tilt_sensor" title="Tilt sensor">tilt sensors</a>, and five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyroscopic_sensor" title="Gyroscopic sensor">gyroscopic sensors</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>' <a href="/wiki/Marconi_Research_Centre" title="Marconi Research Centre">Advanced Technology Centre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Servo_drive" title="Servo drive">servo drive</a> motors rotate the wheels forwards or backwards as needed for balance or propulsion. The rider controls forward and backward movement by leaning the Segway relative to the combined center of mass of the rider and Segway, by holding the control bar closer to or farther from their body. The Segway detects the change in the balance point, and adjusts the speed at which it is balancing the rider accordingly. On older models, steering is controlled by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_grip" title="Twist grip">twist grip</a> on the left handlebar, which simply varies the speeds between the two motors, rotating the Segway PT (a decrease in the speed of the left wheel would turn the Segway PT to the left). Newer models enable the use of tilting the handle bar to steer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Segway.2C_Official_Site_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Segway.2C_Official_Site-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Segways perform best in areas with adequate sidewalks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curb_cuts" title="Curb cuts">curb cuts</a> at intersections, and ramps. They are used in some theme parks by visitors and employees. <a href="/wiki/Angel_Island_(California)" title="Angel Island (California)">Angel Island State Park</a>, in San Francisco Bay in California, offers Segway tours, but prohibits personal Segways except as needed by disabled visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The special police forces trained to protect the public during the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Summer Olympics</a> used the Segway for mobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though a Segway-focused organization, Disability Rights Advocates for Technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> advocates for Segway PT sidewalk and facility access as an <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act_of_1990" title="Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990">ADA</a> issue, Segways cannot be marketed in the US as medical devices: they have not been approved by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> as a medical device and <a href="/wiki/Johnson_%26_Johnson" title="Johnson &amp; Johnson">Johnson &amp; Johnson</a> claims exclusive rights to the medical uses of the balancing technology found in the iBOT and Segway.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Dean Kamen sold the medical rights to the technology of the <a href="/wiki/IBOT" title="IBOT">iBOT</a>, a very stable and mobile powered wheelchair, to Johnson &amp; Johnson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>A version of the Segway i2 is being marketed to the emergency medical services community. Equipped with light bars and a variety of hard and soft cases, it is sealed against wet conditions, and rated for 24 miles (39 kilometres) per charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_regulation_by_country">Use and regulation by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use and regulation by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bahrain">Bahrain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bahrain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Amwaj_Islands" title="Amwaj Islands">Amwaj Islands</a>, a group of artificial man-made islands off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Muharraq" title="Muharraq">Muharraq</a>, Segways are available for short term hire.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Police have begun using Segways to patrol certain public areas, such as Tian'anmen square. Police also use them in Beijing International Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Hong Kong International Airport use many battery powered motorized vehicles in the airport terminal indoor area. Security contractors use the Segway PT to patrol indoor restricted areas of the airport terminal. Cleaning contractors use powered tricycles to patrol. Luggage trolley contractors use two wheeled powered tractors to assist their pulling of about 30 trolleys tied together forming a trolley train.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Segway was introduced in 2010 and has sold around 250 units as of 2014. They are introduced at Ambience complexes in south Delhi and Gurgaon, the <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi_International_Airport" title="Indira Gandhi International Airport">Indira Gandhi International Airport</a> in Delhi, at many golf courses and to promote tourism at places like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kankaria" title="Kankaria">Kankaria</a> Lake, <a href="/wiki/Ahmedabad" title="Ahmedabad">Ahmedabad</a>. Also they are used for recreational purposes at <a href="/wiki/New_Town_Eco_Park" title="New Town Eco Park">New Town Eco Park</a> in Kolkata. Their growth is limited due to cost, high customs and a lack of infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_2014_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_2014-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2006, the Segway was approved for use on sidewalks and other pedestrian designated locations, and on roads without sidewalks, with obstructed sidewalks or sidewalks that lack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curb_cuts" title="Curb cuts">curb cuts</a>. The user must be over 16 years old. No license is required. The maximum allowed speed is 13.5 km/h (8.4 mph), enforced by electronic restriction put in place by the importer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Companies offering tours of <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> use the second generation i2 model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2009 the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Biblical_Zoo" title="Jerusalem Biblical Zoo">Jerusalem Biblical Zoo</a> was the first in the world to offer Segway guided tours in the zoo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaysia">Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On Penang island and in Malacca, Segways are available for short term hire.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In a court, Segway was classified as a motorcycle, owing to the power output;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> however, there is no report of registration. Segway Japan, an authorized dealer, sells Segways only to corporations to use in facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore">Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Segways were introduced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changi_Airport" title="Changi Airport">Changi Airport</a>, initially for workers who collect <a href="/wiki/Baggage_cart" title="Baggage cart">baggage carts</a> but are widely utilised by customer service and security employees. Sentosa island has a <a class="external text" href="http://www.segway-sentosa.com/" rel="nofollow">Segway rental</a> allowing use around the island or in a small circuit.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As part of a modernization drive initiated by the government, the Bangladeshi Police have started to patrol the roads with Segway Electric Vehicles. Dhaka Metropolitan Police (DMP) has already received the first batch of Segways and has showed off in Pahela Baishakh while providing security in Ramna. DMP's modernization drive includes acquisition of indigenous drones, Mobile Command Center, portable sniping zone and now, Segway electric vehicles.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Iloilo_City" title="Iloilo City">Iloilo City</a>'s tourist destination, the riverside Iloilo Esplanade is patrolled by <a href="/wiki/Philippine_National_Police" title="Philippine National Police">Philippine National Police</a> with segways. Iloilo City Government financed the segways.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Segways are used in Europe, but mainly in niche markets (such as guided city tours); they are not commonly used as a means of transportation. Their use on public streets is allowed in most countries, but often with restrictions. A few countries require vehicle insurance and a license plate.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".C3.85land_islands">Åland islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Åland islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A law revision by the Government of Åland concerning "small electrically powered vehicles" means the Segway and all other mainly one person electrical vehicles have been classified as bicycles since March 14, 2012.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Austria">Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The type i2 is (width 63 cm) narrower than 80 cm and slow enough to be legally an (electric) bicycle and therefore has to use cycle-lanes and -paths, otherwise street lanes. The type x2 reaches with its bigger wheels 84 cm width and is therefore an electric vehicle, that needs a license and insurance. Neither type may use sidewalks (lengthwise) or pedestrian zones (unless exemption stated). By 2015 one gets Segways for rent at least in Vienna (in the <a href="/wiki/Prater" title="Prater">Prater</a> amusement park and elsewhere), in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a> (since July 2013), <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiss" title="Fiss">Fiss</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> At least 2 Segways with one blue flashing light are used by police in Vienna (April 2015).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Croatia">Croatia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Croatia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Use of a Segway is allowed within city limits wherever pedestrian and bicycles are allowed, i.e., sidewalks, bicycle paths, parks, etc. Segways can be rented for city tours in cities of <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Split,_Croatia" title="Split, Croatia">Split</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">Czech Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_(01).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_%2801%29.jpg/170px-Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_%2801%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_%2801%29.jpg/255px-Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_%2801%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_%2801%29.jpg/340px-Z%C3%A1kaz_vstupu_chodc%C5%AF_-_jen_za%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_segway_%2801%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

Before 2016, the Ministry of Transport of the Czech Republic enforced the interpretation that Segway PTs fall under pedestrian status. This road sign forbids Segways but allows "normal" pedestrians on the path (the prohibition affects "Segway devices only" as a sort of pedestrians).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Until February 2016, legal status of Segway was controversial and unclear. At least since the autumn of 2010, the Ministry of Transport enforced the interpretation that a rider on the Segway is considered as a pedestrian (with possible reference to the legal definition of a pedestrian which mentions "persons on skis, rollerskates or other similar sport equipment" and with an uttered rationale that the device is quite ineligible to fulfil requirements for vehicles). The central Prague district <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praha_1" title="Praha 1">Praha 1</a> and the city of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, supported by some of transport experts including academician Petr Moos, strongly opposed this interpretation. The ministry was preparing a legal change which would mention PT Segway and skateboards explicitly in the definition of a pedestrian (which should cover also <a href="/wiki/Unicycle" title="Unicycle">unicycles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roller_shoe" title="Roller shoe">roller shoes</a> implicitly). The city of Prague proposed to bring PT transporter to the act as a quite new and special category of road traffic vehicles/participants.</p>
<p>The amendment act 48/2016 Sb., in force since 20 February 2016, defines a new strange term "osobní technický prostředek" (= personal technical device/medium) for "personal transporter with selfbalancing device" and "other similar devices". However, the text of the act uses a term "osobní přepravník" ("personal transporter") in that sense instead. The factual regulation is similar to users of skis and rollerskates, i.e. they fall under rules for pedestrians and in addition they can use cyclist lanes and cyclist paths. Compared to rollerskates, PTs have their speed limited to "speed of walking" at walkways. Municipality can restrict their traffic by municipal decree, but such a restriction needs to be marked by road signs. Since 21 March 2016, a new ordinance of the Ministry of Transport, 84/2016 Sb., which introduced several new road signs, is in force:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>Segways are used by municipal police corps in several cities as <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88" title="Plzeň">Plzeň</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olomouc" title="Olomouc">Olomouc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlovy_Vary" title="Karlovy Vary">Karlovy Vary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Znojmo" title="Znojmo">Znojmo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slan%C3%BD" title="Slaný">Slaný</a>. Since 2014, ambulance Segway is used by the private rescue service Trans Hospital.</p>
<p>Owners and operators of rental Segway transporters are associated in the "Asociace Segway ČR" which had 9 members in August 2014, all their rental shops are in the centre of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>. In October 2012, this association prescribed rules for its members which contain a list of prohibited hazardous frequented localities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Some other operators are not associated and don't respect the rules. Metro daily newspaper in a May 2015 article presented an estimate that there are ca 300 Segways in Prague streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hazard_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-hazard-30">[30]</a></sup> However, since November 2016, Segways are prohibited in the broader centre of Prague.</p>

<p>Massive usage of Segways as well as restrictions are still limited to the area of the broader centre of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>.</p>
<p>On 15 September 2014 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praha_1" title="Praha 1">Praha 1</a> placed to the <a href="/wiki/Kampa_Island" title="Kampa Island">Kampa</a> park the first Czech road signs which prohibits entrance of Segways. The sign consisted from the sign "No entrance for pedestrians" with an additional text sign "JEN ZAŘÍZENÍ SEGWAY" (only Segway devices). These signs were criticized by media and by the Ministry of Transport as confusing and incomprehensible.</p>
<p>Praha 1 prohibited for Segways also the passage of Richter House between Michalská street and Little Square at the Old Town, in 2015 or earlier. Unofficial marking on the floor was used for this prohibition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2_zna.C4.8Dky_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-2_zna.C4.8Dky-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2015, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praha_2" title="Praha 2">Praha 2</a> prohibited Segways in the area of <a href="/wiki/Vy%C5%A1ehrad" title="Vyšehrad">Vyšehrad</a> Fortress. A round sign with the text "SEGWAY" inside was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2_zna.C4.8Dky_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-2_zna.C4.8Dky-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Na_Vy.C5.A1ehrad_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Na_Vy.C5.A1ehrad-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 15 August 2015, the director general of the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_the_Czech_Republic" title="National Library of the Czech Republic">National Library</a> prohibited Segway riding in the area of <a href="/wiki/Clementinum" title="Clementinum">Clementinum</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Town,_Prague" title="Old Town, Prague">Prague Old Town</a>, however Segways were allowed to led from the side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Similarly, Segways were prohibited in the area of the Tyrš House at <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%A1_Strana" title="Malá Strana">Malá Strana</a>, the main building of the Czech organization of <a href="/wiki/Sokol" title="Sokol">Sokol</a>.</p>
<p>On the grounds of new legal definitions and authorization, on 19 July 2016, the Prague Council approved a decree (in force since 3 August 2016) that Segways (strictly speaking all "personal transporters" as defined by law) are forbidden in the whole <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Prague_Conservation_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Prague Conservation Area (page does not exist)">Prague Conservation Area</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Town,_Prague" title="Old Town, Prague">Old Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Town,_Prague" title="New Town, Prague">New Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hrad%C4%8Dany" title="Hradčany">Hradčany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%A1_Strana" title="Malá Strana">Malá Strana</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josefov_(Prague)" title="Josefov (Prague)">Josefov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vy%C5%A1ehrad" title="Vyšehrad">Vyšehrad</a>) as well as in a broad center of the city: the whole district of <a href="/wiki/Prague_7" title="Prague 7">Prague 7</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hole%C5%A1ovice" title="Holešovice">Holešovice</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Bubene%C4%8D" title="Bubeneč">Bubeneč</a> including Stromovka Park), big part of <a href="/wiki/Prague_4" title="Prague 4">Prague 4</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nusle" title="Nusle">Nusle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Podol%C3%AD_(Prague)" title="Podolí (Prague)">Podolí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bran%C3%ADk" title="Braník">Braník</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kr%C4%8D" title="Krč">Krč</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michle" title="Michle">Michle</a>), <a href="/wiki/Karl%C3%ADn" title="Karlín">Karlín</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDi%C5%BEkov" title="Žižkov">Žižkov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vinohrady" title="Vinohrady">Vinohrady</a> etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> However, the restriction became efficient after the prohibition road signs are installed. According to the marking project by TSK (the Prague road management organization), 610 zone signs were installed at 250 places, at the expenses of 4 millions CZK. Implementation of the marking should begin past the official comment procedure, in the second half of November 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> However, the official information campaing "Segway No Way" started in August already.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On 24 November 2016, the Magistrate gave its decision about the signage and the first such sign was installed on 25 November 2016, the remaining in the next two weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Segway is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">moped</a> (<i>knallert</i>). As such vehicles must be fitted with lights, license plates and mechanical brakes, the Segway is effectively banned from public roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A trial where the Segway would be classified as a bicycle has been announced running from June 1, 2010 to April 1, 2011. The trial was extended to December 1, 2011, and later to the end of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Segways are classified as low-power mopeds and therefore require license plates, effectively banning the use on public roads. On March 31, 2015, The Ministry of Transport and Communications of Finland started progress to propose changes to law to allow Segways under 25 km/h on sidewalks and reclassifying them as bicycles. Like bicycles, Segways would be required to include <a href="/wiki/Safety_reflector" title="Safety reflector">safety reflectors</a> and a bell to alert pedestrians and the driver is required to wear a bicycle helmet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Segways, also named "gyropode", are equivalent to pedestrians and obey the same rules and laws. Tours of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">Nice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montpellier" title="Montpellier">Montpellier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> and many others are offered on a daily basis.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Use of a Segway PT i2 is generally allowed on bicycle paths and public roads within city limits since July 25, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Outside city limits, the Segway may not be used on <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">federal motorways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Bundesstraße">federal highways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Landesstraße">state roads</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kreisstra%C3%9Fe" title="Kreisstraße">district roads</a>. Bicycle lanes must be used if present. Riding a Segway on <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">sidewalks</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_zone" title="Pedestrian zone">pedestrian zones</a> for city tours requires a special permit. The Segway is classified as an "electronic mobility aid", a new class of vehicle defined specifically for the Segway PT. Segways used on public roads must be equipped with <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_lighting" title="Bicycle lighting">front and rear lighting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicycle_reflector" title="Bicycle reflector">reflectors</a>, a bell, and an <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_registration_plates_of_Germany#Insurance_plates" title="Vehicle registration plates of Germany">insurance plate</a>. The driver must have procured a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_insurance" title="Vehicle insurance">vehicle insurance</a> and hold at least an <i>M type</i> (<a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">moped</a>) license.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Segways are permitted in most public places. A Segway tour of the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Park" title="Phoenix Park">Phoenix Park</a> is available. They are permitted in certain areas on bicycle paths around <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Airport_Police_(Ireland)" title="Airport Police (Ireland)">Airport Police Service</a> stationed at <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a> use the Segway i2 police patroller model. In 2011, a private tour operator started a City of Dublin Segway Tour. In October 2012 the <a href="/wiki/Garda_S%C3%ADoch%C3%A1na" title="Garda Síochána">Garda Síochána</a> began using two examples in the <a href="/wiki/Grafton_Street" title="Grafton Street">Grafton Street</a> area, funded by the Dublin Business Community.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Use of a Segway is allowed within city limits wherever pedestrians or bicycles are allowed, i.e., sidewalks, bicycle paths, parks, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Lithuania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Policemen in <a href="/wiki/Vilnius" title="Vilnius">Vilnius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaunas" title="Kaunas">Kaunas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palanga" title="Palanga">Palanga</a> use segways to patrol. Also segways are available to rent for tourists in Vilnius.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Luxembourg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Segways are legal on <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a> trails and roads. They are the equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Electric_bicycle" title="Electric bicycle">electrical bikes</a> and obey the same rules and laws. Tours of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourg_(city)" title="Luxembourg (city)">Luxembourg city</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remich" title="Remich">Remich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rumelange" title="Rumelange">Rumelange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schengen,_Luxembourg" title="Schengen, Luxembourg">Schengen</a> are offered on a daily basis.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malta">Malta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Malta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Policemen have started testing Segways on patrol in <a href="/wiki/Valletta" title="Valletta">Valletta</a>. Informed sources said the Malta Police are considering the purchase of a number of Segways fitted for police work, including a siren and space for radio and other equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In April 2008, the Dutch Government announced that it would ease the ban it had imposed in January 2007 that made it illegal to use a Segway on public roads in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Until recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, a tolerance policy was in place due to the inability of the authorities to classify the Segway as a vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> However, certain handicapped people, primarily heart and lung patients, are allowed to use the Segway, but only on the pavement. From 1 July 2008, anyone over the age of 16 is permitted to use a Segway on Dutch roads but users need to buy custom insurance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Amsterdam police officers are testing the Segway. In Rotterdam the Segway is used regularly by police officers and city watches.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Because of the top speed of 20 km/h, the Segway was classified as a <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">moped</a> in Norway. Prior to 2014, there were requirements for registration, insurance, age limit, drivers licenses and helmets to operate a Segway in the country. Therefore, Segways were not originally able to be used legally on public or private roads or on private property in Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Segways became legal in Norway on July 1, 2014 on all public roads with speed limits 60 km/h or less, sidewalks and bicycle lanes for ages 16 and older without requiring registration or insurance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Has been on hire in some towns and cities such as Warsaw, Kraków and Krynica for use on public roads and pavements.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Segways are legal on public paths from age 18 (and below, when accompanied by adults) as an equivalent to pedestrian traffic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and are used by many local police forces (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pol%C3%ADcia_Municipal" title="Polícia Municipal">Polícia Municipal</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and by <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pol%C3%ADcia_Mar%C3%ADtima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Polícia Marítima (page does not exist)">Polícia Marítima</a></i> (a Navy unit), for beach patrolling. They are also used (rented) by tour operators across the country, and by shopping security guards.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>It was unlawful to use a Segway on any public road or pavement in Sweden<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> until 18 December 2008 when the Segway was re-classified as a <i>cykel klass II</i> (class 2 bicycle).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Segways are popular in Stockholm where tours are led through the streets by Segway guides with modified PTs and courses are set out at sites such as the Royal Haga park. On 1 October 2010 the Segway and similar one person electrical vehicles were re-classified as bicycles.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Segway is classified as a light motorcycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Only the PT i2 has been approved for use in Switzerland. The PT i2 may be used on roads provided that it is equipped with a Swiss Road Kit and a license plate. The Swiss Road Kit has front and back lighting, a battery source, and a license plate holder. Use on sidewalks and pedestrian zones is prohibited. An exception is made for handicapped individuals, who must obtain in advance a special authorization from the Swiss Federal Roads Office. The Segway PT i180 may also be registered for use on specific request. However, the PT i180 must be equipped with a left/right turn indicator system before it may be admitted for road use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Distributed in the UK by Segway-UK, the Segway is classified as a powered vehicle and subject to Road Traffic law — with the effect that, because the Segway is deemed not to meet required safety standards, it is unlawful to use a Segway anywhere other than on private property with the owner's permission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> While in opposition in 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats" title="Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrats</a> lobbied the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> Government to change the law to allow Segways to use public cycle lanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In July 2010, a man was charged under the <a href="/wiki/Highway_Act_1835" title="Highway Act 1835">Highway Act 1835</a> in <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a> for riding his Segway on the pavement, and was prosecuted and fined £75 in January 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> His conviction was upheld by the High Court on appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Restrictions on motorized vehicle use are set by provinces individually. In Alberta, Segways cannot legally be driven on public roads including sidewalks abutting public roads. Segways cannot legally be driven on city-owned bicycle paths in Calgary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Segways are allowed on private land with the landowner's permission. (Some malls allow their use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) In British Columbia, Segways cannot legally be operated on B.C. roads or on sidewalks because they cannot be licensed or insured as a vehicle in B.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In Ontario, the Ministry of Transportation started a pilot program allowing Segways to be used by people 14 years or older with a disability, Canada Post door-to-door delivery personnel, and police officers. It was originally planned to end on October 19, 2011, but was extended by two years, and then extended again an additional five years (to October 19, 2018), due to limited participation. Prior to the end of the pilot program, the Ministry of Transportation will assess the data and information gathered from the pilot decide whether to allow Segways and how to legislate them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The company has challenged bans and sought exemption from sidewalk restrictions in over 30 states. The Segway PT has been banned from use on <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">sidewalks</a> and in public transportation in a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities" title="Municipalities">municipalities</a>, often because it is not classified as a device used for medical purposes. Advocacy groups for pedestrians and the blind in the US have been critical of Segway PT use: America Walks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Council_of_the_Blind" title="American Council of the Blind">American Council of the Blind</a> oppose allowing people, even those with disabilities, to drive the Segway PT on sidewalks and have actively lobbied against any such legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Today, Segways are allowed on sidewalks in most states, though local municipalities may forbid them. Many states also allow them on bicycle lanes or on roads with speed limits of up to 25 mph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, the U.S. government Department of Justice—amending regulations that implement title II of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act" title="Americans with Disabilities Act">Americans with Disabilities Act</a> (ADA)—ruled that the Segway is an "other power-driven mobility device" and its use must be permitted unless the covered entity can demonstrate that users cannot operate the class of devices in accordance with legitimate safety requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>A fact sheet published by the US Justice Department states: "People with mobility, circulatory, respiratory, or neurological disabilities use many kinds of devices for mobility. Some use walkers, canes, crutches, or braces. Some use manual or power wheelchairs or electric scooters. In addition, advances in technology have given rise to new devices, such as <i><u>Segways</u><sup>®</sup>,</i> that some people with disabilities use as mobility devices, including many veterans injured while serving in the military. And more advanced devices will inevitably be invented, providing more mobility options for people with disabilities." It clearly gives those with mobility issues, whether certified with a disability or if the user just " states verbally that the OPDMD is being used because of a mobility disability." It further states that "the person using the device to provide credible assurance that the device is used because of a disability. If the person presents a valid, State-issued disability parking placard or card or a State-issued proof of disability, that must be accepted as credible assurance on its face. If the person does not have this documentation, but states verbally that the OPDMD is being used because of a mobility disability, that also must be accepted as credible assurance, unless the person is observed doing something that contradicts the assurance." Entitled users are required to be able to use Segways freely and should not be denied usage no less that a cane or crutches would be allowed to be taken away. There is some allowance in only some very specific circumstances where usage would be considered unsafe. Source: www.ada.gov/opdmd.htm</p>
<p>It would seem that the overreaction of the FAA and almost all airlines and some municipalities such as New York City and it's transit system to ban ALL "hoverboards" would be in conflict wit federal ADA rules. see: <a class="external text" href="http://www.theverge.com/2016/1/27/10842342/nyc-hoverboard-ban-mta-subways-buses" rel="nofollow">www.theverge.com/2016/1/27/10842342/nyc-hoverboard-ban-mta-subways-buses</a> and www.iata.org/whatwedo/cargo/dgr/Documents/small-lithium-battery-powered-vehicles.pdf</p>
<p>Semi-ambulatory Americans have previously benefitied from Segway use, even in New York City. Source: nytimes.com/2004/10/14/technology/circuits/disabled-embrace-segway.html</p>
<p>Latest models of Segways have been approved by UL Labs for fire safety. As of March 2016, Underwriters Laboratories has a testing procedure for hoverboards, UL 2272, At least some Segways have already been approved, including the Segway MiniPro. See: ULStandards.ul.com/standard/?id=2272 Unfortunately, defective batteries in hoverboards sold in 2015 have now raised the barrier to usage and some legal challenges may be required going forward.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Segs4Vets" title="Segs4Vets">Segs4Vets</a> is a nonprofit organization that provide Segway PTs to the men and women of the United States military whose service in Operations <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraqi_Freedom" title="Iraqi Freedom">Iraqi Freedom</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enduring_Freedom" title="Enduring Freedom">Enduring Freedom</a> resulted in permanent disability and difficulty walking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>San Francisco</dt>
</dl>
<p>In November 2002, before it was widely available, the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> banned the Segway PT from <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">sidewalks</a> citing safety concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> However, a number of Segway Tour operations use them in cycle lanes and designated trails.</p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a></dt>
</dl>
<p>In September 2010, Dr. Mary Pat McKay, a professor of emergency medicine and public health at <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">George Washington University</a>, issued a statement that injuries from the transporters were becoming both more common and more serious. McKay's case review in the <a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Emergency_Medicine" title="Annals of Emergency Medicine">Annals of Emergency Medicine</a> noted 41 Segway injuries between April 2005 and November 2008 among GWU emergency room patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The District of Columbia categorizes Segways as a "personal mobility device" which means Segway users follow D.C.'s bicycle laws, which do not require Segway users to wear helmets and other protective gear. Users are not allowed to wear headphones with the exception of hearing aids or other devices that only require the use of one ear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Accidents have continued to occur in the District of Columbia, including a June 2012 incident involving a Dallas, Texas high school student who lost control of his Segway after fainting during a commercially operated Segway tour. Pursuant to the District's Code, Segways are prohibited on sidewalks within the Central Business District—bounded by 23rd Street NW to the west, Massachusetts Avenue to the north, Second Street NE to the east, and D Street to the south—unless operated by a person with a disability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Disney</dt>
</dl>
<p>In February 2004, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Disney</a> banned Segway PTs from its <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">theme parks</a>, stating they had not been approved by the FDA as medical devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In the same month,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Disney began offering Segway tours of its <a href="/wiki/Epcot" title="Epcot">Epcot</a> theme park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In early August 2007, Disney began offering a similar guided tour in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney_California_Adventure_Park" title="Disney California Adventure Park">Disney California Adventure Park</a> park in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Mexico there is no regulation that limits Segway use in public spaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominican_Republic">Dominican Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Dominican Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The devices are used on group ecotours in the area south of Punta Cana.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> laws are determined at the state &amp; territory level, each differing in their adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Road_Rules" title="Australian Road Rules">Australian Road Rules</a>. It is generally illegal to use the two-wheeled electric transporters in public places and on roads throughout Australia.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>, use of Segways is illegal on roads and other public places, but, as of June 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, was permitted around Canberra's Lake Burley Griffin and other tourist attractions, subject to training, safety equipment and speed limit requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, the Segway has been confirmed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roads_and_Traffic_Authority" title="Roads and Traffic Authority">Roads and Traffic Authority</a> as being illegal on both roads and footpaths. "In simple terms, riders are way too exposed to mix with general traffic on a road and too fast, heavy and consequently dangerous to other users on footpaths or cycle paths."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Although this does not render them totally illegal (they may still, for example, be used on private property), their uses are limited enough that they are not sold to the general public.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, the use of the Segway became legal on the 1st of August 2013. Queensland transport Minister Scott Emerson noted that it makes sense for Segways to be allowed on public paths across Queensland, given users wear helmets.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, the law enables Electric Personal Transporters (EPT) (Segways) to be used as part of a supervised commercial tour, being run by an operator that holds the appropriate approvals. You may use an EPT on private property. Tour operators should approach the Local Authority where they wish to operate the tour. Local authorities have ultimate responsibility for approving tour operators within their respective areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Segway_PT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> the Segway PT is classed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobility_device" title="Mobility device">mobility device</a>, in the same category as a mobility scooter or electric wheelchair. Mobility Devices must be ridden on footpaths where possible, at a speed that does not endanger others, and give way to pedestrians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> This ruling might not be consistently applied: in 2011, police in <a href="/wiki/Taupo" title="Taupo">Taupo</a> in New Zealand's the <a href="/wiki/North_Island" title="North Island">North Island</a> had to stop using Segways because there is no separate vehicle classification that applies to them, requiring their registration as roadworthy in the same manner as cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Segways are sometimes used to tow vehicles transporting dairy products such as <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">cream</a> along <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_motorways" title="New Zealand motorways">motorways</a>.</p>
