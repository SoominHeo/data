<p><b>Sergey Mikhaylovich Brin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Серге́й Миха́йлович Брин</span>; born August 21, 1973) is a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a>-born American <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer scientist</a>, internet entrepreneur, and philanthropist.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Together with <a href="/wiki/Larry_Page" title="Larry Page">Larry Page</a>, he co-founded <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>. Brin is the President of Google's parent company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphabet_Inc" title="Alphabet Inc">Alphabet Inc</a>. In October 2016 (the most recent period for which figures are available), Brin was the 12th richest person in the world, with an estimated net worth of <span style="white-space: nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>39.2<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profile_Sergey_Brin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Profile_Sergey_Brin-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin immigrated to the United States with his family from the Soviet Union at the age of 6. He earned his bachelor's degree at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park" title="University of Maryland, College Park">University of Maryland</a>, following in his father's and grandfather's footsteps by studying <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, as well as computer science. After graduation, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> to acquire a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a> in computer science. There he met Page, with whom he later became friends. They crammed their dormitory room with inexpensive computers and applied Brin's <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">data mining</a> system to build a <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a>. The program became popular at Stanford, and they suspended their PhD studies to start up Google in a rented garage.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> referred to Brin as an "<a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> Man", and as someone who believes that "knowledge is always good, and certainly always better than ignorance", a philosophy that is summed up by Google's mission statement, "Organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-8">[8]</a></sup> and unofficial, sometimes controversial motto, "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_be_evil" title="Don't be evil">Don't be evil</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brin was born in Moscow in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, to <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Russia" title="History of the Jews in Russia">Russian Jewish</a> parents, Yevgenia and Mikhail Brin, both graduates of <a href="/wiki/Moscow_State_University" title="Moscow State University">Moscow State University</a> (MSU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YouTube_Freedom_25_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-YouTube_Freedom_25-11">[11]</a></sup> His father is a mathematics professor at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" title="University of Maryland">University of Maryland</a>, and his mother a researcher at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moment_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moment-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1979, when Brin was five years old, his family felt compelled to emigrate out of the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Mark_Malseed" title="Mark Malseed">Mark Malseed</a>, co-author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Google_Story" title="The Google Story">The Google Story</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vise_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vise-15">[15]</a></sup> Sergey's father explained how he was "forced to abandon his dream of becoming an <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomer</a> even before he reached college." He said "<a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party</a> heads barred Jews from upper professional ranks by denying them entry to universities, Jews were excluded from the <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> department, in particular, at the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Moscow_State_University" title="Moscow State University">Moscow State University</a>, because Soviet leaders did not trust them with nuclear rocket research." Mikhail Brin therefore changed his major to mathematics where he received nearly straight A's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moment_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moment-12">[12]</a></sup> In another interview with <a href="/wiki/Dominic_Lawson" title="Dominic Lawson">Dominic Lawson</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i>, Mikhail said: "No one would consider me for <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate school</a> because I was Jewish." He went on to tell Lawson how MSU required Jews to take their entrance exams in different rooms from non-Jewish applicants, and how they were marked on a harsher scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Brin family lived in a three-room apartment in central Moscow, which they also shared with Sergey's paternal grandmother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moment_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Moment-12">[12]</a></sup> Brin told Malseed, "I've known for a long time that my father wasn't able to pursue the career he wanted", but Brin only picked up the details years later after they had settled in the United States. In 1977, after his father returned from a mathematics conference in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, Poland, Mikhail Brin announced that it was time for the family to emigrate. "We cannot stay here any more", he told his wife and mother. At the conference, he was able to "mingle freely with colleagues from the United States, France, England and Germany and discovered that his intellectual brethren in the West were not 'monsters.'" He added, "I was the only one in the family who decided it was really important to leave."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moment_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Moment-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sergey's mother was less willing to leave their home in Moscow, where they had spent their entire lives. Malseed writes, "For Genia, the decision ultimately came down to Sergey. While her husband admits he was thinking as much about his own future as his son's, for her, 'it was 80/20' about Sergey." They formally applied for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exit_visa" title="Exit visa">exit visa</a> in September 1978, and as a result his father was "promptly fired". For related reasons, his mother also had to leave her job. For the next eight months, without any steady income, they were forced to take on temporary jobs as they waited, afraid their request would be denied as it was for many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refuseniks" title="Refuseniks">refuseniks</a>. During this time his parents shared responsibility for looking after him and his father taught himself <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a>. In May 1979, they were granted their official exit visas and were allowed to leave the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moment_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Moment-12">[12]</a></sup> At an interview in October 2000, Brin said, "I know the hard times that my parents went through there and am very thankful that I was brought to the States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the summer of 1990, a few weeks before his 17th birthday, his father led a group of high school math students, including Sergey, on a two-week <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exchange_program" title="Exchange program">exchange program</a> to the Soviet Union. His roommate on the trip was future Carnegie Mellon University computer science professor John Stamper. As Brin recalls, the trip awakened his childhood fear of authority and he remembered that "his first impulse on confronting Soviet oppression had been to throw pebbles at a police car." Malseed adds, "On the second day of the trip, while the group toured a <a href="/wiki/Sanatorium" title="Sanatorium">sanatorium</a> in the countryside near Moscow, Brin took his father aside, looked him in the eye and said, 'Thank you for taking us all out of Russia.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moment_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Moment-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin attended <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">elementary school</a> at Paint Branch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montessori_School" title="Montessori School">Montessori School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Adelphi,_Maryland" title="Adelphi, Maryland">Adelphi, Maryland</a>, but he received further education at home; his father, a professor in the department of mathematics at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" title="University of Maryland">University of Maryland</a>, encouraged him to learn mathematics and his family helped him retain his Russian-language skills. He attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt_High_School_(Greenbelt,_Maryland)" title="Eleanor Roosevelt High School (Greenbelt, Maryland)">Eleanor Roosevelt High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt,_Maryland" title="Greenbelt, Maryland">Greenbelt, Maryland</a>. In September 1990 Brin enrolled in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" title="University of Maryland">University of Maryland</a>, where he received his Bachelor of Science from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_Department_of_Computer_Science" title="University of Maryland Department of Computer Science">Department of Computer Science</a> in 1993 with honors in computer science and mathematics, which is part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_College_of_Computer,_Mathematical,_and_Natural_Sciences" title="University of Maryland College of Computer, Mathematical, and Natural Sciences">University of Maryland College of Computer, Mathematical, and Natural Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resume_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-resume-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin began his graduate study in computer science at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> on a <a href="/wiki/NSF-GRF" title="NSF-GRF">graduate fellowship</a> from the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>. In 1993, he interned at <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Research" title="Wolfram Research">Wolfram Research</a>, the developers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematica" title="Mathematica">Mathematica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resume_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-resume-18">[18]</a></sup> As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> he was on leave from his PhD studies at Stanford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_engine_development">Search engine development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Search engine development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During an orientation for new students at Stanford, he met Larry Page. They seemed to disagree on most subjects. But after spending time together, they "became intellectual soul-mates and close friends". Brin's focus was on developing data mining systems while Page's was in extending "the concept of inferring the importance of a research paper from its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citations" title="Citations">citations</a> in other papers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-8">[8]</a></sup> Together, the pair authored a paper titled "The Anatomy of a Large-Scale <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">Hypertextual</a> Web Search Engine".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>To convert the backlink data gathered by BackRub's <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a> into a measure of importance for a given web page, Brin and Page developed the PageRank algorithm, and realized that it could be used to build a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_engine" title="Search engine">search engine</a> far superior to existing ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-21">[21]</a></sup> The new algorithm relied on a new kind of technology that analyzed the relevance of the <a href="/wiki/Backlink" title="Backlink">backlinks</a> that connected one Web page to another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moschovitis_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moschovitis-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>Combining their ideas, the pair began utilizing Page's dormitory room as a machine laboratory, and extracted spare parts from inexpensive computers to create a device that they used to connect the nascent search engine with Stanford's broadband campus network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-21">[21]</a></sup> After filling Page's room with equipment, they then converted Brin's dorm room into an office and programming center, where they tested their new search engine designs on the Web. The rapid growth of their project caused Stanford's computing infrastructure to experience problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist.com_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Page and Brin used the former's basic <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> programming skills to set up a simple search page for users, as they did not have a web page developer to create anything visually elaborate. They also began using any computer part they could find to assemble the necessary computing power to handle searches by multiple users. As their search engine grew in popularity among Stanford users, it required additional <a href="/wiki/Server_(computing)" title="Server (computing)">servers</a> to process the queries. In August 1996, the initial version of Google, still on the Stanford University website, was made available to Internet users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiredbirth_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-wiredbirth-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>BackRub already exhibited the rudimentary functions and characteristics of a search engine: a query input was entered and it provided a list of backlinks ranked by importance. Page recalled: "We realized that we had a querying tool. It gave you a good overall ranking of pages and ordering of follow-up pages."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive.wired.com_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive.wired.com-25">[25]</a></sup> Page said that in mid-1998 they finally realized the further potential of their project: "Pretty soon, we had 10,000 searches a day. And we figured, maybe this is really real."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist.com_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some compared Page and Brin's vision to the impact of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Johannes Gutenberg</a>, the inventor of modern <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In 1440, Johannes Gutenberg introduced Europe to the mechanical printing press, printing Bibles for mass consumption. The technology allowed for books and manuscripts‍—‌originally replicated by hand‍—‌to be printed at a much faster rate, thus spreading knowledge and helping to usher in the European Renaissance<span class="nowrap"> </span>... Google has done a similar job.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The comparison was also noted by the authors of <i>The Google Story</i>: "Not since Gutenberg<span class="nowrap"> </span>... has any new invention empowered individuals, and transformed access to information, as profoundly as Google."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vise_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vise-15">[15]</a></sup> Also, not long after the two "cooked up their new engine for web searches, they began thinking about information that was at the time beyond the web," such as digitizing books and expanding health information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist.com_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_interests">Other interests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other interests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brin is working on other, more personal projects that reach beyond Google. For example, he and Page are trying to help solve the world's energy and climate problems at Google's philanthropic arm, <a href="/wiki/Google.org" title="Google.org">Google.org</a>, which invests in the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energy</a> industry to find wider sources of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. The company acknowledges that its founders want "to solve really big problems using technology".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-windpower_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-windpower-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2010, for example, they invested in a major offshore wind power development to assist the East coast power grid,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> which will eventually become one of about a dozen offshore wind farms that are proposed for the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A week earlier they introduced a car that, with "artificial intelligence", can drive itself using video cameras and radar sensors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-windpower_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-windpower-27">[27]</a></sup> In the future, drivers of cars with similar sensors would have fewer accidents. These safer vehicles could therefore be built lighter and require less fuel consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> They are trying to get companies to create innovative solutions to increasing the world's energy supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-31">[31]</a></sup> He is an investor in <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Motors" title="Tesla Motors">Tesla Motors</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tesla-motors-investment_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-tesla-motors-investment-32">[32]</a></sup> which has developed the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Roadster" title="Tesla Roadster">Tesla Roadster</a>, a 244-mile (393 km) range <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicle" title="Battery electric vehicle">battery electric vehicle</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">Tesla Model S</a>, a 265-mile (426 km) range <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicle" title="Battery electric vehicle">battery electric vehicle</a>.</p>
<p>In 2004, he and Page were named "Persons of the Week" by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_News_with_Charles_Gibson" title="World News with Charles Gibson">ABC World News Tonight</a></i>. In January 2005 he was nominated to be one of the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a>'s "Young Global Leaders". In June 2008, Brin invested $4.5 million in <a href="/wiki/Space_Adventures" title="Space Adventures">Space Adventures</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a> company. His investment will serve as a deposit for a reservation on one of Space Adventures' proposed flights in 2011. Space Adventures, the only company that sends tourists to space, has sent five of them so far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin and Page jointly own a customized <a href="/wiki/Boeing_767" title="Boeing 767">Boeing 767-200</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Dassault/Dornier_Alpha_Jet" title="Dassault/Dornier Alpha Jet">Dornier Alpha Jet</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dornierjet_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-dornierjet-34">[34]</a></sup> and pay $1.3 million a year to house them and two <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_V" title="Gulfstream V">Gulfstream V</a> jets owned by Google executives at <a href="/wiki/Moffett_Federal_Airfield" title="Moffett Federal Airfield">Moffett Federal Airfield</a>. The aircraft have had scientific equipment installed by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> to allow experimental data to be collected in flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Brin has been involved with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Glass" title="Project Glass">Project Glass</a> program and has demoed eyeglass prototypes. Project Glass is a research and development program by <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> to develop an <a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a> <a href="/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">head-mounted display</a> (HMD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The intended purpose of Project Glass products would be the hands-free displaying of information currently available to most <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> users,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and allowing for interaction with the Internet via <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language</a> voice commands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin was also involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_driverless_car" title="Google driverless car">Google driverless car</a> project. In September 2012, at the signing of the California Driverless Vehicle Bill,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Brin predicted that within five years, robotic cars will be available to the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin is a supporter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_vitro_meat" title="In vitro meat">lab-grown meat</a> and kite-energy systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Economist</i> magazine describes Brin's approach to life, like Page's, as based on a vision summed up by Google's motto, "of making all the world's information 'universally accessible and useful<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship_of_Google_in_China">Censorship of Google in China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Censorship of Google in China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Censorship_in_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Censorship in the People's Republic of China">Censorship in the People's Republic of China</a>.</div>
<p>Remembering his youth and his family's reasons for leaving the Soviet Union, he "agonized over Google's decision to appease the Communist government of China by allowing it to censor search engine results", but he decided that the Chinese would still be better off than without having Google available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 12, 2010, Google reported <a href="/wiki/Operation_Aurora" title="Operation Aurora">a large cyber attack on its computers and corporate infrastructure</a> that began a month earlier, which included accessing two Gmail accounts and the theft of Google's intellectual property. After the attack was determined to have originated in China, the company stated that it would no longer agree to censor its search engine in China and may exit the country altogether. <a href="/wiki/David_Drummond_(Google)" title="David Drummond (Google)">David Drummond</a>, Google's Senior Vice President of Corporate Development, reported that "a primary goal of the attackers was accessing the Gmail accounts of Chinese human rights activists, but that the attack also targeted 20 other large companies in the finance, technology, media and chemical sectors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> It was later reported that the attack had also targeted "one of Google's crown jewels, a password system that controls access by millions of users worldwide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late March 2010, it officially discontinued its China-based search engine while keeping its uncensored Hong Kong site in operation. Speaking for Google, Brin stated during an interview, "One of the reasons I am glad we are making this move in China is that the China situation was really emboldening other countries to try and implement their own firewalls."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-47">[47]</a></sup> During another interview with <i>Der Spiegel</i>, he added, "For us it has always been a discussion about how we can best fight for openness on the Internet. We believe that this is the best thing that we can do for preserving the principles of the openness and freedom of information on the Internet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Byron_Dorgan" title="Byron Dorgan">Byron Dorgan</a> stated that "Google's decision is a strong step in favor of freedom of expression and information." And Congressman <a href="/wiki/Bob_Goodlatte" title="Bob Goodlatte">Bob Goodlatte</a> said, "I applaud Google for its courageous step to stop censoring search results on Google.cn. Google has drawn a line in the sand and is shining a light on the very dark area of individual liberty restrictions in China."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> From the business perspective, many recognize that the move is likely to affect Google's profits. <i>The New Republic</i> adds that "Google seems to have arrived at the same link that was obvious to <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov" title="Andrei Sakharov">Andrei Sakharov</a>: the one between science and freedom," referring to the move as "heroism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sergey_Brin,_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="1000" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Sergey_Brin%2C_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg/250px-Sergey_Brin%2C_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Sergey_Brin%2C_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg/375px-Sergey_Brin%2C_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Sergey_Brin%2C_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg/500px-Sergey_Brin%2C_Web_2.0_Conference.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>In May 2007, Brin married biotech analyst and entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Anne_Wojcicki" title="Anne Wojcicki">Anne Wojcicki</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> They had a son together in December 2008 and a daughter in late 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In August 2013, it was announced that Brin and his then-wife were living separately after Brin had become romantically involved with <a href="/wiki/Google_Glass" title="Google Glass">Google Glass</a>'s marketing director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In June 2015, Brin and Wojcicki finalized their divorce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin's mother, Eugenia, has been diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>. In 2008, he decided to make a donation to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_School_of_Medicine" title="University of Maryland School of Medicine">University of Maryland School of Medicine</a>, where his mother is being treated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Brin used the services of <a href="/wiki/23andMe" title="23andMe">23andMe</a> and discovered that although Parkinson's is generally not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary" title="Hereditary">hereditary</a>, both he and his mother possess a <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/LRRK2" title="LRRK2">LRRK2</a> gene (G2019S) that puts the likelihood of him developing Parkinson's in later years between 20% and 80%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-8">[8]</a></sup> When asked whether ignorance was not bliss in such matters, he stated that his knowledge means that he can now take measures to ward off the disease. An editorial in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> magazine states that "Mr Brin regards his mutation of LRRK2 as a bug in his personal code, and thus as no different from the bugs in computer code that Google’s engineers fix every day. By helping himself, he can therefore help others as well. He considers himself lucky. ... But Mr. Brin was making a much bigger point. Isn’t knowledge always good, and certainly always better than ignorance?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brin and his ex-wife also run The Brin Wojcicki Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dynamodata_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-dynamodata-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_accolades">Awards and accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards and accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002.E2.80.932009">2002–2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2002–2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Brin, along with Larry Page, was named the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technology_Review" title="Technology Review">Technology Review</a> <a href="/wiki/TR35" title="TR35">TR100</a>, as one of the top 100 innovators in the world under the age of 35.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 2003, both Brin and Page received an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary</a> MBA from <a href="/wiki/IE_Business_School" title="IE Business School">IE Business School</a> "for embodying the entrepreneurial spirit and lending momentum to the creation of new businesses...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In 2004, they received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marconi_Foundation" title="Marconi Foundation">Marconi Foundation</a> Prize, the "Highest Award in Engineering", and were elected <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellows</a> of the Marconi Foundation at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>. "In announcing their selection, John Jay Iselin, the Foundation's president, congratulated the two men for their invention that has fundamentally changed the way information is retrieved today."</p>
<p>In 2003, Brin and Page were both Award Recipients and National Finalists for the EY Entrepreneur of the Year Award <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, Brin received the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Achievement" title="Academy of Achievement">Academy of Achievement's Golden Plate Award</a> with Larry Page at a ceremony in Chicago, Illinois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldenplate_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldenplate-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009.E2.80.93present">2009–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Brin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2009–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> decided Brin and Page were the fifth most powerful people in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Earlier that same year, in February, Brin was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>, which is "among the highest professional distinctions accorded to an engineer ... [and] honors those who have made outstanding contributions to engineering research, practice...". He was selected specifically, "for leadership in development of rapid indexing and retrieval of relevant information from the World Wide Web".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In their "Profiles" of Fellows, the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> included a number of earlier awards:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>he was a featured speaker at the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a> and the Technology, Entertainment and Design Conference. ... <i><a href="/wiki/PC_Magazine" title="PC Magazine">PC Magazine</a></i> has praised Google in the Top 100 Web Sites and Search Engines (1998) and awarded Google the Technical Excellence Award, for Innovation in Web Application Development in 1999. In 2000, Google earned a <a href="/wiki/Webby_Award" title="Webby Award">Webby Award</a>, a People's Voice Award for technical achievement, and in 2001, was awarded Outstanding Search Service, Best Image Search Engine, Best Design, Most Webmaster Friendly Search Engine, and Best Search Feature at the Search Engine Watch Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to <i>Forbes</i>, he is the 12th richest person in the world with a personal wealth of US$39.2 billion as of October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FSB_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FSB-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title="intro references a 2016 'rich list' (October 2016)">contradictory</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
