<p><b>Sergei Mironovich Kirov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Серге́й Миро́нович Ки́ров</span>), born <b>Kostrikov</b> (<span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Ко́стриков</span>; 27 March [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 15 March] 1886 – 1 December 1934), was a prominent early <a href="/wiki/Old_Bolshevik" title="Old Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> leader in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Kirov rose through the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party</a> ranks to become head of the party organization in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Leningrad</a>.</p>
<p>On 1 December 1934, Kirov was shot and killed by a gunman at his offices in the <a href="/wiki/Smolny_Institute" title="Smolny Institute">Smolny Institute</a>. Some historians place the blame for his assassination at the hands of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> and believe the <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> organized his execution, but conclusive evidence for this claim remains lacking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Kirov's death served as one of the pretexts for Stalin's escalation of <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">repression</a> against dissident elements of the Party, and disarming of the Party (every Party member was issued with a revolver up to that time, when Stalin had them all taken away), culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a> of the late 1930s in which many of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bolshevik" title="Old Bolshevik">Old Bolsheviks</a> were arrested, expelled from the party, and executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Complicity in Kirov's assassination was a common charge to which the accused confessed in the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Trials" title="Moscow Trials">show trials of the period</a>.</p>
<p>The cities of <a href="/wiki/Kirov,_Kirov_Oblast" title="Kirov, Kirov Oblast">Kirov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kropyvnytskyi" title="Kropyvnytskyi">Kirovohrad</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirovakan" title="Kirovakan">Kirovakan</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirovabad" title="Kirovabad">Kirovabad</a>, as well as a few <a href="/wiki/Kirovsk,_Russia" title="Kirovsk, Russia">Kirovsks</a>, were renamed in Kirov's honor after his assassination. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collapse_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Collapse of the Soviet Union">collapse of the Soviet Union</a>, Kirovakan and Kirovabad returned to their original names: Vanadzor and Ganja, respectively. In order to comply with <a href="/wiki/Decommunization_in_Ukraine" title="Decommunization in Ukraine">decommunization laws</a> Kirovohrad was renamed in July 2016 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_parliament" title="Ukrainian parliament">Ukrainian parliament</a> to Kropyvnytskyi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KcbKcJ16_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-KcbKcJ16-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Kirov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sergey Kirov was born Sergei Mironovich Kostrikov into a poor family in <a href="/wiki/Urzhum,_Urzhumsky_District,_Kirov_Oblast" title="Urzhum, Urzhumsky District, Kirov Oblast">Urzhum</a> (then in <a href="/wiki/Vyatka_Governorate" title="Vyatka Governorate">Vyatka Governorate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>, subsequently in <a href="/wiki/Kirov_Oblast" title="Kirov Oblast">Kirov Oblast</a>) as one of seven children born to Miron Ivanovich Kostrikov and Yekaterina Kuzminichna Kostrikova (<i>née</i> Kazantseva); their first four children had died young, while Anna (born 1883), Sergey (1886) and Yelizaveta (1889) survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELenoe128-129_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELenoe128-129-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:203px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kirov_child.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="201" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Kirov_child.jpg" width="201"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kirov_child.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kirov as a child</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Miron, an alcoholic, abandoned the family around 1890. In 1893, Yekaterina died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. The children's paternal grandmother, Melania Avdeyevna Kostrikova, raised Sergey and his sisters for a brief time, but she could not afford to take care of them all on her small pension of 3 rubles per month. Through her connections, she succeeded in having Sergey placed in an orphanage, but he saw his sisters and grandmother regularly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELenoe129-132_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELenoe129-132-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1901 a group of wealthy benefactors provided a scholarship for him to attend an industrial school at <a href="/wiki/Kazan" title="Kazan">Kazan</a>. After gaining his degree in <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> he moved to <a href="/wiki/Tomsk" title="Tomsk">Tomsk</a> in Siberia. Kirov became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> and joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Social_Democratic_Labor_Party" title="Russian Social Democratic Labor Party">Russian Social Democratic Labor Party</a> (RSDLP) in 1904.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_revolutions">Russian revolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Kirov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Russian revolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kirov took part in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905" title="Russian Revolution of 1905">Russian Revolution of 1905</a>, and was arrested and later released. He joined with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolsheviks</a> soon after being released from prison. In 1906, Kirov was arrested once again, but this time jailed for over three years, charged with printing illegal literature. Soon after his release, he again took part in revolutionary activity. Once again being arrested for printing illegal literature, after a year of custody, Kostrikov moved to the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, where he stayed until the abdication of Tsar <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II</a>.</p>
<p>By this time, Sergei Kostrikov had changed his name to Kirov in order to make his name easier to remember<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, a practice common among Russian revolutionaries of the time. Kostrikove began using the pen name "Kir", first publishing under the pseudonym "Kirov" on April 26, 1912. One account states that he chose the name "Kir" (Cyrus, from the Greek Kūros), after a Christian martyr in third-century Egypt from an Orthodox calendar of saints' days, russifying it by adding an "-ov" ending. A second story is that he based it on the name of the Persian king <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELenoe186_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELenoe186-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kirov became commander of the Bolshevik military administration in <a href="/wiki/Astrakhan" title="Astrakhan">Astrakhan</a>. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">Russian Revolution of 1917</a>, he fought in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a> until 1920. <a href="/wiki/Simon_Sebag_Montefiore" title="Simon Sebag Montefiore">Simon Sebag Montefiore</a> writes: "During the Civil War, Kirov was one of the swashbuckling commissars in the North Caucasus beside Ordzhonikidze and <a href="/wiki/Anastas_Mikoyan" title="Anastas Mikoyan">Mikoyan</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Astrakhan" title="Astrakhan">Astrakhan</a> he enforced Bolshevik power in March 1919 with liberal bloodletting: more than 4,000 were killed. When a bourgeois was caught hiding his own furniture, Kirov ordered him shot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1921, he became manager of the <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> party organization. Kirov was a loyal supporter of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, and in 1926 he was rewarded with the command of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Leningrad</a> party.</p>
<p>As a result, Kirov drew the unwelcome attention of Stalin, particularly after the <a href="/wiki/17th_Congress_of_the_All-Union_Communist_Party_(Bolsheviks)" title="17th Congress of the All-Union Communist Party (Bolsheviks)">1934 party congress</a>, where delegates voting for new Central Committee membership elected Kirov with just three votes against.</p>
<p>Although Kirov had been a strong supporter of Stalin, including the leader's sharp swing to the left during the period of enforced collectivisation and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dekulakisation" title="Dekulakisation">dekulakisation</a></i> (which had seen millions die in a famine), Kirov's speech to the congress suggested he wanted to see a more relaxed approach in the future. Kirov, a lover of the good life and a hardened drinker who would even swear in public, was also highly popular with party cadres who saw this style as a welcome alternative to the increasingly (in public, at least) austere regime promoted by Stalin.</p>
<p>Supposedly, Stalin received far more negative votes than Kirov, although the historical records are not entirely clear. After the party congress, Stalin asked Kirov to work for him in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, assisting the Politburo, but then repeatedly postponed Kirov's transfer, stating that Kirov was temporarily required in Leningrad to finish important party business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-248_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-248-9">[9]</a></sup> Kirov was not invited to certain Politburo meetings, and was kept in Leningrad for over nine months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-248_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-248-9">[9]</a></sup> Kirov's influence continued to grow, and at a plenary session of the Central Committee in November 1934 Kirov urged the adoption of further conciliatory measures by the party in favor of party dissidents, which won enthusiastic applause and approval among the delegates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-248_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-248-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination_and_aftermath">Assassination and aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Kirov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Assassination and aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Leningrad office of the <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> – headed by Kirov's close friend, Filipp Medved – looked after Kirov's security. Stalin allegedly ordered the NKVD <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Commissar" title="People's Commissar">Commissar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genrikh_Yagoda" title="Genrikh Yagoda">Genrikh Yagoda</a>, to replace Medved with Grigory Yeremeyevich Yevdokimov, a close associate of Stalin. However, Kirov intervened and had the order countermanded. According to <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Mikhailovich_Orlov" title="Aleksandr Mikhailovich Orlov">Alexander Orlov</a>, Stalin then ordered Yagoda to arrange the assassination. Yagoda ordered Medved's deputy, Vania Zaporozhets, to undertake the job. Zaporozhets returned to Leningrad in search of an assassin; in reviewing the files he found the name of <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Nikolaev" title="Leonid Nikolaev">Leonid Nikolayev</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlov_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orlov-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AlexanderNikolayev.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="821" data-file-width="1080" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/AlexanderNikolayev.jpg/220px-AlexanderNikolayev.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/AlexanderNikolayev.jpg/330px-AlexanderNikolayev.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/AlexanderNikolayev.jpg/440px-AlexanderNikolayev.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AlexanderNikolayev.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Milda Draule and <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Nikolaev" title="Leonid Nikolaev">Leonid Nikolayev</a></div>

<p>Leonid Nikolayev was well-known to the NKVD, which had arrested him for various petty offences in recent years. Various accounts of his life agree that he was an expelled Party member and failed junior functionary with a murderous grudge and an indifference towards his own survival. He was unemployed, with a wife and child, and in financial difficulties. According to Orlov, Nikolayev had allegedly expressed to a 'friend' a desire to kill the head of the party control commission that had expelled him. His friend reported this to the NKVD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlov_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Orlov-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Zaporozhets then allegedly enlisted Nikolayev's "friend" to contact him, giving him money and a loaded 7.62 mm <a href="/wiki/Nagant_M1895" title="Nagant M1895">Nagant M1895</a> revolver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlov_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Orlov-10">[10]</a></sup> However, Nikolayev's first attempt at killing Kirov failed. On 15 October 1934, Nikolaev packed his Nagant revolver in a briefcase and entered the <a href="/wiki/Smolny_Institute" title="Smolny Institute">Smolny Institute</a> where Kirov worked. Although he was initially passed by the main security desk at Smolny, he was arrested after an alert guard asked to examine his briefcase, which was found to contain the revolver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlov_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Orlov-10">[10]</a></sup> A few hours later, Nikolayev's briefcase and loaded revolver were returned to him, and he was told to leave the building. Though Nikolayev had clearly broken Soviet laws, the security police had inexplicably released him from custody; he was even permitted to retain his loaded pistol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._252_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._252-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>With Stalin's approval, the NKVD had previously withdrawn all but four police bodyguards assigned to Kirov. These four guards accompanied Kirov each day to his offices at the Smolny Institute, and then left. On 1 December 1934, the usual guard post at the entrance to Kirov's offices was left unmanned, even though the building served as the chief offices of the Leningrad party apparatus and as the seat of the local government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlov_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Orlov-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-252_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-252-12">[12]</a></sup> According to some reports, only a single friend, Commissar Borisov, and unarmed bodyguard of Kirov's remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-252_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_pp._247-252-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-13">[13]</a></sup> Other sources<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> state that there may have been as many as nine NKVD guards in the building. Whatever the case, given the circumstances of Kirov's death, as former Soviet official and author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Barmine" title="Alexander Barmine">Alexander Barmine</a> noted, "the negligence of the NKVD in protecting such a high party official was without precedent in the Soviet Union."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._252_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._252-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the afternoon of 1 December, Nikolayev arrived at the Smolny Institute offices. Unopposed, he made his way to the third floor, where he waited in a hallway until Kirov and Borisov stepped into the corridor. Borisov appears to have stayed well behind Kirov, some 20 to 40 paces (some sources allege Borisov parted company with Kirov in order to prepare his luncheon).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-13">[13]</a></sup> As Kirov turned a corner, passing Nikolayev, the latter drew his revolver and shot Kirov in the back of the neck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <i>Sergei Kirov Museum</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirovmuseum_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirovmuseum-14">[14]</a></sup> maintains that the circumstances of Kirov's death "remain unknown to this day." There are no doubts on the aftermath, however: "the bloodiest round of Stalin's terror and repression."</p>
<p>After Kirov's death, Stalin called for swift punishment of the traitors and those found negligent in Kirov's death. Nikolayev was tried alone and secretly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasili_Ulrikh" title="Vasili Ulrikh">Vasili Ulrikh</a>, Chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_Collegium_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_USSR" title="Military Collegium of the Supreme Court of the USSR">Military Collegium of the Supreme Court of the USSR</a>. He was sentenced to death by shooting on 29 December 1934, and the sentence was carried out that very night.</p>
<p>The hapless Commissar Borisov died the day after the assassination, supposedly by falling from a moving truck while riding with a group of NKVD agents. Borisov's wife was committed to an insane asylum. According to Orlov, Nikolayev's mysterious 'friend' and alleged provocateur, who had supplied him with the revolver and money, was later shot on Stalin's personal orders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlov_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Orlov-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nikolayev's mother, brother, sisters, cousin and some other people close to him were arrested and later killed or sent to labor camps. Arrested immediately after the assassination, Nikolayev's wife, Milda Draule, survived her husband by three months before being executed as well. Their infant son (who was named Marx following the Bolshevik naming fashion) was sent into an orphanage. Marx Draule was alive in 2005 when he was officially rehabilitated as a victim of political repressions, and Milda was also found innocent retrospectively. However, Nikolayev was never posthumously acquitted.</p>
<p>Several NKVD officers from the Leningrad branch were convicted of negligence for not adequately protecting Kirov, and sentenced to prison terms of up to ten years. None of these NKVD officers were executed in the aftermath, and none actually served time in prison. Instead, they were transferred to executive posts in Stalin's labor camps for a period of time (in effect, a demotion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._252_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._252-11">[11]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>, these same NKVD officers were shot in 1937 during Stalin's purges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khrushchev.2C_N.S._1989_p._21_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Khrushchev.2C_N.S._1989_p._21-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, a Communist Party communiqué reported that Nikolaev had confessed his guilt, not only as an assassin, but an assassin in the pay of a "fascist power," receiving money from an unidentified 'foreign consul' in Leningrad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._248_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._248-16">[16]</a></sup> 104 defendants who were already in prison at the time of Kirov's assassination and who had no demonstrable connection to Nikolayev were found guilty of complicity in the "fascist plot" against Kirov, and summarily executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._248_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._248-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, a few days later, during a subsequent Communist Party meeting of the Moscow District, the Party secretary announced in a speech that Nikolayev was personally interrogated by Stalin the day after the assassination, an unheard-of event for a party leader such as Stalin:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._249_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._249-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>"Comrade Stalin personally directed the investigation of Kirov's assassination. He questioned Nikolayev at length. The leaders of the Opposition placed the gun in Nikolayev's hand!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._249_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._249-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Other speakers duly rose to condemn the Opposition: "The Central Committee must be pitiless – the Party must be purged... the record of every member must be scrutinized...." No one at the meeting mentioned the initial theory of fascist agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._249_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._249-17">[17]</a></sup> Later, Stalin even used the Kirov assassination to eliminate the remainder of the Opposition leadership against him, accusing <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Zinoviev" title="Grigory Zinoviev">Grigory Zinoviev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lev_Kamenev" title="Lev Kamenev">Lev Kamenev</a>, Abram Prigozhin, and others who had stood with Kirov in opposing Stalin (or simply failed to acquiesce to Stalin's views), of being 'morally responsible' for Kirov's murder, and as such were guilty of complicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._248_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barmine.2C_Alexander_1945_p._248-16">[16]</a></sup> All were removed from the Party apparatus and given prison sentences. While serving their sentences, the Opposition leaders were charged with new crimes, for which they were sentenced to death and shot.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Barmine" title="Alexander Barmine">Alexander Barmine</a>, a Soviet official who knew both Stalin and Kirov, asserted that Stalin arranged the murder with the Soviet secret police, the NKVD, who armed Nikolaev and sent him to assassinate Kirov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author and Marxist scholar <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_Nikolaevsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boris Nikolaevsky (page does not exist)">Boris Nikolaevsky</a> argued: "One thing is certain: the only man who profited by the Kirov assassination was Stalin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Khrushchev, in his secret speech in 1956, said that the murder was organized by <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> agents and that "the circumstances surrounding Kirov's murder hide many things which are inexplicable and mysterious and demand a most careful examination."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marxists.org_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-marxists.org-20">[20]</a></sup> He noticed that the NKVD agents tasked with protecting Kirov were eventually shot in 1937 to "cover up the traces of the organizers of Kirov's killing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marxists.org_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-marxists.org-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pospelov_Commission_investigation">Pospelov Commission investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Kirov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pospelov Commission investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 1955, after <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> assumed control of the Party, the Presidium of the Central Committee (CPSU) entrusted <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Pospelov" title="Pyotr Pospelov">P. N. Pospelov</a>, Secretary of the Central Committee, to form a <a href="/wiki/Pospelov_Commission" title="Pospelov Commission">commission</a> to investigate the repression of the 1930s (this was the same Pospelov who drafted the famous "<a href="/wiki/On_the_Cult_of_Personality_and_Its_Consequences" title="On the Cult of Personality and Its Consequences">Secret Speech</a>" of Khrushchev at the <a href="/wiki/20th_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="20th Congress of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">20th Congress</a>). Khrushchev stated:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="400" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg/220px-Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg/330px-Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/44/Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Official_opening_of_Belomorkanal_article.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kirov and Stalin overseeing the opening of <a href="/wiki/White_Sea%E2%80%93Baltic_Canal" title="White Sea–Baltic Canal">White Sea–Baltic Canal</a> ( part of article from the Soviet newspaper)</div>
</div>
</div>
<dl>
<dd>"It must be asserted that to this day the circumstances surrounding Kirov's murder hide many things which are inexplicable and mysterious and demand a most careful examination. There are reasons for the suspicion that the killer of Kirov, Nikolayev, was assisted by someone from among the people whose duty it was protect the person of Kirov. A month and a half before the killing, Nikolayev was arrested on the grounds of suspicious behaviour, but he was released and not even searched. It is an unusually suspicious circumstance that when the Chekist [Borisov] assigned to protect Kirov was being brought for an interrogation, on 2 December 1934, he was killed in a car "accident" in which no other occupants of the car were harmed. After the murder of Kirov, top functionaries of the Leningrad NKVD were relieved of their duties and were given very light sentences, but in 1937 they were shot. We can assume that they were shot in order to cover the traces of the organizers of Kirov's killing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khrushchev.2C_N.S._1989_p._21_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Khrushchev.2C_N.S._1989_p._21-15">[15]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Pospelov subsequently spoke to Dr. Kirchakov and former nurse Trunina, former members of the party, who had been mentioned in a letter by another member of the commission, (<a href="/wiki/Olga_Shatunovskaya" title="Olga Shatunovskaya">Olga Shatunovskaya</a>), as having knowledge of the Kirov murder. Dr. Kirchakov confirmed that he did talk to Shatunovskaya and Trunina about some of the unexplained aspects of the Kirov murder case, and agreed to provide the Commission with a written deposition. He stressed that his statement was based on the testimony of one Comrade Yan Olsky, a former NKVD officer who was demoted after Kirov's murder and transferred to the People's Supply System<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>In his deposition, Dr. Kirchakov wrote that he had discussed the murder of Kirov and the role of Fyodor Medved with Olsky. Olsky was of the firm opinion that Medved, Kirov's friend and NKVD security chief of the Leningrad branch, was innocent of the murder. Olsky also told Kirchakov that Medved had been barred from the NKVD Kirov assassination investigation. Instead, the investigation was carried out by a senior NKVD chief, <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Agranov" title="Yakov Agranov">Yakov Agranov</a>, and later by another NKVD bureau officer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> whose name he did not remember. During one of the committee sessions, Olskii related he was present when Stalin asked Leonid Nikolaev why Comrade Kirov had been killed. To this Nikolaev replied that he carried out the instruction of the "Chekists" [NKVD] and pointed towards the group of 'Chekists' [NKVD officers] standing in the room; Medved was not amongst them<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Khrushchev's Report, <i>On the Personality Cult and Its Consequences</i> was later read at closed-door Party meetings. Afterward, new material was received by the Pospelov committee, including the statement of Kirov's chauffeur, Kuzin, that Commissar Borisov, Kirov's friend and bodyguard, who was responsible for Kirov's round the clock security at Smolny, was intentionally killed, and that his death in a road accident was not at all natural<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>

<p>The last attempt in the Soviet Union to review the Kirov murder case was the Politburo Commission headed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Nikolaevich_Yakovlev" title="Alexander Nikolaevich Yakovlev">Alexander Nikolaevich Yakovlev</a> which was established in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorbachev" title="Gorbachev">Gorbachev</a> period in 1989. The investigating team included personnel from the USSR Procurator's Office, the Military Procuracy, the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> and various archival administrations. After two years of investigations the working team of the Yakovlev commission concluded that: 'in this affair no materials objectively support Stalin's participation or <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> participation in the organisation and carrying out of Kirov's murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>Kirov was buried in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kremlin_Wall_necropolis" title="Kremlin Wall necropolis">Kremlin Wall necropolis</a> in a <a href="/wiki/State_funeral" title="State funeral">state funeral</a>, with Stalin personally carrying his coffin.</p>
<p>Many cities, streets and factories took his name, including the cities of <a href="/wiki/Kirov,_Kirov_Oblast" title="Kirov, Kirov Oblast">Kirov</a> (formerly Vyatka), <a href="/wiki/Kirovsk,_Murmansk_Oblast" title="Kirovsk, Murmansk Oblast">Kirovsk</a> (<a href="/wiki/Murmansk_Oblast" title="Murmansk Oblast">Murmansk Oblast</a>), Kirovohrad (today <a href="/wiki/Kropyvnytskyi" title="Kropyvnytskyi">Kropyvnytskyi</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>), Kirovabad (today <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganja_(city)" title="Ganja (city)">Ganja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>) and Kirovakan (today <a href="/wiki/Vanadzor" title="Vanadzor">Vanadzor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>), the station Kirovskaya of the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Metro" title="Moscow Metro">Moscow Metro</a> (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chistye_Prudy_(Metro)" title="Chistye Prudy (Metro)">Chistye Prudy</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mariinsky_Ballet" title="Mariinsky Ballet">Kirov Ballet</a>, and the massive <a href="/wiki/Kirov_Plant" title="Kirov Plant">Kirov industrial plant</a> in Saint Petersburg. In order to comply with <a href="/wiki/Decommunization_in_Ukraine" title="Decommunization in Ukraine">(Ukrainian) decommunization laws</a> Kirovohrad was renamed Kropyvnytskyi by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_parliament" title="Ukrainian parliament">Ukrainian parliament</a> on 14 July 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KcbKcJ16_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-KcbKcJ16-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The S. M. Kirov Forestry Academy, in Leningrad, was named after him; today this is the Saint Petersburg State Forest Technical University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the city of <a href="/wiki/Kirov,_Kirov_Oblast" title="Kirov, Kirov Oblast">Kirov</a> a speedskating match, the Kirov Prize, was named for him. This match is the longest enduring annual organised race in speedskating apart from the <a href="/wiki/World_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="World Speed Skating Championships">World Speed Skating Championships</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Speed_Skating_Championships" title="European Speed Skating Championships">European Speed Skating Championships</a>.</p>
<p>The English Communist poet <a href="/wiki/John_Cornford" title="John Cornford">John Cornford</a> wrote an eponymous poem in his honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>For many years, a huge statue of Kirov in granite and bronze dominated the panorama of the city of <a href="/wiki/Baku" title="Baku">Baku</a>. The monument was erected on a hill in 1939 and was dismantled in January 1992, after <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> gained its independence. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirov_class_battlecruiser" title="Kirov class battlecruiser"><i>Kirov</i> class</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlecruisers" title="Battlecruisers">battlecruisers</a> is named in his honor, though the first-of-class vessel originally named <i>Kirov</i> has since been renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Soviet_battlecruiser_Kirov" title="Soviet battlecruiser Kirov">Admiral Ushakov</a></i>.</p>
