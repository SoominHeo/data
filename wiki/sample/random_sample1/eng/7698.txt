<p><b>Sergio Mattarella</b>, <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_of_the_Italian_Republic" title="Order of Merit of the Italian Republic">OMRI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Order_of_Merit_for_Culture_and_Art" title="Italian Order of Merit for Culture and Art">OMCA</a></span> (<small>Italian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[ˈsɛrdʒo mattaˈrɛlla]</a></span>; born 23 July 1941) is an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> politician, lawyer and judge who is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Italy" title="List of Presidents of Italy">12th and current</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_Italy" title="President of Italy">President of Italy</a> since 2015. He was a <a href="/wiki/Italian_Parliament" title="Italian Parliament">member of Parliament</a> from 1983 to 2008, serving as <a href="/wiki/Italian_Minister_of_Education" title="Italian Minister of Education">Minister of Education</a> from 1989 to 1990 and as <a href="/wiki/Italian_Minister_of_Defence" title="Italian Minister of Defence">Minister of Defence</a> from 1999 to 2001. In 2011, he became an elected judge on the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Italy" title="Constitutional Court of Italy">Constitutional Court</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-15_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post-15-1">[1]</a></sup> On 31 January 2015, he was elected by parliament to be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_Italian_Republic" title="List of Presidents of the Italian Republic">12th President of the Italian Republic</a>. He is the first <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicilian</a> to have held the post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergio_Mattarella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sergio Mattarella was born in <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> of a prominent Sicilian family. His father, <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Mattarella" title="Bernardo Mattarella">Bernardo Mattarella</a>, was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-fascism_in_Italy" title="Anti-fascism in Italy">anti-fascist</a> who, alongside <a href="/wiki/Alcide_De_Gasperi" title="Alcide De Gasperi">Alcide De Gasperi</a> and other prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Catholic</a> politicians, helped found the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democracy_(Italy)" title="Christian Democracy (Italy)">Christian Democracy</a> (DC) party, which dominated the Italian political scene for almost fifty years, with Bernardo serving as a minister several times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Sergio Mattarella's brother, <a href="/wiki/Piersanti_Mattarella" title="Piersanti Mattarella">Piersanti Mattarella</a>, was also a Christian Democratic politician and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_of_Sicily" title="President of Sicily">President of Sicily</a> from 1978 until his death in 1980, when he was killed by the <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_Mafia" title="Sicilian Mafia">Sicilian Mafia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ind_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ind-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his youth, Sergio Mattarella was a member of <i><a href="/wiki/Azione_Cattolica" title="Azione Cattolica">Azione Cattolica</a></i>, a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_lay_association" title="Catholic lay association">Catholic lay association</a>. In 1964, he graduated in law at the <a href="/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome" title="Sapienza University of Rome">Sapienza University of Rome</a>; after a few years he started teaching <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_procedure" title="Parliamentary procedure">Parliamentary procedure</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Palermo" title="University of Palermo">University of Palermo</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergio_Mattarella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mattarella entered politics after the assassination of his brother <a href="/wiki/Piersanti_Mattarella" title="Piersanti Mattarella">Piersanti</a> by the Mafia. His parliamentary career began in 1983, when he was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_(Italy)" title="Chamber of Deputies (Italy)">Chamber of Deputies</a> in left-leaning faction of the DC that had supported an agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">Italian Communist Party</a> (PCI) led by <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Berlinguer" title="Enrico Berlinguer">Enrico Berlinguer</a>, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Historic_Compromise" title="Historic Compromise">Historic Compromise</a>. The following year he was entrusted by the Secretary of the Christian Democrat, <a href="/wiki/Ciriaco_De_Mita" title="Ciriaco De Mita">Ciriaco De Mita</a>, to "clean up" the Sicilian faction of the party from Mafia control, at a time when <a href="/wiki/Made_man" title="Made man">made men</a> of <i>Cosa Nostra</i> like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvo_Lima" title="Salvo Lima">Salvo Lima</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vito_Ciancimino" title="Vito Ciancimino">Vito Ciancimino</a> were powerful political figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1985 Mattarella helped the young lawyer <a href="/wiki/Leoluca_Orlando" title="Leoluca Orlando">Leoluca Orlando</a>, who had worked alongside his brother Piersanti during his governorship of Sicily, to become the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Palermo" title="Mayor of Palermo">Mayor of Palermo</a>.</p>
<p>Mattarella was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Italy" title="Cabinet of Italy">Minister for Parliamentary Affairs</a> in the governments led by Christian Democratic <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Italy" title="Prime Minister of Italy">Prime Ministers</a> <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Goria" title="Giovanni Goria">Giovanni Goria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ciriaco_De_Mita" title="Ciriaco De Mita">Ciriaco De Mita</a>, and in 1989 he became <a href="/wiki/Italian_Minister_of_Education" title="Italian Minister of Education">Minister of Education</a> in the sixth cabinet of <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Andreotti" title="Giulio Andreotti">Giulio Andreotti</a>. Mattarella stood down from his position, together with other ministers, in 1990 upon parliament's passing of the Mammì Act, liberalising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_in_Italy" title="Media in Italy">media</a> sector in Italy, which they saw as a favour to the media magnate <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Berlusconi" title="Silvio Berlusconi">Silvio Berlusconi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cedrone-2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cedrone-2015-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990 Mattarella was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Secretaries_of_the_Italian_Christian_Democracy" title="List of Secretaries of the Italian Christian Democracy">Vice-Secretary</a> of Christian Democracy. He left the post two years later to become director of <i><a href="/wiki/Christian_Democracy_(Italy)" title="Christian Democracy (Italy)">Il Popolo</a></i>, the official newspaper of the party. Following the <a href="/wiki/Italian_referendum,_1993" title="Italian referendum, 1993">Italian referendum of 1993</a> he drafted the new electoral law nicknamed <i>Mattarellum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1994, when Christian Democracy was dissolved in the wake of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangentopoli" title="Tangentopoli">Tangentopoli</a></i> corruption scandal, he helped found the <a href="/wiki/Italian_People%27s_Party_(1994)" title="Italian People's Party (1994)">Italian People's Party</a> (PPI), along with its first leader <a href="/wiki/Mino_Martinazzoli" title="Mino Martinazzoli">Mino Martinazzoli</a> and other former Christian Democrats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cedrone-2015_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cedrone-2015-6">[6]</a></sup> In the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_1994" title="Italian general election, 1994">1994 general election</a> (in which the newly founded PPI fared poorly) Martinazzoli was again elected to the Chamber of Deputies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-15_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Post-15-1">[1]</a></sup> He soon found himself engaged in an internal dispute after the election of a new party leader, <a href="/wiki/Rocco_Buttiglione" title="Rocco Buttiglione">Rocco Buttiglione</a>, who wished to steer the Italian People's Party towards an electoral alliance with Berlusconi's <a href="/wiki/Forza_Italia" title="Forza Italia">Forza Italia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cedrone-2015_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cedrone-2015-6">[6]</a></sup> Following Buttiglione's appointment, Mattarella resigned as director of <i>Il Popolo</i> in opposition to this policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Credazzi-1994_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Credazzi-1994-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mattarella was one of the first supporters of the economist <a href="/wiki/Romano_Prodi" title="Romano Prodi">Romano Prodi</a> at the head of the centre-left coalition known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Olive_Tree_(political_coalition)" title="The Olive Tree (political coalition)">The Olive Tree</a> (<i>L'Ulivo</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_1996" title="Italian general election, 1996">1996 general election</a>. After the electoral victory of the centre-left, Mattarella served as President of the PPI's parliamentary group. Two years later, when <a href="/wiki/Prodi_I_Cabinet" title="Prodi I Cabinet">Prodi's first government</a> fell, Mattarella was appointed Deputy Prime Minister and <a href="/wiki/Italian_Minister_of_Defence" title="Italian Minister of Defence">Minister of Defence</a> in the government of <a href="/wiki/Massimo_D%27Alema" title="Massimo D'Alema">Massimo D'Alema</a>, then-leader of the <a href="/wiki/Democrats_of_the_Left" title="Democrats of the Left">Democrats of the Left</a> (DS) . As Defence Minister he supported the <a href="/wiki/NATO_bombing_of_Yugoslavia" title="NATO bombing of Yugoslavia">NATO Intervention in Yugoslavia</a> against the Serbian President <a href="/wiki/Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Slobodan Milošević">Slobodan Milošević</a>; he also approved a reform of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Armed_Forces" title="Italian Armed Forces">Italian Armed Forces</a> which abolished <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a>. After the resignation of D'Alema in 2000, Mattarella kept his position as Minister of Defence in the government of <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_Amato" title="Giuliano Amato">Giuliano Amato</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cedrone-2015_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cedrone-2015-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>In October 2000 the PPI joined with other centrist parties to form an alliance called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democracy_is_Freedom_%E2%80%93_Daisy" title="Democracy is Freedom – Daisy">The Daisy</a> (DL), later to merge into a single party in March 2002. Mattarella was re-elected to the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Parliament" title="Italian Parliament">Italian Parliament</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_2001" title="Italian general election, 2001">2001</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_2006" title="Italian general election, 2006">2006</a> general elections, standing as a candidate for The Daisy in two successive centre-left coalitions – The Olive Tree and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Union_(political_coalition)" title="The Union (political coalition)">The Union</a> (<i>L'Unione</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-15_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Post-15-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 he was one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Italy)" title="Democratic Party (Italy)">Democratic Party</a> (PD), a <a href="/wiki/Big_tent" title="Big tent">big tent</a> centre-left party formed from a merger of left-wing and centrist parties which had been part of The Olive Tree, including The Daisy and the Democrats of the Left (heirs of the Italian Communist Party).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cedrone-2015_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cedrone-2015-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 October 2011 he was elected by the Italian Parliament with 572 votes to be a judge of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Italy" title="Constitutional Court of Italy">Constitutional Court</a>. He was sworn in on 11 October 2011. He served until he was sworn in as president of the Republic of Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="President_of_Italy">President of Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergio_Mattarella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: President of Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="853" data-file-width="1280" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg/220px-Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg/330px-Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg/440px-Oath_of_Mattarella_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On 31 January 2015 Mattarella was <a href="/wiki/Italian_presidential_election,_2015" title="Italian presidential election, 2015">elected</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_Italy" title="President of Italy">President of the Italian Republic</a> at the fourth ballot with 665 votes out of 1,009, with support from the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Italy)" title="Democratic Party (Italy)">Democratic Party</a> (PD), <a href="/wiki/New_Centre-Right" title="New Centre-Right">New Centre-Right</a> (NCD), <a href="/wiki/Civic_Choice" title="Civic Choice">Civic Choice</a> (SC), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Centre_(2008)" title="Union of the Centre (2008)">Union of the Centre</a> (UDC) and <a href="/wiki/Left_Ecology_Freedom" title="Left Ecology Freedom">Left Ecology Freedom</a> (SEL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mattarella was officially endorsed by the Democratic Party, after his name was put forward by the Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Renzi" title="Matteo Renzi">Matteo Renzi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Mattarella replaced <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Napolitano" title="Giorgio Napolitano">Giorgio Napolitano</a>, who had served for nine years, the longest presidency in the history of the Italian Republic. His first statement as new President was: "My thoughts go first and especially to the difficulties and hopes of our fellow citizens".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>His first presidential visit was on the day of his election, when he visited the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fosse_Ardeatine" title="Fosse Ardeatine">Fosse Ardeatine</a> where, in 1944 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> occupation troops killed 335 people as a reprisal for a <a href="/wiki/Italian_resistance_movement" title="Italian resistance movement">partisan</a> attack. Mattarella stated that "Europe and the world must be united to defeat whoever wants to drag us into a new age of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terror</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 May 2015 President Mattarella signed the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_electoral_law" title="Italian electoral law">Italian electoral law</a>, known as <i>Italicum</i>, which provides for a <a href="/wiki/Two-round_system" title="Two-round system">two-round system</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Party-list_proportional_representation" title="Party-list proportional representation">party-list proportional representation</a>, corrected by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majority_bonus" title="Majority bonus">majority bonus</a> and a 3% <a href="/wiki/Election_threshold" title="Election threshold">election threshold</a>. Candidates run for election in 100 multi-member <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constituency" title="Constituency">constituencies</a> with <a href="/wiki/Open_list" title="Open list">open lists</a>, except for a single candidate chosen by each party who is the first to be elected.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_political_crisis">2016 political crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergio_Mattarella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2016 political crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 7 December 2016, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Renzi" title="Matteo Renzi">Matteo Renzi</a> announced his resignation, following the rejection of his proposals in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2016_Italian_constitutional_referendum" title="2016 Italian constitutional referendum">2016 Italian constitutional referendum</a>. On 11 December Mattarella appointed the incumbent Minister of Foreign Affairs <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Gentiloni" title="Paolo Gentiloni">Paolo Gentiloni</a> as new head of the government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergio_Mattarella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was married to Marisa Chiazzese, daughter of Lauro Chiazzese, a professor of <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Palermo" title="University of Palermo">University of Palermo</a>. His wife died in 2012. He has three children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
