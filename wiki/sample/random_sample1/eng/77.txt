<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Omics</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The English-language <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> <b>omics</b> informally refers to a field of study in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> ending in <i>-omics</i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metabolomics" title="Metabolomics">metabolomics</a>. The related suffix <b>-ome</b> is used to address the objects of study of such fields, such as the <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteome" title="Proteome">proteome</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metabolome" title="Metabolome">metabolome</a> respectively. Omics aims at the collective characterization and quantification of pools of biological molecules that translate into the structure, function, and dynamics of an organism or organisms.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Functional_genomics" title="Functional genomics">Functional genomics</a> aims at identifying the functions of as many genes as possible of a given organism. It combines different -omics techniques such as transcriptomics and proteomics with saturated mutant collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The suffix <i>-ome</i> as used in molecular biology refers to a <i>totality</i> of some sort; it is an example of a "neo-suffix" formed by abstraction from various Greek terms in <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-ωμα</span>, a sequence that does not form an identifiable suffix in Greek.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Omicum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="838" data-file-width="1000" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Omicum.jpg/220px-Omicum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Omicum.jpg/330px-Omicum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Omicum.jpg/440px-Omicum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Omicum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Omicum": Building of the <a href="/wiki/Estonian_Biocentre" title="Estonian Biocentre">Estonian Biocentre</a> which houses the <a href="/wiki/Estonian_Genome_Project" title="Estonian Genome Project">Estonian Genome Centre</a> and Institute of Molecular and Cell Biology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tartu" title="University of Tartu">University of Tartu</a> in Tartu, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> (<i>OED</i>) distinguishes three different fields of application for the <i>-ome</i> suffix:</p>
<ol>
<li>in medicine, forming nouns with the sense "swelling, tumour"</li>
<li>in botany or zoology, forming nouns in the sense "a part of an animal or plant with a specified structure"</li>
<li>in cellular and molecular biology, forming nouns with the sense "all constituents considered collectively"</li>
</ol>
<p>The <i>-ome</i> suffix originated as a variant of <i>-oma</i>, and became productive in the last quarter of the 19th century. It originally appeared in terms like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclerome" title="Sclerome">sclerome</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scleroma_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-scleroma-2">[2]</a></sup> or <i><a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizome</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhizome_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhizome-3">[3]</a></sup> All of these terms derive from Greek words in <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-ωμα</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oma_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-oma-4">[4]</a></sup> a sequence that is not a single suffix, but analyzable as <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-ω-μα</span>, the <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-ω-</span> belonging to the word stem (usually a verb) and the <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-μα</span> being a genuine Greek suffix forming abstract nouns.</p>
<p>The OED suggests that its third definition originated as a <a href="/wiki/Back-formation" title="Back-formation">back-formation</a> from <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mitome&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mitome (page does not exist)">mitome</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlHome_:_Oxford_English_Dictionary_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlHome_:_Oxford_English_Dictionary-5">[5]</a></sup> Early attestations include <i><a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a></i> (1916)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlbiome.2C_n._:_Oxford_English_Dictionary_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlbiome.2C_n._:_Oxford_English_Dictionary-6">[6]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a></i> (first coined as German <i>Genom</i> in 1920<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genom_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-genom-7">[7]</a></sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-omic_dict_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-omic_dict-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The association with <i>chromosome</i> in molecular biology is by <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">false etymology</a>. The word <i>chromosome</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">stems</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">χρωμ(ατ)-</span> "colour" and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">σωμ(ατ)-</span> "body".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-omic_dict_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-omic_dict-8">[8]</a></sup> While <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">σωμα</span> "body" genuinely contains the <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-μα</span> suffix, the preceding <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-ω-</span> is not a stem-forming suffix but part of the word's <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">root</a>. Because <i>genome</i> refers to the <i>complete</i> genetic makeup of an organism, a neo-suffix <i>-ome</i> suggested itself as referring to "wholeness" or "completion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liddell_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-liddell-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">Bioinformaticians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_biologist" title="Molecular biologist">molecular biologists</a> figured amongst the first scientists to apply the "-ome" suffix widely. Early advocates included bioinformaticians in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, UK, where there were many early bioinformatics labs such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRC_centre" title="MRC centre">MRC centre</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanger_centre" title="Sanger centre">Sanger centre</a>, and EBI (<a href="/wiki/European_Bioinformatics_Institute" title="European Bioinformatics Institute">European Bioinformatics Institute</a>). For example, the MRC centre carried out the first genome and proteome projects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinds_of_omics_studies">Kinds of omics studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kinds of omics studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genomics">Genomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">Genomics</a>: Study of the <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a> of organisms.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cognitive_genomics" title="Cognitive genomics">Cognitive genomics</a> examines the changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_process" title="Cognitive process">cognitive processes</a> associated with genetic profiles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Comparative_genomics" title="Comparative genomics">Comparative genomics</a>: Study of the relationship of genome structure and function across different biological species or strains.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Functional_genomics" title="Functional genomics">Functional genomics</a>: Describes gene and protein functions and interactions (often uses transcriptomics).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metagenomics" title="Metagenomics">Metagenomics</a>: Study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metagenome" title="Metagenome">metagenomes</a>, i.e., genetic material recovered directly from environmental samples.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neurogenomics" title="Neurogenomics">Neurogenomics</a>: Study of genetic influences on the development and function of the nervous system.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Personal_genomics" title="Personal genomics">Personal genomics</a>: Branch of genomics concerned with the sequencing and analysis of the genome of an individual. Once the genotypes are known, the individual's genotype can be compared with the published literature to determine likelihood of trait expression and disease risk. Helps in Personalized Medicine</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Epigenomics" title="Epigenomics">Epigenomics</a>: Study of the complete set of epigenetic modifications on the genetic material of a cell, known as the epigenome. ChIP-Chip and ChIP-Seq technologies used.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lipidomics">Lipidomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lipidomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lipidome" title="Lipidome">Lipidome</a> is the entire complement of cellular <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>, including the modifications made to a particular set of lipids, produced by an organism or system.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lipidomics" title="Lipidomics">Lipidomics</a>: Large-scale study of pathways and networks of lipids. Mass spectrometry techniques are used.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proteomics">Proteomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proteomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proteome is the entire complement of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, including the modifications made to a particular set of proteins, produced by an organism or system.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">Proteomics</a>: Large-scale study of proteins, particularly their structures and functions. Mass spectrometry techniques are used.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Immunoproteomics" title="Immunoproteomics">Immunoproteomics</a>: study of large sets of proteins (proteomics) involved in the immune response</li>
<li>Nutriproteomics: Identifying the molecular targets of nutritive and non-nutritive components of the diet. Uses proteomics mass spectrometry data for protein expression studies</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Proteogenomics" title="Proteogenomics">Proteogenomics</a>: An emerging field of biological research at the intersection of proteomics and genomics. Proteomics data used for gene annotations.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Structural_genomics" title="Structural genomics">Structural genomics</a>: Study of 3-dimensional structure of every protein encoded by a given genome using a combination of experimental and modeling approaches.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foodomics">Foodomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Foodomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Foodomics" title="Foodomics">Foodomics</a> was defined in 2009 as "a discipline that studies the Food and Nutrition domains through the application and integration of advanced -omics technologies to improve consumer's well-being, health, and knowledge"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transcriptomics">Transcriptomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transcriptomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transcriptome is the set of all <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> molecules, including mRNA, rRNA, tRNA, and other non-coding RNA, produced in one or a population of cells.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcriptomics" title="Transcriptomics">Transcriptomics</a>: Study of transcriptomes, their structures and functions.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metabolomics" title="Metabolomics">Metabolomics</a>: Scientific study of chemical processes involving metabolites. It is a "systematic study of the unique chemical fingerprints that specific cellular processes leave behind", the study of their small-molecule metabolite profiles</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabonomics" title="Metabonomics">Metabonomics</a>: The quantitative measurement of the dynamic multiparametric metabolic response of living systems to pathophysiological stimuli or genetic modification</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition.2C_pharmacology.2C_and_toxicology">Nutrition, pharmacology, and toxicology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nutrition, pharmacology, and toxicology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nutritional_genomics" title="Nutritional genomics">Nutritional genomics</a>: A science studying the relationship between human genome, nutrition and health.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nutrigenetics" title="Nutrigenetics">Nutrigenetics</a> studies the effect of genetic variations on the interaction between diet and health with implications to susceptible subgroups</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nutrigenomics" title="Nutrigenomics">Nutrigenomics</a>: Study of the effects of foods and food constituents on gene expression. Studies the effect of nutrients on the genome, proteome, and metabolome</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pharmacogenomics" title="Pharmacogenomics">Pharmacogenomics</a> investigates the effect of the sum of variations within the human genome on drugs;</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pharmacomicrobiomics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pharmacomicrobiomics (page does not exist)">Pharmacomicrobiomics</a> investigates the effect of variations within the human microbiome on drugs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Toxicogenomics" title="Toxicogenomics">Toxicogenomics</a>: a field of science that deals with the collection, interpretation, and storage of information about gene and protein activity within particular cell or tissue of an organism in response to toxic substances.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous">Miscellaneous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mitointeractome" title="Mitointeractome">Mitointeractome</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychogenomics" title="Psychogenomics">Psychogenomics</a>: Process of applying the powerful tools of genomics and proteomics to achieve a better understanding of the biological substrates of normal behavior and of diseases of the brain that manifest themselves as behavioral abnormalities. Applying psychogenomics to the study of drug addiction, the ultimate goal is to develop more effective treatments for these disorders as well as objective diagnostic tools, preventive measures, and eventually cures.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stem_cell_genomics" title="Stem cell genomics">Stem cell genomics</a>: Helps in stem cell biology. Aim is to establish stem cells as a leading model system for understanding human biology and disease states and ultimately to accelerate progress toward clinical translation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Connectomics" title="Connectomics">Connectomics</a>: The study of the connectome, the totality of the neural connections in the brain.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">Microbiomics</a>: the study of the genomes of the communities of microorganisms that live in the digestive tracts of animals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cellomics" title="Cellomics">Cellomics</a>: Is the quantitative cell analysis and study using bioimaging methods and bioinformatics.</li>
<li>Ethomics: Is the high-throughput machine measurement of animal behaviour<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unrelated_words_in_-omics">Unrelated words in <i>-omics</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Unrelated words in -omics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/-nomics" title="-nomics">-nomics</a></div>
<p>The word “comic” does not use the "omics" suffix; it derives from Greek “κωμ(ο)-” (<i>merriment</i>) + “-ικ(ο)-” (an adjectival suffix), rather than presenting a truncation of “σωμ(ατ)-”.</p>
<p>Similarly, the word “economy” is assembled from Greek “οικ(ο)-” (<i>household</i>) + “νομ(ο)-” (<i>law</i> or <i>custom</i>), and “economic(s)” from “οικ(ο)-” + “νομ(ο)-” + “-ικ(ο)-”. The suffix -omics is sometimes used to create names for schools of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Reaganomics" title="Reaganomics">Reaganomics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_usage">Current usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Current usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_omics_topics_in_biology" title="List of omics topics in biology">List of omics topics in biology</a></div>
<p>Many “omes” beyond the original “<a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>” have become useful and have been widely adopted by research scientists. “<a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">Proteomics</a>” has become well-established as a term for studying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> at a large scale. "Omes" can provide an easy shorthand to encapsulate a field; for example, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interactomics" title="Interactomics">interactomics</a> study is clearly recognisable as relating to large-scale analyses of gene-gene, protein-protein, or protein-ligand interactions. Researchers are rapidly taking up omes and omics, as shown by the explosion of the use of these terms in <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a> since the mid '90s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
