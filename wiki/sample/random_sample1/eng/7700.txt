<p><b>Serhiy Nazarovych Bubka</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk">Сергі́й Наза́рович Бу́бка</span>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Серге́й Наза́рович Бу́бка, <i>Sergey Nazarovich Bubka</i></span>; born 4 December 1963) is a Ukrainian former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pole_vaulter" title="Pole vaulter">pole vaulter</a>. He represented the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> until its dissolution in 1991. Bubka was twice named Athlete of the Year by <i><a href="/wiki/Track_%26_Field_News" title="Track &amp; Field News">Track &amp; Field News</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and in 2012 was one of 24 athletes inducted as inaugural members of the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Athletics_Federations" title="International Association of Athletics Federations">International Association of Athletics Federations</a> Hall of Fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bubka won six consecutive IAAF World Championships, an Olympic gold medal and broke the <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_pole_vault_world_record_progression" title="Men's pole vault world record progression">world record for men's pole vault</a> 35 times. Sergey has also beaten his own record 14 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-3">[3]</a></sup> (17 outdoor and 18 indoor records). He was the first pole vaulter to clear 6.0 metres and 6.10 metres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indoorlist_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-indoorlist-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outdoorlist_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-outdoorlist-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>He held the indoor world record of 6.15 meters, set on 21 February 1993 in <a href="/wiki/Donetsk" title="Donetsk">Donetsk</a>, Ukraine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> for almost 21 years until France's <a href="/wiki/Renaud_Lavillenie" title="Renaud Lavillenie">Renaud Lavillenie</a> cleared 6.16 metres on 15 February 2014 at the <a href="/wiki/Pole_Vault_Stars" title="Pole Vault Stars">same meet</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Druzhba_Arena" title="Druzhba Arena">same arena</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He is the current outdoor world record holder at 6.14 meters, a record he has held since 31 July 1994,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> though since adopting rule 260.18a in 2000 the IAAF regards Lavillenie's record as the official "world record."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bubka is Senior Vice President of the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Athletics_Federations" title="International Association of Athletics Federations">International Association of Athletics Federations</a> (IAAF), serving since 2007, and President of the <a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee_of_Ukraine" title="National Olympic Committee of Ukraine">National Olympic Committee of Ukraine</a>, serving since 2005. He is also an Honorary Member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC), having been involved since 1996.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in Voroshilovgrad (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luhans%27k" title="Luhans'k">Luhans'k</a>), Bubka was a track-and-field athlete in the 100-meter dash and the long jump, but became a world-class champion only when he turned to the pole vault. In 1983, virtually unknown internationally, he won the world championship in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, and the following year set his first world record, clearing 5.85m (19 ft 2 in). Until the dissolution of the USSR in late 1991, Bubka competed for Soviet teams. The Soviet sports system rewarded athletes for setting new world records, and he became noted for establishing new records by slim amounts, sometimes as little as a centimeter higher. This allowed him to collect frequent bonus payments and made Bubka an attraction at track-and-field meets. By 1992, he was no longer bound to the Soviet system, and signed a contract with Nike<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTbigshoes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTbigshoes-10">[10]</a></sup> that rewarded each world record performance with special bonuses of $40,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soresoar_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-soresoar-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has a son who is a professional tennis player, whose name is also <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Bubka_(tennis)" title="Sergei Bubka (tennis)">Sergei</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pole_vault_career">Pole vault career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pole vault career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sergey Bubka started competing on the international athletics scene in 1981 when he participated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1981_European_Athletics_Junior_Championship" title="1981 European Athletics Junior Championship">European Junior Championship</a> finishing seventh. But the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1983_World_Championship_in_Athletics" title="1983 World Championship in Athletics">1983 World Championship</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a> was his actual entry point to the world athletics, where a relatively unknown Bubka snatched the gold, clearing 5.70 metres (18 feet 8 inches). The years that followed witnessed the unparalleled dominance of Bubka, with him setting new records and standards in pole vaulting.</p>
<p>He set his first world record of 5.85m on 26 May 1984 which he improved to 5.88m a week later, and then to 5.90m a month later. He cleared 6.00 metres (19 feet 8 inches) for the first time on 13 July 1985 in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outdoorlist_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-outdoorlist-5">[5]</a></sup> This height had long been considered unattainable. With virtually no opponents, Bubka improved his own record over the next 10 years until he reached his career best and the then world record of 6.14 m (20 feet 1<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> inches) in 1994.</p>
<p>He became the first athlete ever to jump over 6.10 metres, in <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="San Sebastián">San Sebastián</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in 1991. Until January 2014, no other athlete on earth had cleared 6.07, indoors or outdoors. In 1994, he achieved his personal record with a vault of 6.14 meters, long after many commentators assumed the great sportsman was retired. Bubka increased the world record by 21 centimetres (8 inches) in the period from 1984 to 1988, more than other pole vaulters had achieved in the previous 12 years. He cleared 6.00 meters or better on 45 occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As of June 2015, 6 meters had been cleared by all athletes worldwide exactly 100 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bubka officially retired from pole vault in 2001 during a ceremony at his <a href="/wiki/Pole_Vault_Stars" title="Pole Vault Stars">Pole Vault Stars</a> meeting in <a href="/wiki/Donetsk" title="Donetsk">Donetsk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Olympics_curse">Olympics curse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Olympics curse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite his dominance in pole vault, Bubka had a relatively poor record in the Olympic Games. The first Olympics after Bubka's introduction to the international athletics was held in 1984 and was <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics_boycott" title="1984 Summer Olympics boycott">boycotted by the USSR</a> along with the majority of other <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a> countries. Two months before the Games he vaulted 12 cm higher than the eventual Olympic gold medal winner <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Quinon" title="Pierre Quinon">Pierre Quinon</a>. In 1988 Bubka competed in the <a href="/wiki/1988_Summer_Olympics" title="1988 Summer Olympics">Seoul Olympics</a> and won his only Olympic gold medal clearing 5.90 m. In 1992 he failed to clear in his first three attempts (5.70, 5.70, 5.75 m) and was out of the <a href="/wiki/1992_Summer_Olympics" title="1992 Summer Olympics">Barcelona Olympics</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">Atlanta Olympics</a> in 1996 a heel injury caused him to withdraw from the competition without any attempts. In 2000 at the <a href="/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics" title="2000 Summer Olympics">Sydney Olympics</a> he was eliminated from the final after three unsuccessful attempts at 5.70 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_record_progression_by_Bubka">World record progression by Bubka</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World record progression by Bubka">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bubka broke the world record for men's pole vault 35 times during his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-3">[3]</a></sup> He broke the <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_pole_vault_world_record_progression" title="Men's pole vault world record progression">outdoor world record</a> 17 times and the <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_pole_vault_indoor_world_record_progression" title="Men's pole vault indoor world record progression">indoor world record</a> 18 times. Bubka lost his outdoor world record only once in his illustrious career. After <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Vigneron" title="Thierry Vigneron">Thierry Vigneron</a>, of France, broke his record on 31 August 1984 at the Golden Gala international track meet in Rome, Bubka subsequently reclaimed the record on his next run, just minutes later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bubka possessed great strength, speed and gymnastic abilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-3">[3]</a></sup> He gripped the pole higher than most vaulters to get extra leverage, though Bubka himself played down the effect of grip alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interview_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-interview-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>His development and mastery of the <a href="/wiki/Vitaly_Petrov_(coach)" title="Vitaly Petrov (coach)">Petrov</a>/Bubka technical model is also considered a key to his success. (A technical model is a sequence of positions and pressures which describe the method and style form of pole vaulting.) The Petrov/Bubka model is considered superior to many others today, because it allows the vaulter to continuously put energy into the pole while rising towards the bar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most conventional models focus on creating maximum bend in the pole before leaving the ground, by planting the pole heavily on the landing pad. The Petrov/Bubka model follows the technique used by <a href="/wiki/Kjell_Isaksson" title="Kjell Isaksson">Kjell Isaksson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> which concentrates on driving the pole up, rather than bending it while planting it on the landing pad, combined with high running speed. While the traditional models depended on the recoil by bending the pole, the Petrov/Bubka model may exploit the recoil of the pole and exert more energy on the pole during the swinging action.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Today Bubka is a member of the ‘Champions for Peace’ club, a group of 54 elite athletes committed to serving peace in the world through sport, created by <a href="/wiki/Peace_and_Sport" title="Peace and Sport">Peace and Sport</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>-based international organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IAAF">IAAF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: IAAF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bubka has been involved with the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Athletics_Federations" title="International Association of Athletics Federations">International Association of Athletics Federations</a> (IAAF) since 2001 and has served as a Vice President since 2007. During this time, he remained on the Athletes’ Commission (2001–2011) and is also a Council Member for ASOIF, the Association for Summer Olympic International Federations. Bubka commented: “I have been working at the IAAF for a long time and my work is not limited to one area. The good of athletics is something deep in my heart.” Bubka has been IAAF Council Member (2001-), IAAF Senior Vice-President (2007–2011), Vice-President (2011-), IAAF Development Commission Deputy Chairman (2007–2011), then Chairman (2011-), IAAF Athletes Commission member (2001–2011) and IAAF Competition Commission member (2003-). He was also a Coordination Commission Chairman of IAAF World Championships in Daegu 2011 and Moscow 2013.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Olympic_Committee">National Olympic Committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: National Olympic Committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As President of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Olympic_Committee_in_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Olympic Committee in Ukraine (page does not exist)">National Olympic Committee in Ukraine</a> since 2005, he has transformed the organisation into one of the most progressive in the world. It has staff based in all of the nation’s 27 regions with each taking responsibility for delivering an array of programmes designed to bring youngsters into sport, realise the potential of the most able and promote the Olympic Movement and its values. A National Olympic Day, the Olympic Stork which provides Olympic-themed education to more than 250,000 school classes across the country, televised annual awards and an Olympic Academy have all been established under Bubka’s reign. “NOCs must do more than select and send teams to Olympic Games,” says Bubka. “They are at the forefront of efforts to educate young people and help them become involved in sport and adopt a healthy lifestyle. To do that we need to work together globally because if we don’t we risk losing the younger generation.”</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Olympic_Committee_.28IOC.29">International Olympic Committee (IOC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergey_Bubka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: International Olympic Committee (IOC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sergey Bubka first got involved with the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC) in 1996 when he was elected as a Member of the Athletes’ Commission, providing input into the governance of sport from the perspective of an active athlete. Almost 20 years later he is still involved as an Honorary Member. “I knew that I wanted to be involved in running sport and, in particular to be involved in the Olympic Movement,” he said. He became an IOC Member in 1999 and has been involved in a wide range of Commissions, including Chairman of the Evaluation and then the Coordination Commissions for the inaugural Youth Olympic Games in Singapore in 2010. On 28 May 2013 Sergey Bubka announced that he would run for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidents_of_the_International_Olympic_Committee" title="Presidents of the International Olympic Committee">President</a> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/125th_IOC_Session" title="125th IOC Session">125th</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IOC_Session" title="IOC Session">IOC Session</a> in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> he lost the vote to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bach" title="Thomas Bach">Thomas Bach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
