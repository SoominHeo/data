<p><b>Saint Sebastian</b> (died c. 288 AD) was an early <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a>. According to Christian belief, he was killed during the Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>'s persecution of Christians. He is commonly depicted in art and literature tied to a post or tree and shot with arrows. Despite this being the most common artistic depiction of Sebastian, he was, according to legend, rescued and healed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irene_of_Rome" title="Irene of Rome">Irene of Rome</a>. Shortly afterwards he went to Diocletian to warn him about his sins, and as a result was clubbed to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHMI_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-2">[2]</a></sup> He is venerated in the Catholic and Orthodox Churches.</p>
<p>The details of Saint Sebastian's martyrdom were first spoken of by 4th -century bishop <a href="/wiki/Ambrose" title="Ambrose">Ambrose of Milan (Saint Ambrose)</a>, in his sermon (number 22) on <a href="/wiki/Psalm_118" title="Psalm 118">Psalm 118</a>. Ambrose stated that Sebastian came from <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and that he was already venerated there at that time. Saint Sebastian is a popular male saint, especially among athletes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Sebastian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1799" data-file-width="1206" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg/220px-Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg/330px-Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg/440px-Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Josse_Lieferinxe_-_Saint_Sebastian_Interceding_for_the_Plague_Stricken_-_Walters_371995.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Saint Sebastian Interceding for the Plague Stricken</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Josse_Lieferinxe" title="Josse Lieferinxe">Josse Lieferinxe</a>, 1497–1499, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Walters_Art_Museum" title="The Walters Art Museum">The Walters Art Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to Sebastian's 18th-century entry in <i><a href="/wiki/Acta_Sanctorum" title="Acta Sanctorum">Acta Sanctorum</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> still attributed to Ambrose by the 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Bolland" title="Jean Bolland">Jean Bolland</a>, and the briefer account in the 14th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Legenda Aurea</a></i>, he was a man of <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Narbonensis" title="Gallia Narbonensis">Gallia Narbonensis</a> who was taught in Milan. In 283, Sebastian entered the army in Rome under Emperor <a href="/wiki/Carinus" title="Carinus">Carinus</a> to assist the <a href="/wiki/Christian_martyrs" title="Christian martyrs">martyrs</a>. Because of his courage he became one of the captains of the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian Guards</a> under Diocletian and <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">Maximian</a>, who were unaware that he was a Christian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHMI_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to tradition, Marcus and Marcellian were twin brothers from a distinguished family and were <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacons</a>. Both brothers married, and they resided in Rome with their wives and children. The brothers refused to sacrifice to the Roman gods and were arrested. They were visited by their parents Tranquillinus and Martia in prison, who attempted to persuade them to renounce Christianity. Sebastian succeeded in converting Tranquillinus and Martia, as well as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Tiburtius" title="Saint Tiburtius">Saint Tiburtius</a>, the son of Chromatius, the local prefect. Another official, Nicostratus, and his wife Zoe were also converted. It has been said that Zoe had been a mute for six years; however, she made known to Sebastian her desire to be converted to Christianity. As soon as she had, her speech returned to her. Nicostratus then brought the rest of the prisoners; these 16 persons were converted by Sebastian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chromatius and Tiburtius converted; Chromatius set all of his prisoners free from jail, resigned his position, and retired to the country in Campania. Marcus and Marcellian, after being concealed by a Christian named <a href="/wiki/Castulus" title="Castulus">Castulus</a>, were later martyred, as were Nicostratus, Zoe, and Tiburtius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Martyrdom">Martyrdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Sebastian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Martyrdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stsebastian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="323" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Stsebastian.jpg/170px-Stsebastian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Stsebastian.jpg/255px-Stsebastian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/Stsebastian.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stsebastian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Reliquary" title="Reliquary">Reliquary</a> of St Sebastian, around 1497<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>, London)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sebastian had prudently concealed his faith, but in 286 was detected. Diocletian reproached him for his supposed betrayal, and he commanded him to be led to a field and there to be bound to a stake so that certain archers from <a href="/wiki/Mauretania" title="Mauretania">Mauritania</a> would shoot arrows at him. "And the archers shot at him till he was as full of arrows as an <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">urchin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[Note 1]</a></sup> is full of pricks, and thus left him there for dead."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Miraculously, the arrows did not kill him. The widow of Castulus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irene_of_Rome" title="Irene of Rome">Irene of Rome</a>, went to retrieve his body to bury it, and she discovered he was still alive. She brought him back to her house and nursed him back to health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHMI_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sebastian later stood by a staircase where the emperor was to pass and harangued Diocletian for his cruelties against Christians. This freedom of speech, and from a person whom he supposed to have been dead, greatly astonished the emperor; but, recovering from his surprise, he gave orders for his being seized and beat to death with cudgels, and his body thrown into the common sewer. A pious lady, called Lucina, admonished by the martyr in a vision, got it privately removed, and buried it in the catacombs at the entrance of the cemetery of Calixtus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-9">[9]</a></sup> where now stands the Basilica of St. Sebastian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHMI_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sebastian was said to be a defense against <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">the plague</a>. The <i>Golden Legend</i> transmits the episode of a great plague that afflicted the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a> in the time of King Gumburt, which was stopped by the erection of an altar in honor of Sebastian in the Church of Saint Peter in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pavia" title="Province of Pavia">Province of Pavia</a>.</p>

<p>Remains reputed to be those of Sebastian are housed in Rome in the <i>Basilica Apostolorum,</i> built by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Damasus_I" title="Pope Damasus I">Pope Damasus I</a> in 367 on the site of the provisional tomb of Saints <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul</a>. The church, today called <a href="/wiki/San_Sebastiano_fuori_le_mura" title="San Sebastiano fuori le mura">San Sebastiano fuori le mura</a>, was rebuilt in the 1610s under the patronage of <a href="/wiki/Scipione_Borghese" title="Scipione Borghese">Scipione Borghese</a>.</p>
<p>St. Ado, Eginard, Sigebert, and other contemporary authors relate that, in the reign of Louis Debonnair, Pope Eugenius II gave the body of St. Sebastian to Hilduin, Abbot of St. Denys, who brought it into France, and it was deposited at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St._Medard,_Soissons" title="Abbey of St. Medard, Soissons">Saint Medard Abbey</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">Soissons</a>, on the 8th of December, in 826.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sebastian's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranium" title="Cranium">cranium</a> was brought to the town of <a href="/wiki/Ebersberg" title="Ebersberg">Ebersberg</a> (<a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>) in 934. A Benedictine abbey was founded there and became one of the most important pilgrimage sites in southern Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> It is said the silver-encased cranium was used as a cup in which to present wine to the faithful during the feast of Saint Sebastian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art_and_literature">In art and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Sebastian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In art and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2601" data-file-width="2024" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg/240px-Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg/360px-Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg/480px-Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Georges_de_La_Tour_003.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>St. Sebastian tended by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irene_of_Rome" title="Irene of Rome">Saint Irene</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_de_La_Tour" title="Georges de La Tour">Georges de La Tour</a> c 1645</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest representation of Sebastian is a mosaic in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Apollinare_Nuovo" title="Basilica of Sant'Apollinare Nuovo">Basilica of Sant'Apollinare Nuovo</a> (Ravenna, Italy) dated between 527 and 565. The right lateral wall of the basilica contains large mosaics representing a procession of 26 martyrs, led by Saint Martin and including Sebastian. The martyrs are represented in Byzantine style, lacking any individuality, and all have identical expressions.</p>
<p>Another early representation is in a mosaic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> in the Church of <a href="/wiki/San_Pietro_in_Vincoli" title="San Pietro in Vincoli">San Pietro in Vincoli</a> (Rome, Italy), probably made in the year 682. It shows a grown, bearded man in court dress but contains no trace of an arrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The archers and arrows begin to appear by 1000, and ever since have been far more commonly shown than the actual moment of his death by clubbing, so that there is a popular misperception that this is how he died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>As protector of potential plague victims (a connection popularized by the <i>Golden Legend</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup>) and soldiers, Sebastian occupied an important place in the popular medieval mind. He was among the most frequently depicted of all saints by Late Gothic and Renaissance artists, in the period after the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The opportunity to show a semi-nude male, often in a contorted pose, also made Sebastian a favourite subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> His shooting with arrows was the subject of the largest <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> by the <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Playing_Cards" title="Master of the Playing Cards">Master of the Playing Cards</a></i> in the 1430s, when there were few other current subjects with male nudes other than Christ. Sebastian appears in many other <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">prints</a> and paintings, although this was due to his popularity with the faithful. Among many others, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botticelli" title="Botticelli">Botticelli</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perugino" title="Perugino">Perugino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollaiuolo" title="Pollaiuolo">Pollaiuolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Bellini" title="Giovanni Bellini">Giovanni Bellini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guido_Reni" title="Guido Reni">Guido Reni</a> (who painted the subject seven times), <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Mantegna</a> (three times), <a href="/wiki/Hans_Memling" title="Hans Memling">Hans Memling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerrit_van_Honthorst" title="Gerrit van Honthorst">Gerrit van Honthorst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luca_Signorelli" title="Luca Signorelli">Luca Signorelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Greco" title="El Greco">El Greco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Daumier" title="Honoré Daumier">Honoré Daumier</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louise_Bourgeois" title="Louise Bourgeois">Louise Bourgeois</a> all painted Saint Sebastians. An early work by the sculptor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gianlorenzo_Bernini" title="Gianlorenzo Bernini">Gianlorenzo Bernini</a> is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Sebastian_(Bernini)" title="St. Sebastian (Bernini)">Saint Sebastian</a>.</p>
<p>The saint is ordinarily depicted as a handsome youth pierced by arrows. <a href="/wiki/Predella" title="Predella">Predella</a> scenes when required, often depicted his arrest, confrontation with the Emperor, and final beheading. The illustration in the infobox is the <i>Saint Sebastian</i> of <a href="/wiki/Il_Sodoma" title="Il Sodoma">Il Sodoma</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Pitti" title="Palazzo Pitti">Pitti Palace</a>, Florence.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2274" data-file-width="1641" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg/220px-Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg/330px-Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg/440px-Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Woodblock_of_St_Sebastian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Woodblock of St Sebastian from South Germany, <i>circa</i> 1470–1475</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A mainly 17th-century subject, though found in <a href="/wiki/Predella" title="Predella">predella</a> scenes as early as the 15th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> was <i>St Sebastian tended by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irene_of_Rome" title="Irene of Rome">St Irene</a></i>, painted by <a href="/wiki/Georges_de_La_Tour" title="Georges de La Tour">Georges de La Tour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trophime_Bigot" title="Trophime Bigot">Trophime Bigot</a> (four times), <a href="/wiki/Jusepe_de_Ribera" title="Jusepe de Ribera">Jusepe de Ribera</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hendrick_ter_Brugghen" title="Hendrick ter Brugghen">Hendrick ter Brugghen</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Sebastian_Tended_by_Irene_(Ter_Brugghen)" title="Saint Sebastian Tended by Irene (Ter Brugghen)">perhaps his masterpiece</a>) and others. This may have been a deliberate attempt by the Church to get away from the single nude subject, which is already recorded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasari" title="Vasari">Vasari</a> as sometimes arousing inappropriate thoughts among female churchgoers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> The Baroque artists usually treated it as a nocturnal <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a> scene, illuminated by a single candle, torch or lantern, in the style fashionable in the first half of the 17th century. There exist several cycles depicting the life of Saint Sebastian. Among them are the frescos in the "Basilica di San Sebastiano" of Acireale (Italy) with paintings by Pietro Paolo Vasta.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Egon_Schiele" title="Egon Schiele">Egon Schiele</a>, an Austrian Expressionist artist, painted a self-portrait as Saint Sebastian in 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a>'s "Lorca (<a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">Federico García Lorca</a>) Period", he painted Sebastian several times, most notably in his "Neo-Cubist Academy".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1911, the Italian playwright <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabriele_d%27Annunzio" title="Gabriele d'Annunzio">Gabriele d'Annunzio</a> in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a> produced a <a href="/wiki/Mystery_play" title="Mystery play">mystery play</a> on the subject.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The American composer <a href="/wiki/Gian_Carlo_Menotti" title="Gian Carlo Menotti">Gian Carlo Menotti</a> composed a ballet score for a <a href="/wiki/Ballets_Russes" title="Ballets Russes">Ballets Russes</a> production which was first given in 1944.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In his novella <i><a href="/wiki/Death_in_Venice" title="Death in Venice">Death in Venice</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a> hails the "Sebastian-Figure" as the supreme emblem of <a href="/wiki/Apollonian_and_Dionysian" title="Apollonian and Dionysian">Apollonian</a> beauty, that is, the artistry of differentiated forms; beauty as measured by discipline, proportion, and luminous distinctions. This allusion to Saint Sebastian's suffering, associated with the writerly professionalism of the novella's protagonist, Gustav Aschenbach, provides a model for the "heroism born of weakness", which characterizes poise amidst agonizing torment and plain acceptance of one's fate as, beyond mere patience and passivity, a stylized achievement and artistic triumph.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">George Orwell</a>'s futuristic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four">Nineteen Eighty-Four</a></i>, the protagonist <a href="/wiki/Winston_Smith" title="Winston Smith">Winston Smith</a>, at the time he is not aware she actually loves him and hates the Party, is said to have dreams of ravishing the girl Julia, and having her pierced through with arrows like Saint Sebastian.</p>
<p>Sebastian's death was depicted in the 1949 film <i><a href="/wiki/Fabiola_(1949_film)" title="Fabiola (1949 film)">Fabiola</a></i>, in which he was played by <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Girotti" title="Massimo Girotti">Massimo Girotti</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1976, the British director <a href="/wiki/Derek_Jarman" title="Derek Jarman">Derek Jarman</a> made a film, <i><a href="/wiki/Sebastiane" title="Sebastiane">Sebastiane</a></i>, which caused controversy in its treatment of the martyr as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosexual_icon" title="Homosexual icon">homosexual icon</a>", according to a number of critics reflecting a subtext perceptible in the imagery since the Renaissance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent-28">[27]</a></sup> Also in 1976, in the American horror film <i><a href="/wiki/Carrie_(1976_film)" title="Carrie (1976 film)">Carrie</a></i>, a figure of Saint Sebastian (commonly misconstrued as a figure of the crucified Christ) appears in Carrie's prayer closet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally in 1976, a depiction of Saint Sebastian in a fresco restoration in an isolated Italian village is the central motif and cryptic mystery of the <a href="/wiki/Giallo" title="Giallo">giallo</a> horror film <a href="/wiki/The_House_with_Laughing_Windows" title="The House with Laughing Windows">The House with Laughing Windows</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_III" title="The Godfather Part III">The Godfather Part III</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Corleone" title="Michael Corleone">Michael Corleone</a> is awarded the <b>Order of Saint Sebastian</b>, said by some sources to be fictitious.</p>
<p>Pietro Vannucci Perugino’s painting (c. 1495) of Saint Sebastian is featured in the 2001 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Wit_(film)" title="Wit (film)">Wit</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Emma_Thompson" title="Emma Thompson">Emma Thompson</a>. Thompson’s character, as a college student, visits her professor's office, where an almost life-size painting of Saint Sebastian hangs on the wall. Later, when the main character is a professor herself, diagnosed with cancer, she keeps a small print of this same painting of Saint Sebastian next to her hospital bed. The allusion appears to be to Sebastian's stoic martyrdom - a role the Thompson character has willingly accepted for the betterment of all mankind. There may be a touch of authorial (or directorial) cynicism in making this "saintly" connection.</p>
<p>In 2007, artist <a href="/wiki/Damien_Hirst" title="Damien Hirst">Damien Hirst</a> presented Saint Sebastian, Exquisite Pain from his Natural History series. The piece depicts a cow in formaldehyde, bound in metal cable and shot with arrows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>British pop band <a href="/wiki/Alt-J" title="Alt-J">Alt-J</a>'s video for <a href="/wiki/Hunger_of_the_Pine" title="Hunger of the Pine">Hunger of the Pine</a> contains references to the story of Saint Sebastian's death, adapted to fit the lyrics of the song. <a href="/wiki/Tarsem_Singh" title="Tarsem Singh">Tarsem Singh</a>'s video for the <a href="/wiki/R.E.M." title="R.E.M.">R.E.M.</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Losing_My_Religion" title="Losing My Religion">Losing My Religion</a>" makes use of imagery of St. Sebastian, drawing particular inspiration from paintings by Guido Reni<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cecco_del_Caravaggio" title="Cecco del Caravaggio">Caravaggio</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronage">Patronage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Sebastian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Patronage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lodovico_Carracci_(Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3147" data-file-width="4387" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Lodovico_Carracci_%28Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Lodovico_Carracci_%28Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Lodovico_Carracci_%28Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Lodovico_Carracci_%28Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Lodovico_Carracci_%28Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Lodovico_Carracci_%28Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lodovico_Carracci_(Italian_-_St._Sebastian_Thrown_into_the_Cloaca_Maxima_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lodovico_Carracci" title="Lodovico Carracci">Lodovico Carracci</a>'s rare treatment of the subject of <i>St. Sebastian Thrown into the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca_Maxima" title="Cloaca Maxima">Cloaca Maxima</a></i> (1612)</div>

<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, Sebastian is commemorated by an optional memorial on 20 January. In the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Greece" title="Church of Greece">Church of Greece</a>, Sebastian's feast day is on 18 December.</p>
<p>As a protector from the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a>, Sebastian was formerly one of the <a href="/wiki/Fourteen_Holy_Helpers" title="Fourteen Holy Helpers">Fourteen Holy Helpers</a>. The connection of the martyr shot with arrows with the plague is not an intuitive one, however. In Greco-Roman myth, <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, the archer god, is the deliverer from pestilence; the figure of Sebastian <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianizes</a> this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folkloric" title="Folkloric">folkloric</a> association. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronicler" title="Chronicler">chronicler</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Deacon" title="Paul the Deacon">Paul the Deacon</a> relates that, in 680, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> was freed from a raging pestilence by him.</p>
<p>In Catholicism, Sebastian is the patron saint of archers, athletes, and of a holy death.</p>

<p>Sebastian is one of the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saints</a> of the city of <a href="/wiki/Qormi" title="Qormi">Qormi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> along with Saint George.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Sebastian is the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of Acireale, <a href="/wiki/Caserta" title="Caserta">Caserta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petilia_Policastro" title="Petilia Policastro">Petilia Policastro</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melilli" title="Melilli">Melilli</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_(Spain)" title="San Sebastián (Spain)">San Sebastián</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Majorca" title="Palma, Majorca">Palma de Mallorca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huelva" title="Huelva">Huelva</a> in Spain. He is the patron saint of <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. Informally, in the tradition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Brazilian" title="Afro-Brazilian">Afro-Brazilian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syncretic_religion" title="Syncretic religion">syncretic religion</a> <a href="/wiki/Umbanda" title="Umbanda">Umbanda</a>, Sebastian is often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxossi" title="Oxossi">Oxossi</a>, especially in the state of Rio de Janeiro itself.</p>
<p>Feast of St. Sebastian is celebrated among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> communities of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in India, with lot of elegance and colour. Churches are grandiosely illuminated and decorated, with bursting of crackers being a main event in every catholic homes to commemorate the saint. Every parish has its own date of celebration, especially in the districts of <a href="/wiki/Thrissur" title="Thrissur">Thrissur</a> , <a href="/wiki/Ernakulam" title="Ernakulam">Ernakulam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alapuzha" title="Alapuzha">Alapuzha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kottayam" title="Kottayam">Kottayam</a>. Besides many pilgrim centres, churches,shrines and many educational institutions too, throughout Kerala, bear the name of the saint</p>
<p>He is the patron of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Sebastian_College_-_Recoletos" title="San Sebastian College - Recoletos">a college named for him</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> which is adjacent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parish_of_San_Sebastian" title="Parish of San Sebastian">Parish of San Sebastian</a>.</p>
<p>Sebastian is the patron saint of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Bacolod" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Bacolod">Roman Catholic Diocese of Bacolod</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Negros_Occidental" title="Negros Occidental">Negros Occidental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.</p>
<p>Saint Sebastian is the patron of <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Columbus" title="Knights of Columbus">Knights of Columbus</a> Council #4926 in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_San_Jose_in_California" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of San Jose in California">Roman Catholic Diocese of San Jose in California</a>, serving the cities of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">Mountain View</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Altos,_California" title="Los Altos, California">Los Altos</a>.</p>
<p>In his 1906 <i>Reminiscences</i>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schurz" title="Carl Schurz">Carl Schurz</a> recalls the annual "bird shoot" pageant of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhenish</a> town of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Liblar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Liblar (page does not exist)">Liblar</a> which was sponsored by the Saint Sebastian Society, a club of <a href="/wiki/Sharpshooter" title="Sharpshooter">sharpshooters</a> and their sponsors to which nearly every adult member of town belonged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/St._Sebastian_River" title="St. Sebastian River">St. Sebastian River</a> is named for him. It is a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_River_Lagoon" title="Indian River Lagoon">Indian River Lagoon</a> and comprises part of the boundary between <a href="/wiki/Indian_River_County,_Florida" title="Indian River County, Florida">Indian River County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brevard_County,_Florida" title="Brevard County, Florida">Brevard County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. The adjacent city of <a href="/wiki/Sebastian,_Florida" title="Sebastian, Florida">Sebastian, Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Sebastian_River_Preserve_State_Park" title="St. Sebastian River Preserve State Park">St. Sebastian River Preserve State Park</a> are also named for Saint Sebastian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
