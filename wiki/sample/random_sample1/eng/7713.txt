<p><b>Sobek</b> (also called <b>Sebek</b>, <b>Sochet</b>, <b>Sobk</b>, and <b>Sobki</b>), in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a>, <b>Suchos</b> (Σοῦχος) and from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Suchus</i>, was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian" title="Ancient Egyptian">ancient Egyptian</a> deity with a complex and fluid nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Nile_crocodile" title="Nile crocodile">Nile crocodile</a> and is either represented in its form or as a human with a crocodile head. Sobek was also associated with pharaonic power, fertility, and military prowess, but served additionally as a protective deity with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apotropaic" title="Apotropaic">apotropaic</a> qualities, invoked particularly for protection against the dangers presented by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_river" title="Nile river">Nile river</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sobek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sobek enjoyed a longstanding presence in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_deities" title="Ancient Egyptian deities">ancient Egyptian pantheon</a>, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom" title="Old Kingdom">Old Kingdom</a> (c. 2686–2181 BCE) through the Roman period (c. 30 BCE – 350 CE). He is first known from several different <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Texts" title="Pyramid Texts">Pyramid Texts</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom" title="Old Kingdom">Old Kingdom</a>, particularly from spell PT 317.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The spell, which praises the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> as living incarnation of the <a href="/wiki/Nile_crocodile" title="Nile crocodile">crocodile</a> god, reads:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Unas" title="Unas">Unis</a> is Sobek, green of plumage, with alert face and raised fore, the splashing one who came from the thigh and tail of the great goddess in the sunlight ... Unis has appeared as Sobek, <a href="/wiki/Neith" title="Neith">Neith</a>'s son. Unis will eat with his mouth, Unis will urinate and Unis will copulate with his penis. Unis is lord of semen, who takes women from their husbands to the place Unis likes according to his heart's fancy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The origin of his name, <i>Sbk</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WB_IV.2C_95_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WB_IV.2C_95-5">[5]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">ancient Egyptian</a>, is debated among scholars, but many believe that it is derived from a causative of the verb "to impregnate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Though Sobek was worshipped in the Old Kingdom, he truly gained prominence in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a> (c. 2055–1650 BCE), most notably under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelfth_Dynasty" title="Twelfth Dynasty">Twelfth Dynasty</a> king, <a href="/wiki/Amenemhat_III" title="Amenemhat III">Amenemhat III</a>. Amenemhat III had taken a particular interest in the Faiyum region of Egypt, a region heavily associated with Sobek. Amenemhat and many of his dynastic contemporaries engaged in building projects to promote Sobek – projects that were often executed in the Faiyum. In this period, Sobek also underwent an important change: he was often fused with the falcon-headed god of divine kingship, <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>. This brought Sobek even closer with the kings of Egypt, thereby giving him a place of greater prominence in the Egyptian pantheon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The fusion added a finer level of complexity to the god’s nature, as he was adopted into the divine triad of Horus and his two parents: <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zecchi.2C_3_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zecchi.2C_3-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sobek first acquired a role as a <a href="/wiki/Solar_deity" title="Solar deity">solar deity</a> through his connection to Horus, but this was further strengthened in later periods with the emergence of Sobek-Ra, a fusion of Sobek and Egypt's primary sun god, <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>. Sobek-Horus persisted as a figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Kingdom" title="New Kingdom">New Kingdom</a> (1550–1069 BCE), but it was not until the last dynasties of Egypt that Sobek-Ra gained prominence. This understanding of the god was maintained after the fall of Egypt's last native dynasty in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom" title="Ptolemaic Kingdom">Ptolemaic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%86gyptus" title="Ægyptus">Roman Egypt</a> (c. 332 BCE – 390 CE). The prestige of both Sobek and Sobek-Ra endured in this time period and tributes to him attained greater prominence – both through the expansion of his dedicated cultic sites and a concerted scholarly effort to make him the subject of religious doctrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cult_centers">Cult centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sobek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cult centers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The entire Faiyum region – the "Land of the Lake" in Egyptian (specifically referring to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Moeris" title="Lake Moeris">Lake Moeris</a>) – served as a cult center of Sobek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zecchi_153_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zecchi_153-10">[10]</a></sup> Most Faiyum towns developed their own localized versions of the god, such as Soknebtunis at Tebtunis, Sokonnokonni at Bacchias, and Souxei at an unknown site in the area. At Karanis, two forms of the god were worshipped: Pnepheros and Petsuchos. There, mummified crocodiles were employed as cult images of Petsuchos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sobek Shedety, the patron of the Faiyum's centrally located capital, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crocodilopolis" title="Crocodilopolis">Crocodilopolis</a> (or Egyptian "Shedet"), was the most prominent form of the god. Extensive building programs honoring Sobek were realized in Shedet, as it was the capital of the entire Arsinoite <a href="/wiki/Nome_(Egypt)" title="Nome (Egypt)">nome</a> and consequently the most important city in the region. It is thought that the effort to expand Sobek's main temple was initially driven by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_II" title="Ptolemy II">Ptolemy II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zecchi_153_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zecchi_153-10">[10]</a></sup> Specialized priests in the main temple at Shedet functioned solely to serve Sobek, boasting titles like "prophet of the crocodile-gods" and "one who buries of the bodies of the crocodile-gods of the Land of the Lake".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breschiani_203_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Breschiani_203-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Outside the Faiyum, <a href="/wiki/Kom_Ombo" title="Kom Ombo">Kom Ombo</a>, in southern Egypt, was the biggest cultic center of Sobek, particularly during the Ptolemaic and Roman periods. Located about 30 miles (48 km) north of Aswan and was built during the Graeco-Roman period (332 BC AD 395). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>The temple at this site was called the "Per-Sobek", meaning the "house of Sobek".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breschiani_203_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Breschiani_203-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Character_and_surrounding_mythologies">Character and surrounding mythologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sobek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Character and surrounding mythologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sovk_(Suchus,_Cronos,_Satrune),_N372.2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="967" height="349" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Sovk_%28Suchus%2C_Cronos%2C_Satrune%29%2C_N372.2.jpg/220px-Sovk_%28Suchus%2C_Cronos%2C_Satrune%29%2C_N372.2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Sovk_%28Suchus%2C_Cronos%2C_Satrune%29%2C_N372.2.jpg/330px-Sovk_%28Suchus%2C_Cronos%2C_Satrune%29%2C_N372.2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Sovk_%28Suchus%2C_Cronos%2C_Satrune%29%2C_N372.2.jpg/440px-Sovk_%28Suchus%2C_Cronos%2C_Satrune%29%2C_N372.2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Sobek is, above all else, an aggressive and animalistic deity who lives up to the vicious reputation of his patron animal, the large and violent <a href="/wiki/Nile_crocodile" title="Nile crocodile">Nile crocodile</a>. Some of his common epithets betray this nature succinctly, the most notable of which being: "he who loves robbery", "he who eats while he also mates", and "pointed of teeth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, he also displays grand benevolence in more than one celebrated myth. After his association with Horus and consequent adoption into the Osirian triad of Osiris, Isis, and Horus in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a>, Sobek became associated with Isis as a healer of the deceased Osiris (following his violent murder by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_(mythology)" title="Set (mythology)">Set</a> in the central <a href="/wiki/Osiris_myth" title="Osiris myth">Osiris myth</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zecchi.2C_3_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zecchi.2C_3-8">[8]</a></sup> In fact, though many scholars believe that the name of Sobek, <i>Sbk</i>, is derived from <i>s-bAk</i>, "to impregnate", others postulate that it is a participial form of the verb <i>sbq</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WB_IV.2C_95_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-WB_IV.2C_95-5">[5]</a></sup> an alternative writing of <i>sAq</i>, "to unite", thereby meaning <i>Sbk</i> could roughly translate to "he who unites (the dismembered limbs of Osiris)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is from this association with healing that Sobek was considered a protective deity. His fierceness was able to ward off evil while simultaneously defending the innocent. He was thus made a subject of personal piety and a common recipient of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Votive_offerings" title="Votive offerings">votive offerings</a>, particularly in the later periods of ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_history" title="Egyptian history">Egyptian history</a>. It was not uncommon, particularly in Ptolemaic and Roman Egypt, for crocodiles to be preserved as <a href="/wiki/Animal_mummy" title="Animal mummy">mummies</a> in order to present at Sobek's cultic centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ikram.2C_219_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ikram.2C_219-16">[16]</a></sup> Sobek was also offered mummified crocodile eggs, meant to emphasize the cyclical nature of his solar attributes as Sobek-Ra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Likewise, crocodiles were raised on religious grounds as living incarnations of Sobek. Upon their deaths, they were mummified in a grand ritual display as sacred, but earthly, manifestations of their patron god. This practice was executed specifically at the main temple of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crocodilopolis" title="Crocodilopolis">Crocodilopolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These mummified crocodiles have been found with baby crocodiles in their mouths and on their backs. The crocodile – one of the few non-mammals that diligently care for their young – often transports its offspring in this manner. The practice of preserving this aspect of the animal's behavior via mummification is likely intended to emphasize the protective and nurturing aspects of the fierce Sobek, as he protects the Egyptian people in the same manner that the crocodile protects its young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ikram.2C_219_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ikram.2C_219-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Ptolemaic and Roman Egypt, a local monograph called the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Faiyum" title="Book of the Faiyum">Book of the Faiyum</a></i> centered on Sobek with a considerable portion devoted to the journey made by Sobek-Ra each day with the movement of the sun through the sky. The text also focuses heavily on Sobek's central role in creation as a manifestation of Ra, as he is said to have risen from the primal waters of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Moeris" title="Lake Moeris">Lake Moeris</a>, not unlike the <a href="/wiki/Ogdoad_(Egyptian)" title="Ogdoad (Egyptian)">Ogdoad</a> in the traditional creation myth of <a href="/wiki/Hermopolis" title="Hermopolis">Hermopolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many varied copies of the book exist and many scholars feel that it was produced in large quantities as a "best-seller" in antiquity. The integral relationship between the Faiyum and Sobek is highlighted via this text, and his far reaching influence is seen in localities that are outside of the Faiyum as well; a portion of the book is copied on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egyptian</a> (meaning southern Egyptian) <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Kom_Ombo" title="Temple of Kom Ombo">Temple of Kom Ombo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
