<p><i><b>Seventeen</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Magazine" title="Magazine">magazine</a> for <a href="/wiki/Adolescence" title="Adolescence">teenagers</a>. The magazine's reader base is 10-to-21-year-old females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It began as a publication geared towards inspiring teen girls to become model workers and citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fashioning_Teenagers:_A_Cultural_History_of_Seventeen_Magazine_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fashioning_Teenagers:_A_Cultural_History_of_Seventeen_Magazine-3">[3]</a></sup> Soon after its debut, <i>Seventeen</i> took a more fashion and romance-oriented approach in presenting its material while promoting self-confidence in young women. It was first published in September 1944<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Annenberg" title="Walter Annenberg">Walter Annenberg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Publications" title="Triangle Publications">Triangle Publications</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seventeen.27s_early_history"><i>Seventeen'</i>s early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seventeen_(American_magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Seventeen's early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first editor of <i>Seventeen</i>, <a href="/wiki/Helen_Valentine" title="Helen Valentine">Helen Valentine</a>, provided teenage girls with working woman role models and information about their development. <i>Seventeen</i> enhanced the role of teenagers as consumers of popular culture. The concept of "<a href="/wiki/Adolescence" title="Adolescence">teenager</a>" as a distinct demographic originated in that era. In July 1944, <a href="/wiki/King_Features_Syndicate" title="King Features Syndicate">King Features Syndicate</a> began running the comic strip <i><a href="/wiki/Teena" title="Teena">Teena</a></i>, created by cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Hilda_Terry" title="Hilda Terry">Hilda Terry</a>, in which a typical teenager's life was examined. <i>Teena</i> ran internationally in newspapers for twenty years.</p>
<p>After <i>Seventeen</i> was launched in September 1944,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Estelle Ellis Rubenstein, the magazine's promotion director, introduced advertisers to the life of teenage girls through <i>Teena</i>, selling advertising in <i>Seventeen</i> at the same time. From 1945 to 1946, the magazine surveyed teen girls in order to better understand the magazine's audience. The magazine became an important source of information to manufacturers seeking guidance on how to satisfy consumer demand among teenagers. Today, the magazine entertains as well as promotes self-confidence in young women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Plath" title="Sylvia Plath">Sylvia Plath</a> submitted nearly fifty pieces to <i>Seventeen</i> before her first short story, "And Summer Will Not Come Again", was accepted and published in the August 1950 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joyce Walker became the first black model to be featured on the cover of Seventeen magazine in July 1971. In the early 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">Whitney Houston</a> was also featured on the cover of the magazine.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corporation</a> bought Triangle in 1988 and sold <i>Seventeen</i> to <a href="/wiki/Primedia" title="Primedia">K-III Communications</a> (later Primedia) in 1991. Primedia sold the magazine to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst</a> in 2003. <i>Seventeen</i> remains popular on newsstands today despite greater competition.</p>
<p>In 2010, writer <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Keiles" title="Jamie Keiles">Jamie Keiles</a> conducted "The Seventeen Magazine Project", an experiment in which she followed the advice of <i>Seventeen</i> magazine for thirty days.</p>
<p>In 2012, in response to reader protests against the magazine's airbrushing its models' photos, <i>Seventeen</i> ended its practice of using <a href="/wiki/Photo_manipulation" title="Photo manipulation">digital photo manipulation</a> to enhance published photographs. (See more below under <a href="#Controversy">Controversy</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2016, Michelle Tan was fired from her position as Editor in Chief while she was on maternity leave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It was announced shortly thereafter that Michele Promaulayko, who was appointed Editor in Chief of Cosmopolitan, would also serve as Seventeen's editorial director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Starting with their Dec/Jan 2017 issue, the magazine was to start publishing only six issues a year instead of ten, to focus on their online presence to appeal to the Generation Z market.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_editions">International editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seventeen_(American_magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> edition of <i>Seventeen</i> magazine is published by 8 Ink Media based in Cape Town. The editor is Janine Jellars.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippine</a> version is published by <a href="/wiki/Summit_Media" title="Summit Media">Summit Media</a>, but it ceased publication in April 2009.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> American edition is published by Editorial Televisa.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> edition is published by Apricot Publications Pvt. Ltd in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysian</a> version of <i>Seventeen</i> is published by Bluinc.</li>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thai</a> edition of <i>Seventeen</i> is published by Media Transasia Limited in <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> there is no <i>Seventeen</i> magazine, but there is a similar magazine recently touted as a fresher and edgier competition to <i><a href="/wiki/Teen_Vogue" title="Teen Vogue">Teen Vogue</a></i> called <i><a href="/wiki/Company_(magazine)" title="Company (magazine)">Company</a></i>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seventeen_in_other_media"><i>Seventeen</i> in other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seventeen_(American_magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seventeen in other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Seventeen</i> has also published books for teens, addressing such topics as beauty, style, college, health and fitness.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="America.27s_Next_Top_Model"><i>America's Next Top Model</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seventeen_(American_magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: America's Next Top Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Seventeen</i> was a sponsor of <i><a href="/wiki/America%27s_Next_Top_Model" title="America's Next Top Model">America's Next Top Model</a></i>. The winners of <i>America's Next Top Model</i> from seasons 7 through 14 have each graced a cover of <i>Seventeen</i> magazine, including <a href="/wiki/CariDee_English" title="CariDee English">CariDee English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaslene_Gonzalez" title="Jaslene Gonzalez">Jaslene Gonzalez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sal_Stowers" title="Sal Stowers">Sal Stowers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Thompson" title="Whitney Thompson">Whitney Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/McKey_Sullivan" title="McKey Sullivan">McKey Sullivan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teyona_Anderson" title="Teyona Anderson">Teyona Anderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Fox" title="Nicole Fox">Nicole Fox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Krista_White" title="Krista White">Krista White</a>. Originally, the magazine only planned on sponsoring the show from cycles 7 through 10; however, with such a high success rate and a great opportunity the magazine provided for these women, the magazine sponsored the cycles until the show decided to move the winners to <i><a href="/wiki/Vogue_Italia" title="Vogue Italia">Vogue Italia</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyberbu.2F.2Fy"><i>Cyberbu//y</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seventeen_(American_magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cyberbu//y">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, <i>Seventeen</i> worked together with <a href="/wiki/Freeform_(TV_channel)#Sale_to_Disney_and_rebranding_as_ABC_Family_.282001.E2.80.932006.29" title="Freeform (TV channel)">ABC Family</a> to make a film about a girl who gets bullied online called <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberbully_(2011_film)" title="Cyberbully (2011 film)">Cyberbu//y</a></i>. The point was to raise awareness of cyber bullying and to "delete digital drama". The film premiered July 17, 2011 on ABC Family.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seventeen_(American_magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2012, 14-year-old ballerina and blogger Julia Bluhm from <a href="/wiki/Waterville,_Maine" title="Waterville, Maine">Waterville, Maine</a> created a petition on <i><a href="/wiki/Change.org" title="Change.org">Change.org</a></i> titled "Seventeen Magazine: Give Girls Images of Real Girls!' advocating for the magazine publication to vow to print at least one unaltered and Photoshop-FREE monthly photo spread".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Julia Bluhm is a "SPARK Summit activist"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and declared that "as part of SPARK Movement, a girl-fueled, national activist movement, I've been fighting to stop magazines, toy companies, and other big businesses from creating products, photo spreads and ads that hurt girls' and break our self-esteem".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2012 Julia Bluhm, her mother, and a group of fellow SPARK Summit activists were invited to the New York headquarters of <i>Seventeen</i> by editor-in-chief <a href="/wiki/Ann_Shoket" title="Ann Shoket">Ann Shoket</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 July 2012, Bluhm officially won her petition upon receiving almost 85,000 (as of 23 February 2015, 86,435) signatures, resulting in <i>Seventeen</i>'s editorial staff pledging to always feature one photo spread per month without the use of digital photo manipulation.</p>
<p>Furthermore, <i>Seventeen</i>'s <a href="/wiki/Ann_Shoket" title="Ann Shoket">Ann Shoket</a> published The Body Peace Treaty in her editor's letter of the 2012 August issue, as an extension of the magazine's ongoing Body Peace Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
