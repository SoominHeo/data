<p><b>Cecil John Rhodes</b> <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">PC</a> (5 July 1853 – 26 March 1902)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Times27_March_1902_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Times27_March_1902-1">[1]</a></sup> was a British businessman, mining <a href="/wiki/Magnate" title="Magnate">magnate</a> and politician in South Africa, who served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Cape_Colony" title="Prime Minister of the Cape Colony">Prime Minister</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> from 1890 to 1896. An ardent believer in British <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>, Rhodes and his <a href="/wiki/British_South_Africa_Company" title="British South Africa Company">British South Africa Company</a> founded the southern African territory of <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia_(region)" title="Rhodesia (region)">Rhodesia</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>), which the company named after him in 1895. South Africa's <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_University" title="Rhodes University">Rhodes University</a> is also named after him. Rhodes set up the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship" title="Rhodes Scholarship">Rhodes Scholarship</a>, which is funded by his estate, and put much effort towards his vision of a <a href="/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Railway" title="Cape to Cairo Railway">Cape to Cairo Railway</a> through British territory.</p>
<p>The son of a vicar, Rhodes grew up in <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Stortford" title="Bishop's Stortford">Bishop's Stortford</a>, Hertfordshire, and was a sickly child. He was sent to South Africa by his family when he was 17 years old in the hope that the climate might improve his health. He entered the diamond trade at <a href="/wiki/Kimberley,_Northern_Cape" title="Kimberley, Northern Cape">Kimberley</a> in 1871, when he was 18, and over the next two decades gained near-complete domination of the world diamond market. His <a href="/wiki/De_Beers" title="De Beers">De Beers</a> diamond company, formed in 1888, retains its prominence into the 21st century. Rhodes entered the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Parliament of the Cape of Good Hope">Cape Parliament</a> in 1880, and a decade later became Prime Minister. After overseeing the formation of Rhodesia during the early 1890s, he was forced to resign as Prime Minister in 1896 after the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Jameson_Raid" title="Jameson Raid">Jameson Raid</a>, an unauthorised attack on <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kruger" title="Paul Kruger">Paul Kruger</a>'s <a href="/wiki/South_African_Republic" title="South African Republic">South African Republic</a> (or Transvaal). After Rhodes's death in 1902, at the age of 48, he was buried in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matopos_Hills" title="Matopos Hills">Matopos Hills</a> in what is now Zimbabwe. At the time of his death he was already a very controversial figure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of Rhodes's primary motivators in politics and business was his professed belief that the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon race</a> was, to quote his <a href="/wiki/Will_and_testament" title="Will and testament">will</a>, "the first race in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes190258_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes190258-3">[3]</a></sup> Under the reasoning that "the more of the world we inhabit the better it is for the human race",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes190258_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes190258-3">[3]</a></sup> he advocated vigorous <a href="/wiki/Settler_colonialism" title="Settler colonialism">settler colonialism</a> and ultimately a reformation of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> so that each component would be self-governing and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Federation" title="Imperial Federation">represented in a single parliament</a> in London. Ambitions such as these, juxtaposed with his policies regarding indigenous Africans in the Cape Colony—describing the country's black population as largely "in a state of barbarism",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996109_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996109-4">[4]</a></sup> he advocated their governance as a "subject race"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996109_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996109-4">[4]</a></sup> and was at the centre of moves to marginalise them politically—have led recent critics to characterise him as a <a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">white supremacist</a> and "an architect of apartheid."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECastle29_January_2016_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECastle29_January_2016-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historian Richard A. McFarlane has called Rhodes "as integral a participant in southern African and British imperial history as <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> or <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> are in their respective eras in United States history. Most histories of South Africa covering the last decades of the nineteenth century are contributions to the <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a> of Cecil Rhodes." According to McFarlane, the aforementioned historiography "may be divided into two broad categories: <a href="/wiki/Chauvinism" title="Chauvinism">chauvinistic</a> approval or utter vilification".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcFarlane2007_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcFarlane2007-6">[6]</a></sup> Paul Maylam identifies three perspectives: works that attempt to either venerate or debunk Rhodes, and "the intermediate view, according to which Rhodes is not straightforwardly assessed as either hero or villain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaylam20054_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaylam20054-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2283" data-file-width="1483" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg/220px-Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg/330px-Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg/440px-Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cecil_Rhodes_as_a_boy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rhodes as a boy</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg/220px-Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg/330px-Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg/440px-Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhodes_Arts_Complex_and_Bishop%27s_Stortford_Museum_-_geograph.org.uk_-_592543.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Rhodes was born in 1853 in <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Stortford" title="Bishop's Stortford">Bishop's Stortford</a>, Hertfordshire, England. He was the fifth son of the Reverend Francis William Rhodes and his wife Louisa Peacock Rhodes. His father was a <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clergyman" title="Clergyman">clergyman</a> who was proud of never having preached a sermon longer than 10 minutes. His siblings included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_William_Rhodes" title="Francis William Rhodes">Francis William Rhodes</a>, who became an army officer.</p>
<p>Rhodes attended the <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Stortford" title="Bishop's Stortford">Bishop's Stortford</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammar_School" title="Grammar School">Grammar School</a> from the age of nine, but, as a sickly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asthmatic" title="Asthmatic">asthmatic</a> adolescent, he was taken out of grammar school in 1869 and, according to Basil Williams,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams1921_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams1921-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> "continued his studies under his father's eye (...) His health was weak and there were even fears that he might be <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">consumptive</a>, a disease of which several of the family showed symptoms. His father therefore determined to send him abroad to try the effect of a sea voyage and a better climate. Herbert [Cecil's brother] had already set up as a planter in <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Natal" title="Colony of Natal">Natal</a>, South Africa, so Cecil was despatched on a sailing vessel to join Herbert in Natal. The voyage to Durban took him seventy days, and on 1 September 1870 he first set foot on African soil, a tall, lanky, anaemic, fair-haired boy, shy and reserved in bearing." His family's hope was that the climate would improve his health. They expected he would help his older brother Herbert<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup> who operated a cotton farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas1997_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas1997-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When he first came to Africa, Rhodes lived on money lent by his aunt Sophia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFlint2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlint2009-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After a brief stay with the Surveyor-General of Natal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._P.C._Sutherland" title="Dr. P.C. Sutherland">Dr. P.C. Sutherland</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Pietermaritzburg" title="Pietermaritzburg">Pietermaritzburg</a>, Rhodes took an interest in agriculture. He joined his brother Herbert on his cotton farm in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umkomazi" title="Umkomazi">Umkomazi</a> valley in Natal. The land was unsuitable for cotton, and the venture failed.</p>
<p>In October 1871, 18-year-old Rhodes and his brother Herbert left the colony for the diamond fields of <a href="/wiki/Kimberley,_Northern_Cape" title="Kimberley, Northern Cape">Kimberley</a>. Financed by <a href="/wiki/N_M_Rothschild_%26_Sons" title="N M Rothschild &amp; Sons">N M Rothschild &amp; Sons</a>, Rhodes succeeded over the next 17 years in buying up all the smaller diamond mining operations in the Kimberley area. In 1873, he returned to <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> to study at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a>, but stayed there for only one term after which he went back to South Africa. His monopoly of the world's diamond supply was sealed in 1890 through a strategic partnership with the London-based Diamond Syndicate. They agreed to control world supply to maintain high prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEpstein1982_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEpstein1982-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnowles2005_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnowles2005-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Rhodes supervised the working of his brother's claim and <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">speculated</a> on his behalf. Among his associates in the early days were <a href="/wiki/John_X._Merriman" title="John X. Merriman">John X. Merriman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rudd" title="Charles Rudd">Charles Rudd</a>, who later became his partner in the <a href="/wiki/De_Beers" title="De Beers">De Beers Mining Company</a> and the Niger Oil Company.</p>
<p>During the 1880s, <a href="/wiki/Boschendal" title="Boschendal">Cape vineyards</a> had been devastated by a <a href="/wiki/Phylloxera" title="Phylloxera">phylloxera</a> epidemic. The diseased vineyards were dug up and replanted, and farmers were looking for alternatives to wine. In 1892, Rhodes financed The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pioneer_Fruit_Growing_Company" title="Pioneer Fruit Growing Company">Pioneer Fruit Growing Company</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nooitgedacht,_South_Africa" title="Nooitgedacht, South Africa">Nooitgedacht</a>, a venture created by Harry Pickstone, an Englishman who had experience with fruit-growing in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoschendal2007_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoschendal2007-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The shipping magnate <a href="/wiki/Percy_Molteno" title="Percy Molteno">Percy Molteno</a> had just undertaken the first successful refrigerated export to Europe and in 1896, after consulting with Molteno, Rhodes began to pay more attention to export fruit farming and bought farms in Groot Drakenstein, <a href="/wiki/Wellington,_Western_Cape" title="Wellington, Western Cape">Wellington</a> and Stellenbosch. A year later, he bought Rhone and <a href="/wiki/Boschendal" title="Boschendal">Boschendal</a> and commissioned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Herbert_Baker" title="Sir Herbert Baker">Sir Herbert Baker</a> to build him a cottage there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoschendal2007_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoschendal2007-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPicton-Seymour1989_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPicton-Seymour1989-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The successful operation soon expanded into <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Fruit_Farms" title="Rhodes Fruit Farms">Rhodes Fruit Farms</a>, and formed a cornerstone of the modern-day Cape fruit industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOberholster198791_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOberholster198791-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="632" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG/220px-Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG/330px-Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG/440px-Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhodes%27_portrait_bust.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A portrait bust of Rhodes on the first floor of No. 6 <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_Street" title="King Edward Street">King Edward Street</a> marks the place of his residence whilst in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1873, Rhodes left his farm field in the care of his business partner, Rudd, and sailed for England to complete his studies. He was admitted to <a href="/wiki/Oriel_College,_Oxford" title="Oriel College, Oxford">Oriel College, Oxford</a>, but stayed for only one term in 1873. He returned to South Africa and did not return for his second term at Oxford until 1876. He was greatly influenced by <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>'s inaugural lecture at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a>, which reinforced his own attachment to the cause of British <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>. Among his Oxford associates were <a href="/wiki/James_Rochfort_Maguire" title="James Rochfort Maguire">James Rochfort Maguire</a>, later a fellow of <a href="/wiki/All_Souls_College,_Oxford" title="All Souls College, Oxford">All Souls College</a> and a director of the <a href="/wiki/British_South_Africa_Company" title="British South Africa Company">British South Africa Company</a>, and Charles Metcalfe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Due to his university career, Rhodes admired the Oxford "system". Eventually he was inspired to develop his scholarship scheme: "Wherever you turn your eye—except in science—an Oxford man is at the top of the tree".</p>
<p>While attending Oriel College, Rhodes became a <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemason</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_University_Lodge" title="Apollo University Lodge">Apollo University Lodge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApollo_University_Lodge_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApollo_University_Lodge-17">[16]</a></sup> Although initially he did not approve of the organisation, he continued to be a Freemason until his death in 1902. The shortcomings of the Freemasons, in his opinion, later caused him to envisage his own secret society with the goal of bringing the entire world under British rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas1997_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas1997-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Carroll_Quigley" title="Carroll Quigley">Carroll Quigley</a>, he set up the <a href="/wiki/Round_Table_movement" title="Round Table movement">Round Table movement</a> to this end.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diamonds_and_the_establishment_of_De_Beers">Diamonds and the establishment of De Beers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diamonds and the establishment of De Beers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1501" data-file-width="1321" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg/220px-Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg/330px-Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg/440px-Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cecil_Rhodes_by_Violet_manners.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sketch of Rhodes by <a href="/wiki/Violet_Manners,_Duchess_of_Rutland" title="Violet Manners, Duchess of Rutland">Violet Manners</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During his years at Oxford, Rhodes continued to prosper in Kimberley. Before his departure for Oxford, he and C.D. Rudd had moved from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Hole" title="Big Hole">Kimberley Mine</a> to invest in the more costly claims of what was known as old De Beers (<i>Vooruitzicht</i>). It was named after Johannes Nicolaas de Beer and his brother, Diederik Arnoldus, who occupied the farm. After purchasing the land in 1839 from David Danser, a <i><a href="/wiki/Griqua_people" title="Griqua people">Koranna</a></i> chief in the area, David Stephanus Fourie, Claudine Fourie-Grosvenor's forebearer, had allowed the de Beers and various other Afrikaner families to cultivate the land. The region extended from the <a href="/wiki/Modder_River" title="Modder River">Modder River</a> via the Vet River up to the <a href="/wiki/Vaal_River" title="Vaal River">Vaal River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERosenthal1965_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenthal1965-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1874 and 1875, the diamond fields were in the grip of depression, but Rhodes and Rudd were among those who stayed to consolidate their interests. They believed that diamonds would be numerous in the hard <a href="/wiki/Kimberlite" title="Kimberlite">blue ground</a> that had been exposed after the softer, yellow layer near the surface had been worked out. During this time, the technical problem of clearing out the water that was flooding the mines became serious. Rhodes and Rudd obtained the contract for pumping water out of the three main mines. After Rhodes returned from his first term at Oxford he lived with Robert Dundas Graham, who later became a mining partner with Rudd and Rhodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERotberg198876-_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERotberg198876--19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 March 1888, Rhodes and Rudd launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Beers_Consolidated_Mines" title="De Beers Consolidated Mines">De Beers Consolidated Mines</a> after the amalgamation of a number of individual claims. With £200,000 of capital, the company, of which Rhodes was secretary, owned the largest interest in the mine (£200,000 in 1880 = £12.9m in 2004 = $22.5m USD<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>). Rhodes was named the chairman of <a href="/wiki/De_Beers" title="De Beers">De Beers</a> at the company's founding in 1888. De Beers was established with funding from N M Rothschild &amp; Sons Limited in 1887.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[b]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_in_South_Africa">Politics in South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Politics in South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In 1880, Rhodes prepared to enter public life at the Cape. With the earlier incorporation of <a href="/wiki/Griqualand_West" title="Griqualand West">Griqualand West</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Cape_Colony" title="British Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> under the <a href="/wiki/John_Charles_Molteno" title="John Charles Molteno">Molteno Ministry</a> in 1877, the area had obtained six seats in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Parliament of the Cape of Good Hope">Cape House of Assembly</a>. Rhodes chose the rural and predominately <a href="/wiki/Boer" title="Boer">Boer</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> constituency of <a href="/wiki/Barkly_West" title="Barkly West">Barkly West</a>, which would remain faithful to Rhodes until his death.</p>
<p>When Rhodes became a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Parliament" title="Cape Parliament">Cape Parliament</a>, the chief goal of the assembly was to help decide the future of <a href="/wiki/Basutoland" title="Basutoland">Basutoland</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The ministry of <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Sprigg" title="Gordon Sprigg">Sir Gordon Sprigg</a> was trying to restore order after the 1880 rebellion known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun_War" title="Gun War">Gun War</a>. The Sprigg ministry had precipitated the revolt by applying its policy of disarming all native Africans to those of the Basotho nation.</p>
<p>In 1890, Rhodes became Prime Minister of the Cape Colony. He introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glen_Grey_Act" title="Glen Grey Act">Glen Grey Act</a> to push black people from their lands and make way for industrial development. Rhodes's view was that black people needed to be driven off their land to "stimulate them to labour" and to change their habits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[21]</a></sup> "It must be brought home to them", Rhodes said, "that in future nine-tenths of them will have to spend their lives in manual labour, and the sooner that is brought home to them the better."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The growing number of enfranchised black people in the Cape led him to raise the franchise requirements in 1892 to counter this preponderance, with drastic effects on the traditional <a href="/wiki/Cape_Qualified_Franchise" title="Cape Qualified Franchise">Cape Qualified Franchise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup> By simultaneously limiting the amount of land black Africans were legally allowed to hold while tripling the property qualifications required to vote, Rhodes succeeded in disenfranchising the black population, as, to quote <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dowden" title="Richard Dowden">Richard Dowden</a>, most would now "find it almost impossible to get back on the list because of the legal limit on the amount of land they could hold".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> In addition, Rhodes was an early architect of the <a href="/wiki/Natives_Land_Act,_1913" title="Natives Land Act, 1913">Natives Land Act, 1913</a>, which would limit the areas of the country that black Africans were allowed to less than 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coloniallegacymustfallnothisstatue_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-coloniallegacymustfallnothisstatue-26">[24]</a></sup> At the time, Rhodes would argue that "the native is to be treated as a child and denied the franchise. We must adopt a system of despotism, such as works in India, in our relations with the barbarism of South Africa."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996108_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996108-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rhodes also introduced educational reform to the area. His policies were instrumental in the development of <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British imperial</a> policies in South Africa, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hut_tax" title="Hut tax">Hut tax</a>.</p>
<p>Rhodes did not, however, have direct political power over the independent Boer Republic of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transvaal_Republic" title="Transvaal Republic">Transvaal</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He often disagreed with the Transvaal government's policies, which he considered unsupportive of mine-owners' interests. In 1895, believing he could use his influence to overthrow the Boer government,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Rhodes supported the infamous <a href="/wiki/Jameson_Raid" title="Jameson Raid">Jameson Raid</a>, an attack on the Transvaal with the tacit approval of Secretary of State for the Colonies <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">Joseph Chamberlain</a>. The raid was a catastrophic failure. It forced Cecil Rhodes to resign as Prime Minister of the Cape Colony, sent his oldest brother Col. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_William_Rhodes" title="Francis William Rhodes">Frank Rhodes</a> to jail in Transvaal convicted of <a href="/wiki/High_treason" title="High treason">high treason</a> and nearly sentenced to death, and contributed to the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expanding_the_British_Empire">Expanding the British Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Expanding the British Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhodes_and_the_imperial_factor">Rhodes and the imperial factor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rhodes and the imperial factor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2681" data-file-width="2069" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png/170px-Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png/255px-Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png/340px-Punch_Rhodes_Colossus.png 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>Rhodes used his wealth and that of his business partner <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Beit" title="Alfred Beit">Alfred Beit</a> and other investors to pursue his dream of creating a <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> in new territories to the north by obtaining <a href="/wiki/Mineral_rights" title="Mineral rights">mineral concessions</a> from the most powerful <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_Africa" title="Indigenous peoples of Africa">indigenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">chiefs</a>. Rhodes' competitive advantage over other mineral prospecting companies was his combination of wealth and astute political instincts, also called the 'imperial factor', as he often collaborated with the British Government. He befriended its local representatives, the British <a href="/wiki/Commissioner" title="Commissioner">Commissioners</a>, and through them organised British <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorates</a> over the mineral concession areas via separate but related treaties. In this way he obtained both legality and security for mining operations. He could then attract more investors. Imperial expansion and capital investment went hand in hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The imperial factor was a double-edged sword: Rhodes did not want the <a href="/wiki/Bureaucrat" title="Bureaucrat">bureaucrats</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Office" title="Colonial Office">Colonial Office</a> in London to interfere in the Empire in Africa. He wanted British settlers and local politicians and governors to run it. This put him on a collision course with many in Britain, as well as with British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missionaries" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a>, who favoured what they saw as the more ethical direct rule from London. Rhodes prevailed because he would pay the cost of administering the territories to the north of South Africa against his future mining profits. The Colonial Office did not have enough funding for this. Rhodes promoted his business interests as in the strategic interest of Britain: preventing the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germans</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boers" title="Boers">Boers</a> from moving into south-central Africa. Rhodes' companies and agents cemented these advantages by obtaining many mining concessions, as exemplified by the Rudd and Lochner Concessions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaties.2C_concessions_and_charters">Treaties, concessions and charters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Treaties, concessions and charters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodes had already tried and failed to get a mining concession from <i><a href="/wiki/Lobengula" title="Lobengula">Lobengula</a></i>, king of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ndebele_people" title="Northern Ndebele people">Ndebele</a> of <a href="/wiki/Matabeleland" title="Matabeleland">Matabeleland</a>. In 1888 he tried again. He sent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Smith_Moffat" title="John Smith Moffat">John Moffat</a>, son of the missionary <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moffat_(missionary)" title="Robert Moffat (missionary)">Robert Moffat</a>, who was trusted by Lobengula, to persuade the latter to sign a treaty of friendship with Britain, and to look favourably on Rhodes' proposals. His associate Charles Rudd, together with Francis Thompson and Rochfort Maguire, assured Lobengula that no more than ten white men would mine in Matabeleland. This limitation was left out of the document, known as the <a href="/wiki/Rudd_Concession" title="Rudd Concession">Rudd Concession</a>, which Lobengula signed. Furthermore, it stated that the mining companies could do anything necessary to their operations. When Lobengula discovered later the true effects of the concession, he tried to renounce it, but the British Government ignored him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Company's early days, Rhodes and his associates set themselves up to make millions (hundreds of millions in current pounds) over the coming years through what has been described as a "<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suppressio_veri" title="wiktionary:suppressio veri">suppressio veri</a></i> ... which must be regarded as one of Rhodes's least creditable actions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blake55_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-blake55-29">[27]</a></sup> Contrary to what the British government and the public had been allowed to think, the Rudd Concession was not vested in the British South Africa Company, but in a short-lived ancillary concern of Rhodes, Rudd and a few others called the <i>Central Search Association</i>, which was quietly formed in London in 1889. This entity renamed itself the <i>United Concessions Company</i> in 1890, and soon after sold the Rudd Concession to the Chartered Company for 1,000,000 shares. When Colonial Office functionaries discovered this chicanery in 1891, they advised <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Colonies" title="Secretary of State for the Colonies">Secretary of State for the Colonies</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Holland,_1st_Viscount_Knutsford" title="Henry Holland, 1st Viscount Knutsford">Knutsford</a> to consider revoking the concession, but no action was taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blake55_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-blake55-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Armed with the Rudd Concession, in 1889 Rhodes obtained a <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> from the British Government for his <a href="/wiki/British_South_Africa_Company" title="British South Africa Company">British South Africa Company</a> (BSAC) to rule, police, and make new treaties and concessions from the <a href="/wiki/Limpopo_River" title="Limpopo River">Limpopo River</a> to the great lakes of Central Africa. He obtained further concessions and treaties north of the <a href="/wiki/Zambezi" title="Zambezi">Zambezi</a>, such as those in <a href="/wiki/Barotseland" title="Barotseland">Barotseland</a> (the Lochner Concession with King <i><a href="/wiki/Lewanika" title="Lewanika">Lewanika</a></i> in 1890, which was similar to the Rudd Concession); and in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mweru" title="Lake Mweru">Lake Mweru</a> area (<a href="/wiki/Alfred_Sharpe" title="Alfred Sharpe">Alfred Sharpe</a>'s 1890 <a href="/wiki/Kazembe" title="Kazembe">Kazembe</a> concession). Rhodes also sent Sharpe to get a concession over mineral-rich <a href="/wiki/Katanga_Province" title="Katanga Province">Katanga</a>, but met his match in ruthlessness: when Sharpe was rebuffed by its ruler <i><a href="/wiki/Msiri" title="Msiri">Msiri</a></i>, King <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II_of_Belgium" title="Leopold II of Belgium">Leopold II of Belgium</a> obtained a concession over Msiri's dead body for his <a href="/wiki/Congo_Free_State" title="Congo Free State">Congo Free State</a>.</p>
<p>Rhodes also wanted <a href="/wiki/Bechuanaland_Protectorate" title="Bechuanaland Protectorate">Bechuanaland Protectorate</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>) incorporated in the BSAC charter. But three <a href="/wiki/Tswana_people" title="Tswana people">Tswana</a> kings, including <a href="/wiki/Khama_III" title="Khama III">Khama III</a>, travelled to Britain and won over British public opinion for it to remain governed by the British Colonial Office in London. Rhodes commented: "It is humiliating to be utterly beaten by these niggers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParsons1993179.E2.80.93181-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Office" title="Colonial Office">Colonial Office</a> also decided to administer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Central_Africa" title="British Central Africa">British Central Africa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Nyasaland, today's Malawi</a>) owing to<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the activism of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scots</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missionaries" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> trying to end the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> Rhodes paid much of the cost so that the British Central Africa Commissioner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Harry_Johnston" title="Sir Harry Johnston">Sir Harry Johnston</a>, and his successor Alfred Sharpe, would assist with security for Rhodes in the BSAC's north-eastern territories. Johnston shared Rhodes' expansionist views, but he and his successors were not as pro-settler as Rhodes, and disagreed on dealings with Africans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rhodesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The BSAC had its own police force, the <a href="/wiki/British_South_Africa_Police" title="British South Africa Police">British South Africa Police</a>, which was used to control <a href="/wiki/Matabeleland" title="Matabeleland">Matabeleland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mashonaland" title="Mashonaland">Mashonaland</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The company had hoped to start a "new <a href="/wiki/Witwatersrand" title="Witwatersrand">Rand</a>" from the ancient gold mines of the <i><a href="/wiki/Shona_people" title="Shona people">Shona</a></i>. Because the gold deposits were on a much smaller scale, many of the white settlers who accompanied the BSAC to Mashonaland became farmers rather than miners. When the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ndebele_people" title="Northern Ndebele people">Ndebele</a> and the Shona—the two main, but rival, peoples—separately rebelled against the coming of the European settlers, the BSAC defeated them in the <a href="/wiki/First_Matabele_War" title="First Matabele War">First Matabele War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Matabele_War" title="Second Matabele War">Second Matabele War</a>. Shortly after learning of the assassination of the Ndebele spiritual leader, <i>Mlimo</i>, by the American scout <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Russell_Burnham" title="Frederick Russell Burnham">Frederick Russell Burnham</a>, Rhodes walked unarmed into the Ndebele stronghold in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matobo_Hills" title="Matobo Hills">Matobo Hills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanton2015321_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanton2015321-30">[28]</a></sup> He persuaded the <i><a href="/wiki/Impi" title="Impi">Impi</a></i> to lay down their arms, thus ending the Second Matabele War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarwell2001539.E2.80.93_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarwell2001539.E2.80.93-31">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of 1894, the territories over which the BSAC had concessions or treaties, collectively called "Zambesia" after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zambezi_River" title="Zambezi River">Zambezi River</a> flowing through the middle, comprised an area of 1,143,000 km² between the <a href="/wiki/Limpopo_River" title="Limpopo River">Limpopo River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>. In May 1895, its name was officially changed to "Rhodesia", reflecting Rhodes' popularity among settlers who had been using the name informally since 1891. The designation <a href="/wiki/Southern_Rhodesia" title="Southern Rhodesia">Southern Rhodesia</a> was officially adopted in 1898 for the part south of the Zambezi, which later became Zimbabwe; and the designations <a href="/wiki/North-Western_Rhodesia" title="North-Western Rhodesia">North-Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/North-Eastern_Rhodesia" title="North-Eastern Rhodesia">North-Eastern Rhodesia</a> were used from 1895 for the territory which later became <a href="/wiki/Northern_Rhodesia" title="Northern Rhodesia">Northern Rhodesia</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGray1956_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGray1956-32">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGray1954_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGray1954-33">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rhodes decreed in his will that he was to be buried in Matobo Hills. After his death in the Cape in 1902, his body was transported by train to <a href="/wiki/Bulawayo" title="Bulawayo">Bulawayo</a>. His burial was attended by Ndebele chiefs, who asked that the firing party should not discharge their rifles as this would disturb the spirits. Then, for the first time, they gave a white man the Matabele royal salute, <i>Bayete</i>. Rhodes is buried alongside <a href="/wiki/Leander_Starr_Jameson" title="Leander Starr Jameson">Leander Starr Jameson</a> and 34 British soldiers killed in the <a href="/wiki/Shangani_Patrol" title="Shangani Patrol">Shangani Patrol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDomville-Fife190089_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDomville-Fife190089-34">[32]</a></sup> Despite occasional efforts to return his body to the United Kingdom, his grave remains there still, "part and parcel of the history of Zimbabwe" and attracts thousands of visitors each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaing22_February_2012_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaing22_February_2012-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Cape_to_Cairo_Red_Line.22">"Cape to Cairo Red Line"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title='Edit section: "Cape to Cairo Red Line"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Railway" title="Cape to Cairo Railway">Cape to Cairo Railway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Road" title="Cape to Cairo Road">Cape to Cairo Road</a></div>
<p>One of Rhodes' dreams (and the dream of many other members of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>) was for a "red line" on the map from the Cape to Cairo (on geo-political maps, British dominions were always denoted in red or pink). Rhodes had been instrumental in securing southern African states for the Empire. He and others felt the best way to "unify the possessions, facilitate governance, enable the military to move quickly to hot spots or conduct war, help settlement, and foster trade" would be to build the "<a href="/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Railway" title="Cape to Cairo Railway">Cape to Cairo Railway</a>".</p>
<p>This enterprise was not without its problems. France had a rival strategy in the late 1890s to link its colonies from west to east across the continent and the Portuguese produced the "<a href="/wiki/Pink_Map" title="Pink Map">Pink Map</a>", representing their claims to sovereignty in Africa. Ultimately, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a> proved to be the main obstacles to the British dream until the United Kingdom seized <a href="/wiki/Tanganyika" title="Tanganyika">Tanganyika</a> from the Germans as a <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate" title="League of Nations mandate">League of Nations mandate</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_views">Political views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CecilRhodes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="752" data-file-width="564" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/CecilRhodes.jpg/170px-CecilRhodes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/CecilRhodes.jpg/255px-CecilRhodes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/CecilRhodes.jpg/340px-CecilRhodes.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CecilRhodes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cecil Rhodes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rhodes wanted to expand the British Empire because he believed that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a> was destined to greatness. In his last will and testament, Rhodes said of the British, "I contend that we are the first race in the world, and that the more of the world we inhabit the better it is for the human race. I contend that every acre added to our territory means the birth of more of the English race who otherwise would not be brought into existence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes190258_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes190258-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rhodes wanted to make the British Empire a superpower in which all of the British-dominated countries in the empire, including Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and Cape Colony, would be <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Federation" title="Imperial Federation">represented in the British Parliament</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERotberg1988150_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERotberg1988150-36">[34]</a></sup> Rhodes included American students as eligible for the Rhodes scholarships. He said that he wanted to breed an American elite of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosopher-kings" title="Philosopher-kings">philosopher-kings</a> who would have the United States rejoin the British Empire. As Rhodes also respected and admired the Germans and their <a href="/wiki/Kaiser" title="Kaiser">Kaiser</a>, he allowed German students to be included in the Rhodes scholarships. He believed that eventually the United Kingdom (including Ireland), the US, and Germany together would dominate the world and ensure perpetual peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFlint2009_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlint2009-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rhodes's views on race have been debated. Critics on the left have labelled him as an "architect of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> and a "<a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">white supremacist</a>", particularly since 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coloniallegacymustfallnothisstatue_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-coloniallegacymustfallnothisstatue-26">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilsonrhodesmustfall_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilsonrhodesmustfall-38">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhodestogandhi_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhodestogandhi-39">[37]</a></sup> According to Magubane, Rhodes was "unhappy that in many Cape Constituencies, Africans could be decisive if more of them exercised this right to vote under current law [referring to the Cape Qualified Franchise]," with Rhodes arguing that "the native is to be treated as a child and denied the franchise. We must adopt a system of despotism, such as works in India, in our relations with the barbarism of South Africa".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996108_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996108-27">[25]</a></sup> Rhodes advocated the governance of indigenous Africans living in the Cape Colony "in a state of barbarism and communal tenure" as "a subject race. I do not go so far as the member for Victoria West, who would not give the black man a vote. ... If the whites maintain their position as the supreme race, the day may come when we shall be thankful that we have the natives with us in their proper position."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996109_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMagubane1996109-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>However historian Raymond C. Mensing, notes that Rhodes has the reputation as the most flamboyant exemplar of the British imperial spirit, and always believed that British institutions were the best. Mensing argues that Rhodes quietly developed a more nuanced concept of imperial federation in Africa and that his mature views were more balanced and realistic. Rhodes was not a biological or maximal racist. Despite his support for what became the basis for the apartheid system, he is best seen as a cultural or minimal racist, according to Mensing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On domestic politics within Britain Rhodes was a supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup> Rhodes' only major impact was his large-scale support of the Irish nationalist party, led by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Charles Stewart Parnell</a> (1846–1891).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rhodes worked well with the Afrikaners in the Cape Colony. He supported teaching Dutch as well as English in public schools. While Prime Minister of the Cape Colony, he helped to remove most of their legal disabilities. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFlint2009_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlint2009-11">[10]</a></sup> He was a friend of <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hendrik_Hofmeyr_(Onze_Jan)" title="Jan Hendrik Hofmeyr (Onze Jan)">Jan Hofmeyr</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Afrikaner_Bond" title="Afrikaner Bond">Afrikaner Bond</a>, and it was largely because of Afrikaner support that he became Prime Minister of the Cape Colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup> Rhodes advocated greater self-government for the Cape Colony, in line with his preference for the empire to be controlled by local settlers and politicians rather than by London</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_relationships">Personal relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Personal relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodes never married, pleading, "I have too much work on my hands" and saying that he would not be a dutiful husband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlomer1984_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlomer1984-44">[42]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some writers and academics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAldrich_and_Wotherspoon2001370-371_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAldrich_and_Wotherspoon2001370-371-45">[43]</a></sup> have suggested that Rhodes may have been homosexual. The scholar Richard Brown observed: "On the issue of Rhodes' sexuality... there is, once again, simply not enough reliable evidence to reach firm, irrefutable conclusions. It is inferred, but not proven, that Rhodes was homosexual and it is assumed (but not proven) that his relationships with men were sometimes physical. Neville Pickering is described as Rhodes' lover in spite of the absence of decisive evidence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown1990_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown1990-46">[44]</a></sup> Rhodes was close to Pickering; he returned from negotiations for Pickering's 25th birthday in 1882. On that occasion, Rhodes drew up a new will leaving his estate to Pickering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlomer1984_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlomer1984-44">[42]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Two years later, Pickering suffered a riding accident. Rhodes nursed him faithfully for six weeks, refusing even to answer telegrams concerning his business interests. Pickering died in Rhodes's arms, and at his funeral, Rhodes was said to have wept with fervour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAldrich_and_Wotherspoon2001370-371_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAldrich_and_Wotherspoon2001370-371-45">[43]</a></sup> Brown comments: "there is still the simpler but major problem of the extraordinarily thin evidence on which the conclusions about Rhodes are reached. Rhodes himself left few details... Indeed, Rhodes is a singularly difficult subject... since there exists little intimate material – no diaries and few personal letters."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown1990_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown1990-46">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>Pickering's successor was <a href="/wiki/Henry_Latham_Currey" title="Henry Latham Currey">Henry Latham Currey</a>, the son of an old friend, who had become Rhodes's private secretary in 1884.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECurrey_and_Simons198626_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurrey_and_Simons198626-47">[45]</a></sup> When Currey became engaged in 1894, Rhodes was deeply mortified and their relationship ended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rhodes also remained close to <a href="/wiki/Leander_Starr_Jameson" title="Leander Starr Jameson">Leander Starr Jameson</a> after the two had met in Kimberley, where they shared a bungalow. Jameson nursed Rhodes during his final illness, was a trustee of his estate and residuary beneficiary of his will, which allowed him to continue living in Rhodes' mansion after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMassie1991218.2C_230_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMassie1991218.2C_230-49">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Princess_Radziwi.C5.82.C5.82">Princess Radziwiłł</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Princess Radziwiłł">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the last years of his life, Rhodes was <a href="/wiki/Stalking" title="Stalking">stalked</a> by Polish princess <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Radziwill" title="Catherine Radziwill">Catherine Radziwiłł</a>, born <a href="/wiki/Rzewuski_family" title="Rzewuski family">Rzewuska</a>, married into a <a href="/wiki/Szlachta" title="Szlachta">noble</a> Polish family called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radziwi%C5%82%C5%82" title="Radziwiłł">Radziwiłł</a>. Radziwiłł falsely claimed that she was engaged to Rhodes, or that they were having an affair. She asked him to marry her, but Rhodes refused. In reaction, she accused him of loan fraud. He had to go to trial and testify against her accusation. She wrote a biography of Rhodes called <i>Cecil Rhodes: Man and Empire Maker</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERadziwill1918_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERadziwill1918-50">[48]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Her accusations were eventually proven to be false.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Boer_War">Second Boer War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Second Boer War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kimberley" title="Siege of Kimberley">Siege of Kimberley</a></div>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a> Rhodes went to <a href="/wiki/Kimberley,_Northern_Cape" title="Kimberley, Northern Cape">Kimberley</a> at the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kimberley" title="Siege of Kimberley">siege</a>, in a calculated move to raise the political stakes on the government to dedicate resources to the defence of the city. The military felt he was more of a liability than an asset and found him intolerable. The officer commanding the garrison of Kimberley, Lieutenant Colonel <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kekewich" title="Robert Kekewich">Robert Kekewich</a>, experienced serious personal difficulties with Rhodes because of the latters' inability to co-operate;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPhelan1913_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPhelan1913-53">[51]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Despite these differences, Rhodes' company was instrumental in the defence of the city, providing water, refrigeration facilities, constructing fortifications, manufacturing an <a href="/wiki/Armoured_train" title="Armoured train">armoured train</a>, shells and a one-off gun named <a href="/wiki/Long_Cecil" title="Long Cecil">Long Cecil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts1976_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts1976-54">[52]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rhodes used his position and influence to lobby the British government to relieve the siege of Kimberley, claiming in the press that the situation in the city was desperate. The military wanted to assemble a large force to take the Boer cities of <a href="/wiki/Bloemfontein" title="Bloemfontein">Bloemfontein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pretoria" title="Pretoria">Pretoria</a>, but they were compelled to change their plans and send three separate smaller forces to relieve the sieges of Kimberley, <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Mafeking" title="Siege of Mafeking">Mafeking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ladysmith" title="Siege of Ladysmith">Ladysmith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson2007131.E2.80.93_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson2007131.E2.80.93-55">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cecil_Rhodes_funeral.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Although Rhodes remained a leading figure in the politics of southern Africa, especially during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a>, he was dogged by ill health throughout his relatively short life. He was sent to Natal aged 16 because it was believed the climate might help problems with his heart. On returning to England in 1872 his health again deteriorated with heart and lung problems, to the extent that his doctor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Morell_Mackenzie" title="Sir Morell Mackenzie">Sir Morell Mackenzie</a>, believed he would only survive six months. He returned to Kimberley where his health improved. From age 40 his heart condition returned with increasing severity until his death from heart failure in 1902, aged 48, at his seaside cottage in Muizenberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Times27_March_1902_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Times27_March_1902-1">[1]</a></sup> The Government arranged an epic journey by train from the Cape to Rhodesia, with the funeral train stopping at every station to allow mourners to pay their respects. He was finally laid to rest at World's View, a hilltop located approximately 35 kilometres (22 mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Bulawayo" title="Bulawayo">Bulawayo</a>, in what was then <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a>. Today, his grave site is part of <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo National Park</a>, Zimbabwe.</p>
<p>The continued presence of Rhodes' grave in the Matopos hills has not been without controversy in contemporary Zimbabwe. In December 2010 <a href="/wiki/Cain_Mathema" title="Cain Mathema">Cain Mathema</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bulawayo" title="Bulawayo">governor of Bulawayo</a>, branded Rhodes' grave outside the country's second city of Bulawayo an "insult to the African ancestors" and said he believed its presence had brought bad luck and poor weather to the region. The grave site is considered an important national and historic monument on protected land which attracts many tourist visitors every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGore22_February_2013_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGore22_February_2013-56">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2012, Mugabe loyalists and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZANU-PF" title="ZANU-PF">ZANU-PF</a> activists visited the grave site demanding permission from the local chief to exhume Rhodes' remains and return them to Britain. This was considered a nationalist political stunt in the run up to an election, rather than representing any genuine national desire to remove the grave. Local Chief Masuku and Godfrey Mahachi, one of the country's foremost archaeologists, strongly expressed their opposition to the grave being removed due to its historical significance to Zimbabwe. President Robert Mugabe has also opposed the move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaing22_February_2012_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaing22_February_2012-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, he was voted 56th in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SABC3" title="SABC3">SABC3</a> television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_South_Africans_(television_series)" title="Great South Africans (television series)">Great South Africans</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlair19_Oct_2004_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlair19_Oct_2004-57">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>At his death he was considered one of the wealthiest men in the world. In his first will, written in 1877 before he had accumulated his wealth, Rhodes wanted to create a <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a> that would bring the whole world under British rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas1997_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas1997-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The exact wording from this will is:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>To and for the establishment, promotion and development of a Secret Society, the true aim and object whereof shall be for the extension of British rule throughout the world, the perfecting of a system of emigration from the United Kingdom, and of colonisation by British subjects of all lands where the means of livelihood are attainable by energy, labour and enterprise, and especially the occupation by British settlers of the entire Continent of Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a>, the Valley of the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a>, the Islands of Cyprus and <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Candia</a>, the whole of South America, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Islands of the Pacific</a> not heretofore possessed by Great Britain, the whole of the Malay Archipelago, the seaboard of China and Japan, the ultimate recovery of the United States of America as an integral part of the British Empire, the inauguration of a system of Colonial representation in the Imperial Parliament which may tend to weld together the disjointed members of the Empire and, finally, the foundation of so great a Power as to render wars impossible, and promote the best interests of humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERotberg1988101-102_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERotberg1988101-102-58">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson1999_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson1999-59">[57]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rhodes' final will<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes1902_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes1902-60">[58]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> left a large area of land on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a> to the South African nation. Part of this estate became the upper campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cape_Town" title="University of Cape Town">University of Cape Town</a>, another part became the <a href="/wiki/Kirstenbosch_National_Botanical_Garden" title="Kirstenbosch National Botanical Garden">Kirstenbosch National Botanical Garden</a>, while much was spared from development and is now an important conservation area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhodes_Scholarship">Rhodes Scholarship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Rhodes Scholarship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In his last will and testament, he provided for the establishment of the famous <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship" title="Rhodes Scholarship">Rhodes Scholarship</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes190223-45_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes190223-45-61">[59]</a></sup> the world's first international study programme. The scholarship enabled students from <a href="/wiki/Territorial_evolution_of_the_British_Empire" title="Territorial evolution of the British Empire">territories under British rule or formerly under British rule</a> and from <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a> to study at Rhodes' alma mater, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhodes190223-45_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhodes190223-45-61">[59]</a></sup> Rhodes' aims were to promote leadership marked by public spirit and good character, and to "render war impossible" by promoting friendship between the great powers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Memorial" title="Rhodes Memorial">Rhodes Memorial</a> stands on Rhodes' favourite spot on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Peak_(Cape_Town)" title="Devil's Peak (Cape Town)">Devil's Peak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, with a view looking north and east towards the <a href="/wiki/Great_North_Road,_Zambia" title="Great North Road, Zambia">Cape to Cairo</a> route. From 1910 to 1984 Rhodes' house in Cape Town, <a href="/wiki/Groote_Schuur" title="Groote Schuur">Groote Schuur</a>, was the official Cape residence of the Prime Ministers of South Africa and continued as a presidential residence of P. W. Botha and F. W. De Klerk.</p>
<p>His birthplace was established in 1938 as the Rhodes Memorial Museum, now known as <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Arts_Complex" title="Rhodes Arts Complex">Bishops Stortford Museum</a>. The cottage in <a href="/wiki/Muizenberg" title="Muizenberg">Muizenberg</a> where he died is a provincial heritage site in the Western Cape Province of South Africa. The cottage today is operated as a museum by the Muizenberg Historical Conservation Society, and is open to the public. A broad display of Rhodes material can be seen, including the original De Beers board room table around which diamonds worth billions of dollars were traded.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rhodes University College, now <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_University" title="Rhodes University">Rhodes University</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Grahamstown" title="Grahamstown">Grahamstown</a>, was established in his name by his trustees and founded by Act of Parliament on 31 May 1904.</p>
<p>The residents of Kimberley elected to build a memorial in Rhodes' honour in their city, which was unveiled in 1907. The 72-ton bronze statue depicts Rhodes on his horse, looking north with map in hand, and dressed as he was when met the Ndebele after their <a href="/wiki/Second_Matabele_War" title="Second Matabele War">rebellion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaylam200556_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaylam200556-63">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Must_Fall" title="Rhodes Must Fall">Rhodes Must Fall</a></div>
<p>Memorials to Rhodes have been opposed since at least the 1950s, when some <a href="/wiki/Afrikaners" title="Afrikaners">Afrikaner</a> students demanded the removal of a Rhodes statue at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cape_Town" title="University of Cape Town">University of Cape Town</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[62]</a></sup> A 2015 movement, known as "Rhodes Must Fall" (or #RhodesMustFall on social media) began with student protests at the University of Cape Town that were successful in getting university authorities to remove the Rhodes statue from the campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhodesmoved_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rhodesmoved-65">[63]</a></sup> The protest also had the broader goal of highlighting what the activists considered the lack of systemic post-apartheid racial transformation in South African institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhodesfall_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rhodesfall-66">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following a series of discords and <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a> at the University of Cape Town, various allied movements both in South Africa and other countries have been launched in opposition to the very existence of Cecil Rhodes memorials. These include a campaign to change the name of <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_University" title="Rhodes University">Rhodes University</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RUnamechange_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-RUnamechange-67">[65]</a></sup> and to remove a statue of Rhodes from <a href="/wiki/Oriel_College,_Oxford" title="Oriel College, Oxford">Oriel College, Oxford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>An official statement by the University of Cape Town stated that "the behaviour by RMF members is criminal and has exceeded all possible limits of lawful protest action. We are deeply concerned for the safety of students and staff." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Rhodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Rhodes is thought to have served as the primary inspiration and model for the character of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Rolles_Weston" title="Edward Rolles Weston">Edward Rolles Weston</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.S._Lewis" title="C.S. Lewis">C.S. Lewis</a> novels, <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_the_Silent_Planet" title="Out of the Silent Planet">Out of the Silent Planet</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Perelandra" title="Perelandra">Perelandra</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>'s sarcastic summation of Rhodes ("I admire him, I frankly confess it; and when his time comes I shall buy a piece of the rope for a keepsake"), from Chapter LXIX of <i><a href="/wiki/Following_the_Equator" title="Following the Equator">Following the Equator</a></i>, still often appears in collections of famous insults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETwain1898_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETwain1898-70">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[c]</a></sup></li>
<li>The will of Cecil Rhodes is the central theme in the science fiction book <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Work_of_Time" title="Great Work of Time">Great Work of Time</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Crowley" title="John Crowley">John Crowley</a>, an alternate history in which the Secret Society stipulated in the will was indeed established. Its members eventually achieve the secret of time travel and use it to restrain World War I and prevent World War II, and to perpetuate the world ascendancy of the British Empire up to the end of the Twentieth Century. The book contains a vivid description of Cecil Rhodes himself, seen through the eyes of a traveller from the future British Empire.</li>
<li>In the British film <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodes_of_Africa" title="Rhodes of Africa">Rhodes of Africa</a></i> (1936, directed by Austrian filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Berthold_Viertel" title="Berthold Viertel">Berthold Viertel</a>), Rhodes was portrayed by Canadian actor <a href="/wiki/Walter_Huston" title="Walter Huston">Walter Huston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1996, BBC-TV made an eight-part television drama about Rhodes called <i>Rhodes: The Life and Legend of Cecil Rhodes</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[70]</a></sup> It was produced by David Drury and written by Antony Thomas. It tells the story of Rhodes' life through a series of flashbacks of conversations between him and Princess Catherine Radziwiłł and also between her and people who knew him. It also shows the story of how she stalked and eventually ruined him. In the serial, Cecil Rhodes is played by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Shaw" title="Martin Shaw">Martin Shaw</a>, the younger Cecil Rhodes is played by his son Joe Shaw, and Princess Radziwiłł is played by <a href="/wiki/Frances_Barber" title="Frances Barber">Frances Barber</a>. In the serial Rhodes is portrayed as ruthless and greedy. The serial also suggests that he was homosexual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGodwin11_January_1998_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGodwin11_January_1998-74">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Wilbur_Smith" title="Wilbur Smith">Wilbur Smith</a> <a href="/wiki/Wilbur_Smith#The_Ballantyne_Series" title="Wilbur Smith">"Ballantyne"</a> series of novels feature Rhodes. These novels also strongly suggest that he was homosexual.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Rhodes was played by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marian_(actor)" title="Ferdinand Marian (actor)">Ferdinand Marian</a> in the Nazi 1941 propaganda movie <i><a href="/wiki/Ohm_Kr%C3%BCger" title="Ohm Krüger">Ohm Krüger</a></i>, where he - like all other British characters in the film - was presented as an outright villain.</li>
<li>In 1901, Rhodes bought <a href="/wiki/Dalham_Hall" title="Dalham Hall">Dalham Hall</a>, Suffolk. In 1902 Colonel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_William_Rhodes" title="Francis William Rhodes">Francis William Rhodes</a> erected the village hall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDalham_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDalham-75">[72]</a></sup> in the village of <a href="/wiki/Dalham" title="Dalham">Dalham</a>, to commemorate the life of his brother, who had died before taking possession of the estate.</li>
<li>Rhodes was a peripheral but influential character in the historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Covenant_(novel)" title="The Covenant (novel)">The Covenant</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Michener" title="James Michener">James Michener</a>.</li>
<li>His memorial at Devil's Peak also served as a temple in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Sinbad" title="The Adventures of Sinbad">The Adventures of Sinbad</a></i> episode "The Return of the Ronin".</li>
</ul>
