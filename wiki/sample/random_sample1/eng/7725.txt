<p><b>St Mary-le-Bow</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛər/ 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span> is an historic church rebuilt after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_1666" title="Great Fire of 1666">Great Fire of 1666</a> by Sir <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> on the main east–west thoroughfare, <a href="/wiki/Cheapside" title="Cheapside">Cheapside</a>. According to tradition a true <a href="/wiki/Cockney" title="Cockney">Cockney</a> must be born within earshot of the sound of Bow Bells (which refers to this church's bells rather than <a href="/wiki/Bow_Church" title="Bow Church">St Mary and Holy Trinity, Bow Road</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Bow,_London" title="Bow, London">Bow</a>, an outlying village until the 19th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bells">Bells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary-le-Bow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Weights in <a href="/wiki/Hundredweight" title="Hundredweight">hundredweights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quarter_(unit)#Weight" title="Quarter (unit)">quarters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The previous "great bell at Bow",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the tenor bell of the peal installed in 1762 and destroyed in an air raid of 1941, weighed 58 hundredweight, with six tons of ironwork braces cut into the inside walls of the tower as reinforcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Earlier still, the first great bell was a byword for having a sonorous tone as, in 1588, pamphleteer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Greene_(dramatist)" title="Robert Greene (dramatist)">Robert Greene</a> sarcastically likens the verse of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a> to the bell's "mouth-filling" resonance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ordinarily, distances by road from London are now measured from <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross" title="Charing Cross">Charing Cross</a> but, before the late 18th century, they were measured from the <a href="/wiki/London_Stone" title="London Stone">London Stone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cannon_Street" title="Cannon Street">Cannon Street</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Cornhill,_London#History" title="Cornhill, London">Standard</a> in Cornhill. However, on the road from London to <a href="/wiki/Lewes" title="Lewes">Lewes</a>, the mileage is taken from the church door of St Mary-le-Bow. To note the reference used, mileposts along the way are marked with a cast-iron depiction of a bow and four bells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Archaeological evidence indicates that a church existed on this site in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxon" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> times. A medieval version of the church had been destroyed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Tornado_of_1091" title="London Tornado of 1091">London Tornado of 1091</a>, one of the earliest recorded (and one of the most violent) <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> in Britain, although the newly completed arched crypt survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VCH_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-VCH-11">[11]</a></sup> During the later Norman period the church, known as “St Mary de Arcubus”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> was rebuilt and was famed for the arches (“bows”) of stone. At that period the 12 feet 6 inches (3.8 m) high vaulted crypt—although only accessible from within the church—had windows and buttresses visible from the street. However, the anecdotalist and historian <a href="/wiki/John_Stow" title="John Stow">John Stow</a> dates the titular arches, made of <a href="/wiki/Caen_stone" title="Caen stone">Caen stone</a> in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Crown_steeple" title="Crown steeple">crown steeple</a> and supporting a <a href="/wiki/Roof_lantern" title="Roof lantern">lantern</a>, to no earlier than 1515.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VCH_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-VCH-11">[11]</a></sup> This is the form of the steeple in the <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Agas" title="Ralph Agas">Agas</a> woodcut of 1561 (right). This erroneous explanation for the source of the name gained some traction in the centuries to follow, including an endorsement by <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Pugin" title="Augustus Pugin">Augustus Pugin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>From at least the 13th century, the church was a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Peculiar" title="Royal Peculiar">peculier</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Canterbury" title="Diocese of Canterbury">Diocese of Canterbury</a> and the seat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Arches" title="Court of Arches">Court of Arches</a>, to which it gave the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VCH_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-VCH-11">[11]</a></sup> The “bow bells”, which could be heard as far away as <a href="/wiki/Hackney_Marshes" title="Hackney Marshes">Hackney Marshes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> were once used to <a href="/wiki/Curfew_bell" title="Curfew bell">order a curfew</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VCH_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-VCH-11">[11]</a></sup> This building burned in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a> of 1666.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="679" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg/220px-Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg/330px-Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg/440px-Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cheapside_and_Bow_Church_engraved_by_W.Albutt_after_T.H.Shepherd_publ_1837_edited.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
St Mary-le-Bow in an 1837 engraving</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The church with its steeple had been a landmark of London. Considered the second most important church in the City of London after <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a>, St-Mary-le-Bow was one of the first churches to be rebuilt after the fire by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a> and his office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The current structure was built to the designs of Wren between 1671 and 1673; the 223-foot (68 m) steeple was completed in 1680. The mason-contractor was Thomas Cartwright,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> one of the leading London mason-contractors and carvers of his generation.</p>
<p>In 1914, a stone from the crypt of St Mary-le-Bow church was placed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinity_Church,_New_York" title="Trinity Church, New York">Trinity Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, in commemoration of the fact that King <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> granted the <a href="/wiki/Vestry" title="Vestry">vestry</a> of Trinity Church the same privileges as St Mary-le-Bow vestry, the forerunner of lower-tier local government. Since the early 1940s, a recording of the Bow Bells made in 1926 has been used by the <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a> as an interval signal for the English-language broadcasts. It is still used today preceding some English-language broadcasts.</p>
<p>Much of the current building was destroyed by a <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> bomb during <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a> on 10 May 1941,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> during which fire the bells crashed to the ground. Restoration under the direction of Laurence King<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> began in 1956 (with internal fittings by Faith-Craft, part of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Faith" title="Society of the Faith">Society of the Faith</a>). The bells as listed above, cast in 1956, were eventually installed to resume ringing in 1961. The church was formally reconsecrated in 1964, having achieved designation as a <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade I listed building</a> on 4 January 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHLE_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHLE-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the church is a memorial to members of the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> resistance who died in the Second World War, which is in two parts; a <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">commemorative plaque</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_George_and_the_Dragon" title="Saint George and the Dragon">Saint George and the Dragon</a>.</p>
<p>In the churchyard is a statue of Captain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Smith_of_Jamestown" title="John Smith of Jamestown">John Smith of Jamestown</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and former parishioner of the church.</p>
<p>Since 1989, there has been a restaurant in the crypt: Café Below. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Services_today">Services today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary-le-Bow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Services today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_elaborate_pendant_cross,_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3088" data-file-width="2056" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/The_elaborate_pendant_cross%2C_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg/170px-The_elaborate_pendant_cross%2C_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/The_elaborate_pendant_cross%2C_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg/255px-The_elaborate_pendant_cross%2C_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/The_elaborate_pendant_cross%2C_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg/340px-The_elaborate_pendant_cross%2C_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_elaborate_pendant_cross,_St_Mary_le_Bow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The pendant cross, St Mary le Bow</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>St Mary-le-Bow ministers to the <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial industry</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livery_Company" title="Livery Company">livery companies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Consequently, services feature weekday morning and evening led prayers lasting just a quarter of an hour generally at 08:15 (except Tuesdays) and 17:45.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> There is a memorial in the church to the first Governor in Australia, Admiral <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Phillip" title="Arthur Phillip">Arthur Phillip</a>, who was born in the parish. Through this connection the Rector of St Mary-le-Bow is the <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">Chaplain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Britain%E2%80%93Australia_Society" title="Britain–Australia Society">Britain–Australia Society</a>.</p>
<p>It is still home to the <a href="/wiki/Arches_Court" title="Arches Court">Court of Arches</a> today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organ">Organ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary-le-Bow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2,_London,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6922" data-file-width="7000" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_Mary-le-Bow_Church_Interior_2,_London,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The organ</div>

<p>The organ is a two-manual and pedal design by Kenneth Tickell and Company, with design and construction initiated in 2004. It occupies the case of the previous <a href="/wiki/Rushworth_and_Dreaper" title="Rushworth and Dreaper">Rushworth and Dreaper</a> organ (from the 1960s). The inaugural recital was given by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Trotter" title="Thomas Trotter">Thomas Trotter</a> in September 2010. The resident <a href="/wiki/Organist" title="Organist">organist</a> is <a href="/wiki/Alan_Wilson_(composer)" title="Alan Wilson (composer)">Alan Wilson</a>.</p>
