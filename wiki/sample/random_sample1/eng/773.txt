<p><b>ATPases</b> (<a href="/wiki/Enzyme_Commission_number" title="Enzyme Commission number">EC</a> <a class="external text" href="http://enzyme.expasy.org/EC/3.6.1.3" rel="nofollow">3.6.1.3</a>, <i>adenylpyrophosphatase</i>, <i>ATP monophosphatase</i>, <i>triphosphatase</i>, <i>SV40 T-antigen</i>, <i>adenosine 5'-triphosphatase</i>, <i>ATP hydrolase</i>, <i>complex V (mitochondrial electron transport)</i>, <i>(Ca<sup>2+</sup> + Mg<sup>2+</sup>)-ATPase</i>, <i>HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>-ATPase</i>, <i>adenosine triphosphatase</i>) are a class of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> that <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyze</a> the <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a> of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> into <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> and a free <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate ion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Dephosphorylation" title="Dephosphorylation">dephosphorylation</a> reaction releases <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, which the enzyme (in most cases) harnesses to drive other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> that would not otherwise occur. This process is widely used in all known forms of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>.</p>
<p>Some such enzymes are <a href="/wiki/Integral_membrane_protein" title="Integral membrane protein">integral membrane proteins</a> (anchored within <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">biological membranes</a>), and move <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutes</a> across the membrane, typically against their concentration gradient. These are called <i>transmembrane ATPases</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATPase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="285" data-file-width="635" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg/250px-Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg/375px-Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg/500px-Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_sodium-potassium_pump-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NaKATPase" title="NaKATPase">Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>ATPase</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Transmembrane ATPases import many of the metabolites necessary for <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and export toxins, wastes, and solutes that can hinder cellular processes. An important example is the sodium-potassium exchanger (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NaKATPase" title="NaKATPase">Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>ATPase</a>) that maintains the <a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">cell membrane potential</a>. And another example is the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_potassium_ATPase" title="Hydrogen potassium ATPase">hydrogen potassium ATPase</a> (H<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>ATPase or gastric proton pump) that acidifies the contents of the stomach.</p>
<p>Besides exchangers, other categories of transmembrane ATPase include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-transport" title="Co-transport">co-transporters</a> and pumps (however, some exchangers are also pumps). Some of these, like the Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>ATPase, cause a net flow of charge, but others do not. These are called "electrogenic" and "nonelectrogenic" transporters, respectively.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATPase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The coupling between ATP hydrolysis and transport is more or less a strict chemical reaction, in which a fixed number of solute molecules are transported for each ATP molecule that is hydrolyzed; for example, 3 Na<sup>+</sup> ions out of the cell and 2 K<sup>+</sup> ions inward per ATP hydrolyzed, for the Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> exchanger.</p>
<p>Transmembrane ATPases harness the chemical potential energy of ATP, because they perform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>: they transport solutes in a direction opposite to their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" title="Thermodynamic">thermodynamically</a> preferred direction of movement—that is, from the side of the membrane where they are in low concentration to the side where they are in high concentration. This process is considered <a href="/wiki/Active_transport" title="Active transport">active transport</a>.</p>
<p>For example, the blocking of the vesicular H<sup>+</sup>-ATPases would increase the pH inside vesicles and decrease the pH of the cytoplasm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmembrane_ATP_synthases">Transmembrane ATP synthases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATPase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transmembrane ATP synthases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthase</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthase</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Anabolism" title="Anabolism">anabolic</a> enzyme that harnesses the energy of a transmembrane <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> gradient as an energy source for adding an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic_phosphate" title="Inorganic phosphate">inorganic phosphate</a> group to a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">adenosine diphosphate</a> (ADP) to form a molecule of adenosine triphosphate (ATP).</p>
<p>This enzyme works when a proton moves down the concentration gradient, giving the enzyme a spinning motion. This unique spinning motion bonds ADP and P together to create ATP.</p>
<p>ATP synthase can also function in reverse, that is, use energy released by ATP hydrolysis to pump protons against their electrochemical gradient.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATPase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are different types of ATPases, which can differ in function (ATP synthesis and/or hydrolysis), structure (F-, V- and A-ATPases contain rotary motors) and in the type of ions they transport.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/F-ATPase" title="F-ATPase">F-ATPases</a> (F1FO-ATPases) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_membrane" title="Plasma membrane">plasma membranes</a> are the prime producers of ATP, using the proton gradient generated by <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a> (mitochondria) or <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> (chloroplasts).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/V-ATPase" title="V-ATPase">V-ATPases</a> (V1VO-ATPases) are primarily found in eukaryotic vacuoles, catalysing ATP hydrolysis to transport solutes and lower pH in organelles like <a href="/wiki/Proton_pump" title="Proton pump">proton pump</a> of lysosome.</li>
<li><b>A-ATPases</b> (A1AO-ATPases) are found in <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a> and function like F-ATPases</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-ATPase" title="P-ATPase">P-ATPases</a> (E1E2-ATPases) are found in bacteria, fungi and in eukaryotic plasma membranes and organelles, and function to transport a variety of different ions across membranes.</li>
<li><b>E-ATPases</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell-surface" title="Cell-surface">cell-surface</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> that hydrolyze a range of NTPs, including extracellular ATP.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="P-ATPase">P-ATPase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATPase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: P-ATPase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-ATPase" title="P-ATPase">P-ATPase</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-ATPase" title="P-ATPase">P-ATPases</a> (sometime known as E1-E2 ATPases) are found in bacteria and also in eukaryotic plasma membranes and organelles. Its name is due to short time attachment of inorganic phosphate at the aspartate residues at the time of activation. Function of P-ATPase is to transport a variety of different compounds, like ions and phospholipids, across a membrane using ATP hydrolysis for energy. There are many different classes of P-ATPases, which transports a specific type of ion. P-ATPases may be composed of one or two polypeptides, and can usually take two main conformations, E1 and E2.</p>
