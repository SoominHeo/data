<p><b>Saison</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, "season," <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[sɛ.zɔ̃]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">pale ale</a> that is generally around 7% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">ABV</a>, highly carbonated, fruity, spicy, and often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottle_conditioned" title="Bottle conditioned">bottle conditioned</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Beer_style" title="Beer style">beer style</a>, saison began as a <a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">pale ale</a> brewed in the cooler, less active months in farmhouses in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>, the French-speaking region of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, and stored for drinking in the summer months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> These farmhouse beers would have been of a lower ABV than modern saisons—around 3 to 3.5% ABV on average, rising in the early 20th century to between 4.5 and 6.5% ABV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strength_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-strength-2">[2]</a></sup> They were served to farm workers who were entitled to up to five litres each workday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> Brewing outside the summer months was common for all brewers before the discovery of refrigeration, due to the likelihood of the beer spoiling while fermenting in the summer, the height of airborne bacteria activity. Farmers possibly also brewed during the cooler months to provide work for their permanent staff during the quieter period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baets98_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baets98-3">[3]</a></sup> After brewing, the beer was stored until the summer when the main consumers would be seasonal workers ("saisonniers").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, saisons did not share enough identifiable characteristics to pin them down as a specific style, but rather were a group of refreshing summer ales made by farmers. Each farm brewer would make his own distinctive version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baets98_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baets98-3">[3]</a></sup> Although most commercial examples now range from 5 to 8% ABV, originally saisons were meant to be refreshing and it is thought they had alcohol levels ranging from 3 to 3.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strength_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-strength-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been a rise in interest in the style, with Saison Dupont being named "the Best Beer in the World" by the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Men%27s_Journal" title="Men's Journal">Men's Journal</a></i> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Modern saisons are generally highly carbonated, fruity and spicy—sometimes from the addition of spices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The type of <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a> determines the colour of the saison, and although most saisons are of a cloudy golden colour as result of the grist being mostly pale or <a href="/wiki/Pilsner" title="Pilsner">pilsner</a> malt, the use of darker malts results in some saisons being reddish-amber. Some recipes also use <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>. Spices such as orange zest, <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a> may be used. Some spice character may come through due to the production of <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> during <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> at warm temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarkowski2004.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DLutJAAAAYAAJ.26q.3DSaison.2Bspice.2Bcharacter.26dq.3DSaison.2Bspice.2Bcharacter.26hl.3Den.26ei.3DbbC0TJKgJY7Hswbtv52mCA.26sa.3DX.26oi.3Dbook_result.26ct.3Dresult.26resnum.3D1.26ved.3D0CC0Q6AEwAA_166.5D_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarkowski2004.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DLutJAAAAYAAJ.26q.3DSaison.2Bspice.2Bcharacter.26dq.3DSaison.2Bspice.2Bcharacter.26hl.3Den.26ei.3DbbC0TJKgJY7Hswbtv52mCA.26sa.3DX.26oi.3Dbook_result.26ct.3Dresult.26resnum.3D1.26ved.3D0CC0Q6AEwAA_166.5D-6">[6]</a></sup> Modern examples brewed in the US tend to copy the yeast used by the <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Brewery" title="Dupont Brewery">Dupont Brewery</a>, which ferments better at warmer temperatures like 29 to 35 °C (84 to 95 °F) than the standard 18 to 24 °C (64 to 75 °F) fermenting temperature used by other Belgian ales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarkowski2004168.E2.80.93173_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarkowski2004168.E2.80.93173-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
