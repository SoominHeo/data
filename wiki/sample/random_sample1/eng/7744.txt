<p><b>Kazuyo Sejima</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">妹島 和世</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sejima Kazuyo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born 29 October 1956)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a>. She is known for designs with clean <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">modernist</a> elements such as slick, clean, and shiny surfaces made of glass, marble, and metals. She also uses squares and cubes, which can be found in her designs in various degrees. Large windows allow natural light to enter a space and create a fluid transition between interior and exterior. It is this connection of two spaces from which she draws her inspiration.</p>
<p>Kazuyo Sejima, along with and <a href="/wiki/Ryue_Nishizawa" title="Ryue Nishizawa">Ryue Nishizawa</a>, has worked on several projects in Germany, Switzerland, France, England, the Netherlands, United States, and Spain. Many of their designs like the <a href="/wiki/Rolex_Learning_Center" title="Rolex Learning Center">Rolex Learning Center</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPFL" title="EPFL">EPFL</a> the <a href="/wiki/New_Museum" title="New Museum">New Museum</a> in the Bowery District in New York City as well as the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Museum_of_Art#Glass_Pavilion" title="Toledo Museum of Art">Glass Pavilion</a> for the Toledo Museum of Art involve glass and public open space to interact with the world around the architecture. Such design elements can be found abundantly in their designs.</p>
<p>In 2010, Sejima was the second woman to receive the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pritzker_Prize" title="Pritzker Prize">Pritzker Prize</a>, which was awarded jointly with <a href="/wiki/Ryue_Nishizawa" title="Ryue Nishizawa">Ryue Nishizawa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kazuyo_Sejima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sejima was born on 29 October 1956 in <a href="/wiki/Ibaraki_Prefecture" title="Ibaraki Prefecture">Ibaraki Prefecture</a>, Japan. In 1981, after graduating from <a href="/wiki/Japan_Women%27s_University" title="Japan Women's University">Japan Women's University</a> with a master's degree in Architecture, she joined <a href="/wiki/Toyo_Ito" title="Toyo Ito">Toyo Ito</a> and Associates.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kazuyo_Sejima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After apprenticing with <a href="/wiki/Toyo_Ito" title="Toyo Ito">Toyo Ito</a>, Sejima established Kazuyo Sejima &amp; Associates in 1987. One of her first hires was <a href="/wiki/Ryue_Nishizawa" title="Ryue Nishizawa">Ryue Nishizawa</a>, a student who had worked with Sejima at Toyo Ito and Associates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After working for Sejima for several years, he wanted to leave to start his own firm, but Sejima persuaded him to stay and form a partnership. In 1995, the two founded the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>-based firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanaa_(firm)" title="Sanaa (firm)">SANAA</a> (Sejima and Nishizawa and Associates).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2010, Sejima was appointed director of architecture sector for the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>, which she curated for the 12th Annual International Architecture Exhibition. She was the first woman ever selected for this position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2010, she was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pritzker_Prize" title="Pritzker Prize">Pritzker Prize</a>, together with Ryue Nishizawa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pritzker_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pritzker-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When describing Sejima's work, a viewer would notice that she mostly uses glass in all of her work. She likes slick surfaces such as marble, and her buildings mainly display an arrange of curves within the architecture of the building as well as on the surface. Kazuyo successfully combines the building with the surrounding areas. The use of a lot of sheer glasses and clear glass is used, allowing for a person to look at the outdoors, while also looking at themselves and the reflections the outside world creates on the inside of the building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<li>2010 – <a href="/wiki/Pritzker_Architecture_Prize" title="Pritzker Architecture Prize">Pritzker Architecture Prize</a> 2010, U.S.A.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professorship">Professorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kazuyo_Sejima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Professorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sejima teaches as a Visiting Professor, both at <a href="/wiki/Tama_Art_University" title="Tama Art University">Tama Art University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan_Women%27s_University" title="Japan Women's University">Japan Women's University</a> in Tokyo. From 2005 to 2008, together with Nishizawa, she held the Jean Labatut Professorship at the School of Architecture at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, where she also served on the advisory council for several years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Kazuyo Sejima has also taught at the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_F%C3%A9d%C3%A9rale_de_Lausanne" title="École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne">Polytechnique de Lausanne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a>.</p>
