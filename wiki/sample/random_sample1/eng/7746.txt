<p>The <b>Széchenyi Chain Bridge</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>Széchenyi lánchíd</i></span>, <small>Hungarian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Hungarian" title="Help:IPA for Hungarian">[ˈseːt͡ʃeːɲi ˈlaːnt͡shiːd]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridge</a> that spans the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">River Danube</a> between <a href="/wiki/Buda" title="Buda">Buda</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pest_(city)" title="Pest (city)">Pest</a>, the western and eastern sides of <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>. Designed by the English Engineer <a href="/wiki/William_Tierney_Clark" title="William Tierney Clark">William Tierney Clark</a>, it was the first permanent bridge across the Danube in Hungary, and was opened in 1849.</p>
<p>It is anchored on the Pest side of the river to Széchenyi (formerly Roosevelt) Square, adjacent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gresham-palota" title="Gresham-palota">Gresham Palace</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Hungarian Academy of Sciences">Hungarian Academy of Sciences</a>, and on the Buda side to Adam Clark Square, near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_Kilometre_Stone_(Budapest)" title="Zero Kilometre Stone (Budapest)">Zero Kilometre Stone</a> and the lower end of the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_Castle_Hill_Funicular" title="Budapest Castle Hill Funicular">Castle Hill Funicular</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Buda_Castle" title="Buda Castle">Buda Castle</a>.</p>
<p>The bridge has the name of <a href="/wiki/Istv%C3%A1n_Sz%C3%A9chenyi" title="István Széchenyi">István Széchenyi</a>, a major supporter of its construction, attached to it, but is most commonly known as the <i>Chain Bridge</i>. At the time of its construction, it was regarded as one of the modern world's engineering wonders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has asserted an enormous significance in the country's economic, social and cultural life, much as the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridge</a> has in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its decorations made of <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, and its construction, radiating calm dignity and balance, have elevated the Chain Bridge to a high stature in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It became a symbol of advancement, national awakening, and the linkage between East and West.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The bridge was designed by the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> Engineer <a href="/wiki/William_Tierney_Clark" title="William Tierney Clark">William Tierney Clark</a> in 1839, following an initiative by the influential Count <a href="/wiki/Istv%C3%A1n_Sz%C3%A9chenyi" title="István Széchenyi">István Széchenyi</a>, with construction supervised locally by Scottish Engineer Adam Clark (no relation). It is a larger scale version of William Tierney Clark's earlier <a href="/wiki/Marlow_Bridge" title="Marlow Bridge">Marlow Bridge</a>, across the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/Marlow,_Buckinghamshire" title="Marlow, Buckinghamshire">Marlow</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.</p>
<p>It was funded to a considerable extent by the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> merchant <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Sinas" title="Georgios Sinas">Georgios Sinas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who had considerable financial and land interests in the city and whose name is inscribed on the base of the south western foundation of the bridge on the Buda side.</p>
<p>The bridge was opened in 1849, after the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1848" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1848">Hungarian Revolution of 1848</a>, and thus became the first permanent bridge in the Hungarian capital. At the time, its centre span of 202 metres (663 ft) was one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_largest_suspension_bridges" title="List of largest suspension bridges">largest in the world</a>. The lions at each of the abutments were carved in stone by the sculptor, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A1nos_Marschalk%C3%B3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="János Marschalkó (page does not exist)">János Marschalkó</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are visibly similar in design to the famous bronze lions of <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a> by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Henry_Landseer" title="Edwin Henry Landseer">Edwin Henry Landseer</a> with Marochetti (commissioned 1858, installed 1867), but they were earlier - installed 1852.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bridges_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bridges-6">[6]</a></sup> They are also smaller (and appear from below to lack tongues). The bridge was given its current name in 1898.</p>
<p>It was designed in sections and shipped from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> to Hungary for final construction.</p>
<p>The bridge's cast iron structure was updated and strengthened in 1914. In World War II, the bridge was blown up on 18 January 1945 by the retreating Germans during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Budapest" title="Siege of Budapest">Siege of Budapest</a>, with only the towers remaining. It was rebuilt, and it reopened in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bridges_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-bridges-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_Bridge_(Budapest)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001 Hungarian stunt pilot <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_Besenyei" title="Péter Besenyei">Péter Besenyei</a> flew upside down under the bridge, a manoeuvre that became a standard in Red Bull air races today. The bridge is featured in <i><a href="/wiki/I_Spy_(film)" title="I Spy (film)">I Spy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Au_Pair_(film)" title="Au Pair (film)">Au Pair</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Walking_with_the_Enemy" title="Walking with the Enemy">Walking with the Enemy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_(2015_film)" title="Spy (2015 film)">Spy</a></i>. 'Also, in Katy Perry's music video <a href="/wiki/Firework_(song)" title="Firework (song)">Firework</a>, it is featured at the beginning. The climax scene in <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Bollywood</a> blockbuster <a href="/wiki/Hum_Dil_De_Chuke_Sanam" title="Hum Dil De Chuke Sanam">Hum Dil De Chuke Sanam</a> was shot on this bridge.</p>
<p>The inscription on each side of the bridge is to "Clark Adam", the bridge builder's name in the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_name_order" title="Eastern name order">Eastern name order</a>.</p>
<p>A plaque on the Pest side of the river reads "To commemorate the only two surviving bridges designed by William Tierney Clark: The Széchenyi Chain Bridge over the Danube at Budapest and the suspension bridge over the Thames at Marlow, England."</p>
<p>The bridge featured prominently <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Barney" title="Matthew Barney">Matthew Barney</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cremaster_5" title="Cremaster 5">Cremaster 5</a>, serving as a reference to <a href="/wiki/Harry_Houdini" title="Harry Houdini">Harry Houdini</a>.</p>
