<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cytoplasm</h1>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>, the <b>cytoplasm</b> is the material within a living cell, excluding the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>. It comprises <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a> (the gel-like substance enclosed within the cell membrane) and the <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a>'s internal sub-structures. All of the contents of the cells of <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryote</a> organisms (such as <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, which lack a cell nucleus) are contained within the cytoplasm. Within the cells of <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryote</a> organisms the contents of the cell nucleus are separated from the cytoplasm, and are then called the <a href="/wiki/Nucleoplasm" title="Nucleoplasm">nucleoplasm</a>. The cytoplasm is about 80% water and usually colorless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The submicroscopic ground cell substance or cytoplasmatic matrix which remains after exclusion the cell <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> and particles is <a href="/wiki/Groundplasm" title="Groundplasm">groundplasm</a>. It is the hyaloplasm of light microscopy, and high complex, polyphasic system in which all of resolvable cytoplasmic elements of are suspended, including the larger organelles such the <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosomes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, the plant <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a> droplets, and <a href="/wiki/Vacuole" title="Vacuole">vacuoles</a>.</p>
<p>It is within the cytoplasm that most cellular activities occur, such as many <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a> including <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a>, and processes such as <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell division</a>. The concentrated inner area is called the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasm" title="Endoplasm">endoplasm</a> and the outer layer is called the <a href="/wiki/Cell_cortex" title="Cell cortex">cell cortex</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Ectoplasm_(cell_biology)" title="Ectoplasm (cell biology)">ectoplasm</a>.</p>
<p>Movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcium_ion" title="Calcium ion">calcium ions</a> in and out of the cytoplasm is a <a href="/wiki/Recognition_signal" title="Recognition signal">signaling</a> activity for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolic" title="Metabolic">metabolic</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, movement of the cytoplasm around vacuoles is known as <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_streaming" title="Cytoplasmic streaming">cytoplasmic streaming</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term was introduced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolf_von_K%C3%B6lliker" title="Rudolf von Kölliker">Rudolf von Kölliker</a> in 1863, originally as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Protoplasm" title="Protoplasm">protoplasm</a>, but later it has come to mean the cell substance and organelles outside the nucleus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been certain disagreement on the definition of cytoplasm, as some authors prefer to exclude from it some organelles, especially the <a href="/wiki/Vacuole" title="Vacuole">vacuoles</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and sometimes the <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_nature">Physical nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The physical properties of the cytoplasm have been contested in recent years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It remains uncertain how the varied components of the cytoplasm interact to allow movement of particles<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="what particles? (September 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a> while maintaining the cell’s structure. The flow of cytoplasmic components plays an important role in many cellular functions which are dependent on the <a href="/wiki/Semipermeable_membrane" title="Semipermeable membrane">permeability</a> of the cytoplasm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An example of such function is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_signalling" title="Cell signalling">cell signalling</a>, a process which is dependent on the manner in which signaling molecules are allowed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffuse" title="Diffuse">diffuse</a> across the cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> While small signaling molecules like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcium_ions" title="Calcium ions">calcium ions</a> are able to diffuse with ease, larger molecules and subcellular structures often require aid in moving through the cytoplasm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup> The irregular dynamics of such particles have given rise to various theories on the nature of the cytoplasm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_sol-gel">As a sol-gel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As a sol-gel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There has long been evidence that the cytoplasm behaves like a <a href="/wiki/Sol-gel" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is thought that the component molecules and structures of the cytoplasm behave at times like a disordered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloidal" title="Colloidal">colloidal</a> solution (sol) and at other times like an integrated network, forming a solid mass (gel). This theory thus proposes that the cytoplasm exists in distinct fluid and solid phases depending on the level of interaction between cytoplasmic components, which may explain the differential dynamics of different particles observed moving through the cytoplasm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_glass">As a glass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As a glass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recently it has been proposed that the cytoplasm behaves like a <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>-forming liquid approaching the <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition" title="Glass transition">glass transition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup> In this theory, the greater the concentration of cytoplasmic components, the less the cytoplasm behaves like a liquid and the more it behaves as a solid glass, freezing larger cytoplasmic components in place (it is thought that the cell's metabolic activity is able to fluidize the cytoplasm to allow the movement of such larger cytoplasmic components).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup> A cell's ability to vitrify in the absence of metabolic activity, as in dormant periods, may be beneficial as a defence strategy. A solid glass cytoplasm would freeze subcellular structures in place, preventing damage, while allowing the transmission of very small proteins and metabolites, helping to kickstart growth upon the cell's revival from <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_perspectives">Other perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There has been research examining the motion of cytoplasmic particles independent of the nature of the cytoplasm. In such an alternative approach, the aggregate random forces within the cell caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_proteins" title="Motor proteins">motor proteins</a> explain the non-<a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a> of cytoplasmic constituents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constituents">Constituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Constituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three major elements of the cytoplasm are the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_inclusions" title="Cytoplasmic inclusions">inclusions</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cytosol">Cytosol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cytosol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">Cytosol</a></div>
<p>The cytosol is the portion of the cytoplasm not contained within membrane-bound organelles. Cytosol makes up about 70% of the cell volume and is a complex mixture of <a href="/wiki/Cytoskeleton" title="Cytoskeleton">cytoskeleton</a> filaments, dissolved molecules, and water. The cytosol's filaments include the <a href="/wiki/Protein_filament" title="Protein filament">protein filaments</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actin_filament" title="Actin filament">actin filaments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microtubule" title="Microtubule">microtubules</a> that make up the cytoskeleton, as well as soluble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> and small structures such as <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosomes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteasome" title="Proteasome">proteasomes</a>, and the mysterious <a href="/wiki/Vault_(organelle)" title="Vault (organelle)">vault complexes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The inner, granular and more fluid portion of the cytoplasm is referred to as endoplasm.</p>

<p>Due to this network of fibres and high concentrations of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, an effect called <a href="/wiki/Macromolecular_crowding" title="Macromolecular crowding">macromolecular crowding</a> occurs and the cytosol does not act as an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_solution" title="Ideal solution">ideal solution</a>. This crowding effect alters how the components of the cytosol interact with each other.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organelles">Organelles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organelles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">Organelles</a></div>
<p>Organelles (literally "little organs"), are usually membrane-bound structures inside the cell that have specific functions. Some major organelles that are suspended in the cytosol are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Golgi_apparatus" title="Golgi apparatus">Golgi apparatus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacuoles" title="Vacuoles">vacuoles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lysosomes" title="Lysosomes">lysosomes</a>, and in plant cells, <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cytoplasmic_inclusions">Cytoplasmic inclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cytoplasmic inclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_inclusion" title="Cytoplasmic inclusion">Cytoplasmic inclusion</a></div>
<p>The inclusions are small particles of insoluble substances suspended in the cytosol. A huge range of inclusions exist in different cell types, and range from crystals of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxalate" title="Calcium oxalate">calcium oxalate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a> in plants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prychid1999_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prychid1999-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prychid2003_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prychid2003-14">[14]</a></sup> to granules of energy-storage materials such as <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Polyhydroxybutyrate" title="Polyhydroxybutyrate">polyhydroxybutyrate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A particularly widespread example are <a href="/wiki/Lipid_droplet" title="Lipid droplet">lipid droplets</a>, which are spherical droplets composed of lipids and proteins that are used in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes as a way of storing lipids such as <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sterol" title="Sterol">sterols</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Lipid droplets make up much of the volume of <a href="/wiki/Adipocyte" title="Adipocyte">adipocytes</a>, which are specialized lipid-storage cells, but they are also found in a range of other cell types.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_and_research">Controversy and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytoplasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversy and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cytoplasm, mitochondria and most organelles are contributions to the cell from the maternal gamete. Contrary to the older information that disregards any notion of the cytoplasm being active, new research has shown it to be in control of movement and flow of nutrients in and out of the cell by <a href="/wiki/Viscoelasticity" title="Viscoelasticity">viscoplastic behavior</a> and a measure of the reciprocal rate of bond breakage within the cytoplasmic network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viscoplasticity_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Viscoplasticity-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The material properties of the cytoplasm remain an ongoing investigation. Recent measurements using <a href="/wiki/Force_spectrum_microscopy" title="Force spectrum microscopy">force spectrum microscopy</a> reveal that the cytoplasm can be likened to an elastic solid, rather than a viscoelastic fluid.</p>
