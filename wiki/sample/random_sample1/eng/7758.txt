<p><b>Centaurus</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a bright constellation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_sky" title="Southern sky">southern sky</a>. One of the <a href="/wiki/88_modern_constellations_by_area" title="88 modern constellations by area">largest constellations</a>, Centaurus was included among the 48 <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a> listed by the 2nd-century astronomer <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, and it remains one of the 88 modern constellations. In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, Centaurus represents a <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaur</a>; a creature that is half human, half horse (another constellation named after a centaur is one from the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">Sagittarius</a>). Notable stars include <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a>, the nearest star system to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, its neighbour in the sky <a href="/wiki/Beta_Centauri" title="Beta Centauri">Beta Centauri</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V766_Centauri" title="V766 Centauri">V766 Centauri</a>, one of the largest stars yet discovered. The constellation also contains <a href="/wiki/Omega_Centauri" title="Omega Centauri">Omega Centauri</a>, the brightest <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a> as visible from Earth and one of the largest known.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stars">Stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Centaurus contains several very bright stars because of its position in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>; in addition, its alpha and beta stars are used to find the constellation <a href="/wiki/Crux" title="Crux">Crux</a>. The constellation has 281 stars above magnitude 6.5, meaning that they are visible to the unaided eye, the most of any constellation. <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a>, the closest star system to the Sun, has a high <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a>; it will be a mere half-degree from <a href="/wiki/Beta_Centauri" title="Beta Centauri">Beta Centauri</a> in approximately 4000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_star_system" title="Triple star system">triple star system</a> that contains <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">Proxima Centauri</a>, the nearest star to the Sun. Traditionally called Rigil Kentaurus or Toliman, meaning "foot of the centaur", the system has an overall magnitude of -0.28 and is 4.4 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from Earth. The primary and secondary are both yellow-hued stars; the primary, is of magnitude -0.01 and the secondary is of magnitude 1.35. Proxima, the tertiary star, is a <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a> of magnitude 11.0; it is almost 2 degrees away from the primary and secondary and has a period of approximately one million years. Also a <a href="/wiki/Flare_star" title="Flare star">flare star</a>, Proxima has minutes-long outbursts where it brightens by over a magnitude. The primary and secondary have a period of 80 years and will be closest to each other as seen from Earth in 2037 and 2038.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to Alpha Centauri (the <a href="/wiki/List_of_brightest_stars" title="List of brightest stars">third-brightest star</a> in the sky), a second <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">first magnitude</a> star, Beta Centauri, is part of Centaurus. Also called Hadar and Agena, Beta Centauri is a double star; the primary is a blue-hued giant star of magnitude 0.6, 525 light-years from Earth. The secondary is of magnitude 4.0 and has a very small separation. A bright <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> in Centaurus is <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Centauri" title="Gamma Centauri">Gamma Centauri</a>, which appears to the naked eye at magnitude 2.2. The primary and secondary are both blue-white hued stars of magnitude 2.9; their period is 85 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Centaurus also has many dimmer double stars and binary stars. <a href="/wiki/3_Centauri" title="3 Centauri">3 Centauri</a> is a double star with a blue-white hued primary of magnitude 4.6 and a secondary of magnitude 6.1. The primary is 298 light-years from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Centaurus is home to many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_stars" title="Variable stars">variable stars</a>. <a href="/wiki/R_Centauri" title="R Centauri">R Centauri</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Mira_variable" title="Mira variable">Mira variable</a> star with a minimum magnitude of 11.8 and a maximum magnitude of 5.3; it is 2,100 light-years from Earth and has a period of 18 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/V810_Centauri" title="V810 Centauri">V810 Centauri</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiregular_variable" title="Semiregular variable">semiregular variable</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BPM_37093" title="BPM 37093">BPM 37093</a> is a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a> star whose <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atoms are thought to have formed a crystalline structure. Since <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> also consists of carbon arranged in a crystalline lattice (though of a different configuration), scientists have nicknamed this star "Lucy" after the Beatles song "<i><a href="/wiki/Lucy_in_the_Sky_with_Diamonds" title="Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds">Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds</a></i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep-sky_objects">Deep-sky objects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deep-sky objects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Omega_Centauri" title="Omega Centauri">ω Centauri</a> (NGC 5139), despite being listed as the constellation's "omega" star, is in fact a naked-eye <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a>, located at a distance of 17,000 light-years with a diameter of 150 light-years. It is the largest and brightest globular cluster in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, at ten times the size of the next-largest cluster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-objects_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-objects-4">[4]</a></sup> it has a magnitude of 3.7. It is also the most <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminous</a> globular cluster in the Milky Way, at over one million <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup> Omega Centauri is classified as a Shapley class VIII cluster, which means that its center is loosely concentrated. It is also the only globular cluster to be designated with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayer_letter" title="Bayer letter">Bayer letter</a>; the globular cluster <a href="/wiki/47_Tucanae" title="47 Tucanae">47 Tucanae</a> is the only one designated with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flamsteed_number" title="Flamsteed number">Flamsteed number</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevy2005161_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevy2005161-5">[5]</a></sup> It contains several million stars, most of which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_dwarf" title="Yellow dwarf">yellow dwarf</a> stars, but also possesses <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-type_star" title="B-type star">blue-white</a> stars; the stars have an average age of 12 billion years. This has prompted suspicion that Omega Centauri was the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">core</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy" title="Dwarf galaxy">dwarf galaxy</a> that had been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_merger" title="Galactic merger">absorbed</a> by the Milky Way. Omega Centauri was determined to be nonstellar in 1677 by the English astronomer <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">Edmond Halley</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-objects_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-objects-4">[4]</a></sup> though it was visible as a star to the ancients. Its status as a globular cluster was determined by <a href="/wiki/James_Dunlop" title="James Dunlop">James Dunlop</a> in 1827.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevy2005163_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevy2005163-6">[6]</a></sup> To the unaided eye, Omega Centauri appears fuzzy and is obviously non-circular; it is approximately half a degree in diameter, the same size as the full Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Centaurus is also home to open clusters. <a href="/wiki/NGC_3766" title="NGC 3766">NGC 3766</a> is an open cluster 6,300 light-years from Earth that is visible to the unaided eye. It contains approximately 100 stars, the brightest of which are 7th magnitude. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_5460&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NGC 5460 (page does not exist)">NGC 5460</a> is another naked-eye open cluster, 2,500 light-years from Earth, that has an overall magnitude of 6 and contains approximately 40 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is one bright <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> in Centaurus, <a href="/wiki/NGC_3918" title="NGC 3918">NGC 3918</a>, also known as the Blue Planetary. It has an overall magnitude of 8.0 and a central star of magnitude 11.0; it is 2600 light-years from Earth. The Blue Planetary was discovered by <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">John Herschel</a> and named for its color's similarity to <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, though the nebula is apparently three times larger than the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Centaurus is rich in galaxies as well. <a href="/wiki/NGC_4622" title="NGC 4622">NGC 4622</a> is a face-on <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxy</a> located 200 million light-years from Earth (redshift 0.0146). Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_arms" title="Spiral arms">spiral arms</a> wind in both directions, which makes it nearly impossible for astronomers to determine the rotation of the galaxy. Astronomers theorize that a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_merger" title="Galaxy merger">collision</a> with a smaller companion galaxy near the core of the main galaxy could have led to the unusual spiral structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-objects_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-objects-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NGC_5253" title="NGC 5253">NGC 5253</a>, a peculiar <a href="/wiki/Irregular_galaxy" title="Irregular galaxy">irregular galaxy</a>, is located near the border with Hydra and <a href="/wiki/Messier_83" title="Messier 83">M83</a>, with which it likely had a close gravitational interaction 1-2 billion years ago. This may have sparked the galaxy's high rate of star formation, which continues today and contributes to its high surface brightness. NGC 5253 includes a large nebula and at least 12 large star clusters. In the eyepiece, it is a small galaxy of magnitude 10 with dimensions of 5 arcminutes by 2 arcminutes and a bright nucleus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201340_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201340-7">[7]</a></sup> NGC 4945 is a spiral galaxy seen edge-on from Earth, 13 million light-years away. It is visible with any amateur telescope, as well as <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> under good conditions; it has been described as "shaped like a candle flame", being long and thin (16' by 3'). In the eyepiece of a large telescope, its southeastern dust lane becomes visible. Another galaxy is <a href="/wiki/NGC_5102" title="NGC 5102">NGC 5102</a>, found by star-hopping from <a href="/wiki/Iota_Centauri" title="Iota Centauri">Iota Centauri</a>. In the eyepiece, it appears as an elliptical object 9 arcminutes by 2.5 arcminutes tilted on a southwest-northeast axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201341_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201341-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the closest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galaxy" title="Active galaxy">active galaxies</a> to Earth is the <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A" title="Centaurus A">Centaurus A</a> galaxy, NGC 5128, at a distance of 11 million light-years (redshift 0.00183). It has a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a> at its core, which expels massive jets of matter that emit <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron radiation</a>. Astronomers posit that its dust lanes, not common in <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxies</a>, are due to a previous <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_merger" title="Galaxy merger">merger</a> with another galaxy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-objects_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-objects-4">[4]</a></sup> probably a <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxy</a>. NGC 5128 appears in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_spectrum" title="Optical spectrum">optical spectrum</a> as a fairly large elliptical galaxy with a prominent <a href="/wiki/Dust_lane" title="Dust lane">dust lane</a>. Its overall magnitude is 7.0, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup> and it has been seen under perfect conditions with the naked eye, making it one of the most distant objects visible to the unaided observer. In equatorial and southern latitudes, it is easily found by <a href="/wiki/Star_hopping" title="Star hopping">star hopping</a> from Omega Centauri. In small telescopes, the dust lane is not visible; it begins to appear with about 4 inches of aperture under good conditions. In large amateur instruments, above about 12 inches in aperture, the dust lane's west-northwest to east-southeast direction is easily discerned. Another dim dust lane on the east side of the 12-arcminute-by-15-arcminute galaxy is also visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201340_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201340-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ESO_270-17&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ESO 270-17 (page does not exist)">ESO 270-17</a>, also called the Fourcade-Figueroa Object, is a low-surface brightness object believed to be the remnants of a galaxy; it does not have a core and is very difficult to observe with an amateur telescope. It measures 7 arcminutes by 1 arcminute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201341_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDalrymple201341-8">[8]</a></sup> It likely originated as a spiral galaxy and underwent a catastrophic gravitational interaction with Centaurus A around 500 million years ago, stopping its rotation and destroying its structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESteinicke2007182_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinicke2007182-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/NGC_4650A" title="NGC 4650A">NGC 4650A</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Polar-ring_galaxy" title="Polar-ring galaxy">polar-ring galaxy</a> located at a distance of 136 million light-years from Earth (redshift 0.01). It has a central core made of older stars that resembles an <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxy</a>, and an outer ring of young stars that orbits around the core. The plane of the outer ring is distorted, which suggests that NGC 4650A is the result of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxy_collision" title="Galaxy collision">galaxy collision</a> about a billion years ago. This galaxy has also been cited in studies of <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>, because the stars in the outer ring orbit too quickly for their collective mass. This suggests that the galaxy is surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter_halo" title="Dark matter halo">dark matter halo</a>, which provides the necessary mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-objects_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-objects-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the closest <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a> to Earth is the <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_Cluster" title="Centaurus Cluster">Centaurus Cluster</a>, located at a distance of 160 million light-years (<a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> 0.0114). It has a cooler, denser central region of gas and a hotter, more diffuse outer region. The <a href="/wiki/Intracluster_medium" title="Intracluster medium">intracluster medium</a> in the Centaurus Cluster has a high concentration of metals (elements heavier than helium) due to a large number of <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>. This cluster also possesses a plume of gas whose origin is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-objects_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-objects-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>While Centaurus now has a high southern latitude, at the dawn of civilization it was an equatorial constellation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_(astronomy)" title="Precession (astronomy)">Precession</a> has been slowly shifting it southward for millennia, and it is now close to its maximal southern <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a>. Thousands of years from now Centaurus will, once again, be at lower latitudes and be visible worldwide.</p>
<p>The figure of Centaurus can be traced back to a Babylonian constellation known as the Bison-man (MUL.GUD.ALIM). This being was depicted in two major forms: firstly, as a 4-legged bison with a human head, and secondly, as a being with a man's head and torso attached to the rear legs and tail of a bull or bison. It has been closely associated with the Sun god Utu-Shamash from very early times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Greeks depicted the constellation as a <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaur</a> and gave it its current name. It was mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus" title="Eudoxus of Cnidus">Eudoxus</a> in the 4th century BCE and <a href="/wiki/Aratus" title="Aratus">Aratus</a> in the 3rd century BCE. In the 2nd century AD, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a> catalogued 37 stars in Centaurus. Large as it is now, in earlier times it was even larger, as the constellation <a href="/wiki/Lupus_(constellation)" title="Lupus (constellation)">Lupus</a> was treated as an <a href="/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy)" title="Asterism (astronomy)">asterism</a> within Centaurus, portrayed in illustrations as an unspecified animal either in the centaur's grasp or impaled on its spear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Crux" title="Crux">Southern Cross</a>, which is now regarded as a separate constellation, was treated by the ancients as a mere asterism formed of the stars composing the centaur's legs. Additionally, what is now the minor constellation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circinus_(constellation)" title="Circinus (constellation)">Circinus</a> was treated as undefined stars under the centaur's front hooves.</p>
<p>According to the Roman poet <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a> (<i>Fasti</i> v.379), the constellation honors the centaur <a href="/wiki/Chiron" title="Chiron">Chiron</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup> who was tutor to many of the earlier Greek heroes including <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> (Hercules), <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jason" title="Jason">Jason</a>, the leader of the Argonauts. However, most authorities consider <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">Sagittarius</a> to be the civilized Chiron, while Centaurus represents a more uncouth member of the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagittarius_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagittarius-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The legend associated with Chiron says that he was accidentally poisoned with an arrow shot by Hercules, and was subsequently placed in the heavens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion2001108-111-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equivalents">Equivalents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equivalents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">Chinese astronomy</a>, the stars of Centaurus are found in three areas: the <a href="/wiki/Azure_Dragon" title="Azure Dragon">Azure Dragon of the East</a> (東方青龍, <i>Dōng Fāng Qīng Lóng</i>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vermilion_Bird_(Chinese_constellation)" title="Vermilion Bird (Chinese constellation)">Vermillion Bird of the South</a> (南方朱雀, <i>Nán Fāng Zhū Què</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_constellations#The_Southern_Asterisms" title="Chinese constellations">Southern Asterisms</a> (近南極星區, <i>Jìnnánjíxīngōu</i>). Not all of the stars of Centaurus can be seen from China, and the unseen stars were classified among the Southern Asterisms by <a href="/wiki/Xu_Guangqi" title="Xu Guangqi">Xu Guangqi</a>, based on his study of western star charts. However, most of the brightest stars of Centaurus, including <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">α Cen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theta_Centauri" title="Theta Centauri">θ Cen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_Centauri" title="Epsilon Centauri">ε Cen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eta_Centauri" title="Eta Centauri">η Cen</a>, can be seen in the Chinese sky.</p>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynesian_people" title="Polynesian people">Polynesian peoples</a> considered the stars of Centaurus to be a constellation as well. On <a href="/wiki/Pukapuka" title="Pukapuka">Pukapuka</a>, Centaurus had two names: <i>Na Mata-o-te-tokolua</i> and <i>Na Lua-mata-o-Wua-ma-Velo</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, the constellation was called by four names: <i>O-nga-tangata</i>, <i>Tautanga-ufi</i>, <i>Mamangi-Halahu</i>, and <i>Mau-kuo-mau</i>. Alpha and Beta Centauri were not named specifically by the people of Pukapuka or Tonga, but they were named by the people of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tuamotus" title="Tuamotus">Tuamotus</a>. In Hawaii, the name for Alpha Centauri was either <i>Melemele</i> or <i>Ka Maile-hope</i> and the name for Beta Centauri was either <i>Polapola</i> or <i>Ka Maile-mua</i>. In the Tuamotu islands, Alpha was called <i>Na Kuhi</i> and Beta was called <i>Tere</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakemson1941281_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakemson1941281-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Namesakes">Namesakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centaurus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Namesakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> ships, <a href="/wiki/USS_Centaurus_(AKA-17)" title="USS Centaurus (AKA-17)">USS Centaurus (AKA-17)</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=USS_Centaurus_(AK-264)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="USS Centaurus (AK-264) (page does not exist)">USS Centaurus (AK-264)</a>, are named after the constellation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Centaurus" title="The Centaurus">The Centaurus</a> is a Mega Mall and commercial/residential complex in Islamabad, Pakistan. Construction started in 2005 and the three 41-storey towers, the tallest structures in Islamabad, were completed by late 2012. The shopping mall was officially opened on February 17, 2013. The Centaurus originally included a 7 star hotel, construction of which is yet to begin.</p>
