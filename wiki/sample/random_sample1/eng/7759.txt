<p><b>Delta Centauri</b> (δ Cen, δ Centauri) is a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> in the southern <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Centaurus" title="Centaurus">Centaurus</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_visual_magnitude" title="Apparent visual magnitude">apparent visual magnitude</a> of this star is +2.57,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aj84_1713_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-aj84_1713-2">[2]</a></sup> making it readily visible to the naked eye. Based upon <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> measurements, it is located at a distance of about 410 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (130 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a>) from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa474_2_653_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-aaa474_2_653-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Delta_Centauri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>δ Centauri is a <a href="/wiki/Shell_star" title="Shell star">shell star</a>, with a distinctive spectrum created by material thrown off into a disk by its rapid rotation. It is also a <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable star</a> whose brightness varies from magnitude +2.51 to +2.65. It has been classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_Cassiopeiae_variable" title="Gamma Cassiopeiae variable">γ Cassiopeiae type</a> variable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa488_3_L67_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa488_3_L67-10">[10]</a></sup> The energy from this star is being radiated at an <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a> of over 22,000 K from the outer envelope, giving it the blue-white hue of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-type_star" title="B-type star">B-type star</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csiro_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-csiro-11">[11]</a></sup> It has a radius of 6.5 times the <a href="/wiki/Solar_radius" title="Solar radius">radius of the Sun</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa505_2_687_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-aaa505_2_687-7">[7]</a></sup> and 8.7 times the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">Sun's mass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa505_2_687_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-aaa505_2_687-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stellar classification of this star is B2 IVne,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houk1979_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-houk1979-4">[4]</a></sup> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminosity_class" title="Luminosity class">luminosity class</a> of IV indicating that this is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgiant_star" title="Subgiant star">subgiant star</a> that has exhausted the hydrogen at its core and begun to <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">evolve</a> away from the <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a>. The star is spinning rapidly, with the resulting <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler effect</a> giving its <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> broad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_line" title="Absorption line">absorption lines</a> as indicated by the 'n'. The suffix 'e' means this is a classical <a href="/wiki/Be_star" title="Be star">Be star</a>, which is a type of hot star that has not yet evolved into a <a href="/wiki/Supergiant_star" title="Supergiant star">supergiant</a> and is surrounded by circumstellar gas. The presence of this gas creates an <a href="/wiki/Infrared_excess" title="Infrared excess">excess emission of infrared</a>, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a> in the star's spectrum. Most of it is concentrated around the equator, forming a disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa505_2_687_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-aaa505_2_687-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the variation in this star may be explained by assuming it is a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> system. This proposed secondary star would need to have about 4–7 times the Sun's mass and be orbiting with a period of at least 4.6 years at a minimum separation of 6.9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_Unit" title="Astronomical Unit">Astronomical Units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa488_3_L67_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-aaa488_3_L67-10">[10]</a></sup> δ Centauri shares a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_proper_motion" title="Common proper motion">common proper motion</a> with the nearby stars HD 105382 and HD 105383, so they may form a small cluster or perhaps a triple star system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa488_3_L67_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-aaa488_3_L67-10">[10]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a> member of the Lower-Centaurus Crux sub-group in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scorpius-Centaurus_Association" title="Scorpius-Centaurus Association">Scorpius-Centaurus</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_association" title="Stellar association">OB association</a>, the nearest such of association of co-moving massive stars to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa216_1_44_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa216_1_44-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Delta_Centauri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">馬尾</span> (<span lang="zh-Latn" xml:lang="zh-Latn">Mǎ Wěi</span>), meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Chinese_constellations#The_Southern_Asterisms_.28.E8.BF.91.E5.8D.97.E6.A5.B5.E6.98.9F.E5.8D.80.29" title="Chinese constellations">Horse's Tail</a></i>, refers to an asterism consisting of δ Centauri, <a href="/wiki/G_Centauri" title="G Centauri">G Centauri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rho_Centauri" title="Rho Centauri">ρ Centauri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Consequently, δ Centauri itself is known as <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">馬尾三</span> (<span lang="zh-Latn" xml:lang="zh-Latn">Mǎ Wěi sān</span>, English: the Third Star of Horse's Tail.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> From this Chinese name, the name <b>Ma Wei</b> was appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The people of <a href="/wiki/Aranda_people" title="Aranda people">Aranda</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luritja" title="Luritja">Luritja</a> tribe around <a href="/wiki/Hermannsburg" title="Hermannsburg">Hermannsburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Australia" title="Central Australia">Central Australia</a> named <b>Iritjinga</b>, "The Eagle-hawk", a quadrangular arrangement comprising this star, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Centauri" title="Gamma Centauri">γ Cen</a> (Muhlifain), <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Crucis" title="Gamma Crucis">γ Cru</a> (Gacrux), and <a href="/wiki/Delta_Crucis" title="Delta Crucis">δ Cru</a> (Palida).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
