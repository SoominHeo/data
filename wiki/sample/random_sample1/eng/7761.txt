<p>California's <b>Central Valley</b> is a large, flat valley that dominates the geographical center of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. It is 40 to 60 miles (60 to 100 km) wide and stretches approximately 450 miles (720 km) from north-northwest to south-southeast, inland from and parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> coast. It covers approximately 18,000 square miles (47,000 km<sup>2</sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-area_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-area-1">[1]</a></sup> about 11% of California's total land area (or about the size of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>). Bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> to the east and the <a href="/wiki/California_Coast_Ranges" title="California Coast Ranges">Coast Ranges</a> to the west, it is California's single most productive <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> region and one of the most productive in the world, providing more than half of the fruits, vegetables and nuts grown in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> More than 7 million acres (28,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of the valley are irrigated via an extensive system of reservoirs and canals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The valley also has many major cities, including the state capital <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>; as well as <a href="/wiki/Redding,_California" title="Redding, California">Redding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">Stockton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modesto,_California" title="Modesto, California">Modesto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a>.</p>
<p>The Central Valley <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> comprises 60,000 square miles (160,000 km<sup>2</sup>), or over a third of California. Its three main drainage systems are the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Valley" title="Sacramento Valley">Sacramento Valley</a> in the north, which receives well over 20 inches (510 mm) of rain annually; the drier <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> in the south, and the <a href="/wiki/Tulare_Lake" title="Tulare Lake">Tulare Basin</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid desert</a> climate at the southernmost end. The <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River" title="Sacramento River">Sacramento</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_River" title="San Joaquin River">San Joaquin</a> river systems drain their respective valleys and meet to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento-San_Joaquin_River_Delta" title="Sacramento-San Joaquin River Delta">Sacramento-San Joaquin River Delta</a>, a large expanse of interconnected <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream beds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slough_(hydrology)" title="Slough (hydrology)">sloughs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and peat islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-every_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-every-4">[4]</a></sup> The delta empties into the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>, and then ultimately flows into the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-every_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-every-4">[4]</a></sup> The waters of the Tulare Basin essentially never flow to the ocean (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Kings_River_(California)" title="Kings River (California)">Kings River</a> waters diverted northward for irrigation), though they are connected by man-made canals to the San Joaquin and could drain there again naturally if they were ever to rise high enough.</p>
<p>The valley encompasses all or parts of 18 <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a> <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a>: <a href="/wiki/Butte_County,_California" title="Butte County, California">Butte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colusa_County,_California" title="Colusa County, California">Colusa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placer_County,_California" title="Placer County, California">Placer</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_County,_California" title="San Joaquin County, California">San Joaquin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_County,_California" title="Sacramento County, California">Sacramento</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shasta_County,_California" title="Shasta County, California">Shasta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_County,_California" title="Stanislaus County, California">Stanislaus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sutter_County,_California" title="Sutter County, California">Sutter</a>, <a <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Central Valley is commonly known to residents simply as "the Valley". Older names include "the Great Valley", a name still often seen in scientific references (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Great_Valley_Sequence" title="Great Valley Sequence">Great Valley Sequence</a>), and "Golden Empire", a <a href="/wiki/Boosterism" title="Boosterism">booster</a> name which is still referred to by some organizations (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Golden_Empire_Transit" title="Golden Empire Transit">Golden Empire Transit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_Empire_Council" title="Golden Empire Council">Golden Empire Council</a>, etc.).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries_and_population">Boundaries and population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Boundaries and population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Central Valley is outlined by the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Mountains" title="Tehachapi Mountains">Tehachapi</a> mountain ranges on the east, and the <a href="/wiki/California_Coast_Ranges" title="California Coast Ranges">California Coast Ranges</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> on the west. The broad valley floor is carpeted by vast agricultural regions, and dotted with numerous population centers. Subregions and their counties commonly associated with the valley include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-overview-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>North Sacramento Valley (<a href="/wiki/Shasta_County,_California" title="Shasta County, California">Shasta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tehama_County,_California" title="Tehama County, California">Tehama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_County,_California" title="Glenn County, California">Glenn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butte_County,_California" title="Butte County, California">Butte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colusa_County,_California" title="Colusa County, California">Colusa</a>)</li>
<li>Sacramento Metro (<a href="/wiki/Sacramento_County,_California" title="Sacramento County, California">Sacramento</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Dorado_County,_California" title="El Dorado County, California">El Dorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sutter_County,_California" title="Sutter County, California">Sutter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yuba_County,_California" title="Yuba County, California">Yuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yolo_County,_California" title="Yolo County, California">Yolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placer_County,_California" title="Placer County, California">Placer</a>)</li>
<li>North San Joaquin (<a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_County,_California" title="San Joaquin County, California">San Joaquin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_County,_California" title="Stanislaus County, California">Stanislaus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merced_County,_California" title="Merced County, California">Merced</a>)</li>
<li>South San Joaquin (<a href="/wiki/Madera_County,_California" title="Madera County, California">Madera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresno_County,_California" title="Fresno County, California">Fresno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kings_County,_California" title="Kings County, California">Kings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tulare_County,_California" title="Tulare County, California">Tulare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kern_County,_California" title="Kern County, California">Kern County</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>There are four main population centers in the Central Valley, each roughly equidistant from the next, from south to north: <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Redding,_California" title="Redding, California">Redding</a>. While there are many communities large and small between these cities (see below), these four cities act as hubs for regional commerce and transportation.</p>

The largest city is <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno</a>, followed by the state capital, <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>. The following metropolitan and micropolitan statistical areas listed from largest to smallest:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landforms">Landforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The flatness of the valley floor contrasts with the rugged hills or gentle mountains that are typical of most of California's terrain. The valley is thought to have originated below sea level as an offshore area depressed by <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Farallon_Plate" title="Farallon Plate">Farallon Plate</a> into a trench further offshore. The <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Fault" title="San Joaquin Fault">San Joaquin Fault</a> is a notable seismic feature of the Central Valley.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1437" data-file-width="2875" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg/220px-I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg/330px-I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg/440px-I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:I-5_between_Tracy_and_Patterson_CA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The valley was later enclosed by the uplift of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Ranges" title="Coast Ranges">Coast Ranges</a>, with its original outlet into <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay" title="Monterey Bay">Monterey Bay</a>. Faulting moved the Coast Ranges, and a new outlet developed near what is now <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>. Over the millennia, the valley was filled by the sediments of these same ranges, as well as the rising <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> to the east; that filling eventually created an extraordinary flatness just barely above sea level; before California's massive flood control and aqueduct system was built, the annual snow melt turned much of the valley into an inland sea.</p>
<p>The one notable exception to the flat valley floor is <a href="/wiki/Sutter_Buttes" title="Sutter Buttes">Sutter Buttes</a>, the remnants of an extinct volcano just to the northwest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuba_City" title="Yuba City">Yuba City</a> which is 44 miles (71 km) north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento" title="Sacramento">Sacramento</a>.</p>
<p>Another significant geologic feature of the Central Valley lies hidden beneath the delta. The <a href="/wiki/Stockton_Arch" title="Stockton Arch">Stockton Arch</a> is an upwarping of the crust beneath the valley sediments which extends southwest to northeast across the valley.</p>
<p>Physiographically, the Central Valley lies within the California Trough physiographic section, which is part of the larger Pacific Border province, which in turn is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_States_Pacific_Mountain_System" title="Geography of the United States Pacific Mountain System">Pacific Mountain System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-Water_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-Water-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The "Central Valley grassland" is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nearctic" title="Nearctic">Nearctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Grassland#Temperate_grasslands" title="Grassland">temperate and subtropical grasslands, savannas, and shrub lands</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> which was once a diverse grassland containing areas of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Desert_grassland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Desert grassland (page does not exist)">desert grassland</a> (at the southern end), <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Riverside_woodland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Riverside woodland (page does not exist)">riverside woodland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marsh</a>, several types of seasonal <a href="/wiki/Vernal_pool" title="Vernal pool">vernal pools</a>, and large lakes such as now-dry <a href="/wiki/Tulare_Lake" title="Tulare Lake">Tulare Lake</a> (the largest lake in the United States west of the Mississippi), <a href="/wiki/Buena_Vista_Lake" title="Buena Vista Lake">Buena Vista Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kern_Lake_(Kern_County)" title="Kern Lake (Kern County)">Kern Lake</a>. However, much of the Central Valley environment has been removed or altered by human activity including the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exotic_plant" title="Exotic plant">exotic plants</a>, especially grasses. The oak woodlands and <a href="/wiki/Chaparral" title="Chaparral">chaparral</a> that fringe the valley have been categorized as the <a href="/wiki/California_interior_chaparral_and_woodlands" title="California interior chaparral and woodlands">California interior chaparral and woodlands</a> ecoregion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wetlands have been the target of rescue operations to restore areas nearly destroyed by agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The dominant grass of the valley was <i><a href="/wiki/Nassella_pulchra" title="Nassella pulchra">Nassella pulchra</a></i> mixed with other species, but today only 1% of the grassland in the valley is original and intact. Grassland flowers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_poppy" title="California poppy">California poppy</a> (<i>Eschscholzia californica</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupin" title="Lupin">lupins</a>, and purple owl's clover (<i><a href="/wiki/Castilleja_exserta" title="Castilleja exserta">Castilleja exserta</a></i>) which can still be seen, especially in <a href="/wiki/Antelope_Valley" title="Antelope Valley">Antelope Valley</a> in the Tehachapi hills. Riverside trees include willows, western sycamore (<i><a href="/wiki/Platanus_racemosa" title="Platanus racemosa">Platanus racemosa</a></i>), box elder (<i><a href="/wiki/Acer_negundo" title="Acer negundo">Acer negundo</a></i>), Fremont cottonwood (<i><a href="/wiki/Populus_fremontii" title="Populus fremontii">Populus fremontii</a></i>), and the endemic valley oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_lobata" title="Quercus lobata">Quercus lobata</a></i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Central Valley was once home to large populations of <a href="/wiki/Pronghorn" title="Pronghorn">pronghorn</a> antelope (<i>Antilocapra americana</i>), <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a> including the endemic <a href="/wiki/Tule_elk" title="Tule elk">tule elk</a> subspecies (<i>Cervus elaphus nannodes</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mule_deer" title="Mule deer">mule deer</a> (<i>Odocoileus hemionus</i>), <a href="/wiki/California_ground_squirrel" title="California ground squirrel">California ground squirrels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gopher_(animal)" title="Gopher (animal)">gophers</a>, mice, hare, rabbits and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_rat" title="Kangaroo rat">kangaroo rats</a>, along with their predators including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_kit_fox" title="San Joaquin kit fox">San Joaquin kit fox</a> which is now an endangered subspecies surviving on the hillsides of the San Joaquin Valley. The wetlands of the Valley were an important habitat for wintering waterbirds and migrating birds of other kinds. Reptiles and amphibians of the valley include the endemic San Joaquin coachwhip snake (<i><a href="/wiki/Masticophis_flagellum" title="Masticophis flagellum">Masticophis flagellum</a> ruddocki</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blunt-nosed_leopard_lizard" title="Blunt-nosed leopard lizard">blunt-nosed leopard lizard</a> (<i>Gambelia sila</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilbert%27s_skink" title="Gilbert's skink">Gilbert's skink</a> (<i>Eumeces gilberti</i>) and the western aquatic <a href="/wiki/Garter_snake" title="Garter snake">garter snake</a> (<i>Thamnophis couchii</i>). There are also a number of endemic invertebrates. The Central Valley is also home to a wide variety of endemic fish species, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Pikeminnow" title="Sacramento Pikeminnow">Sacramento Pikeminnow</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Perch" title="Sacramento Perch">Sacramento Perch</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Blackfish" title="Sacramento Blackfish">Sacramento Blackfish</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento_Splittail" title="Sacramento Splittail">Sacramento Splittail</a>, among others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_preservation">Threats and preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Threats and preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Agriculture, especially the creation of grazing land, and the draining of lakes and rivers have radically altered the habitats of the Valley. Most of the grassland has been overtaken by new species, most of the vernal pools have been drained or otherwise destroyed with only those on the higher slopes remaining, the marshland has mostly been drained too, and the riverbank woodlands have nearly all been affected.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Valley_Grasslands_State_Park" title="Great Valley Grasslands State Park">Great Valley Grasslands State Park</a> preserves an example of the <a href="/wiki/Category:Native_grasses_of_California" title="Category:Native grasses of California">native grass</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> in the valley, while oak savanna habitats remain near <a href="/wiki/Visalia,_California" title="Visalia, California">Visalia</a>. There are areas of wetland and riverside woodland in the north especially on the Sacramento River system including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_Conservancy" title="Nature Conservancy">Nature Conservancy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cosumnes_River_Preserve" title="Cosumnes River Preserve">Cosumnes River Preserve</a> just south of Sacramento, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gray_Lodge_Wildlife_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gray Lodge Wildlife Area (page does not exist)">Gray Lodge Wildlife Area</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butte_Sink_Wildlife_Management_Area" title="Butte Sink Wildlife Management Area">Butte Sink Wildlife Management Area</a>, and other patches in the delta area. Remaining vernal pools include <a href="/wiki/Pixley_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Pixley National Wildlife Refuge">Pixley National Wildlife Refuge</a> between <a href="/wiki/Tulare,_California" title="Tulare, California">Tulare, California</a> and Bakersfield and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jepson_Prairie_Preserve&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jepson Prairie Preserve (page does not exist)">Jepson Prairie Preserve</a> in the delta. There are large blocks of desert scrubland in the southern San Joaquin Valley and in the <a href="/wiki/Carrizo_Plain" title="Carrizo Plain">Carrizo Plain</a> which is just outside the valley but has a similar landscape.</p>
<p>All these patches of natural habitat are disconnected and this is particularly damaging for wildlife that is used to migrating along the rivers of the valley, birds such as warblers and cuckoos, and reptiles and amphibians. Meanwhile, clearance and alteration are ongoing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The valley gives its name to <a href="/wiki/Coccidioidomycosis" title="Coccidioidomycosis">Valley Fever</a> which is primarily a disease of the lungs that is common in the southwestern United States and northwestern Mexico. It is caused by the fungus <a href="/wiki/Coccidioides_immitis" title="Coccidioides immitis">Coccidioides immitis</a>, which grows in soils in areas of low rainfall, high summer temperatures, and moderate winter temperatures. These fungal spores become airborne when the soil is disturbed by winds, construction, or farming. This illness frequently takes many weeks or months to resolve. Occasionally Valley Fever is life-threatening and even fatal.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The northern Central Valley has a hot <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Csa</i>); the more southerly parts in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainshadow" title="Rainshadow">rainshadow</a> zones are dry enough to be Mediterranean steppe (<i>BShs</i>, as around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresno" title="Fresno">Fresno</a>) or even low-latitude desert (<i>BWh</i>, as in areas around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakersfield" title="Bakersfield">Bakersfield</a>). It is hot and dry during the summer and cool and damp in winter, when frequent ground fog known regionally as "<a href="/wiki/Tule_fog" title="Tule fog">tule fog</a>" can obscure vision. Summer daytime temperatures approach 100 °F (38 °C), and common <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> might bring temperatures exceeding 115 °F (46 °C). Mid-autumn to mid-spring comprises the <i>rainy season</i> — although during the late summer, southeasterly winds aloft can bring thunderstorms of tropical origin, mainly in the southern half of the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> but occasionally to the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Valley" title="Sacramento Valley">Sacramento Valley</a>. The northern half of the Central Valley receives greater precipitation than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semidesert" title="Semidesert">semidesert</a> southern half. Frost occurs at times in the fall months, but snow will occur occasionally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tule_fog">Tule fog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tule fog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tule_fog" title="Tule fog">Tule fog</a></div>
<p><b>Tule fog</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a thick ground <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> that settles along the length of the Great Central Valley. Tule fog forms during the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall" title="Fall">fall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> (California's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_season" title="Rain season">rainy season</a>) after the first significant rainfall. The official time frame for tule fog to form is from November 1 to March 31. This phenomenon is named after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tule" title="Tule">tule grass</a> wetlands (<i>tulares</i>) of the Central Valley. Accidents caused by the tule fog are the leading cause of weather-related casualties in California.</p>

<p>Two major river systems drain and define the two parts of the Central Valley. The <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River" title="Sacramento River">Sacramento River</a>, along with its tributaries the <a href="/wiki/Feather_River" title="Feather River">Feather River</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_River" title="American River">American River</a>, flows southwards through the Sacramento Valley for about 447 miles (719 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup> In the San Joaquin Valley, the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_River" title="San Joaquin River">San Joaquin River</a> flows roughly northwest for 365 miles (587 km), picking up tributaries such as the <a href="/wiki/Merced_River" title="Merced River">Merced River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuolumne_River" title="Tuolumne River">Tuolumne River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_River" title="Stanislaus River">Stanislaus River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mokelumne_River" title="Mokelumne River">Mokelumne River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> The Central Valley <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> encompasses over a third of California at 60,000 square miles (160,000 km<sup>2</sup>), with 46 percent draining into the Sacramento River, 26 percent into the San Joaquin, and 27 percent into Tulare Lake.</p>
<p>In the south part of the San Joaquin Valley, the <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">alluvial fan</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kings_River_(California)" title="Kings River (California)">Kings River</a> and another one from Coast Ranges streams have created a divide and resultantly the currently dry Tulare basin of the Central Valley, into which flow four major Sierra Nevada rivers, the Kings, <a href="/wiki/Kaweah_River" title="Kaweah River">Kaweah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tule_River" title="Tule River">Tule</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kern_River" title="Kern River">Kern</a>. This basin, usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endorheic" title="Endorheic">endorheic</a>, formerly filled during heavy snowmelt and spilled out into the San Joaquin River. Called <a href="/wiki/Tulare_Lake" title="Tulare Lake">Tulare Lake</a>, it is usually dry nowadays because the rivers feeding it have been diverted for agricultural purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rivers of the Central Valley converge in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento-San_Joaquin_Delta" title="Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta">Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta</a>, a complex network of marshy channels, distributaries and sloughs that wind around islands mainly used for agriculture. Here the freshwater of the rivers merges with tidewater, and eventually reach the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> after passing through <a href="/wiki/Suisun_Bay" title="Suisun Bay">Suisun Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Pablo_Bay" title="San Pablo Bay">San Pablo Bay</a>, upper <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> and finally the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate" title="Golden Gate">Golden Gate</a>. Many of the islands now lie below sea level because of intensive agriculture, and have a high risk of flooding, which would cause salt water to rush back into the delta, especially when there is too little fresh water flowing in from the Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Sacramento River carries far more water than the San Joaquin, with an estimated 22 million acre feet (27 km<sup>3</sup>) of virgin annual runoff, as compared to the San Joaquin's approximately 6 million acre feet (7.4 km<sup>3</sup>). Intensive agricultural and municipal water consumption has reduced the present rate of outflow to about 17 million acre feet (21 km<sup>3</sup>) for the Sacramento and 3 million acre feet (3.7 km<sup>3</sup>) for the San Joaquin; however, these figures still vary widely from year to year. Over 25 million people, living both in the valley and in other regions of the state, rely on the water carried by these rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering">Engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Runoff from the Sierra Nevada flows into the Central Valley and provides one of the largest water resources of California. The Sacramento River is the second largest river to empty into the Pacific from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_United_States" title="Continental United States">Continental United States</a>, behind only the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River" title="Columbia River">Columbia River</a> and greater than the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[25]</a></sup> Combined with the fertile and expansive area of the Central Valley's floor, the Central Valley is ideal for agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, the Central Valley is one of the most productive growing regions of the United States. But to enable this, water control was needed to prevent rivers from overflowing during the spring snowmelt while drying up in the summer and autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVPhist_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CVPhist-30">[27]</a></sup> As a result, many large dams, including <a href="/wiki/Shasta_Dam" title="Shasta Dam">Shasta Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oroville_Dam" title="Oroville Dam">Oroville Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Folsom_Dam" title="Folsom Dam">Folsom Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Melones_Dam" title="New Melones Dam">New Melones Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Pedro_Dam" title="Don Pedro Dam">Don Pedro Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friant_Dam" title="Friant Dam">Friant Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pine_Flat_Dam" title="Pine Flat Dam">Pine Flat Dam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Dam" title="Isabella Dam">Isabella Dam</a>, were constructed on rivers entering the Central Valley, many part of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_Project" title="Central Valley Project">Central Valley Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVPhist_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-CVPhist-30">[27]</a></sup> These dams have had a profound impact on the physical, economic, cultural, and ecological state of Central Valley: for example, enabling development of its vast agricultural resources but leading to the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Chinook_salmon" title="Chinook salmon">Chinook salmon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Post-World War II demand for urban development in California's urban areas, most notably the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inland_Empire_(California)" title="Inland Empire (California)">Inland Empire</a>/<a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, required water resources to support it. Moreover, rapid development of agriculture in the southern Central Valley required far more water than available locally. The <a href="/wiki/Feather_River" title="Feather River">Feather River</a> in the Sacramento Valley was looked to as a water source, leading to public financing of the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Water_Project" title="California State Water Project">California State Water Project</a>. This transports water to the southern San Joaquin Valley and vast urban lands south of the Tehachapi Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Runoff from the Sacramento and San Joaquin Rivers is intercepted in the delta by the state Department of Water Resources through a series of massive pumps, which divert water into the <a href="/wiki/California_Aqueduct" title="California Aqueduct">California Aqueduct</a> that runs south along the entire length of the San Joaquin Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[30]</a></sup> In parallel, pumps operated by the Bureau of Reclamation divert water into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_Mendota_Canal" title="Delta Mendota Canal">Delta Mendota Canal</a>. The flow of the Sacramento River is further supplemented by a tunnel from the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_River_(California)" title="Trinity River (California)">Trinity River</a> (a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Klamath_River" title="Klamath River">Klamath River</a>, northwest of the Sacramento Valley) near <a href="/wiki/Redding,_California" title="Redding, California">Redding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[31]</a></sup> Cities of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>, also needing great amounts of water, built aqueducts from the <a href="/wiki/Mokelumne_River" title="Mokelumne River">Mokelumne River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuolumne_River" title="Tuolumne River">Tuolumne River</a> that run east to west across the middle part of the Central Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>Most lowlands of the Central Valley are prone to flooding, especially in the old <a href="/wiki/Tulare_Lake" title="Tulare Lake">Tulare Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buena_Vista_Lake" title="Buena Vista Lake">Buena Vista Lake</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kern_Lake_(Kern_County)" title="Kern Lake (Kern County)">Kern Lake</a> rivers. The <a href="/wiki/Kings_River_(California)" title="Kings River (California)">Kings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaweah_River" title="Kaweah River">Kaweah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tule_River" title="Tule River">Tule</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kern_River" title="Kern River">Kern</a> rivers originally flowed into these seasonal lakes, which would expand each spring to flood large parts of the southern San Joaquin Valley. Due to the construction of farms, towns and infrastructure in these lakebeds while preventing them from flooding with levee systems, the risk of floods damaging properties increased greatly.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Flood_of_1862#Flooding_of_the_Central_Valley" title="Great Flood of 1862">Great Flood of 1862</a> was the worst flood that the Central Valley has experienced in recorded history, flooding most of the valley and putting some places as much as 20 feet under water.</p>
<p>Major public works projects beginning in the 1930s sought to reduce the amount of snowmelt flooding by the building of large dams. In 2003, it was determined that Sacramento had both the least protection against and nearly the highest risk of flooding. Congress then granted a $220 million loan for upgrades in Sacramento County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[34]</a></sup> Other counties in the valley that face flooding often are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuba_County,_CA" title="Yuba County, CA">Yuba</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanislaus_County,_CA" title="Stanislaus County, CA">Stanislaus</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_County,_CA" title="San Joaquin County, CA">San Joaquin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agriculture is the primary industry in most of the Central Valley. A notable exception to the predominance of agriculture has been the Sacramento area, where the large and stable workforce of government employees helped steer the economy away from agriculture. Despite state hiring cutbacks and the closure of several military bases, Sacramento's economy has continued to expand and diversify and now more closely resembles that of the nearby San Francisco Bay Area. Primary sources of population growth are people migrating from the San Francisco Bay Area seeking lower housing costs, as well as immigration from Asia, Central America, Mexico, Ukraine and the rest of the former Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-overview-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnography">Ethnography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ethnography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After English and Spanish, <a href="/wiki/Hmong_language" title="Hmong language">Hmong</a> is the third most commonly spoken language in the Central Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Central Valley is one of the world's most productive agricultural regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-every_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-every-4">[4]</a></sup> More than 230 crops are grown there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-every_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-every-4">[4]</a></sup> On less than 1 percent of the total farmland in the United States, the Central Valley produces 8 percent of the nation's agricultural output by value: $43.5 billion USD in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[37]</a></sup> Its agricultural productivity relies on irrigation from both surface water diversions and groundwater pumping from wells. About one-sixth of the irrigated land in the U.S. is in the Central Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Virtually all non-tropical crops are grown in the Central Valley, which is the primary source for a number of food products throughout the United States, including tomatoes, almonds, grapes, cotton, apricots, and asparagus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are 6,000 <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a> growers that produced more than 1.8 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> in 2013, about 60 percent of the world's supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAOSTAT_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAOSTAT-44">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The top four counties in agricultural sales in the U.S. are in the Central Valley (2007 Data). They are Fresno County (#1 with $3.731 billion in sales), Tulare County (#2 with $3.335 billion), Kern County (#3 with $3.204), and Merced County (#4 with $2.330 billion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-overview-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early farming was concentrated close to the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, where the water table was high year round and water transport more readily available, but subsequent irrigation projects have brought many more parts of the valley into productive use. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_Project" title="Central Valley Project">Central Valley Project</a> was formed in 1935 to redistribute and store water for agricultural and municipal purposes with dams and canals. The even larger <a href="/wiki/California_State_Water_Project" title="California State Water Project">California State Water Project</a> was formed in the 1950s and construction continued throughout the following decades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Farmworkers_Association_.28NFWA.29">National Farmworkers Association (NFWA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: National Farmworkers Association (NFWA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was in the Central Valley, especially in and around <a href="/wiki/Delano,_California" title="Delano, California">Delano</a>, that farm labor leader <a href="/wiki/Cesar_Chavez" title="Cesar Chavez">Cesar Chavez</a> organized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_American" title="Mexican American">Mexican American</a> grape pickers into a <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">union</a> in the 1960s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Farmworkers_Association" title="National Farmworkers Association">National Farmworkers Association</a> (NFWA), in order to improve their working conditions.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="San_Joaquin_Valley_congestion">San Joaquin Valley congestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Valley_(California)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: San Joaquin Valley congestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Since the 1980s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakersfield" title="Bakersfield">Bakersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porterville,_California" title="Porterville, California">Porterville</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresno" title="Fresno">Fresno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visalia,_California" title="Visalia, California">Visalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tracy,_California" title="Tracy, California">Tracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modesto,_California" title="Modesto, California">Modesto</a> and many other towns and cities have exploded in both area and population, as housing values along the coast increased. Many people from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> relocated to growing San Joaquin Valley suburbs in search of more affordable housing while retaining employment outside the Valley. This has led to <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a> between their Valley residences and their Bay Area employment with accompanying <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>. Air pollution became a principal environmental and health concern as long ago as the 1960s, and resulted in the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a> in 1967.</p>

<p>Highways <a href="/wiki/Interstate_5_in_California" title="Interstate 5 in California">Interstate 5</a> and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_99" title="California State Route 99">CA 99</a> run, roughly parallel, north-south through the valley, meeting at its north and south ends. <a href="/wiki/Interstate_80_in_California" title="Interstate 80 in California">Interstate 80</a> crosses it northeast-southwest from <a href="/wiki/Rocklin,_California" title="Rocklin, California">Rocklin</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacaville" title="Vacaville">Vacaville</a>.</p>
<p>The state water project's <a href="/wiki/Oroville_Dam" title="Oroville Dam">Oroville Dam</a> in the Sacramento Valley provides water and power for the <a href="/wiki/California_Aqueduct" title="California Aqueduct">California Aqueduct</a> in the San Joaquin Valley. The aqueduct runs from Clifton Court Forebay in the Delta southwards across the <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Ranges" title="Transverse Ranges">Transverse Ranges</a>. The federal (<a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_Project" title="Central Valley Project">Central Valley Project</a>) includes numerous facilities between <a href="/wiki/Shasta_Dam" title="Shasta Dam">Shasta Dam</a> in the north to Bakersfield in the south. <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Gas_and_Electric_Company" title="Pacific Gas and Electric Company">Pacific Gas and Electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Area_Power_Administration" title="Western Area Power Administration">Western Area Power Administration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Edison" title="Southern California Edison">Southern California Edison</a> have interconnected electric transmission systems connecting the north and south ends of the Central Valley (examples include <a href="/wiki/Path_15" title="Path 15">Path 15</a>, <a href="/wiki/Path_26" title="Path 26">Path 26</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Path_66" title="Path 66">Path 66</a>).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BNSF" title="BNSF">BNSF</a> (Burlington Northern Santa Fe Railway) and <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific Railroad</a> both have railway lines in the Central Valley. The BNSF Bakersfield Subdivision runs from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakersfield" title="Bakersfield">Bakersfield</a> to Calwa, four miles (6 km) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresno" title="Fresno">Fresno</a>. From Calwa the BNSF Stockton Subdivision continues to <a href="/wiki/Port_Chicago,_California" title="Port Chicago, California">Port Chicago</a>, west of Antioch. The Union Pacific Railroad Martinez Subdivision runs from Port Chicago through Martinez, <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_California" title="Richmond, California">Richmond</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emeryville" title="Emeryville">Emeryville</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>. The UP's Fresno Subdivision runs from <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">Stockton</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakersfield" title="Bakersfield">Bakersfield</a> and traffic passes through a large and historical yard in <a href="/wiki/Roseville,_California" title="Roseville, California">Roseville</a> before heading to Oregon or Nevada. <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> operates six daily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Joaquins" title="San Joaquins">San Joaquins</a> trains over these lines. <a href="/wiki/California_High-Speed_Rail" title="California High-Speed Rail">California High-Speed Rail</a>, linking northern and southern California, broke ground in the Central Valley in 2015.</p>
