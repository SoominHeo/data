<p><i><b>Self</b></i> is a novel by <a href="/wiki/Yann_Martel" title="Yann Martel">Yann Martel</a>. It tells the story of a traveling writer who wakes up one morning to discover that he has become a woman. It was first published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knopf_Canada" title="Knopf Canada">Knopf Canada</a> in <a href="/wiki/1996_in_literature" title="1996 in literature">1996</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Self_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The narrator, at first male, explains various events from his early childhood, living with a traveling family who finally settle in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. He goes on to explain events from his years in private school (including his parents' death), until he graduates and travels to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, where he, on his eighteenth birthday, wakes up as a female.</p>
<p>Surprisingly unfazed by her transformation, the narrator concludes her trip and begins <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> back in the fictional Roetown. She begins writing, and keeps travel in her life, eventually visiting such places as <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, to name a few. She shares romances with a select few — males and females alike. Eventually she gets published, and after graduating, moves to <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, where she gets a job as a waitress while continuing to write. At her job she meets Tito, her final love. But as the novel is nearing a conclusion, she is suddenly <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">raped</a> by a vicious neighbour in her secluded apartment and her body reverts to being a male again.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Self_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martel described <i>Self</i> as the crucible of a thematic interest in religious faith that later informed his second work, <i>Life of Pi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Martel also reported in interviews that in writing the book's rape scene he contemplated the relationship between religion and evil: "It made me think about how people live with evil. What interested me in religion is its claim to go beyond the bounds of human existence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Some reviewers noted an autobiographical strand in the book,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> whose hero is, like Martel, the child of Canadian diplomats and a writer achieving recognition at a young age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is set partly in <a href="/wiki/Peterborough,_Ontario" title="Peterborough, Ontario">Peterborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, where Martel was a student at <a href="/wiki/Trent_University" title="Trent University">Trent University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Self_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Self</i> was Martel's first novel, and followed the publication in 1993 of <i>The Facts Behind the Helsinki Roccamatios</i>, his first collection of short stories. The novel, in Martel's own words, initially "vanished quickly and quietly",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> though it was shortlisted for the 21st Chapters/Books In Canada First Novel Award, then Canada's most valuable first-novel award with a prize of 5,000 Canadian dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>More critical attention fell upon the book when Martel's second novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Life_of_Pi" title="Life of Pi">Life of Pi</a></i>, won the 2002 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" title="Man Booker Prize">Man Booker Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMH_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMH-8">[8]</a></sup> A reviewer for the <i>Sydney Morning Herald</i>, noting that Martel himself had called the novel "terrible" and expressed a wish that it "disappear", agreed that the work suffered from a "serious crisis of identity", and lacked the power of <i>Life of Pi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMH_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-SMH-8">[8]</a></sup> The <i>Montreal Mirror</i> went further, calling <i>Self</i> "lame... A pastiche of autobiography and post-modern plot twists, it was haunted by an off-putting tone of smug precociousness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <i>Toronto Star</i> reviewer objected to <i>Self</i>'s protagonist's "self-satisfied air", but praised the work for its deft touch and compelling narrative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A writer in <i>The Independent</i> described the book's handling of gender change as "crude confusion",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> while <i>The Hindu</i> described the book as "interesting ideas juxtaposed against not-so-inspiring writing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">Publication history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Self_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publication history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martel received in 1991 a grant of $18,000 from the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Council" title="Canada Council">Canada Council</a> to write <i>Self</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which was published in Canada in 1996, with a British launch the same November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
