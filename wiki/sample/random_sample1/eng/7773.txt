<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Black Shuck</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

Artist's impression of the Black Shuck. Commonly described features include large red eyes, bared teeth and shaggy black fur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lore_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lore-1">[1]</a></sup></div>

<p><b>Black Shuck</b>, <b>Old Shuck</b>, <b>Old Shock</b> or simply <b>Shuck</b> is the name given to a <a href="/wiki/Ghost" title="Ghost">ghostly</a> <a href="/wiki/Black_dog_(ghost)" title="Black dog (ghost)">black dog</a> which is said to roam the coastline and countryside of <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>. Accounts of the animal form part of the <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fens" title="Fens">fens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name <i>Shuck</i> may derive from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word <i>scucca</i> meaning "<a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demon</a>", or possibly from the local <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> word <i>shucky</i> meaning "shaggy" or "hairy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eberhart_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-eberhart-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Black Shuck is one of many ghostly black dogs recorded across the British Isles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Sometimes recorded as an omen of death, sometimes a more companionable animal, it is classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptid" title="Cryptid">cryptid</a>, and there are varying accounts of the animal's appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eberhart_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-eberhart-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood-6">[6]</a></sup> Writing in 1877, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Rye" title="Walter Rye">Walter Rye</a> stated that Shuck was "the most curious of our local apparitions, as they are no doubt varieties of the same animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its alleged appearance in 1577 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bungay,_Suffolk" title="Bungay, Suffolk">Bungay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blythburgh" title="Blythburgh">Blythburgh</a> is a particularly famous account of the beast, and images of black sinister dogs have become part of the iconography of the area and have appeared in <a href="/wiki/Black_dog_ghosts_in_popular_culture#Black_Shuck" title="Black dog ghosts in popular culture">popular culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lore_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lore-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For centuries, inhabitants of England have told tales of a large black dog with malevolent flaming eyes (or in some variants of the legend a single eye) that are red or alternatively green. They are described as being 'like <a href="/wiki/Saucer" title="Saucer">saucers</a>'. According to reports, the beast varies in size and stature from that of simply a large dog to being the size of a calf or even a horse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lore_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-lore-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood-6">[6]</a></sup> Sometimes Black Shuck is recorded as having appeared headless, and at other times as floating on a carpet of <a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">mist</a>.</p>
<p>According to folklore, the spectre haunts the landscapes of East Anglia, primarily coastline, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graveyard" title="Graveyard">graveyards</a>, sideroads, <a href="/wiki/Junction_(road)" title="Junction (road)">crossroads</a>, bodies of water and dark <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eberhart_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-eberhart-4">[4]</a></sup> W. A. Dutt, in his 1901 <i>Highways &amp; Byways in East Anglia</i> describes the creature thus:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He takes the form of a huge black dog, and prowls along dark lanes and lonesome field footpaths, where, although his howling makes the hearer's blood run cold, his footfalls make no sound. You may know him at once, should you see him, by his fiery eye; he has but one, and that, like the <a href="/wiki/Cyclops" title="Cyclops">Cyclops</a>', is in the middle of his head. But such an encounter might bring you the worst of luck: it is even said that to meet him is to be warned that your death will occur before the end of the year. So you will do well to shut your eyes if you hear him howling; shut them even if you are uncertain whether it is the dog fiend or the voice of the wind you hear. Should you never set eyes on our Norfolk Snarleyow you may perhaps doubt his existence, and, like other learned folks, tell us that his story is nothing but the old Scandinavian myth of the black hound of <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>, brought to us by the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> who long ago settled down on the Norfolk coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is Dutt's description which gave rise to one misnomer for Black Shuck as "Old Snarleyow"; in the context of his description it is a comparative to <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Marryat" title="Frederick Marryat">Frederick Marryat</a>'s 1837 novel <i>Snarleyyow, or the Dog Fiend</i>, which tells the tale of a troublesome ship's dog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to some legends, the dog's appearance bodes ill to the beholder - for example in the <a href="/wiki/Maldon,_Essex" title="Maldon, Essex">Maldon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengie_peninsula" title="Dengie peninsula">Dengie</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, the most southerly point of sightings, where seeing Black Shuck means the observer's almost immediate death. However, more often than not, stories tell of Black Shuck terrifying his victims, but leaving them alone to continue living normal lives; in some cases it has supposedly happened before close relatives to the observer die or become ill.</p>
<p>By contrast, in other tales the animal is regarded as relatively benign and said to accompany women on their way home in the role of protector rather than a portent of ill omen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some black dogs have been said to help lost travellers find their way home and are more often helpful than threatening; Sherwood notes that benign accounts of the dog become more regular towards the end of the 19th and throughout the 20th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sightings">Sightings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sightings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_at_Peterborough_Abbey">Appearance at Peterborough Abbey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearance at Peterborough Abbey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dr Simon Sherwood suggests that the earliest surviving description of devilish black hounds is an account of an incident in the <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Cathedral" title="Peterborough Cathedral">Peterborough Abbey</a> recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Chronicle" title="Peterborough Chronicle">Peterborough Chronicle</a> (one version of the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>) around 1127:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Let no-one be surprised at the truth of what we are about to relate, for it was common knowledge throughout the whole country that immediately after [Abbot Henry of Poitou's arrival at Peterborough Abbey] - it was the Sunday when they sing Exurge Quare - many men both saw and heard a great number of huntsmen hunting. The huntsmen were black, huge and hideous, and rode on black horses and on black he-goats and the hounds were jet black with eyes like saucers and horrible. This was seen in the very deer park of the town of Peterborough and in all the woods that stretch from that same town to <a href="/wiki/Stamford,_Lincolnshire" title="Stamford, Lincolnshire">Stamford</a>, and in the night the monks heard them sounding and winding their horns. Reliable witnesses who kept watch in the night declared that there might well have been as many as twenty or thirty of them winding their horns as near they could tell. This was seen and heard from the time of his arrival all through Lent and right up to Easter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This account also appears to describe the Europe-wide phenomenon of a <a href="/wiki/Wild_Hunt" title="Wild Hunt">Wild Hunt</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_in_Bungay_and_Blythburgh">Appearance in Bungay and Blythburgh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appearance in Bungay and Blythburgh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Staunge_and_terrible_Wunder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="252" height="379" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/A_Staunge_and_terrible_Wunder.jpg/200px-A_Staunge_and_terrible_Wunder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/44/A_Staunge_and_terrible_Wunder.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Staunge_and_terrible_Wunder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title page of Rev. Abraham Fleming's account of the appearance of the ghostly black dog "Black Shuck" at the church of Bungay, Suffolk: "A straunge, and terrible wunder wrought very late in the parish church of Bongay: a town of no great distance from the citie of Norwich, namely the fourth of this August, in ye yeere of our Lord 1577".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the most notable reports of Black Shuck is of his appearance at the churches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bungay,_Suffolk" title="Bungay, Suffolk">Bungay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blythburgh" title="Blythburgh">Blythburgh</a> in Suffolk. On 4 August 1577, at Blythburgh, Black Shuck is said to have burst in through the doors of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity_Church,_Blythburgh" title="Holy Trinity Church, Blythburgh">Holy Trinity Church</a> to a clap of thunder. He ran up the nave, past a large congregation, killing a man and boy and causing the church <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steeple_(architecture)" title="Steeple (architecture)">steeple</a> to collapse through the roof. As the dog left, he left scorch marks on the north door which can be seen at the church to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The encounter on the same day at <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Church,_Bungay" title="St Mary's Church, Bungay">St Mary's Church, Bungay</a> was described in <i>A Straunge and Terrible Wunder</i> by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Fleming" title="Abraham Fleming">Abraham Fleming</a> in 1577:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This black dog, or the divel in such a linenesse (God hee knoweth al who worketh all,) running all along down the body of the church with great swiftnesse, and incredible haste, among the people, in a visible fourm and shape, passed between two persons, as they were kneeling uppon their knees, and occupied in prayer as it seemed, wrung the necks of them bothe at one instant clene backward, in somuch that even at a mome[n]t where they kneeled, they stra[n]gely dyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Fleming was a translator and editor for several printing houses in London, and therefore probably only published his account based on exaggerated <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral accounts</a>. Other local accounts attribute the event to the <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">Devil</a> (Fleming calls the animal "the Divel in such a likeness"). The scorch marks on the door are referred to by the locals as "the devil’s fingerprints", and the event is remembered in this verse:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>All down the church in midst of fire, the hellish monster flew, and, passing onward to the quire, he many people slew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Dr David Waldron and Christopher Reeve suggest that a fierce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_storm" title="Electrical storm">electrical storm</a> recorded by contemporary accounts on that date, coupled with the trauma of the ongoing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a>, may have led to the accounts entering folklore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hauntings_of_Littleport">Hauntings of Littleport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hauntings of Littleport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Littleport,_Cambridgeshire#Black_dog_hauntings" title="Littleport, Cambridgeshire">Littleport, Cambridgeshire § Black dog hauntings</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Littleport,_Cambridgeshire" title="Littleport, Cambridgeshire">Littleport</a>, Cambridgeshire is home to two different legends of spectral black dogs, which have been linked to the Black Shuck folklore, but differ in significant aspects: local folklorist W.H. Barrett relates the story of a huge black dog haunting the area after being killed rescuing a local girl from a lustful friar in pre-reformation times,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TFF01_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-TFF01-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFT01_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-CFT01-17">[17]</a></sup> while fellow folklorist Enid Porter relates stories of a black dog haunting the <a href="/wiki/A10_road_(England)" title="A10 road (England)">A10 road</a> after its owner drowned in the nearby <a href="/wiki/River_Great_Ouse" title="River Great Ouse">River Great Ouse</a> in the 1800s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCF01_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCF01-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MC01_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MC01-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_between_Dereham_and_Swanton_Morley">Appearance between Dereham and Swanton Morley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Appearance between Dereham and Swanton Morley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>John Harries reported that in November 1945 he was followed by a black dog, which kept pace with him at speeds up to 20 mph and stopped when he stopped, as he cycled from <a href="/wiki/Dereham" title="Dereham">Dereham</a> to <a href="/wiki/RAF_Swanton_Morley" title="RAF Swanton Morley">RAF Swanton Morley</a>, where it vanished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eberhart_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-eberhart-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_exhumation">Possible exhumation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Shuck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Possible exhumation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 2014 a large dog was excavated at <a href="/wiki/Leiston_Abbey" title="Leiston Abbey">Leiston Abbey</a> and was linked to the legend of Black Shuck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Carbon dating</a> of the bones "indicated a date of either 1650-1690, 1730-1810 or post 1920" and the animal "was likely to have been interred when there was no surface trace of the original building remaining".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> East Anglian poet and songwriter, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Newell_(musician)" title="Martin Newell (musician)">Martin Newell</a>, wrote about the discovery and retold some of the stories he heard from locals while preparing his epic poem, <i>Black Shuck: The Ghost Dog of Eastern England</i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The odd thing is that people today still claim sightings of Black Dog. While researching my own project, I was surprised at how seriously the story was taken, especially in the north of the region. "Ah, you’re writing about that now, are you?" a <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> shopkeeper asked me. "Well, be careful."</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>One local woman told me that she’d seen Black Shuck early one summer morning in the 1950s, near <a href="/wiki/Cromer" title="Cromer">Cromer</a>, when returning from a dance. A <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> man said he’d seen the dog one evening on the marshes near <a href="/wiki/Felixstowe" title="Felixstowe">Felixstowe</a>. I later read an old newspaper story of an encounter in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>. The account was given of a midwife who had been cycling home after a delivery during the 1930s. One winter’s night, she claimed, she was followed by the creature through the lanes near <a href="/wiki/Tolleshunt_D%27Arcy" title="Tolleshunt D'Arcy">Tolleshunt Darcy</a>. She added that the dog was huge and no matter how fast she pedalled it seemed to effortlessly keep up with her. The apparition, which remained silent throughout, then suddenly vanished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
