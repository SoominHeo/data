<p>The <b>Shakespeare Programming Language</b> (<b>SPL</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Esoteric_programming_language" title="Esoteric programming language">esoteric programming language</a> designed by Jon Åslund and Karl Hasselström.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup> Like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chef_(programming_language)" title="Chef (programming language)">Chef programming language</a>, it is designed to make programs appear to be something other than programs; in this case, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespearean</a> plays.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_character" title="Fictional character">character</a> list in the beginning of the program declares a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stack_(data_structure)" title="Stack (data structure)">stacks</a>, naturally with names like "Romeo" and "Juliet". These characters enter into dialogue with each other in which they manipulate each other's topmost values, push and pop each other, and do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I/O" title="I/O">I/O</a>. The characters can also ask each other questions which behave as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conditional_(programming)" title="Conditional (programming)">conditional statements</a>. On the whole, the programming model is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly language</a> but much more verbose.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_in_Shakespeare">Programming in Shakespeare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Programming in Shakespeare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first line in a Shakespeare program is called the 'title'. The compiler considers anything from the first line to the first period to be a comment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dramatis_Personae">Dramatis Personae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dramatis Personae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is the section where variables are declared. Each variable can hold a signed integer value and is of the following form:</p>
<pre>
Name, Description
</pre>
<p>Where <code>Name</code> is the name of the variable and <code>Description</code> is ignored by the compiler. The compiler will only recognize names that correspond to actual Shakespearean characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_and_scenes">Acts and scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acts and scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A piece of code in Shakespeare is broken into <code>Acts</code> which contain <code>Scenes</code> in which characters (variables) interact. Each <code>Act</code> and <code>Scene</code> is numbered with a Roman numeral and serves as a <code>GOTO</code> label. Any code after the colon is considered a comment. They are written in the form:</p>
<pre>
Act I: Hamlet's insults and flattery.
Scene I: The insulting of Romeo.
</pre>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enter.2C_exit_and_exeunt">Enter, exit and exeunt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Enter, exit and exeunt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Individual lines of code generally take the form of a piece of dialogue spoken by one character to another; this is how the value of a variable (the character spoken to) is assigned, changed, or output. A character can only be addressed as "you". Thus, there must typically be exactly two characters "on stage" whenever lines are spoken: one to speak, and the other to be spoken to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup> To call a variable to the stage the <code>Enter</code> command is used with a list of one or more characters. The <code>Exit</code> command tells exactly one listed character to leave the stage. <code>Exeunt</code> calls more than one character to leave, or in the case that no characters are listed all the characters will leave the stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup> The following format is used:</p>
<pre>
[Enter Juliet]
[Enter Romeo and Juliet]
[Exit Romeo]
[Exeunt Romeo and Juliet]
[Exeunt]
</pre>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lines">Lines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lines are represented as dialogue spoken by a character, and consist of at least one sentence. Each sentence may assign a new value to a variable, direct a variable to output its value, or direct it to receive an input. Lines can also manipulate stacks or act as if/then or goto statements. A line starts with a character's name and a colon. Since this character is the speaker, the other character on stage is the variable that is addressed as "you".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Constants_and_assignment_of_values">Constants and assignment of values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Constants and assignment of values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Constants are represented by combinations of nouns and adjectives; the language recognizes a finite list of each, and both lists are separated into those having positive, negative, or neutral tone, as perceived by Åslund and Hasselström. Positive and neutral nouns have a value of 1 and negative nouns have a value of -1. Any adjective multiplies a noun by 2, and adjectives can be compounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup> Possessive pronouns are ignored by the parser, while words pertaining to basic arithmetic are recognized as operations, such as "sum", "quotient", and "cube". A sentence that assigns a value to a character starts with "You", may optionally continue with "are as [any adjective] as", and then gives the mathematical formula in nouns, adjectives, variables, and operations for the new value. Examples of such lines follow:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<pre>
Hamlet:
 You lying stupid fatherless big smelly half-witted coward!

Juliet:
 You are as villainous as the square root of Romeo!
</pre>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Input_and_output">Input and output</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Input and output">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Lines can also call for a variable to give output or receive input. "Open your heart" outputs the variable's numerical value, while "Speak your mind" outputs the corresponding ASCII character. "Listen to your heart" or "Open your mind" cause the variable to receive input from the user; the former for a number and the latter for a character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditional_statements_and_gotos">Conditional statements and gotos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conditional statements and gotos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An if/then statement is phrased as a question posed by a character. The words "as [any adjective] as" represent a test for equality, while "better" and "worse" correspond to greater than and less than, respectively. A subsequent line, starting "if so" or "if not", determines what happens in response to the truth or falsehood of the original condition. A goto statement begins "Let us", "We shall", or "We must", continues "return to" or "proceed to", and then gives an act or scene. A scene will be parsed as that scene in the current act; a goto statement cannot call a scene in a different act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup> A conditional statement to call a goto would look like this:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<pre>
Juliet:
 Am I better than you?

Hamlet:
 If so, let us proceed to scene III.
</pre>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pushing_and_popping_stacks">Pushing and popping stacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pushing and popping stacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Each variable is a <a href="/wiki/Stack_(abstract_data_type)" title="Stack (abstract data type)">stack</a>. A variable will have an integer pushed onto its stack if a line tells the character to "remember" an appropriate value, such as "Remember me", or "Remember yourself". The topmost value in the stack is popped (i.e., the variable assumes this value) if the character is told to "recall" anything; all text after this word is treated as a comment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourceforge_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-sourceforge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_code">Example code</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shakespeare_Programming_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Example code">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is the standard "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hello_World" title="Hello World">Hello World</a>" program (that outputs or displays "Hello, World!" to the user) in SPL.</p>
<pre>
<a href="/wiki/Romeo" title="Romeo">Romeo</a>, a young man with a remarkable patience.
<a href="/wiki/Juliet" title="Juliet">Juliet</a>, a likewise young woman of remarkable grace.
<a href="/wiki/Ophelia" title="Ophelia">Ophelia</a>, a remarkable woman much in dispute with Hamlet.
<a href="/wiki/Prince_Hamlet" title="Prince Hamlet">Hamlet</a>, the flatterer of Andersen Insulting A/S.
</pre>
<pre>
                   Act I: Hamlet's insults and flattery.
                   Scene I: The insulting of Romeo.
[Enter Hamlet and Romeo]
Hamlet:
You lying stupid fatherless big smelly half-witted coward! You are as
stupid as the difference between a handsome rich brave hero and thyself!
Speak your mind!
You are as brave as the sum of your fat little stuffed misused dusty
old rotten codpiece and a beautiful fair warm peaceful sunny summer's
day. You are as healthy as the difference between the sum of the
sweetest reddest rose and my father and yourself! Speak your mind!
You are as cowardly as the sum of yourself and the difference
between a big mighty proud kingdom and a horse. Speak your mind.
Speak your mind!
[Exit Romeo]
                   Scene II: The praising of Juliet.
[Enter Juliet]
Hamlet:
Thou art as sweet as the sum of the sum of Romeo and his horse and his
black cat! Speak thy mind!
[Exit Juliet]
                   Scene III: The praising of Ophelia.
[Enter Ophelia]
Hamlet:
Thou art as lovely as the product of a large rural town and my amazing
bottomless embroidered purse. Speak thy mind!
Thou art as loving as the product of the bluest clearest sweetest sky
and the sum of a squirrel and a white horse. Thou art as beautiful as
the difference between Juliet and thyself. Speak thy mind!
[Exeunt Ophelia and Hamlet]
</pre>
<pre>
                   Act II: Behind Hamlet's back.
                   Scene I: Romeo and Juliet's conversation.
[Enter Romeo and Juliet]
Romeo:
Speak your mind. You are as worried as the sum of yourself and the
difference between my small smooth hamster and my nose. Speak your
mind!
Juliet:
Speak YOUR mind! You are as bad as Hamlet! You are as small as the
difference between the square of the difference between my little pony
and your big hairy hound and the cube of your sorry little
codpiece. Speak your mind!
[Exit Romeo]
                   Scene II: Juliet and Ophelia's conversation.
[Enter Ophelia]
Juliet:
Thou art as good as the quotient between Romeo and the sum of a small
furry animal and a leech. Speak your mind!
Ophelia:
Thou art as disgusting as the quotient between Romeo and twice the
difference between a mistletoe and an oozing infected blister! Speak
your mind!
[Exeunt]
</pre>
