<p><b>Shane Black</b> (born December 16, 1961)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmref_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-filmref-1">[1]</a></sup> is an American screenwriter, director, producer and actor, known for writing the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action film</a> hit <i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon" title="Lethal Weapon">Lethal Weapon</a></i>, and his directorial debut with the film <i><a href="/wiki/Kiss_Kiss_Bang_Bang" title="Kiss Kiss Bang Bang">Kiss Kiss Bang Bang</a></i> in 2005. His acting credits include appearing in the original <i><a href="/wiki/Predator_(film)" title="Predator (film)">Predator</a></i> film, for which he is currently set to write and direct in the <a href="/wiki/Predator_(franchise)" title="Predator (franchise)">next installment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chitwood_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chitwood-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, he co-wrote and directed <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_3" title="Iron Man 3">Iron Man 3</a></i>, which as of mid-2016 ranks as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_films#Highest-grossing_films" title="List of highest-grossing films">tenth highest-grossing film worldwide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxofficemojo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-boxofficemojo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shane_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black was born and raised in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barbara_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-barbara-5">[5]</a></sup> the son of Paul and Patricia Ann Black. His father was in the printing business,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmref_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-filmref-1">[1]</a></sup> and helped Black get an interest in <a href="/wiki/Hardboiled" title="Hardboiled">hardboiled</a> fiction, such as the works of <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Spillane" title="Mickey Spillane">Mickey Spillane</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Matt_Helm" title="Matt Helm">Matt Helm</a> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After living in the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Burrell,_Pennsylvania" title="Lower Burrell, Pennsylvania">Lower Burrell</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Lebanon,_Pennsylvania" title="Mount Lebanon, Pennsylvania">Mount Lebanon, Pennsylvania</a>, his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Fullerton,_California" title="Fullerton, California">Fullerton, California</a>, during his sophomore year of high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barbara_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-barbara-5">[5]</a></sup> There he attended <a href="/wiki/Sunny_Hills_High_School" title="Sunny Hills High School">Sunny Hills High School</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> where he majored in film and theater. While Black had a long history writing comic strips, short stories, and journalism, only during his senior year did he decide to make a living from it once his classmate <a href="/wiki/Fred_Dekker" title="Fred Dekker">Fred Dekker</a> showed him a science fiction script he did for an assignment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-lat-6">[6]</a></sup> Black's older brother Terry also wrote short stories and decided to move into screenplays starting with 1988's <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Heat_(1988_film)" title="Dead Heat (1988 film)">Dead Heat</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shane_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Screenwriting_and_acting">Screenwriting and acting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shane_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Screenwriting and acting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After graduating, Black worked as a typist for a temp agency, a data entry clerk for the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a> and an usher in a <a href="/wiki/Westwood,_Los_Angeles" title="Westwood, Los Angeles">Westwood</a> movie theater. Eventually he asked for financial support of his parents during the six month development of a script, <i>The Shadow Company</i>, a supernatural thriller set in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-lat-6">[6]</a></sup> With Dekker's help, the script landed him an agent and several lunch meetings with mid-level studio executives. This attracted <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> executives, who were interested in having Black rewrite scripts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ny_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ny-9">[9]</a></sup> Eventually Black wrote an action film script, <i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon" title="Lethal Weapon">Lethal Weapon</a></i>, in about six weeks, which landed him a $250,000 deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Bros" title="Warner Bros">Warner Bros</a>. During the rewrites, Black asked producer <a href="/wiki/Joel_Silver" title="Joel Silver">Joel Silver</a> for a small acting role in another film Silver was preparing at the time, <i><a href="/wiki/Predator_(film)" title="Predator (film)">Predator</a></i>, a film for which Black also made uncredited contributions to the script. At the same time, Black helped Dekker write <i><a href="/wiki/The_Monster_Squad" title="The Monster Squad">The Monster Squad</a></i>, which along with <i>Lethal Weapon</i> and <i>Predator</i> came out in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-lat-6">[6]</a></sup> Since then, Black has acted in five additional films and in one television episode for the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Justice" title="Dark Justice">Dark Justice</a></i>.</p>
<p>Once Warner Bros. requested a <i>Lethal Weapon</i> sequel, Black wrote the first draft of <i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon_2" title="Lethal Weapon 2">Lethal Weapon 2</a></i> with the help of novelist <a href="/wiki/Warren_Murphy" title="Warren Murphy">Warren Murphy</a>. Although it wasn't used, Black said in later interviews that Warner Bros. didn't like his original script for <i>Lethal Weapon 2</i>, which was also titled <i>Play Dirty</i>, because of how dark and violent it was and due to his decision to kill off main character Martin Riggs in the ending of the script. Nevertheless, other people thought that his script was brilliant, and he himself considers it to be his best work and the best script he has ever written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Although many fans have tried to find a copy of it, Black's version of the script was never released.</p>
<p>Feeling burnt out and having conflicts with the studio, Black left the project after six months, earning only $125,000 (out of a $250,000 payment split with Murphy) for his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-lat-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ny_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ny-9">[9]</a></sup> After two sabbatical years, Black decided to take on an old idea of his that emerged during the production of <i>Lethal Weapon</i> and turn it into a full screenplay. The result, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Boy_Scout" title="The Last Boy Scout">The Last Boy Scout</a></i>, earned him $1.75 million in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ny_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ny-9">[9]</a></sup> Black would also earn $1 million for his rewrite of <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Action_Hero" title="Last Action Hero">Last Action Hero</a></i> in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He would set a record by receiving $4 million for writing <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Kiss_Goodnight" title="The Long Kiss Goodnight">The Long Kiss Goodnight</a></i> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Directing">Directing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shane_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Directing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Black made his directorial debut with 2005's <i><a href="/wiki/Kiss_Kiss_Bang_Bang" title="Kiss Kiss Bang Bang">Kiss Kiss Bang Bang</a></i>, and later directed (and co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/Drew_Pearce" title="Drew Pearce">Drew Pearce</a>) 2013's <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_3" title="Iron Man 3">Iron Man 3</a></i>, which ranks as the tenth highest-grossing film of all time worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxofficemojo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-boxofficemojo-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Black next directed the murder conspiracy thriller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nice_Guys" title="The Nice Guys">The Nice Guys</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Russell_Crowe" title="Russell Crowe">Russell Crowe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Gosling" title="Ryan Gosling">Ryan Gosling</a>, and produced by <a href="/wiki/Joel_Silver" title="Joel Silver">Joel Silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Warner Bros. handles North American rights to the film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which was released on May 20, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2014, it was also announced that Black would co-write with Fred Dekker<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chitwood_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chitwood-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and direct alone the next film in the <i><a href="/wiki/Predator_(franchise)" title="Predator (franchise)">Predator</a></i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chitwood_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chitwood-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The film will be titled <i>The Predator</i> and will be released on March 2, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Black's next projects include an adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Doc_Savage#Other_adaptations" title="Doc Savage">Doc Savage</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <i>The Destroyer</i>, based on the 1985 film <i><a href="/wiki/Remo_Williams:_The_Adventure_Begins" title="Remo Williams: The Adventure Begins">Remo Williams: The Adventure Begins</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Fred_Ward" title="Fred Ward">Fred Ward</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shane_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Black has a recognizable writing style characterized by stories in which <a href="/wiki/Buddy_film" title="Buddy film">two main characters become friends</a> and trade witty dialogue, featuring labyrinthine crime plots and set at <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmastime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW-23">[23]</a></sup> The quips he incorporates into his dialogue are referred to as "Shane Blackisms", in which jokes about the story situations are included in the scene directions of the script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He also sometimes directs comments at studio executives and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Script_reader" title="Script reader">script readers</a>. <p>This approach, which Black summed as "more open to the reader" and aimed at "trying to keep people awake", was described by himself as a combination of <a href="/wiki/William_Goldman" title="William Goldman">William Goldman</a>, his mentor in screenwriting, and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hill_(director)" title="Walter Hill (director)">Walter Hill</a>, who had a "terse and Spartan, punchy prose".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Black gave a list of techniques he uses when writing films in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has used kidnapping as a plot device in several films: <i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon" title="Lethal Weapon">Lethal Weapon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Boy_Scout" title="The Last Boy Scout">The Last Boy Scout</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Kiss_Goodnight" title="The Long Kiss Goodnight">The Long Kiss Goodnight</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kiss_Kiss_Bang_Bang" title="Kiss Kiss Bang Bang">Kiss Kiss Bang Bang</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_3" title="Iron Man 3">Iron Man 3</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nice_Guys" title="The Nice Guys">The Nice Guys</a></i>.</p>
<p>Black explains that Christmas, which has been used as a backdrop in <i>Lethal Weapon</i>, <i>Kiss Kiss Bang Bang</i>, <i>Iron Man 3</i> and <i>The Nice Guys</i>, is a touchstone for him, explaining:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-EW-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Christmas represents a little stutter in the march of days, a hush in which we have a chance to assess and retrospect our lives. I tend to think also that it just informs as a backdrop. The first time I noticed it was <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Days_of_the_Condor" title="Three Days of the Condor">Three Days of the Condor</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Pollack" title="Sydney Pollack">Sydney Pollack</a> film, where Christmas in the background adds this really odd, chilling counterpoint to the espionage plot. I also think that Christmas is just a thing of beauty, especially as it applies to places like Los Angeles, where it's not so obvious, and you have to dig for it, like little nuggets. One night, on Christmas Eve, I walked past a Mexican lunch wagon serving tacos, and I saw this little string, and on it was a little broken plastic figurine, with a light bulb inside it, of the Virgin Mary. And I thought, that's just a little hidden piece of magic. You know, all around the city are little slices, little icons of Christmas, that are as effective and beautiful in and of themselves as any 40-foot Christmas tree on the lawn of the White House. So that, in a lot of words, is the answer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-EW-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shane_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black received the Distinguished Screenwriter Award from the <a href="/wiki/Austin_Film_Festival" title="Austin Film Festival">Austin Film Festival</a> October 21, 2006. In 2005, he received the Best Original Screenplay award for <i>Kiss Kiss Bang Bang</i> from the San Diego Film Critics Association.</p>
