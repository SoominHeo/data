<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Ex post facto</i> law</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>An <b><i>ex post facto</i> law</b> (corrupted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>ex postfacto</i></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a> </small>'out of the aftermath') is a <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> that retroactively changes the legal consequences (or status) of actions that were committed, or relationships that existed, before the enactment of the law. In <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>, it may <a href="/wiki/Criminalization" title="Criminalization">criminalize</a> actions that were legal when committed; it may aggravate a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> by bringing it into a more severe category than it was in when it was committed; it may change the <a href="/wiki/Punishment" title="Punishment">punishment</a> prescribed for a crime, as by adding new penalties or extending sentences; or it may alter the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rules_of_evidence" title="Rules of evidence">rules of evidence</a> in order to make conviction for a crime likelier than it would have been when the deed was committed. Conversely, a form of <i>ex post facto</i> law commonly called an <i><a href="/wiki/Amnesty_law" title="Amnesty law">amnesty law</a></i> may decriminalize certain acts. A <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardon</a> has a similar effect, in a specific case instead of a class of cases. Other legal changes may alleviate possible punishments (for example by replacing the death sentence with lifelong imprisonment) retroactively. Such legal changes are also known by the Latin term <i><b>in mitius</b></i>.</p>
<p>A law may have an <i>ex post facto</i> effect without being technically <i>ex post facto</i>. For example, when a previous law is <a href="/wiki/Repeal" title="Repeal">repealed</a> or otherwise nullified, it is no longer applicable to situations to which it had been, even if such situations arose before the law was voided. The principle of prohibiting the continued application of such laws is called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nullum_crimen,_nulla_poena_sine_praevia_lege_poenali" title="Nullum crimen, nulla poena sine praevia lege poenali">nullum crimen, nulla poena sine praevia lege poenali</a></i>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">European Continental systems</a>. This is related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principle_of_legality" title="Principle of legality">principle of legality</a>.</p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common-law</a> <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdictions</a> do not permit retroactive criminal legislation, though new <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a> generally applies to events that occurred before the judicial decision. <i>Ex post facto</i> laws are expressly forbidden by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> in Article 1, Section 9, Clause 3 (with respect to federal laws) and Article 1, Section 10 (with respect to state laws). In some nations that follow the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_system" title="Westminster system">Westminster system</a> of government, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <i>ex post facto</i> laws are technically possible, because the doctrine of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentary_supremacy" title="Parliamentary supremacy">parliamentary supremacy</a> allows <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">Parliament</a> to pass any law it wishes. In a nation with an entrenched <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_rights" title="Bill of rights">bill of rights</a> or a written <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a>, <i>ex post facto</i> legislation may be prohibited.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ex_post_facto_laws_by_country"><i>Ex post facto</i> laws by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ex post facto laws by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> has no strong constitutional prohibition on <i>ex post facto</i> laws, although narrowly retroactive laws might violate the constitutional <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a> principle. Australian courts normally interpret statutes with a strong presumption that they do not apply retroactively.</p>
<p>Retroactive laws designed to prosecute what was perceived to have been a blatantly unethical means of <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">tax avoidance</a> were passed in the early 1980s by the <a href="/wiki/Fraser_Government" title="Fraser Government">Fraser government</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Bottom_of_the_harbour_tax_avoidance" title="Bottom of the harbour tax avoidance">Bottom of the harbour tax avoidance</a>). Similarly, the retroactive effect of legislation <a href="/wiki/Criminalization" title="Criminalization">criminalizing</a> certain war crimes retroactively have been held to be constitutional (see <i><a href="/wiki/Polyukhovich_v_Commonwealth" title="Polyukhovich v Commonwealth">Polyukhovich v Commonwealth</a></i>).</p>
<p>The government will sometimes make a press release that it intends to change the tax law with effect from the date and time of the press release, before legislation is introduced into parliament.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the 5th Article, section XXXVI of the Brazilian Constitution, laws cannot have "ex post facto" effects that affect acquired rights, accomplished juridical acts and <i>res judicata</i>.</p>
<p>The same article in section XL prohibits <i>ex post facto</i> <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal laws</a>. Like France, there is an exception when retroactive criminal laws benefit the accused person.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <i>ex post facto</i> criminal laws are constitutionally prohibited by <a href="/wiki/Section_11_of_the_Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Section 11 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms">paragraph 11(g)</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Charter of Rights and Freedoms">Charter of Rights and Freedoms</a>. Also, under paragraph 11(i) of the Charter, if the punishment for a crime has varied between the time the crime was committed and the time of sentencing following a conviction, the convicted person is entitled to the lesser punishment.</p>
<p>Because section 11 of the Charter is among the sections that can be overridden under <a href="/wiki/Section_33_of_the_Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Section 33 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms">section 33</a> (the notwithstanding clause), Parliament could in theory enact <i>ex post facto</i> laws by invoking section 33. However, the federal Parliament (which has the sole power to enact laws punishable for violation by two years or more in penitentiary) has never attempted to enact an <i>ex post facto</i> law (or any other law) using section 33.</p>
<p>It should be emphasized that the Charter prohibition applies only to criminal law. Changes to <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil law</a> in Canada can be, and occasionally are, enacted <i>ex post facto</i>. In one example, convicted murderer <a href="/wiki/Colin_Thatcher" title="Colin Thatcher">Colin Thatcher</a> was ordered to forfeit proceeds from a book he had published (after being paroled from prison) under a <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> law. Although the law was passed long after Thatcher's murder conviction, the courts have ruled that such laws prescribe only <a href="/wiki/Civil_penalty" title="Civil penalty">civil penalties</a> (as opposed to additional criminal penalties) and are thus not subject to Charter restrictions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally, the Finnish legal system does not permit <i>ex post facto</i> laws, especially those that would expand criminal responsibility. They are not expressly forbidden but instead the ban is derived from more general legal principles and basic rights. In civil matters, such as taxation, <i>ex post facto</i> laws may be made in some circumstances.</p>
<p>However, there have been three exceptional instances when <i>ex post facto</i> criminal laws have been used in Finland.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Civil_War" title="Finnish Civil War">Finnish Civil War</a> of 1918, the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Finland" title="Parliament of Finland">Parliament of Finland</a> passed a law setting up tribunals to try suspected rebels. These tribunals issued death sentences in many cases, although very few of those accused could have committed a crime that carried the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Finland" title="Capital punishment in Finland">death penalty</a> under Finnish law in force during the war. Several hundred people were executed under what was arguably an <i>ex post facto</i> legal arrangement. During the war, and before the tribunals were set up, thousands of people had been executed without trial by both sides.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Finland was under pressure to convict political leaders whom the Allied powers considered responsible for Finnish involvement in the war. An <i>ex post facto</i> law was passed in the autumn of 1945 to permit <a href="/wiki/War-responsibility_trials_in_Finland" title="War-responsibility trials in Finland">prosecution for war responsibility</a>, and eventually eight politicians were convicted.</p>
<p>In another post-war case, the <a href="/wiki/Weapons_Cache_Case" title="Weapons Cache Case">weapons cache case</a>, an <i>ex post facto</i> law was passed in 1947 so that military personnel could be prosecuted for unofficially preparing for guerrilla resistance in case of Soviet occupation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, so-called "<i>lois rétroactives</i>" (retroactive laws) are technically prohibited by Article 2 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_Civil" title="Code Civil">Code Civil</a>, which states that: "Legislation provides only for the future; it has no retrospective operation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In practice however, since the Code Civil does not have the status of constitutional legislation and can therefore be overruled by subsequent laws, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conseil_Constitutionnel" title="Conseil Constitutionnel">Conseil Constitutionnel</a> has determined that retroactive laws can be passed within certain limits – such as in the case of financial or tax legislation –, particularly where it is considered to be in the "general interest"; this has been demonstrated by a series of decisions handed down by the Conseil Constitutionnel concerning retroactive tax laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, in criminal law, <i>ex post facto</i> sanctions are effectively forbidden as per Article 112-1 of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=French_Penal_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="French Penal Code (page does not exist)">French Penal Code</a>, except in cases wherein the retroactive application benefits the accused person (called retroactivity "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_mitius" title="In mitius">in mitius</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soyer_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soyer-3">[3]</a></sup> They are also considered unconstitutional, since the principle of non-retroactivity is laid down in Article 8 of the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen" title="Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen">Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen</a>, which has constitutional status under French law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Terr.C3.A9_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Terr.C3.A9-4">[4]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89puration_l%C3%A9gale" title="Épuration légale">épuration légale</a></i> trials held after the 1944 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberation_of_France" title="Liberation of France">liberation of France</a> introduced the status of <i><a href="/wiki/Indignit%C3%A9_nationale" title="Indignité nationale">indignité nationale</a></i> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_collaborators" title="Nazi collaborators">Nazi collaborators</a> as a way to avoid ex port facto law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 103 of the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">German basic law</a> requires that an act may be punished only if it has already been punishable by law at the time it was committed (specifically: by <i>written</i> law, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> following civil law).</p>
<p>Some scholars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> and politicians like <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Taft" title="Robert A. Taft">Robert A. Taft</a>, at the time U.S. Senator from Ohio, asserted that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" title="Nuremberg Trials">Nuremberg Trials</a> following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> were based on <i>ex post facto</i> law, because the Allies did not negotiate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Charter_of_the_International_Military_Tribunal" title="London Charter of the International Military Tribunal">London Charter</a>, defining crimes against humanity and creating the International Military Tribunal, until well after the acts charged. Others, including the International Military Tribunal, argued that the London Charter merely restated and provided jurisdiction to prosecute offenses that were already made unlawful by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kellogg-Briand_Pact" title="Kellogg-Briand Pact">Kellogg-Briand Pact</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Covenant_of_the_League_of_Nations" title="Covenant of the League of Nations">Covenant of the League of Nations</a>, and the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_(1899_and_1907)" title="Hague Conventions (1899 and 1907)">Hague Conventions</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The problem of <i>ex post facto</i> law was also relevant in the 1990s as there was a discussion about the trials against East German soldiers who killed fugitives on the Inner-German border (<a href="/wiki/Schie%C3%9Fbefehl#After_reunification" title="Schießbefehl"><i>Mauerschützen-Prozesse</i></a> - <i>Wall-shooters'/ -guards' trials</i>). German courts in these cases recurred to the <a href="/wiki/Radbruch_formula" title="Radbruch formula">Radbruch formula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, the parliament established a 98% punitive tax on any income over two million forints received either as a retirement package or as severance pay in the last five years in the government sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In India, without using the expression "<i>ex post facto law</i>", the underlying principle has been adopted in the article 20(1) of the Indian Constitution in the following words:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>No person shall be convicted of any offence except for violation of a law in force at the time of the commission of the act charged as an offence, nor be subjected to a penalty greater than that which have been inflicted under the law in force at the time of commission of the offence.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Further, what article 20(1) prohibits is conviction and sentence under an <i>ex post facto</i> law for acts done prior thereto, but not the enactment or validity of such a law. There is, thus, a difference between the Indian and the American positions on this point; whereas in the United States, an <i>ex post facto</i> law is in itself invalid, it is not so in India. The courts may also interpret a law in such a manner that any objection against it of retrospective operation may be removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>An example for retrospective law in India is the Karnataka Schedule Caste and Scheduled Tribes (Prohibition of Transfer of Certain Lands) Act, 1978<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> constitution prohibits trying citizens under retroactive laws in any circumstance. This was tested in 2004 when the conviction of one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2002_Bali_terrorist_bombing" title="2002 Bali terrorist bombing">Bali bombers</a> under retroactive anti-terrorist legislation was quashed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ex post facto</i> laws, in all contexts, are prohibited by Article 169 (Chapter 11) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Islamic_Republic_of_Iran" title="Constitution of Islamic Republic of Iran">Iran's constitution</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The imposition of retroactive criminal sanctions is prohibited by Article 15.5.1° of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">constitution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Retroactive changes of the civil law have also been found to violate the constitution when they would have resulted in the loss in a right to damages before the courts, the Irish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_(Ireland)" title="Supreme Court (Ireland)">Supreme Court</a> having found that such a right is a constitutionally protected property right.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> enacted the 1950 "Nazi and Nazi Collaborators (Punishment) Law" for the purpose of punishing acts that occurred during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, when Israel did not exist as a state. The law was used to punish <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Eichmann" title="Adolf Eichmann">Adolf Eichmann</a> and others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 25, paragraph 2, of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy" title="Constitution of Italy">Italian Constitution</a>, establishing that "nobody can be punished but according to a law come into force before the deed was committed", prohibits indictment pursuant a retroactive law. Article 11 of preliminary provisions to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_civil_code" title="Italian civil code">Italian Civil Code</a> and Article 3, paragraph 1, of the Statute of taxpayer's rights, prohibit retroactive laws on principle: such provisions can be derogated, however, by acts having force of the ordinary law; on the contrary, non-retroactivity in criminal law is thought absolute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 39 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Japan" title="Constitution of Japan">constitution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> prohibits the retroactive application of laws. Article 6 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_Code_of_Japan" title="Criminal Code of Japan">Criminal Code of Japan</a> further states that if a new law comes into force after the deed was committed, the lighter punishment must be given.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Lithuania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lithuania has no constitutional prohibition on <i>ex post facto</i> laws. Retroactive criminal sanctions are prohibited by Article 2, Part 1 (Chapter 1) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_Code" title="Criminal Code">Criminal Code</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Lithuania" title="Republic of Lithuania">Republic of Lithuania</a>. Retroactive administrative sanctions are prohibited by Article 8 of the Administrative Code of the Republic of Lithuania.</p>
<p>Lithuanian lawyer Dainius Žalimas contends that there has been retroactive application of the law on Genocide (and subsequently adopted articles of the Criminal Code) against participants in Soviet repressions against Lithuanian guerilla fighters and their supporters, and gives examples of such decisions. The Article 99 of the Criminal Code of the Republic of Lithuania was introduced only in September 26, 2000 and therefore can't be used in events of 1944-1953.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the first and second paragraphs of the Article 14th of the Mexican Constitution, retroactive application of the law is prohibited if it is on detriment of a person rights. But the new law can be applied if it benefits the person.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 4 of the Law on General Provisions (in effect since 1838) states that "The law has no retroactive effect".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Article 1 of Criminal Law states that no act is punishable without a pre-existing law, and that in the case an act was punishable but the law was changed after the criminal act the "most favorable" (to the suspect) of the two laws will apply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Civil Law there is no such provision.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Section 7 of the Interpretation Act 1999 stipulates that enactments do not have retrospective effect. The <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Bill_of_Rights_Act_1990" title="New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990">New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990</a> also affirms New Zealand's commitment to the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>, with section 26 preventing the application of retroactive penalties. This is further reinforced under section 6(1) of the current Sentencing Act 2002 which provides, "[p]enal enactments not to have retrospective effect to disadvantage of offender" irrespective of any provision to the contrary.</p>
<p>Section 26 of the Bill of Rights and the previous sentencing legislation, the Criminal Justice Act 1985, caused significant digression among judges when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_New_Zealand" title="Parliament of New Zealand">New Zealand Parliament</a> introduced legislation that had the effect of enacting a retrospective penalty for crimes involving an element of home invasion. Ultimately, the discrepancy was restricted with what some labelled artificial logic in the cases of <i>R v Pora</i> and <i>R v Poumako</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 97 of the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> constitution prohibits any law to be given retroactive effect. The prohibition applies to both criminal and civil laws, but in some civil cases, only particularly unreasonable effects of retroactivity will be found unconstitutional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 12 of the constitution of Pakistan prohibits any law to be given retroactive effect by stating:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>12.1 - No law shall authorize the punishment of a person:-</li>
<li>12.1.a - for an act or omission that was not punishable by law at the time of the act or omission; or</li>
<li>12.1.b - for an offence by a penalty greater than, or of a kind different from, the penalty prescribed by law for that offence at the time the offence was committed.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1987 Constitution of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> categorically prohibits the passing of any <i>ex post facto</i> law. Article III (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights" title="Bill of Rights">Bill of Rights</a>), Section 22 specifically states: "No <i>ex post facto</i> law or <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_attainder" title="Bill of attainder">bill of attainder</a> shall be enacted."</p>
<p>However, the <a href="/wiki/Cybercrime_Prevention_Act_of_2012" title="Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012">Cybercrime Prevention Act</a>, which went into effect on October 3, 2012, is criticized for being <i>ex post facto</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Retroactive application of law is prohibited by the Article 3 of the Polish <a href="/wiki/Civil_code" title="Civil code">civil code</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_rule" title="Legal rule">legal rule</a> prohibiting such retroactive application is commonly memorised as a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> sentence <i>Lex retro non agit</i> ("A law does not apply retroactively"). The said article, however, allows retroactive application of an Act of Parliament if it is expressly understood from its text or purpose.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 18 of the Portuguese constitution forbids the retroactive application of any law the restricts right; article 29 of the Portuguese Constitution forbids retroactive application of criminal law; article 103 forbids the application of retroactive taxes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">Romania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Romania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 15 (2) of the Romanian constitution provides that the law shall only act for the future, except for the more favourable criminal or administrative law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ex post facto</i> punishment in criminal and administrative law is prohibited by article 54 of the constitution; <i>ex post facto</i> tax laws by article 57 of the constitution.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 9.3 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution" title="Spanish Constitution">Spanish Constitution</a> guarantees the principle of non-retroactivity of punitive provisions that are not favorable to or restrictive of individual rights. Therefore, "ex post facto" criminal laws or any other retroactive punitive provisions are constitutionally prohibited.</p>
<p>As well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statute_law" title="Statute law">Statute law</a> mentioned above, this now also includes '<a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">court-made law</a>'. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parot_doctrine_(Spain)" title="Parot doctrine (Spain)">Parot doctrine</a>, in which terrorists were denied the right (enshrined in a 1973 Statute) to earn a reduction in the length of their sentences by a Spanish court ruling in 2006 was judged by the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> to be contrary to relevant articles on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECHR#Article_7_-_retroactivity" title="ECHR">retroactivity</a> &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECHR#Article_5_-_liberty_and_security" title="ECHR">liberty and security</a> in 2013.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Section 35(3) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_African_Bill_of_Rights" title="South African Bill of Rights">South African Bill of Rights</a> prohibits <i>ex post facto</i> criminal laws, except that acts which violated international law at the time they were committed may be prosecuted even if they were not illegal under national law at the time. It also prohibits retroactive increases of criminal punishments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, retroactive penal sanctions and other retroactive legal effects of criminal acts due the State are prohibited by chapter 2, section 10 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Sweden#Instrument_of_Government" title="Constitution of Sweden">Instrument of Government</a> (<i>Regeringsformen</i>). Retroactive taxes or charges are not prohibited, but they can have retroactive effect reaching back only to the time when a new tax bill was proposed by the government. The retroactive effect of a tax or charge thus reaches from that time until the bill is passed by the parliament.</p>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Act_of_Succession" title="Swedish Act of Succession">Swedish Act of Succession</a> was changed in 1979, and the throne was inherited regardless of sex, the inheritance right was withdrawn from all the descendants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_XIV_John" title="Charles XIV John">Charles XIV John</a> (king 1818-44) except the current king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_XVI_Gustaf" title="Carl XVI Gustaf">Carl XVI Gustaf</a>. Thereby, the heir-apparent title was transferred from the new-born <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Carl_Philip" title="Prince Carl Philip">Prince Carl Philip</a> to his older sister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Princess_Victoria" title="Crown Princess Victoria">Crown Princess Victoria</a>.</p>
<p>The Swedish Parliament voted in 2004 to abolish <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_tax" title="Inheritance tax">inheritance tax</a> by January 1, 2005. However, in 2005 they retro-actively decided to move the date to December 17, 2004. The main reason was abolishing inheritance tax for the many Swedish victims of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">2004 Indian Ocean earthquake</a>, which took place on December 26.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Ex post facto</i> punishment is prohibited by Article 38 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Turkey" title="Constitution of Turkey">Constitution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. It states:</p>
<ul>
<li>c1. No one shall be punished for any act which does not constitute a criminal offence under the law in force at the time committed; no one shall be given a heavier penalty for an offence other than the penalty applicable at the time when the offence was committed.</li>
<li>c2. The provisions of the above paragraph shall also apply to the statute of limitations on offences and penalties and on the results of conviction.</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, the article does not prohibit <i>in mitius</i> laws, i.e. cases wherein the retroactive application benefits the accused person.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <i>ex post facto</i> laws are frowned upon, but are permitted by virtue of the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>. Historically, all acts of Parliament before 1793 were <i>ex post facto</i> legislation, inasmuch as their date of effect was the first day of the session in which they were passed. This situation was rectified by the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament_(Commencement)_Act_1793" title="Acts of Parliament (Commencement) Act 1793">Acts of Parliament (Commencement) Act 1793</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some laws are still passed retrospectively: e.g., the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pakistan_Act_1990&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pakistan Act 1990 (page does not exist)">Pakistan Act 1990</a> (by which the United Kingdom amended its legislation consequent to the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> having re-admitted Pakistan as a member) was one such law; despite being passed on 29 June 1990, section 2 subsection 3 states that "This Act shall be deemed to have come into force on 1st October 1989", nine months before it was enacted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NA_PA1990_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NA_PA1990-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Retrospective <i>criminal</i> laws are prohibited by Article 7 of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, to which the United Kingdom is a signatory, but several noted legal authorities have stated their opinion that parliamentary sovereignty takes priority even over this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/War_Crimes_Act_1991" title="War Crimes Act 1991">War Crimes Act 1991</a> created an <i>ex post facto</i> jurisdiction of British courts over <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crimes</a> committed during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. Another important example of a case which shows the doctrine of parliamentary supremacy in action is <a href="/wiki/Burmah_Oil_Co_Ltd_v_Lord_Advocate" title="Burmah Oil Co Ltd v Lord Advocate">Burmah Oil Co Ltd v Lord Advocate</a>, which retrospectively changed the law on compensation resulting from <a href="/wiki/Scorched_earth" title="Scorched earth">scorched earth</a> actions in Burma during the war. More recently, the <a href="/wiki/Police_(Detention_and_Bail)_Act_2011" title="Police (Detention and Bail) Act 2011">Police (Detention and Bail) Act 2011</a> retroactively overrode a controversial court judgment resulting from an error in the drafting of the <a href="/wiki/Police_and_Criminal_Evidence_Act_1984" title="Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984">Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984</a> that would potentially have invalidated thousands of criminal convictions.</p>
<p>Taxation law has on multiple occasions been changed to retrospectively disallow <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">tax avoidance</a> schemes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The most significant example known concerns Double-taxation Treaty Arrangements where the <a href="/wiki/Finance_Act_2008" title="Finance Act 2008">Finance Act 2008</a> with <a href="/wiki/BN66" title="BN66">BN66</a> retrospectively amended 1987 legislation creating large tax liabilities for 3,000 people where no liability existed before.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the Congress is prohibited from passing <i>ex post facto</i> laws by clause 3 of <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution#Section_9:_Limits_on_Congress" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Section 9</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. The states are prohibited from passing <i>ex post facto</i> laws by clause 1 of <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution#Section_10:_Limits_on_the_States" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Section 10</a>. This is one of the relatively few restrictions that the United States Constitution made to both the power of the federal and state governments before the <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourteenth Amendment</a>. Over the years, when deciding <i>ex post facto</i> cases, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">United States Supreme Court</a> has referred repeatedly to its ruling in <i><a href="/wiki/Calder_v._Bull" title="Calder v. Bull">Calder v. Bull</a></i>, in which Justice <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Chase" title="Samuel Chase">Samuel Chase</a> held that the prohibition applied only to criminal matters, not civil matters, and established four categories of unconstitutional <i>ex post facto</i> laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The case dealt with the Article I, Section 10, prohibition on <i>ex post facto</i> laws, because it concerned a Connecticut state law.</p>
<p>Not all laws with retroactive effects have been held to be unconstitutional. One current U.S. law that has a retroactive effect is the <a href="/wiki/Adam_Walsh_Child_Protection_and_Safety_Act" title="Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act">Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act</a> of 2006. This law imposes new registration requirements on convicted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_offenders" title="Sex offenders">sex offenders</a> and also applies to offenders whose crimes were committed before the law was enacted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in <i><a href="/wiki/Smith_v._Doe" title="Smith v. Doe">Smith v. Doe</a></i> (2003) that forcing sex offenders to register their whereabouts at regular intervals, and the posting of personal information about them on the Internet, do not violate the constitutional prohibition against <i>ex post facto</i> laws, because these laws do not impose any kind of punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Starkey v. Oklahoma Department Of Corrections</i>, the Supreme Court of the State of Oklahoma found the Oklahoma Sex Offender Registration Act, or SORA, to be punitive in nature, if not in intent. While the law in question has been ruled as not being retroactive in nature, the Oklahoma Department of Corrections had been applying the new legislation retroactively, and "also find the Department's retroactive application of the level assignment provisions of 57 O.S. Supp. 2007, 582.1 - 582.5, as amended, violates the ex post facto clause."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Controversy has also arisen with regard to <a href="/wiki/Sexually_violent_predator_laws" title="Sexually violent predator laws">sexually violent predator (SVP) laws</a>, which allow the indefinite commitment of a person with a mental abnormality which predisposes them to molest children. This issue arose in the case <i><a href="/wiki/Kansas_v._Hendricks" title="Kansas v. Hendricks">Kansas v. Hendricks</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendricks_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hendricks-25">[25]</a></sup> In <i>Hendricks</i>, a man with a long history of sexually molesting children was scheduled to be released from prison shortly after the enactment of Kansas's SVP act. Rather than being released, he was committed on the grounds that he had a mental abnormality. Hendricks contested the law on <i>ex post facto</i> and double jeopardy grounds. The Supreme Court of Kansas invalidated the Act, but the Supreme Court of the United States reversed the decision and ruled that the law was constitutional on the basis that the law did not impose a criminal punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendricks_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hendricks-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_Violence_Offender_Gun_Ban" title="Domestic Violence Offender Gun Ban">Domestic Violence Offender Gun Ban</a>, where firearms prohibitions were imposed on those convicted of misdemeanor domestic-violence offenses and on subjects of restraining orders (which do not require criminal conviction). These individuals can now be sentenced to up to ten years in a <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Prisons" title="Federal Bureau of Prisons">federal prison</a> for possession of a firearm, regardless of whether the weapon was legally possessed when the law was passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The law has been legally upheld because it is considered regulatory, not punitive; it is a <a href="/wiki/Status_offense" title="Status offense">status offense</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, in <i>Calder v. Bull</i>, the court expressly stated that a law that "mollifies" a criminal act was merely retrospective, and was not an <i>ex post facto</i> law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Scholars have argued that, as a historical matter, the phrase <i>ex post facto</i> referred to civil as well as criminal laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/United_States_administrative_law" title="United States administrative law">administrative law</a>, federal agencies may apply their rules retroactively if Congress has authorized them to; otherwise, retroactive application is generally prohibited. Retroactive application of regulations is disfavored by the courts for several reasons. The courts uphold retroactive regulation where Congress has expressly granted such retroactive power to the agency, as they did in <i>Bowen v. Georgetown University Hospital</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rules as they relate to the effects of <i>ex post facto</i> upon the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Federal_Sentencing_Guidelines" title="United States Federal Sentencing Guidelines">U.S. Federal Sentencing Guidelines</a> can be found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S.S.G." title="U.S.S.G.">U.S.S.G.</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.ussc.gov/Guidelines/2012_Guidelines/Manual_HTML/1b1_11.htm" rel="nofollow">§ 1B1.11</a> (2012).</p>
<p>See also <i><a href="/wiki/Bouie_v._City_of_Columbia" title="Bouie v. City of Columbia">Bouie v. City of Columbia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rogers_v._Tennessee" title="Rogers v. Tennessee">Rogers v. Tennessee</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stogner_v._California" title="Stogner v. California">Stogner v. California</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Austria_v._Altmann" title="Republic of Austria v. Altmann">Republic of Austria v. Altmann</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/James_Bamford" title="James Bamford">James Bamford</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Samuels_v._McCurdy" title="Samuels v. McCurdy">Samuels v. McCurdy</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment_by_international_organizations_and_treaties">Treatment by international organizations and treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Treatment by international organizations and treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_criminal_law">International criminal law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: International criminal law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/International_criminal_law" title="International criminal law">international criminal law</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg trials</a> prosecuted <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crimes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> perpetrated in World War II. Although the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Charter" title="Nuremberg Charter">Nuremberg Charter</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Procedural_law" title="Procedural law">procedural law</a> under which the trials were held, postdated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-E_Day" title="V-E Day">V-E Day</a>, the tribunal rejected the defence that the criminal law was <i>ex post facto</i>, arguing it derived from earlier treaties like the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_of_1899_and_1907" title="Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907">Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a> established in 2002 cannot prosecute crimes committed before 2002.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights_and_related_treaties">Universal Declaration of Human Rights and related treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Universal Declaration of Human Rights and related treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 11, paragraph 2 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> provides that no person be held guilty of any criminal law that did not exist at the time of offence nor suffer any penalty heavier than what existed at the time of offence. It does however permit application of either domestic or international law.</p>
<p>Very similar provisions are found in Article 15, paragraph 1 of the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a>, replacing the term "penal offence" with "criminal offence". It also adds that if a lighter penalty is provided for after the offence occurs, that lighter penalty shall apply retroactively. Paragraph 2 adds a provision that paragraph 1 does not prevent trying and punishing for an act that was criminal according to the general principles of law recognized by the community of nations. Specifically addressing the use of the death penalty, article 6, paragraph 2 provides in relevant part that a death sentence may only be imposed "for the most serious crimes in accordance with the law in force at the time of the commission of the crime".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples.27_Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 2, paragraph 7 of the <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</a> provides in part that "[n]o one may be condemned for an act or omission which did not constitute a legally punishable offense at the time it was committed. No penalty may be inflicted for an offense for which no provision was made at the time it was committed."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Declaration_of_the_Rights_and_Duties_of_Man">American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 25 of the <a href="/wiki/American_Declaration_of_the_Rights_and_Duties_of_Man" title="American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man">American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man</a> provides in part that "[n]o person may be deprived of his liberty except in the cases and according to the procedures established by pre-existing law." The right to be tried in accordance to "pre-existing law" is reiterated in article 26.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_Charter_on_Human_Rights">Arab Charter on Human Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Arab Charter on Human Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 15 of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Charter_on_Human_Rights" title="Arab Charter on Human Rights">Arab Charter on Human Rights</a> provides that "[n]o crime and no penalty can be established without a prior provision of the law. In all circumstances, the law most favorable to the defendant shall be applied."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Convention_on_Human_Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: European Convention on Human Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Effectively all European states (except <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>), including all <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> states, are bound by the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. <a href="/wiki/Article_7_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 7 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 7 of the convention</a> mirrors the language of both paragraphs of Article 15 of the International Covenant on Political and Civil Rights, with the exception that it does not include that a subsequent lighter penalty must apply.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_form_and_usage">Grammatical form and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_post_facto_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Grammatical form and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phrase may not appear to be grammatically correct in Latin if it is interpreted to comprise the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">preposition</a> <i>ex</i>, the preposition <i>post</i>, and a <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a> with the wrong <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical case</a> to agree with <i>post</i>. Indeed, the <i>Digesta Iustiniani</i> (15.3.10.8.3, 20.1.22.pr2, etc.) shows a two-word phrase, <i>ex postfacto</i>: <i>out of a postfactum (an after-deed)</i>, or more naturally, <i>from a law passed afterward</i>. This same work, however, also makes use of the three-word phrase <i>ex post facto</i>, (2.14.17.4.2, 4.6.17.1.1, <i>passim</i>), suggesting that <i>post</i> might best be understood as an adverb. This adverbial usage of <i>post</i> is not at all unusual. For example, the Classical author/advocate/politician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_Tullius_Cicero" title="Marcus Tullius Cicero">Marcus Tullius Cicero</a> employs phrases such as <i>multis post annis</i> (<i>De Re Publica</i> 2.5.8 and elsewhere).</p>
<p>Therefore, <i>ex post facto</i> or <i>ex postfacto</i> is natively an adverbial phrase, a usage demonstrated by the sentence "He was convicted <i>ex post facto</i> (from a law passed after his crime)." The law itself would rightfully be a <i>postfactum</i> law (<i>lex postfacta</i>); nevertheless, despite its redundant or circular nature, the phrase <i>an ex post facto law</i> is used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
