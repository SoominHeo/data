<p><i><b>Sonatine</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ソナチネ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>Sonachine</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a 1993 Japanese <a href="/wiki/Yakuza_film" title="Yakuza film">yakuza film</a> directed, written and edited by <a href="/wiki/Takeshi_Kitano" title="Takeshi Kitano">Takeshi Kitano</a>, who also stars in the film. It won numerous awards and became one of Kitano's most successful and praised films, garnering him a sizable international fan base.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonatine_(1993_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kitano plays Murakawa, a <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Yakuza" title="Yakuza">yakuza</a> enforcer who has grown tired of gangster life. He is sent by his boss to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a>, supposedly to mediate a dispute between their allies, the Nakamatsu and Anan clans. Murakawa openly suspects the assignment is an attempt to have him removed and even beats up one of his colleagues, Takahashi, whom he distrusts, but ends up going with his men. He finds that the dispute is insignificant, and while wondering why he was sent to Okinawa at all, the group's temporary headquarters are bombed and they are then ambushed in a bar, leaving several of his men dead.</p>
<p>Fleeing to the seaside, the survivors take refuge in a remote beach house belonging to a brother of one of the Nakamatsu members and decide to wait for the trouble to blow over. Whilst spending time at the beach, the group engages in childish games and pranks and begins to enjoy themselves. However, the games frequently have a violent undertone. When two of his men alternate shooting at a beer can on each other's head, Murakawa turns it into a game of <a href="/wiki/Russian_roulette" title="Russian roulette">Russian roulette</a>. Putting the seemingly loaded gun to his head, he pulls the trigger on the last chamber. The chamber is revealed to be empty and Murakawa is unharmed.</p>
<p>Murakawa later dreams of the Russian roulette game, although in his dream, the revolver is loaded and he is killed. When he wakes up, he walks down to the shore. He sees a car pull up, and a man drags a woman into the sand and attempts to rape her. Murakawa stoically watches for a while and then walks past them. When the man realizes Murakawa has been there the whole time and shouts at him, Murakawa headbutts him. The man pulls out a knife and threatens Murakawa. Murakawa then shoots the man, but to his companions, he claims the woman shot him. She then joins Murakawa and the gang at the beach house, and comes frequently to visit, spending time with Murakawa.</p>
<p>Later, an assassin disguised as a fisherman appears. He kills several people, including the boss of the Nakamatsu clan and one of Murakawa's men, in the middle of a frisbee match. Learning that Takahashi is arriving in Okinawa, Murakawa and two of his surviving men visit his hotel. Unable to find him at first, they unexpectedly run into Takahashi and the assassin in the elevator, which results in a shootout, killing the assassin and Murakawa's men. Murakawa learns from interrogating Takahashi that their boss had intended all along to partner with the Anan clan and had sent Murakawa on a suicide mission to take over his turf. He also learns that the boss will be meeting with the Anan that night in a hotel. Takahashi is killed and Murakawa sets off with the only survivor of the group, a member of the Nakamatsu clan, who helps him by rigging the electricity in the hotel to go off at a certain time. Murakawa tells the woman that he may come back, and the woman promises to wait for him.</p>
<p>Later that night, while waiting for all the yakuza to arrive, the Nakamatsu member asks Murakawa to take him with him, but admits that he has had enough when Murakawa asks. When the electricity goes off, Murakawa goes into the hotel and slaughters both clans with an assault rifle. The next morning, while the woman continues to wait for him, Murakawa drives to a spot near the beach and commits <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> by shooting himself in the head. The scene then switches to the car and the horizon and slowly fades.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonatine_(1993_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was conceived with four basic scenes; Yakuza having to go to Okinawa, Yakuza arriving in Okinawa, the machine-gun shootout, and the main character shooting himself in the head. Kitano said his shooting technique is spontaneous in that he allowed the film to fill in the space between these four scenes itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The title <i>Sonatine</i> comes from the musical term <a href="/wiki/Sonatina" title="Sonatina">sonatina</a>. Kitano said that when learning the piano, when the learner gets to sonatinas they have to decide where they want to go, whether it is to classical, jazz or popular music; marking the point of crucial decision making. This refers to the character Murakawa in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_is_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-what_is-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's poster is of a <a href="/wiki/Humphead_wrasse" title="Humphead wrasse">Napoleon fish</a> being pierced with a spear. Kitano said this type of fish used to be very common in the oceans south of Japan, but has been decreasing. He used the image simply because the contrast of the "beautifully shaped" fish being speared seemed striking to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_is_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-what_is-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonatine_(1993_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Sonatine</i> was a commercial failure upon its initial release in Japan, however, Western critical reception has been generally positive. Review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reports that 88% of 25 professional critics have given the film a positive review, with a rating average of 7.4 out of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>, who gave the film three and a half out of four stars, said it shows that gangster films do not need to have "stupid dialogue, nonstop action and gratuitous gore" and that it reminded him of <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Samoura%C3%AF" title="Le Samouraï">Le Samouraï</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Rob Mackie called it "a largely peaceful, contemplative work, punctuated by moments of extreme violence" and gave it four out of five stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Scott Tobias of <a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a> summed the movie up as "a fresh take on the age-old yakuza genre that's infused by odd flourishes of style and playfulness, and jarring outbursts of humor and violence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a></i> named <i>Sonatine</i> second on their list of The 25 Best Yakuza Movies, behind only <i><a href="/wiki/Battles_Without_Honor_and_Humanity" title="Battles Without Honor and Humanity">Battles Without Honor and Humanity</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Jasper Sharp, writing for the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>, listed it as one of the 10 great Japanese gangster movies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's theatrical release in Japan was a commercial failure as Kitano was only perceived as a popular <a href="/wiki/Owarai" title="Owarai">owarai comedian</a>, and the audience was not prepared, nor capable, to accept him as a credible gangster noir character. However, with Kitano not yet famous abroad, the film benefited from this different situation, especially in the European market.</p>

<p><i>Sonatine</i> was screened in the <a href="/wiki/Un_Certain_Regard" title="Un Certain Regard">Un Certain Regard</a> section at the <a href="/wiki/1993_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1993 Cannes Film Festival">1993 Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-festival-cannes.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-festival-cannes.com-12">[12]</a></sup> French publisher and notorious movie-goer, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Dionnet" title="Jean-Pierre Dionnet">Jean-Pierre Dionnet</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canal_%2B" title="Canal +">Canal +</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_Canal" title="Studio Canal">Studio Canal</a>), reported in an interview, that someone convinced <a href="/wiki/Alain_Delon" title="Alain Delon">Alain Delon</a> to watch <i>Sonatine</i> arguing that Kitano was a fan of <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Samoura%C3%AF" title="Le Samouraï">Le Samouraï</a></i>. Delon was taken aback, and talking about Kitano's acting, said "What's THAT? [...], this is not an actor [...], he only has three facial expressions and he almost doesn't talk on top of this." Most professionals around Dionnet had the same reaction, but the French publisher was both struck and puzzled by this new genre. He contacted the Japanese distributor in order to buy the license for <i>Sonatine</i>, but his request was rejected. Dionnet had to insist for several months to finally discover that the <a href="/wiki/Shochiku" title="Shochiku">Shochiku</a> didn't want to release <i>Sonatine</i> abroad, claiming the film was "too Japanese" and would not be accepted, nor understood, by western audiences. Eventually Dionnet learned that the distributor didn't want to release the license because of its commercial failure in Japan. Dionnet had an agreement with the Shochiku arguing that the French audience did not know Kitano's career and would accept his violent character more easily. He bought <i>Sonatine</i> and three additional Kitano films, <i><a href="/wiki/Violent_Cop_(1989_film)" title="Violent Cop (1989 film)">Violent Cop</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boiling_Point_(1990_film)" title="Boiling Point (1990 film)">Boiling Point</a></i> and the latest, <i><a href="/wiki/Kids_Return" title="Kids Return">Kids Return</a></i>, all of which performed poorly in Japan. In 1995, <i>Sonatine</i> entered the 13th <a href="/wiki/Festival_du_Film_Policier_de_Cognac" title="Festival du Film Policier de Cognac">Festival du Film Policier de Cognac</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, where it was critically acclaimed. <i>Sonatine</i>, followed by the three other films were broadcast on the French channel Canal+ a few months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Then a couple of years later on the Franco-German public channel <a href="/wiki/Arte" title="Arte">Arte</a>. A video release followed, including a <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> edition available in Dionnet's collection "Asian Classics".</p>
<p>As soon as 1995, Takeshi Kitano played the role of a yakuza in American director <a href="/wiki/Robert_Longo" title="Robert Longo">Robert Longo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">SF</a> <a href="/wiki/Thriller_film" title="Thriller film">thriller</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Johnny_Mnemonic_(film)" title="Johnny Mnemonic (film)">Johnny Mnemonic</a></i>. In North America <i>Sonatine</i> was released in theaters in April 1998 and <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a> released a subtitled video edition in 2000 as part of his <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Thunder_Pictures" title="Rolling Thunder Pictures">Rolling Thunder Pictures</a> collection. The same year, Kitano was convinced by his producer to go in the United States where he filmed his first (and last) film outside Japan. <i><a href="/wiki/Brother_(2000_film)" title="Brother (2000 film)">Brother</a></i> was shot in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> with an American crew and local actors including <a href="/wiki/Omar_Epps" title="Omar Epps">Omar Epps</a>. In an interview, Kitano admitted he was not fully satisfied with the final result of <i>Brother</i> and that he regretted his "Hollywood" adventure which was supposed to bring him a broader audience with a higher exposure. Kitano confessed he had no intention of shooting outside Japan any more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonatine_(1993_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Sonatine</i> won the Cariddi D'oro award for Best Film at the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Taormina_Film_Fest" title="Taormina Film Fest">Taormina Film Fest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-office_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-office-15">[15]</a></sup> Aya Kokumai received the Best New Encouragement award at the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Japan_Film_Professional_Awards" title="3rd Japan Film Professional Awards">3rd Japan Film Professional Awards</a> for her performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The film's soundtrack won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Academy_Award" title="Japanese Academy Award">Japanese Academy Award</a> for Music in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-music_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-music-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1995, it was awarded Critic's Choice at the <a href="/wiki/Festival_du_Film_Policier_de_Cognac" title="Festival du Film Policier de Cognac">Festival du Film Policier de Cognac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
