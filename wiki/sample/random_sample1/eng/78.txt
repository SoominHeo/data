<p><i><b>13</b></i> is the sixth studio album by English <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Blur_(band)" title="Blur (band)">Blur</a>, released on 15 March 1999. Jettisoning the <a href="/wiki/Britpop" title="Britpop">Britpop</a> sound of Blur's early career as well as the <a href="/wiki/Lo-fi_music" title="Lo-fi music">lo-fi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> of previous album, <i><a href="/wiki/Blur_(Blur_album)" title="Blur (Blur album)">Blur</a></i> (1997), <i>13</i> contains elements of <a href="/wiki/Experimental_music" title="Experimental music">experimental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_music" title="Psychedelic music">psychedelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a>.</p>
<p>Recording took place from June to October 1998 in London and <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>. The album marks a departure of the band's longtime producer, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Street" title="Stephen Street">Stephen Street</a>, with his role being filled by <a href="/wiki/William_Orbit" title="William Orbit">William Orbit</a>. Relationships between the band members were reported to be strained, with members frequently missing from the sessions. Lyrically, the album is significantly darker and more innovative than Blur's previous efforts, being heavily inspired by <a href="/wiki/Damon_Albarn" title="Damon Albarn">Damon Albarn</a>'s breakup with long-term girlfriend, <a href="/wiki/Justine_Frischmann" title="Justine Frischmann">Justine Frischmann</a>, whose relationship had become increasingly strained over the years.</p>
<p><i>13</i> was released on 15 March 1999 and entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> at number one, making it Blur's fourth consecutive studio album to reach the top spot. The album was later <a href="/wiki/British_Phonographic_Industry#Sales_certificates" title="British Phonographic Industry">certified Platinum</a>. <i>13</i> also reached number one in Norway and charted within the top 20 in many other countries. The album produced three singles, which charted at number 2, number 11 and number 14 respectively on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>. <i>13</i> was well-received critically, with a score of 79 on Metacritic, which equates to a tag of "Generally favorable reviews". <i>13</i> received a nomination for Best Album at the 2000 <a href="/wiki/NME_Awards" title="NME Awards">NME Awards</a>. It was also nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Prize" title="Mercury Prize">Mercury Prize</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JFrischmannGuitar.tiff"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="607" data-file-width="406" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/JFrischmannGuitar.tiff/lossless-page1-125px-JFrischmannGuitar.tiff.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/JFrischmannGuitar.tiff/lossless-page1-188px-JFrischmannGuitar.tiff.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/JFrischmannGuitar.tiff/lossless-page1-250px-JFrischmannGuitar.tiff.png 2x" width="125"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JFrischmannGuitar.tiff" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Much of Albarn's lyrics were inspired by his split with <a href="/wiki/Justine_Frischmann" title="Justine Frischmann">Justine Frischmann</a> (pictured).</div>

</div>
<p>Blur's previous studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Blur_(Blur_album)" title="Blur (Blur album)">Blur</a></i> (1997), had seen the band move away from the Britpop movement and take on a more <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">Indie rock</a> influenced direction, primarily under the suggestion of guitarist <a href="/wiki/Graham_Coxon" title="Graham Coxon">Graham Coxon</a>. The press and the industry had feared that the change in style would not be taken well with the public, and therefore the album would be commercially unsuccessful as a result. Despite these concerns, <i>Blur</i> had become an unexpected success, particularly in America, where the album had been certified Gold. However, the band still wanted to innovate, so they decided to embrace a different sound. <a href="/wiki/Bassist" title="Bassist">Bassist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alex_James_(musician)" title="Alex James (musician)">Alex James</a> stated: "I think you just have to keep changing. That sort of thinking was, sort of, key."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulse_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pulse-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band's leader <a href="/wiki/Damon_Albarn" title="Damon Albarn">Damon Albarn</a> had been in a long-term relationship with <a href="/wiki/Justine_Frischmann" title="Justine Frischmann">Justine Frischmann</a>, of the Britpop band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastica_(band)" title="Elastica (band)">Elastica</a>. Their relationship was highly publicised, the couple being described by John Harris as "proto-<a href="/wiki/Victoria_Beckham" title="Victoria Beckham">Posh</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Beckham" title="David Beckham">Becks</a> for the indie-rock constituency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> However their relationship became strained over time, stated reasons including Albarn's desire to have children as well as Frischmann's continued friendship with <a href="/wiki/Brett_Anderson" title="Brett Anderson">Brett Anderson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Suede_(band)" title="Suede (band)">Suede</a> who had shared a rivalry with Albarn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indie_soap_opera_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indie_soap_opera-6">[6]</a></sup> Albarn's lyrics and attitude had reflected this in the eyes of the other band members, with Coxon pointing out, "I didn't have much of a clue that things were going wrong between Damon and Justine but it was probably easy to guess."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulse_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-pulse-4">[4]</a></sup> After one last holiday together in Bali in late 1997 in an attempt to rekindle their relationship, the couple finally split.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indie_soap_opera_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Indie_soap_opera-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-7">[7]</a></sup> Albarn later commented: "That relationship just absolutely crashed. I mean, it really was a spectacularly sad end."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulse_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-pulse-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his breakup, Albarn started sharing a flat with artist <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Hewlett" title="Jamie Hewlett">Jamie Hewlett</a> whom he had met through Coxon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patterson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patterson-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-select_.28party.29_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-select_.28party.29-9">[9]</a></sup> Around this time, Albarn had started to broaden his musical output. Whilst he was working on <i>13</i>, there were various reports that he and Hewlett were working on a secret project, which turned out to be <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_band" title="Virtual band">virtual band</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q2001_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Q2001-10">[10]</a></sup> Albarn also started working on film soundtracks: including <i><a href="/wiki/Ravenous_(soundtrack)" title="Ravenous (soundtrack)">Ravenous</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ordinary_Decent_Criminal#Soundtrack" title="Ordinary Decent Criminal">Ordinary Decent Criminal</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/101_Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="101 Reykjavík">101 Reykjavík</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Long_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Long-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>13</i> is Blur's first album without longtime producer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Street" title="Stephen Street">Stephen Street</a>. Instead the band 'unanimously' decided that they wanted <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a> artist, <a href="/wiki/William_Orbit" title="William Orbit">William Orbit</a> to produce the album after being impressed by his remix of their track, "Movin' On", included on the remix compilation, <i><a href="/wiki/Bustin%27_%2B_Dronin%27" title="Bustin' + Dronin'">Bustin' + Dronin'</a></i> (1998).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patterson_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Patterson-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sillitoe_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sillitoe-13">[13]</a></sup> Albarn commented that "it was such a personal thing going on, we needed to have someone who didn't really know us". He also described Orbit as being "like a <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrist" title="Psychiatrist">psychiatrist</a>". When asked if his replacement had come as a shock, Street stated, "I just think they wanted to stretch out a bit more and, having made five albums with me, the best way to do that was to work with someone different who would approach the project in a different way. I understand that perfectly and certainly wasn't offended. I did five albums with the band and I must admit I thought each one would be the last because they were bound to want to try something new."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sillitoe_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sillitoe-13">[13]</a></sup> Albarn described the decision to not work with Street as "difficult", going on to say "he'll be forever part of what we are, and ironically, he gave us the tools we needed to go it alone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eccleston_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eccleston-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tension in the studio ran high during the recording sessions. In Orbit's words, "There was a battle between Damon's more experimental direction, and Graham's punk one, and Graham prevailed. If that tension had been growing on previous LPs, it came to a head here."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hasted_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hasted-15">[15]</a></sup> "Things were starting to fall apart between the four of us," drummer <a href="/wiki/Dave_Rowntree" title="Dave Rowntree">Dave Rowntree</a> later revealed. "It was quite a sad process making it. People were not turning up to the sessions, or turning up drunk, being abusive and storming off."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulse_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-pulse-4">[4]</a></sup> "I had songs," <a href="/wiki/Alex_James_(musician)" title="Alex James (musician)">Alex James</a> remarked. "I played them to William. He liked them. But I was sulking. I didn't play them to the others… Now I know how <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a> felt."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Coxon admitted, "I was really out there around <i>13</i>, which made for some pretty great noise but I was probably a bit of a crap to be around."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulse_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-pulse-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>"1992" had originally been recorded as a demo in 1992 and was lost until Damon found it again on a tape six years later. "Mellow Song" was demoed as a jam session known as "Mellow Jam" and was later included as the b-side of "<a href="/wiki/Tender_(song)" title="Tender (song)">Tender</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style_and_composition">Musical style and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Musical style and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>13</i> sees the band moving further away from their Britpop past into more cerebral and denser musical territory. Some of the songs, however, are evocative of songs from their previous efforts, such as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bugman_(song)" title="Bugman (song)">Bugman</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Coffee_%26_TV" title="Coffee &amp; TV">Coffee &amp; TV</a>" and "1992". The instrumental closer "Optigan 1" was created using an <a href="/wiki/Optigan" title="Optigan">Optigan</a> optical organ. The album is in the style of a loose concept album, much like other Blur albums, in this case about life and relationships. Much of the album was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Damon_Albarn" title="Damon Albarn">Damon Albarn</a>'s breakup with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastica_(band)" title="Elastica (band)">Elastica</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Justine_Frischmann" title="Justine Frischmann">Justine Frischmann</a>. Two of the singles, "<a href="/wiki/Tender_(song)" title="Tender (song)">Tender</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/No_Distance_Left_to_Run" title="No Distance Left to Run">No Distance Left to Run</a>", describe Albarn's love for Frischmann and his struggle to move on. The album is named after the band's recording studio as well as the number of tracks on the album (bar the hidden tracks).</p>
<p>The album features several short hidden tracks at the end of songs, stretching the playing time out; examples of this are "Coffee &amp; TV", "B.L.U.R.E.M.I.", "Battle" and "<a href="/wiki/Caramel_(Blur_song)" title="Caramel (Blur song)">Caramel</a>", the latter of which features two hidden tracks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artwork">Artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cover is a portion of an oil painting by <a href="/wiki/Graham_Coxon" title="Graham Coxon">Graham Coxon</a> called <i>Apprentice</i>. The album's singles also have cover art by Coxon. The numbers 1 and 3 have been painted so they also form the letter 'B' - revealed on the back cover to be for 'Blur'. This was not present on the original <i>Apprentice</i>, nor was the "shine" on the figure's head, which appears on <i>13</i>. These additions were made long after the original <i>Apprentice</i>, which was painted in 1996. The band's logo does not appear on the album in any form, aside from a sticker on the CD packaging. The logo is also absent from the single covers.</p>

<p><i>13</i> holds an overall approval rating of 79 out of 100 on online review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> based on 17 reviews, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meta_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meta-17">[17]</a></sup> Tom Doyle of <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> called <i>13</i> "a dense, fascinating, idiosyncratic and accomplished art rock album",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Q-2">[2]</a></sup> while Brent DiCrescenzo of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> stated that "Blur have finally found a sound to match their name."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-22">[22]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a>'</i> Sarah Zupko praised Blur's new musical approach and wrote that "proving they have the goods of a truly exceptional band, Blur has done what the greats have done before them—evolved."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Popmatters_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Popmatters-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rob_Sheffield" title="Rob Sheffield">Rob Sheffield</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> called the album "their sloppiest, most playful set, spiking the mix with church organ, electric piano and shambling drum loops."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-RS-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a more mixed assessment, Heather Phares of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> felt that "the group's ambitions to expand their musical and emotional horizons result in a half-baked baker's dozen of songs, featuring some of their most creative peaks and self-indulgent valleys."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-18">[18]</a></sup> Keith Cameron of <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> concluded that <i>13</i> was "Blur's most inconsistent and infuriating statement thus far. Infuriating, because divested of four solid-gone clunkers <i>13</i> could pass muster as the best of Blur."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-NME-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a three-star honorable mention rating, indicating "an enjoyable effort consumers attuned to its overriding aesthetic or individual vision may well treasure",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and remarked that "halfway there, it sits down in the middle of the road and won't budge."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for the hit single "Coffee &amp; TV" cemented Blur's reputation as a cult band in the U.S. with its protagonist <i>Milky</i>. The video gained heavy airplay on many modern rock channels in America.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>13</i> was nominated in the Album Of The Year category at the 2000 <a href="/wiki/NME_Awards" title="NME Awards">NME Awards</a>, losing to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Soft_Bulletin" title="The Soft Bulletin">The Soft Bulletin</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/The_Flaming_Lips" title="The Flaming Lips">The Flaming Lips</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_.28NME.29_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_.28NME.29-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vblur_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-vblur-31">[31]</a></sup> <i>13</i> was also nominated for the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Prize" title="Mercury Prize">Mercury Prize</a>, being Blur's second album to receive a nomination. The award was eventually given to <a href="/wiki/Talvin_Singh" title="Talvin Singh">Talvin Singh</a> for <a href="/wiki/OK_(Talvin_Singh_album)" title="OK (Talvin Singh album)">Ok</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercury_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mercury-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All tracks produced by William Orbit, except "Trailerpark" produced by Blur and "I Got Law" (demo version) produced by Damon Albarn.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=13_(Blur_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Trailerpark" was originally intended for the <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> album <i><a href="/wiki/Chef_Aid:_The_South_Park_Album" title="Chef Aid: The South Park Album">Chef Aid</a></i>, but was rejected by <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rubin" title="Rick Rubin">Rick Rubin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Japanese bonus track "I Got Law" (demo version) was developed into the <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a> hit single "<a href="/wiki/Tomorrow_Comes_Today" title="Tomorrow Comes Today">Tomorrow Comes Today</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>As well as having 13 tracks, Coxon also uses '13th' chords on several of the tracks including "Bugman", "Coffee &amp; TV" &amp; "B.L.U.R.E.M.I."</p>
<p>"Swamp Song" was originally going to appear on the "Tender" single as a B-side, but was put on the album at the last minute.</p>
