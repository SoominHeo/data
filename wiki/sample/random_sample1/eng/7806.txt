<p><i><b>Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode I</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ソニック・ザ・ヘッジホッグ<ruby><rb>4</rb> <rp>(</rp><rt>フォー</rt><rp>)</rp></ruby> エピソードI</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sonikku za Hejjihoggu Fō: Episōdo Wan</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/2010_in_video_gaming" title="2010 in video gaming">2010</a> <a href="/wiki/Episodic_video_game" title="Episodic video game">episodic</a> <a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">platform video game</a> in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(series)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (series)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i> series. The game was developed by <a href="/wiki/Dimps" title="Dimps">Dimps</a>, with assistance from <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Team" title="Sonic Team">Sonic Team</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dimpsreveal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dimpsreveal-7">[7]</a></sup> and published by <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>. The game was intended to emulate the original <i>Sonic the Hedgehog</i> titles on the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a>.</p>
<p><i>Episode I</i> was released in October 2010 for <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a>, <a href="/wiki/WiiWare" title="WiiWare">WiiWare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OctRelease_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-OctRelease-1">[1]</a></sup> A release for <a href="/wiki/Windows_Phone_7" title="Windows Phone 7">Windows Phone 7</a> followed in June 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phone_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Phone-2">[2]</a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> in January 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steam_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steam-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Android_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Android-4">[4]</a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/BlackBerry_PlayBook" title="BlackBerry PlayBook">BlackBerry PlayBook</a> in July 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlackBerry_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BlackBerry-5">[5]</a></sup> and for <a href="/wiki/Ouya" title="Ouya">Ouya</a> in July 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ouya_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ouya-6">[6]</a></sup> A direct follow-up, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_4:_Episode_II" title="Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode II">Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode II</a></i>, was released in 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_the_Hedgehog_4:_Episode_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="253" data-file-width="450" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg/220px-Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg/330px-Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg/440px-Sonic_4_Screenshot.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><i>Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode I</i> is a <a href="/wiki/2D_computer_graphics" title="2D computer graphics">2D</a> <a href="/wiki/Side-scrolling_video_game" title="Side-scrolling video game">side-scrolling</a> platformer reminiscent of the original <i>Sonic</i> games for the Sega Mega Drive/Genesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_interview_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot_interview-8">[8]</a></sup> Players control <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(character)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (character)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_day_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-last_day-9">[9]</a></sup> using mechanics such as the original spin dash and power-ups such as shields, invincibility and speed shoes. Sonic is also able to use a homing attack, which homes in on a targeted enemy or object.</p>
<p>Unlike the first three classic <i>Sonic</i> games, after completing the first act, the player is able to freely choose which zone to play via a world map, or choose to play the zones sequentially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Each of the four zones contain three acts, followed by a separate <a href="/wiki/Boss_(video_gaming)" title="Boss (video gaming)">boss</a> battle act, rather than the boss being included at the end of the third act. Each boss act is unlocked by completing all three acts in that zone. The boss battles, similar to the zones themselves, are inspired by the boss battles from older <i>Sonic</i> titles, although there are variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Once all acts and boss battles within each zone are cleared, players gain access to a fifth and final zone.</p>
<p>Special Stages in <i>Episode I</i>, accessed by finishing an act with at least 50 rings and then jumping into the Giant Ring that appears at the end of the stage, are based on the "Special Stages" in the original <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(1991_video_game)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (1991 video game)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i>. The player must tilt the playing field in order to navigate Sonic through a maze within a set time limit, collecting rings to open gates and time bonuses along the way, with the goal of each stage being to collect a Chaos Emerald.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the first time since <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_%26_Knuckles" title="Sonic &amp; Knuckles">Sonic &amp; Knuckles</a></i>, Sonic can transform into Super Sonic during regular levels after collecting all the Chaos Emeralds from the Special Stages, and at least 50 rings within the current act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game is playable in up to <a href="/wiki/1080p" title="1080p">1080p</a> <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">HD</a> on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_interview_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot_interview-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/480p" title="480p">480p</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Announcement_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Announcement-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mobile_device" title="Mobile device">mobile</a> versions of the game can be fully controlled with either the <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">accelerometers</a> or virtual buttons on the <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a>. Certain levels also make specific use of the accelerometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Players are also able to upload their game information to online leaderboards to compare level completion times and high scores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_interview_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot_interview-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_the_Hedgehog_4:_Episode_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode I</i> is set after the events of <i>Sonic &amp; Knuckles</i> (1994). With the Death Egg destroyed, Sonic heads off to explore new territories alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Official_site_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Official_site-17">[17]</a></sup> However, antagonist <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Eggman" title="Doctor Eggman">Dr. Eggman</a> has survived their last encounter, and has revisited and improved some of his creations from previous games, attempting to get rid of Sonic as the first part of his new grandiose scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Official_site_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Official_site-17">[17]</a></sup> Sonic's battle with Eggman culminates with Sonic defeating Eggman in his new base, the E.G.G. Station.</p>
<p>If all seven Chaos Emeralds are collected, a brief post-credits <a href="/wiki/Cutscene" title="Cutscene">cutscene</a> shows a silhouette of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Sonic_the_Hedgehog_video_game_characters#Metal_Sonic" title="List of Sonic the Hedgehog video game characters">Metal Sonic</a>, hinting at the <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_4:_Episode_II" title="Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode II">next episode</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_the_Hedgehog_4:_Episode_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The game's existence was first revealed on <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a> on September 9, 2009, under the code name <i>Project Needlemouse</i> (the name was derived from Mr. Needlemouse, an early <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">prototype</a> of Sonic pitched to <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> by artist <a href="/wiki/Naoto_Ohshima" title="Naoto Ohshima">Naoto Ohshima</a>). The site published a brief teaser trailer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and an interview with Sega's Ken Balough who acknowledged that "old-school Sonic fans have long asked to see Sonic return to a more 2D style of gameplay," stating that "Needlemouse is that critical first step that brings Sonic back to his 2D roots."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_interview_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot_interview-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The decision to create the game using a 2D graphical style was made after considering older <i>Sonic</i> fans' possible reactions to the game versus those of younger players:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">This is sort of recognition that <i>Sonic</i> is a brand that has a wide variety of fans in a wide variety of age groups. We have a fan base that's very young, and they want a little more of a <i><a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a></i>-ish kind of gameplay ... But we also have an older fan base who liked Sonic as he was in the Genesis days. And if we want to keep that fan base happy, we should provide games that cater to them as well. So it's more of a splitting a little bit of the brand.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td class="cquotecite" colspan="3" style="border: none; padding-right: 4%; font-size: smaller; text-align: right;"><cite>— Ken Balough of Sega</cite></td>
</tr>

<p>On January 11, 2010, Sega of America began a "character countdown" on their blog, removing potential playable characters from a list, which by January 14, 2010 revealed the game would feature Sonic as the only playable character. Also revealed was concept art which showcased classic enemies from early <i>Sonic</i> games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_day_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-last_day-9">[9]</a></sup> On February 2, Sega updated the front page of Sega of America's website with a short <a href="/wiki/Flash_animation" title="Flash animation">flash animation</a> featuring a sample of music from the game, reminiscent of Mega Drive/Genesis sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The game's proper title was revealed on February 4, 2010, as <i>Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode I</i>, along with the debut of in-game footage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Announcement_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Announcement-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game was developed primarily by Dimps, under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Team" title="Sonic Team">Sonic Team</a>. <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Iizuka_(game_designer)" title="Takashi Iizuka (game designer)">Takashi Iizuka</a>, who has worked on various <i>Sonic</i> games, notably as senior <a href="/wiki/Level_design#Level_designer" title="Level design">level designer</a> for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_3_%26_Knuckles" title="Sonic 3 &amp; Knuckles">Sonic 3 &amp; Knuckles</a></i>, served as the game's producer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dimpsreveal_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dimpsreveal-7">[7]</a></sup> Music and sound were composed by <a href="/wiki/Jun_Senoue" title="Jun Senoue">Jun Senoue</a>, also a mainstay of the series, and were intended to be reminiscent of classic Genesis titles, via the use of drum samples from the original <i>Sonic</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_2_(16-bit)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog 2 (16-bit)">Sonic the Hedgehog 2</a></i>, and relatively <a href="/wiki/Low_fidelity" title="Low fidelity">lo-fi</a> <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synth</a> samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not long after <i>Sonic 4</i> was officially announced, rumors surfaced that the game was available to members of the Xbox 360's online game testing service for developers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PartnerNet" title="PartnerNet">PartnerNet</a>. On April 3, PartnerNet was shut down due to the leaks. The leaked footage led to harsh criticism of the <a href="/wiki/Game_physics" title="Game physics">game physics</a> and certain level designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 20, 2010, Sega announced that <i>Sonic 4</i> would be available on the <a href="/wiki/App_Store_(iOS)" title="App Store (iOS)">App Store</a> for the iOS, and that the game was to be moved from its original summer 2010 deadline to late 2010, so as to extend development time. Izuka claims that this was because although he planned for all versions to be similar, two levels that worked on iOS did not work as well for <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">consoles</a>. The iOS version retains these levels, which utilise accelerometer controls, while new levels were built for the console versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The announcement of the delay was also accompanied by an interview with Sega's Ken Balough, in which it was explained that Sega had taken note of fan reaction to the game and were incorporating that feedback into the ongoing development. Areas specifically mentioned were a reworking of the "floaty" nature of the gravity and physics, and a redesign of some levels and elements in order to accommodate more of the momentum-driven gameplay style that was a staple of earlier entries in the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Sonic the Hedgehog 4: Episode I</i> received generally positive reviews. Aggregating review websites <a href="/wiki/GameRankings" title="GameRankings">GameRankings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gave the Wii version 75.42% and 81/100,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRWII_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRWII-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCWII_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-MCWII-31">[31]</a></sup> the Xbox 360 version 74.85% and 72/100<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRX360_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRX360-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCX360_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-MCX360-33">[33]</a></sup> the PlayStation 3 version 73.23% and 74/100,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRPS3_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRPS3-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCPS3_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-MCPS3-32">[32]</a></sup> and the iOS version 74.29% and 70/100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRiOS_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRiOS-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCiOS_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-MCiOS-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>'s Hilary Goldstein gave the game a score of 8.0 and an Editor's Choice award, calling it "short but sweet and well worth downloading."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rate_ign_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-rate_ign-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GamesRadar" title="GamesRadar">GamesRadar</a>'s Justin Towell gave it a score of 9/10, calling it "deserving of the name, <i>Sonic the Hedgehog 4</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rate_gradar_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-rate_gradar-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a>'s Kat Bailey gave it a B rank, praising the game's speed while criticizing some occasional poor design, such as boss battles and puzzles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/GameTrailers" title="GameTrailers">GameTrailers</a> gave it a 7.5, calling it a worthy investment for <i>Sonic</i> fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTrailers_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-GTrailers-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Digital_Spy" title="Digital Spy">Digital Spy</a> gave it three out of five stars, with reviewer Liam Martin answering the question of whether or not it lived up to its hype: "Despite some issues with the size of the game and its difficulty (it's very easy), the answer is yes, but only just."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Digital_Spy_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Digital_Spy-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurogamer" title="Eurogamer">Eurogamer</a>'s Ellie Gibson gave it a 9/10, calling it "gloriously fun" and "brilliantly paced."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euro_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Euro-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Criticism of the game focused on poor physics and lack of momentum; both aspects were often cited as being inferior to the momentum-based physics from the 16-bit Genesis games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Speaking with <i><a href="/wiki/VG247" title="VG247">VG247</a></i>, Sega West CEO Mike Hayes stated that <i>Episode I</i> cleared over one million downloads across all the platforms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> with the PlayStation 3 version selling the most copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
