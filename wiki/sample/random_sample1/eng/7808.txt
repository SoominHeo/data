<p><i><b>Sonic Adventure</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ソニックアドベンチャー</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sonikku Adobenchā</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">platform</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Team" title="Sonic Team">Sonic Team</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>. It was originally released for the <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Dreamcast</a> as a launch title in Japan in 1998, and worldwide the following year. It was the first game in the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(series)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (series)"><i>Sonic the Hedgehog</i> series</a> since <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_%26_Knuckles" title="Sonic &amp; Knuckles">Sonic &amp; Knuckles</a></i> in 1994, and was the best-selling game on the Dreamcast.</p>
<p>After series villain <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Eggman" title="Doctor Eggman">Doctor Eggman</a> releases an ancient monster, protagonist <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(character)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (character)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a> vows to stop him collecting the Chaos Emeralds. The other playable characters are flying fox <a href="/wiki/Tails_(character)" title="Tails (character)">Miles "Tails" Prower</a>, love interest <a href="/wiki/Amy_Rose" title="Amy Rose">Amy Rose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knuckles_the_Echidna" title="Knuckles the Echidna">Knuckles the Echidna</a>, who plans to reassemble the Master Emerald, angler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_the_Cat" title="Big the Cat">Big the Cat</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-102_Gamma" title="E-102 Gamma">E-102 Gamma</a>, one of Eggman's robots.</p>
<p><i>Sonic Adventure</i> received generally positive reviews for its graphics, gameplay, and soundtrack. It became the best-selling Dreamcast game. A sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Adventure_2" title="Sonic Adventure 2">Sonic Adventure 2</a></i>, was released in 2001 for the Dreamcast. Enhanced ports of the game were released in 2003 for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube" title="Nintendo GameCube">Nintendo GameCube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>. It has since been re-released as a downloadable game for <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a> via <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a>, and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> via <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> in 2010. It is also included in the <i><a href="/wiki/Dreamcast_Collection" title="Dreamcast Collection">Dreamcast Collection</a></i> for the Xbox 360.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sonic_Adventure_screenshot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="238" data-file-width="320" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/58/Sonic_Adventure_screenshot.jpg/220px-Sonic_Adventure_screenshot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/58/Sonic_Adventure_screenshot.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

<p><i>Sonic Adventure</i> is a platform game in which the player controls six different characters: the titular <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(character)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (character)">Sonic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tails_(character)" title="Tails (character)">Tails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knuckles_the_Echidna" title="Knuckles the Echidna">Knuckles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amy_Rose" title="Amy Rose">Amy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_the_Cat" title="Big the Cat">Big</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-102_Gamma" title="E-102 Gamma">E-102 Gamma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They must stop <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Eggman" title="Doctor Eggman">Doctor Eggman</a> from stealing the seven Chaos Emeralds and feeding them to Chaos, a monster known as "the God of Destruction", and defeat them both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first main game in the <i>Sonic the Hedgehog</i> series to feature free-roaming 3D gameplay, <i>Sonic Adventure</i> features two game modes: Adventure and Trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual07_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual07-3">[3]</a></sup> There are different goals for each character in the game: Sonic involves high speed gameplay, while Tails must reach the end of the level before Sonic does, and Knuckles searches the area for shards of the Master Emerald.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Each of the three characters retain many of their trademark moves from previous <i>Sonic</i> games, such as Tails being able to fly for short periods of time, and Knuckles being able to glide through the air, though they can use updated techniques as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The rest of the playable cast is rounded out by Amy, who must use her hammer to escape from Eggman's E-100 Zero robot chasing after her, Gamma, who must use its laser beam to shoot through levels to reach a target, and Big, who <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishes</a> in efforts to find his friend, Froggy. The player is given five <a href="/wiki/Health_(gaming)#Icon-based_health" title="Health (gaming)">lives</a>, which are lost when any character is attacked by an enemy with no rings in their possession, falls into a pit, or drowns. If all lives are lost at any point in the game, the "<a href="/wiki/Game_over" title="Game over">Game Over</a>" screen will appear, in which the player can continue from the last level played by selecting "Continue".</p>
<p>The "Adventure Mode" is a one-player game which consists of two areas, the "Adventure Field" and an "Action Stage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual09_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual09-6">[6]</a></sup> The Adventure Field contains several "Events" that are encountered in the course of the story, while the Action Stages have each character fulfilling a goal to clear the stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual09_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manual09-6">[6]</a></sup> Players may also find hidden Chao Gardens, a protective environment inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Chao_(Sonic)" title="Chao (Sonic)">Chao</a>, a sort of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtual_pet" title="Virtual pet">virtual pet</a>. The player can hatch, raise and interact with a Chao.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual15_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual15-7">[7]</a></sup> Chao can be taken with the player by downloading the minigame <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_recurring_characters_from_Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(games)#Chaos" title="List of recurring characters from Sonic the Hedgehog (games)">Chao Adventure</a></i> to their <a href="/wiki/VMU" title="VMU">VMU</a>, or in the GameCube version, a <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Advance" title="Sonic Advance">Sonic Advance</a></i> or various other Game Boy Advance Sonic games. The player can also raise their stats by giving them small animals that they found by defeating the robots, which improves their performance in Chao Races. There are also eggs hidden throughout the Adventure Fields which can produce special types of Chao.</p>
<p>By playing through Action Stages and Subgames, searching through the Adventure Fields or winning Chao Races, players can earn Emblems. In the case of Action Stages, each one has three Emblems, which can be earned by replaying the stages and fulfilling certain objectives, such as beating the level within a time limit. In <i>Sonic Adventure DX</i>, these can unlock hidden extras such as <a href="/wiki/Game_Gear" title="Game Gear">Game Gear</a> games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Sonic_the_Hedgehog_video_game_characters" title="List of Sonic the Hedgehog video game characters">List of Sonic the Hedgehog video game characters</a></div>
<p>The story is told from the perspectives of six different characters, each of their narratives intersecting with one another before converging in a final chapter. The titular protagonist of the game is <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(character)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (character)">Sonic</a>, a hedgehog with the ability to run at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> speeds who has returned home after a long journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual18_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual18-9">[9]</a></sup> His longtime friend is <a href="/wiki/Tails_(character)" title="Tails (character)">Tails</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">fox</a> who can fly using his two tails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual20_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual20-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Knuckles_the_Echidna" title="Knuckles the Echidna">Knuckles</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Echidna" title="Echidna">echidna</a> with the ability to glide; he is the guardian of the Master Emerald.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual22_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual22-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amy_Rose" title="Amy Rose">Amy</a> is a pink hedgehog and views Sonic as her love interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual24_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual24-12">[12]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-102_Gamma" title="E-102 Gamma">E-102 Gamma</a> is a gunner robot belonging to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_recurring_characters_from_Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(games)#E-100_Series" title="List of recurring characters from Sonic the Hedgehog (games)">E-100 Series</a> tasked with serving his creator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual26_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual26-13">[13]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_the_Cat" title="Big the Cat">Big</a> is a giant cat interested in fishing and is usually accompanied by his friend, Froggy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual28_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual28-14">[14]</a></sup> The characters are aided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tikal_the_Echidna" title="Tikal the Echidna">Tikal</a>, a mysterious female echidna who appears whenever Sonic, Tails, Knuckles, Amy, E-102 Gamma and Big are sent back in time, and also gives out hints to the player.</p>
<p>The main antagonist is <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Eggman" title="Doctor Eggman">Dr. Ivo "Eggman" Robotnik</a>, an evil scientist who plans to use the Chaos Emeralds to conquer Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual31_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual31-15">[15]</a></sup> Aiding Eggman is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaos_(Sonic_the_Hedgehog)" title="Chaos (Sonic the Hedgehog)">Chaos</a>, a liquified life-form who takes on more powerful forms each time he collects a Chaos Emerald.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual31_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manual31-15">[15]</a></sup> Chaos was previously sealed by Tikal after her father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Sonic_the_Hedgehog_video_game_characters#Pachacamac" title="List of Sonic the Hedgehog video game characters">Pachacamac</a> attempted to steal the Chaos Emeralds from the shrine, but has been freed due to Eggman's actions. Also aiding Eggman is the E-Series, a line of robots created by Eggman to facilitate his conquest. All of them have letters of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> as part of their name. E-100 Alpha (better known as Zero), the first E-series robot constructed as a prototype,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gamma_Profile_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gamma_Profile-16">[16]</a></sup> is dispatched to find Amy's friend Birdie. Other E-Series robots, including E-101 Beta, E-103 Delta, E-104 Epsilon, and E-105 Zeta, are demoted to lesser positions following Gamma's successes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Story">Story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tikal, the daughter of the Ancient Echidna Tribe's ruler Pachacamac, discovers the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaos_Emeralds" title="Chaos Emeralds">Chaos Emeralds</a> and the Master Emerald, a group of mysterious emeralds with unlimited power. She befriends a peaceful entity known as Chaos, the guardian of the emeralds, as well as the Chao. Despite warnings from Tikal, Pachacamac and the tribe attack the shrine, attempting to use the emeralds for world domination. After Chaos transforms into Perfect Chaos and kills the tribe, Tikal seals the entity and herself inside the Master Emerald.</p>
<p>Three thousand years later, Knuckles the Echidna guards the Master Emerald on the floating Angel Island when Doctor Ivo "Eggman" Robotnik shatters the Master Emerald and sets Chaos free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Angel Island falls into the sea below, scattering the Master Emerald's shards and the seven Chaos Emeralds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He resolves to search for the shards of the Master Emerald. Meanwhile, in the city of Station Square, Sonic the Hedgehog battles Chaos after witnessing an attack by the local police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> At the Mystic Ruins, Miles "Tails" Prower tests his Chaos Emerald-powered airplane, the Tornado, but it malfunctions and crashes into the beach of Emerald Coast below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> After Sonic rescues Tails, the two arrive at his workshop in the Mystic Ruins, where they are confronted by Eggman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> While Sonic is successful in defeating Eggman, the mad scientist steals Tails' Emerald and transforms Chaos before revealing his ultimate plan: with the power harnessed from the Emeralds, Chaos will eventually become invincible and destroy Station Square and Eggman will build Robotnikland over the city's ruins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Sonic and Tails resolve to search for the emeralds to prevent Eggman from getting them first, but eventually lose the ones they collect later on to Eggman and Chaos. Eggman then escapes to his giant airship, the Egg Carrier, after which Sonic and Tails then attempt to pursue the ship in the Tornado, but they are sent falling out of the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eggman then activates E-102 Gamma,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> who fights E-101 Beta during his training. Eggman has the E-Series robots search for Froggy, a frog who has eaten Chaos's tail as well as a Chaos Emerald. Gamma finds Froggy but is chased back to the ship by Froggy's owner Big the Cat. Meanwhile, Tails discovers another emerald and uses it to prepare the Tornado II plane to find Sonic while Sonic lands in Station Square once again. While lamenting her time with Sonic, Amy Rose discovers a Flicky bird in possession of a Chaos Emerald and names him Birdie. However, having discovered Birdie's location, Eggman dispatches the robot Zero to chase down the Flicky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> After Sonic turns down an offer to protect Birdie, Zero captures Amy and Birdie and detains them both on the Egg Carrier. Using the Tornado II, Sonic and Tails follow them on board. Knuckles also boards the aircraft after collecting pieces of the Master Emerald and seeing a vision of the Egg Carrier.</p>
<p>On board the Egg Carrier, Eggman removes the E-Series robots for their failure before stationing Gamma in Amy's cell. However, Gamma witnesses Beta being rebuilt and Amy convinces Gamma that he should not work for Eggman. In a confusion of newly discovered emotions, Gamma releases Amy and Birdie. As Sonic, Tails and Knuckles enter the carrier and destroy it from the inside, Amy is pursued by Zero and reunites with Sonic and Tails before Eggman steals Birdie's Emerald. While Sonic defeats Gamma, Amy helps him spare Gamma's life, but the ship loses altitude and Tails, Amy, and Gamma flee. Sonic defeats Chaos, now with six Chaos Emeralds and his tail restored, and pursues Eggman. Meanwhile, Big retrieves Froggy from inside Chaos before finding the Tornado II, and Knuckles steals back the six Chaos Emeralds; they both flee when it crashes into the ocean below.</p>
<p>Upon erasing his master registration, Gamma resolves to track down his brother robots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> After freeing the animals inside the robots, he confronts Beta in his rebuilt form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Though Gamma defeats Beta, he is destroyed in the struggle and Birdie's parents are released from within the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Pursued by Zero, Amy travels to the Egg Carrier and reunites Birdie with its family, but she is forced to confront Zero when he knocks down the Flicky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Amy destroys the robot and sees Birdie and his family off, vowing to do her best to earn Sonic's respect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Elsewhere, Tails chases Eggman and stops him from detonating a missile in the center of Station Square. Eggman uses the Egg Walker to attack the city, but Tails defeats him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Sonic navigates Final Egg, Eggman's base at the Mystic Ruins, and confronts the scientist in his Egg Viper. After avoiding contact with its missiles, Sonic destroys the Egg Viper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next day, Knuckles finishes restoring the Master Emerald,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> but Angel Island falls into the sea once again when Chaos attacks him and Eggman. Chaos reabsorbs the Chaos Emeralds and locates the seventh aboard the Tornado II, which crashed after Big used it to escape the Egg Carrier. Having acquired all seven Chaos Emeralds, Chaos transforms into Perfect Chaos, draining the Chaos Emeralds of their negative energy, and destroys Station Square in a massive flood. The Chaos Emeralds are re-gathered by Tails, Knuckles, Amy, Big, and the pink Flicky that fueled Gamma. They bring the emeralds to Sonic.</p>
<p>Having taken each main character into the past numerous times earlier, Tikal demands that Chaos must be sealed back in the Master Emerald. Sonic objects, saying that Chaos's heart would still be filled with anger and sadness if he were to be sealed inside the Master Emerald. Instead, with the cheering of his friends and the people of Station Square, Sonic uses the Chaos Emeralds' still-present positive energy to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(series)#Super_transformations" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (series)">Super Sonic</a> and face off against Perfect Chaos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Following his defeat, Chaos discovers that the Chao thrive in Station Square, quelling his anger. United, Tikal and Chaos disappear before Sonic chases after a fleeing Eggman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The development of <i>Sonic Adventure</i> began around April 1997 with a development team of 30 members. After developing several titles for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a>, producer <a href="/wiki/Yuji_Naka" title="Yuji Naka">Yuji Naka</a> worked mainly on the game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nights_into_Dreams..." title="Nights into Dreams...">Nights into Dreams...</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Saturn" title="Sega Saturn">Sega Saturn</a>. Sonic Team started to work on an original 3D <i>Sonic</i> title for the Saturn, but development was ultimately shifted to the <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Dreamcast</a>; according to Naka, remnants of the project can be seen in the compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Jam" title="Sonic Jam">Sonic Jam</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The development team focused on graphics and high resolution for this game, with Naka reflecting that "[w]e have pushed the Dreamcast as far as we can at present".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unveils_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-unveils-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Director <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Iizuka" title="Takashi Iizuka">Takashi Iizuka</a>, having developed <i>Nights into Dreams...</i>, proposed to Naka that they should develop a <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video game</a>-style <i>Sonic</i> game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directorscut_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-directorscut-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1001_Games_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-1001_Games-40">[40]</a></sup> In order to achieve a more natural, realistic feel to the exotic levels like ruins and jungles, the core members of the Sonic Team traveled to <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. The team visited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cancun" title="Cancun">Cancun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, and used pictures taken from their journey as "textures in the games." For Tails' sandboarding, the development team used a group of people boarding on sand dunes in <a href="/wiki/Ica,_Peru" title="Ica, Peru">Ica, Peru</a> as a reference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directorscut_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-directorscut-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Naka aimed to create levels that would take the player at least five minutes to complete, yet retain similar gameplay to the Mega Drive titles. Following the creation of the basic level maps, Naka wondered "why don't we use this map for other characters?" This led to the introduction of Big and E-102.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unveils_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-unveils-38">[38]</a></sup> The development team conducted surveys of fans to ensure that the final product, especially the characters, would please them. The character of Sonic was redesigned for the game by Sonic Team artist Yuji Uekawa, with longer legs and spines that Iizuka subsequently noted were more suitable for 3D than Sonic's original and more compact design, which was meant to be seen from the side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Certain levels, such as "Lost World," were rebuilt dozens of times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1001_Games_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-1001_Games-40">[40]</a></sup> Sega made it a top priority to keep the game a secret until shortly before its release. Despite these efforts, screenshots were leaked onto the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> in mid-1998. Naka presented the game to <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(magazine)" title="Edge (magazine)">Edge</a></i> in mid-August, and official announcement fell on August 22 in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unveils_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-unveils-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 1999, Sega's vice president Tadahiko Hirose announced that <i>Sonic Adventure</i> would be released along with <i>Virtua Fighter 3tb</i> and <i>Sega Rally 2</i> as launch titles for the Dreamcast's international release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In June 1999, Sega announced that <i>Sonic Adventure</i> would be one of the five titles in the Dreamcast's "Masterpiece Collection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The North American version of the game includes Japanese and English-language audio tracks, as well as Japanese, English, Spanish, French and German subtitles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Online gameplay and downloadable content were also added for the localization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The game's music was written by <a href="/wiki/Jun_Senoue" title="Jun Senoue">Jun Senoue</a>, Fumie Kumatani, Kenichi Tokoi, and <a href="/wiki/Masaru_Setsumaru" title="Masaru Setsumaru">Masaru Setsumaru</a>, and contains vocal performances from <a href="/wiki/Tony_Harnell" title="Tony Harnell">Tony Harnell</a>, Karen Brake, <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Saunders" title="Marlon Saunders">Marlon Saunders</a>, Dred Foxx, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Poley" title="Ted Poley">Ted Poley</a>, Nikki Gregoroff, and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Gioeli" title="Johnny Gioeli">Johnny Gioeli</a>, the latter of whom would later join Senoue to form the band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crush_40" title="Crush 40">Crush 40</a>. The development team preferred the use of "hot, funky, and <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock 'n' roll</a>" music over the traditional <a href="/wiki/Electropop" title="Electropop">electropop</a>-based music present in earlier <i>Sonic</i> games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unveils_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-unveils-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Company_Profile_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Company_Profile-47">[47]</a></sup> Several soundtrack albums for the game were released. <i>Sonic Adventure Songs With Attitude ~Vocal mini-Album~</i>, with character theme tracks by Senoue, Kumatani, and Tokoi, was released in Japan by Marvelous Entertainment on December 2, 1998. The full soundtrack, titled <i>Sonic Adventure "Digi-LOG Conversation" Original Sound Track</i>, was released in Japan on January 29, 1999. For the twentieth anniversary of the <i>Sonic</i> series, <i>Sonic Adventure Original Soundtrack 20th Anniversary Edition</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> on May 18, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> On September 10, 2014, a two volume original soundtrack was also released on iTunes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_versions_and_releases">Alternate versions and releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alternate versions and releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sonic_Adventure_International"><i>Sonic Adventure International</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sonic Adventure International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The international version of <i>Sonic Adventure</i> was re-released in Japan on October 14, 1999 under the title <i><b>Sonic Adventure International</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ソニックアドベンチャー・インターナショナル</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sonikku Adobenchā Intānashonaru</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sonic_Adventure_DX:_Director.27s_Cut"><i>Sonic Adventure DX: Director's Cut</i></span><i><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sonic Adventure DX: Director's Cut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></i></h3>
<p>In 2003, an <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">enhanced port</a> was released for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube" title="Nintendo GameCube">GameCube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> titled <i><b>Sonic Adventure DX: Director's Cut</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ソニックアドベンチャー デラックス</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sonikku Adobenchā Derakkusu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. <i>Sonic Adventure DX</i> includes a Mission Mode, which included 60 missions to complete throughout the Adventure Fields and Action Stages. It also contains <a href="/wiki/Compilations_in_the_Sonic_series" title="Compilations in the Sonic series">an unlockable compilation</a> that features emulations of all twelve <i>Sonic the Hedghog</i> titles released for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Game_Gear" title="Sega Game Gear">Sega Game Gear</a>. These include <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Drift" title="Sonic Drift">Sonic Drift</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Chaos" title="Sonic Chaos">Sonic Chaos</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Labyrinth" title="Sonic Labyrinth">Sonic Labyrinth</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog:_Triple_Trouble" title="Sonic the Hedgehog: Triple Trouble">Sonic the Hedgehog: Triple Trouble</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Drift_2" title="Sonic Drift 2">Sonic Drift 2</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tails%27_Skypatrol" title="Tails' Skypatrol">Tails' Skypatrol</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Blast" title="Sonic Blast">Sonic Blast</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tails_Adventure" title="Tails Adventure">Tails Adventure</a></i>, and the 8-bit versions of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(8-bit)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (8-bit)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_2_(8-bit)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog 2 (8-bit)">Sonic the Hedgehog 2</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_Spinball" title="Sonic the Hedgehog Spinball">Sonic the Hedgehog Spinball</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Robotnik%27s_Mean_Bean_Machine" title="Dr. Robotnik's Mean Bean Machine">Dr. Robotnik's Mean Bean Machine</a></i>. Also, if all Emblems are collected, the player can play as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_Sonic" title="Metal Sonic">Metal Sonic</a> in Sonic's stages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_releases">Digital releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Digital releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 10, 2010, Sega officially announced that <i>Sonic Adventure</i> would be released on both the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a> and the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> in Fall 2010. The <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a> version was released on September 15, 2010, while the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> version was released on September 20, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The initial release is based on the PC version of <i>Sonic Adventure DX</i> despite the game simply being called "<i>Sonic Adventure</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-1up-52">[52]</a></sup> The DLC package, simply titled "Sonic Adventure DX Upgrade", has launched alongside the game on XBLA, and PSN, which upgrades the game to include all of the features from the GameCube and PC versions with the exception of the Game Gear games and Dreamcast DLC. This version also includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_Sonic" title="Metal Sonic">Metal Sonic</a> as an unlockable character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Sonic Adventure DX</i> was released again on <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a> on March 4, 2011 as a digital download for Windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Sonic Adventure</i> received generally favorable reviews from critics. It became one of the few <a href="/wiki/Sega_All_Stars" title="Sega All Stars">Sega All Stars</a> games. The Japanese video game magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> gave the game a score of 38/40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Although criticized for its camera system, framerate issues, and fishing stages with Big, the game was still highly praised for retaining the fast and enjoyable gameplay that Sonic was known for in 2D as well as the game's graphics, multiple character storylines, and soundtrack. Brandon Justice of <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> rated the game an 8.6/10, criticizing various glitches and voice acting while praising the game's visuals and gameplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> By August 2006, <i>Sonic Adventure</i> had sold over 2.5 million units worldwide, making it the best-selling Dreamcast game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> This includes one million sold in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of its release, <i>Adventure</i> was acclaimed by the <i>Arcade</i> magazine as a "quantum leap forward" in aesthetics and visual detail in video games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arcade_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-arcade-60">[60]</a></sup> estimated by <i><a href="/wiki/Hyper_(magazine)" title="Hyper (magazine)">Hyper</a></i> to exceed that which was possible on high-end PCs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_and_Video_Games" title="Computer and Video Games">Computer and Video Games</a></i> stated shortly after release that "<i>Sonic Adventure</i> is one of the best games ever made" and marveled that "many things you thought were impossible to see and experience in computer games are now here."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(magazine)" title="Edge (magazine)">Edge</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s preview stated that graphical features like an "amazingly detailed cityscape" showed off the Dreamcast's potential, comparing it to <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Mario_64" title="Super Mario 64">Super Mario 64</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s role for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a>, and exclaimed that "as a showcase of what the machine can do, <i>Sonic Adventure</i> is perfect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Speculation arose that the game could save the Dreamcast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arcade_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-arcade-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> which had not sold well so far by the end of 1998,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arcade_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-arcade-60">[60]</a></sup> or even re-establish <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> as the dominant console manufacturer after the relatively unsuccessful Saturn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the original Dreamcast version of <i>Adventure</i> was positively received, later re-releases were not as acclaimed. <i>Sonic Adventure DX</i> received more mixed reviews, with the GameCube version receiving a 64.43% average on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GameRankings.com" title="GameRankings.com">GameRankings.com</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Matt Casamassina of <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> criticized the port for not addressing major problems of the original Dreamcast release, giving it a "Mediocre" rating of 5.0. Giancarlo Varanini of <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a> voiced similar disapproval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Reviews for the PlayStation Network and Xbox Live Arcade versions of <i>Sonic Adventure</i> were negative. IGN's Arthur Gies gave the Xbox 360 version a score of 3.5/10 and calling it "so fundamentally flawed that it borders on unplayable--the sections that move the fastest, that work best, require the least input from the player."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a> criticized both the game's dated mechanics and the perceived lack of effort put into the port, which "feels like it wasn't even tuned for the Xbox 360 controller."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-1up-52">[52]</a></sup> By contrast, Gamer 2.0 claimed that the controls were "slightly more responsive than those of the Dreamcast original,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and <i>PlayStation LifeStyle</i> scored it 8/10, complimenting the "frantic, fun gameplay" and "tons of replayability", concluding that "If you manage to overlook the glitches and dinky camera, you'll easily find a gem (or emerald) in this Dreamcast classic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion_and_tie-ins">Promotion and tie-ins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Promotion and tie-ins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the official launch of the Dreamcast in the U.S., Sega of America formed an exclusive deal with <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Video" title="Hollywood Video">Hollywood Video</a> to allow customers to rent the Dreamcast console along with <i>Sonic Adventure</i>. This promotion began on July 15, 1999 and took place at 1,055 Hollywood Video stores across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hollywoodvideo_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-hollywoodvideo-72">[72]</a></sup> This non-retail version of the game that was included, titled <i>Sonic Adventure: Limited Edition</i>, featured an initial English of the game's text and voice overs, as well as improvements to Japanese version's controls and camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-limitededition1_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-limitededition1-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-limitededition2_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-limitededition2-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Sonic Adventure</i> received a comic book adaptation courtesy of <a href="/wiki/Archie_Comics" title="Archie Comics">Archie Comics</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(comic_book)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (comic book)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i> on-going series. The story arc took place in issues #79-84 and Sonic Super Special #13, originally published from November 1999 to May 2000. This adaptation is notable for its permanent integration of characters and story-lines from the video game into the pre-existing continuity of the comic series, providing explanations for the altered character design for Amy Rose and the history of Station Square hidden beneath Sonic's planet, Mobius. Selected portions of these issues (with the exception of issue #79) were reprinted in Archie's <i>Sonic Super Special Magazine</i> #2 (February 2012).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-American_(Campbell%27s)" title="Franco-American (Campbell's)">Franco-American</a>'s Sonic the Hedgehog pasta featured <i>Sonic Adventure</i> inspired labeling for a limited time and offered consumers a chance to obtain a free Archie Sonic comic book.</p>
<p>In 2000, Toy Island produced multiple lines of <i>Sonic Adventure</i> toys, featuring 5" action figures, 10" talking figures, and 3" bendy figures. Several of these figures were later reissued as part of their <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_X" title="Sonic X">Sonic X</a></i> lines in 2004.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sonic_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following <i>Sonic Adventure</i>, Sonic Team developed <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Adventure_2" title="Sonic Adventure 2">Sonic Adventure 2</a></i>, the final <i>Sonic</i> game for the Dreamcast. <i>Adventure</i> also introduced two characters that would appear in later games and other media—Big and Gamma—as well as the ubiquitous Chao creatures. The plot of <i>Sonic Adventure</i> would later be adapted as a six-episode story arc in the second season of the 2003 animated series <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_X" title="Sonic X">Sonic X</a></i>.</p>
<p>Writers at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GamesRadar" title="GamesRadar">GamesRadar</a> have stated that, as <i>Sonic Adventure</i> was one of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_video_game_consoles_(sixth_generation)" title="History of video game consoles (sixth generation)">sixth generation</a> console games, "the gaming world was changed forever" despite presence of some glitches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Travis Fahs of IGN asserted in 2010 that it is still remembered as the most impressive <i>Sonic</i> game from after the Genesis era and that, despite Sega's efforts, no post-Dreamcast <i>Sonic</i> game has achieved similar success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Taylor Cocke of <a href="/wiki/Joystiq" title="Joystiq">Joystiq</a> has stated that the original <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(1991_video_game)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (1991 video game)">Sonic the Hedgehog</a></i> and <i>Adventure</i> both succeeded and innovated in gaming—among 2D and 3D games, respectively—by feeling "good to play" and making effective use of linear level design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> In 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/GamePro" title="GamePro">GamePro</a></i> listed the game as the seventh best platformer of all time, asserting that while it had not aged well in certain respects, its core gameplay "is still some of the best" in the entire series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> According to game journalists Rusel DeMaria and Johnny L. Wilson, <i>Sonic Adventure</i> "wasn't a strong enough title" and "failed to catch on with players in nearly the way that <i>Mario 64</i> had done," "though it had many fascinating features, including the use of the <a href="/wiki/Tamagotchi" title="Tamagotchi">Tamagotchi</a>-like memory card to incubate eggs for little pet creatures" and "some good action segments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> In a 2006 retrospective, <i><a href="/wiki/Retro_Gamer" title="Retro Gamer">Retro Gamer</a></i>'s Sean Smith noted that while <i>Adventure</i> "has its share of detractors", it "still looks luscious and plays extremely well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Company_Profile_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Company_Profile-47">[47]</a></sup> Writing in <a href="/wiki/1001_Video_Games_You_Must_Play_Before_You_Die" title="1001 Video Games You Must Play Before You Die">1001 Video Games You Must Play Before You Die</a>, Kieth Stuart described <i>Adventure</i> as "Sonic Team's flawed masterpiece", praising its "vast, twisting environments" and noting "From driving bumper karts in Twinkle Park to legging it down the side of a skyscraper in Speed Highway, the game brilliantly captures traditional <i>Sonic</i> elements."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1001_Games_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-1001_Games-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Sega released <i><a href="/wiki/Sonic_Generations" title="Sonic Generations">Sonic Generations</a></i>, a game that remade aspects of various past games from the franchise. The PlayStation 3, Xbox 360, and PC version contained a remade "Speed Highway" level, and a remade battle with Perfect Chaos. The <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">Nintendo 3DS</a> version contained a remade version of the "Emerald Coast" level.</p>
