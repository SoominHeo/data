<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Oligosaccharide</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>An <b>oligosaccharide</b> (from the Greek <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%80%CE%BB%CE%AF%CE%B3%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:ὀλίγος">ὀλίγος</a> <i>olígos</i>, "a few", and σάκχαρ <i>sácchar</i>, "sugar") is a <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">saccharide</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> containing a small number (typically two to ten<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">simple sugars (monosaccharides)</a>. Oligosaccharides can have many functions including cell recognition and cell binding. For example, glycolipids have an important role in the immune response.</p>
<p>In general, they are found either <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond"><i>N</i>- or <i>O</i>-linked</a> to compatible <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid#List_of_standard_amino_acids" title="Amino acid">amino acid side-chains</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> or to <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a> <a href="/wiki/Moiety_(chemistry)" title="Moiety (chemistry)">moieties</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycans" title="Glycans">glycans</a>). N-linked oligosaccharides are found attached to asparagine via a beta linkage to the amine nitrogen of the side chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Alternately, <a href="/wiki/O-linked_glycosylation" title="O-linked glycosylation">O-linked</a> oligosaccharides are generally attached to threonine or serine on the alcohol group of the side chain.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glycosylation">Glycosylation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Glycosylation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In biology, glycosylation is the co-translational process by which a carbohydrate is covalently attached to an organic molecule – creating structures such as glycoproteins and glycolipids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="N-linked_Oligosaccharides">N-linked Oligosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: N-linked Oligosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N_Linked_Oligosh.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="452" data-file-width="666" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/N_Linked_Oligosh.png/220px-N_Linked_Oligosh.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/N_Linked_Oligosh.png/330px-N_Linked_Oligosh.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/N_Linked_Oligosh.png/440px-N_Linked_Oligosh.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N_Linked_Oligosh.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of N-linked oligosaccharide, shown here with GlcNAc. X is any amino acid except proline.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>N-linked glycosylation involves oligosaccharide attachment to asparagine via a beta linkage to the amine nitrogen of the side chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> The process of N-linked glycosylation occurs cotranslationally, or concurrently while the proteins is being translated. Since it is added cotranslationally, it is believed that N-linked glycosylation helps determine the folding of polypeptides due to the hydrophilic nature of sugars. All N-linked Oligosaccharides are composed of a core of five sugars - known as a pentasaccharide.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/N-linked_glycosylation" title="N-linked glycosylation">N-glycosylation</a> for eukaryotes, the oligosaccharide substrate is assembled right at the membrane of the ER.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a>, this process occurs at the plasma membrane. In both cases, the acceptor substrate is an <a href="/wiki/Asparagine" title="Asparagine">asparagine</a> residue.They are small carbohydrates which are formed by condensation of 2-9 monosacchrides. This asparagine has increased nucleophilicity in the amide group. The unique arrangement of N-linked oligosaccharides usually has the oligosaccharide linked to the amide nitrogen of the Asn residue, in the sequence Asn-X-Ser/Thr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> X can be any amino acid except for <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a> (though it is rare to see Asp, Glu, Leu, or Trp).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="O-linked_Oligosaccharides">O-linked Oligosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: O-linked Oligosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1481" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png/220px-Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png/330px-Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png/440px-Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oolinked_Oligosaccharide.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of O-Linked oligosaccharide with β-Galactosyl-(1n3)-α-N-acetylgalactosaminyl-Ser/ Thr.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Oligosaccharides that participate in O-linked glycosylation are attached to <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">threonine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Serine" title="Serine">serine</a> on the alcohol group of the side chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> O-linked glycosylation occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Golgi_apparatus" title="Golgi apparatus">golgi apparatus</a>, in which monosaccharide units are added to a complete polypeptide chain. Cell surface proteins and extracellular proteins are O-glycosylated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Glycosylation sites in O-linked oligosaccharides are specified only in the secondary and tertiary structures of the polypeptide, which will dictate where glycosyltransferases will add sugars.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glycosylated_biomolecules">Glycosylated biomolecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Glycosylated biomolecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both glycoproteins and glycolipids have a covalently attached <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> attached to their respective molecule. They are very abundant on the surface of the cell, and their interactions contribute to the overall stability of the cell.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glycoproteins">Glycoproteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Glycoproteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">Glycoproteins</a> have distinct Oligosaccharide structures which contribute greatly to various properties of the glycoproteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is these properties that become important for critical functions such as <a href="/wiki/Antigenicity" title="Antigenicity">antigenicity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a>, and resistance to <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">proteases</a>. Glycoproteins are relevant as <a href="/wiki/Cell_surface_receptor" title="Cell surface receptor">cell-surface receptors</a>, cell-adhesion molecules, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunoglobulins" title="Immunoglobulins">immunoglobulins</a>, and tumor antigens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glycolipids">Glycolipids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Glycolipids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glycolipid" title="Glycolipid">Glycolipids</a> are important for cell recognition, and are important for modulating the function of membrane proteins that act as receptors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup> Glycolipids are lipid molecules bound to oligosaccharides, generally present in the <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a>. Additionally, they can serve as receptors for cellular recognition and cell signaling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup> The head of the oligosaccharide serves as a binding partner in <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptor</a> activity. The binding mechanisms of receptors to the oligosaccharides depends on the composition of the oligosaccharide sugar are exposed/presented out of the membrane. There is great biodiversity in the binding mechanisms of glycolipids, which is what makes them such a target for pathogens as a site for interaction and entrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Chaperone_(protein)" title="Chaperone (protein)">chaperone</a> activity of glycolipids has been studied for its relevance to HIV infection. The exposure of these glycolipids can also serve as a portal for other viral and toxic substances, which bind to certain receptors on the cell surface and initiate an invasion of the toxin into the cell.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_Recognition">Cell Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cell Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All cells are coated in either glycoproteins or glycolipids, both of which help determine cell types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lectin" title="Lectin">Lectins</a>, or proteins that bind carbohydrates, which can recognize very specific oligosaccharides and provide useful information for cell recognition due to oligosaccharide binding.</p>
<p>An important example of oligosaccharide cell recognition is the role of glycolipids in determining <a href="/wiki/Blood_type" title="Blood type">blood types</a>. The various blood types are distinguished by the glycan modification present on the surface of blood cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> These can be visualized using mass spectrometry. The oligosaccharides found on the A, B, and H <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigen</a> occur on the non-reducing ends of the oligosaccharide. The H antigen (which indicates an O blood type) serves as a precursor for the A and B antigen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, a person with A blood type will have the A antigen and H antigen present on the glycolipids of the red blood cell plasma membrane. A person with B blood type will have the B and H antigen present. A person with AB blood type will have A, B, and H antigens present. And finally, a person with O blood type will only have the H antigen present. This means all blood types have the H antigen, which explains why the O blood type is known as the "universal donor".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_Adhesion">Cell Adhesion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cell Adhesion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many cells produce specific carbohydrate-binding ligands, known as lectins, which mediate cell-adhesion with oligosaccharides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Selectin" title="Selectin">Selectins</a> - a family of lectins - mediate certain cell-cell adhesion processes, including those of leukocytes to endothelial cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> In an immune response, endothelial cells can express certain selectins transiently in response to damage or injury to the cells. In response, a reciprocal selectin-oligosaccharide interaction will occur between the two molecules which allows the white blood cell to help eliminate the infection or damage.Protein-Carbohydrate bonding is often mediated by hydrogen bonding and van der Waals forces.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_mother-to-child_transmission_of_HIV-1">Role in mother-to-child transmission of HIV-1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Role in mother-to-child transmission of HIV-1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mixed_feeding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mixed feeding (page does not exist)">Mixed-feeding</a> (breast-milk and formula or other non-human feed) is the highest prevalence factor of postnatal transmission of <a href="/wiki/Subtypes_of_HIV" title="Subtypes of HIV">HIV-1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, most exclusively breast-fed infants do not contract the virus from the infected mother, despite the continuous exposure. The specific glycans that are present in human breast milk can actually inhibit HIV-1 transfer by competing with the HIV-1 surface glycoprotein gp120 for binding to dendritic ICAM3-grabbing non-integrin (DC-SIGN). Human milk contains a high quantity of complex oligosaccharides that carry multiple <a href="/wiki/Lewis_antigen_system" title="Lewis antigen system">Lewis antigen</a> glycans. These <a href="/wiki/Human_milk_oligosaccharide" title="Human milk oligosaccharide">human milk oligosaccharides (HMOs)</a> reduce gp120 binding by more than 80%, which proves in inhibitory effects of human milk on HIV-1 mother-to-child transmission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fructooligosaccharide" title="Fructooligosaccharide">Fructo-oligosaccharides</a> (FOS), which are found in many vegetables, consist of short chains of <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> molecules. (<a href="/wiki/Inulin" title="Inulin">Inulin</a> has a much higher <a href="/wiki/Degree_of_polymerization" title="Degree of polymerization">degree of polymerization</a> than FOS and is a polysaccharide.) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactooligosaccharides" title="Galactooligosaccharides">Galactooligosaccharides</a> (GOS), which also occur naturally, consist of short chains of <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a> molecules. These compounds can be only partially digested by humans.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mannan_Oligosaccharide_based_nutritional_supplements_(MOS)" title="Mannan Oligosaccharide based nutritional supplements (MOS)">Mannan oligosaccharides</a> (MOS) are widely used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_feed" title="Compound feed">animal feed</a> to improve gastrointestinal health, energy levels and performance. They are normally obtained from the yeast cell walls of <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>. Research at the University of Illinois has demonstrated that mannan oligosaccharides differ from other oligosaccharides in that they are not fermentable and their primary mode of actions include agglutination of type-1 fimbrae pathogens and immunomodulation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oligosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oligosaccharides are one of the components of <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">fibre</a>, found in plants. FOS and inulin are found naturally in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_artichoke" title="Jerusalem artichoke">Jerusalem artichoke</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burdock" title="Burdock">burdock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicory" title="Chicory">chicory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leek_(vegetable)" title="Leek (vegetable)">leeks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asparagus" title="Asparagus">asparagus</a>. FOS products derived from chicory root contain significant quantities of inulin, a fiber widely distributed in fruits, vegetables and plants. Inulin is a significant part of the daily diet of most of the world’s population. FOS can also be synthesized by enzymes of the fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_niger" title="Aspergillus niger">Aspergillus niger</a></i> acting on sucrose. GOS is naturally found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soybeans" title="Soybeans">soybeans</a> and can be synthesized from <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> (milk sugar). FOS, GOS, and inulin are available as nutritional supplements in capsules, tablets, and as a powder.</p>
<p>Not all natural oligosaccharides occur as components of glycoproteins or glycolipids. Some, such as the <a href="/wiki/Raffinose" title="Raffinose">raffinose</a> series, occur as storage or transport <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a> in plants. Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Maltodextrin" title="Maltodextrin">maltodextrins</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellodextrins" title="Cellodextrins">cellodextrins</a>, result from the microbial breakdown of larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>.</p>
