<p><i><b>The 120 Days of Sodom, or the School of Libertinage</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<i>Les 120 Journées de Sodome ou l'école du libertinage</i>) is a novel by the French writer and nobleman <a href="/wiki/Marquis_de_Sade" title="Marquis de Sade">Donatien Alphonse François, Marquis de Sade</a>. Described as both pornographic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willsher.2C_Kim_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willsher.2C_Kim-2">[2]</a></sup> and erotic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it was written in 1785.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It tells the story of four wealthy male <a href="/wiki/Libertine" title="Libertine">libertines</a> who resolve to experience the ultimate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_pleasure" title="Sexual pleasure">sexual gratification</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orgies" title="Orgies">orgies</a>. To do this, they seal themselves away for four months in an inaccessible <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> in the heart of the Black Forest,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with a <a href="/wiki/Harem" title="Harem">harem</a> of 46 victims, mostly young male and female teenagers, and engage four female <a href="/wiki/Brothel" title="Brothel">brothel</a> keepers to tell the stories of their lives and adventures. The women's narratives form an inspiration for the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">sexual abuse</a> and torture of the victims, which gradually mounts in intensity and ends in their <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">slaughter</a>.</p>
<p>The work went unpublished until the early twentieth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2015_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2015-5">[5]</a></sup> In recent times it has been translated into many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Languages" title="Languages">languages</a>, including English, Japanese, Spanish, Russian and German. It remains a highly controversial book, having been <a href="/wiki/List_of_books_banned_by_governments" title="List of books banned by governments">banned by some governments</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> due to its explicit nature and themes of sexual violence and extreme cruelty, but remains of significant interest to students and historians.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_120_Days_of_Sodom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sade wrote <i>The 120 Days of <a href="/wiki/Sodom_and_Gomorrah" title="Sodom and Gomorrah">Sodom</a></i> in the space of thirty-seven days in 1785 while he was imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/Bastille" title="Bastille">Bastille</a>. Being short of writing materials and fearing confiscation, he wrote it in tiny writing on a continuous, twelve-metre-long (39.37 feet) roll of paper, made up of individual small pieces of paper smuggled into the prison and glued together. When the Bastille was stormed and looted on July 14, 1789, at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, Sade believed the work was lost forever and later wrote that he "wept tears of blood" over its loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2015_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2015-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the long scroll of paper on which it was written was found hidden in walls of his cell, having escaped the attentions of the looters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2015_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2015-5">[5]</a></sup> It was first published in 1904<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2015_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2015-5">[5]</a></sup> by the Berlin psychiatrist <a href="/wiki/Iwan_Bloch" title="Iwan Bloch">Iwan Bloch</a> (who used a pseudonym, "Dr. Eugen Dühren", to avoid controversy). It was not until the latter half of the 20th century that it became more widely available in countries such as United Kingdom, the United States and France. The original is located in the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Lettres_et_Manuscrits" title="Musée des Lettres et Manuscrits">Musée des Lettres et Manuscrits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, France. It was purchased from a Swiss collector for €7 Million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willsher.2C_Kim_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Willsher.2C_Kim-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessments">Assessments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_120_Days_of_Sodom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Assessments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sade described his work as "the most impure tale that has ever been told since the world began."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The first publisher of the work, Dr. Bloch, regarded its thorough categorisation of all manner of sexual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetishes" title="Fetishes">fetishes</a> as having "scientific importance...to doctors, jurists, and anthropologists." He equated it with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krafft-Ebing" title="Krafft-Ebing">Krafft-Ebing</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychopathia_Sexualis_(book)" title="Psychopathia Sexualis (book)">Psychopathia Sexualis</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">Feminist</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Simone_de_Beauvoir" title="Simone de Beauvoir">Simone de Beauvoir</a> wrote an essay titled "Must We Burn Sade?", protesting the destruction of <i>The 120 Days of Sodom</i> because of the light it sheds on humanity's darkest side when, in 1955, French authorities planned on destroying it and three other major works by Sade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Camille_Paglia" title="Camille Paglia">Camille Paglia</a> considers Sade's work a "satirical response to <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a>" in particular, and the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> concept of man's innate <a href="/wiki/Good_and_evil" title="Good and evil">goodness</a> in general. <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Deleuze" title="Gilles Deleuze">Gilles Deleuze</a> considers <i>The 120 Days</i> along with the rest of Sade's corpus in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Leopold_von_Sacher-Masoch" title="Leopold von Sacher-Masoch">Sacher-Masoch</a>, remarking, "the work of Sade and Masoch cannot be regarded as pornography; it merits the more exalted title of 'pornology' because its erotic language cannot be reduced to the elementary functions of ordering and describing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_120_Days_of_Sodom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The 120 Days of Sodom</i> is set in a remote <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> castle, high in the mountains and surrounded by forests, detached from the rest of the world, either at the end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a>'s reign or at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9gence" title="Régence">Régence</a>.</p>
<p>The novel takes place over five months, November to March. Four wealthy <a href="/wiki/Libertine" title="Libertine">libertines</a> lock themselves in a castle, the Château de Silling, along with a number of victims and accomplices. (The description of Silling matches Sade's own castle, the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Lacoste" title="Château de Lacoste">Château de Lacoste</a>.) Since they state that the sensations produced by the organs of hearing are the most erotic, they intend to listen to various tales of depravity from four veteran prostitutes, which will inspire them to engage in similar activities with their victims.</p>
<p>It is not a complete novel. Only the first section is written in detail. After that, the remaining three parts are written as a draft, in note form, with Sade's notes to himself still present in most translations. Either at the outset, or during the writing of the work, Sade had evidently decided he would not be able to complete it in full and elected to write out the remaining three-quarters in brief and finish it later.</p>
<p>The story does portray some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_humor" title="Black humor">black humor</a>, and Sade seems almost light-hearted in his introduction, referring to the reader as "friendly reader". In this introduction, he contradicts himself, at one point insisting that one should not be horrified by the 600 passions outlined in the story because everybody has their own tastes, but at the same time going out of his way to warn the reader of the horrors that lie ahead, suggesting that the reader should have doubts about continuing. Consequently, he glorifies as well as vilifies the four main protagonists, alternately declaring them freethinking heroes and debased villains, often in the same passage.</p>

<p>The four principal characters are incredibly wealthy men, who are libertine, incredibly ruthless, and "...lawless and without religion, whom crime amused, and whose only interest lay in his passions...and had nothing to obey but the imperious decrees of his perfidious lusts." It is no coincidence that they are authority figures in terms of their occupations. Sade despised religion and authority and in many of his works he enjoyed mocking them by portraying priests, bishops, judges and the like as sexual perverts and criminals. They are:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Duc de Blangis – aged fifty, an <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocrat</a> who acquired his wealth by poisoning his mother for the purposes of inheritance, prescribing the same fate to his sister when she found out about his plot. Blangis is described as being tall, strongly built and highly sexually potent, although it is emphasised that he is a complete coward, and proud of it too.</li>
<li>The Bishop (l’Évêque) – Blangis' brother. He is forty-five, a scrawny and weak man, "with a nasty mouth." Very much given to sodomy, especially passive sodomy. He enjoys combining murder with sex. He refuses to have vaginal intercourse.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_120_Days_of_Sodom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novel is set out to a strict timetable. For each of the first four months, November to February, the prostitutes take turns to tell five stories each day, relating to the fetishes of their most interesting clients, and thus totalling 150 stories for each month (in theory at least; Sade made a few mistakes as he was apparently unable to go back and review his work as he went along). These passions are separated into four categories – simple, complex, criminal, and murderous – escalating in complexity and savagery.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>November: the simple passions</b> – these anecdotes are the only ones written in detail. They are only considered 'simple' in terms of them not including actual <a href="/wiki/Sexual_penetration" title="Sexual penetration">sexual penetration</a>. The anecdotes include men who like to masturbate in the faces of seven-year-old girls, and indulge in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urine_drinking" title="Urine drinking">urine drinking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coprophagia" title="Coprophagia">coprophagia</a>/<a href="/wiki/Scatology" title="Scatology">scatology</a>. As they do throughout the story-telling sections, the four libertines – Blangis, the Bishop, Curval and Durcet – indulge in activities similar to those they've heard with their daughters and the kidnapped children.</li>
<li><b>December: the complex passions</b> – these anecdotes involve more extravagant perversions, such as men who vaginally rape female children, indulge in <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">flagellation</a>. Tales of men who indulge in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrilegious" title="Sacrilegious">sacrilegious</a> activities are also recounted, such as a man who enjoyed having sex with nuns whilst watching <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> being performed. The female children are deflowered vaginally during the evening orgies with other elements of that month's stories – such as whipping – occasionally thrown in.</li>
<li><b>January: the criminal passions</b> – tales are told of perverts who indulge in criminal activities, albeit stopping short of murder. They include men who <a href="/wiki/Anal_sex" title="Anal sex">sodomise</a> girls as young as three, men who prostitute their own daughters to other perverts and watch the proceedings, and others who mutilate women by tearing off fingers or burning them with red-hot pokers. During the month, the four libertines begin having anal sex with the sixteen male and female children who, along with the other victims, are treated more brutally as time goes on, with regular beatings and whippings.</li>
<li><b>February: the murderous passions</b> – the final 150 anecdotes are those involving murder. They include perverts who skin children alive, <a href="/wiki/Disembowelment" title="Disembowelment">disembowel</a> pregnant women, burn alive entire families, and kill newborn babies in front of their mothers. The final tale is the only one since the simple passions of November written in detail. It features the 'Hell Libertine' who <a href="/wiki/Masturbation" title="Masturbation">masturbates</a> whilst watching fifteen teenage girls being simultaneously tortured to death. During this month, the libertines brutally kill three of the four daughters they have between them, along with four of the female children and two of the male ones. The murder of one of the girls, 15-year-old Augustine, is described in great detail, with the tortures she is subjected to including having flesh stripped from her limbs, her vagina being mutilated and her intestines being pulled out of her sliced-open belly and burned.</li>
<li><b>March</b> – this is the shortest of the segments, Sade summarising things even more by this final point in the novel. He lists the days on which the surviving children and many of the other characters are disposed of, although he does not give any details. Instead he leaves a footnote to himself pointing out his intention on detailing things more in a future revision.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is perhaps significant that Sade was interested in the manner in which sexual fetishes are developed, as are his primary characters, who urge the storytellers to remind them, in later stages, as to what the client in that particular anecdote enjoyed doing in their younger years. There are therefore a number of recurring figures, such as a man who, in the early tales, enjoys pricking women's breasts with pins and, at his reappearance in the tales in the 'murderous passions' category, delights in killing women by raping them atop a bed of nails. At the end of the novel, Sade draws up a list of the characters with a note of those who were killed and when, and also those who survived.</p>
<p>In the novel it is normal, even routine, to enjoy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedophilic" title="Pedophilic">pedophilic</a> pleasure from interactions with very young children, both male and female. A lot of attention is given to <a href="/wiki/Coprophilia" title="Coprophilia">feces</a>, which the men consume as a delicacy. The place designated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shitting" title="Shitting">shitting</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">chapel</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptations">Film adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_120_Days_of_Sodom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Film adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the final vignette of <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Age_d%27Or" title="L'Age d'Or">L'Age d'Or</a></i> (1930), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrealist" title="Surrealist">surrealist</a> film directed by <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a> and written by Buñuel and <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Intertitle" title="Intertitle">intertitle</a> narration tells of an orgy of 120 days of depraved acts—a reference to <i>The 120 Days of Sodom</i>—and tells us that the survivors of the orgy are ready to emerge. From the door of a castle emerges the Duc de Blangis, who is supposed to look like a mythical figure. When a young girl runs out of the castle, the Duc comforts the girl, but then escorts her back inside. A loud scream is then heard and he reemerges with blood on his robes and missing his beard.</p>
<p>In 1975, <a href="/wiki/Pier_Paolo_Pasolini" title="Pier Paolo Pasolini">Pier Paolo Pasolini</a> turned the book into a film, <i><a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%B2,_or_the_120_Days_of_Sodom" title="Salò, or the 120 Days of Sodom">Salò, or the 120 Days of Sodom</a></i> (<i>Salò o le 120 giornate di Sodoma</i>). The film is transposed from 18th-century France to the last days of Mussolini's regime in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Republic of Salò</a>. <i>Salò</i> is commonly listed among the most controversial films ever made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_manuscript">Original manuscript</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_120_Days_of_Sodom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Original manuscript">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Viscount <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Noailles" title="Charles de Noailles">Charles de Noailles</a>, whose wife <a href="/wiki/Marie-Laure_de_Noailles" title="Marie-Laure de Noailles">Marie-Laure</a> was a direct descendant of de Sade, bought the manuscript in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciolino5_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciolino5-12">[12]</a></sup> It was inherited by their daughter Natalie, who kept it in a drawer on the family estate. She would occasionally bring it out and show it to guests, among them the writer <a href="/wiki/Italo_Calvino" title="Italo Calvino">Italo Calvino</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciolino5_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-sciolino5-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ms. de Noailles later entrusted the manuscript to a friend, Jean Grouet. In 1982, Grouet betrayed her trust and smuggled the Sodom manuscript into Switzerland, where he sold it to Gérard Nordmann for $60,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciolino5_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-sciolino5-12">[12]</a></sup> An international legal wrangle ensued, with a French court ordering it to be returned to the Noailles family, only to be overruled in 1998 by a Swiss court that declared it had been bought by the collector in good faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willsher.2C_Kim_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Willsher.2C_Kim-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ms. de Noailles filed suit in France, and in 1990 France's highest court ordered the return of the manuscript. Switzerland had not yet signed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_Convention_for_the_Safeguarding_of_Intangible_Cultural_Heritage" title="UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of Intangible Cultural Heritage">UNESCO convention</a> for restitution of stolen cultural objects, so de Noailles had to take the case through the Swiss courts. The Swiss federal court sided with Nordmann, ruling in 1998 that he had bought the manuscript in good faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciolino5_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-sciolino5-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was first put on display near Geneva in 2004. <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Lh%C3%A9ritier" title="Gérard Lhéritier">Gérard Lhéritier</a>, president and founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristophil" title="Aristophil">Aristophil</a>, a company specializing in rare manuscripts, bought the <a href="/wiki/Scroll" title="Scroll">scroll</a> for 7m Euros, and in 2014 put it on display at his <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Lettres_et_Manuscrits" title="Musée des Lettres et Manuscrits">Musée des Lettres et Manuscrits</a> (Museum of Letters and Manuscripts) in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willsher.2C_Kim_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Willsher.2C_Kim-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2015_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2015-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
