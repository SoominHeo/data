<p>The <b>Sorbian languages</b> (<a href="/wiki/Upper_Sorbian_language" title="Upper Sorbian language">Upper Sorbian</a>: <i>Serbsce</i>; <a href="/wiki/Lower_Sorbian_language" title="Lower Sorbian language">Lower Sorbian</a>: <i>Serbski</i>) are two closely related languages spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Sorbs" title="Sorbs">Sorbs</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavic</a> minority in the <a href="/wiki/Lusatia" title="Lusatia">Lusatia</a> region of eastern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. They are classified under the <a href="/wiki/West_Slavic_languages" title="West Slavic languages">West Slavic</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a>. Historically the languages have also been known as <b><a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Wendish_(disambiguation)" title="Wendish (disambiguation)">Wendish</a></b> or <b>Lusatian</b>. Their collective <a href="/wiki/ISO_639" title="ISO 639">ISO 639</a>-2 code is <tt>wen</tt>. They are closely related to <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kashubian_language" title="Kashubian language">Kashubian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faska_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Faska-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two literary languages: <b><a href="/wiki/Upper_Sorbian_language" title="Upper Sorbian language">Upper Sorbian</a></b> (<span lang="hsb" xml:lang="hsb"><i>hornjoserbsce</i></span>), spoken by about 40,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>, and <b><a href="/wiki/Lower_Sorbian_language" title="Lower Sorbian language">Lower Sorbian</a></b> (<span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>dolnoserbski</i></span>) spoken by about 10,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>. The area where the two languages are spoken is known as <a href="/wiki/Lusatia" title="Lusatia">Lusatia</a> (<span lang="hsb" xml:lang="hsb"><i>Łužica</i></span> in Upper Sorbian, <span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>Łužyca</i></span> in Lower Sorbian, or <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Lausitz</i></span> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorbian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the settlement of the formerly Germanic territories (the part largely corresponding to the former <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>) by the Sorbs' Slavic ancestors in the 5th and 6th centuries, the Sorbian language (or its predecessors) had been in use in much of what was the southern half of East Germany for several centuries, and still had its stronghold in (Upper and Lower) Lusatia, where it enjoys national protection and fostering to the present day. Outside Lusatia, it has been superseded by German, following official discrimination from the 13th century on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faska_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Faska-2">[2]</a></sup> The printed language developed around the main <a href="/wiki/Bible_translations_into_Sorbian" title="Bible translations into Sorbian">Bible translations into Sorbian</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorbian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Germany, Upper and Lower Sorbian are officially recognized and protected as minority languages. In the home areas of the Sorbs, both languages are officially equal to German.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="744" data-file-width="992" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg/220px-Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg/330px-Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg/440px-Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bautzen_Ortsschild.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A bilingual sign in <a href="/wiki/Bautzen" title="Bautzen">Bautzen</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Bautzen" title="Bautzen">Bautzen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Lusatia" title="Upper Lusatia">Upper Lusatia</a> is the centre of Upper Sorbian culture. Bilingual signs can be seen around the city, including the name of the city, "Bautzen/<span lang="hsb" xml:lang="hsb"><i>Budyšin</i></span>".</p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Cottbus" title="Cottbus">Cottbus</a> (<span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>Chóśebuz</i></span>) is considered the cultural centre of Lower Sorbian; here too bilingual signs are found.</p>
<p>Sorbian has also been spoken in the small Sorbian ("Wendish") settlement of <a href="/wiki/Serbin,_Texas" title="Serbin, Texas">Serbin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lee_County,_Texas" title="Lee County, Texas">Lee County, Texas</a>, and it is possible that a few speakers still remain there. Until recently newspapers were published in Sorbian there. The local dialect has been heavily influenced by surrounding speakers of German and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.</p>
<p>While the old German-derived labels "Wend" and "Wendish", which once denoted "Slav(ic)" generally, have been retained in American and Australian communities, they are today mostly unusual in place of "Sorb" and "Sorbian" with reference to Sorbian communities in Germany, because many Sorbs consider such words to be offensive.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_features">Linguistic features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorbian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistic features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both Upper and Lower Sorbian have the <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> for <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronouns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>; very few living Indo-European languages retain this as a productive feature of the grammar. For example, the word <span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>ruka</i></span> is used for one hand, <span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>ruce</i></span> for two hands, and <span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>ruki</i></span> for more than two hands. As with most of the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, Sorbians uses no articles.</p>
