<p><i><b>All Killer, No Filler</b></i> is the debut studio album by Canadian <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Sum_41" title="Sum 41">Sum 41</a>, released on May 7, 2001. It was certified <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> in the United States, Canada and in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite mixed reviews, the album was a commercial success, peaking at No. 13 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>. The single, "<a href="/wiki/Fat_Lip" title="Fat Lip">Fat Lip</a>" peaked at No. 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_Rock_(chart)" title="Mainstream Rock (chart)"><i>Billboard</i> Rock Tracks</a>. In recent years, the album has received highly positive reviews, and has been praised as the album that gained the band success. The album has been considered by critics and fans as a <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">pop punk</a> classic.</p>
<p>As of September 2011, the album has sold over 1.9 million copies in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=All_Killer,_No_Filler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>All Killer No Filler</i>, which was Sum 41's first full-length album, was released on May 7, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird26_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bird26-3">[3]</a></sup> The album's name is taken from a <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Lee_Lewis" title="Jerry Lee Lewis">Jerry Lee Lewis</a> compilation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird26_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bird26-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Fat_Lip" title="Fat Lip">Fat Lip</a>", the album's first single, achieved significant chart and commercial success; it topped the U.S. Billboard Modern Rock Tracks chart as well as many other charts around the world. The song remains the band's most successful song to date. After "Fat Lip", two more singles, entitled "<a href="/wiki/In_Too_Deep_(Sum_41_song)" title="In Too Deep (Sum 41 song)">In Too Deep</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Motivation_(Sum_41_song)" title="Motivation (Sum 41 song)">Motivation</a>", were released from the album. "In Too Deep" peaked at #10 on the Modern Rock Tracks chart, while "Motivation" peaked at #24 on the same chart. Though not singles, music videos were made for the tracks "Pain For Pleasure" and "Nothing on My Back". The album peaked at #13 on the Billboard 200 chart and at #9 on the Top Canadian Albums chart.</p>
<p>Two of the album's songs, namely "Fat Lip" and "In Too Deep", were featured in the film <a href="/wiki/American_Pie_2" title="American Pie 2">American Pie 2</a>. The song "Summer" was originally released on <i><a href="/wiki/Half_Hour_of_Power" title="Half Hour of Power">Half Hour of Power</a></i>, but was re-recorded for <i>All Killer No Filler</i>.</p>
<p>On April 6, 2011 a special edition of the album was released to celebrate the 10th anniversary of <i>All Killer No Filler</i>, exclusive to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Japan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Japan-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The album's style has been described as <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">pop punk</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-am_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-am-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Angelo_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Angelo-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Skate_punk" title="Skate punk">skate punk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beam_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beam-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NOFX" title="NOFX">NOFX</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Punk_in_Drublic" title="Punk in Drublic">Punk in Drublic</a></i> was a considerable influence on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Sum 41 vocalist <a href="/wiki/Deryck_Whibley" title="Deryck Whibley">Deryck Whibley</a> cites <a href="/wiki/Rancid_(band)" title="Rancid (band)">Rancid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennywise_(band)" title="Pennywise (band)">Pennywise</a> as influences on <i>All Killer, No Filler</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The band has mentioned <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a> as their main influence for the album. "I was about 14 when ‘<a href="/wiki/Dookie" title="Dookie">Dookie</a>’ came out," Sum 41 vocalist Deryck Whibley says. "I remember seeing the video for '<a href="/wiki/Basket_Case_(song)" title="Basket Case (song)">Basket Case</a>' for the first time... It had so much energy and it was so different. I'd never seen anything like it before. From then I was instant fan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolutepunk" title="Absolutepunk">Absolutepunk</a> described the album as "the album that your parents don't want you to discover at age 11 when you're just starting to think that school is bullshit and the only thing that really matters is that ridiculously cute girl who honestly treats you like shit", as well as adding "The lyrics are broad enough that everyone can relate to them, but specific enough that each song makes you think of a certain person or situation in your life. The perfect balance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbsolutePunk_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AbsolutePunk-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Steve Jocz wrote "Pain for Pleasure" in 10 minutes while he was on the toilet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McMahon20_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-McMahon20-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Despite initially receiving mixed reviews in 2001, the album has received retrospective acclaim. The album's change in critical response has been believed to be because of the band's success following the album's release. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> said "It would be a mistake to view Sum 41 as just another second-rate band cashing in on the early-'00s punk-pop boom, even if it did recruit Jerry Finn to produce All Killer No Filler." Top40.com ranked the album as the 9th greatest pop punk album of all time. The album was included in <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Sound" title="Rock Sound">Rock Sound</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 101 Modern Classics list at number 46.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The album was included at number 11 on <i>Rock Sound</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "The 51 Most Essential Pop Punk Albums of All Time" list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/BuzzFeed" title="BuzzFeed">BuzzFeed</a> included the album at number 4 on their "36 Pop Punk Albums You Need To Hear Before You F——ing Die" list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuzzFeed_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuzzFeed-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
