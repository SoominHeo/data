<p>A <b>lawsuit</b> (or <b>suit in law</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup>) is "a vernacular term for a suit, action, or cause instituted or depending between two private persons in the courts of law." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> The term refers to any proceeding by a party or parties against another in a court of law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sometimes, the term "lawsuit" is in reference to a civil action brought in a <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">court</a> of law in which a <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">plaintiff</a>, a party who claims to have incurred loss as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a>'s actions, demands a <a href="/wiki/Legal_remedy" title="Legal remedy">legal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Equitable_remedy" title="Equitable remedy">equitable remedy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a> is required to respond to the plaintiff's <a href="/wiki/Complaint" title="Complaint">complaint</a>. If the plaintiff is successful, <a href="/wiki/Judgment_(law)" title="Judgment (law)">judgment</a> is in the plaintiff's favor, and a variety of <a href="/wiki/Court_order" title="Court order">court orders</a> may be issued to enforce a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right" title="Right">right</a>, award damages, or impose a temporary or permanent <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunction</a> to prevent an act or compel an act. A <a href="/wiki/Declaratory_judgment" title="Declaratory judgment">declaratory judgment</a> may be issued to prevent future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_dispute" title="Legal dispute">legal disputes</a>.</p>
<p>A lawsuit may involve <a href="/wiki/Dispute_resolution" title="Dispute resolution">dispute resolution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Private_law" title="Private law">private law</a> issues between <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individuals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_entity" title="Business entity">business entities</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organizations</a>. A lawsuit may also enable the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> to be treated as if it were a private party in a civil case, as plaintiff, or <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a> regarding an injury, or may provide the state with a civil cause of action to enforce certain laws.</p>
<p>The conduct of a lawsuit is called <b>litigation</b>. The plaintiffs and defendants are called <i>litigants</i> and the attorneys representing them are called <i>litigators</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> The term <i>litigation</i> may also refer to <a href="/wiki/Criminal_procedure" title="Criminal procedure">criminal trial</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules_of_procedure_and_complications">Rules of procedure and complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rules of procedure and complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rules of criminal or <a href="/wiki/Civil_procedure" title="Civil procedure">civil procedure</a> govern the conduct of a lawsuit in the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> <a href="/wiki/Adversarial_system" title="Adversarial system">adversarial system</a> of dispute resolution. Procedural rules are constrained and informed by separate <a href="/wiki/Statutory_law" title="Statutory law">statutory laws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case laws</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Constitutional_provision&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Constitutional provision (page does not exist)">constitutional provisions</a> that define the rights of the parties to a lawsuit (see especially <a href="/wiki/Due_process" title="Due process">due process</a>), though the rules generally reflect this legal context on their face. The details of the procedure differ greatly from jurisdiction to jurisdiction, and often from court to court even within the same jurisdiction. These rules of the particular procedures are very important for litigants to know, because the litigants are the ones who dictate the timing and progression of the lawsuit. Litigants are responsible to obtain the suited result and the timing of reaching this result. Failure to comply with the procedural rules may result in serious limitations that can affect the ability of one to present claims or defenses at any subsequent trial, or even promote the dismissal of the lawsuit altogether.</p>
<p>Though the majority of lawsuits are settled before ever reaching a state of trial,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> they can still be very complicated to litigate. This is particularly true in <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federal</a> systems, where a federal court may be applying state law (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Erie_doctrine" title="Erie doctrine"><i>Erie</i> doctrine</a>, for example in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>), or vice versa. It is also possible for one state to apply the law of another in cases where additionally it may not be clear which level (or location) of court actually has <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> over the claim or <a href="/wiki/Personal_jurisdiction" title="Personal jurisdiction">personal jurisdiction</a> over the defendant, or whether the plaintiff has <a href="/wiki/Standing_(law)" title="Standing (law)">standing</a> to participate in a lawsuit. About 98 percent of civil cases in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_federal_courts" title="United States federal courts">United States federal courts</a> are resolved without a trial. Domestic courts are also often called upon to apply foreign law, or to act upon foreign defendants, over whom they may not even have the ability to even enforce a judgment if the defendant's assets are theoretically outside their reach.</p>
<p>Lawsuits can become additionally complicated as more parties become involved (see <a href="/wiki/Joinder" title="Joinder">joinder</a>). Within a "single" lawsuit, there can be any number of claims and defenses (all based on numerous laws) between any number of plaintiffs or defendants. Each of these participants can bring any number of cross claims and counterclaims against each other, and even bring additional parties into the suit on either side after it progresses. In reality however, courts typically have some power to sever claims and parties into separate actions if it is more efficient to do so. A court can do this if there is not a sufficient overlap of factual issues between the various associates, separating the issues into different lawsuits.</p>
<p>The official ruling of a lawsuit can be somewhat misleading because post-ruling outcomes are often not listed on the internet. For example, in the case of William J. Ralph Jr. v. Lind-Waldock &amp; Company,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> (September 1999) one would assume that Mr. Ralph lost the case when in fact, upon review of the evidence, it as found that Mr. Ralph was correct in his assertion that improper activity took place on the part of Lind-Waldock, and Mr. Ralph settled with Lind-Waldock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cases such as this illustrate the need for more comprehensive information than mere internet searches when researching legal decisions. While online searches are appropriate for many legal situations, they are not appropriate for all.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following is a generalized description of how a lawsuit may proceed in a common law jurisdiction:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pleading">Pleading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pleading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pleading" title="Pleading">Pleading</a></div>
<p>A lawsuit begins when a complaint or petition is filed with the court. This complaint should explicitly state that one or more plaintiffs seek(s) damages or <a href="/wiki/Equity_(law)" title="Equity (law)">equitable relief</a> from one or more stated defendants, and also should identify the legal and factual bases for doing so. It is important that the "plaintiff selects the proper venue with the proper jurisdiction to bring his lawsuit." The clerk of a court signs or stamps the court seal upon a <a href="/wiki/Summons" title="Summons">summons</a> or citation, which is then <a href="/wiki/Service_of_process" title="Service of process">served</a> by the plaintiff upon the defendant, together with a copy of the complaint. This service notifies the defendants that they are being sued and that they are limited in the amount of time of a reply. The service provides a copy of the complaint in order to notify the defendants of the nature of the claims. Once the defendants are served with the summons and complaint, they are subject to a time limit to file an <a href="/wiki/Answer" title="Answer">answer</a> stating their defenses to the plaintiff's claims, which includes any challenges to the court's jurisdiction, and any counterclaims they wish to assert against the plaintiff.</p>
<p>In a handful of jurisdictions (notably, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>) a lawsuit begins when one or more plaintiffs properly serve a summons and complaint upon the defendant(s). In such jurisdictions, nothing must be filed with the court until a dispute develops requiring actual judicial intervention. A <a class="external text" href="http://www.americanbar.org/groups/public_education/resources/law_related_education_network/how_courts_work/pleadings.html/" rel="nofollow">complaint</a> can be considered one of the more important steps in pleading during a civil case. This document states the actions that the plaintiff intends to take and also the factual and reasonable basis for the actions. This essentially sets the framework for the entire case and the claim that will be held noteworthy throughout the entire lawsuit.</p>
<p>If the defendant chooses to file an answer within the time permitted, the answer must address each of the plaintiffs' allegations. The defendant has three choices to make, which include either admitting to the allegation, denying it, or pleading a lack of sufficient information to admit or deny the allegation. Some jurisdictions, like California, still authorize general denials of each and every allegation in the complaint. At the time the defendant files an answer, the defendant also raises all "affirmative" defenses. The defendant may also assert counterclaims for damages or equitable relief against the plaintiff. For example, in the case of "compulsory counterclaims," the defendant must assert some form of counterclaim or risk having the counterclaim barred in any subsequent proceeding. In the case of making a counterclaim, the defendant is making a motion directed towards the plaintiff claiming that he/she was injured in some way or would like to sue the plaintiff. The plaintiff in this example would then receive some amount of time to make a reply to this counterclaim. The defendant may also file a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_party_complaint" title="Third party complaint">third party complaint</a>", which is the defendant's privilege to join another party or parties in the action with the belief that those parties may be liable for some or all of the plaintiff's claimed damages. An answer from the defendant in response to the claims made against him/her, can also include additional facts or a so-called "excuse" for the plead. Filing an answer "joins the cause" and moves the case into the pre-trial phase.</p>
<p>Instead of filing an answer within the time specified in the summons, the defendant can choose to dispute the validity of the complaint by filing a <a href="/wiki/Demurrer" title="Demurrer">demurrer</a> (in the handful of jurisdictions where that is still allowed) or one or more "pre-answer motions," such as a motion to dismiss. It is important that the motion be filed within the time period specified in the summons for an answer. If all of the above motions are denied by the trial court, and the defendant loses on all appeals from such denials (if that option is available), and finally the defendant <i>must</i> file an answer.</p>
<p>Usually the <a href="/wiki/Pleading" title="Pleading">pleadings</a> are drafted by a <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a>, but in many courts persons can file papers and represent themselves, which is called appearing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro_se" title="Pro se">pro se</a></i>. Many courts have a <a href="/wiki/Pro_se_clerk" title="Pro se clerk"><i>pro se</i> clerk</a> to assist people without lawyers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pretrial_discovery">Pretrial discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pretrial discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Discovery_(law)" title="Discovery (law)">Discovery (law)</a></div>
<p>A pretrial discovery can be defined as "the formal process of exchanging information between the parties about the witnesses and evidence they’ll present at trial" and allows for the evidence of the trial to be presented to the parties before the initial trial begins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> The early stages of the lawsuit may involve initial disclosures of evidence by each party and <a href="/wiki/Discovery_(law)" title="Discovery (law)">discovery</a>, which is the structured exchange of <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">evidence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(law)" title="Deposition (law)">statements</a> between the parties. Discovery is meant to eliminate surprises, clarify what the lawsuit is about, and also to make the parties decide if they should settle or drop <a href="/wiki/Frivolous_litigation" title="Frivolous litigation">frivolous claims</a> and/or defenses. At this point the parties may also engage in pretrial motions to exclude or include particular legal or factual issues before trial.</p>
<p>There is also the ability of one to make an under oath statement during the pretrial, also known as a deposition. The deposition can be used in the trial or just in the pretrial, but this allows for both parties to be aware of the arguments or claims that are going to be made by the other party in the trial. It is notable that the depositions can be written or oral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the close of discovery, the parties may either pick a <a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">jury</a> and then have a <a href="/wiki/Jury_trial" title="Jury trial">trial by jury</a> or the case may proceed as a bench trial. A bench trial is only heard by the judge if the parties waive a jury trial or if the right to a jury trial is not guaranteed for their particular claim (such as those under <a href="/wiki/Equity_(law)" title="Equity (law)">equity</a> in the U.S.) or for any lawsuits within their jurisdiction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resolution">Resolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Resolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Trial" title="Trial">Trial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judgment_(law)" title="Judgment (law)">Judgment (law)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">Settlement (litigation)</a></div>
<p>Usually, lawsuits end in a settlement, with an empirical analysis finding that less than 2% of cases end with a trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[9]</a></sup> It is sometimes said that 95% of cases end in settlement; few jurisdictions report settlements, but empirical analysis suggests that the settlement rate varies by type of lawsuit, with torts settling around 90% of the time and overall civil cases settling 50% of the time; other cases end due to <a href="/wiki/Default_judgment" title="Default judgment">default judgment</a>, lack of a valid claim, and other reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>At trial, each person presents witnesses and the evidence collected is recorded. After this occurs, the judge or jury renders their decision. Generally speaking, the plaintiff has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_burden_of_proof" title="Legal burden of proof">burden of proof</a> in making his claims, however, the defendant may have the burden of proof on other issues, such as <a href="/wiki/Affirmative_defense" title="Affirmative defense">affirmative defenses</a>. The attorneys are held responsible in devising a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial_strategy" title="Trial strategy">trial strategy</a> that ensures they meet the necessary elements of their case or (when the opposing party has the burden of proof) to ensure the opponent will not be able to meet his or her burden.</p>
<p>There are numerous motions that either party can file throughout the lawsuit to terminate it "prematurely"—before submission to the judge or jury for final consideration. These motions attempt to persuade the judge, through legal argument and sometimes accompanying evidence, that there is no reasonable way that the other party could legally win and therefore there is no sense in continuing with the trial. Motions for <a href="/wiki/Summary_judgment" title="Summary judgment">summary judgment</a>, for example, can usually be brought before, after, or during the actual presentation of the case. Motions can also be brought after the close of a trial to undo a jury verdict contrary to law or against the weight of the evidence, or to convince the judge to change the decision or grant a new trial.</p>
<p>Also, at any time during this process from the filing of the complaint to the final judgment, the plaintiff may withdraw the complaint and end the whole matter, or the defendant may agree to a settlement. If the case settles, the parties might choose to enter into a stipulated judgment with the settlement agreement attached, or the plaintiff may simply file a <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_dismissal" title="Voluntary dismissal">voluntary dismissal</a>, so that the settlement agreement is never entered into the court record.</p>
<p>The decisions that the jury makes are not put into effect until the judge makes a judgment, which is the approval to have this trial information be filed in public records. In a civil case, the judge is allowed at this time to make changes to the verdict that the jury came up with by either adding on or reducing the punishment. In criminal cases the situation is a little different, because in this case the judge does not have the authority to change the jury decision.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appeal">Appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Appeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">Appeal</a></div>
<p>After a final decision has been made, either party or both may <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a> from the judgment if they believe there had been a procedural error made by the trial court. It isn't necessarily an automatic appeal after every judgment has been made, however, if there is a legal basis for the appeal, then one has the right to do so. The prevailing party may appeal, for example, if they wanted a larger award than was granted. The <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">appellate court</a> (which may be structured as an intermediate appellate court) and/or a higher court then affirms the judgment, declines to hear it (which effectively affirms it), reverses—or vacates and remands. This process would then involve sending the lawsuit back to the lower trial court to address an unresolved issue, or possibly request for a whole new trial. Some lawsuits go up and down the appeals ladder repeatedly before final resolution.</p>
<p>The appeal is a review for errors rather than a new trial, so the appellate court will defer to the discretion of the original trial court if an error is not clear. The initial step in making an appeal consists of the petitioner filing a notice of appeal and then sending in a brief, a written document stating reason for appeal, to the court. Decisions of the court can be made immediately after just reading the written brief, or there can also be oral arguments made by both parties involved in the appeal. The appellate court then makes the decision about what errors were made when the law was looked at more closely in the lower court. There were no errors made, the case would then end, but if the decision was reversed, the appellate court would then send the case back down to the lower court level. There, a new trial will be held and new information taken into account.</p>
<p>Some jurisdictions, notably the United States, but prevalent in many other countries, prevent parties from relitigating the facts on appeal, due to a history of unscrupulous lawyers deliberately reserving such issues in order to ambush each other in the appellate courts (the "invited error" problem). The idea is that it is more efficient to force all parties to fully litigate all relevant issues of fact before the trial court. Thus, a party who does not raise an issue of fact at the trial court level generally cannot raise it on appeal.</p>
<p>When the lawsuit is finally resolved, or the allotted time to appeal has expired, the matter is <i><a href="/wiki/Res_judicata" title="Res judicata">res judicata</a></i>, meaning the plaintiff may not bring another action based on the same claim again. In addition, other parties who later attempt to re-litigate a matter already ruled on in a previous lawsuit will be <i><a href="/wiki/Estoppel" title="Estoppel">estopped</a></i> from doing so.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enforcement">Enforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a final judgment is entered, the plaintiff is usually barred under the doctrine of <i><a href="/wiki/Res_judicata" title="Res judicata">res judicata</a></i> from relitigating any of the issues, even under different legal theories. Judgments are typically a monetary award. If the defendant fails to pay, the court has various powers to seize any of the defendant's assets located within its jurisdiction, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Writ_of_execution" title="Writ of execution">Writ of execution</a></li>
<li>Bank account <a href="/wiki/Garnishment" title="Garnishment">garnishment</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lien" title="Lien">Liens</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wage_garnishment" title="Wage garnishment">Wage garnishment</a></li>
</ul>
<p>If all assets are located elsewhere, the plaintiff must file another suit in the appropriate court to seek enforcement of the other court's previous judgment. This can be a difficult task when crossing from a court in one state or nation to another, however, courts tend to grant each other respect when there is not a clear legal rule to the contrary. A defendant who has no assets in <i>any</i> jurisdiction is said to be "judgment-proof."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dionne000_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dionne000-11">[10]</a></sup> The term is generally a colloquialism to describe an impecunious defendant.</p>
<p>Indigent judgment-proof defendants are no longer imprisoned; debtor's prisons have been outlawed by statute, constitutional amendment, or international human rights treaties in the vast majority of common law jurisdictions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 18th and 19th centuries, it was common for lawyers to speak of bringing an "action" at law and a "suit" in <a href="/wiki/Equity_(law)" title="Equity (law)">equity</a>. An example of that distinction survives today in the text of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Act_of_1871" title="Civil Rights Act of 1871">Civil Rights Act of 1871</a>. The fusion of common law and equity in England in the <a href="/wiki/Judicature_Acts" title="Judicature Acts">Judicature Acts</a> of 1873 and 1875 led to the collapse of that distinction, so it became possible to speak of a "lawsuit." In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Rules_of_Civil_Procedure" title="Federal Rules of Civil Procedure">Federal Rules of Civil Procedure</a> (1938) abolished the distinction between actions at law and suits in equity in federal practice, in favor of a single form referred to as a "civil action."</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> the term "claim" is far more common; the person initiating proceedings is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claimant" title="Claimant">claimant</a>.</p>
<p>American terminology is slightly different, in that the term "claim" refers only to a particular count (or cause of action) in a lawsuit. Americans also use "claim" to describe a demand filed with an insurer or administrative agency. If the claim is denied, then the claimant (or policyholder or applicant) files a lawsuit with the courts and later participates in the lawsuit.</p>
<p>In medieval times, both "action" and "suit" had the approximate meaning of some kind of legal proceeding, but an action terminated when a judgment was rendered, while a suit also included the execution of the judgment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing">Financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawsuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Particularly in the United States, plaintiffs and defendants who lack financial resources for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litigation" title="Litigation">litigation</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Attorney%27s_fee" title="Attorney's fee">attorney's fees</a> may be able to obtain <a href="/wiki/Legal_financing" title="Legal financing">legal financing</a>. Legal financing companies can provide a cash advance to litigants in return for a share of the ultimate <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a> or award. If the case ultimately loses, the litigant does not have to pay any of the money funded back. <a href="/wiki/Legal_financing" title="Legal financing">Legal financing</a> is different from a typical bank <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loan</a> in that the legal financing company does not look at credit history or employment history. Litigants do not have to repay the cash advance with monthly payments, but do have to fill out an application so that the legal financing company can review the merits of the case.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Legal_financing" title="Legal financing">Legal financing</a> can be a practical means for litigants to obtain financing while they wait for a monetary settlement or an award in their <a href="/wiki/Personal_injury" title="Personal injury">personal injury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_compensation" title="Workers' compensation">workers' compensation</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> lawsuit. Often, plaintiffs who were injured or forced to leave their jobs still have <a href="/wiki/Mortgage_loan" title="Mortgage loan">mortgages</a>, rent, medical expenses, or other bills to pay. Other times, litigants may simply need money to pay for the costs of litigation and attorneys' fees, and for this reason, many litigants turn to reputable legal financing companies to apply for a cash advance to help pay for bills.</p>
<p>Defendants, civil rights organizations, public interest organizations, and government public officials can all set up an account to pay for litigation costs and legal expenses. These <a href="/wiki/Legal_defense_fund" title="Legal defense fund">legal defense funds</a> can have large membership counts where the members contribute to the fund. Unlike legal financing from legal financing companies, legal defense funds provide a separate account for litigation rather than a one-time cash advancement, nevertheless, both are used for purposes of financing litigation and legal costs.</p>
<p>There was a study conducted in the <i>Supreme Court Economic Review</i> that shows why litigation financing can be practical and beneficial to the overall court system and lawsuits within the court. This study concluded that the new rules that were set for litigation financing actually did produce more settlements. Under conservative rules, there tended to be fewer settlements, however under the older rules they tended to be larger on average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Legal financing can become an issue in some cases, varying from case to case and person to person. It can be beneficial in many situations, however also detrimental in others.</p>
