<p><i><b>Limnophila aromatica</b></i> (synonym: <i><b>Limnophila chinensis</b></i> <b>var.</b> <i><b>aromatica</b></i>; also called <b>rice paddy herb</b>) is a tropical <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Plantago" title="Plantago">plantain</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Plantaginaceae" title="Plantaginaceae">Plantaginaceae</a>. It is native to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, where it flourishes in hot temperatures and grows most often in watery environments, particularly in flooded rice fields. It is called <i>NgÃ² Ã´m</i> or <i>ngÃ² om</i> or <i>ngá»</i> in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and used as an herb and also cultivated for use as an <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquarium</a> plant. The plant was introduced to North America in the 1970s due to Vietnamese immigration following the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. It is called "roum om" (ááá¢á) in Khmer or Phnom Penh dialect "ma om" (ááá¢á). It is used in all traditional Cambodian soup dishes and most often in Vietnamese cuisine. It can grow in flooded rice paddies during <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a> but it grows best on drained but still wet sandy soil of harvested rice paddies for a few months after the rainy season ended. It dies out soon after it flowers. Rural Cambodians often harvest them and put them on the roof of their houses to dry for later use.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnophila_aromatica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Limnophila aromatica</i> was formerly classified as a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Figwort" title="Figwort">figwort</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Scrophulariaceae" title="Scrophulariaceae">Scrophulariaceae</a>, but is now classified in plantain family, Plantaginaceae.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">Culinary use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnophila_aromatica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Culinary use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>L. aromatica</i> has a flavor and aroma reminiscent of both <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cumin" title="Cumin">cumin</a>. It is used most often in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_Vietnam" title="Cuisine of Vietnam">Vietnamese cuisine</a>, where it is called <i>ngÃ² om</i>. It is an ingredient in <i><a href="/wiki/Canh_chua" title="Canh chua">canh chua</a></i>, a sweet and sour seafood soup which also includes <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a>,not to be confused with <i><a href="/wiki/Eryngium_foetidum" title="Eryngium foetidum">ngÃ² gai</a></i> which is also added as an accompaniment to the noodle soup called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph%E1%BB%9F" title="Phá»">phá»</a></i>. In <a href="/wiki/Thai_cuisine" title="Thai cuisine">Thai cuisine</a> it is known as <i>phak kayang</i> and is also used to make om.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
