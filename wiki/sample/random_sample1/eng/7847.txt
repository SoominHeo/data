<p><b>Sócrates Brasileiro Sampaio de Souza Vieira de Oliveira</b>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">MD</a> (19 February 1954 – 4 December 2011), simply known as <b>Sócrates</b>, was a Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">footballer</a> who played as an <a href="/wiki/Midfielder#Attacking_midfielder" title="Midfielder">attacking midfielder</a>. His doctorate in <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">medicine</a> and his political awareness earned him the nickname "Doctor Socrates".</p>
<p>Easily recognizable for his beard and headband, Sócrates became the "symbol of cool for a whole generation of football supporters".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obituary_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-2">[2]</a></sup> He was considered to be one of the greatest midfielders ever to play the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1983, he was named <a href="/wiki/South_American_Footballer_of_the_Year" title="South American Footballer of the Year">South American Footballer of the Year</a>. In 2004 he was named by <a href="/wiki/Pel%C3%A9" title="Pelé">Pelé</a> in the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_100" title="FIFA 100">FIFA 100</a> list of the world's greatest living players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sócrates played for <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil</a> for seven years, scoring 22 goals and representing the nation in two <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">World Cups</a>. He <a href="/wiki/Captain_(association_football)" title="Captain (association football)">captained</a> the team in the <a href="/wiki/1982_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1982 FIFA World Cup">1982 FIFA World Cup</a>; playing in midfield alongside <a href="/wiki/Zico" title="Zico">Zico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paulo_Roberto_Falc%C3%A3o" title="Paulo Roberto Falcão">Falcão</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89der_Aleixo_de_Assis" title="Éder Aleixo de Assis">Éder</a>, considered one of the greatest Brazilian national teams ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_noflair_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_noflair-5">[5]</a></sup> He also appeared in the <a href="/wiki/1979_Copa_Am%C3%A9rica" title="1979 Copa América">1979</a> and <a href="/wiki/1983_Copa_Am%C3%A9rica" title="1983 Copa América">1983 Copa América</a>. At club level, Sócrates played for <a href="/wiki/Botafogo_Futebol_Clube_(SP)" title="Botafogo Futebol Clube (SP)">Botafogo-SP</a> before joining <a href="/wiki/Sport_Club_Corinthians_Paulista" title="Sport Club Corinthians Paulista">Corinthians</a> in 1978. He moved to Italy to play for <a href="/wiki/ACF_Fiorentina" title="ACF Fiorentina">Fiorentina</a>, returning to Brazil in 1985 to end his career.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_career">Club career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Club career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sócrates was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bel%C3%A9m_do_Par%C3%A1,_Brazil" title="Belém do Pará, Brazil">Belém do Pará</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obituary_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-2">[2]</a></sup> He began playing football professionally in 1974 for <a href="/wiki/Botafogo_Futebol_Clube_(SP)" title="Botafogo Futebol Clube (SP)">Botafogo-SP</a> in his native <a href="/wiki/Ribeir%C3%A3o_Preto" title="Ribeirão Preto">Ribeirão Preto</a>, but spent the majority of his career (1978 to 1984) with <a href="/wiki/Sport_Club_Corinthians_Paulista" title="Sport Club Corinthians Paulista">Corinthians</a>, scoring 41 goals in 59 <a href="/wiki/Campeonato_Brasileiro_S%C3%A9rie_A" title="Campeonato Brasileiro Série A">Série A</a> games, and 172 goals in 297 matches in total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formidable_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-formidable-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1984%E2%80%9385_Serie_A" title="1984–85 Serie A">1984–85</a>, aged 30, Sócrates had his first experience abroad, playing in <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> with <a href="/wiki/ACF_Fiorentina" title="ACF Fiorentina">Fiorentina</a>. He returned to his country after that sole season, representing <a href="/wiki/Clube_de_Regatas_do_Flamengo" title="Clube de Regatas do Flamengo">Flamengo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santos_FC" title="Santos FC">Santos</a> and former club Botafogo-SP, and retiring in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formidable_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-formidable-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, more than a decade after retiring, 50-year-old Sócrates agreed to a one-month player-coaching deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garforth_Town_F.C." title="Garforth Town F.C.">Garforth Town</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Counties_East_Football_League" title="Northern Counties East Football League">Northern Counties East Football League</a> in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He made his only appearance for the club on 20 November, against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tadcaster_Albion_F.C." title="Tadcaster Albion F.C.">Tadcaster Albion</a>, coming on as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substitute_(football)" title="Substitute (football)">substitute</a> twelve minutes from time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factfile_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-factfile-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_career">International career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sócrates was capped 60 times for <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil</a> between May 1979 and June 1986, scoring 22 goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factfile_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-factfile-8">[8]</a></sup> He <a href="/wiki/Captain_(association_football)" title="Captain (association football)">captained</a> the national team at the <a href="/wiki/1982_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1982 FIFA World Cup">1982 FIFA World Cup</a>, and also appeared in the <a href="/wiki/1986_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1986 FIFA World Cup">1986 World Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formidable_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-formidable-6">[6]</a></sup> In the latter edition, he scored twice, starting with the game's only goal against <a href="/wiki/Spain_national_football_team" title="Spain national football team">Spain</a> in the group stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> he added another in the <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament" title="Single-elimination tournament">round-of-16</a> 4–0 win over <a href="/wiki/Poland_national_football_team" title="Poland national football team">Poland</a>, shooting his <a href="/wiki/Penalty_kick_(association_football)" title="Penalty kick (association football)">penalty kick</a> without running; in the following game, against <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France</a>, he tried to convert it in the same fashion, but had his <a href="/wiki/Penalty_shoot-out_(association_football)" title="Penalty shoot-out (association football)">shootout</a> attempt saved by <a href="/wiki/Goalkeeper_(association_football)" title="Goalkeeper (association football)">goalkeeper</a> <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%ABl_Bats" title="Joël Bats">Joël Bats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7SINS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-7SINS-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sócrates also represented his country at the <a href="/wiki/1979_Copa_Am%C3%A9rica" title="1979 Copa América">1979</a> and <a href="/wiki/1983_Copa_Am%C3%A9rica" title="1983 Copa América">1983 Copa América</a> tournaments. In the latter he appeared in only one game, the second leg of <a href="/wiki/1983_Copa_Am%C3%A9rica#Finals" title="1983 Copa América">the final</a> against <a href="/wiki/Uruguay_national_football_team" title="Uruguay national football team">Uruguay</a> (1–1 home draw, 1–3 aggregate loss).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_of_play">Style of play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style of play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sócrates was a technical <a href="/wiki/Playmaker" title="Playmaker">playmaker</a>, known for great through passes and his vision on the field, as well as his physical strength. He was also a two-footed player and a prolific goal scorer. His ability to read the game was highly valued, and his signature move was the blind heel pass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He was a key member of the Brazilian national team of the early to mid-1980s, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Wilson_(writer)" title="Jonathan Wilson (writer)">Jonathan Wilson</a> said that "Socrates was the brain of Brazil. He might not quite have had the flair of <a href="/wiki/Zico" title="Zico">Zico</a>, but he was the central intelligence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Former coach at Fiorentina, <a href="/wiki/Giancarlo_De_Sisti" title="Giancarlo De Sisti">Giancarlo De Sisti</a>, said: "Socrates was a very intelligent man, he had great class."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sócrates was the firstborn child of Raimundo and Guiomar Vieira. He was born in Belém, Pará, and relocated with his family to Ribeirão Preto, São Paulo, in January 1960 after his father Raimundo earned an important position as revenue supervisor. This job acquisition earned Sócrates' father the status of a small-town hero in Igarapé-Açu, where the family lived at the time. His father's new salary allowed Sócrates to attend the best school in Ribeirão Preto, Colégio Marista. In a biography written by the journalist Tom Cardoso, it is revealed that the small library Sócrates' father had built in his home, containing philosophy books and other works, came under threat as of the coup d'état on March 31, 1964. Sócrates watched his father rid himself of books that he so loved; "In 1964, I saw my father tear many books, because of the revolution. I thought that was absurd, because the library was the thing he liked best. That was when I felt that something was not right. But I only understood well much later, in college." At age 8, Sócrates was exposed to the repercussions of the military dictatorship's censorship. His childhood was marked by this event which he came to comprehend as an adult later in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sócrates lived in Ribeirão Preto with his wife and six children. He was a columnist for a number of newspapers and magazines, writing not only about sports, but also politics and economics. He frequently appeared on Brazilian TV programmes as a football pundit. At the time of his death, Sócrates was writing a fictional book about the 2014 World Cup in Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sócrates was a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">doctor of medicine</a>, a rare achievement for a professional footballer (he was a graduate of the <a href="/wiki/Faculdade_de_Medicina_de_Ribeir%C3%A3o_Preto" title="Faculdade de Medicina de Ribeirão Preto">Faculdade de Medicina de Ribeirão Preto</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Even rarer is the fact that he earned the degree while concurrently playing professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a>. After retiring as a player he practised medicine at <a href="/wiki/Ribeir%C3%A3o_Preto" title="Ribeirão Preto">Ribeirão Preto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obituary_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was also noted for being an intellectual, a heavy drinker and a smoker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7SINS_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-7SINS-10">[10]</a></sup> His younger brother <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%AD" title="Raí">Raí</a> was also a footballer and an attacking midfielder, being a member of the Brazilian team that won the World Cup in <a href="/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1994 FIFA World Cup">1994</a>, notably playing for <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_FC" title="São Paulo FC">São Paulo</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Paris_Saint-Germain_F.C." title="Paris Saint-Germain F.C.">Paris Saint-Germain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAI_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-RAI-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his time at Corinthians, Sócrates co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Corinthians_Democracy" title="Corinthians Democracy">Corinthians Democracy</a> movement, in opposition to the then-ruling <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_military_government" title="Brazilian military government">military government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Sócrates and his team mates protested against the regime's treatment of footballers, and showed support to the wider movement for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratisation" title="Democratisation">democratisation</a>, by wearing shirts with "Democracia" written on them during games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soc_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-soc-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 16, 1984 he spoke out in support of <a href="/wiki/Diretas_J%C3%A1" title="Diretas Já">Diretas Já</a> (Free Elections Now), a popular movement that called for direct presidential elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In <a class="external text" href="http://www.aljazeera.com/programmes/footballrebels/2013/03/2013312145718474996.html" rel="nofollow"><i>Socrates and the Corinthians' Democracy</i></a> Juca Kfouri, a Brazilian journalist, recalls how, "Socrates took the risk of saying, in front of two million people gathered on the cathedral square, that if direct presidential elections weren’t accepted by the regime, he’d go play in Italy.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> By hinging his transfer abroad on the outcome of a constitutional amendment, Socrates' political legacy began to form. His denunciation of the military dictatorship and fight to redemocratize Brazil extended his legacy beyond the football field.</p>
<p>Sócrates stated that three of his childhood heroes were <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He also said that "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lula" title="Lula">Lula</a> was good" but that he had "earned a mere seven or so out of ten" for his way of governing Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_death">Legacy and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pel%C3%A9" title="Pelé">Pelé</a> named Sócrates in the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_100" title="FIFA 100">FIFA 100</a> list of the world's greatest living players in March 2004 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Soccer_Magazine" title="World Soccer Magazine">World Soccer</a> named him one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_soccer_players_of_the_20th_century" title="World soccer players of the 20th century">100 best footballers</a> in history. In October 2008, he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team#Brazilian_Football_Museum_.E2.80.93_Hall_of_Fame" title="Brazil national football team">Pacaembu Brazilian Football Museum Hall of Fame</a>.</p>
<p>The Brazilian's health started to deteroriate, on 19 August 2011, Sócrates was admitted to intensive care in the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_Hospital" title="Albert Einstein Hospital">Albert Einstein Hospital</a> in São Paulo with <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_bleeding" title="Gastrointestinal bleeding">gastrointestinal bleeding</a> secondary to <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a> and was discharged nine days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The following month he spent 17 days in hospital with a liver ailment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On 1 December 2011, he was hospitalised with food poisoning which developed into <a href="/wiki/Septic_shock" title="Septic shock">septic shock</a> and he was put on life-support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He died on 4 December 2011 at the age of 57.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He was survived by his wife and six children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/President_of_Brazil" title="President of Brazil">President of Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dilma_Rousseff" title="Dilma Rousseff">Dilma Rousseff</a>, paid tribute, saying Brazil had lost "one of its most cherished sons". "On the field, with his talent and sophisticated touches, he was a genius. Off the field... he was active politically, concerned with his people and his country."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corinthians fans held up signs in tribute and there was a moment of silence before the team's match against Palmeiras (a 0–0 draw which secured Corinthians their first Brazilian title for six years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup> The result matched a professed desire of Sócrates, who had once stated his wish "to die on a Sunday when Corinthians won a trophy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Fiorentina held a minute's silence before their league match against <a href="/wiki/A.S._Roma" title="A.S. Roma">Roma</a>, and the players wore black armbands in tribute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Former Brazil striker <a href="/wiki/Ronaldo_(Brazilian_footballer)" title="Ronaldo (Brazilian footballer)">Ronaldo</a> tweeted: "Sad start to the day. Rest in peace Dr. Socrates."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup> Zico called him "unique".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup> Italy's <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Rossi" title="Paolo Rossi">Paolo Rossi</a> described the death as "a piece of our history that's broken off and gone away".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_dies_at_57_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_dies_at_57-3">[3]</a></sup> Garforth chairman Simon Clifford paid tribute to the "great grace" of Sócrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garforth_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-garforth-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myths">Myths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%B3crates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Myths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a persistent myth that Sócrates studied medicine in Dublin, Ireland, and that during this time he played reserve football for <a href="/wiki/University_College_Dublin_A.F.C." title="University College Dublin A.F.C.">University College Dublin</a>. The rumour gained some credibility following articles in several newspapers apparently confirming it, in one case even citing a confirmation by a named source within the <a href="/wiki/Football_Association_of_Ireland" title="Football Association of Ireland">Football Association of Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The story is, however, untrue, and has been debunked in other newspaper articles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and denied by the Dublin college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
