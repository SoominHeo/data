<p><b>Soft serve</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a> that is softer than regular ice creams as a result of air being introduced during freezing. Soft serve ice cream has been sold commercially since the late 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_serve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> weekend of 1934, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Carvel" title="Tom Carvel">Tom Carvel</a>, the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Carvel_(restaurant)" title="Carvel (restaurant)">Carvel</a> brand and franchise, suffered a flat tire in his ice cream truck in <a href="/wiki/Hartsdale,_New_York" title="Hartsdale, New York">Hartsdale, New York</a>. He pulled into a parking lot and began selling his melting ice cream to vacationers driving by. Within two days he had sold his entire supply of ice cream and concluded that both a fixed location and soft (as opposed to hard) frozen desserts were potentially good business ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1936, Carvel opened his first store on the original broken down truck site and developed a secret soft serve ice cream formula as well as patented super low temperature ice cream machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dairy_Queen" title="Dairy Queen">Dairy Queen</a> also claims to have invented soft serve. In 1938, near <a href="/wiki/Moline,_Illinois" title="Moline, Illinois">Moline, Illinois</a>, J. F. McCullough and his son, Alex, developed their soft serve formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Their first sales experiment was August 4, 1938, in <a href="/wiki/Kankakee,_Illinois" title="Kankakee, Illinois">Kankakee, Illinois</a> at the store of their friend, Sherb Noble. Within two hours of the "all you can eat" trial sale, they had dished out more than 1,600 servings — more than one every 4.5 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the late 1940s, future <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">UK Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher</a> worked briefly as a chemist for food manufacturer <a href="/wiki/J._Lyons_and_Co." title="J. Lyons and Co.">J. Lyons and Co.</a>, at a time when the company had partnered with the United States distributor <a href="/wiki/Mister_Softee" title="Mister Softee">Mister Softee</a> and was developing a soft-serve recipe that was compatible with the American machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thatcher_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-thatcher-6">[6]</a></sup> Thatcher's precise role at Lyons is unclear, but she is reported to have worked on the company's ice cream products, as well as cakes and pies. A common anecdote, probably spread by her political opponents, is that by "inventing" soft serve ice cream, Thatcher "added air, lowered quality and raised profits", used as a metaphor for her policies later as prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thatcher_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-thatcher-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, ice cream machine manufacturers introduced mechanized air pumps into vending machines, providing better aeration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thatcher_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-thatcher-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_serve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4272" data-file-width="2848" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG/220px-Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG/330px-Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG/440px-Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gdansk_tourist_pictures_2009_0314.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Soft serve is generally lower in milk-fat (3% to 6%) than <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a> (10% to 18%) and is produced at a temperature of about −4 °C compared to ice cream, which is <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">stored</a> at −15 °C. Soft serve contains air, introduced at the time of freezing. The air content, called <i>overrun</i>, can vary from 0% to 60% of the total volume of finished product. The amount of air alters the taste of the finished product. Product with low quantities of air has a heavy, icy taste and appears more yellow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Product with higher air content tastes creamier, smoother and lighter and appears whiter. The optimum quantity of air is determined by the other ingredients and individual taste. It is generally accepted that the ideal air content should be between 33% and 45% of volume. More than this and the product loses taste, tends to shrink as it loses air and melts more quickly than that with less air.</p>
<p>Some forms of soft serve, like many other processed foods, have <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a>.</p>
<p>All ice cream must be frozen quickly to avoid crystallization. With soft serve, this is accomplished by a special machine at the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_sale" title="Point of sale">point of sale</a>. Pre-mixed product (see definitions below) is introduced to the storage chamber of the machine where it is kept at 3 °C. When product is drawn from the draw valve, fresh mix combined with the targeted quantity of air is introduced to the freezing chamber either by gravity or pump. It is then churned and quick frozen and stored until required.</p>
<p>Pre-mix can be obtained in several forms:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fresh liquid that requires constant refrigeration until needed. It can be stored for 5 to 7 days before spoiling by bacterial contamination. Quality can be severely compromised by bacterial contamination and handlers must exercise caution to maintain quality.</li>
<li>A powdered mix. This is a dried version of the liquid mix. It has the advantage of easy distribution and can be stored for long periods of time without spoiling. Water must be added prior to being churned and frozen. The disadvantage is that water quality cannot be guaranteed and some operators can put too much water in to make it go further. It also should be refrigerated to 3 degrees Celsius prior to use, as airborne and waterborne bacteria can infect it immediately and can grow quickly if the product is warm. Residual bacteria in the refrigerated storage compartment can also be activated by warm product being introduced.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-temperature_processing" title="Ultra-high-temperature processing">Ultra heat treated</a> mix, a liquid that has been sterilized and packed in sealed, sterile bags. It can last a very long time without refrigeration and can be poured into the soft serve freezer immediately upon opening. However it should be refrigerated to 3 degrees Celsius prior to use for the same reasons outlined above. At the time of opening, quality can be guaranteed and bacterial counts are zero. Where it is available, health authorities consider it the safest form of soft serve mix on the market. It was first developed for commercial use in New Zealand in 1988 in a joint venture between <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tatua_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tatua Foods (page does not exist)">Tatua Foods</a>, a dairy company and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bernie_Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bernie Cook (page does not exist)">Bernie Cook</a>, owner of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Boy_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blue Boy (company) (page does not exist)">Blue Boy</a>, a mobile franchise network.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_serve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Various linguistic communities use various terms to refer to soft-serve ice cream:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>American ice cream</b>, translated to the local language, is the term used in many European countries and in Israel, alongside the term <b>Italian ice cream</b>.</li>
<li><b>Cream ice cream</b> (<i>krémfagylalt</i>) is the term sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.</li>
<li><b>Creemee</b>: A term popular in New England. Colloquially accepted to have originated in Vermont.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Mr Whippy</b> is the term used for soft-serve in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, or a <b>99</b> if a chocolate flake is added (<a href="/wiki/99_Flake" title="99 Flake">99 Flake</a>), especially when sold from an ice cream van.</li>
<li><b>Soft ice cream</b> is cometimes used for soft serve in <a href="/wiki/Greater_China" title="Greater China">Greater China</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">软冰淇淋</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">ruǎn bīngqílín</span></i>), (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">軟雪糕</span>; <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>: <i><span lang="yue-jyutping" xml:lang="yue-jyutping">jyun<sup>5</sup> syut<sup>8</sup> gou<sup>1</sup></span></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">霜淇淋</span>) are the Chinese terms used in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> respectively.</li>
<li><b>Softcream</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ソフトクリーム</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>sofutokuriimu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is used to describe an analogous product in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, that can be either savory or sweet, with uniquely Asian flavors such as powdered tea, <a href="/wiki/Wasabi" title="Wasabi">wasabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ume" title="Ume">ume</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plum" title="Plum">plum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">rose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kabocha" title="Kabocha">kabocha</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_pumpkin" title="Japanese pumpkin">Japanese pumpkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grape</a>, among others.</li>
<li><b>Soft ice</b> <i>Softeis</i> (Germany), <i>softijs</i> (Netherlands and Flanders), <i>softis</i> (Norway)) is the term used for soft-serve in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> and several other places in Europe.</li>
<li><b>Soft ice cream</b> <i>Gelat tou</i> (<a href="/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra">Andorra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>)</li>
<li><b>Softee</b> or <b>softie</b> is the term used for soft-serve in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</li>
<li><b>Italian ice cream</b> (<i>glace à l'italienne</i> (France), <i>sorvete italiano</i> (Brazil), <i>lody włoskie</i> (Poland)) is the term used in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.</li>
<li><b>Soft ice cream</b> (<i>mjukglass</i>) is the term used for soft-serve in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.</li>
<li><b>Machine ice cream</b> (<i>helado de maquina</i> (Dominican Republic), <i>inghetata la dozator</i> (Romania), <i>παγωτό μηχανής</i> (Greece)) is the term used for soft-serve in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.</li>
<li><b>Semi-frozen</b> (<i>semi-frio</i>) is the term used in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.</li>
<li><b>Soft whip</b> is the term used in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. When served in a <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream_cone" title="Ice cream cone">cone</a> with a chocolate <a href="/wiki/Flake_(chocolate_bar)" title="Flake (chocolate bar)">flake</a> it is commonly referred to as a <a href="/wiki/99_Flake" title="99 Flake">99</a>.</li>
<li><b>Candy</b> (with Spanish pronunciation) is the name used in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. It can be found in nearly all ice cream parlors.</li>
<li><b>Merry Cream</b> is the name used in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>.</li>
<li><b>Pehmis</b>, short for <i>pehmytjäätelö</i> (soft ice cream) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> of Nestlé used in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>.</li>
</ul>
