<p>The traditional East Asian calendars divide a year into 24 <a href="/wiki/Solar_term" title="Solar term">solar terms</a>. <b>Xiǎohán</b>, <b>Shōkan</b>, <b>Sohan</b>, or <b>Tiểu hàn</b>(<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: 小寒; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>xiǎohán</i>; <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Japanese" title="Romanization of Japanese">rōmaji</a>: <i>shōkan</i>; <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">소한</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">romaja</a>: <i>sohan</i>; <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: <span lang="vi" xml:lang="vi"><i>tiểu hàn</i></span>; literally: "minor cold") is the 23rd solar term. It begins when the Sun reaches the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_longitude" title="Celestial longitude">celestial longitude</a> of 285° and ends when it reaches the longitude of 300°. It more often refers in particular to the day when the Sun is exactly at the celestial longitude of 285°. In the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, it usually begins around 5 January and ends around 20 January.</p>
