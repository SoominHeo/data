<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fasces</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Fasces</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>, (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Fasci</i></span>, <small>Latin pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Latin" title="Help:IPA for Latin">[ˈfa.skeːs]</a></span>, a <i><a href="/wiki/Plurale_tantum" title="Plurale tantum">plurale tantum</a></i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:fascis#Latin" title="wikt:en:fascis">fascis</a></i>, meaning "bundle")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a bound bundle of wooden rods, sometimes including an axe with its blade emerging. The fasces had its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscan civilization</a>, and was passed on to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, where it symbolized a <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrate</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>. The image has survived in the modern world as a representation of magisterial or collective power. The fasces frequently occurs as a <a href="/wiki/Charge_(heraldry)" title="Charge (heraldry)">charge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>, it is present on an older design of the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_dime" title="Mercury dime">Mercury dime</a> and behind the podium in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>, it is used as the symbol of a number of Italian syndicalist groups, including the <a href="/wiki/Unione_Sindacale_Italiana" title="Unione Sindacale Italiana">Unione Sindacale Italiana</a>, and it was the origin of the name of the <a href="/wiki/National_Fascist_Party" title="National Fascist Party">National Fascist Party</a> in Italy (from which the term <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a> is derived).</p>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a> - also a symbol with a long previous history - was deeply stigmatized by its association with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, the Fasces did not undergo a similar process because of association with Italian Fascism; its use remains acceptable in various legitimate contexts.</p>
<p>It should not be confused with the related term <i><a href="/wiki/Fess" title="Fess">fess</a></i>, which in <a href="/wiki/French_heraldry" title="French heraldry">French heraldry</a> is called a <i>fasce</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_symbolism">Origin and symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fasces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little is known about the Etruscans, but a few artifacts have been found showing a thin bundle of rods surrounding a two-headed axe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Fasces symbolism might be derived via the Etruscans from the eastern Mediterranean, with the <a href="/wiki/Labrys" title="Labrys">labrys</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> double-headed axe, later incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">praetorial</a> fasces. There is little archaeological evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>, the <i>fasces</i> had developed into a thicker bundle of <a href="/wiki/Betula_pubescens" title="Betula pubescens">birch</a> rods, sometimes surrounding a single-headed axe and tied together with a red leather ribbon into a cylinder. On certain special occasions, the fasces might be decorated with a <a href="/wiki/Laurel_wreath" title="Laurel wreath">laurel wreath</a>.</p>
<p>The symbolism of the fasces suggested strength through unity (see <a href="/wiki/Unity_makes_strength" title="Unity makes strength">Unity makes strength</a>); a single rod is easily broken, while the bundle is very difficult to break. This symbolism occurs in <a href="/wiki/Aesop%27s_Fables" title="Aesop's Fables">Aesop's fable</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Old_Man_and_his_Sons" title="The Old Man and his Sons">The Old Man and his Sons</a>. A similar story is told about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulgar" title="Bulgar">Bulgar</a> Khan <a href="/wiki/Kubrat" title="Kubrat">Kubrat</a>, giving rise to the Bulgarian National motto "Union gives strength" (Съединението прави силата). The axe represented the power over life or death through the death penalty, although no Roman magistrate could summarily execute a Roman citizen after passage of the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Tables" title="Twelve Tables">laws of the twelve tables</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Bundled birch twigs symbolise <a href="/wiki/Corporal_punishment" title="Corporal punishment">corporal punishment</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Birching" title="Birching">birching</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Republican_Rome">Republican Rome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fasces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Republican Rome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>fasces lictoriae</i> ("bundles of the lictors") <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolised</a> power and authority (<i><a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">imperium</a></i>) in ancient <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, beginning with the early <a href="/wiki/Roman_Kingdom" title="Roman Kingdom">Roman Kingdom</a> and continuing through the Republican and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Rome" title="Imperial Rome">Imperial</a> periods. By Republican times, use of the fasces was surrounded with tradition and protocol. A corps of <i>apparitores</i> (subordinate officials) called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lictors" title="Lictors">lictors</a> each carried fasces before a <a href="/wiki/Roman_magistrate" title="Roman magistrate">magistrate</a>, in a number corresponding to his rank. Lictors preceded <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">consuls</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Proconsul" title="Proconsul">proconsuls</a>), <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">praetors</a> (and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propraetor" title="Propraetor">propraetors</a>), <a href="/wiki/Roman_dictator" title="Roman dictator">dictators</a>, curule <a href="/wiki/Aedile" title="Aedile">aediles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quaestor" title="Quaestor">quaestors</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Flamen_Dialis" title="Flamen Dialis">Flamen Dialis</a> during <a href="/wiki/Roman_triumph" title="Roman triumph">Roman triumphs</a> (public celebrations held in Rome after a military conquest)</p>
<p>According to Livy, the lictors were likely an Etruscan tradition, adopted by Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The highest magistrate, the <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_dictator" title="Roman dictator">dictator</a></i>, was entitled to twenty-four lictors and fasces, the consul to twelve, the proconsul eleven, the praetor six (two within the <i><a href="/wiki/Pomerium" title="Pomerium">pomerium</a></i>), the propraetor five, and the curule aediles two.</p>
<p>Another part of the symbolism developed in Republican Rome was the inclusion of a single-headed axe in the fasces, with the blade projecting from the bundle. The axe indicated that the magistrate's judicial powers (<i><a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">imperium</a></i>) included capital punishment. Fasces carried within the <i><a href="/wiki/Pomerium" title="Pomerium">Pomerium</a></i>—the boundary of the sacred inner city of Rome—had their axe blades removed; within the city, the power of life and death rested with the people through their assemblies. During times of emergency, however, the Roman Republic might choose a dictator to lead for a limited time period, who was the only magistrate to be granted capital punishment authority within the Pomerium. Lictors attending the dictator kept the axes in their fasces even inside the Pomerium—a sign that the dictator had the ultimate power in his own hands. There were exceptions to this rule: in 48 BC, guards holding bladed fasces guided <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publius_Servilius_Vatia_Isauricus_(consul_48_BCE)" title="Publius Servilius Vatia Isauricus (consul 48 BCE)">Vatia Isauricus</a> to the tribunal of <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Caelius_Rufus" title="Marcus Caelius Rufus">Marcus Caelius</a>, and Vatia Isauricus used one to destroy Caelius's magisterial chair (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curule_Chair" title="Curule Chair">sella curulis</a></i>).</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>An occasional variation on the fasces was the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Laurel_wreath" title="Laurel wreath">laurel wreath</a>, symbolizing victory. This occurred during the celebration of a <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_triumph" title="Roman triumph">Triumph</a></i> - essentially a victory parade through Rome by a returning victorious general. Previously, all Republican Roman commanding generals had held high office with imperium, and so, already were entitled to the lictors and fasces.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fasces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is related to the modern Italian word <i><a href="/wiki/Fascio" title="Fascio">fascio</a></i>, used in the twentieth century to designate peasant cooperatives and industrial workers' unions.</p>
<p>Numerous governments and other authorities have used the image of the <i>fasces</i> for a <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">power</a> since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. It also has been used to hearken back to the Roman republic, particularly by those who see themselves as modern-day successors to the old republic and/or its ideals.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Ecuador" title="Coat of arms of Ecuador">The Ecuadorian coat of arms</a> incorporated the fasces in 1830, although it had already been in use in the coat of arm of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Colombia" title="Gran Colombia">Gran Colombia</a> since 1821.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Italian_Fascism" title="Italian Fascism">Italian Fascism</a>, which derives its name from the <i>fasces</i>, arguably used this symbolism the most in the twentieth century. The <a href="/wiki/British_Union_of_Fascists" title="British Union of Fascists">British Union of Fascists</a> also used it in the 1930s. The <i>fasces</i>, as a widespread and long-established symbol in the West, however, has avoided the <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">stigma</a> associated with much of <a href="/wiki/Fascist_symbolism" title="Fascist symbolism">fascist symbolism</a>, and many authorities continue to display them, including the federal government of the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fasces_in_the_United_States">Fasces in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fasces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fasces in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:National_Guard_Bureau_(insignia).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="746" data-file-width="807" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/National_Guard_Bureau_%28insignia%29.svg/220px-National_Guard_Bureau_%28insignia%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/National_Guard_Bureau_%28insignia%29.svg/330px-National_Guard_Bureau_%28insignia%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/National_Guard_Bureau_%28insignia%29.svg/440px-National_Guard_Bureau_%28insignia%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:National_Guard_Bureau_(insignia).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/National_Guard_Bureau" title="National Guard Bureau">National Guard Bureau</a> insignia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several offices and institutions in the United States have incorporated representations of the <i>fasces</i> into their iconography.</p>
<ul>
<li>The reverse of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_Dime" title="Mercury Dime">Mercury Dime</a>, the design used until the adoption of the current FDR dime in 1945, features a fasces.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Oval_Office" title="Oval Office">Oval Office</a>, above the door leading to the exterior walkway, and above the corresponding door on the opposite wall, which leads to the president's private office. Note: the fasces depicted have no axes, possibly because in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>, the blade was always removed from the bundle whenever the <i>fasces</i> were carried inside the city, in order to symbolize the rights of citizens against arbitrary state power (see above).</li>
<li>Two fasces appear on either side of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the United States">flag of the United States</a> behind the podium in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>.</li>
<li>The official <a href="/wiki/Seal_of_the_United_States_Senate" title="Seal of the United States Senate">seal of the United States Senate</a> has as one component a pair of crossed fasces.</li>
<li>Fasces ring the base of the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Freedom" title="Statue of Freedom">Statue of Freedom</a> atop the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a> building.</li>
<li>A frieze on the facade of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court_building" title="United States Supreme Court building">United States Supreme Court building</a> depicts the figure of a Roman <a href="/wiki/Centurion" title="Centurion">centurion</a> holding a fasces, to represent "order".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The grand seal of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> inside Memorial Church is flanked by two inward-pointing fasces. The seal is located directly below the 112 m (368 ft) steeple and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_United_States" title="Great Seal of the United States">Great Seal of the United States</a> inside the Memorial Room. The walls of the room list the names of Harvard students, faculty, and alumni who gave their lives in service of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> along with an empty tomb depicting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alma_Mater" title="Alma Mater">Alma Mater</a> holding a slain Harvard student.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_of_the_United_States" title="National Guard of the United States">National Guard</a> uses the fasces on the seal of the <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_Bureau" title="National Guard Bureau">National Guard Bureau</a>, and it appears in the insignia of Regular Army officers assigned to National Guard liaison and in the insignia and unit symbols of National Guard units themselves. For instance, the regimental crest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/71st_Infantry_Regiment_(New_York)" title="71st Infantry Regiment (New York)">71st Infantry Regiment (New York)</a> of the New York National Guard consisted of a gold fasces set on a blue background.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mace_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Mace of the United States House of Representatives">Mace of the United States House of Representatives</a>, designed to resemble fasces, consists of thirteen ebony rods bound together in the same fashion as the fasces, topped by a silver eagle on a globe.</li>
<li>The main entrance hallways in the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_State_Capitol" title="Wisconsin State Capitol">Wisconsin State Capitol</a> have lamps that are decorated with stone fasces motifs. In the woodwork before the podium of the speaker of the assembly is carved several double-bladed fasces, whereas in the woodwork before the podium of the senate president are several single-bladed fasces.</li>
<li>At the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial" title="Lincoln Memorial">Lincoln Memorial</a>, Lincoln's seat of state bears the fasces—without axes—on the fronts of its arms. Fasces also appear on the pylons flanking the main staircase leading into the memorial.</li>
<li>The official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" title="Seal (device)">seal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Tax_Court" title="United States Tax Court">United States Tax Court</a> bears the fasces at its center.</li>
<li>Four fasces flank the two bronze plaques on either side of the bust of Lincoln memorializing his <a href="/wiki/Gettysburg_Address" title="Gettysburg Address">Gettysburg Address</a> at Gettysburg, Pennsylvania.</li>
<li>The fasces appears on the <a href="/wiki/Seal_of_Colorado" title="Seal of Colorado">state seal of Colorado</a>, U.S., beneath the "All-seeing eye" (or <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Providence" title="Eye of Providence">Eye of Providence</a>) and above the mountains and mines.</li>
<li>The hallmark of the <a href="/wiki/Kerr_%26_Co" title="Kerr &amp; Co">Kerr &amp; Co</a> silver company was a fasces.</li>
<li>On the seal of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> borough of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, a figure carries a fasces; the seal appears on the borough flag. Fasces also can be seen in the stone columns at <a href="/wiki/Grand_Army_Plaza" title="Grand Army Plaza">Grand Army Plaza</a> and on a flagpole in <a href="/wiki/Washington_Square_Park" title="Washington Square Park">Washington Square Park</a>.</li>
<li>Used as part of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Columbus" title="Knights of Columbus">Knights of Columbus</a> emblem (designed in 1883).</li>
<li>Many local police departments use the fasces as part of their badges and other symbols. For instance, the top border of the Los Angeles Police Department badge features a fasces. (1940)</li>
<li>Commercially, a small fasces appeared at the top of one of the insignia of the <a href="/wiki/Hupmobile" title="Hupmobile">Hupmobile</a> car.</li>
<li>A fasces appears on the <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_(Houdon)" title="George Washington (Houdon)">statue of George Washington</a>, made by Jean-Antoine Houdon that is now in the Virginia State Capital. Fasces are used as posts of the 1818 cast-iron fence surrounding the capitol building.</li>
<li>Columns in the form of fasces line the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_City_Hall" title="Buffalo City Hall">Buffalo City Hall</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/VAW-116" title="VAW-116">VAW-116</a> have a fasces on their unit insignia.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Coit_Tower" title="Coit Tower">Coit Tower</a> has two fasces-like insignia (without the axe) carved above its entrance, flanking a <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(mythology)" title="Phoenix (mythology)">Phoenix</a>.</li>
<li>The seal of the United States Courts Administrative Office includes a fasces behind crossed quill and scroll.</li>
<li>In the Washington Monument, there is a statue of George Washington leaning on a fasces.</li>
<li>A fasces is a common element in U.S. Army Military Police heraldry, most visibly on the shoulder sleeve insignia of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18th_Military_Police_Brigade" title="18th Military Police Brigade">18th Military Police Brigade</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/42nd_Military_Police_Brigade" title="42nd Military Police Brigade">42nd Military Police Brigade</a>.</li>
<li>Two monuments erected in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> at the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Century_of_Progress_Exposition" title="Century of Progress Exposition">Century of Progress Exposition</a> are adorned with fasces. The monument to <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus_(Grant_Park)" title="Christopher Columbus (Grant Park)">Christopher Columbus</a> (1933) in <a href="/wiki/Grant_Park_(Chicago)" title="Grant Park (Chicago)">Grant Park</a> has them on the ends of its <a href="/wiki/Exedra" title="Exedra">exedra</a>. The <i>Balbo Monument</i> in <a href="/wiki/Burnham_Park_(Chicago)" title="Burnham Park (Chicago)">Burnham Park</a>, (1934) a gift from <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>, has the vandalized remains of fasces on all four corners of its <a href="/wiki/Plinth" title="Plinth">plinth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fasces_in_France">Fasces in France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fasces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fasces in France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A review of the images included in <i>Les Grands Palais de France</i> Fontainebleau <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> reveals that French architects used the Roman fasces (<i>faisceaux romains</i>) as a decorative device as early as the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIII" title="Louis XIII">Louis XIII</a> (1610–1643) and continued to employ it through the periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a>'s Empire (1804–1815).</p>
<p>The fasces typically appeared in a context reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> and/or of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> has used many references to the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> in its imagery. During the <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">First Republic</a>, topped by the <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_cap" title="Phrygian cap">Phrygian cap</a>, the fasces is a tribute to the Roman Republic and means that power belongs to the people. It also symbolizes the "unity and indivisibility of the Republic",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elysee1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-elysee1-10">[10]</a></sup> as stated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Constitution" title="French Constitution">French Constitution</a>. <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1848" title="French Revolution of 1848">In 1848</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_of_National_Defense" title="Government of National Defense">after 1870</a>, it appears on the <a href="/wiki/Seal_(emblem)" title="Seal (emblem)">seal</a> of the French Republic, held by <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(goddess)" title="Liberty (goddess)">Liberty</a>. There is the fasces in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Emblem_of_France" title="National Emblem of France">arms of the French Republic</a> with the "RF" for <i>République française</i> (see image below), surrounded by leaves of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olive_tree" title="Olive tree">olive tree</a> (as a symbol of <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">peace</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> (as a symbol of <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>). While it is used widely by French officials, this symbol never was officially adopted by the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elysee1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-elysee1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fasces appears on the helmet and the buckle insignia of the French Army's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Autonomous_Corps_of_Military_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Autonomous Corps of Military Justice (page does not exist)">Autonomous Corps of Military Justice</a>, as well as on that service's distinct cap badges for the prosecuting and defending lawyers in a court-martial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">
<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_modern_authorities_and_movements">Other modern authorities and movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fasces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other modern authorities and movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s tomb is flanked by marble fasces.</li>
</ul>
<p>The following cases all involve the adoption of the fasces as a symbol or icon, although no physical re-introduction has occurred.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aiguillette" title="Aiguillette">Aiguillettes</a> worn by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aide_de_camp" title="Aide de camp">aides-de-camp</a> in many Commonwealth armed forces bear the fasces on the metal points; the origin of this is unknown, as the fasces is an uncommon symbol in British/Commonwealth heraldry and insignia.</li>
<li>The badge of the Spanish gendarmerie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardia_Civil_(Spain)" title="Guardia Civil (Spain)">Guardia Civil</a> includes a fasces.</li>
<li>Both the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Police_Service" title="Norwegian Police Service">Norwegian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_police" title="Swedish police">Swedish police</a> have double fasces in their logos.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miners_Flag" title="Miners Flag">Miners Flag</a> (also known as the "Diggers' Banner"), the standard of nineteenth-century gold-miners in the colony of Victoria, in Australia, included the fasces as a symbol of unity and strength of common purpose. This flag symbolized the movement prior to the rebellion at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eureka_Stockade" title="Eureka Stockade">Eureka Stockade</a> (1854).</li>
<li>The coat of arms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ecuador" title="Republic of Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, which also features on its national flag, has included a fasces since 1822.</li>
<li>The coat of arms of <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> features two fasces which form a diagonal cross.</li>
<li>The coat of arms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Cuba" title="Republic of Cuba">Cuba</a> features a fasces.</li>
<li>The third flag of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Colombia" title="Gran Colombia">Gran Colombia</a>, a former nation in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, depicted a large fasces entwined with several arrows.</li>
<li>The coat of arms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte_de_Santander" title="Norte de Santander">Norte de Santander</a>, a department of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, and of its capital <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BAcuta" title="Cúcuta">Cúcuta</a>, both feature a fasces.</li>
<li>The coat of arms of the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Police" title="Romanian Police">Romanian Police</a> features two crossed fasces.</li>
<li>The Grand Coat of Arms of <a href="/wiki/Vilnius" title="Vilnius">Vilnius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> features a fasces.</li>
<li>The crests of many collegiate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternities" title="Fraternities">fraternities and sororities</a> feature the fasces, including those of <a href="/wiki/Chi_Phi" title="Chi Phi">Chi Phi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Phi_Delta" title="Alpha Phi Delta">Alpha Phi Delta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Alpha_Mu" title="Sigma Alpha Mu">Sigma Alpha Mu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psi_Upsilon" title="Psi Upsilon">Psi Upsilon</a>.</li>
<li>The symbol of the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_(Uruguay)" title="National Party (Uruguay)">National Party (Uruguay)</a> (Partido Nacional) includes a fasces.</li>
<li>On the entrance of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_Laeken" title="Royal Palace of Laeken">Royal Palace of Laeken</a> in Belgium.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/British_Union_of_Fascists" title="British Union of Fascists">British Union of Fascists</a> originally used the fasces on their flag until adopting the <a href="/wiki/Flash_and_Circle" title="Flash and Circle">Flash and Circle</a>.</li>
</ul>
