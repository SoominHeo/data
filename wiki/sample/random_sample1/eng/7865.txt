<p><b>Undergarments</b> are items of <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">clothing</a> worn beneath outer clothes, usually in direct contact with the skin, although they may comprise more than a single layer. They serve to keep outer garments from being soiled or damaged by <a href="/wiki/Human_waste" title="Human waste">bodily excretions</a>, to lessen the <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> of outerwear against the skin, to shape the body, and to provide concealment or support for parts of it. In cold weather, <a href="/wiki/Long_underwear" title="Long underwear">long underwear</a> is sometimes worn to provide additional warmth. Special types of undergarments have religious significance. Some items of clothing are designed as undergarments, while others, such as <a href="/wiki/T-shirt" title="T-shirt">T-shirts</a> and certain types of shorts, are appropriate both as undergarments and as outer clothing. If made of suitable material or <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a>, some undergarments can serve as <a href="/wiki/Nightwear" title="Nightwear">nightwear</a> or <a href="/wiki/Swimsuit" title="Swimsuit">swimsuits</a>, and some are intended for <a href="/wiki/Sexual_attraction" title="Sexual attraction">sexual attraction</a> or visual appeal.</p>
<p>Undergarments are generally of two types, those that are worn to cover the torso and those that are worn to cover the waist and legs, although there are also garments which cover both. Different styles of undergarments are generally worn by females and males. Undergarments commonly worn by females today include <a href="/wiki/Bra" title="Bra">bras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panties" title="Panties">panties</a> (known in the United Kingdom as <a href="/wiki/Panties" title="Panties">knickers</a>), while males often wear <a href="/wiki/Briefs" title="Briefs">briefs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boxer_briefs" title="Boxer briefs">boxer briefs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boxer_shorts" title="Boxer shorts">boxer shorts</a>. Items commonly worn by both sexes include T-shirts, <a href="/wiki/Sleeveless_shirt" title="Sleeveless shirt">sleeveless shirts</a> (also called singlets or tank tops), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bikini_(underwear)" title="Bikini (underwear)">bikini underwear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thong_(clothing)" title="Thong (clothing)">thongs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/G-string" title="G-string">G-strings</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<p>Undergarments are known by a number of terms. <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/underclothes" title="wikt:underclothes">Underclothes</a></i>, <i>underclothing</i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/underwear" title="wikt:underwear">underwear</a></i> are formal terms, while undergarments may be more casually called, in Australia, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reg_Grundys" title="wikt:Reg Grundys">Reg Grundys</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Rhyming_slang" title="Rhyming slang">rhyming slang</a> for <i>undies</i>) and <i>Reginalds</i>, and, in the United Kingdom, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/smalls" title="wikt:smalls">smalls</a></i> (from the earlier <i>smallclothes</i>) and (historically) <i>unmentionables</i>. In the United States, women's underwear may be known as <i>delicates</i> due to the recommended washing machine cycle or because they are, simply put, delicate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Women's undergarments collectively are called <i><a href="/wiki/Lingerie" title="Lingerie">lingerie</a></i>. They also are called <i>intimate clothing</i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intimate" title="wikt:intimate">intimates</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Undershirt" title="Undershirt">undershirt</a> (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vest" title="wikt:vest">vest</a></i> in the United Kingdom) is a piece of underwear covering the <a href="/wiki/Torso" title="Torso">torso</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Underpants" title="Underpants">underpants</a> (often <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pants" title="wikt:pants">pants</a></i> in the United Kingdom), <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drawers" title="wikt:drawers">drawers</a>, and shorts cover the <a href="/wiki/Sex_organ" title="Sex organ">genitals</a> and buttocks. Terms for specific undergarments are shown in the table <a href="#Types_and_styles">below</a>.</p>
<p>Not wearing underpants under outer clothing is known in American slang as <i>freeballing</i> for men and as <i><a href="/wiki/Going_commando" title="Going commando">going commando</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> for either sex. The act of a woman not wearing a bra is sometimes referred to as <i>freeboobing</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span></h2>
<p>Underwear is worn for a variety of reasons. They keep outer garments from being soiled by <a href="/wiki/Perspiration" title="Perspiration">perspiration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menstrual_blood" title="Menstrual blood">menstrual blood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Women's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brassiere" title="Brassiere">brassieres</a> provide support for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breasts" title="Breasts">breasts</a>, and men's <a href="/wiki/Briefs" title="Briefs">briefs</a> serve the same function for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male_reproductive_system_(human)" title="Male reproductive system (human)">male genitalia</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Corset" title="Corset">corset</a> may be worn as a <a href="/wiki/Foundation_garment" title="Foundation garment">foundation garment</a> to alter a woman's <a href="/wiki/Female_body_shape" title="Female body shape">body shape</a>. For additional support and protection when playing sports, men often wear more tightly fitting underwear, including <a href="/wiki/Jockstrap" title="Jockstrap">jockstraps</a> and jockstraps with cup pocket and <a href="/wiki/Jockstrap#Protective_cup" title="Jockstrap">protective cup</a>. Women may wear <a href="/wiki/Sports_bra" title="Sports bra">sports bras</a> which provide greater support, thus increasing comfort and reducing the chance of damage to the <a href="/wiki/Ligament" title="Ligament">ligaments</a> of the chest during high-impact exercises such as jogging.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In cold climates, underwear may constitute an additional layer of clothing helping to keep the wearer warm. Underwear may also be used to preserve the wearer's <a href="/wiki/Modesty" title="Modesty">modesty</a> – for instance, some women wear <a href="/wiki/Camisole" title="Camisole">camisoles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slip_(clothing)" title="Slip (clothing)">slips</a> (<a href="/wiki/Petticoat" title="Petticoat">petticoats</a>) under clothes that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheer_(textile)" title="Sheer (textile)">sheer</a>. Conversely, some types of underwear can be worn for sexual titillation, such as <a href="/wiki/Edible_underwear" title="Edible underwear">edible underwear</a>, crotchless panties and <a href="/wiki/Thong_(clothing)" title="Thong (clothing)">thongs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some items of clothing are designed exclusively as underwear, while others such as <a href="/wiki/T-shirt" title="T-shirt">T-shirts</a> and certain types of shorts are suitable both as underwear and as outer clothing. The suitability of underwear as outer clothing is, apart from the indoor or outdoor climate, largely dependent on societal norms, fashion and the requirements of the law. If made of suitable material, some underwear can serve as <a href="/wiki/Nightwear" title="Nightwear">nightwear</a> or <a href="/wiki/Swimsuit" title="Swimsuit">swimsuits</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_functions">Religious functions</span></h3>
<p>Undergarments can also have religious significance:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Judaism</b>. To conform with societal dress codes, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tallit_katan" title="Tallit katan">tallit katan</a></i> is often worn beneath the shirt.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><b>Mormonism</b>. Following their <a href="/wiki/Endowment_(Mormonism)" title="Endowment (Mormonism)">endowment</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_(Mormonism)" title="Temple (Mormonism)">temple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mormonism" title="Mormonism">Mormons</a> wear special <a href="/wiki/Temple_garment" title="Temple garment">temple garments</a> which help them to remember the teachings of the temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Sikhism</b>. One of the five articles of faith (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Five_Ks" title="Five Ks">panj kakaar</a></i>) worn by <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikh</a> men and women is a certain style of underpants similar to <a href="/wiki/Boxer_shorts" title="Boxer shorts">boxer shorts</a> and known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Kacchera" title="Kacchera">kacchera</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><b>Zoroastrianism</b>. Zoroastrians wear an undershirt called a <i><a href="/wiki/Sedreh" title="Sedreh">Sedreh</a></i> that is fastened with a sacred girdle around the waist known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Kushti" title="Kushti">Kushti</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_history">Ancient history</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1646" data-file-width="1090" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg/170px-PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg/255px-PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg/340px-PiazzaArmerina-Mosaik-Bikini.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Loincloth" title="Loincloth">loincloth</a> is the simplest form of underwear; it was probably the first undergarment worn by human beings. In warmer climates the loincloth was often the only clothing worn (effectively making it an outer garment rather than an undergarment), as was doubtless its origin, but in colder regions the loincloth often formed the basis of a person's clothing and was covered by other garments. In most ancient civilizations, this was the only undergarment available.</p>
<p>A loincloth may take three major forms. The first, and simplest, is simply a long strip of material which is passed between the legs and then around the waist. Archaeologists have found the remains of such loincloths made of leather dating back 7,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> The ancient Hawaiian <i>malo</i> was of this form, as are several styles of the Japanese <i><a href="/wiki/Fundoshi" title="Fundoshi">fundoshi</a></i>. Another form is usually called a <a href="/wiki/Cache-sexe" title="Cache-sexe">cache-sexe</a>: a triangle of cloth is provided with strings or loops, which are used to fasten the triangle between the legs and over the genitals. <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Tutankhamun" title="Tutankhamun">Tutankhamun</a> (1341 BC – 1323 BC) was found buried with numerous <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> loincloths of this style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> An alternate form is more <a href="/wiki/Skirt" title="Skirt">skirt</a>-like: a cloth is wrapped around the hips several times and then fastened with a <a href="/wiki/Girdle" title="Girdle">girdle</a>.</p>
<p>Men are said to have worn loincloths in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>, though it is unclear whether Greek women wore undergarments. There is some speculation that only slaves wore loincloths and that citizens did not wear undergarments beneath their <a href="/wiki/Chiton_(costume)" title="Chiton (costume)">chitons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">Mosaics</a> of the Roman period indicate that women (primarily in an athletic context, whilst wearing nothing else) sometimes wore <i>strophiae</i> (breastcloths) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brassiere" title="Brassiere">brassieres</a> made of soft leather, along with <i><a href="/wiki/Subligaculum" title="Subligaculum">subligacula</a></i> which were either in the form of shorts or loincloths. <i>Subligacula</i> were also worn by men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fabric used for loincloths may have been <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Linsey-woolsey" title="Linsey-woolsey">linsey-woolsey</a> blend. Only the upper classes could have afforded imported silk.</p>
<p>The loincloth continues to be worn by people around the world – it is the traditional form of undergarment in many Asian societies, for example. In various, mainly tropical, cultures, the traditional male dress may still consist of only a single garment below the waist or even none at all, with underwear as optional, including the Far-Eastern <i><a href="/wiki/Dhoti" title="Dhoti">dhoti</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lungi" title="Lungi">lungi</a></i>, or the Scottish <a href="/wiki/Kilt" title="Kilt">kilt</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_Renaissance">Middle Ages and Renaissance</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, western men's underwear became looser fitting. The loincloth was replaced by loose, <a href="/wiki/Trousers" title="Trousers">trouser</a>-like clothing called <a href="/wiki/Braies" title="Braies">braies</a>, which the wearer stepped into and then laced or tied around the waist and legs at about mid-calf. Wealthier men often wore <a href="/wiki/Chausses" title="Chausses">chausses</a> as well, which only covered the legs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> Braies were a type of trouser worn by Celtic and Germanic tribes in antiquity and by Europeans subsequently into the Middle Ages. In the later Middle Ages they were used exclusively as undergarments.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tizian_081.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3051" data-file-width="1682" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Tizian_081.jpg/170px-Tizian_081.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Tizian_081.jpg/255px-Tizian_081.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Tizian_081.jpg/340px-Tizian_081.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tizian_081.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 1532–1533 portrait by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>, in a codpiece</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, braies had become shorter to accommodate longer styles of chausses. Chausses were also giving way to form-fitting <a href="/wiki/Hose_(clothing)" title="Hose (clothing)">hose</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> which covered the legs and feet. Fifteenth-century hose were often particolored, with each leg in a different-coloured fabric or even more than one colour on a leg. However, many types of braies, chausses and hose were not intended to be covered up by other clothing, so they were not actually underwear in the strict sense.</p>
<p>Braies were usually fitted with a front flap that was buttoned or tied closed. This <a href="/wiki/Codpiece" title="Codpiece">codpiece</a> allowed men to <a href="/wiki/Urination" title="Urination">urinate</a> without having to remove the braies completely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> Codpieces were also worn with hose when very short <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(clothing)" title="Doublet (clothing)">doublets</a> – vest- (UK: waistcoat-) like garments tied together in the front and worn under other clothing – were in fashion, as early forms of hose were open at the <a href="/wiki/Crotch" title="Crotch">crotch</a>. <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a> began padding his codpiece, which caused a spiralling trend of larger and larger codpieces that only ended by the end of the 16th century. It has been speculated that the King may have had the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">sexually transmitted disease</a> <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a>, and his large codpiece may have included a bandage soaked in medication to relieve its symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> Henry VIII also wanted a healthy son and may have thought that projecting himself in this way would portray fertility. Codpieces were sometimes used as a pocket for holding small items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2716" data-file-width="1670" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg/170px-Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg/255px-Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg/340px-Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_Vernon_big.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A lady in her private <a href="/wiki/Boudoir" title="Boudoir">boudoir</a>; wearing an informal embroidered jacket over her rose-pink <a href="/wiki/Corset" title="Corset">corset</a> or simple <a href="/wiki/Bodice" title="Bodice">bodice</a> and decorated <a href="/wiki/Petticoat" title="Petticoat">petticoat</a>, <i>c.</i> 1600</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Over the upper part of their bodies, both medieval men and women usually wore a close-fitting shirt-like garment called a <a href="/wiki/Chemise" title="Chemise">chemise</a> in France, or a smock or shift in England. The forerunner of the modern-day shirt, the chemise was tucked into a man's braies, under his outer clothing. Women wore a chemise underneath their <a href="/wiki/Gown" title="Gown">gowns</a> or <a href="/wiki/Robe" title="Robe">robes</a>, sometimes with <a href="/wiki/Petticoat" title="Petticoat">petticoats</a> over the chemise. Elaborately <a href="/wiki/Quilting" title="Quilting">quilted</a> petticoats might be displayed by a cut-away dress, in which case they served a skirt rather than an undergarment. During the 16th century, the <a href="/wiki/Farthingale" title="Farthingale">farthingale</a> was popular. This was a petticoat stiffened with <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reed</a> or <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> rods so that it stood out from a woman's body like a cone extending from the waist.</p>
<p>Corsets also began to be worn about this time. At first they were called pairs of bodies, which refers to a stiffened decorative <a href="/wiki/Bodice" title="Bodice">bodice</a> worn on top of another bodice stiffened with <a href="/wiki/Buckram" title="Buckram">buckram</a>, reeds, canes, <a href="/wiki/Baleen" title="Baleen">whalebone</a> or other materials. These were not the small-waisted, curved corsets familiar from the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, but straight-lined stays that flattened the bust.</p>
<p>There is a myth that <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusaders</a>, worried about the <a href="/wiki/Fidelity" title="Fidelity">fidelity</a> of their wives, forced them to wear <a href="/wiki/Chastity_belt" title="Chastity belt">chastity belts</a>. There is no reference, image, or surviving belt to support this story. Most historians of this period are of the view that a chastity belt was worn to prevent sexual assault and that the woman kept the key.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Men's braies and hose were eventually replaced by simple cotton, silk or <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> drawers, which were usually knee-length trousers with a button flap in the front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Medieval people wearing only tunics, without underpants, can be seen on works like <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder_-_The_Ass_in_the_School_-_WGA03526.jpg" title="commons:File:Pieter Bruegel the Elder - The Ass in the School - WGA03526.jpg">The Ass in the School</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder" title="Pieter Bruegel the Elder">Pieter Bruegel the Elder</a>, in the <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Les_Tr%C3%A8s_Riches_Heures_du_duc_de_Berry_f%C3%A9vrier.jpg" title="commons:File:Les Très Riches Heures du duc de Berry février.jpg">Très Riches Heures du duc de Berry</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limbourg_Brothers" title="Limbourg Brothers">Limbourg Brothers</a> or in the <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:15th-century_unknown_painters_-_Grimani_Breviary_-_The_Month_of_February_-_WGA15776.jpg" title="commons:File:15th-century unknown painters - Grimani Breviary - The Month of February - WGA15776.jpg">Grimani Breviary: The Month of February</a> by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Horenbout" title="Gerard Horenbout">Gerard Horenbout</a>.</p>
<p>In 2012, findings in <a href="/wiki/Lengberg_Castle" title="Lengberg Castle">Lengberg Castle</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, showed that <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">lace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> brassiere-like garments, one of which greatly resembled the modern bra, date back to hundreds of years before it was thought to exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enlightenment_and_Industrial_Age">Enlightenment and Industrial Age</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tight_lacing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="409" data-file-width="300" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3d/Tight_lacing.jpg/170px-Tight_lacing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3d/Tight_lacing.jpg/255px-Tight_lacing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/3d/Tight_lacing.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tight_lacing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Tight Lacing, or Fashion Before Ease</i>, an early-1770s <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirical</a> drawing by John Collet</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Spinning_jenny" title="Spinning jenny">spinning jenny</a> machines and the <a href="/wiki/Cotton_gin" title="Cotton gin">cotton gin</a> in the second half of the 18th century made cotton fabrics widely available. This allowed factories to <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass-produce</a> underwear, and for the first time, people began buying undergarments in stores rather than making them at home.</p>
<p>Women's <a href="/wiki/History_of_corsets#16th_to_late_18th_centuries" title="History of corsets">stays</a> of the 18th century were laced behind and drew the shoulders back to form a high, round bosom and erect posture. Coloured stays were popular. With the relaxed country styles of the end of the century, stays became shorter and were <a href="/wiki/Bone_(corsetry)" title="Bone (corsetry)">unboned</a> or only lightly boned, and were now called corsets. As tight waists became fashionable in the 1820s, the corset was again boned and laced to form the figure. By the 1860s, a tiny ("wasp") waist came to be seen as a symbol of beauty, and the corsets were stiffened with whalebone or steel to accomplish this. Tight lacing of a corset sometimes led to a woman needing to retire to the <a href="/wiki/Fainting_room" title="Fainting room">fainting room</a>. By the 1880s, the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_dress_reform" title="Victorian dress reform">dress reform</a> movement was campaigning against the pain and damage to internal organs and bones caused by <a href="/wiki/Tightlacing" title="Tightlacing">tight lacing</a>. Inez Gaches-Sarraute invented the "health corset", with a straight-fronted bust made to help support the wearer's muscles.</p>
<p>The corset was usually worn over a thin shirt-like shift of linen or cotton or <a href="/wiki/Muslin" title="Muslin">muslin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Skirt" title="Skirt">Skirt</a> styles became shorter and long drawers called <a href="/wiki/Pantalettes" title="Pantalettes">pantalettes</a> or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pantaloons" title="wikt:pantaloons">pantaloons</a> kept the legs covered. Pantalettes originated in France in the early 19th century, and quickly spread to Britain and America. Pantalettes were a form of <a href="/wiki/Leggings" title="Leggings">leggings</a> or long drawers. They could be one-piece or two separate garments, one for each leg, attached at the waist with buttons or laces. The crotch was left open for hygiene reasons.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Perfect_Health_Corset.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="310" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Perfect_Health_Corset.png/220px-Perfect_Health_Corset.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f5/Perfect_Health_Corset.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Perfect_Health_Corset.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Health corsets" in 1883</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As skirts became fuller from the 1830s, women wore many petticoats to achieve a fashionable bell shape. By the 1850s, stiffened <a href="/wiki/Crinoline" title="Crinoline">crinolines</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Hoop_skirt" title="Hoop skirt">hoop skirts</a> allowed ever wider skirts to be worn. The <a href="/wiki/Bustle" title="Bustle">bustle</a>, a frame or pad worn over the buttocks to enhance their shape, had been used off and on by women for two centuries, but reached the height of its popularity in the later 1880s, and went out of fashion for good in the 1890s. Women dressed in crinolines often wore drawers under them for modesty and warmth.</p>
<p>Another common undergarment of the late-19th century for men, women and children was the <a href="/wiki/Union_suit" title="Union suit">union suit</a>. Invented in <a href="/wiki/Utica,_New_York" title="Utica, New York">Utica, New York</a>, and patented in 1868, this was a one-piece front-buttoning garment usually made of knitted material with sleeves extending to the wrists and legs down to the ankles. It had a buttoned flap (known colloquially as the "access hatch", "drop seat" or "fireman's flap") in the back to ease visits to the toilet. The union suit was the precursor of <a href="/wiki/Long_underwear" title="Long underwear">long johns</a>, a two-piece garment consisting of a long-sleeved top and long pants possibly named after American boxer <a href="/wiki/John_L._Sullivan" title="John L. Sullivan">John L. Sullivan</a> who wore a similar garment in the ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Jockstrap" title="Jockstrap">jockstrap</a> was invented in 1874, by C.F. Bennett of a Chicago sporting goods company, Sharp &amp; Smith, to provide comfort and support for bicycle jockeys riding the <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestone</a> streets of Boston, Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1897 Bennett's newly formed Bike Web Company patented and began mass-producing the Bike Jockey Strap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>By the early 20th century, the mass-produced undergarment industry was booming, and competition forced producers to come up with all sorts of innovative and gimmicky designs to compete. The <a href="/wiki/Hanes" title="Hanes">Hanes</a> company emerged from this boom and quickly established itself as a top manufacturer of union suits, which were common until the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> Textile technology continued to improve, and the time to make a single union suit dropped from days to minutes.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, designers of women's undergarments relaxed the corset. The invention of new, flexible but supportive materials allowed whalebone and steel bones to be removed. The emancipation or <a href="/wiki/Liberty_bodice" title="Liberty bodice">liberty bodice</a> offered an alternative to constricting corsets, and in Australia and the UK the liberty bodice became a standard item for girls as well as women.</p>
<p>Men's underwear was also on the rise. Benjamin Joseph Clark, a migrant to <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> from <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, opened a <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capitalist</a> firm named Bossier in <a href="/wiki/Bossier_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Bossier Parish, Louisiana">Bossier Parish</a>. One product manufactured by his firm was tightly fitting boxers that resembled modern underwear. Though the company was bankrupt by the early 1900s, it had some impact on men's underwear design.</p>
<p>Underwear advertising first made an appearance in the 1910s. The first underwear print advertisement in the US appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Evening_Post" title="The Saturday Evening Post">The Saturday Evening Post</a></i> in 1911 and featured oil paintings by <a href="/wiki/J._C._Leyendecker" title="J. C. Leyendecker">J. C. Leyendecker</a> of the "Kenosha Klosed Krotch". Early underwear advertisements emphasised durability and comfort, and fashion was not regarded as a selling point.</p>
<p>By the end of the 1910s, Chalmers Knitting Company split the union suit into upper and lower sections, effectively inventing the modern undershirt and drawers. Women wore lacier versions of this basic duo known as the camisole and tap pants.</p>

<p>In 1912, the US had its first professional underwear designer. Lindsay "Layneau" Boudreaux, a French immigrant, established the short-lived panty company Layneau. Though her company closed within one year, it had a significant impact on many levels. Boudreaux showed the world that an American woman could establish and run a company, and she also caused a revolution in the underwear industry.</p>
<p>In 1913, a New York socialite named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Phelps_Jacob" title="Mary Phelps Jacob">Mary Phelps Jacob</a> created the first modern brassiere by tying two handkerchiefs together with ribbon. Jacob's original intention was to cover the whalebone sticking out of her corset, which was visible through her sheer dress. Jacob began making brassieres for her family and friends, and news of the garment soon spread by word of mouth. By 1914, Jacob had a patent for her design and was marketing it throughout the US. Although women had worn brassiere-like garments in years past, Jacob's was the first to be successfully marketed and widely adopted.</p>
<p>By the end of the decade, trouser-like "<a href="/wiki/Bloomers_(clothing)" title="Bloomers (clothing)">bloomers</a>", which were popularized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amelia_Jenks_Bloomer" title="Amelia Jenks Bloomer">Amelia Jenks Bloomer</a> (1818–1894) but invented by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Smith_Miller" title="Elizabeth Smith Miller">Elizabeth Smith Miller</a>, gained popularity with the so-called <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Girl" title="Gibson Girl">Gibson Girls</a> who enjoyed pursuits such as cycling and tennis. This new female athleticism helped push the corset out of style. The other major factor in the corset's demise was the fact that metal was globally in short supply during the First World War. Steel-laced corsets were dropped in favour of the brassiere.</p>

<p>Meanwhile, World War I soldiers were issued button-front shorts as underwear. The buttons attached to a separate piece of cloth, or "yoke", sewn to the front of the garment, and tightness of fit was adjusted by means of ties on the sides. This design proved so popular that it began to supplant the union suit in popularity by the end of the war. <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">Rayon</a> garments also became widely available in the post-war period.</p>
<p>In the 1920s, manufacturers shifted emphasis from durability to comfort. Union suit advertisements raved about patented new designs that reduced the number of buttons and increased accessibility. Most of these experimental designs had to do with new ways to hold closed the crotch flap common on most union suits and drawers. A new woven cotton fabric called <a href="/wiki/Nainsook" title="Nainsook">nainsook</a> gained popularity in the 1920s for its durability. Retailers also began selling preshrunk undergarments.</p>
<p>Also in the 1920s, as <a href="/wiki/Hemline" title="Hemline">hemlines</a> of women's dresses rose, women began to wear stockings to cover the exposed legs. Women's bloomers also became much shorter. The shorter bloomers became looser and less supportive as the boyish <a href="/wiki/Flapper" title="Flapper">flapper</a> look came into fashion. By the end of the decade, they came to be known as "step-ins", very much like modern panties but with wider legs. They were worn for the increased flexibility they afforded.</p>
<p>As dancing became a favourite pastime of young flappers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garter_belts" title="Garter belts">garter belt</a> was invented to keep stockings from falling. The increased sexual awareness of the flapper also made underwear sexier than ever before. It was the flappers who ushered in the era of lingerie.</p>
<p>In 1928, <a href="/wiki/Maidenform" title="Maidenform">Maidenform</a>, a company operated by <a href="/wiki/Ida_Rosenthal" title="Ida Rosenthal">Ida Rosenthal</a>, a Russian immigrant, developed the brassiere and introduced modern cup sizes for bras.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s_and_1940s">1930s and 1940s</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maninboxers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Maninboxers.jpg/170px-Maninboxers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Maninboxers.jpg/255px-Maninboxers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Maninboxers.jpg/340px-Maninboxers.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maninboxers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A man wearing a pair of <a href="/wiki/Boxer_shorts" title="Boxer shorts">boxer shorts</a></div>

<p>Modern men's underwear was largely an invention of the 1930s. On 19 January 1935, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coopers_Inc." title="Coopers Inc.">Coopers Inc.</a> sold the world's first briefs in Chicago. Designed by an "apparel engineer" named Arthur Kneibler, briefs dispensed with leg sections and had a Y-shaped overlapping fly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> The company dubbed the design the "Jockey" since it offered a degree of support that had previously only been available from the jockstrap. Jockey briefs proved so popular that over 30,000 pairs were sold within three months of their introduction. Coopers, having renamed the company <a href="/wiki/Jockey_International" title="Jockey International">Jockey</a>, sent its "Mascul-line" plane to make special deliveries of "masculine support" briefs to retailers across the US. In 1938, when Jockeys were introduced in the UK, they sold at the rate of 3,000 a week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In this decade, companies also began selling buttonless drawers fitted with an elastic waistband. These were the first true boxer shorts, which were named for their resemblance to the shorts worn by <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">professional fighters</a>. Scovil Manufacturing introduced the <a href="/wiki/Snap_fastener" title="Snap fastener">snap fastener</a> at this time, which became a popular addition to various kinds of undergarments.</p>
<p>Women of the 1930s brought the corset back, now called the "<a href="/wiki/Girdle" title="Girdle">girdle</a>". The garment lacked the whalebone and metal supports and usually came with a brassiere (now usually called a "bra") and attached garters.</p>
<p>During World War II, elastic waistbands and metal snaps gave way once again to button fasteners due to rubber and metal shortages. Undergarments were harder to find as well, since soldiers abroad had priority to obtain them. By the end of the war, Jockey and Hanes remained the industry leaders in the US, but Cluett, Peabody and Company made a name for itself when it introduced a preshrinking process called "<a href="/wiki/Sanforization" title="Sanforization">Sanforization</a>", invented by Sanford Cluett in 1933, which came to be licensed by most major manufacturers.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, some women adopted the corset once again, now called the "<a href="/wiki/Waist_cincher" title="Waist cincher">waspie</a>" for the <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasp</a>-shaped <a href="/wiki/Waist" title="Waist">waistline</a> it gave the wearer. Many women began wearing the strapless bra as well, which gained popularity for its ability to push the breasts up and <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_enhancement" title="Cleavage enhancement">enhance cleavage</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s_and_60s">1950s and 60s</span></h3>
<p>Before the 1950s, underwear consisted of simple, white pieces of clothing which were not to be shown in public. In the 1950s, underwear came to be promoted as a fashion item in its own right, and came to be made in prints and colours. Manufacturers also experimented with rayon and newer fabrics like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dacron" title="Dacron">Dacron</a>, nylon and <a href="/wiki/Spandex" title="Spandex">Spandex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> By 1960, men's underwear was regularly printed in loud patterns, or with messages or images such as cartoon characters.</p>
<p>Women's undergarments began to emphasize the breasts instead of the waist. The decade saw the introduction of the bullet bra pointed bust, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Dior" title="Christian Dior">Christian Dior</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Christian_Dior#The_New_Look" title="Christian Dior">New Look</a>", which featured pointed cups. The original <a href="/wiki/Wonderbra" title="Wonderbra">Wonderbra</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brassiere_designs" title="Brassiere designs">push-up bra</a> by <a href="/wiki/Frederick%27s_of_Hollywood" title="Frederick's of Hollywood">Frederick's of Hollywood</a> finally hit it big. Women's panties became more colourful and decorative, and by the mid-1960s were available in two abbreviated styles called the hip-hugger and the <a href="/wiki/Bikini" title="Bikini">bikini</a> (named after the Pacific Ocean <a href="/wiki/Bikini_Atoll" title="Bikini Atoll">island</a> of that name), frequently in sheer nylon fabric.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pantyhose" title="Pantyhose">Pantyhose</a>, also called <i>tights</i> in British English, which combined panties and hose into one garment, made their first appearance in 1959,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belkin1986_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belkin1986-10">[10]</a></sup> invented by Glen Raven Mills of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. The company later introduced seamless pantyhose in 1965, spurred by the popularity of the <a href="/wiki/Miniskirt" title="Miniskirt">miniskirt</a>. By the end of the decade, the girdle had fallen out of favour as women chose sexier and lighter alternatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the emergence of the woman's movement in the United States sales for pantyhose dropped off during the later half of the 1960s having soared initially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belkin1986_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Belkin1986-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Underwear as fashion reached its peak in the 1970s and 1980s, and underwear advertisers forgot about comfort and durability, at least in advertising. <a href="/wiki/Sexual_attraction" title="Sexual attraction">Sex appeal</a> became the main selling point, in swimwear as well, bringing to fruition a trend that had been building since at least the flapper era.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sleeveless_shirt" title="Sleeveless shirt">tank top</a>, an undershirt named after the type of swimwear dating from the 1920s known as a tank suit or <a href="/wiki/Maillot" title="Maillot">maillot</a>, became popular warm-weather casual outerwear in the US in the 1980s. Performers such as <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyndi_Lauper" title="Cyndi Lauper">Cyndi Lauper</a> were also often seen wearing their undergarments on top of other clothes.</p>
<p>Although worn for decades by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exotic_dancer" title="Exotic dancer">exotic dancers</a>, in the 1980s the <a href="/wiki/G-string" title="G-string">G-string</a> first gained popularity in South America, particularly in Brazil. Originally a style of <a href="/wiki/Swimsuit" title="Swimsuit">swimsuit</a>, the back of the garment is so narrow that it disappears between the buttocks. By the 1990s the design had made its way to most of the Western world, and <a href="/wiki/Thong_(clothing)" title="Thong (clothing)">thong underwear</a> became popular. Today, the thong is one of the fastest-selling styles of underwear among women, and is also worn by men.</p>
<p>While health and practicality had previously been emphasized, in the 1970s retailers of men's underpants began focusing on fashion and sex appeal. Designers such as <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Klein" title="Calvin Klein">Calvin Klein</a> began featuring near-naked models in their advertisements for white <a href="/wiki/Briefs" title="Briefs">briefs</a>. The increased wealth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_community" title="Gay community">gay community</a> helped to promote a diversity of undergarment choices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philosophy_of_Andy_Warhol" title="The Philosophy of Andy Warhol">The Philosophy of Andy Warhol</a></i> (1975),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I told B I needed some socks too and at least 30 pairs of Jockey shorts. He suggested I switch to Italian-style <a href="/wiki/Briefs" title="Briefs">briefs</a>, the ones with the T-shaped crotch that tends to build you up. I told him I'd tried them once, in Rome, the day I was walking through a <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Taylor" title="Elizabeth Taylor">Liz Taylor</a> movie – and I didn't like them because they made me too self-aware. It gave me the feeling girls must have when they wear uplift bras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-15"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Warhol liked his Jockey <a href="/wiki/Briefs" title="Briefs">briefs</a> so much that he used a pair as a canvas for one of his dollar-sign paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-16"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK in the 1970s, tight jeans gave briefs a continued edge over boxer shorts among young men, but a decade later boxers were given a boost by <a href="/wiki/Nick_Kamen" title="Nick Kamen">Nick Kamen</a>'s performance in <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co." title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co.">Levi</a>'s "Launderette" TV commercial for its <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Levi%27s_501_jean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Levi's 501 jean (page does not exist)">501 jeans</a>, during which he stripped down to a pair of white boxer shorts in a public <a href="/wiki/Self-service_laundry" title="Self-service laundry">laundromat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-17"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup> Briefs however remained popular in America from the 1950s (amongst young men) until the mid 1990s and in Australia the brief remains popular today and has become iconic.</p>
<p>The 1990s saw the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Boxer_briefs" title="Boxer briefs">boxer briefs</a>, which take the longer shape of boxers but maintain the tightness of briefs. <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">Hip hop</a> stars popularized "<a href="/wiki/Sagging_(fashion)" title="Sagging (fashion)">sagging</a>", in which loosely fitting jeans or shorts were allowed to droop below the waist, exposing the waistband or a greater portion of boxer shorts, briefs, or boxer briefs worn underneath. The chiseled muscularity of <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wahlberg" title="Mark Wahlberg">Mark Wahlberg</a> (then known as Marky Mark) in a series of 1990s underwear advertisements for Calvin Klein briefs led to his success as a white hip hop star and a <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood</a> actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-18"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashions_and_trends">Fashions and trends</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Not_wearing_underpants">Not wearing underpants</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Going_commando" title="Going commando">Going commando</a></div>
<p>Some people choose not to wear any underpants, a practice sometimes referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Going_commando" title="Going commando">going commando</a>", for comfort, to enable their outer garments (particularly those which are form-fitting) to look more flattering, to avoid creating a <a href="/wiki/Panty_line" title="Panty line">panty line</a>, because they find it sexually exciting, or because they do not see any need for them.</p>
<p>Certain types of clothes, such as <a href="/wiki/Cycling_shorts" title="Cycling shorts">cycling shorts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kilt" title="Kilt">kilts</a>, are designed to be worn or are traditionally worn without underpants. This also applies for most clothes worn as <a href="/wiki/Nightwear" title="Nightwear">nightwear</a> and as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimwear" title="Swimwear">swimwear</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposed_underwear">Exposed underwear</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sagging_(fashion)" title="Sagging (fashion)">Sagging (fashion)</a></div>
<p>Underwear is sometimes partly exposed for fashion reasons or to titillate. A woman may, for instance, allow the top of her brassiere to be visible from under her <a href="/wiki/Collar_(clothing)" title="Collar (clothing)">collar</a>, or wear a see-through <a href="/wiki/Blouse" title="Blouse">blouse</a> over it. Some men wear T-shirts underneath partly or fully unbuttoned shirts.</p>
<p>A common style among young men is to allow the trousers to <a href="/wiki/Sagging_(fashion)" title="Sagging (fashion)">sag</a> below the waist, thus revealing the waistband or a greater portion of whatever underwear the man is wearing. A woman wearing low-rise trousers may expose the upper rear portion of her thong underwear is said to display a "<a href="/wiki/Whale_tail" title="Whale tail">whale tail</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Market">Market</span></h3>
<p>In January 2008 it was reported that, according to <a href="/wiki/Market_research" title="Market research">market research</a> firm <a href="/wiki/Mintel" title="Mintel">Mintel</a>, the men's underwear market in the UK was worth <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>674 million, and volume sales of men's underpants rose by 24% between 2000 and 2005. British manufacturers and retailers claim that most British men prefer "trunks", or short boxer briefs. The director of menswear of major British retailer <a href="/wiki/Marks_%26_Spencer" title="Marks &amp; Spencer">Marks &amp; Spencer</a> (M&amp;S), which sells 40 million pairs of men's underpants a year, was quoted as saying that while boxer shorts were still the most popular at M&amp;S, demand was easing off in favour of hipster trunks similar in design to the <a href="/wiki/Swimsuit#Men.27s_swimsuits_or_swimming_trunks" title="Swimsuit">swimming trunks</a> worn by actor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Craig" title="Daniel Craig">Daniel Craig</a> in the <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(2006_film)" title="Casino Royale (2006 film)">Casino Royale</a></i> (2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_6-19"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designers_and_retailers">Designers and retailers</span></h3>
<p>A number of major <a href="/wiki/Designer_label" title="Designer label">designer labels</a> are renowned for their underwear collections, including <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Klein" title="Calvin Klein">Calvin Klein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolce_%26_Gabbana" title="Dolce &amp; Gabbana">Dolce &amp; Gabbana</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Perla_(clothing)" title="La Perla (clothing)">La Perla</a>. Likewise, specialist underwear brands are constantly emerging, such as <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Christian" title="Andrew Christian">Andrew Christian</a>, <a href="/wiki/2(x)ist" title="2(x)ist">2(x)ist</a>, C-IN2, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ergowear_(Brand)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ergowear (Brand) (page does not exist)">Ergowear</a>, Ginch Gonch, Kitts, Lord, Obviously, and <a href="/wiki/Papi_(brand)" title="Papi (brand)">Papi</a>.</p>
<p>Specialist retailers of underwear include <a href="/wiki/High_Street" title="High Street">high street</a> stores <a href="/wiki/La_Senza" title="La Senza">La Senza</a> (Canada), <a href="/wiki/Agent_Provocateur_(lingerie)" title="Agent Provocateur (lingerie)">Agent Provocateur</a> (UK), <a href="/wiki/Victoria%27s_Secret" title="Victoria's Secret">Victoria's Secret</a> (USA), and GapBody, the lingerie division of the <a href="/wiki/Gap_Inc." title="Gap Inc.">Gap</a> established in 1998 (USA). Online retailer, <a href="/wiki/Freshpair" title="Freshpair">Freshpair</a>, emerged in 2000 in New York and in 2008 <a href="/wiki/Abercrombie_%26_Fitch" title="Abercrombie &amp; Fitch">Abercrombie &amp; Fitch</a> opened a new chain of stores, <a href="/wiki/Gilly_Hicks" title="Gilly Hicks">Gilly Hicks</a>, to compete with other underwear retailers.</p>
