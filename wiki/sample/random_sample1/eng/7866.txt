<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pinky swear</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>To <b>pinky swear</b>, or to make a <b>pinky promise</b>, is the entwining of the <a href="/wiki/Little_finger#Cultural_significance" title="Little finger">little fingers ("pinkies")</a> of two people to signify that a promise has been made.</p>
<p>In the United States, the pinky swear has existed since at least 1860, when <i>Dictionary of Americanisms</i> listed the following accompanying promise:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Pinky, pinky bow-bell,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Whoever tells a lie</i></dd>
<dd><i>Will sink down to the bad place</i>  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>]</dd>
<dd><i>And never rise up again.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinky_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pinky-1">[1]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Pinky swearing presumably started in Japan, where it is called <i><b>yubikiri</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">指切り</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "finger cut-off")</span> and often additionally confirmed with the vow "Finger cut-off, ten thousand fist-punchings, whoever lies has to swallow thousand needles." <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">「指切りげんまんうそついたら針千本のます」</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"Yubikiri genman uso tsuitara hari senbon nomasu"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The gesture may be connected to the Japanese belief that soulmates are connected by a <a href="/wiki/Red_string_of_fate" title="Red string of fate">red string of fate</a> attached to each of their pinkies.</p>
<p>In Japan, the pinky swear originally indicated that the person who breaks the promise must <a href="/wiki/Yubitsume" title="Yubitsume">cut off their pinky finger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (April 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Pinky_swear#Dubious" title="Talk:Pinky swear">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> In modern times, pinky swearing is a more informal way of sealing a promise. It is most common among school-age children and close friends. The pinky swear signifies a promise that can never be broken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
