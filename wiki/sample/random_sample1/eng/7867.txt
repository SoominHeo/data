<p><b>Saunders Mac Lane</b> (4 August 1909 – 14 April 2005) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> who co-founded <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a> with <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Eilenberg" title="Samuel Eilenberg">Samuel Eilenberg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saunders_Mac_Lane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mac Lane was born in <a href="/wiki/Norwich,_Connecticut" title="Norwich, Connecticut">Norwich, Connecticut</a>, near where his family lived in <a href="/wiki/Taftville,_Connecticut" title="Taftville, Connecticut">Taftville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup> He was christened "Leslie Saunders MacLane", but "Leslie" fell into disuse because his parents, Donald MacLane and Winifred Saunders, came to dislike it. He began inserting a space into his surname because his first wife found it difficult to type the name without a space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was the oldest of three brothers; one of his brothers, Gerald MacLane, also became a mathematics professor at <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University" title="Purdue University">Purdue University</a>. Another sister died as a baby. His father and grandfather were both ministers; his grandfather had been a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a>, but was kicked out of the church for believing in <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, and his father was a <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregationalist</a>. His mother, née Winifred Saunders, studied at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Holyoke_College" title="Mount Holyoke College">Mount Holyoke College</a> and taught English, Latin, and mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In high school, Mac Lane's favorite subject was <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. While in high school, his father died, and he came under his grandfather's care. His half-uncle, a lawyer, determined to send him to <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, where many of his relatives had been educated, and paid his way there beginning in 1926. As a freshman, he became disillusioned with chemistry. His mathematics instructor, <a href="/wiki/Lester_S._Hill" title="Lester S. Hill">Lester S. Hill</a>, coached him for a local mathematics competition which he won, setting the direction for his future work. He went on to study mathematics and physics as a double major, taking courses from <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Beams" title="Jesse Beams">Jesse Beams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_William_Brown" title="Ernest William Brown">Ernest William Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Lawrence" title="Ernest Lawrence">Ernest Lawrence</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._S._C._Northrop" title="F. S. C. Northrop">F. S. C. Northrop</a>, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%98ystein_Ore" title="Øystein Ore">Øystein Ore</a>, among others. He graduated from Yale with a B.A. in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup> During this period, he published his first scientific paper, in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and co-authored with <a href="/wiki/Irving_Langmuir" title="Irving Langmuir">Irving Langmuir</a>.</p>
<p>In 1929, at a party of Yale football supporters in <a href="/wiki/Montclair,_New_Jersey" title="Montclair, New Jersey">Montclair, New Jersey</a>, Mac Lane (there to be presented with a prize for having the best grade point average yet recorded at Yale) had met <a href="/wiki/Robert_Maynard_Hutchins" title="Robert Maynard Hutchins">Robert Maynard Hutchins</a>, the new president of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, who encouraged him to go there for his graduate studies and soon afterwards offered him a scholarship. Mac Lane neglected to actually apply to the program, but showed up and was admitted anyway. At Chicago, the subjects he studied included <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a> with <a href="/wiki/E._H._Moore" title="E. H. Moore">E. H. Moore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> with <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Eugene_Dickson" title="Leonard Eugene Dickson">Leonard Eugene Dickson</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Calculus_of_variations" title="Calculus of variations">calculus of variations</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Ames_Bliss" title="Gilbert Ames Bliss">Gilbert Ames Bliss</a>, and logic with <a href="/wiki/Mortimer_J._Adler" title="Mortimer J. Adler">Mortimer J. Adler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1931, having earned his master's degree and feeling restless at Chicago, he earned a fellowship from the Institute of International Education and became one of the last Americans to study at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of Göttingen">University of Göttingen</a> prior to its decline under the Nazis. His greatest influences there were <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bernays" title="Paul Bernays">Paul Bernays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Weyl" title="Hermann Weyl">Hermann Weyl</a>. By the time he finished his doctorate in 1934, Bernays had been forced to leave because he was Jewish, and Weyl became his main examiner. At Göttingen, Mac Lane also studied with <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Herglotz" title="Gustav Herglotz">Gustav Herglotz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Noether" title="Emmy Noether">Emmy Noether</a>. Within days of finishing his degree, he married Dorothy Jones, from Chicago, and soon returned to the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saunders_Mac_Lane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1934 through 1938, Mac Lane held short term appointments at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>. He then held a tenure track appointment at Harvard from 1938 to 1947. In 1941, while giving a series of visiting lectures at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>, he met <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Eilenberg" title="Samuel Eilenberg">Samuel Eilenberg</a> and began what would become a fruitful collaboration on the interplay between algebra and topology. In 1944 and 1945, he also directed Columbia University's Applied Mathematics Group, which was involved in the war effort as a contractor for the <a href="/wiki/Applied_Mathematics_Panel" title="Applied Mathematics Panel">Applied Mathematics Panel</a>; the mathematics he worked on in this group concerned <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> for <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">fire-control systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1947, he accepted an offer to return to Chicago, where (in part because of the university's involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, and in part because of the administrative efforts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshall_Stone" title="Marshall Stone">Marshall Stone</a>) many other famous mathematicians and physicists had also recently moved. He traveled as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellow" title="Guggenheim Fellow">Guggenheim Fellow</a> to <a href="/wiki/ETH_Zurich" title="ETH Zurich">ETH Zurich</a> for the 1947–1948 term, where he worked with <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Hopf" title="Heinz Hopf">Heinz Hopf</a>. Mac Lane succeeded Stone as department chair in 1952, and served for six years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mac Lane was vice president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society" title="American Philosophical Society">American Philosophical Society</a>, and president of the <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a>. While presiding over the <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Association_of_America" title="Mathematical Association of America">Mathematical Association of America</a> in the 1950s, he initiated its activities aimed at improving the teaching of modern mathematics. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Board" title="National Science Board">National Science Board</a>, 1974–1980, advising the American government. In 1976, he led a delegation of mathematicians to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> to study the conditions affecting mathematics there. Mac Lane was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 1949, and received the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> in 1989.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saunders_Mac_Lane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After a thesis in <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_logic" title="Mathematical logic">mathematical logic</a>, his early work was in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_theory_(mathematics)" title="Field theory (mathematics)">field theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valuation_(algebra)" title="Valuation (algebra)">valuation theory</a>. He wrote on <a href="/wiki/Valuation_ring" title="Valuation ring">valuation rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Witt_vector" title="Witt vector">Witt vectors</a>, and separability in infinite <a href="/wiki/Field_extension" title="Field extension">field extensions</a>. He started writing on <a href="/wiki/Group_extension" title="Group extension">group extensions</a> in 1942, and in 1943 began his research on what are now called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eilenberg%E2%80%93Mac_Lane_space" title="Eilenberg–Mac Lane space">Eilenberg–MacLane spaces</a> K(<i>G</i>,<i>n</i>), having a single non-trivial <a href="/wiki/Homotopy_group" title="Homotopy group">homotopy group</a> <i>G</i> in dimension <i>n</i>. This work opened the way to <a href="/wiki/Group_cohomology" title="Group cohomology">group cohomology</a> in general.</p>
<p>After introducing, via the <a href="/wiki/Eilenberg%E2%80%93Steenrod_axioms" title="Eilenberg–Steenrod axioms">Eilenberg–Steenrod axioms</a>, the abstract approach to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homology_theory" title="Homology theory">homology theory</a>, he and Eilenberg originated <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a> in 1945. He is especially known for his work on <a href="/wiki/Coherence_theorem" title="Coherence theorem">coherence theorems</a>. A recurring feature of category theory, <a href="/wiki/Abstract_algebra" title="Abstract algebra">abstract algebra</a>, and of some other mathematics as well, is the use of <a href="/wiki/Commutative_diagram" title="Commutative diagram">diagrams</a>, consisting of arrows (<a href="/wiki/Morphism" title="Morphism">morphisms</a>) linking objects, such as <a href="/wiki/Product_(category_theory)" title="Product (category theory)">products</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coproduct" title="Coproduct">coproducts</a>. According to McLarty (2005), this diagrammatic approach to contemporary mathematics largely stems from Mac Lane (1948). Mac Lane also coined the term <a href="/wiki/Yoneda_lemma" title="Yoneda lemma">Yoneda lemma</a> for a lemma which is an essential background to many central concepts of category theory and which was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Nobuo_Yoneda" title="Nobuo Yoneda">Nobuo Yoneda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mac Lane had an exemplary devotion to writing approachable texts, starting with his very influential <i>A Survey of Modern Algebra</i>, coauthored in 1941 with <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Birkhoff" title="Garrett Birkhoff">Garrett Birkhoff</a>. From then on, it was possible to teach elementary modern algebra to undergraduates using an English text. His <i><a href="/wiki/Categories_for_the_Working_Mathematician" title="Categories for the Working Mathematician">Categories for the Working Mathematician</a></i> remains the definitive introduction to category theory.</p>
<p>Mac Lane supervised the Ph.Ds of, among many others, <a href="/wiki/David_Eisenbud" title="David Eisenbud">David Eisenbud</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Alvin_Howard" title="William Alvin Howard">William Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irving_Kaplansky" title="Irving Kaplansky">Irving Kaplansky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Morley" title="Michael D. Morley">Michael Morley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anil_Nerode" title="Anil Nerode">Anil Nerode</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Solovay" title="Robert Solovay">Robert Solovay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_G._Thompson" title="John G. Thompson">John G. Thompson</a>.</p>
<p>In addition to reviewing a fair bit of his mathematical output, the obituary articles McLarty (2005, 2007) clarify Mac Lane's contributions to the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mathematics" title="Philosophy of mathematics">philosophy of mathematics</a>. Mac Lane (1986) is an approachable introduction to his views on this subject.</p>
