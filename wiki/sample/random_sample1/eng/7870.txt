<p><i><b>Gymnadenia conopsea</b></i>, commonly known as the <b>fragrant orchid</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plant</a> belonging to the family <a href="/wiki/Orchidaceae" title="Orchidaceae">Orchidaceae</a> native to northern Europe.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gymnadenia_conopsea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the genus <i>Gymnadenia</i> is formed from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> words <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CF%85%CE%BC%CE%BD%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:γυμνός">γυμνός</a></span> (<i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">gymnós</span></i>, "nude") and <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%B4%CE%AE%CE%BD" title="wikt:ἀδήν">ἀδήν</a></span> (<i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">adēn</span></i>, "gland") and refers to the characteristics of the organs for secreting nectar. The specific Latin name "conopsea" derives from the Greek <i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">κώνωψ</span></i> (<i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">kónops</span></i>), literally meaning "mosquito-like", probably because of the similarity of the long spur of the flower with the mouthparts of a <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquito</a>.</p>
<p>The scientific binomial name of this plant was initially <i>Orchis conopsea</i>, proposed by the Swedish naturalist and botanist Carl von Linné (1707–1778) in his <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></span></i> of 1753. The name has been subsequently amended to the one currently accepted (<i>Gymnadenia conopsea</i>), by the British botanist Robert Brown (1773–1858) in 1813. In German, this plant is called <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Mücken-Händelwurz</span></i> or <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Mücken Nacktdrüse</span></i>; in French, is called <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">gymnadénie moucheron</span></i> or <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">orchis moucheron</span></i>; in Italy, it is called <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it">manina rosea</span></i> ("pink hand"); in Croatian, it is known under <i><span lang="hr" xml:lang="hr">mrežasti vranjak</span></i>.</p>

<p><i>Gymnadenia conopsea</i> reaches on average 20–60 centimetres (7.9–23.6 in) of height, with a maximum of 80 centimetres (31 in). These plants are bulbous geophytes, as they bring their buds in underground tubers or bulbs, organs that annually produce new stems, leaves and flowers. Furthermore, these orchids are "terrestrial", because unlike "epiphyte" species do not live at the expense of other plants of major sizes.</p>
<p>The stem is leafy and robust, with a striated surface. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are long, narrow and lanceolate and vary from 3 to 7. The leaf color is gray-green. Size of leaf: width 1 to 2 cm, length 10 – 25 cm.</p>
<p>These orchids have two ovoidal bulbs, deeply webbed and with many small and short lobes. Size of tubers: 1 to 3.5 cm.</p>
<p>The inflorescence is 5–25 centimetres (2.0–9.8 in) long and it is composed of flowers gathered in dense cylindrical spikes (up to 50 flowers per spike). These inflorescences are scented and genes underlying eugenol (a volatile scent compound) production have been identified in Gymnadenia conopsea, Gymnadenia odoratissima and Gymnadenia densiflora <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The flowers are petiolated, placed in the axils of long bracts and reach on average 8–14 centimetres (3.1–5.5 in). They have a distinctive three lobed lip and long spurs. Their light scent is similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloves" title="Cloves">cloves</a>. Their colors vary from white and pink to pink-purple, more rarely white. These flowers bloom in the Summer, from June to July. They are <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphrodite</a> and pollinated by insects (<a href="/wiki/Entomophily" title="Entomophily">entomophily</a>), including <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>. The species is almost exclusively pollinated by moths (Lepidoptera). The most common pollinators are the small elephant hawk-moth (<i><a href="/wiki/Deilephila_porcellus" title="Deilephila porcellus">Deilephila porcellus</a></i>), hummingbird hawk-moth (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroglossum_stellatarum" title="Macroglossum stellatarum">Macroglossum stellatarum</a></i>), silver Y (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autographa_gamma" title="Autographa gamma">Autographa gamma</a></i>), burnished brass (<i><a href="/wiki/Diachrysia_chrysitis" title="Diachrysia chrysitis">Diachrysia chrysitis</a></i>) and large yellow underwing (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noctua_pronuba" title="Noctua pronuba">Noctua pronuba</a></i>). Fruit set is high with an average of 73%. The seeds germination is conditioned by the presence of specific fungi.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gymnadenia_conopsea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This plant is quite common throughout <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a> with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Dinaric_Alps" title="Dinaric Alps">Dinaric Alps</a>. In Asia it is common in areas to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gymnadenia_conopsea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species habitat includes mountain meadows and pastures, grassland and fens. They grow on siliceous and calcareous substrate, mildly damp and with low nutritional value, at an altitude of 0–2,400 metres (0–7,874 ft) above sea level.</p>
