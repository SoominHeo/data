<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hand puppet</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>hand puppet</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">puppet</a> that is controlled by the hand or hands that occupies the interior of the puppet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair.2C_A_p.15_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair.2C_A_p.15-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glove_puppetry" title="Glove puppetry">Glove puppets</a> are a variation of hand puppets. Rod puppets require one of the puppeteer's hands inside the puppet glove holding a rod which controls the head, and the puppet's body then hangs over most or all of the forearm of the puppeteer, and possibly extends further. Other parts of the puppet may be controlled by different means, e.g., by rods operated by the puppeteer's free hand, or strings or levers pulled the head or body. A smaller variety, <b>simple hand puppets</b> often have no significant manipulable parts at all. <b>Finger puppets</b> are not hand puppets as they are used only on a finger.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_hand_puppets">Simple hand puppets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_puppet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Simple hand puppets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simple_hand_puppet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="450" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Simple_hand_puppet.jpg/200px-Simple_hand_puppet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Simple_hand_puppet.jpg/300px-Simple_hand_puppet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Simple_hand_puppet.jpg/400px-Simple_hand_puppet.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simple_hand_puppet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A simple toy dog hand puppet.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The simplest hand puppets are those with few or no moving parts. They can be stiff, made from e.g. a hard <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>, but are more often flexible, made from <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">fabric</a>, possibly with some stuffing and attached decorations for eyes, nose, and so on. The mouth may be a mere decoration that does not open and close, or the thumb may enter a separate pocket from the rest of the fabric and so simulate a <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>, allowing the puppet to talk.</p>
<p>Simple hand puppets are usually not much larger than the hand itself. A <a href="/wiki/Sock_puppet" title="Sock puppet">sock puppet</a> is a particularly simple type of hand puppet made from a sock. A <b>glove puppet</b> is slightly more complex, with an internal division for fingers allowing independent manipulation of a character's arms. The unconsumed hand of the puppeteer is usually concealed from the audience to maintain the illusion of the puppet.</p>
<p>Simple hand puppets, especially popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Licensed_character" title="Licensed character">licensed characters</a>, are sometimes distributed as children <a href="/wiki/Toy" title="Toy">toys</a> or <a href="/wiki/Party_favor" title="Party favor">party favors</a>. Children usually like to experiment in play with a puppet creating voices and movements and in many cases staging a strictly private performance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rod_puppets">Rod puppets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_puppet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rod puppets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guiermo-rods-50pct.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="304" data-file-width="331" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Guiermo-rods-50pct.png/200px-Guiermo-rods-50pct.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Guiermo-rods-50pct.png/300px-Guiermo-rods-50pct.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/05/Guiermo-rods-50pct.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guiermo-rods-50pct.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rod_puppet" title="Rod puppet">rod puppet</a> with typical rods to control the arms.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A rod puppet is made out of wood wire and string and is manipulated with wooden or wire rods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Rod puppets can sometimes have a complete working hinged mouth but many do not. A rod puppet can have a fixed facial expression. Arms are usually a requirement as rods are attached to them. A fish rod puppet could have a rod attached to the tail to manipulate this section of the puppet. Sometimes special variants exist with additional manipulable parts: (e.g., eyelids that open and close). Many rod puppets depict only the upper half of the character, from the waist up, with the stage covering the missing remainder, but variations sometimes have legs. The legs usually just dangle, but in special cases the legs may be controlled either from behind the stage using rods from below.These are mostly used at carnivals or fairs. A very common example of rod puppets are those of <a href="/wiki/The_Muppets" title="The Muppets">The Muppets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_puppet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_positioning">Basic positioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_puppet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Basic positioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with any stage performer, the puppet should generally face the audience; but may turn to one side or the other. There are times when a puppet does turn its back to an audience just like an actor. Puppets generally should look out towards an audience and not up at the ceiling unless they wish an audience to follow their line of vision. Generally a hand or glove puppet should talk a lot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair.2C_A_p.15_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair.2C_A_p.15-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the puppet stage is normally positioned higher than the seated audience (in order to best hide the puppeteers and allow for the puppet to be the focus of the audience), the puppet should be able to lean forward slightly with its head tilted sometimes down, in order to make eye contact with the audience. A puppet that fails to do this can appear to be staring over the heads of the audience. A puppet that looks at the ceiling sends of the signal that it is not interested in the audience.</p>
<p>Vertically, the puppet should be kept elevated, with its shoulders well above the stage. Ideally, most of the puppet's torso should be kept visible to the audience at all times.</p>
<p>This is to the manner in which the puppet's mouth opens and closes in order to appear to form words or sounds, similar to <a href="/wiki/Lip_sync" title="Lip sync">lip syncing</a>. The puppet's lower jaw (<a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>) should open downward, with the upper jaw, which is usually contiguous with the rest of the head, not raising much. This mimics the way the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> mouth works. Puppets of course are not human and have all manner of moving their mouths. They can make facial movements no human can possibly attempt.</p>
<p>The basic mainstay of diction for most puppeteers is <i>syllabic diction</i>, i.e., opening the puppet's mouth once for each syllable, closing it at the end of the syllable. Another common novice mistake is to reverse this, closing the mouth on each syllable, which makes it look as if the puppet is biting off its words; this effect should be carefully avoided.</p>
<p>When the puppet must speak very rapidly, a variant on syllabic diction may be used where some syllables are omitted. It is important to open the puppet's mouth for the first and last syllables as well as all long or emphasized syllables, but most audiences will not notice if some of the unimportant syllables are omitted, provided it happens fairly quickly. As the puppet's speech (or song lyrics) slows down, it becomes more and more important to include every syllable.</p>
<p>More advanced forms of diction are possible. Ultimately the experienced puppeteer will master <i>phonetic diction</i>, in which the degree to which the puppet's mouth is open at any given point mimics the motion of the human mouth forming the same series of <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">sounds</a>; thus, the mouth will be open wide for a long O vowel, briefly close almost completely when forming a <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plosive_consonant" title="Plosive consonant">stop</a>, and so forth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Body_movement">Body movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hand_puppet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Body movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1366" data-file-width="2048" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg/250px-Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg/375px-Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg/500px-Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Puppet_Bleeckie_sliding.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bleeckie, a rod puppet from a series of web videos, ready to slide</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the most important techniques in puppetry is continuous motion. A puppet that remains still has a dull, lifeless appearance and is said to be <i>dead</i>. Motion should shift from one portion of the puppet to another, so that one moment the puppet is moving its head and the next moment shifting its torso or repositioning an arm. The puppet may shift from side to side, look around, lean or straighten, fidget (with part of the stage, its own clothing or hair, or any available object), cross or uncross its arms, sigh, tilt its head, or make any number of other small motions, in order to continue to appear lifelike. A puppet should however not move when another puppet is speaking. To do so confuses an audience as to which particular puppet is speaking at any given time. Maintaining clear focus for an audience in a puppet performance is extremely important.</p>
