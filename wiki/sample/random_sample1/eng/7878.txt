<p><b>Saul Bellow</b> (born <b>Solomon Bellows</b>; 10 June 1915 – 5 April 2005) was a Canadian-American writer. For his literary work, Bellow was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize for Literature</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Arts" title="National Medal of Arts">National Medal of Arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is the only writer to win the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award_for_Fiction" title="National Book Award for Fiction">National Book Award for Fiction</a> three times<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winners_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-winners-2">[2]</a></sup> and he received the National Book Foundation's lifetime <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award#Medal_for_Distinguished_Contribution_to_American_Letters" title="National Book Award">Medal for Distinguished Contribution to American Letters</a> in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-medal-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the words of the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Committee" title="Nobel Committee">Nobel Committee</a>, his writing exhibited "the mixture of rich <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picaresque" title="Picaresque">picaresque</a> novel and subtle analysis of our culture, of entertaining adventure, drastic and tragic episodes in quick succession interspersed with philosophic conversation, all developed by a commentator with a witty tongue and penetrating insight into the outer and inner complications that drive us to act, or prevent us from acting, and that can be called the dilemma of our age."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize-4">[4]</a></sup> His best-known works include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Augie_March" title="The Adventures of Augie March">The Adventures of Augie March</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Henderson_the_Rain_King" title="Henderson the Rain King">Henderson the Rain King</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Herzog_(novel)" title="Herzog (novel)">Herzog</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Sammler%27s_Planet" title="Mr. Sammler's Planet">Mr. Sammler's Planet</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Seize_the_Day_(novel)" title="Seize the Day (novel)">Seize the Day</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Humboldt%27s_Gift" title="Humboldt's Gift">Humboldt's Gift</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ravelstein" title="Ravelstein">Ravelstein</a></i>. Widely regarded as one of the 20th century's greatest authors, Bellow has had a "huge literary influence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bellow said that of all his characters Eugene Henderson, of <i><a href="/wiki/Henderson_the_Rain_King" title="Henderson the Rain King">Henderson the Rain King</a></i>, was the one most like himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-6">[6]</a></sup> Bellow grew up as an insolent slum kid, a "thick-necked" rowdy, and an immigrant from Quebec. As <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hitchens" title="Christopher Hitchens">Christopher Hitchens</a> describes it, Bellow's fiction and principal characters reflect his own yearning for transcendence, a battle "to overcome not just ghetto conditions but also ghetto psychoses."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Bellow's protagonists, in one shape or another, all wrestle with what Corde (Albert Corde, the dean in "The Dean's December") called "the big-scale insanities of the 20th century." This transcendence of the "unutterably dismal" (a phrase from <i><a href="/wiki/Dangling_Man" title="Dangling Man">Dangling Man</a></i>) is achieved, if it can be achieved at all, through a "ferocious assimilation of learning" (Hitchens) and an emphasis on nobility.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Saul Bellow was born Solomon Bellows<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Name_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Name-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Lachine,_Quebec" title="Lachine, Quebec">Lachine, Quebec</a>, two years after his parents, Lescha (née Gordin) and Abraham Bellows,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-google-11">[11]</a></sup> emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, Russia. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Name_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Name-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> Bellow's family was <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_Jews" title="Lithuanian Jews">Lithuanian-Jewish</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent-13">[13]</a></sup> his father was born in <a href="/wiki/Vilnius" title="Vilnius">Vilnius</a>. Bellow celebrated his birthday in June, although he may have been born in July (in the Jewish community, it was customary to record the Hebrew date of birth, which does not always coincide with the Gregorian calendar).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Of his family's emigration, Bellow wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The retrospective was strong in me because of my parents. They were both full of the notion that they were falling, falling. They had been prosperous cosmopolitans in Saint Petersburg. My mother could never stop talking about the family <a href="/wiki/Dacha" title="Dacha">dacha</a>, her privileged life, and how all that was now gone. She was working in the kitchen. Cooking, washing, mending... There had been servants in Russia... But you could always transpose from your humiliating condition with the help of a sort of embittered irony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A period of illness from a respiratory infection at age eight both taught him self-reliance (he was a very fit man despite his sedentary occupation) and provided an opportunity to satisfy his hunger for reading: reportedly, he decided to be a writer when he first read <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">Harriet Beecher Stowe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">Uncle Tom's Cabin</a>.</i></p>
<p>When Bellow was nine, his family moved to the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Park,_Chicago" title="Humboldt Park, Chicago">Humboldt Park</a> neighborhood on the <a href="/wiki/West_Side,_Chicago" title="West Side, Chicago">West Side</a> of Chicago, the city that formed the backdrop of many of his novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> Bellow's father, Abraham, had become an onion importer. He also worked in a bakery, as a coal delivery man, and as a bootlegger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> Bellow's mother, Liza, died when he was 17. He was left with his father and brother Maurice. His mother was deeply religious, and wanted her youngest son, Saul, to become a rabbi or a concert violinist. But he rebelled against what he later called the "suffocating orthodoxy" of his religious upbringing, and he began writing at a young age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> Bellow's lifelong love for the Bible began at four when he learned <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>. Bellow also grew up reading <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> and the great <a href="/wiki/Russian_literature" title="Russian literature">Russian novelists</a> of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> In Chicago, he took part in <a href="/wiki/Anthroposophy" title="Anthroposophy">anthroposophical studies</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthroposophical_Society_of_Chicago" title="Anthroposophical Society of Chicago">Anthroposophical Society of Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Bellow attended Tuley High School on Chicago's west side where he befriended fellow writer <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Rosenfeld" title="Isaac Rosenfeld">Isaac Rosenfeld</a>. In his 1959 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Henderson_the_Rain_King" title="Henderson the Rain King">Henderson the Rain King</a></i>, Bellow modeled the character King Dahfu on Rosenfeld.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_career">Education and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Education and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bellow attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> but later transferred to <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a>. He originally wanted to study literature, but he felt the English department was anti-Jewish. Instead, he graduated with honors in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and sociology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It has been suggested Bellow's study of anthropology had an influence on his literary style, and anthropological references pepper his works.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bellow later did graduate work at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a>.</p>
<p>Paraphrasing Bellow's description of his close friend <a href="/wiki/Allan_Bloom" title="Allan Bloom">Allan Bloom</a> (see <i><a href="/wiki/Ravelstein" title="Ravelstein">Ravelstein</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/John_Podhoretz" title="John Podhoretz">John Podhoretz</a> has said that both Bellow and Bloom "inhaled books and ideas the way the rest of us breathe air."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesonline_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-timesonline-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1930s, Bellow was part of the Chicago branch of the <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a> Writer's Project, which included such future Chicago literary luminaries as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wright_(author)" title="Richard Wright (author)">Richard Wright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Algren" title="Nelson Algren">Nelson Algren</a>. Many of the writers were radical: if they were not members of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_USA" title="Communist Party USA">Communist Party USA</a>, they were sympathetic to the cause. Bellow was a <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyist</a>, but because of the greater numbers of <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinist</a>-leaning writers he had to suffer their taunts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1941 Bellow became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalized" title="Naturalized">naturalized</a> US citizen, after discovering upon attempting to enlist in the armed forces that he had immigrated to the United States illegally as a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SlateHitchens_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SlateHitchens-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1943, <a href="/wiki/Maxim_Lieber" title="Maxim Lieber">Maxim Lieber</a> was his literary agent.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Bellow joined the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Merchant_Marine" title="United States Merchant Marine">merchant marine</a> and during his service he completed his first novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Dangling_Man" title="Dangling Man">Dangling Man</a></i> (1944) about a young Chicago man waiting to be drafted for the war.</p>
<p>From 1946 through 1948 Bellow taught at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a>, living on Commonwealth Avenue, in St. Paul, Minnesota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1948, Bellow was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellowship" title="Guggenheim Fellowship">Guggenheim Fellowship</a> that allowed him to move to Paris, where he began writing <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Augie_March" title="The Adventures of Augie March">The Adventures of Augie March</a></i> (1953). Critics have remarked on the resemblance between Bellow's <a href="/wiki/Picaresque_novel" title="Picaresque novel">picaresque novel</a> and the great 17th Century Spanish classic <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quixote</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The book starts with one of American literature's most famous opening paragraphs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr-24">[24]</a></sup> and it follows its titular character through a series of careers and encounters, as he lives by his wits and his resolve. Written in a colloquial yet philosophical style, <i>The Adventures of Augie March</i> established Bellow's reputation as a major author.</p>
<p>In 1958, Bellow once again taught at the University of Minnesota. During this time, he and his wife Sasha received psychoanalysis from University of Minnesota Psychology Professor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Meehl" title="Paul Meehl">Paul Meehl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the spring term of 1961 he taught creative writing at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="University of Puerto Rico">University of Puerto Rico</a> at <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Piedras,_Puerto_Rico" title="Río Piedras, Puerto Rico">Río Piedras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> One of his students was <a href="/wiki/William_Kennedy_(author)" title="William Kennedy (author)">William Kennedy</a>, who was encouraged by Bellow to write fiction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Chicago_and_mid-career">Return to Chicago and mid-career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Return to Chicago and mid-career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bellow lived in New York City for a number of years, but he returned to Chicago in 1962 as a professor at the Committee on Social Thought at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>. The committee's goal was to have professors work closely with talented graduate students on a multi-disciplinary approach to learning. Bellow taught on the committee for more than 30 years, alongside his close friend, the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Allan_Bloom" title="Allan Bloom">Allan Bloom</a>.</p>
<p>There were also other reasons for Bellow's return to Chicago, where he moved into the <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_Chicago" title="Hyde Park, Chicago">Hyde Park</a> neighborhood with his third wife, Susan Glassman. Bellow found Chicago vulgar but vital, and more representative of America than New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He was able to stay in contact with old high school friends and a broad cross-section of society. In a 1982 profile, Bellow's neighborhood was described as a high-crime area in the city's center, and Bellow maintained he had to live in such a place as a writer and "stick to his guns."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bellow hit the bestseller list in 1964 with his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Herzog_(novel)" title="Herzog (novel)">Herzog</a></i>. Bellow was surprised at the commercial success of this cerebral novel about a middle-aged and troubled college professor who writes letters to friends, scholars and the dead, but never sends them. Bellow returned to his exploration of mental instability, and its relationship to genius, in his 1975 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Humboldt%27s_Gift" title="Humboldt's Gift">Humboldt's Gift</a></i>. Bellow used his late friend and rival, the brilliant but self-destructive poet <a href="/wiki/Delmore_Schwartz" title="Delmore Schwartz">Delmore Schwartz</a>, as his model for the novel's title character, Von Humboldt Fleisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlas_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-29">[29]</a></sup> Bellow also used Rudolf Steiner's spiritual science, anthroposophy, as a theme in the book, having attended a study group in Chicago. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nobel_Prize_and_later_career">Nobel Prize and later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nobel Prize and later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="450" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg/220px-SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg/330px-SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg/440px-SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SaulBellowAndKeithBotsford.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saul Bellow (left) with <a href="/wiki/Keith_Botsford" title="Keith Botsford">Keith Botsford</a>, around 1992</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Propelled by the success of <i>Humboldt's Gift</i>, Bellow won the Nobel Prize in literature in 1976. In the 70-minute address he gave to an audience in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, Bellow called on writers to be beacons for civilization and awaken it from intellectual torpor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlas_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following year, the <a href="/wiki/National_Endowment_for_the_Humanities" title="National Endowment for the Humanities">National Endowment for the Humanities</a> selected Bellow for the <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Lecture" title="Jefferson Lecture">Jefferson Lecture</a>, the U.S. federal government's highest honor for achievement in the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>. Bellow's lecture was entitled "The Writer and His Country Look Each Other Over."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jefflect_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-jefflect-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>From December 1981 to March 1982, Bellow was the Visiting Lansdowne Scholar at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Victoria" title="University of Victoria">University of Victoria</a> (B.C.),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and also held the title Writer-in-Residence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bellow traveled widely throughout his life, mainly to Europe, which he sometimes visited twice a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlas_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-29">[29]</a></sup> As a young man, Bellow went to <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> to meet <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Leon Trotsky</a>, but the expatriate Russian revolutionary was assassinated the day before they were to meet. Bellow's social contacts were wide and varied. He tagged along with <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> for a magazine profile he never wrote, he was close friends with the author <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Ellison" title="Ralph Ellison">Ralph Ellison</a>. His many friends included the journalist <a href="/wiki/Sydney_J._Harris" title="Sydney J. Harris">Sydney J. Harris</a> and the poet <a href="/wiki/John_Berryman" title="John Berryman">John Berryman</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>While sales of Bellow's first few novels were modest, that turned around with <i><a href="/wiki/Herzog_(novel)" title="Herzog (novel)">Herzog</a></i>. Bellow continued teaching well into his old age, enjoying its human interaction and exchange of ideas. He taught at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="University of Puerto Rico">University of Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bard_College" title="Bard College">Bard College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>, where he co-taught a class with <a href="/wiki/James_Wood_(critic)" title="James Wood (critic)">James Wood</a> ('modestly absenting himself' when it was time to discuss <i>Seize the Day</i>). In order to take up his appointment at Boston, Bellow moved in 1993 from Chicago to <a href="/wiki/Brookline,_Massachusetts" title="Brookline, Massachusetts">Brookline, Massachusetts</a>, where he died on 5 April 2005, at age 89. He is buried at the Jewish cemetery Shir HeHarim of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brattleboro" title="Brattleboro">Brattleboro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>.</p>
<p>Bellow was married five times, with all but his last marriage ending in divorce. His son by his first marriage, Greg Bellow, became a psychotherapist; Greg Bellow published <i>Saul Bellow’s Heart: A Son’s Memoir</i> in 2013, nearly a decade after his father's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods-34">[34]</a></sup> Bellow's son by his second marriage, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Bellow" title="Adam Bellow">Adam</a>, published a nonfiction book <i>In Praise of Nepotism</i> in 2003. Bellow's wives were Anita Goshkin, Alexandra (Sondra) Tsachacbasov, Susan Glassman, <a href="/wiki/Alexandra_Bellow" title="Alexandra Bellow">Alexandra Ionescu Tulcea</a> and Janis Freedman. In 1999, when he was 84, Bellow had his fourth child and first daughter, with Freedman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>While he read voluminously, Bellow also played the violin and followed sports. Work was a constant for him, but he at times toiled at a plodding pace on his novels, frustrating the publishing company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlas_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>His early works earned him the reputation as a major novelist of the 20th century, and by his death he was widely regarded as one of the greatest living novelists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> He was the first writer to win three National Book Awards in all award categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winners_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-winners-2">[2]</a></sup> His friend and protege <a href="/wiki/Philip_Roth" title="Philip Roth">Philip Roth</a> has said of him, "The backbone of 20th-century American literature has been provided by two novelists—William Faulkner and Saul Bellow. Together they are the Melville, Hawthorne, and Twain of the 20th century." <a href="/wiki/James_Wood_(critic)" title="James Wood (critic)">James Wood</a>, in a eulogy of Bellow in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">The New Republic</a></i>, wrote:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I judged all modern prose by his. Unfair, certainly, because he made even the fleet-footed—the Updikes, the DeLillos, the Roths—seem like monopodes. Yet what else could I do? I discovered Saul Bellow's prose in my late teens, and henceforth, the relationship had the quality of a love affair about which one could not keep silent. Over the last week, much has been said about Bellow's prose, and most of the praise—perhaps because it has been overwhelmingly by men—has tended toward the robust: We hear about Bellow's mixing of high and low registers, his Melvillean cadences jostling the jivey Yiddish rhythms, the great teeming democracy of the big novels, the crooks and frauds and intellectuals who loudly people the brilliant sensorium of the fiction. All of this is true enough; John Cheever, in his journals, lamented that, alongside Bellow's fiction, his stories seemed like mere suburban splinters. Ian McEwan wisely suggested last week that British writers and critics may have been attracted to Bellow precisely because he kept alive a Dickensian amplitude now lacking in the English novel. [...] But nobody mentioned the beauty of this writing, its music, its high lyricism, its firm but luxurious pleasure in language itself. [...] [I]n truth, I could not thank him enough when he was alive, and I cannot now.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes_and_style">Themes and style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Themes and style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The author's works speak to the disorienting nature of modern civilization, and the countervailing ability of humans to overcome their frailty and achieve greatness (or at least awareness). Bellow saw many flaws in modern civilization, and its ability to foster madness, materialism and misleading knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Principal characters in Bellow's fiction have heroic potential, and many times they stand in contrast to the negative forces of society. Often these characters are Jewish and have a sense of alienation or otherness.</p>
<p>Jewish life and identity is a major theme in Bellow's work, although he bristled at being called a "Jewish writer." Bellow's work also shows a great appreciation of America, and a fascination with the uniqueness and vibrancy of the American experience.</p>
<p>Bellow's work abounds in references and quotes from the likes of <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust" title="Marcel Proust">Marcel Proust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a>, but he offsets these high-culture references with jokes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup> Bellow interspersed autobiographical elements into his fiction, and many of his principal characters were said to bear a resemblance to him.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism.2C_controversy_and_conservative_cultural_activism">Criticism, controversy and conservative cultural activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saul_Bellow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism, controversy and conservative cultural activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Amis" title="Martin Amis">Martin Amis</a> described Bellow as "The greatest American author ever, in my view".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>His sentences seem to weigh more than anyone else's. He is like a force of nature... He breaks all the rules [...] [T]he people in Bellow's fiction are real people, yet the intensity of the gaze that he bathes them in, somehow through the particular, opens up into the universal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linda_Grant_(novelist)" title="Linda Grant (novelist)">Linda Grant</a>, "What Bellow had to tell us in his fiction was that it was worth it, being alive."</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>His vigour, vitality, humour and passion were always matched by the insistence on thought, not the predigested cliches of the mass media or of those on the left, which had begun to disgust him by the Sixties... It's easy to be a 'writer of conscience'—anyone can do it if they want to; just choose your cause. Bellow was a writer about conscience and consciousness, forever conflicted by the competing demands of the great cities, the individual's urge to survival against all odds and his equal need for love and some kind of penetrating understanding of what there was of significance beyond all the racket and racketeering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On the other hand, Bellow's detractors considered his work conventional and old-fashioned, as if the author was trying to revive the 19th-century European novel. In a private letter, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a> once referred to Bellow as a "miserable mediocrity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Journalist and author <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rosenbaum" title="Ron Rosenbaum">Ron Rosenbaum</a> described Bellow's <i><a href="/wiki/Ravelstein" title="Ravelstein">Ravelstein</a></i> (2000) as the only book that rose above Bellow's failings as an author. Rosenbaum wrote,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>My problem with the pre-Ravelstein Bellow is that he all too often strains too hard to yoke together two somewhat contradictory aspects of his being and style. There's the street-wise Windy City wiseguy and then—as if to show off that the wiseguy has Wisdom—there are the undigested chunks of arcane, not entirely impressive, philosophic thought and speculation. Just to make sure you know his novels have intellectual heft. That the world and the flesh in his prose are both figured and transfigured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sam_Tanenhaus" title="Sam Tanenhaus">Sam Tanenhaus</a> wrote in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Book_Review" title="New York Times Book Review">New York Times Book Review</a></i> in 2007:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>But what, then, of the many defects—the longueurs and digressions, the lectures on anthroposophy and religion, the arcane reading lists? What of the characters who don't change or grow but simply bristle onto the page, even the colorful lowlifes pontificating like fevered students in the seminars Bellow taught at the University of Chicago? And what of the punitively caricatured ex-wives drawn from the teeming annals of the novelist's own marital discord?</p>
</blockquote>
<p>But Tanenhaus went on to answer his question:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Shortcomings, to be sure. But so what? Nature doesn't owe us perfection. Novelists don't either. Who among us would even recognize perfection if we saw it? In any event, applying critical methods, of whatever sort, seemed futile in the case of an author who, as Randall Jarrell once wrote of Walt Whitman, 'is a world, a waste with, here and there, systems blazing at random out of the darkness'—those systems 'as beautifully and astonishingly organized as the rings and satellites of Saturn.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/V._S._Pritchett" title="V. S. Pritchett">V. S. Pritchett</a> praised Bellow, finding his shorter works to be his best. Pritchett called Bellow's novella <i>Seize the Day</i> a "small gray masterpiece."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As he grew older, Bellow moved decidedly away from leftist politics and became identified with cultural conservatism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlas_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> His opponents included <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>, campus activism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Bellow also thrust himself into the often contentious realm of Jewish and African-American relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Bellow was critical of <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">multiculturalism</a> and once said to an interviewer: "Who is the <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Tolstoy</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Zulus</a>? The <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust" title="Marcel Proust">Proust</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">Papuans</a>? I'd be glad to read him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Bellow distanced himself somewhat from these remarks, which he characterized as "off the cuff obviously and pedantic certainly." He, however, stood by his criticism of multiculturalism, writing:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In any reasonably open society, the absurdity of a petty thought-police campaign provoked by the inane magnification of "discriminatory" remarks about the Papuans and the Zulus would be laughed at. To be serious in this fanatical style is a sort of Stalinism -- the Stalinist seriousness and fidelity to the party line that senior citizens like me remember all too well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite his identification with Chicago, he kept aloof from some of that city's more conventional writers. In a 2006 interview with <i>Stop Smiling</i> magazine, <a href="/wiki/Studs_Terkel" title="Studs Terkel">Studs Terkel</a> said of Bellow: "I didn't know him too well. We disagreed on a number of things politically. In the protests in the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a>'s <i>Armies of the Night</i>, when Mailer, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lowell" title="Robert Lowell">Robert Lowell</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Goodman_(writer)" title="Paul Goodman (writer)">Paul Goodman</a> were marching to protest the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, Bellow was invited to a sort of counter-gathering. He said, 'Of course I'll attend'. But he made a big thing of it. Instead of just saying OK, he was proud of it. So I wrote him a letter and he didn't like it. He wrote me a letter back. He called me a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalinist" title="Stalinist">Stalinist</a>. But otherwise, we were friendly. He was a brilliant writer, of course. I love <i>Seize the Day</i>."</p>
<p>Attempts to name a street after Bellow in his Hyde Park neighborhood were scotched by local alderman on the grounds that Bellow had made remarks about the neighborhood's current inhabitants that they considered racist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
