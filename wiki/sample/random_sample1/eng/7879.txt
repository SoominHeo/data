<p><b>Soul jazz</b> is a development of <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> incorporating strong influences from <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">gospel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> in music for small groups, often an <a href="/wiki/Organ_trio" title="Organ trio">organ trio</a> featuring a <a href="/wiki/Hammond_organ" title="Hammond organ">Hammond organ</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soul_jazz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Soul jazz is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Hard_bop" title="Hard bop">hard bop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gridley_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gridley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanner_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanner-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup> “Hard bop was an “opening out” in many directions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> Mark C. Gridley, writing for the <i><a href="/wiki/All_Music_Guide_to_Jazz" title="All Music Guide to Jazz">All Music Guide to Jazz</a></i>, explains that soul jazz more specifically refers to music with "an earthy, bluesy melodic concept" and "repetitive, dance-like rhythms.... Note that some listeners make no distinction between 'soul-jazz" and 'funky hard bop,' and many musicians don't consider 'soul-jazz' to be continuous with 'hard bop.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gridley_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gridley-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roy_Carr" title="Roy Carr">Roy Carr</a> describes soul jazz as an outgrowth of hard bop, " and "soul" appearing in a jazz context as early as the mid-1950s to describe "gospel-informed, down-home, call-and-response blues."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup> Carr also notes the acknowledged influence of <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a>' small group recordings (which included saxophonists <a href="/wiki/David_%22Fathead%22_Newman" title='David "Fathead" Newman'>David "Fathead" Newman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hank_Crawford" title="Hank Crawford">Hank Crawford</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Horace_Silver" title="Horace Silver">Horace Silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art_Blakey" title="Art Blakey">Art Blakey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannonball_Adderley" title="Cannonball Adderley">Cannonball Adderley</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Milt_Jackson" title="Milt Jackson">Milt Jackson</a>. The 1955–1965 years were a time of both consolidation and expansion yet the exact nature of those shifts in perspective among jazz musicians…too satisfied with defining the music by using such clichés as “soul,” “funk” and “returning to the roots.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>“F. Scott Fitzgerald famously dubbed the 1920’s the Jazz Age.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Soul jazz developed in the late 1950s, reaching public awareness with the release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cannonball_Adderley_Quintet_in_San_Francisco" title="The Cannonball Adderley Quintet in San Francisco">The Cannonball Adderley Quintet in San Francisco</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Cannonball Adderley noted: "We were pressured quite heavily by <a href="/wiki/Riverside_Records" title="Riverside Records">Riverside Records</a> when they discovered there was a word called 'soul'. We became, from an image point of view, soul jazz artists. They kept promoting us that way and I kept deliberately fighting it, to the extent that it became a game."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> While soul jazz was most popular during the mid-to-late 1960s, many soul jazz performers, and elements of the music, remain popular. The Jazz Crusaders, for example, evolved from soul jazz to <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul music</a>, becoming <a href="/wiki/The_Crusaders" title="The Crusaders">The Crusaders</a> in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup> Carr places <a href="/wiki/David_Sanborn" title="David Sanborn">David Sanborn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maceo_Parker" title="Maceo Parker">Maceo Parker</a> in a line of <a href="/wiki/Alto_saxophone" title="Alto saxophone">alto saxophonists</a> that includes <a href="/wiki/Earl_Bostic" title="Earl Bostic">Earl Bostic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tab_Smith" title="Tab Smith">Tab Smith</a>, with Adderley, followed by <a href="/wiki/Lou_Donaldson" title="Lou Donaldson">Lou Donaldson</a>, as the strongest links in the chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some well-known soul jazz recordings are <a href="/wiki/Lee_Morgan" title="Lee Morgan">Lee Morgan</a>'s <i>The Sidewinder</i> (1963), <a href="/wiki/Frank_Foster_(musician)" title="Frank Foster (musician)">Frank Foster</a>'s <i>Samba Blues</i> (1963), <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Hancock" title="Herbie Hancock">Herbie Hancock</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Cantaloupe_Island" title="Cantaloupe Island">Cantaloupe Island</a>" (1964) (which was popularized further when sampled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US3" title="US3">US3</a> on "<a href="/wiki/Cantaloop_(Flip_Fantasia)" title="Cantaloop (Flip Fantasia)">Cantaloop (Flip Fantasia)</a>" almost 30 years later, in 1993), Horace Silver's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_for_My_Father" title="Song for My Father">Song for My Father</a>" (1964) (quoted by <a href="/wiki/Steely_Dan" title="Steely Dan">Steely Dan</a> in their "<a href="/wiki/Rikki_Don%27t_Lose_That_Number" title="Rikki Don't Lose That Number">Rikki Don't Lose That Number</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Lewis" title="Ramsey Lewis">Ramsey Lewis</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_%27In%27_Crowd_(song)" title="The 'In' Crowd (song)">The In Crowd</a>" (a top-five hit in 1965<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup>), and Cannonball Adderley's "<a href="/wiki/Mercy,_Mercy,_Mercy" title="Mercy, Mercy, Mercy">Mercy, Mercy, Mercy</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup> (1966) (also popularized further when covered as a top 40 pop song by <a href="/wiki/The_Buckinghams" title="The Buckinghams">The Buckinghams</a>). <a href="/wiki/Les_McCann" title="Les McCann">Les McCann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Harris" title="Eddie Harris">Eddie Harris</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Swiss_Movement" title="Swiss Movement">Swiss Movement</a></i> (1969) was a <a href="/wiki/Hit_record" title="Hit record">hit record</a>, as was the accompanying <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">single</a> "Compared To What", with both selling millions of units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carr_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-carr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performers">Performers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soul_jazz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Performers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Important soul jazz organists include <a href="/wiki/Bill_Doggett" title="Bill Doggett">Bill Doggett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Earland" title="Charles Earland">Charles Earland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Holmes_(organist)" title="Richard Holmes (organist)">Richard "Groove" Holmes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Les_McCann" title="Les McCann">Les McCann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_McDuff" title="Jack McDuff">"Brother" Jack McDuff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McGriff" title="Jimmy McGriff">Jimmy McGriff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lonnie_Smith_(jazz_musician)" title="Lonnie Smith (jazz musician)">Lonnie Smith</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_John_Patton" title="Big John Patton">Big John Patton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Patterson_(organist)" title="Don Patterson (organist)">Don Patterson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Scott" title="Shirley Scott">Shirley Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hank_Marr" title="Hank Marr">Hank Marr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Wilson" title="Reuben Wilson">Reuben Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Smith_(musician)" title="Jimmy Smith (musician)">Jimmy Smith</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johnny_Hammond_Smith" title="Johnny Hammond Smith">Johnny Hammond Smith</a>.</p>
<p>Tenor <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> are also important in soul jazz; soul jazz tenors include <a href="/wiki/Gene_Ammons" title="Gene Ammons">Gene Ammons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_%22Lockjaw%22_Davis" title='Eddie "Lockjaw" Davis'>Eddie "Lockjaw" Davis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Harris" title="Eddie Harris">Eddie Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston_Person" title="Houston Person">Houston Person</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Turrentine" title="Stanley Turrentine">Stanley Turrentine</a>; guitarists include <a href="/wiki/Grant_Green" title="Grant Green">Grant Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Burrell" title="Kenny Burrell">Kenny Burrell</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Benson" title="George Benson">George Benson</a>. Other important contributors include alto saxophonists Lou Donaldson and Hank Crawford, trumpeter <a href="/wiki/Blue_Mitchell" title="Blue Mitchell">Blue Mitchell</a>, and drummer <a href="/wiki/Idris_Muhammad" title="Idris Muhammad">Idris Muhammad</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%A9" title="Né">né</a> Leo Morris).</p>
