<p><b>Solenodons</b> (meaning "slotted-tooth") are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venomous" title="Venomous">venomous</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>, burrowing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Solenodontidae</b>. Only one <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, <i>Solenodon</i>, is known, although a few other genera were erected at one time and are now regarded as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junior_synonym" title="Junior synonym">junior synonyms</a>. Solenodontidae is interesting to <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetics</a> researchers because of its retention of primitive mammal characteristics; their species resemble very closely those that lived near the end of the age of the dinosaurs. In 2016, solenodons were confirmed by genetic analysis as predating the mass extinction of the dinosaurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DChou88_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DChou88-4">[4]</a></sup> They are one of two families of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soricomorphs_of_the_Caribbean" title="Soricomorphs of the Caribbean">Caribbean soricomorphs</a>; it is uncertain whether the other family, <a href="/wiki/Nesophontes" title="Nesophontes">Nesophontidae</a>, which became extinct during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>, was closely related to solenodons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WA01_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-WA01-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two living solenodon species are the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_solenodon" title="Cuban solenodon">Cuban solenodon</a> (<i>Solenodon cubanus</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Hispaniolan_solenodon" title="Hispaniolan solenodon">Hispaniolan solenodon</a> (<i>Solenodon paradoxus</i>). The Hispaniolan solenodon covers a wide range of habitats on the island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> from lowland dry forest to highland pine forest. Two other described species became extinct during the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> genera, such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Apternodus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Apternodus (page does not exist)">Apternodus</a></i>, have been suggested as relatives of <i>Solenodon</i>, but the origins of the animal remain obscure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WA01_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-WA01-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solenodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solenodon_cubanus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="671" data-file-width="562" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Solenodon_cubanus.jpg/220px-Solenodon_cubanus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Solenodon_cubanus.jpg/330px-Solenodon_cubanus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Solenodon_cubanus.jpg/440px-Solenodon_cubanus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solenodon_cubanus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cuban solenodon at New York Zoo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Solenodons resemble very large <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>, and are often compared to them; with extremely elongated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous" title="Cartilaginous">cartilaginous</a> snouts, long, naked, scaly tails, hairless feet, and small eyes. The Cuban solenodon is generally smaller than its Hispaniolan counterpart. It is also a rusty brown with black on its throat and back. The Hispaniolan solenodon is a darker brown with yellowish tint to the face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The snout is flexible and, in the Hispaniolan solenodon, actually has a ball-and-socket joint at the base to increase its mobility. This allows the animal to investigate narrow crevices where potential prey may be hiding. Solenodons are also noted for the glands in their inguinal and groin areas that secrete what is described as a musky, goat-like odor. Solenodons range from 28 to 32 cm (11 to 13 in) from nose to rump, and weigh between 0.7 and 1.0 kg (1.5 and 2.2 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solenodons have a few unusual traits, one of them being the position of the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teat" title="Teat">teats</a> on the female, almost on the buttocks of the animal, and another being the <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venomous</a> <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> that flows from modified salivary glands in the <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a> through grooves on the second lower <a href="/wiki/Incisor" title="Incisor">incisors</a> ("solenodon" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> "grooved tooth"). Solenodons are among a handful of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venomous_mammals" title="Venomous mammals">venomous mammals</a>. Fossil records show that some other now-extinct mammal groups also had the dental venom delivery system, indicating that the solenodon's most distinct characteristic may have been a more general ancient mammalian characteristic that has been lost in most modern mammals and is only retained in a couple of very ancient lineages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morelle2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morelle2-7">[7]</a></sup> The solenodon has often been called a "living fossil" because it has been around virtually unchanged for the past 76 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrank_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-jrank-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is not known exactly how long solenodons can live in the wild. However, certain individuals of the Cuban species have been recorded to have lived for up to five years in captivity and individuals of the Hispaniolan species for up to eleven years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>West Indian natives have long known about the venomous character of the solenodon bite. Scientific studies on the nature of the tiny mammal's saliva show that it is very similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotoxic" title="Neurotoxic">neurotoxic</a> venom of certain snakes. Solenodons create venom in enlarged submaxillary glands, and only inject venom through their bottom set of teeth. The symptoms of a solenodon bite include general depression, breathing difficulty, paralysis, and convulsions; large enough doses have resulted in death in lab studies on mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their diets consist largely of insects, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, but they also eat vertebrate <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, and perhaps even some living vertebrate prey, such as small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-6">[6]</a></sup> They have also been known to feed on fruits, roots, and vegetables. Based on observation of the solenodon in captivity, they have only been known to drink while bathing. Solenodons have a relatively unspecialised, and almost complete dentition, with a <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dental formula</a> of: <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;" title="upper dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.3.3</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;" title="lower dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.3.3</span></span></span>.</p>
<p>Solenodons find food by sniffing the ground until they come upon their prey. If the prey is small enough, the solenodon will consume it immediately. After coming across the prey, the solenodon will bring the forelimbs up to either side of the prey and then move the head forward, opening the jaw and properly catching its prey.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While sniffing for food, the solenodon can get through physical barriers with the help of its sharp claws.</p>
<p>There has been research that suggests that males and females of the two species have different eating habits. The female has a habit of scattering the food to make sure that no morsel of food is missed as it is foraging. The male was noted to use its tongue to lap up the food and using the lower jaw as a scoop. However, these specimens were studied in captivity, so these habits may not be found in the wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solenodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solenodons give birth in a nesting burrow, to one or two young. The young remain with the mother for several months, and initially follow the mother about by hanging on to her elongated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teat" title="Teat">teats</a>. Once they reach adulthood, solenodons are solitary animals, which rarely interact except to breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reproductive rate of solenodons is relatively low; each year they will only produce two litters. Breeding has no specific time period and can occur at any time. Males will not aid in the care for the young. The mother will nurse her offspring using her two nipples, which are placed toward the back of the animal. However, if the litter consists of three offspring, one will become malnourished and die, due to a lack of feeding opportunity. The nursing period can last for up to seventy-five days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrank_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-jrank-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solenodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solenodons make their homes in bushy areas in forests. During the daytime they seek refuge in caves, burrows, or hollow logs. They are easily provoked and will therefore fly into rages of biting and screaming without warning. They run and climb quite fast, despite only ever touching the ground with toes. Solenodons are said to give off grunts similar to that of a pig or bird-call when feeling threatened. As far as predation goes, solenodons use a series of clicking noises, which create sound waves that bounce back off of objects in its path. This form of echolocation is the main way in which a solenodon is able to navigate as well as find its food sources, because solenodons have extremely small eyes and poor vision. This well developed hearing combined with its above average sense of smell helps the solenodon survive despite its lack of visual adequacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solenodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both extant species are <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> due to predation by the <a href="/wiki/Small_Asian_mongoose" title="Small Asian mongoose">small Asian mongoose</a> (<i>Herpestes javanicus auropunctatus</i>), which was introduced in colonial times to hunt snakes and rats, as well as by feral cats and dogs. The Cuban solenodon was thought to have been extinct until a live specimen was found in 2003. <a href="/wiki/Marcano%27s_solenodon" title="Marcano's solenodon">Marcano's solenodon</a> (<i>Solenodon marcanoi</i>) became extinct in the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>. The Hispaniolan solenodon was also once thought to be extinct, probably more because of its secretive and elusive behavior than to low population numbers. Recent studies have proven that the species is widely distributed through the island of Hispaniola, but it does not tolerate habitat degradation.</p>
<p>A 1981 study of the Hispaniolan solenodon in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> found that the species was “functionally extinct”, with the exception of a small population in the area of Massif de la Hotte. A follow-up study, in 2007, noted that the solenodon was still thriving in the area, even though the region has had an increase in human population density in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Human activity has also had an adverse effect on the Solenodon population. Human development on Cuba and Hispaniola has resulted in fragmentation and habitat loss, further contributing to the reduction of the solenodon's range and numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Sierra de Bahoruco, a mountain range in the south-west of the Dominican Republic that straddles the border with Haiti, was examined by conservation teams looking for solenodons. The work occurred during the day when the animals were asleep in burrows so that they could be viewed with an infrared camera. When researchers search for solenodons in daylight, they look for the following clues to their presence:</p>
<ul>
<li>Nearby nose-poke holes; holes that the creatures make in the ground with their long noses to probe the earth as they look for insects they can hunt and eat. After a relatively long period of time they will be covered in leaves, but a fresh hole will be covered in moist soil.</li>
<li>Nearby scratches in logs that were made with their long claws.</li>
<li>A strong musty goat-like smell seeping out of a burrow. The pungent odor indicates that the burrow is active and a solenodon may be present sleeping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morelle_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morelle-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A solenodon was captured in 2008 during a month-long expedition in the Dominican Republic, thereby allowing researchers the rare opportunity to examine it in daylight. The <a href="/wiki/Durrell_Wildlife_Conservation_Trust" title="Durrell Wildlife Conservation Trust">Durrell Wildlife Conservation Trust</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithological_Society_of_Hispaniola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ornithological Society of Hispaniola (page does not exist)">Ornithological Society of Hispaniola</a> were able to take measurements and DNA from the creature before it was released. It was the only trapping made from the entire month-long expedition. The new information gathered was significant because little information is known about its current ecology, its behavior, its population status, and its genetics, and without that knowledge it is difficult for researchers to design effective conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morelle2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morelle2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solenodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the arrival of Europeans, the solenodon was the apex predator on the island of Hispaniola. Consequently, the solenodon never evolved defense mechanisms, which is why it is so threatened by predators like dogs, cats, and mongooses. Snakes and birds of prey are also threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The solenodon has no known negative effects on human populations. In addition, it serves as both pest control, helping ecosystems by keeping down the population of invertebrates, and a means of spreading fruit seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, the solenodon is one of the last two surviving native insectivorous mammals found in the Caribbean, and one of the only two remaining endemic terrestrial mammal species of Hispaniola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the survival of the solenodon is uncertain, talk of conservation has been underway through the "Last Survivors Project", which has been collaborating with the Dominican government. In 2009, a five-year plan for conservation was funded which has been put in place to conduct field research, discover the best means by which to bring about their conservation, and organize monitoring tools to ensure their long-term survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the aims of the conservation efforts is to increase local awareness of the species, particularly in the Dominican Republic. The Ornithological Society of Hispaniola showed pictures of the solenodon to the locals in both countries, and few knew what they were due to their nocturnal nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morelle_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morelle-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
