<p><i><b>Iris ruthenica</b></i>, sometimes called 'Ever Blooming Iris' (in the UK), 'Russian Iris', 'Pilgrim Iris' and 'Hungarian Iris' (in Europe), is a species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_(plant)" title="Iris (plant)">Iris</a></i>- subgenus <a href="/wiki/Iris_subg._Limniris" title="Iris subg. Limniris">Limniris</a>. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhizomatous" title="Rhizomatous">rhizomatous</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a>, with a wide distribution, ranging from eastern Europe to Central Asia. It has grass-like leaves, thick stem and violet or bluish lavender flowers which are marked with violet veining.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Iris ruthenica</i> is very variable and hybrids can look very similar to <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_uniflora" title="Iris uniflora">Iris uniflora</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup> The other species in the <i>Iris series Ruthenicae</i>. It can be variable with its leaf length and width, and flower height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has a creeping rhizome,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup> (about 3-5mm in diameter) which is branched and has fibrous roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup> The creeping rhizome forms a clump or a grass-like tuft plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has bright green leaves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup> or greyish green leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup> That are tall and thin, and grass-like,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup> measuring between 10 – 40 cm (8–13 in) long and 2 – 6 mm wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup> The leaves can grow longer than the flower stem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The plant (stem and flowers) grows to a height of between 3–20 cm <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup> ref name=signa/&gt;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup>(12 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The thick stem is 2–3 cm wide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup> can grow to heights of between 3–20 cm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup> It has the remains of last years leaves at the base of the stem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It blooms in spring,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup> (between May, June and July in the UK)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-signa_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-signa-13">[13]</a></sup> or early to mid summer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup> with one normally, but occasionally 2 fragrant flowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-signa_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-signa-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The large flowers are between 3–5 cm in diameter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup> with a cylindric,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup> perianth tube measuring 0.5--1.5 cm long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup> The flowers come in a range of blue shades between violet <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-signa_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-signa-13">[13]</a></sup> and bluish lavender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup> Which are marked with violet veining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup> The falls (measuring 4.5–5 cm) are white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup> The standards are (measuring 4–6 cm) are almost erect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup> The bracts (measuring 3–5 cm ) are greenish with pink margins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup> violet blue stigma,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup> and milky white anthers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has a globose (globe-like) to ovoid shaped seed capsule (measuring 1.2--1.5 cm) in June–August (after the flowering period is over).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup> Once they are ripe, the seed capsules fully open and all the seeds are dispersed in one movement. Unlike other iris species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup> The seeds are pyriform (pear-shaped) and have an aril (white appendage on the edge of the seed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup> The aril disappears soon after and shrivels up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemistry">Biochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As most irises are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a>, having two sets of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosomes" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a>. Which can be used to identify hybrids and classification of groupings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClaireAustin_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClaireAustin-15">[15]</a></sup> It has a chromosome count: 2n=84 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-signa_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-signa-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iapt_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-iapt-16">[16]</a></sup> (found by Simonet in 1934).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-america_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-america-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is written as 紫苞鸢尾 in Chinese script,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and known as 'zi bao yuan wei'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is named after the region of the '<a href="/wiki/Ruthenia" title="Ruthenia">Ruthenia</a>', in <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has several common names; 'Ever Blooming Iris' (in the UK),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-20">[20]</a></sup> 'Russian Iris',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> 'Pilgrim Iris' (sometimes called a synonym of Iris ruthenica),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-america_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-america-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockgarden_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-rockgarden-22">[22]</a></sup> and 'Hungarian Iris' in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is known as <i>ungersk iris</i> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-grin-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Iris ruthenica</i> was first published by <a href="/wiki/John_Bellenden_Ker_Gawler" title="John Bellenden Ker Gawler">John Bellenden Ker Gawler</a> in Botanical Magazine in 1808.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-grin-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It was later published in 1811, as 'Iris ruthenica' with the common name 'Pigmy Iris' in <a href="/wiki/Curtis%27s_Botanical_Magazine" title="Curtis's Botanical Magazine">Curtis's Botanical Magazine</a>, vol. 34, table 1393.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-america_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-america-17">[17]</a></sup> Pigmy Iris is now used as the common name of <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_pumila" title="Iris pumila">Iris pumila</a></i>.</p>
<p>It was mentioned the journals of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_Beechey" title="Frederick William Beechey">Captain Beechy</a>'s Voyage (in 1825).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beechy_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-beechy-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is mentioned in Cherepanov's Vascular Plants of Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vascular_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-vascular-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was verified by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Research_Service" title="Agricultural Research Service">Agricultural Research Service</a> on 42 October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-grin-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Iris ruthenica</i> is an accepted name by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">RHS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Iris ruthenica</i> is <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">native</a> to a wide region, including temperate Asia and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-grin-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is found in southern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloraofUSSR_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-FloraofUSSR-10">[10]</a></sup> through Central Asia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> (including <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkestan" title="Turkestan">Turkestan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tien Shen</a> mountain range,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greentours_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-greentours-28">[28]</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-European-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-signa_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-signa-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stebbings_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-stebbings-29">[29]</a></sup> Within Europe, it is found in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-grin-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is listed with <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_bloudowii" title="Iris bloudowii">Iris bloudowii</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_humilis" title="Iris humilis">Iris humilis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_lactea" title="Iris lactea">Iris lactea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_sibirica" title="Iris sibirica">Iris sibirica</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_tenuifolia" title="Iris tenuifolia">Iris tenuifolia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_tigridia" title="Iris tigridia">Iris tigridia</a></i> as being found in the <a href="/wiki/Altai-Sayan_region" title="Altai-Sayan region">Altai-Sayan region</a> (where Russia, China, Mongolia and Kazakhstan come together).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is found on dry meadows (including grass plains and steppes), pine and birch forest edges and edges of woodland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-13"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clareaustin_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-clareaustin-8">[8]</a></sup> It can also be found in forest clearings in the forest-meadow mountain belt. Forming a thicket ground-cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Mongolia it is found under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinus_sylvestris" title="Pinus sylvestris">Pinus sylvestris</a>/<a href="/wiki/Betula_platyphylla" title="Betula platyphylla">Betula platyphylla</a> subtaiga forests, in montane meadow steppes with <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Festuca_lenensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Festuca lenensis (page does not exist)">Festuca lenensis</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Artemisia_sericea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Artemisia sericea (page does not exist)">Artemisia sericea</a></i> and in <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_sibirica" title="Pinus sibirica">Pinus sibirica</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Picea_obovata" title="Picea obovata">Picea obovata</a></i> dark taiga forests (within the upper montane belt with <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_saxatilis" title="Rubus saxatilis">Rubus saxatilis</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lathyrus_humilis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lathyrus humilis (page does not exist)">Lathyrus humilis</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elsewhere in Eurasia, it is found in the Larch forests of Altai and <a href="/wiki/Sayan_Mountains" title="Sayan Mountains">Sayan mountains</a> including Tuva).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also found in <a href="/wiki/Tuva" title="Tuva">Tuvan</a> Forests as a subcanopy woody species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the Altai Mountains, it is found with other mountain flowers including Siberian Dogs-tooth Violet (<i><a href="/wiki/Erythronium_krylovii" title="Erythronium krylovii">Erythronium krylovii</a></i>), Altai Foxtail Lily (<i><a href="/wiki/Eremurus" title="Eremurus">Eremurus</a></i>), a variety of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxifrage" title="Saxifrage">Saxifrages</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Aquilegia" title="Aquilegia">Aquilegia</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gentiana_grandiflora&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gentiana grandiflora (page does not exist)">Gentiana grandiflora</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Papaver_nudicaule" title="Papaver nudicaule">Papaver nudicaule</a></i> and the yellow <i><a href="/wiki/Iris_bloudowii" title="Iris bloudowii">Iris bloudowii</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greentours_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-greentours-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>At altitudes of between 1800 and 3600m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpine_3-14"><a href="#cite_note-alpine-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">hardy</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USDA" title="USDA">USDA</a> Zone 2<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup> or Zone 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iris ruthenica does not flower very well in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup> It is best cultivated in fertile soils that do not dry out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup> It is best suited for Rock Gardens or at the front of a flower border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardy_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-hardy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClaireAustin_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ClaireAustin-15">[15]</a></sup> Although sinks or troughs could be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup> It also grows well on dry peat banks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stebbings_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-stebbings-29">[29]</a></sup> It is tolerant of semi-shade, but prefers full sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botanic_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-botanic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike many other irises, it can only be moved with success, during the spring and summer when it is in full growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassidy_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Iris ruthenica</i> is grown in several Russian botanical gardens including, Barnaul, Ivanovo, Irkutsk, Kirov, Moscow, Rostov-on-Don, St. Petersburg, Stavropol, Tomsk, Omsk, Novosibirsk and Chita.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propagation">Propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It can be propagated by division or by seed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup> The seeds should be sown in the autumn and the rhizomes divided in early spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onego_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-onego-5">[5]</a></sup> The seeds germinate fairly quickly and new plants are easily raised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dykes_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-dykes-14">[14]</a></sup> But the young plants must not dry out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungarian_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-hungarian-11">[11]</a></sup> The old and damaged rhizomes should be removed before replanting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-differences_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-differences-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrids_and_Cultivars">Hybrids and Cultivars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iris_ruthenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hybrids and Cultivars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Iris ruthenica var. nana</i> was once thought to be a smaller variety of Iris ruthenica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-america_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-america-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> but this is now considered a synonym.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plantlist_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-plantlist-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although, <i>Iris ruthenica var. brevituba</i> which has a small perianth tube<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efloras_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-efloras-2">[2]</a></sup> and violet flowers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jardin_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-jardin-35">[35]</a></sup> it is also now considered a variant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iris ruthenica has the following known cultivars;</p>
