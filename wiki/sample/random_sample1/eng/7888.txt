<p><b>Leaf warblers</b> are small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> belonging mainly to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Phylloscopus</b></i>. The genus was introduced by the German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> in 1826.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The genus name <i>Phylloscopus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phullon</i>, "leaf", and <i>skopos</i>, "seeker" (from <i>skopeo</i>, "to watch").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-job_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leaf warblers were formerly included in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> but are now considered to belong to the <a href="/wiki/Phylloscopidae" title="Phylloscopidae">Phylloscopidae</a>, a family created in 2006. The genus is closely related to <i><a href="/wiki/Seicercus" title="Seicercus">Seicercus</a></i> and some species have been moved between the two genera in recent classification attempts. Leaf warblers are active, constantly moving, often flicking their wings as they glean the foliage for insects along the branches of trees and bushes. They forage at various levels within forests, from the top canopy to the understorey. Most of the species are markedly territorial both in their summer and winter quarters.</p>
<p>Most are greenish or brownish above and off-white or yellowish below. Compared to some other "<a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a>", their songs are very simple</p>
<p>Species breeding in temperate regions are usually strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>.</p>
