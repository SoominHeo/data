<p>The <b>red crossbill</b> (<i>Loxia curvirostra</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae, also known as the <b>common crossbill</b> in Eurasia. Crossbills have distinctive mandibles, crossed at the tips, which enable them to extract seeds from conifer cones and other fruits.</p>
<p>Adults are often brightly coloured, with red or orange males and green or yellow females, but there is wide variation in colour, beak size and shape, and call types, leading to different classifications of variants, some of which have been named as subspecies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_crossbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crossbills are characterized by the mandibles crossing at their tips, which gives the group its English name. Using their crossed mandibles for leverage, crossbills are able to efficiently separate the scales of conifer cones and extract the seeds on which they feed. Adult males tend to be red or orange in colour, and females green or yellow, but there is much variation.</p>
<p>In North America, nine distinct red crossbill variants (referred to as call types) differing in vocalizations as well as beak size and shape are recognized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Each call type evolved to specialize on different species of conifer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_irruption">Breeding and irruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_crossbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Breeding and irruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3487" data-file-width="3481" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg/220px-Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg/330px-Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg/440px-Loxia_curvirostra_poliogyna_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.220_T%C3%A9bessa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The red crossbill breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> forests of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Some populations breed in <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> forests in certain areas of all three continents, and in North America, also in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas-fir" title="Douglas-fir">Douglas-fir</a>. It nests in <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>, laying 3â5 eggs.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="259" data-file-width="426" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg/220px-Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg/330px-Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matthew_Paris_1251_sketch_of_Crossbill_eating_fruit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mediaeval sketch by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Paris" title="Matthew Paris">Matthew Paris</a> in his <i>Chronica Majora</i> (1254) of a crossbill holding a fruit in its beak, with the Latin words <i>Alaudis parum majores</i> ('a little bigger than larks').</div>
</div>

<p>This <a href="/wiki/Crossbill" title="Crossbill">crossbill</a> is mainly resident, but often <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">irrupts</a> south when its food source fails. These irruptions led in the twentieth century to the establishment of permanent breeding colonies in England, and more recently in Ireland. This species forms flocks outside the breeding season, often mixed with other crossbills.</p>
<p>The first known irruption, recorded in England by the chronicler <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Paris" title="Matthew Paris">Matthew Paris</a>, was in 1254; the next, also in England, appears to have been in 1593 (by which time the earlier irruption had apparently been entirely forgotten, since the crossbills were described as "unknown" in England).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The engraver <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a> wrote that "It sometimes is met with in great numbers in this country, but its visits are not regular",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bewick_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bewick-5">[5]</a></sup> adding that many hundreds arrived in 1821. Bewick then cites Matthew Paris as writing "In 1254, in the fruit season, certain wonderful birds, which had never before been seen in England, appeared, chiefly in the orchards. They were a little bigger than Larks, and eat the pippins of the apples <i>[pomorum grana]</i> but no other part of them... They had the parts of the beak crossed <i>[cancellatas]</i> by which they divided the apples as with a forceps or knife. The parts of the apples which they left were as if they had been infected with poison."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bewick_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bewick-5">[5]</a></sup> Bewick further records an account by Sir <a href="/wiki/Roger_Twysden" title="Roger Twysden">Roger Twysden</a> for the <i>Additions to the Additamenta of Matt. Paris</i> "that in the apple season of 1593, an immense multitude of unknown birds came into England ... swallowing nothing but the pippins, <i>[granella ipsa sive acinos]</i> and for the purpose of dividing the apple, their beaks were admirably adapted by nature, for they turn back, and strike one point upon the other, so as to show ... the transverse <a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">sickles</a>, one turned past the other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bewick_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bewick-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_crossbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1696" data-file-width="1971" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg/220px-Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg/330px-Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg/440px-Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crossbill_woodcut_by_Thomas_Bewick.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
'The Cross-Bill' <a href="/wiki/Wood_engraving" title="Wood engraving">wood engraving</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_British_Birds" title="A History of British Birds">A History of British Birds</a></i> (1847 edition)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The genus name <i>Loxia</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>loxos</i>, "crosswise", and <i>curvirostra</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for <i>curved bill".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-job_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">[6]</a></sup></i></p>
<p>This species is difficult to separate from <a href="/wiki/Parrot_crossbill" title="Parrot crossbill">parrot crossbill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_crossbill" title="Scottish crossbill">Scottish crossbill</a>, both of which breed within its Eurasian range, as <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> distinctions from those two species are negligible, though the head and bill are smaller than in either of the other species. Care is needed in identification, especially in Eurasia, where the <i>glip</i> or <i>chup</i> call is probably the best indicator. The identification problem is less severe in North America, where only red crossbill and <a href="/wiki/Two-barred_crossbill" title="Two-barred crossbill">White-winged crossbill</a> occur. However, there has been debate as to whether different call types should be considered separate species. For example, the <a href="/wiki/South_Hills_crossbill" title="South Hills crossbill">South Hills crossbill</a>, occurring in the South Hills and <a href="/wiki/Albion_Mountains" title="Albion Mountains">Albion Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> has been described as a new species (<i>Loxia sinesciuris</i>) because it shows a very low degree of hybridization with the red crossbill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sinesciuris_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sinesciuris-7">[7]</a></sup> There are also genetic differences between the call type populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Nevertheless, few <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologists</a> have chosen to give these forms species status.</p>
<p>Some large-billed, pine-feeding populations currently assigned to this species in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean</a> area may possibly be better referred to either parrot crossbill or to new species in their own right, but more research is needed. These include Balearic crossbill <i>L. curvirostra balearica</i> and North African crossbill <i>L. curvirostra poliogyna</i>, feeding primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleppo_pine" title="Aleppo pine">Aleppo pine</a> (<i>Pinus halepensis</i>); Cyprus crossbill <i>L. curvirostra guillemardi</i>, feeding primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_black_pine" title="European black pine">European black pine</a> (<i>Pinus nigra</i>); and an as-yet unidentified crossbill with a parrot crossbill-size bill feeding primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_pine" title="Bosnian pine">Bosnian pine</a> (<i>Pinus heldreichii</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>. These populations also differ on plumage, with the Balearic, North African and Cyprus races having yellower males, and the Balkan type having deep purple-pink males; this however merely reflects the differing <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanin</a> content of the cones they feed on, as these pigments are transferred to the feathers.</p>
