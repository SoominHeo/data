<p><b>Salisbury</b> (various pronunciations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Country_dialects" title="West Country dialects">locally</a> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">SAWZ</span></span></b>-bree</i></a></span>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_city" title="Cathedral city">cathedral city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, England, and the only <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_English_counties" title="List of English counties">county</a>. It is the third-largest settlement in the county, after <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chippenham" title="Chippenham">Chippenham</a>, with a population of 40,302, unusually declining from 45,000 at the 2006 census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011census_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-2011census-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city is located in the southeast of Wiltshire, near the edge of <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a>. <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral" title="Salisbury Cathedral">Its cathedral</a> was formerly <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Cathedral" title="Old Sarum Cathedral">located to the north</a> at <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum" title="Old Sarum">Old Sarum</a>; following its relocation, a settlement grew up around it, drawing residents from Old Sarum and <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Wiltshire" title="Wilton, Wiltshire">Wilton</a>. The new town received its city charter in 1227 under the name <span class="nowrap"><b>New Sarum</b></span>, which continued to be its official name until <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">2009</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_City_Council" title="Salisbury City Council">Salisbury City Council</a> was established. It sits at the confluence of five rivers: the <a href="/wiki/River_Nadder" title="River Nadder">Nadder</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Ebble" title="River Ebble">Ebble</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Wylye" title="River Wylye">Wylye</a>, and <a href="/wiki/River_Bourne,_Wiltshire" title="River Bourne, Wiltshire">Bourne</a> are tributary to the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Hampshire" title="River Avon, Hampshire">Avon</a>, which flows to the south coast and into the sea at <a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset" title="Christchurch, Dorset">Christchurch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>. <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_railway_station" title="Salisbury railway station">Salisbury railway station</a> serves the city and is a regional interchange, at the crossing point between the <a href="/wiki/West_of_England_Main_Line" title="West of England Main Line">West of England Main Line</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wessex_Main_Line" title="Wessex Main Line">Wessex Main Line</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a>, is about 8 miles (13 km) northwest of Salisbury and greatly aids the local economy. The city itself, <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum" title="Old Sarum">Old Sarum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral" title="Salisbury Cathedral">present cathedral</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Cathedral" title="Old Sarum Cathedral">ruins of the former one</a> also attract visitors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name Salisbury, which is first recorded around the year 900 as <i>Searobyrig</i>, is a partial translation of the original Celtic name <i>Sorviodunum</i>, with <i>*dūnon</i>, meaning "stronghold" (in reference to the fort that stood at Old Sarum), replaced by its Old English equivalent <i>byrig</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[note 1]</a></sup> The first part of the name is of obscure origin. The form "Sarum" is a Latinization of <i>Sar</i>, a medieval abbreviation for "Sarisberie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Salisbury appeared in the Welsh <i><a href="/wiki/Brut_y_Brenhinedd" title="Brut y Brenhinedd">Chronicle of the Britons</a></i> as <i>Caer-Caradog</i>, <i>Caer-Gradawc</i>, and <i>Caer-Wallawg.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> Cair-Caratauc, one of the 28 British cities listed in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Britons" title="History of the Britons">History of the Britons</a></i>, has also been identified with Salisbury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mommy_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mommy-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shusher_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-shusher-11">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Sarum">Old Sarum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Old Sarum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum" title="Old Sarum">Old Sarum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Cathedral" title="Old Sarum Cathedral">Old Sarum Cathedral</a></div>
<p>The hilltop at <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum" title="Old Sarum">Old Sarum</a> lies near the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Europe" title="Neolithic Europe">Neolithic</a> sites of <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avebury" title="Avebury">Avebury</a> and shows some signs of early settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> It commanded a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salient_(territory)" title="Salient (territory)">salient</a> between the <a href="/wiki/River_Bourne,_Wiltshire" title="River Bourne, Wiltshire">River Bourne</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Hampshire" title="River Avon, Hampshire">Avon</a> near a crossroads of several early trade routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilt193_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilt193-13">[12]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">Iron Age</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_hillforts" title="British hillforts">hillfort</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oppidum" title="Oppidum">oppidum</a>) was constructed around it sometime between 600 and 300 <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilt193_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-wilt193-13">[12]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Romans" title="Ancient Romans">Romans</a> may have occupied the site or left it in the hands of an allied tribe. Amid the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxon_invasions_of_Britain" title="Saxon invasions of Britain">Saxon invasions</a>, Old Sarum fell to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_kings_of_Wessex" title="List of kings of Wessex">King</a> <a href="/wiki/Cynric_of_Wessex" title="Cynric of Wessex">Cynric</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a> in 552.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leeds_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leeds-14">[13]</a></sup> Preferring settlements in bottomland like nearby <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Wiltshire" title="Wilton, Wiltshire">Wilton</a>, the Saxons largely ignored Old Sarum until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking_invasions_of_England" title="Viking invasions of England">Viking invasions</a> led <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_English_kings" title="List of English kings">King</a> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred</a></span> to restore its fortifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilt193_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-wilt193-13">[12]</a></sup> Along with <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Wiltshire" title="Wilton, Wiltshire">Wilton</a>, however, it was abandoned by its residents to be sacked and burned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Danish_kings" title="List of Danish kings">Dano</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Norwegian_kings" title="List of Norwegian kings">Norwegian king</a> <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Forkbeard" title="Sweyn Forkbeard">Sweyn Forkbeard</a> in 1003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> It subsequently became the site of <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Wiltshire" title="Wilton, Wiltshire">Wilton</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_mint" title="Royal mint">mint</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilt193_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-wilt193-13">[12]</a></sup> Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest_of_England" title="Norman Conquest of England">Norman invasion</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey_castle" title="Motte-and-bailey castle">motte-and-bailey castle</a> was constructed by 1070.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilt193_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-wilt193-13">[12]</a></sup> The castle was held directly by the Norman kings; its <a href="/wiki/Castellan" title="Castellan">castellan</a> was generally also the <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Wiltshire" title="High Sheriff of Wiltshire">sheriff of Wiltshire</a>.</p>
<p>In 1075, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_London_in_1075" title="Council of London in 1075">Council of London</a> established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_Herman" title="Bishop Herman">Herman</a> as the first <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Salisbury" title="Bishop of Salisbury">bishop of Salisbury</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bho_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-bho-16">[15]</a></sup> uniting his former sees of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Sherborne" title="Bishop of Sherborne">Sherborne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ramsbury" title="Bishop of Ramsbury">Ramsbury</a> into a single diocese which covered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_English_counties" title="List of English counties">counties</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>. (He had earlier planned to move his seat to <a href="/wiki/Malmesbury" title="Malmesbury">Malmesbury</a> but was blocked by <a href="/wiki/Malmesbury_Abbey" title="Malmesbury Abbey">its monks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earl_Godwin" title="Earl Godwin">Earl Godwin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>) Hermann and his successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Osmund" title="Saint Osmund">Saint Osmund</a> began the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Cathedral" title="Old Sarum Cathedral">first Salisbury cathedral</a> but neither lived to see its completion in 1092.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bho_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-bho-16">[15]</a></sup> Osmund was a cousin of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_England" title="Norman England">England</a>; he was responsible for the codification of the <a href="/wiki/Sarum_Rite" title="Sarum Rite">Sarum Rite</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> the compilation of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> (which was probably presented to William at Old Sarum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilt193_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-wilt193-13">[12]</a></sup>), and—after centuries of advocacy from Salisbury's bishops—was finally canonized by <a href="/wiki/List_of_popes" title="List of popes">Pope</a> <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Callixtus_III" title="Callixtus III">Callixtus III</a></span> in 1457.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Cathedral" title="Old Sarum Cathedral">cathedral</a> was <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecrated</a> on 5 April 1092 but suffered extensive damage in a storm, traditionally said to have occurred only five days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_of_Salisbury" title="Roger of Salisbury">Bishop Roger</a> was a close ally of <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I</a></span> who served as his viceroy during the king's absence to <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Normandy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogereb_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogereb-23">[22]</a></sup> and directed the royal administration and <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">exchequer</a> along with his extended family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> He refurbished and expanded Old Sarum's cathedral in the 1110s and began work on a royal palace during the 1130s, prior to his arrest by Henry's successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_of_England" title="Stephen of England">Stephen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogereb_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-rogereb-23">[22]</a></sup> After this arrest, the castle at Old Sarum was allowed to fall into disrepair, but the sheriff and castellan continued to administer the area under the king's authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-storytime_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-storytime-25">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Sarum">New Sarum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: New Sarum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Salisbury" title="Bishop of Salisbury">Bishop</a> <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Walter" title="Hubert Walter">Hubert Walter</a> was instrumental in the negotiations with <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Third_Crusade" title="Third Crusade">Third Crusade</a>, but he spent little time in his diocese prior to his elevation to <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">archbishop of Canterbury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timetimetime_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-timetimetime-26">[25]</a></sup> The brothers <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Poore" title="Herbert Poore">Herbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Poore" title="Richard Poore">Richard Poore</a> succeeded him and began planning the relocation of the cathedral into the valley almost immediately. Their plans were approved by <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_English_kings" title="List of English kings">King</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_the_Lion-hearted" title="Richard the Lion-hearted">Richard I</a></span> but repeatedly delayed: Herbert was first forced into exile in <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Normandy</a> in the 1190s by the hostility of his archbishop <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Walter" title="Hubert Walter">Walter</a> and then again to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a> in the 1210s owing to royal hostility following the <a href="/wiki/Interdiction#England" title="Interdiction">papal interdiction</a> against <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Lackland" title="John Lackland">King John</a></span>. The secular authorities were particularly incensed, according to tradition, owing to some of the clerics debauching the castellan's female relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-storytime_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-storytime-25">[24]</a></sup> In the end, the clerics were refused permission to reënter the city walls following their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rogation" title="Rogation">rogations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Procession#Roman_Catholics" title="Procession">processions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lele_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-lele-27">[26]</a></sup> This caused <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Blois" title="Peter of Blois">Peter of Blois</a> to describe the church as "a captive within the walls of the citadel like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ark_of_God" title="Ark of God">ark of God</a> in the profane house of <a href="/wiki/Baal" title="Baal">Baal</a>". He advocated</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>“Let us descend into the plain! There are rich fields and fertile valleys abounding in the fruits of the earth and watered by the living stream. There is a seat for the Virgin Patroness of our church to which the world cannot produce a parallel.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-review-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>His successor and brother <a href="/wiki/Richard_Poore" title="Richard Poore">Richard Poore</a> who finally removed the cathedral to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_town#United_Kingdom" title="New town">new town</a> on his estate at <b>Veretes Sarisberias</b> ("Old Salisburies") in 1220. The site was at "Myrifield" ("Merryfield"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leddy_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-leddy-29">[28]</a></sup> a meadow near the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/River_Nadder" title="River Nadder">River Nadder</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Hampshire" title="River Avon, Hampshire">Avon</a>. It was first known as "New Sarum"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-review-28">[27]</a></sup> or <span class="nowrap"><b>New Saresbyri</b></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lele_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-lele-27">[26]</a></sup> The town was laid out on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grid_pattern" title="Grid pattern">grid</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="600" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg/220px-Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg/330px-Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg/440px-Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Salisbury_Cathedral_West_Front.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Great West Front of <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral" title="Salisbury Cathedral">Salisbury Cathedral</a>.</div>

<p>Work on the new cathedral building—the present <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral" title="Salisbury Cathedral">Salisbury Cathedral</a>—began in 1221. The site was supposedly established by <a href="/wiki/Belomancy" title="Belomancy">shooting an arrow</a> from Old Sarum, although this is certainly a legend: the distance is over 3 km (1.9 mi). (The legend is sometimes emended to claim that the arrow struck a white deer, which continued to run and died on the spot where the cathedral now rests.) The structure was built upon wooden faggots on a gravel bed with unusually shallow foundations of 18 inches (46 cm) and the main body was completed in only 38 years. (The 123 m or 404 ft tall spire, the tallest in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, was built later.) With royal approval, many of the stones for the new cathedral were taken from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Cathedral" title="Old Sarum Cathedral">old one</a>; others came from <a href="/wiki/Chilmark_Quarries" title="Chilmark Quarries">Chilmark</a>. They were probably transported by <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">ox</a>-cart owing to the obstruction to boats on the <a href="/wiki/River_Nadder" title="River Nadder">River Nadder</a> caused by its many weirs and watermills. The cathedral is considered a masterpiece of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_English_Period" title="Early English Period">Early English architecture</a>. The spire's <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_cathedral_clock" title="Salisbury cathedral clock">large clock</a> was installed in 1386, the oldest surviving mechanical clock in the world. The Cathedral also contains the best-preserved of the four surviving copies of the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a>.</p>
<p>New Sarum was made a <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of cities in the United Kingdom">city</a> by a charter from <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_English_kings" title="List of English kings">King</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a></span> in 1227<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-easty_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-easty-30">[29]</a></sup> and, by the 14th century, was the largest settlement in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>. The city wall surrounds the Close and was built in the 14th century, again with stones removed from the former cathedral at Old Sarum. The wall now has five gates: the High Street Gate, <span class="nowrap">St Ann's</span> Gate, the Queen's Gate, and <span class="nowrap">St Nicholas's</span> Gate were the original ones while a fifth was constructed in the 19th century to allow access to <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Wordsworth%27s_School" title="Bishop Wordsworth's School">Bishop Wordsworth's School</a> in the Cathedral Close. During his time in the city, the composer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handel" title="Handel">Handel</a> stayed in a room above St Ann's gate. The original site of the city at Old Sarum, meanwhile, fell into disuse. It continued as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotten_borough" title="Rotten borough">rotten borough</a>: at the time of its abolishment during the reforms of 1832, its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> represented three households.</p>

<p>In May 1289, there was uncertainty about the future of <a href="/wiki/Margaret,_Maid_of_Norway" title="Margaret, Maid of Norway">Margaret, Maid of Norway</a>, and her father sent ambassadors to <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>. Edward met <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce" title="Robert the Bruce">Robert the Bruce</a> and others at Salisbury in October 1289, which resulted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Salisbury_(1289)" title="Treaty of Salisbury (1289)">Treaty of Salisbury</a>, under which Margaret would be sent to Scotland before 1 November 1290 and any agreement on her future marriage would be delayed until she was in Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1450, a number of riots broke out in Salisbury at roughly the same time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_Cade" title="Jack Cade">Jack Cade</a> led a famous rebellion through London. The riots occurred for related reasons, although the declining fortunes of Salisbury's cloth trade may have also been influential. The violence peaked with the murder of the bishop <a href="/wiki/William_Ayscough" title="William Ayscough">William Ayscough</a>, who been involved with the government. In 1483, a large-scale rebellion against <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard III</a> broke out, led by his own 'kingmaker', <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stafford,_2nd_Duke_of_Buckingham" title="Henry Stafford, 2nd Duke of Buckingham">Henry Stafford, 2nd Duke of Buckingham</a>. After the revolt collapsed, Buckingham was executed at Salisbury, near the Bull's Head Inn. In 1664, an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">act</a> for making the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon,_Hampshire" title="River Avon, Hampshire">River Avon</a> navigable from <a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset" title="Christchurch, Dorset">Christchurch</a> to the city of New Sarum was passed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> and the work completed, only for the project to be ruined shortly thereafter by a major flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> Soon after, during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_London" title="Great Plague of London">Great Plague of London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> held court in Salisbury's cathedral close.</p>
<p>Salisbury was the site chosen to assemble <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a>'s forces to resist the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a>. He arrived to lead his approximately <span class="nowrap">19 000</span> men on 19 November 1688. His troops were not keen to fight Mary or her husband William, and the loyalty of many of James's commanders was in doubt. The first blood was shed at <a href="/wiki/Wincanton" title="Wincanton">Wincanton</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>. In Salisbury, James heard that some of his officers had deserted, such as <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hyde,_3rd_Earl_of_Clarendon" title="Edward Hyde, 3rd Earl of Clarendon">Edward Hyde</a>, and he broke out in a <a href="/wiki/Nosebleed" title="Nosebleed">nosebleed</a> which he took as an omen that he should retreat. His commander in chief, the <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Duras,_2nd_Earl_of_Feversham" title="Louis de Duras, 2nd Earl of Feversham">Earl of Feversham</a>, advised retreat on 23 November, and the next day <a href="/wiki/John_Churchill,_1st_Duke_of_Marlborough" title="John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough">John Churchill</a> deserted to William. On 26 November, James's own daughter, <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Princess Anne</a>, did the same, and James returned to London the same day, never again to be at the head of a serious military force in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of the <a href="/wiki/1948_Summer_Olympics" title="1948 Summer Olympics">1948 Summer Olympics</a>, held in London, a relay of runners carried the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_Flame" title="Olympic Flame">Olympic Flame</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium" title="Wembley Stadium">Wembley Stadium</a>, where the Games were based, to the sailing centre at <a href="/wiki/Torbay" title="Torbay">Torbay</a> via <a href="/wiki/Slough" title="Slough">Slough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basingstoke" title="Basingstoke">Basingstoke</a>, Salisbury, and <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salisbury">Salisbury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Salisbury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1972 <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act</a> eliminated the administration of the City of New Sarum under its former charters, but its successor—<a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_County_Council" title="Wiltshire County Council">Wiltshire County</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_District" title="Salisbury District">Salisbury District</a>—continued to be accorded its former city status. The name was finally formally emended from "New Sarum" to "Salisbury" during the <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">2009 changes</a> occasioned by the 1992 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1992" title="Local Government Act 1992">Local Government Act</a>, which established the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_City_Council" title="Salisbury City Council">Salisbury City Council</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_City_Council" title="Salisbury City Council">Salisbury City Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_Council" title="Wiltshire Council">Wiltshire Council</a></div>
<p>Salisbury now falls under two authorities created in 2009, <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_City_Council" title="Salisbury City Council">Salisbury City Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_Council" title="Wiltshire Council">Wiltshire Council</a>. It was once at the heart of the now defunct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salisbury_(district)" title="Salisbury (district)">Salisbury District</a>, which oversaw most of south Wiltshire as well as the city. When Wiltshire's local government was reorganised under a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">unitary authority</a> in April 2009, Salisbury City Council was formed, although with fewer responsibilities than the former district council. There are 8 <a href="/wiki/Wards_and_electoral_divisions_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Wards and electoral divisions of the United Kingdom">electoral wards</a> in Salisbury. The city has one Member of Parliament for the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Salisbury (UK Parliament constituency)">Salisbury constituency</a>, currently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Glen_(Conservative_politician)" title="John Glen (Conservative politician)">John Glen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>) who was first elected in the 2010 election.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Salisbury is located in a valley. The geology of the area, like much of South Wiltshire and <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, is largely <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>. The rivers which flow through the city have been redirected, and along with landscaping, have been used to feed into public gardens. They are popular in the summer, particularly the Queen Elizabeth Gardens, as the water there is shallow and slow-flowing enough to enter safely. Close to Queen Elizabeth Gardens are <a href="/wiki/Water-meadow" title="Water-meadow">water-meadows</a>, where the water is controlled by <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a>. Because of the low-lying land, the rivers are prone to flooding particularly during the winter months. The <i>Town Path</i>, a walkway that links Harnham with the rest of the city, is at times impassable.</p>
<p>A cause of concern to the people of Salisbury is the lack of adequate roads. Although there is no motorway to link the ports of <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, traffic passes around the city's ring-road via the A36 to <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>.</p>

<p>There are civil <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airfield" title="Airfield">airfields</a> at <a href="/wiki/Old_Sarum_Airfield" title="Old Sarum Airfield">Old Sarum</a> (where the experimental aircraft the <a href="/wiki/Edgley_Optica" title="Edgley Optica">Edgley Optica</a> was developed and tested) and at <a href="/wiki/Thruxton,_Hampshire" title="Thruxton, Hampshire">Thruxton</a> near <a href="/wiki/Andover,_Hampshire" title="Andover, Hampshire">Andover</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Salisbury experiences an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Cfb</i>) similar to almost all of the United Kingdom. The nearest Met Office weather station to Salisbury is Boscombe Down, about 6 miles to the north of the city centre. In terms of the local climate, Salisbury is amongst the sunniest of Inland areas in the UK, averaging over 1650 hours of sunshine in a typical year. Temperature extremes since 1960 have ranged from −12.4 °C (9.7 °F) in January 1963<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> to 34.5 °C (94.1 °F) during July 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> The lowest temperature to be recorded in recent years was −10.1 °C (13.8 °F) during December 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> of Salisbury – which excludes some of the city's suburbs such as <a href="/wiki/Laverstock_and_Ford" title="Laverstock and Ford">Laverstock, Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Britford" title="Britford">Britford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harnham" title="Harnham">Harnham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Odstock" title="Odstock">Odstock</a> – had a population of 40,302 at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 census</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-census_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-census-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larger_Urban_Zone" title="Larger Urban Zone">urban zone</a>, which contains the wards immediately surrounding the city, had a population of 62,216 at the 2011 Census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> The wards included in this figure are <a href="/wiki/Laverstock_and_Ford" title="Laverstock and Ford">Laverstock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Britford" title="Britford">Britford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Downton,_Wiltshire" title="Downton, Wiltshire">Downton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alderbury" title="Alderbury">Alderbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odstock" title="Odstock">Odstock</a> and the neighbouring town of <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Wiltshire" title="Wilton, Wiltshire">Wilton</a>, among others, however does not include the towns of <a href="/wiki/Amesbury" title="Amesbury">Amesbury</a> or <a href="/wiki/Romsey" title="Romsey">Romsey</a> as these support their own local populations and are further afield.</p>
<p>At the 2011 census the population of the civil parish was 95.73% white (91.00% <a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">White British</a>), 2.48% <a href="/wiki/British_Asian" title="British Asian">Asian</a> (0.74% <a href="/wiki/British_Indian" title="British Indian">Indian</a>, 0.41% <a href="/wiki/British_Bangladeshi" title="British Bangladeshi">Bangladeshi</a>, 0.40% <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Chinese</a>), 0.45% black and 1.15% mixed race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>86.43% of the civil parish's population were born in England, 3.94% were born elsewhere in the UK and 4.94% were born elsewhere in the EU (including the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>), while 4.70% of the population were born outside the EU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>62.49% of the civil parish's population declared their religion as Christianity, while 27.09% stated "no religion" and 8.02% declined to state their religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neighbourhood_Statistics_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neighbourhood_Statistics-43">[42]</a></sup> 0.79% of the population declared their religion as <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, 0.41% as <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, 0.40% as <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> and 0.80% as some other religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neighbourhood_Statistics_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neighbourhood_Statistics-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>95.89% of the civil parish's population considered their "main language" to be English, while 1.12% considered it to be <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, 0.28% considered it to be <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> and 0.24% considered it to be <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> 99.43% of the population claimed to be able to speak English well or very well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, 22.33% of Salisbury's population were aged between 30–44, 42.76% were over 45, and only 13.3% were between 18–29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="500" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg/220px-Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg/330px-Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg/440px-Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Salisbury_Market_20040724.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 15th-century <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Poultry_Cross_(Salisbury)" title="The Poultry Cross (Salisbury)">Poultry Cross</a></i> originally marked the section of the market trading in poultry.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Salisbury holds a Charter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marketplace" title="Marketplace">market</a> on Tuesdays and Saturdays and has held markets regularly since 1227. In the 15th century the Market Place had four crosses. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Poultry_Cross_(Salisbury)" title="The Poultry Cross (Salisbury)">Poultry Cross</a></i> whose name describes its market. The cheese and milk cross indicated that market which was in the triangle between the HSBC bank and the Salisbury Library. There was a third cross near the site of the present war memorial and this marked a woollen and yarn market. A fourth cross called Barnwell or Barnards Cross was situated around the Culver Street, Barnard Street area, this marked a cattle and livestock market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Today only the Poultry Cross remains, to which <a href="/wiki/Flying_buttress" title="Flying buttress">flying buttresses</a> were added in 1852.</p>
<p>In 1226, <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">King Henry III</a> granted the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Salisbury" title="Bishop of Salisbury">Bishop of Salisbury</a> a charter to hold a <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a> lasting 8 days from the Feast of the <a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption of Mary</a> (15 August).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fairs_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-fairs-49">[48]</a></sup> Over the centuries the dates for the fair have moved around, but in its modern guise, a <a href="/wiki/Travelling_funfair" title="Travelling funfair">funfair</a> is now held in the Market Place for three days from the third Monday in October. However, there is still an ancient law stating that the fair can be held in the Cathedral Close.</p>
<p>From 1833 to its demolition in the mid-1980s, the Salisbury Gas Light &amp; Coke Company, who ran the city's gasworks, were one of the major employers in the area. The company was formed in 1832 with a share capital of £8,000, and its first chairman was <a href="/wiki/William_Pleydell-Bouverie,_3rd_Earl_of_Radnor" title="William Pleydell-Bouverie, 3rd Earl of Radnor">The 3rd Earl of Radnor</a>. The company was incorporated by a private <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> in 1864, and the Gas Orders Confirmation Act 1882 empowered the company to raise capital of up to £40,000. At its peak, the gasworks were producing not only <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a> but also <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> which was sold off as the by-product of gas-making. Ammonical liquor which came out as another by-product in the making of gas, was mixed with sulphuric acid, dried and ground to make a powder which was sold as an agricultural fertiliser. The clinker from the retort house was sold to a firm in London to be used as purifier beds in the construction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewage_works" title="Sewage works">sewage works</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shopping centres include The Old George Mall, The Maltings, Winchester Street and the Crosskeys precinct. Major employers include <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_District_Hospital" title="Salisbury District Hospital">Salisbury District Hospital</a>. Closure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friends_Life" title="Friends Life">Friends Life</a> office, the second largest employer, was announced in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="500" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg/220px-Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg/330px-Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg/440px-Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Salisbury_High_Street_20040724.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Salisbury High Street</div>

<p>Salisbury was an important centre for music in the 18th century. The <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammarian</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Harris_(grammarian)" title="James Harris (grammarian)">James Harris</a>, a friend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handel" title="Handel">Handel</a>, directed concerts at the Assembly Rooms for almost 50 years up to his death in 1780, with many of the most famous musicians and singers of the day performing there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Salisbury holds an annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Day" title="St George's Day">St George's Day</a> pageant, the origins of which are claimed to go back to the 13th century.</p>
<p>Salisbury has a strong artistic community, with galleries situated in the city centre, including one in the public library. In the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">John Constable</a> made a number of celebrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_art" title="Landscape art">landscape paintings</a> featuring the cathedral's spire and the surrounding countryside. Salisbury's annual International Arts Festival, started in 1973, and held in late May to early June, provides a programme of theatre, live music, dance, public sculpture, street performance and art exhibitions. Salisbury also houses a producing theatre – <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Playhouse" title="Salisbury Playhouse">Salisbury Playhouse</a> – which produces between eight and ten plays a year, as well as welcoming touring productions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salisbury_Museum">Salisbury Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Salisbury Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg/220px-Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg/330px-Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg/440px-Kings_House_Salisbury_Museum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Salisbury Museum, housed in the King's House</div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salisbury_Museum" title="Salisbury Museum">Salisbury Museum</a> is housed in <a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_House,_Salisbury" title="The King's House, Salisbury">The King's House</a>, a Grade I listed building whose history dates back to the 13th century, opposite the west front of the cathedral.</p>
<p>The permanent Stonehenge exhibition gallery has interactive displays about Stonehenge and the archaeology of south Wiltshire, and its collections include the skeleton of the <a href="/wiki/Amesbury_Archer" title="Amesbury Archer">Amesbury Archer</a>, which is on display.</p>
<p>The Pitt Rivers display holds a collection from General <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Pitt_Rivers" title="Augustus Pitt Rivers">Augustus Pitt Rivers</a>.</p>
<p>The costume gallery showcases costume and textiles from the area with costumes for children to try on while imagining themselves as characters from Salisbury's past.</p>
<p>Also in Salisbury, the former home of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Edward_Heath" title="Sir Edward Heath">Sir Edward Heath</a> is now open as a museum. The house, <a href="/wiki/Arundells" title="Arundells">Arundells</a>, is in the Cathedral Close.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twinning.2C_Brother.2C_Sister_Cities">Twinning, Brother, Sister Cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Twinning, Brother, Sister Cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salisbury has been <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with <a href="/wiki/Saintes,_Charente-Maritime" title="Saintes, Charente-Maritime">Saintes</a>, France, since 1990;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twinning_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-twinning-53">[52]</a></sup> and with <a href="/wiki/Xanten" title="Xanten">Xanten</a>, Germany, since 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twinning_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-twinning-53">[52]</a></sup> Salisbury is also a sister city of <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_North_Carolina" title="Salisbury, North Carolina">Salisbury, North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_Maryland" title="Salisbury, Maryland">Salisbury, Maryland</a>, both of which are in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twinning_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-twinning-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous schools in and around Salisbury. The city has the only <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar schools</a> in Wiltshire: <a href="/wiki/South_Wilts_Grammar_School_for_Girls" title="South Wilts Grammar School for Girls">South Wilts Grammar School for Girls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Wordsworth%27s_School" title="Bishop Wordsworth's School">Bishop Wordsworth's School</a>, which is for boys and is located in the Cathedral Close. Also in the Close is <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral_School" title="Salisbury Cathedral School">Salisbury Cathedral School</a>. Other schools in or near the city include the <a href="/wiki/Chafyn_Grove_School" title="Chafyn Grove School">Chafyn Grove School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leehurst_Swan_School" title="Leehurst Swan School">Leehurst Swan School</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Godolphin_School" title="Godolphin School">Godolphin</a> senior and prep school, <a href="/wiki/St_Edmund%27s_Girls%27_School" title="St Edmund's Girls' School">St Edmund's Girls' School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarum_Academy" title="Sarum Academy">Sarum Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Joseph%27s_Catholic_School,_Laverstock" title="St Joseph's Catholic School, Laverstock">St Joseph's Catholic School</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Wiltshire_UTC" title="South Wiltshire UTC">South Wiltshire UTC</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sixth_form" title="Sixth form">Sixth form</a> education is offered by <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Sixth_Form_College" title="Salisbury Sixth Form College">Salisbury Sixth Form College</a>, while the Salisbury campus of <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_College" title="Wiltshire College">Wiltshire College</a> offers a range of <a href="/wiki/Further_education" title="Further education">further education</a> courses, as well as some <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> courses in association with <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth_University" title="Bournemouth University">Bournemouth University</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sarum_College" title="Sarum College">Sarum College</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theological_college" title="Theological college">theological college</a> located within the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_close" title="Cathedral close">Cathedral close</a> in Salisbury.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main transport links for the city are the roads. Salisbury lies on the intersection of the <a href="/wiki/A30_road" title="A30 road">A30</a>, the <a href="/wiki/A36_road" title="A36 road">A36</a> and the <a href="/wiki/A338_road" title="A338 road">A338</a> and is at the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A343_road" title="A343 road">A343</a>, <a href="/wiki/A345_road" title="A345 road">A345</a>, <a href="/wiki/A354_road" title="A354 road">A354</a> and <a href="/wiki/A360_road" title="A360 road">A360</a>. Car parks around the periphery of the city are linked to the city centre by a park and ride scheme (see details in the bus section below).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are bus links to Southampton, <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a> and Andover working seven days a week with limited services on Sundays. <a href="/wiki/Wilts_%26_Dorset" title="Wilts &amp; Dorset">Wilts &amp; Dorset</a> are the main local bus company, part of the Go-Ahead group. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stagecoach_in_Hampshire" title="Stagecoach in Hampshire">Stagecoach in Hampshire</a> runs the number 87 to Andover every two hours from Salisbury and also every other journey on route 8 to Andover via <a href="/wiki/Amesbury" title="Amesbury">Amesbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidworth" title="Tidworth">Tidworth</a> along with Wilts &amp; Dorset. Bodman's also runs the number 24 bus between Salisbury and Warminster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> which replaced the X4/X5 service which used to run between Salisbury and Bath.</p>
<p>Salisbury also has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Park_and_Ride" title="Park and Ride">Park and Ride</a> bus scheme with five sites around the city. These park and ride sites attempt to relieve pressure on the city centre but run at an annual loss of £1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sjpr_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-sjpr-55">[54]</a></sup> The Park and Ride sites, which are funded by <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_Council" title="Wiltshire Council">Wiltshire Council</a> and cost £2.50 for parking and bus transport for an individual or £3.50 for up to six passengers, are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> 501 Beehive – |A345 Castle Road to the north, 502 <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Wiltshire" title="Wilton, Wiltshire">Wilton</a> – A36 Wilton Road to the west, 503 Britford – A338 <a href="/wiki/Downton,_Wiltshire" title="Downton, Wiltshire">Downton</a> Road to the south, 504 London Road – A30 London Road to the northeast, 505 Petersfinger – A36 Southampton Road, to the southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sjpr_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-sjpr-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salisbury_railway_station" title="Salisbury railway station">Salisbury railway station</a> is the crossing point of the <a href="/wiki/West_of_England_Main_Line" title="West of England Main Line">West of England Main Line</a>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Waterloo" title="London Waterloo">London Waterloo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Exeter_St_Davids_railway_station" title="Exeter St Davids railway station">Exeter St Davids</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wessex_Main_Line" title="Wessex Main Line">Wessex Main Line</a> from Bristol to Southampton. The station is operated by <a href="/wiki/South_West_Trains" title="South West Trains">South West Trains</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Great_Western" title="First Great Western">First Great Western</a> hourly trains call from <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Central_railway_station" title="Cardiff Central railway station">Cardiff Central</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bristol_Temple_Meads" title="Bristol Temple Meads">Bristol Temple Meads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bath_Spa_railway_station" title="Bath Spa railway station">Bath Spa</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southampton_Central" title="Southampton Central">Southampton Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Harbour_railway_station" title="Portsmouth Harbour railway station">Portsmouth Harbour</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_and_leisure">Sport and leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sport and leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city has a <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team, <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_F.C." title="Salisbury F.C.">Salisbury F.C.</a>, who play in the <a href="/wiki/Wessex_Football_League" title="Wessex Football League">Wessex Football League</a>. Their games are at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Raymond_McEnhill_Stadium" title="The Raymond McEnhill Stadium">The Raymond McEnhill Stadium</a> on the northern edge of the city. Also in the city are other <a href="/wiki/Non-League_football" title="Non-League football">non-league</a> clubs, <a href="/wiki/Bemerton_Heath_Harlequins_F.C." title="Bemerton Heath Harlequins F.C.">Bemerton Heath Harlequins F.C.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laverstock_%26_Ford_F.C." title="Laverstock &amp; Ford F.C.">Laverstock &amp; Ford F.C.</a>, the latter playing at The Dell.</p>
<p>Salisbury Rugby Club, which is based at Castle Road, has produced several England Internationals in recent years and continues to nurture future talent through a buoyant Mini and Youth section. It is seen as one of the prime rugby nurturing centres nationwide, regularly providing County Championship winning sides, County Players at all ages, and academy players for the professional game. Their junior section has also won National tournaments on a regular basis playing finals at Twickenham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Wilts Cricket Club, which is based at Wilton Road play in the Southern Premier Cricket League. The club has won the Southern Premier Cricket League five times in the past 25 years. They produce many juniors which represent both Hampshire and Wiltshire at county level and the junior teams have continuous success in regional and national competitions.</p>
<p>Winterbourne Cricket Club is based in <a href="/wiki/Winterbourne_Gunner" title="Winterbourne Gunner">Winterbourne Gunner</a>; and Farley Cricket Club is based at Coronation Field, <a href="/wiki/Farley,_Wiltshire" title="Farley, Wiltshire">Farley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> Salisbury Hockey Club is also based at the Salisbury and South Wilts Sports Club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Five Rivers Leisure Centre and Swimming Pool is located just outside the ring road and was opened in 2002. <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Racecourse" title="Salisbury Racecourse">Salisbury Racecourse</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Flat_racing" title="Flat racing">flat racing</a> course to the south-west of the city. Five Rivers Indoor Bowls Club and Salisbury Snooker Club share a building on Tollgate Road (behind the College). The snooker club also has sections for pool and darts. The Bishop's Walk on the edge of the city provides a popular viewing point.</p>
<p>The Old Sarum Airfield, North of Salisbury city centre, is home to a variety of aviation based businesses including traditional flying schools and the <a class="external text" href="http://disabledflying.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">APT Charitable Trust</a>.</p>
<p>The local theatre is the Salisbury Playhouse. The City Hall is an entertainment venue and hosts comedy, musical performances (including those by the resident <a href="/wiki/Musical_Theatre_Salisbury" title="Musical Theatre Salisbury">Musical Theatre Salisbury</a>) as well as seminars and conventions. Salisbury Arts Centre<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> has exhibitions and workshops.</p>
<p>Salisbury is well-supplied with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">pubs</a>. 'The Haunch of Venison', overlooking the Poultry Cross, still operates from a 14th-century building. One of its attractions is a cast of a <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummified</a> hand, supposedly severed during a game of cards. The hand vanished in 2004 as a publicity stunt for the pub but later reappeared and can still be seen there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup> The Rai d’Or has original deeds dating from 1292. It was the home of Agnes Bottenham who used the profits of the tavern to found Trinity Hospital next door around 1380.</p>
<p>Some buildings in Salisbury are reputed to be haunted. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghosts_in_European_culture" title="Ghosts in European culture">Ghost</a> tours are popular with locals and visitors. One such building is the local Odeon cinema located in the Hall of John Halle – the oldest building in the UK to contain a cinema. The <a href="/wiki/Debenhams" title="Debenhams">Debenhams</a> department store is said to be haunted by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stafford,_2nd_Duke_of_Buckingham" title="Henry Stafford, 2nd Duke of Buckingham">Henry Stafford, 2nd Duke of Buckingham</a> – the store is on the site where he was beheaded in 1483.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salisbury is served by two local <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio stations</a>. <a href="/wiki/Spire_FM" title="Spire FM">Spire FM</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Local_Radio" title="Independent Local Radio">Independent Local Radio</a> station, and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Wiltshire" title="BBC Wiltshire">BBC Wiltshire</a> is the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Local_Radio" title="BBC Local Radio">BBC Local Radio</a> public service station for the whole county. Regional television services are provided by <a href="/wiki/BBC_South" title="BBC South">BBC South</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridian_Broadcasting" title="Meridian Broadcasting">ITV Meridian</a>.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Journal" title="Salisbury Journal">Salisbury Journal</a></i> is the local paid-for <a href="/wiki/Weekly_newspaper" title="Weekly newspaper">weekly newspaper</a> which is available in shops every Thursday, with some home deliveries coming on Wednesday night. The local free weekly newspaper from the same publisher is the <i>Avon Advertiser</i>, which is delivered to houses in Salisbury and the surrounding area.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Local_television_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local television in the United Kingdom">local television</a> channel called That's Salisbury was launched on 7 May 2015 and broadcasts from studios at Scots Lane.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salisbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Salisbury is the origin of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy%27s_Wessex" title="Thomas Hardy's Wessex">"Melchester"</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a>'s novels, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Jude_the_Obscure" title="Jude the Obscure">Jude the Obscure</a></i> (1895).</li>
<li>A lively account of the Salisbury markets, as they were in 1842, is contained in Chapter 5 of <i><a href="/wiki/Martin_Chuzzlewit" title="Martin Chuzzlewit">Martin Chuzzlewit</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>.</li>
<li>The fictitious Kingsbridge Cathedral in TV miniseries, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pillars_of_the_Earth_(TV_miniseries)" title="The Pillars of the Earth (TV miniseries)">The Pillars of the Earth</a></i> (2010) based on a historical novel by the same name by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Follett" title="Ken Follett">Ken Follett</a> is modelled on the cathedrals of Wells and Salisbury. The final aerial shot of the series is of <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Cathedral" title="Salisbury Cathedral">Salisbury Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>The novel <i><a href="/wiki/Sarum_(novel)" title="Sarum (novel)">Sarum</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Rutherfurd" title="Edward Rutherfurd">Edward Rutherfurd</a> describes the history of Salisbury.</li>
<li>The novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spire" title="The Spire">The Spire</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Golding" title="William Golding">William Golding</a> tells the story of the building of the spire of an unnamed cathedral similar to Salisbury Cathedral.</li>
<li>The Starbridge series (six novels) of <a href="/wiki/Susan_Howatch" title="Susan Howatch">Susan Howatch</a> is laid in a cathedral city similar to Salisbury during the 1930s and 1960s. It tracks various strands of religious thought and action in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.</li>
<li>The detective <a href="/wiki/Lord_Peter_Wimsey" title="Lord Peter Wimsey">Lord Peter Wimsey</a> visits Salisbury in <i><a href="/wiki/Whose_Body%3F" title="Whose Body?">Whose Body?</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_L._Sayers" title="Dorothy L. Sayers">Dorothy L. Sayers</a>, the first novel in which he appears.</li>
</ul>
