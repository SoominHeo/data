<p>The <b>Salt Palace</b> was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indoor_arena" title="Indoor arena">indoor arena</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>. It was built on land that was once the "Little Tokyo" area of the city. Construction was pushed by Salt Lake's <a href="/wiki/List_of_bids_for_the_Winter_Olympics" title="List of bids for the Winter Olympics">bid</a> committee for the <a href="/wiki/1972_Winter_Olympics" title="1972 Winter Olympics">1972 Winter Olympics</a>, Gen. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maxwell_E._Rich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maxwell E. Rich (page does not exist)">Maxwell E. Rich</a>, president of the Greater Salt Lake Chamber of Commerce, Gov. <a href="/wiki/Calvin_L._Rampton" title="Calvin L. Rampton">Calvin L. Rampton</a>, and Salt Lake Tribune publisher <a href="/wiki/John_W._Gallivan" title="John W. Gallivan">John W. Gallivan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1972_bid_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-1972_bid-5">[5]</a></sup> The Salt Palace was completed in 1969 at the cost of $17 million and sat 10,725.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1972_bid_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-1972_bid-5">[5]</a></sup> The arena, later expanded to 12,666 seats,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delta_Center_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delta_Center-6">[6]</a></sup> was the home of the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Stars" title="Utah Stars">Utah Stars</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Basketball_Association" title="American Basketball Association">American Basketball Association</a> from 1970 to 1975, the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Golden_Eagles" title="Salt Lake Golden Eagles">Salt Lake Golden Eagles</a> hockey club from 1969 to 1991, and the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Utah Jazz</a> from 1979 to 1991. In 1994, three years after the Jazz moved into the Delta Center (now <a href="/wiki/Vivint_Smart_Home_Arena" title="Vivint Smart Home Arena">Vivint Smart Home Arena</a>), the Salt Palace was demolished. A <a href="/wiki/Salt_Palace" title="Salt Palace">convention center of the same name</a> stands on the site today. From 1980 until the arena's closing, the arena was officially called the Salt Palace Acord Arena in memory of Thayne and Lorraine Acord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basketball:_Utah_Stars_to_Jazz">Basketball: Utah Stars to Jazz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salt_Palace_(arena)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basketball: Utah Stars to Jazz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Stars" title="Los Angeles Stars">Los Angeles Stars</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Basketball_Association" title="American Basketball Association">American Basketball Association</a> (ABA) moved to Salt Lake City following the 1969-70 season, the Salt Palace had a major tenant. The Stars were a major success initially, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Colonels" title="Kentucky Colonels">Kentucky Colonels</a> in the ABA Finals and capturing the ABA Championship in 1970-71, behind Finals MVP <a href="/wiki/Zelmo_Beaty" title="Zelmo Beaty">Zelmo Beaty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remembertheaba.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-remembertheaba.com-8">[8]</a></sup> The Stars set an ABA attendance record in that season (6,100 per game), and would continue to draw well and field excellent teams in the following seasons. The team reached the ABA Finals again in 1973-74, before losing to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Nets" title="New York Nets">New York Nets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_Erving" title="Julius Erving">Julius Erving</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, the franchise declined with a 38-46 season in 1974-75, despite drawing 8,500 fans per game. Financial problems plagued owners of the franchise and the team folded on December 2, 1975 (4-12 record) after the franchise could not make payroll. On May 19, 1976, the ABA <a href="/wiki/Spirits_of_St._Louis" title="Spirits of St. Louis">Spirits of St. Louis</a> announced that they planned to relocate to Salt Lake City and the Salt Palace as the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Rockies" title="Utah Rockies">Utah Rockies</a> for the 1976–77 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, negotiations for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABA-NBA_merger" title="ABA-NBA merger">ABA-NBA merger</a> were completed and the Spirits/Rockies were one of two ABA teams disbanded in the merger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remembertheaba.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-remembertheaba.com-8">[8]</a></sup> The fan support that the Stars received established Salt Lake City as a viable basketball market, setting the stage for the NBA's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Jazz_(NBA_team)" title="New Orleans Jazz (NBA team)">New Orleans Jazz</a> to relocate and become the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Utah Jazz</a> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="AC.2FDC_concert_deaths">AC/DC concert deaths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salt_Palace_(arena)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: AC/DC concert deaths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 18, 1991, three teenagers were killed at an <a href="/wiki/AC/DC" title="AC/DC">AC/DC</a> concert at the Salt Palace. When AC/DC took the stage, the crowd rushed towards the stage, trampling the three. Security tried to get the band to stop playing but failed to tell the band that people were being trampled for nearly 20 minutes, although the band stopped playing as soon as they discovered what had happened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Blame was pointed at several different groups, including the fans, the band, the security personnel, and the Salt Palace's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seating" title="Seating">festival seating</a> arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The families of the victims sued AC/DC, as well as other groups associated with the concert, in connection with the deaths, and eventually settled out of court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
