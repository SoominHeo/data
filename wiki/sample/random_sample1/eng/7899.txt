<p><i><b>Dogtooth</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Κυνόδοντας</span>, <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">translit.</a> <i>Kynodontas</i>) is a 2009 Greek film directed by <a href="/wiki/Yorgos_Lanthimos" title="Yorgos Lanthimos">Yorgos Lanthimos</a> about a husband and wife who keep their children ignorant of the world outside their property well into adulthood. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Christos_Stergioglou" title="Christos Stergioglou">Christos Stergioglou</a>, Michelle Valley, <a href="/wiki/Angeliki_Papoulia" title="Angeliki Papoulia">Angeliki Papoulia</a>, Mary Tsoni, and Christos Passalis.</p>
<p>Lanthimos' second feature film<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-festival-cannes_profile_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-festival-cannes_profile-4">[4]</a></sup> won the <a href="/wiki/Un_Certain_Regard" title="Un Certain Regard">Prix Un Certain Regard</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2009_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2009 Cannes Film Festival">2009 Cannes Film Festival</a> and was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Best Foreign Language Film</a> at the <a href="/wiki/83rd_Academy_Awards" title="83rd Academy Awards">83rd Academy Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscar_83_nom_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscar_83_nom-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogtooth_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A couple and their adult son and two adult daughters live in a fenced compound. The children have no knowledge of the outside world; their parents say they will be ready to leave once they lose a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogtooth_(anatomy)" title="Dogtooth (anatomy)">dogtooth</a>, and that one can only leave safely by car. The children entertain themselves with <a href="/wiki/Endurance_game" title="Endurance game">endurance games</a>, such as holding their hands under hot water. They believe they have a brother on the other side of the fence to whom they throw supplies. The parents reward good behaviour with stickers and bad with violence.</p>
<p>The father pays an employee of his factory, Christina, to come to the house and have sex with the son. Frustrated by the son's refusal to give her <a href="/wiki/Cunnilingus" title="Cunnilingus">cunnilingus</a>, Christina trades her headband with the elder daughter in exchange for cunnilingus from her. The elder daughter later convinces the younger daughter to lick her shoulder by bartering the headband. Later, the younger daughter volunteers to lick the elder again. The elder has nothing to offer in exchange, but the younger does not mind and experiments by licking other body parts.</p>
<p>The father visits a dog training facility and demands to have his dog returned. The trainer refuses because the dog has not finished its training, and asks: "Do we want an animal or a friend?"</p>
<p>When the children are terrified by a stray cat in the garden, the son kills it with a pair of pruning shears. Deciding to take advantage of the incident, the father shreds his clothes, covers himself in fake blood, and tells his children that their unseen brother was killed by a cat, the most dangerous creature. After he teaches them to bark on all fours to fend off cats, the family holds a memorial service for the brother.</p>
<p>Christina again barters for oral sex from the elder daughter. The daughter rejects her offer of hair gel and demands the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> film tapes in her bag. She watches the films in secret and afterwards recreates scenes and quotes their dialogue. When the father discovers the tapes, he beats her with one of them, then goes to Christina's flat and hits her with her VCR, cursing her future children to be corrupted by "bad influences".</p>
<p>The parents decide that, with Christina no longer available, they will have the son choose one of his sisters as a new sexual partner. After fondling both sisters with his eyes closed, he chooses the elder. She is uncomfortable during their sex and afterwards recites threatening dialogue from the film <i><a href="/wiki/Rocky_IV" title="Rocky IV">Rocky IV</a></i> to her brother.</p>
<p>During a performance for the parents' wedding anniversary, the younger daughter stops to rest, but the elder continues and dances the choreography from the film <i><a href="/wiki/Flashdance" title="Flashdance">Flashdance</a></i>, disturbing her parents. Later, she knocks out one of her dogteeth with a dumbbell and hides in the boot of her father's car. The father discovers her tooth fragments and searches for her outside fruitlessly. He drives to work the next day; the car sits outside the factory, unattended.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogtooth_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Dogtooth</i> was the feature film début for Boo Productions, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athens,_Greece" title="Athens, Greece">Athens</a>-based advertising company. The Greek Film Center supported the project with about <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>200,000 and much of the production was done with help from volunteers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Another €50,000 was offered by the production studio, bringing the overall budget to €250,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-budget_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-budget-2">[2]</a></sup> Anna Kalaitzidou and Christos Passalis were stage actors who were cast after having worked with Lanthimos earlier. Mary Tsoni was not a professional actress, but a singer in a <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a> band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Lanthimos had an open approach to both acting and visual style, as he thought it would look fake if he involved too much in the details. It wasn't until the rehearsals started that he began to develop the idea of how the film should be shot, a style where he tried to combine a realistic environment with "really strict framing and a cool, surreal look to go with the narrative".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esm_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-esm-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogtooth_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film premiered on 18 May at the <a href="/wiki/2009_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2009 Cannes Film Festival">2009 Cannes Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PressKit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PressKit-9">[9]</a></sup> and went on to screen within such festivals as <a href="/wiki/Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="Toronto International Film Festival">Toronto International Film Festival</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Film_Festival" title="Maryland Film Festival">Maryland Film Festival</a>. It was released in Greece on 11 November the same year through Feelgood Entertainment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Verve Pictures picked up the British distribution rights and launched it on 23 April 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esm_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-esm-8">[8]</a></sup> The American premiere was on 25 June 2010, managed by <a href="/wiki/Kino_International_(company)" title="Kino International (company)">Kino International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogtooth_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In its home country, Greek critic Dimitris Danikas gave the film a rating of eight out of ten ("with enthusiasm") and characterizes it as "black, surreal, nightmarish." He believes that <i>Dogtooth</i> is as important for Greek cinema as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodoros_Angelopoulos" title="Theodoros Angelopoulos">Theodoros Angelopoulos</a>' 1970 film <i>Reconstruction</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danikas_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Danikas-13">[13]</a></sup> He goes on to say "Lanthimos composes and goes from one level to another like a wildcat-creator, constantly and continuously maintaining the same rigorous style. Hence the <a href="/wiki/Aphasia" title="Aphasia">aphasia</a>; hence the uniformity; hence the submission and the scheduled mass culture; hence also the serial killer; hence, however, the disobedience, the anarchy. As I said at the beginning: <i>Dogtooth</i> has the surrealism of <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Buñuel</a>, the scalpel of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Haneke" title="Michael Haneke">Haneke</a>, the underground horror of a thriller without the splatter. Perfect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danikas_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Danikas-13">[13]</a></sup> Danikas characterized <i>Dogtooth's</i> Academy Award nomination as "the greatest Greek triumph of recent years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danikas_Oscar_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Danikas_Oscar-14">[14]</a></sup> Columnist Dimitris Bouras, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Kathimerini" title="Kathimerini">Kathimerini</a></i>, refers to "the beneficial effects that the prestigious award could have" and believes that the nomination reveals three interesting facts: "1) in Greece we need to be extroverts (and not only in cinema), 2) exportable product is whatever has an identity, 3) <i>Dogtooth</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s nomination is like an investment – <a href="/wiki/Manna" title="Manna">manna</a> from the heaven of Hollywood for the developing Greek cinema."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kathimerini_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kathimerini-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogtooth_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of July 2014 the film had a 92% approval rating from critics at <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes-16">[16]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">The Scotsman</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Alistair Harkness hailed director Lanthimos as "a bold new voice on the world cinema scene, someone who might soon be elevated to a similar position as those twin pillars of Euro provocation: <a href="/wiki/Lars_von_Trier" title="Lars von Trier">Lars von Trier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Haneke" title="Michael Haneke">Michael Haneke</a>," although the film is "not... designed simply to shock in the way von Trier's work often does,... nor does it have that annoyingly prescriptive, punitive air of superiority favoured by Haneke's films."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> praised the filmmakers' technique, finding it "superbly shot, with some deadpan, elegant compositions, and intentionally skewiff framings".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradshaw_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradshaw-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i>) gave the film three out of four stars. He noted the director's "complete command of visuals and performances. His cinematography is like a series of family photographs of a family with something wrong with it. His dialogue sounds composed entirely of sentences memorized from tourist phrase books."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-19">[19]</a></sup> Mark Olsen of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> said "All of the film's purposeful weirdness is conveyed with an unaffected simplicity that recalls the dead-aim haphazard compositions of photographer <a href="/wiki/William_Eggleston" title="William Eggleston">William Eggleston</a>." He concluded that "as a film, it's pure and singular, but it's not quite fully formed enough to be what one could call truly visionary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olsen_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olsen_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">A. O. Scott</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote that the film "at times seems as much an exercise in perversity as an examination of it" and "The static wide-screen compositions are beautiful and strange, with the heads and limbs of the characters frequently cropped. The light is gauzy and diffuse, helping to produce an atmosphere that is insistently and not always unpleasantly dreamlike. You might think of paintings by <a href="/wiki/Balthus" title="Balthus">Balthus</a> or maybe <a href="/wiki/Alex_Katz" title="Alex Katz">Alex Katz</a>, though the implied stories in those pictures are more genuinely evocative and haunting than the actual narrative of <i>Dogtooth</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several reviewers, such as Harkness and Bradshaw, made comparisons to the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Fritzl_case" title="Fritzl case">Fritzl case</a>, although they pointed out that the screenplay had been written before the case emerged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradshaw_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradshaw-18">[18]</a></sup> Scott, like Ebert, made references to <a href="/wiki/Homeschooling" title="Homeschooling">homeschooling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-21">[21]</a></sup> Resemblances have been noted to the 1972 Mexican film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Castle_of_Purity" title="The Castle of Purity">The Castle of Purity</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MovieMarker_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MovieMarker-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVClub_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVClub-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's larger meanings eluded easy expression. Scott called the film "a conversation piece. Though the conversation may... be more along the lines of: 'What was that?' 'I don’t know. Weird.' 'Yeah.' [shudder]. 'Weird.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-21">[21]</a></sup> Olsen saw <i>Dogtooth</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s substance as "part enigma, part allegory and even part sci-fi in its creation of a completely alternate reality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olsen_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-20">[20]</a></sup> While Ebert found a "message...: God help children whose parents insanely demand unquestioning obedience to their deranged standards.... [S]ome have even described the film as a comedy. I wasn't laughing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-19">[19]</a></sup> For Bradshaw, the film investigates "the essential strangeness of something society insists is the benchmark of normality: the family, a walled city state with its own autocratic rule and untellable secrets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradshaw_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bradshaw-18">[18]</a></sup> Harkness notes the "absolute mockery the situation makes of the perfect family ideal" where "Lanthimos isn't interested in making specific political or social points and he refuses to offer any clarifying backstory"; he found <i>Dogtooth</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s oddness "as organic and playful as its impact is incisor sharp." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dogtooth_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greek Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/George_Papandreou" title="George Papandreou">George Papandreou</a> ended the Cabinet meeting on 25 January 2011 by saying "The news that the film <i>Dogtooth</i> by Yorgos Lanthimos is nominated for Best Foreign Language Film goes far beyond the world of cinema, arts and culture. It concerns the whole country, its people, the new generation of artists who follow the motto "Yes, we can do it" during difficult times."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zoumboulakis_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zoumboulakis-24">[24]</a></sup> He continued by saying "I won't say that the news shows that miracles happen, because the success of Yorgos Lanthimos is based on hard work, talent and his endless potential. Features that characterize the creative forces which lead Greece to a new era; forces which deserve our support and they will have it. Bravo Yorgos."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zoumboulakis_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zoumboulakis-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was chosen unanimously by the Greek Film Committee to represent Greece at the Oscars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enet.gr_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-enet.gr-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
