<p><b><span class="nowrap">(15760) 1992 QB<sub>1</sub></span></b> was the first <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian object</a> to be discovered after <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">Charon</a>. It was discovered in 1992 by <a href="/wiki/David_C._Jewitt" title="David C. Jewitt">David C. Jewitt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jane_X._Luu" title="Jane X. Luu">Jane X. Luu</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea_Observatory" title="Mauna Kea Observatory">Mauna Kea Observatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">classical Kuiper belt object</a> and gave rise to the name <i>cubewano</i> for this kind of object, after the "QB1" portion of its <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">designation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">Decoding its provisional designation</a>, "QB<sub>1</sub>" reveals that it was the 27th object found in the second half of August of that year.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(15760)_1992_QB1#endnote_1">[1]</a></sup> Over 1,500 further objects have been found beyond Neptune, a good number of which are classical Kuiper belt objects.</p>
<p>The discoverers suggested the name "Smiley" for <span class="nowrap">(15760) 1992 QB<sub>1</sub></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but the name was already used for an asteroid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1613_Smiley" title="1613 Smiley">1613 Smiley</a>, named after the American astronomer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hugh_Smiley" title="Charles Hugh Smiley">Charles Hugh Smiley</a>. It has received the number 15760<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buie-2">[2]</a></sup> and remains unnamed; it is normally referred to simply as "QB1", even though this is technically ambiguous without the year of discovery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(15760)_1992_QB1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><small><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_1"><b><a href="#ref_1">^</a></b></span> Asteroid provisional designations follow a format, in which the year it was discovered comes first, followed by the half-month it was discovered alphabetically (e.g. A=January 1–15, B=January 16–31 and so on,) and then the order of its discovery alphabetically (skipping the letter I) followed by a number (e.g. 1992 QA, 1992 QB, 1992 QC ... 1992 QY, 1992 QZ, 1992 QA1, 1992 QB1 and so on.) According to this, Q=August 16–31 and B1=25+2=27.</small></p>
