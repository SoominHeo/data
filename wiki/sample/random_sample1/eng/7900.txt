<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Surface-supplied diving</h1>

<p><b>Surface-supplied diving</b> refers to <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">diving</a> using equipment supplied with <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable#Diver" title="Umbilical cable">diver's umbilical</a> from the surface, either from the shore or from a <a href="/wiki/Diving_support_vessel" title="Diving support vessel">diving support vessel</a>, sometimes indirectly via a <a href="/wiki/Diving_bell" title="Diving bell">diving bell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernhardt_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gernhardt-2">[2]</a></sup> This is different from <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a>, where the diver's breathing equipment is completely self-contained and there is no link to the surface. The primary advantages of conventional surface supplied diving are lower risk of drowning and considerably larger breathing gas supply than scuba, allowing longer working periods and safer decompression.</p>
<p>The copper helmeted free-flow <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">standard diving dress</a> is the version which made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_diving" title="Commercial diving">commercial diving</a> a viable occupation, and although still used in some regions, this heavy equipment has been superseded by lighter <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet#Free-flow_helmets" title="Diving helmet">free-flow helmets</a>, and to a large extent, <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet#Lightweight_demand_helmets" title="Diving helmet">lightweight demand helmets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Full_face_diving_mask#Band_masks" title="Full face diving mask">band masks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-face_diving_masks" title="Full-face diving masks">full-face diving masks</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathing_gases" title="Breathing gases">Breathing gases</a> used include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heliox" title="Heliox">heliox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">nitrox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)" title="Trimix (breathing gas)">trimix</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Saturation_diving" title="Saturation diving">Saturation diving</a> is a mode of surface supplied diving in which the divers live under pressure in a <a href="/wiki/Saturation_diving#Architecture_of_a_surface_saturation_facility" title="Saturation diving">saturation system</a> or <a href="/wiki/Underwater_habitat" title="Underwater habitat">underwater habitat</a> and are <a href="/wiki/Decompression_(diving)" title="Decompression (diving)">decompressed</a> only at the end of a tour of duty.</p>
<p>Airline, or hookah diving, and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compressor_diving" title="Compressor diving">compressor diving</a>" are lower technology variants also using a breathing air supply from the surface.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several different arrangements exist for supplying breathing gas to divers from the surface:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">Standard</a> or Heavy gear – The historical copper helmet, canvas suit and weighted boots is still used for commercial work in some parts of the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Scuba replacement – A surface-supplied arrangement where both the primary and reserve air supplies are from high-pressure cylinders. The rest of the system is identical to the standard surface supply configuration, and the full umbilical system, communications and surface air panel are used. This is more portable than most compressors and is used by commercial diving contractors as a substitute for scuba with most of the advantages and disadvantages of a regular compressor fed surface supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMCAD022_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMCAD022-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:149</sup></li>
<li>Hookah – A basic form of surface-supplied diving in which the air supply is via a single hose is often referred to as airline or Hookah (occasionally Hooka) diving. This often uses a standard scuba second stage as the delivery unit, but is also used with light full-face masks. Bailout gas may be carried, but this is not always the case. Commercial diamond divers working in the shallow zone off the west coast of South Africa under the codes of practice of the Department of Minerals and Energy use half mask and demand valve hookah, and no bailout as standard practice. Their safety record is relatively poor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snuba" title="Snuba">Snuba</a> and SASUBA – A system used to supply air from a cylinder mounted on a float to a recreational diver tethered by a short (approximately 6m) hose through a scuba regulator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattle1995_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-seattle1995-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Compressor diving – An even more basic system is the "Compressor diving" arrangement used in the Philippines for fishing. This rudimentary and highly hazardous system uses a large number of small bore plastic tubes connected to a single compressor to supply a large number of divers simultaneously. The delivery end of the hose is unencumbered by any mechanism or mouthpiece, and is simply held by the divers' teeth. Air supply is free flow and often unfiltered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Into_the_blue_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Into_the_blue-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives">Alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scuba_set" title="Scuba set">Scuba</a>, which is commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diving" title="Recreational diving">recreational diving</a>, is the main alternative to surface-supplied diving equipment. Scuba is available in open circuit and <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> configurations.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_diving_suit" title="Atmospheric diving suit">Atmospheric diving suits</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/JIM_suit" title="JIM suit">JIM suit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Newtsuit" title="Newtsuit">Newtsuit</a> isolate the occupant from the ambient pressure, but are bulky and extremely expensive.</li>
<li>Manned and unmanned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submersibles" title="Submersibles">submersibles</a> (ROVs and AUVs) have their applications, but lack the dexterity of a diver at present (2011).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_diving" title="Free diving">Free diving</a>, or breathhold diving, is extremely limited in duration and relatively high risk.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG/220px-Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG/330px-Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG/440px-Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scaphandrier_en_milieu_pollu%C3%A9_-_casque_aquadyne_ah3.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Free flow helmets are commonly used for diving in contaminated water</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Surface-supplied diving equipment and techniques are mainly used in <a href="/wiki/Professional_diving" title="Professional diving">professional diving</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_diving" title="Military diving">military diving</a> due to the greater cost and complexity of owning and operating the equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beyerstein_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beyerstein-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This type of equipment is used in <a href="/wiki/Saturation_diving" title="Saturation diving">saturation diving</a>, as the gas supply is relatively secure, and the diver can not bail out to the surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beyerstein_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beyerstein-6">[6]</a></sup> and for diving in contaminated water, where the diver must be protected from the environment, and helmets are generally used for environmental isolation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been development of low cost airline systems for shallow recreational diving, where limited training is offset by physically limiting the depth accessible.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">Standard diving dress</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deane_helmet_1842.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="400" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Deane_helmet_1842.jpg/220px-Deane_helmet_1842.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Deane_helmet_1842.jpg/330px-Deane_helmet_1842.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Deane_helmet_1842.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deane_helmet_1842.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1842 sketch of the Deane brothers' diving helmet, the first surface-supplied diving dress equipment in the world.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first successful surface-supplied diving dress equipment was produced by the brothers <a href="/wiki/Charles_Anthony_Deane" title="Charles Anthony Deane">Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Deane_(inventor)" title="John Deane (inventor)">John Deane</a> in the 1820s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Inspired by a fire accident he witnessed in a stable in England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> he designed and patented a "Smoke Helmet" to be used by firemen in smoke-filled areas in 1823. The apparatus comprised a copper helmet with an attached flexible collar and garment. A long leather hose attached to the rear of the helmet was to be used to supply air - the original concept being that it would be pumped using a double bellows. A short pipe allowed breathed air to escape. The garment was constructed from leather or airtight cloth, secured by straps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deane_helmet_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deane_helmet-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The brothers had insufficient funds to build the equipment themselves, so they sold the patent to their employer, Edward Barnard. It was not until 1827 that the first smoke helmets were built, by German-born British engineer <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Siebe" title="Augustus Siebe">Augustus Siebe</a>. In 1828 they decided to find another application for their device and converted it into a <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">diving helmet</a>. They marketed the helmet with a loosely attached "diving suit" so that a diver could perform salvage work but only in a full vertical position, otherwise water entered the suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deane_helmet_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deane_helmet-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3666" data-file-width="2939" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG/170px-Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG/255px-Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG/340px-Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Divers_-_Illustrated_London_News_Feb_6_1873-2.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Augustus_Siebe" title="Augustus Siebe">Siebe's</a> improved design in 1873.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1829 the Deane brothers sailed from <a href="/wiki/Whitstable" title="Whitstable">Whitstable</a> for trials of their new underwater apparatus, establishing the diving industry in the town. In 1834 Charles used his diving helmet and suit in a successful attempt on the wreck of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_George_(1756)" title="HMS Royal George (1756)"><i>Royal George</i></a> at <a href="/wiki/Spithead" title="Spithead">Spithead</a>, during which he recovered 28 of the ship's cannon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1836, John Deane recovered from the discovered <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Rose" title="Mary Rose">Mary Rose</a></i> shipwreck timbers, guns, longbows, and other items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1836 the Deane brothers had produced the world's first diving manual, <i>Method of Using Deane's Patent Diving Apparatus</i> which explained in detail the workings of the apparatus and pump, plus safety precautions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1830s the Deane brothers asked Siebe to apply his skill to improve their underwater helmet design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acott_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-acott-15">[15]</a></sup> Expanding on improvements already made by another engineer, George Edwards, Siebe produced his own design; a <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">helmet</a> fitted to a full length watertight <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a> <a href="/wiki/Diving_suit" title="Diving suit">diving suit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The real success of the equipment was a <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">valve</a> in the helmet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Siebe introduced various modifications on his diving dress design to accommodate the requirements of the salvage team on the wreck of the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_George_(1756)" title="HMS Royal George (1756)">HMS <i>Royal George</i></a>, including making the helmet be detachable from the <a href="/wiki/Corselet" title="Corselet">corselet</a>; his improved design gave rise to the typical <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">standard diving dress</a> which revolutionised <a href="/wiki/Underwater" title="Underwater">underwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, underwater <a href="/wiki/Marine_salvage" title="Marine salvage">salvage</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_diving" title="Commercial diving">commercial diving</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_diving" title="Naval diving">naval diving</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acott_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-acott-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg/300px-Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg/450px-Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg/600px-Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Commercial_diving_equipment_at_Eudi_Show_2006_adventurediving.it.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Surface- supplied commercial diving equipment on display at a trade show</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The essential aspect of surface-supplied diving is that breathing gas is supplied from the surface, either from a specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diving_compressor" title="Diving compressor">diving compressor</a>, high-pressure cylinders, or both. In commercial and military surface-supplied diving, a backup source of breathing gas should always be present in case the primary supply fails. The diver may also wear a cylinder called a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bail-out_bottle" title="Bail-out bottle">bail-out bottle</a>," which can provide self-contained breathing gas in an emergency. Thus, the surface-supplied diver is much less likely to have an "out-of-air" emergency than a SCUBA diver as there are normally two alternative air sources available. Surface-supplied diving equipment usually includes communication capability with the surface, which adds to the safety and efficiency of the working diver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward2006_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ward2006-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surface-supplied equipment is required under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">US Navy</a> operational guidance for diving in harsh <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">contaminated environments</a> which was drawn up by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_Experimental_Diving_Unit" title="United States Navy Experimental Diving Unit">Navy Experimental Diving Unit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNcontam_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNcontam-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surface-supplied diving equipment is required for a large proportion of the commercial diving operations conducted in many countries, either by direct legislation, or by authorised codes of practice, as in the case of IMCA operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMCACoP_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMCACoP-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lightweight_demand_helmets">Lightweight demand helmets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lightweight demand helmets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet#Lightweight_demand_helmets" title="Diving helmet">Lightweight demand helmets</a></div>
<p>Lightweight demand helmets are rigid structures which fully enclose the head of the diver and supply breathing gas "on demand". The flow of gas from the supply line is activated by inhalation reducing the pressure in the helmet to slightly below ambient, and a diaphragm in the demand valve senses this pressure difference and moves a lever to open the valve to allow breathing gas to flow into the helmet. This flow continues until the pressure inside the helmet again balances the ambient pressure and the lever returns to the shut position. This is exactly the same principle as used for Scuba demand valves, and in some cases the same components are used. Sensitivity of the lever can often be adjusted by the diver by turning a knob on the side of the demand valve. Lightweight demand helmets are available in open circuit systems (used when breathing standard air) and closed circuit (reclaim) systems (which may be used in order to reduce costs when breathing mixed gas such as <a href="/wiki/Heliox" title="Heliox">heliox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)" title="Trimix (breathing gas)">trimix</a>: the exhaled gas is returned to the surface, scrubbed of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, re-oxygenated, and returned to the diver).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The helmet may be of metal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> or reinforced plastic composite (GRP), and is either connected to a neck dam or clamped directly to a drysuit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The neck dam is the lower part of the helmet, which seals against the neck of the diver in the same way that the neck seal of a dry suit works. Neck dams may have neoprene or latex seals, depending on diver preference. Attachment to the neck dam is critical to diver safety and a reliable locking mechanism is needed to ensure that it is not inadvertently released during a dive. When using a dry suit, the neck dam may be permanently omitted and the lower part of the helmet assembly attached directly to the suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "Lightweight" is relative; the helmets are only light in comparison with the old copper hats. They are supported only by the head and neck of the diver, and are uncomfortably heavy (Weight of KM 77 = 32.43 pounds) out of the water, as they must be ballasted for neutral buoyancy during the dive, so they don't tend to lift the diver's head with excess buoyancy. There is little difference in weight between the metal shell and GRP shell helmets because of this ballasting, and the weight is directly proportional to the total volume - smaller helmets are lighter. To avoid fatigue, divers avoid donning the helmet until just prior to entering the water. Having the helmet supported by the head has the advantage that the diver can turn the helmet to face the job without having to turn the entire upper torso. This is particularly an advantage when looking upwards. This allows the helmet to have a relatively small faceplate, which reduces overall volume and hence the weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demand breathing systems reduce the amount of gas required to adequately ventilate the diver, as it needs only to be supplied when the diver inhales, but the slightly increased work of breathing caused by this system is a disadvantage at extreme levels of exertion, where free-flow systems may be better. The demand system is also quieter than free-flow, particularly during the non-inhalation phase of breathing. This can make voice communication more effective. The breathing of the diver is also audible to the surface team over the communications system, and this helps to monitor the condition of the diver and is a valuable safety feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_circuit_demand_helmets">Open circuit demand helmets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Open circuit demand helmets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet#Open_circuit_demand_helmet" title="Diving helmet">Open circuit demand helmet</a></div>
<p>The open circuit demand system exhausts gas to the environment at ambient pressure (or a very small difference from ambient pressure required to open the exhaust valve). As a result, all exhaled gas is lost to the surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_4th_Ed_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_4th_Ed-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:Ch4</sup> For most surface orientated commercial diving where air is the breathing gas in use, this is no problem, as air is cheap and freely available. Even with nitrox it is generally more cost effective to use open circuit, as oxygen is an easily available and relatively inexpensive gas, and blending nitrox is technologically simple, both to mix and to analyse.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reclaim_helmets">Reclaim helmets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reclaim helmets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet#Reclaim_helmets" title="Diving helmet">Reclaim helmets</a></div>
<p>In the case of compressed air, or Nitrox mixtures, the exhaled gas is not valuable enough to justify the expense of recycling, but helium-based mixtures are considerably more expensive, and as the depth increases, the amount of gas used (in terms of mass, or number of molecules) increases in direct proportion to the ambient pressure. As a result, gas cost is a significant factor in deep open circuit diving with helium-based mixtures for long periods. By using a return line for the exhaled gas, it can be recompressed and used again, almost indefinitely. It is necessary to remove carbon dioxide from the reclaimed gas, but this is relatively cheap and uncomplicated. It is generally removed by a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">scrubber</a>, which is a filter packed with a chemical which reacts with and removes the carbon dioxide from the gas. The reclaimed gas is also filtered to remove odour and microorganisms, and oxygen is added to the required concentration. The gas is compressed for storage between uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahlen_1995_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahlen_1995-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gasmizer_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gasmizer-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_2016_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford_2016-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a technical problem with recovery of the exhaled gas. Simply venting it to a return hose through a non-return valve will not work, as the hose must be maintained at exactly the ambient pressure at the depth of the helmet, otherwise the gas from the helmet will either free-flow out under pressure, or not flow out at all because of back pressure. This obstacle is overcome by using an exhaust valve working on the same principle as the demand valve, which opens the exhaust valve by using the leverage of a diaphragm sensing the pressure difference between the helmet interior pressure and the ambient pressure, This only requires the pressure in the reclaim hose to be lower than ambient at the diver to function. The same principle is used in a <a href="/wiki/Diving_chamber" title="Diving chamber">diving chamber</a>'s Built-in breathing system (BIBS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gasmizer_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gasmizer-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_2016_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford_2016-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_flow_helmet">Free flow helmet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Free flow helmet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet#Free-flow_helmets" title="Diving helmet">Diving helmet § Free-flow helmets</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg/220px-AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg/330px-AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg/440px-AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AH3_free_flow_helmet_front_viewDSC01101.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Front view of an AH3 free flow diving helmet</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A free flow helmet supplies a continuous flow of air to the diver, and he breathes this as it flows past. Work of breathing is minimal, but flow rate must be high if the diver works hard, and this is noisy, affecting communications and requiring hearing protection to avoid damage to the ears. This type of helmet is popular where divers have to work hard in relatively shallow water for long periods. It is also useful when diving in contaminated environments, where the helmet is sealed onto a dry suit, and the entire system is kept at a slight positive pressure by adjusting the back-pressure of the exhaust valve, to ensure that there is no leakage into the helmet. This type of helmet is often large in volume, and as it is attached to the suit, it does not move with the head. The diver must move his body to face anything he wants to see. For this reason the faceplate is large and there is often an upper window or side windows to improve the field of vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Band_mask">Band mask</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Band mask">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A band mask is a heavy duty full-face mask with many of the characteristics of a lightweight demand helmet. In structure it is the front section of a lightweight helmet from above the faceplate to below the demand valve and exhaust ports, including the bailout block and communications connections on the sides. This rigid frame is attached to a neoprene hood by a metal clamping band, hence the name. It is provided with a padded sealing surface around the frame edge which is held firmly against the diver's face by a rubber "spider", a multiple strap arrangement with a pad behind the diver's head, and usually five straps which hook onto pins on the band. The straps have several holes so the tension can be adjusted to get a comfortable seal. A band mask is heavier than other full face masks, but lighter than a helmet, and can be donned more quickly than a helmet. They are often used by the standby diver for this reason.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full-face_mask">Full-face mask</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Full-face mask">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-face_mask" title="Full-face mask">Full-face mask</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="543" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG/220px-Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG/330px-Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG/440px-Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Full_face_diving_mask_-_ocean_reef.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A diver wearing an Ocean Reef full face mask</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A full-face mask encloses both mouth and nose, which reduces the risk of the diver losing the air supply compared to a half mask and demand valve. Some models require a bailout block to provide alternative breathing gas supply from the umbilical and bailout cylinder, but are not suitable for accepting an alternative air supply from a rescue diver, while a few models accept a secondary demand valve which can be plugged into an accessory port (Draeger, Apeks and Ocean Reef).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The unique Kirby Morgan 48 SuperMask has a removable DV pod which can be unclipped to allow the diver to breathe from a standard scuba demand valve with mouthpiece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M-48_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-M-48-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the improvement in diver safety provided by the more secure attachment of the breathing apparatus to the diver's face, some models of full face mask can fail catastrophically if the faceplate is broken or detached from the skirt, as there is then no way to breathe from the mask. This can be mitigated by carrying a standard secondary second stage, and preferably also a spare half mask.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A full face mask is lighter and more comfortable for swimming than a helmet or band mask, and usually provides an improved field of vision, but it is not as secure, and does not provide the same level of protection as the heavier and more sturdily constructed equipment. The two types of equipment have different ranges of application. Most full face masks are adaptable for use with scuba or surface supply. The full face mask does not usually have a bailout block fitted, and this is usually attached to the diver's harness, with a single hose to supply the mask from main or bailout gas which is selected at the block. The strap arrangement for full face masks is usually quite secure, but not as secure as a bandmask or helmet, and it is possible for it to be dislodged in the water. However it is also quite practicable for a trained diver to replace and clear a full face mask under water without assistance, so this is more an inconvenience than a disaster unless the diver is rendered unconscious at the same time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diver.27s_umbilical">Diver's umbilical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diver's umbilical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The umbilical contains a hose to supply the breathing gas and usually several other components. These usually include a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Strength_member&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Strength member (page does not exist)">strength member</a>, which may be the airline hose or a rope, a communications cable (comms wire), and a pneumofathometer hose. When needed, a hot water supply line, helium reclaim line, and/or video camera and lighting cables may be included. These components are neatly twisted into a multistrand cable, and are deployed as a single unit. The diver's end has underwater connectors for the electrical cables, and the air hoses are usually connected to the helmet, band mask, or bailout block by JIC fittings. A screw-down caribiner or similar connector is provided on the strength member for attachment to the diver's harness, and may be used to lift the diver in an emergency. Similar connections are provided for attachment to the diving bell, if used, or to the surface gas panel and communications equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDM-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diver.27s_harness">Diver's harness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Diver's harness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The diver's harness is an item of strong webbing, and sometimes cloth, which is fastened around a diver over the exposure suit, and allows the diver to be lifted without risk of falling out of the harness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup> Several types are in use.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jacket_harness">Jacket harness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Jacket harness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg/220px-Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg/330px-Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg/440px-Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diver_harness_with_weight_pocketsPA268054.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Front view of jacket style diver harness with removable weight pockets</div>

<p>The jacket harness is a waistcoat (vest) style garment with strong adjustable webbing straps which are adjustable and securely buckled over the shoulders, across the chest and waist, and through the crotch or around each thigh, so that the diver can not slide out under any predictable circumstance. The harness is fitted with several heavy duty D-rings, fixed to the webbing in such a way that the full weight of the diver and all his equipment can be safely supported. A minimum strength of 500kgf is recommended or required by some codes of practice. A jacket harness is usually provided with webbing straps or a cloth pocket on the back to support the bailout cylinder, and may have a variety of pockets to carry tools, and may also carry ditchable or fixed main weights. There are usually several strong D-rings to secure the umbilical and other equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bell_harness">Bell harness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Bell harness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A bell harness has the same function as a jacket harness, but lacks the cloth jacket component, and is made entirely of webbing, with a similar configuration of straps. It too may have a means of carrying a bailout cylinder, or the bailout cylinder may be carried on a separate backpack.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Harness_with_buoyancy_compensation">Harness with buoyancy compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Harness with buoyancy compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The AP Valves Mk4 Jump Jacket is a harness with integral buoyancy jacket specifically designed for commercial diving work with helmets and bells. There is a direct feed to the jacket from the main air supply, from the pneumo line and from bailout, and a system which allows the diver's pneumo to be directly connected to another diver's helmet as an emergency air supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airline">Airline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Airline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Hookah, Sasuba and <a href="/wiki/Snuba" title="Snuba">Snuba</a> systems are categorised as "airline" equipment, as they do not include the communications, lifeline and pneumofathometer hose characteristic of a full diver's umbilical.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most hookah diving uses a demand system based on a standard scuba second stage, but there have been special purpose free flow full face masks specifically intended for hookah diving (see photos).</p>
<p>Their field of application is very different from full surface-supplied diving. Hookah is generally used for shallow water work in low-hazard applications, such as archaeology, aquaculture, and aquarium maintenance work. Sasuba and Snuba are mainly a shallow water recreational application for low-hazard sites. Sasuba Hookah diving equipment is also used for yacht or boat maintenance and hull cleaning, swimming pool maintenance, shallow underwater inspections.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The systems used to supply air are compressed air systems which push air down a hose to a diving regulator breathing apparatus, are either 12 volt electrical air pumps, gasoline powered pumps, or several scuba tank floating systems with airlines to the diver. These hookah diving systems usually limit the hose length to allow less than 7 metres depth. The exception is the gasoline unit which requires a much higher level of training and topside supervision for safe use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A notable exception to this trend is the inshore diamond diving operations on the west coast of South Africa, where hookah is still the standard equipment for diamondiferous gravel extraction in the hostile conditions of the surf zone, where the water temperature is usually around 8 to 10 °C, visibility is usually low, and surge is often strong. Divers work shifts of about two hours with crowbar and suction hose, and are heavily weighted to stay in place while working, and the standard method of ascent is to ditch the weighted harness and regulator and make a free swimming ascent. The next diver will free dive down the airline, fit the regulator and shrug into the harness before continuing with the job.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Until the South African abalone fishery was closed, hookah was the only mode of diving permitted for harvesting wild abalone, and several aspects of this practice were in direct contravention of the diving regulations at the time. Abalone divers were not allowed to have a standby diver on the boat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>A gas panel or gas manifold<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup> is the control equipment for supplying the breathing gas to the divers. Primary and reserve gas is supplied to the panel through shutoff valves from a low-pressure compressor or high-pressure storage cylinders ("bombs", "quads", or "kellys"). The gas pressure may be controlled at the panel by an industrial <a href="/wiki/Pressure_regulator" title="Pressure regulator">pressure regulator</a>, or it may already be regulated closer to the source (at the compressor, or at the storage cylinder outlet). The supply gas pressure is monitored on a gauge at the panel, and an over-pressure valve is fitted in case the supply pressure is too high. The gas panel may be operated by the diving supervisor if the breathing gas is air or a fixed ratio premix, but if the composition must be controlled or monitored during the dive it is usual for a dedicated gas panel operator, or "gas man" to do this work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDM-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a set of valves and gauges for each diver to be supplied from the panel. These include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDM-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>A main supply valve with non-return valve, which supplies gas to the main gas supply hose of the umbilical. This is usually a quarter-turn valve, as it must be quick to operate and obvious whether it is open or closed.</li>
<li>A pneumofathometer supply valve, which supplies gas to the pneumofathometer for the diver. This valve is usually near the main supply valve but with a different handle. It is usually a needle type valve as it must be finely adjustable, but it must also be large enough to allow a fairly high flow rate, as the air may be used as an alternative breathing air source, or to fill small lift bags.</li>
<li>A pneumofathometer gauge is connected to the pneumo line. This is a high resolution pressure gauge calibrated in feet sea water (fsw) and/or metres sea water (msw). and is used to measure the depth of the diver by allowing air to flow through the pneumo hose and out the end attached to the diver. When the air supply is shut off, and the flow stops, the gauge indicates the pressure at the open end at the diver.</li>
<li>Each pneumofathometer gauge has an overpressure valve to protect it against gas supply at higher pressure than it is designed to take. This is essential as the main supply pressure is significantly higher than the maximum depth pressure on the pneumo gauge. There is also often a snubbing valve or orifice between the pneumo line and the gauge to restrict flow into the gauge and ensure that the overpressure valve can adequately relieve the pressure.</li>
<li>Some gas panels have a separate supply gauge for each diver downstream of the supply valve, but this is not standard practice.</li>
</ul>
<p>The gas panel may be fairly large and mounted on a board for convenience of use, or may be compact and mounted inside a portable box, for ease of transport. Gas panels are usually for one, two or three divers. In some countries, or under some codes of practice, the surface standby diver must be supplied from a separate panel to the working diver/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-pressure_breathing_air_compressor">Low-pressure breathing air compressor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Low-pressure breathing air compressor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg/220px-Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg/330px-Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg/440px-Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Low_pressure_breathing_air_compressor_P3118541.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A low-pressure compressor on site providing breathing air for surface-supplied divers</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A low-pressure compressor is often the air supply of choice for surface-supplied diving, as it is virtually unlimited in the amount of air it can supply, provided the delivery volume and pressure are adequate for the application. A low-pressure compressor can run for tens of hours, needing only refueling, periodical filter drainage and occasional running checks, and is therefore more convenient than high-pressure storage cylinders for primary air supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-CDM-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is however, critical to diver safety that the compressor is suitable for breathing air delivery, uses a suitable oil, is adequately filtered, and takes in clean and uncontaminated air. Positioning of the intake opening is important, and may have to be changed if the relative wind direction changes, to ensure that no engine exhaust gas enters the intake. Various national standards for breathing air quality may apply.</p>
<p>Power for portable compressors is usually a 4-stroke petrol (gasoline) engine. Larger, trailer mounted compressors, may be diesel powered. Permanently installed compressors on dive support boats are likely to be powered by 3-phase electric motors.</p>
<p>The compressor should be provided with an accumulator and a relief valve. The accumulator functions as an additional water trap, but the main purpose is to provide a reserve volume of pressurised air. The relief valve allows any excess air to be released back to the atmosphere while retaining the appropriate supply pressure in the accumulator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-CDM-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-pressure_reserve_gas">High-pressure reserve gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: High-pressure reserve gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An alternative to a low-pressure compressor for gas supply is high-pressure storage cylinders feeding through a pressure regulator which will be set to the required supply pressure for the depth and equipment in use. In practice HP storage may be used for either reserve gas supply or both main and reserve gas supplies to a gas panel. High-pressure bulk cylinders are quiet in operation and provide gas of known quality (if it has been tested). This allows the relatively simple and reliable use of nitrox mixtures in surface-supplied diving. Bulk cylinders are also quiet in operation compared to a low-pressure compressor, but have the obvious limitation of amount of gas available. The usual configurations for surface-supplied bulk gas storage are large single cylinders of around 50 litres water capacity, often referred to as "J"s or "bombs", "quads", which are a group (sometimes, but not necessarily four in number) of similar cylinders mounted on a frame and connected together to a common supply fitting, and "kellys" which are a group of "tubes" (long large volume pressure vessels) usually mounted in a <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_container" title="Intermodal container">container frame</a>, and usually connected together to a common connection fitting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMCAD043_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMCAD043-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communications_system">Communications system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Communications system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg/220px-Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg/330px-Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg/440px-Surface_supply_diving_communications_box_P3103773.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>Both hard-wired (cable) and through-water electronic voice communications systems may be used with surface-supplied diving. Wired systems are more popular as there is a physical connection to the diver for gas supply in any case, and adding a cable does not change the handling characteristics of the system. Wired communications systems are still more reliable and simpler to maintain than through-water systems. The communications equipment is relatively straightforward and may be of the two-wire or four-wire type. Two wire systems use the same wires for surface to diver and diver to surface messages, whereas four wire systems allow the diver's messages and the surface operator's messages to use separate wire pairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardwire_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardwire-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a two wire system the standard arrangement for diver communications is to have the diver's side normally on, so that the surface team can hear anything from the diver at all times except when the surface is sending a message. In a four-wire system the diver's side is always on, even when the surface operator is talking. This is considered an important safety feature, as the surface team can monitor the diver's breathing sounds, which can give early warning of problems developing, and confirms that the diver is alive. In a four wire system the diver to surface channel can be on all the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardwire_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hardwire-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Helium divers may need a decoder system (unscrambler) which reduces the frequency of the sound to make it more intelligible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:Ch4</sup></p>
<p>Closed circuit video is now also popular, as this allows the surface personnel to see what the diver is doing, which is particularly useful for inspection work, as a non-diving specialist can see the underwater equipment in real time and direct the diver to look at particular features of interest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Dry bells may have a through water communication system fitted as a backup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bailout_gas_supply">Bailout gas supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Bailout gas supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg/220px-Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg/330px-Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg/440px-Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bailout_block_on_a_KM18_band_mask_PA268043.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The bailout block on a KM18 band mask, showing the bailout valve (upper left), the non-return valve for main air supply (lower left), and the free-flow valve (right)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bailout gas is usually carried by the diver in a scuba cylinder, mounted on the back of the harness in the same position as is used with recreational scuba. The size of the cylinder will depend on operational variables. There should be sufficient gas to enable the diver to reach a place of safety on the bailout gas in an emergency. For surface oriented dives, this may require gas for decompression, and bailout sets generally start at about 7 litres internal capacity and can be larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inshore_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-inshore-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bell diving bailout options: For bell dives there is no requirement for decompression gas, as the bell itself carries bailout gas. However at extreme depths the diver will use gas fast, and there have been cases where twin 10 litre 300 bar sets were required to supply sufficient gas. Another option which has been used for extreme depth is a rebreather bailout set. A limitation for this service is that the diver must be able to get in and out of the bell while wearing the bailout equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Mounting options: The bailout cylinder may be mounted with the valve at the top or at the bottom, depending on local codes of practice. A generally used arrangement is to mount the cylinder with the valve up, as this is better protected while kitting up, and the cylinder valve is left fully open while the diver is in the water. This means that the regulator and supply hose to the bailout block will be pressurised during the dive, and ready for immediate use by opening the bailout valve on the harness or helmet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inshore_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-inshore-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bailout block is a small manifold fitted either to the harness where it is in a convenient but protected position, commonly on the right side on the waist strap, or on the helmet, also usually on the right side of the temple, with the valve knob to the side to distinguish it from the free-flow or defogging valve which is commonly to the front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RRR10115_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-RRR10115-38">[38]</a></sup> The bailout block has a connection for the main gas supply from the umbilical through a non-return valve. This route can not be closed and supplies the helmet demand valve and free flow valve under normal circumstances. The bailout gas from the back mounted cylinder passes through a conventional scuba first stage at the cylinder valve, to the bailout block, where it is normally isolated by the bailout valve. When the diver needs to switch over to bailout gas he simply opens the bailout valve and the gas is supplied to the helmet or mask. As the valve is normally closed, a leak in the first stage regulator seat will cause the interstage pressure to rise, and unless an overpressure relief valve is fitted to the first stage the hose may burst. Aftermarket overpressure valves are available which can be fitted into a standard low-pressure port of most first stages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Bailout supply pressure options: If the interstage pressure for the bailout regulator is lower than the main supply pressure, the main supply will override the bailout gas, and continue to flow. This can be a problem if the diver switches to bailout because main supply is contaminated. If on the other hand, bailout pressure is higher than main supply pressure, the bailout gas will override the main gas supply if the valve is opened. This will result in the bailout gas being used up if the valve leaks. The diver should periodically check that bailout pressure is still sufficient for the rest of the dive, and abort the dive if it is not. For this reason the bailout regulator must be fitted with a submersible pressure gauge to which the diver can refer to check the pressure. This is usually clipped off or tucked into the harness on the left side, where it can be easily reached to read, but is unlikely to snag on anything.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buoyancy_control">Buoyancy control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Buoyancy control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surface-supplied divers may be required to work in mid-water or on the bottom. They must be able to stay down without effort, and this usually requires weighting. When working in mid-water the diver may wish to be neutrally buoyant or negative, and when working on the bottom he will usually want to be several kilos negative. The only time the diver may want to be positively buoyant is when on the surface or during a limited range of emergencies where uncontrolled ascent is less life-threatening than remaining under water. Surface-supplied divers generally have a secure supply of breathing gas, and there are very few occasions where weighs should be jettisoned, so in most cases the surface-supplied diver weighting arrangement does not provide for quick release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On those occasions when surface supplied divers need variable buoyancy, it may be provided by inflation of the <a href="/wiki/Dry_suit" title="Dry suit">dry suit</a>, if used, or by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buoyancy_control_device" title="Buoyancy control device">buoyancy control device</a> similar in principle to those used by <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba divers</a>, or both.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight_systems">Weight systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Weight systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The diver needs to stay on the bottom to work some of the time, and may need to have neutral buoyancy some of the time. The diving suit is usually buoyant, so added weight is usually necessary. This can be provided in several ways. Unwanted positive buoyancy is dangerous to a diver who may need to spend significant time decompressing during the ascent, so the weights are usually attached securely to prevent accidental loss.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight_belts">Weight belts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Weight belts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Weight belts for surface supplied diving are usually provided with buckles which can not accidentally be released, and the weight belt is often worn under the jacket harness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight_harnesses">Weight harnesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Weight harnesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When large amounts of weight are needed, a harness may be used to carry the load on the diver's shoulders, rather than around the waist, where it may tend to slip down into an uncomfortable position if the diver is working in a vertical posture, which is often the case. Sometimes this is a separate harness, worn under the safety harness, with pockets at the sides to carry the weights, and sometimes it is an integrated system, which carries the weight in pockets built into or externally attached to the safety harness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trim_weights">Trim weights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Trim weights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>If the diver needs to adjust trim for greater comfort and efficiency while working, trim weights of various types may be added to the harness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighted_boots">Weighted boots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Weighted boots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Weighted boots of several styles may be used if the diver will be working heavy. Some are in the form of clogs which strap on over the boots, and others use lead inner soles. Ankle weights are also an option, but less comfortable. These weights give the diver better stability when working upright on the bottom, which can significantly improve productivity for some kinds of work.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_protection">Thermal protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Thermal protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wetsuit" title="Wetsuit">Wetsuits</a> are economical and used where the water temperature is not too low - more than about 65 °F (18 °C), the diver will not be spending too long in the water, and the water is reasonably clean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dry_suit" title="Dry suit">Dry suits</a> are better thermal protection than most wetsuits, and isolate the diver from the environment more effectively than other exposure suits. When diving in contaminated water, a drysuit with integral boots, sealed dry gloves and a helmet sealed directly to the suit provides the best environmental isolation. The suit material must be selected to be compatible with the expected contaminants. Thermal undersuits can be matched to the expected water temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_water_suit" title="Hot water suit">Hot water suits</a> provide active warming which is particularly suitable for use with helium based breathing gases. Heated water is provided from the surface through a hose in the umbilical, and water flow can be adjusted to suit the diver's needs. Heated water continuously flows into the suit and is distributed by perforated internal tubes down the front and back of the torso and along the limbs. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-8"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment_maintenance_and_testing">Equipment maintenance and testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Equipment maintenance and testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All components of a surface supplied diving system are required to be maintained in good working condition and may be required to be tested or calibrated at specified intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Christensen_27-9"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Christensen-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diving_procedures">Diving procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Diving procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a large number of standard procedures associated with surface-supplied diving. Some of these have their equivalents in Scuba, and others are very different. Details will vary depending on the equipment used, as manufacturers will specify some checks and procedures in detail, and the order may vary to some extent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_working_diver">The working diver</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: The working diver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Preparation of the working diver for the dive is very much a routine, but details depend on the diving equipment and the task, and to some extent on the site, particularly aspects of accessibility.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_for_diving">Preparation for diving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Preparation for diving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Before a diving operation it is usually necessary to set up the surface supply equipment. There are a number of components which must be connected in the correct order, with checks at various stages to ensure that there are no leaks and everything functions correctly. Most diving contractors will have comprehensive checklists that are used to ensure that the equipment is connected in the appropriate sequence and all checks are done. Some checks are critical to the safety of the diver. The compressor must be set up so that it gets uncontaminated air to the intake. Filters should be checked in case they need to be changed. Air supply hoses will be connected to the air panel and checked for leaks, umbilicals connected to the panels and helmets, and the communications equipment connected and tested. Before the umbilical is connected to the helmet or full face mask, the umbilical should be blown through to ensure there is no dirt inside, and the non return valve on the bailout block must be given a function test. This is important, as it is there to prevent backflow of air up the umbilical if the line is cut, and if it fails the diver may suffer a helmet squeeze, or a neck dam flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compared to scuba diving, dressing the diver in is a relatively laborious process, as the equipment is bulky and fairly heavy, and several components are connected together by hoses. This is more so with helmets, and less so with light full-face masks. It is not usual for the diver to do all the dressing in without the assistance of a diver's tender, who will also manage the umbilical during the dive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Exposure suit – The diver will wear an exposure suit appropriate for the planned dive time, breathing gas and water temperature, and also influenced by the level of exertion expected during the dive.</li>
<li>Harness – After putting on the exposure suit and checking any seals and zips, the diver will put on the harness. The topside crew will usually help as the bailout cylinder will be already mounted, and usually also attached to the helmet, making this a cumbersome procedure, easiest if the diver is seated.</li>
<li>Weights – The weights will be put onto the diver at some time during the dressing procedure, but the stage where this is done depends on what weighting system is used.</li>
<li>Bailout – The bailout cylinder is usually strapped to the harness and connected to the helmet before the diver is dressed in.</li>
<li>Helmet – The helmet is usually put on last, as it is heavy and uncomfortable out of the water. Some divers can put on their own helmet, but it is usual for the topside crew to do most of the locking on to the neck dam, and check that there are no obvious faults with the seal.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are a series of pre-dive checks which are done after the diver is locked into the helmet, and before he is committed to the water. These should be done every time a diver is prepared for a dive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Comms check – The diver and comms operator check that the voice communications system is working both ways and they can hear each other clearly. This also ensures that the operator is sure which comms channel connects to the specific diver.</li>
<li>Breathing checks – The diver breathes on main air supply to ensure that the demand valve is delivering gas at low work of breathing, without free flow, and that the umbilical is connected to the correct valve on the panel.</li>
<li>Bailout checks – The diver operates the bailout system to ensure that he can reach and operate the valve and it turns smoothly, the pressure in the cylinder is adequate for the planned dive profile and is ready for immediate use, and reports bailout readiness to the supervisor by "On at the tap, off at the hat, Pressure...bar" or equivalent.</li>
</ul>
<p>Surface checks are done after the diver enters the water, but before he is allowed to descend. They are checks which can not be done as effectively, or at all, in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-7"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Wet comms check – Once in the water, the comms should be checked again to make sure it is still working adequately. It is possible that water will cause the comms to fail or deteriorate when the contacts get wet.</li>
<li>Helmet seal – The helmet seals and neckdam should not allow water to enter the helmet. This can only be checked when in the water.</li>
<li>Pneumo bubbles – The diver calls for the air panel operator to open the pneumofathometer valve to check that the line is not blocked, and that it is connected to the correct place on the panel.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_procedures">Emergency procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Emergency procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The diver must be able to deal with the following emergencies. Some are life-threatening, whereas others are more inconveniences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_9_20-8"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_9-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_10_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_10-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C2DTS_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-C2DTS-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Bailout to back gas, in the case of a failure of gas supply from the umbilical, or if the main air supply is contaminated.</li>
<li>Pneumo breathing, if the main air supply is cut, but the pneumo hose is intact. Pneumo gas can also be supplied by the standby diver</li>
<li>Voice communications failure is not usually an emergency, but can adversely affect work effectiveness and expose the diver to higher risk if anything else goes wrong. Ability to communicate with line signals can help here, particularly to assist in the decision whether the dive should be aborted, and if there are other more urgent problems.</li>
<li>Helmet flood. Depending on the severity of the flood, this can range from an annoyance to an emergency. A slow leak can be controlled by opening the free flow valve, which will drive a moderate flow of water out of the exhaust valve. A neck dam failure usually has this effect.</li>
<li>Broken faceplate. This is a real emergency, but very unlikely as the faceplate is usually a highly impact-resistant polymer and should not shatter. It can be mitigated by opening the free flow valve and holding the opening level, facing down, and breathing very carefully. a small hole or crack can be covered with a hand to slow the leak.</li>
<li>Demand valve failure. This is a minor problem if there is a free flow valve, but the dive will normally be terminated, as the bailout will not last long if needed.</li>
<li>Exhaust valve failure, like demand valve failure, can be dealt with by opening the free flow valve and ensuring a constant outflow of air.</li>
<li>Vomiting in the helmet. This can be a real emergency and life-threatening if not handled effectively, as the diver can aspirate the vomit and asphyxiate. Once again, the action is to open the free flow valve, preferably before vomiting, and to inhale as carefully as possible. If there is no free flow valve, as on a full face mask, the purge button should clear the demand valve and oro-nasal mask, and the mask can be rinsed by lifting the bottom edge away from the face to let in some water, before purging again.</li>
<li>Hot water supply failure. This can be life-threatening for deep heliox diving, and there is not much the diver can do but head back to the bell immediately.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wet_bell_and_stage_emergency_procedures">Wet bell and stage emergency procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Wet bell and stage emergency procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Emergency procedures for wet bell and diving stages include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C2DTS_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-C2DTS-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDM_11_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDM_11-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMCACoP_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMCACoP-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>loss of main gas supply to the bell</li>
<li>Recovery of a distressed diver to the bell</li>
<li>Abandonment of the bell or stage</li>
<li>Deployment of a surface standby diver</li>
<li>Loss of heated water supply (for hot water suits)</li>
<li>Voice communications failures</li>
<li>Dynamic position runoff: Yellow and Red alerts.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stand-by_diver">Stand-by diver</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Stand-by diver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stand by diver will be prepared in the same way as the working diver, but will not enter the water until needed. He will usually be prepared to the stage of readiness to enter the water, and then will remove his mask, or have his helmet removed and will then sit in as comfortable a place as can be found, so that in case of an emergency he can be readied for action in as short a time as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuRec_dive_manual_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuRec_dive_manual-42">[42]</a></sup> This often means setting up some form of shelter from the weather, and heat and sunshine are usually more of a problem than cold and wet. It is frequently necessary to cool the standby diver to avoid overheating, and dehydration can also be a problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CADC_2015_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-CADC_2015-43">[43]</a></sup> When the working diver is using a helmet, the standby diver may use a full face mask or bandmask, as this makes it quicker to get into the water in an emergency. The stand by diver's job is to wait until something goes wrong, and then be sent in to sort it out. For this reason a stand by diver should be one of the best divers on the team regarding diving skills and strength, but does not have to be expert at the work skills for the specific job. When deployed, the standby diver will normally follow the umbilical of the diver who is in trouble, as unless it has been severed, it will reliably lead to the correct diver. The standby diver must maintain communications with the supervisor throughout the dive and is expected to give a running commentary of progress so that the supervisor and surface crew know as much as possible what is happening and can plan accordingly, and must take the necessary steps to resolve incidents, which may involve supply of emergency air or locating and rescuing an injured or unconscious diver. In bell diving, the bellman is the standby diver, and may have to recover a distressed diver to the bell and give first aid if necessary and possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMCACoP_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-IMCACoP-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>A rescue tether is a short length of rope or webbing with a clip at one or both ends, which the stand-by diver uses to clip the unresponsive diver to his harness to free up both hands during a recovery. This can be useful if he needs to climb a structure, shotline or topographical feature, and the umbilicals can not be safely used to lift the divers due to snags or sharp edges.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_health_and_safety_issues">Occupational health and safety issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Occupational health and safety issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_diving_hazards_and_precautions" title="List of diving hazards and precautions">List of diving hazards and precautions</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_signs_and_symptoms_of_diving_disorders" title="List of signs and symptoms of diving disorders">List of signs and symptoms of diving disorders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_factors_in_diving_safety" title="Human factors in diving safety">Human factors in diving safety</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">Divers</a> face specific physical and <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> risks when they go <a href="/wiki/Underwater" title="Underwater">underwater</a> with <a href="/wiki/Diving_equipment" title="Diving equipment">diving equipment</a>, or use high pressure <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hazard" title="Hazard">hazard</a> is any agent or situation that poses a level of threat to life, health, property, or environment. Most hazards remain dormant or potential, with only a theoretical risk of harm, and when a hazard becomes active, and produces undesirable consequences, it is called an incident and may culminate in an emergency or accident. Hazard and vulnerability interact with likelihood of occurrence to create risk, which can be the probability of a specific undesirable consequence of a specific hazard, or the combined probability of undesirable consequences of all the hazards of a specific activity. A hazard that is understood and acknowledged may present a lower risk if appropriate precautions are taken, and the consequences may be less severe if mitigation procedures are planned and in place.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The presence of a combination of several hazards simultaneously is common in diving, and the effect is generally increased risk to the diver, particularly where the occurrence of an incident due to one hazard triggers other hazards with a resulting cascade of incidents. Many diving fatalities are the result of a cascade of incidents overwhelming the diver, who should be able to manage any single reasonably foreseeable incident. The use of surface supplied breathing gas reduces one of the most significant hazards in diving, that of loss of breathing gas supply, and mitigates that risk by the use of a suitable emergency gas supply, usually in the form of a scuba bailout set, which is intended to provide the diver with sufficient breathing gas to reach a place of relative safety with more breathing gas available.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The risk of the diver getting lost or being unable to call for assistance is also drastically reduced in comparison with most scuba, as the diver is physically connected to the surface control point by the umbilical, making it relatively simple for the standby diver to get to a diver in distress, and the standard application of hard-wired voice communications allows the surface team to constantly monitor the diver's breathing sounds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The assessed risk of a dive would generally be considered unacceptable if the diver is not expected to cope with any single reasonably foreseeable incident with a significant probability of occurrence during that dive. Precisely where the line is drawn depends on circumstances. Professional diving operations tend to be less tolerant of risk than recreational, particularly technical divers, who are less constrained by occupational health and safety legislation. This is one of the factors driving the use of surface supplied equipment where reasonably practicable for professional work.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diving_disorders" title="Diving disorders">Diving disorders</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_conditions" title="Medical conditions">medical conditions</a> specifically arising from <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">underwater diving</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Medical_sign" title="Medical sign">signs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symptoms" title="Symptoms">symptoms</a> of these may present during a dive, on surfacing, or up to several hours after a dive. Surface supplied divers have to breathe a gas which is at the same pressure as their surroundings (<a href="/wiki/Ambient_pressure" title="Ambient pressure">ambient pressure</a>), which can be much greater than on the surface. The ambient pressure underwater increases by 1 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmosphere</a> (100 kPa) for every 10 metres (33 ft) of depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BE9_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BE9-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The principal disorders are: <a href="/wiki/Decompression_illness" title="Decompression illness">decompression illness</a> (which covers <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_gas_embolism" title="Arterial gas embolism">arterial gas embolism</a>); <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis" title="Nitrogen narcosis">nitrogen narcosis</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_nervous_syndrome" title="High pressure nervous syndrome">high pressure nervous syndrome</a>; <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity" title="Oxygen toxicity">oxygen toxicity</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_barotrauma" title="Pulmonary barotrauma">pulmonary barotrauma</a> (burst lung). Although some of these may occur in other settings, they are of particular concern during diving activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BE9_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-BE9-45">[45]</a></sup> Long term diving disorders include <a href="/wiki/Dysbaric_osteonecrosis" title="Dysbaric osteonecrosis">dysbaric osteonecrosis</a>, which is associated with decompression sickness. These disorders are caused by <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> at the high pressures encountered at depth, and divers may breathe a gas mixture different from air to mitigate these effects. <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">Nitrox</a>, which contains more <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and less <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, is commonly used as a breathing gas to reduce the risk of decompression sickness at depths to about 40 metres (130 ft). <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> may be added to reduce the amount of nitrogen and oxygen in the gas mixture when diving deeper, to reduce the effects of narcosis and to avoid the risk of oxygen toxicity. This is complicated at depths beyond about 150 metres (500 ft), because a helium–oxygen mixture (<a href="/wiki/Heliox" title="Heliox">heliox</a>) then causes high pressure nervous syndrome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BE9_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-BE9-45">[45]</a></sup> More exotic mixtures such as <a href="/wiki/Hydreliox" title="Hydreliox">hydreliox</a>, a hydrogen–helium–oxygen mixture, are used at extreme depths to counteract this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abraini1994_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abraini1994-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressor_diving">Compressor diving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface-supplied_diving&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Compressor diving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Compressor diving</b> is a method of surface-supplied diving used in some tropical sea areas including the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. The divers swim with a <a href="/wiki/Diving_mask" title="Diving mask">half mask</a> covering the eyes-and-nose and (often home-made) fins and are supplied air from the boat by plastic hoses from an industrial low-pressure <a href="/wiki/Air_compressor" title="Air compressor">air compressor</a> of the type commonly used to supply <a href="/wiki/Jackhammer" title="Jackhammer">jackhammers</a>. There is no reduction valve; the diver holds the hose end in his mouth with no demand valve or <a href="/wiki/Mouthpiece_(scuba)" title="Mouthpiece (scuba)">mouthpiece</a>. Excess air spills out through the lips. If several people are compressor diving from the same boat, several line tenders are needed in the boat to stop the airlines from getting tangled and kinked and so blocked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Into_the_blue_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Into_the_blue-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compressor diving is the most common method used to fish for Caribbean spiny lobster (<i><a href="/wiki/Panulirus_argus" title="Panulirus argus">Panulirus argus</a></i>) in the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> However, it is illegal because it contributes to overfishing, is environmentally destructive, and is harmful to the health of the fishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> When fishing with compressors, fishers either use gaffs or harpoons to spear lobsters immediately upon sight, killing or injuring the lobsters before they can be checked for eggs or assessed as legally sized. Compressors allow fishers to fish in deeper waters for longer periods of time, facilitating reef damage as fishers search for lobsters hidden underneath corals and other living refuges. The misuse of compressors has also resulted in health problems for many fishers, such as respiratory problems, limb paralysis, and death due to <a href="/wiki/Decompression_illness" title="Decompression illness">decompression illness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>This method of diving is commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> waters for <i>pa-aling</i> fishing, which is fishing with big nets on coral reef areas where a surface-dragged net would snag on coral; the compressor air hoses are also used to make a curtain of bubbles to corral and herd the fish into the nets, since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muro-ami" title="Muro-ami">muro-ami</a> fishing was stopped in the area. At least one <i>pa-aling</i> fishing fleet has been found and arrested in a protected fishery area. Compressor diving was shown, and so called, used for <i>pa-aling</i> fishing, in episode 1 (Oceans: Into the Blue) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_television" title="BBC television">BBC television</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Human_Planet" title="Human Planet">Human Planet</a></i>. The cameramen used ordinary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba_gear" title="Scuba gear">scuba gear</a>, but one of them had a trial-dive with the crew's compressor-diving gear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Into_the_blue_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Into_the_blue-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
