<p>The <i><b>History of Song</b></i> or <i><b>Song Shi</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">宋史</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Sòng Shǐ</span></i>) is one of the official Chinese historical works known as the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">Twenty-Four Histories</a> of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> that records the history of the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279). It was commissioned in 1343 and compiled under the direction of First Minister <a href="/wiki/Toqto%27a_(Yuan_dynasty)" title="Toqto'a (Yuan dynasty)">Toqto'a</a> and Prime Minister <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alutu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alutu (page does not exist)">Alutu</a> (阿鲁图/阿魯圖) during the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> (1279–1368) at the same time as the <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Liao" title="History of Liao">History of Liao</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Jin" title="History of Jin">History of Jin</a></i>. Running to a total of 496 chapters, the <i>History of Song</i> includes biographies of the Song Emperors along with contemporary records and biographical sketches of Song dynasty politicians, soldiers and philosophers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_process">Publication process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Publication process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the early years of the Yuan dynasty, its progenitor <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a> ordered the compilation of a History of the Song dynasty but due to style issues, this was not completed during his reign as emperor. In March 1343, the third year of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukhaantu_Khan,_Emperor_Huizong_of_Yuan" title="Ukhaantu Khan, Emperor Huizong of Yuan">Ukhaantu Khan, Emperor Huizong of Yuan</a>'s Zhizheng <a href="/wiki/Chinese_era_name" title="Chinese era name">Era</a> (至正年号), an Imperial edict ordered the creation of histories of the Song, Liao and Jin Dynasties. Under the overall supervision of Toktogan, Temür Daš (Chinese <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tiemuertashi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tiemuertashi (page does not exist)">Tiemuertashi</a> 铁木儿塔识/鐵木兒塔識), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=He_Weiyi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="He Weiyi (page does not exist)">He Weiyi</a>, (贺惟一/賀惟一), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qiyan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhang Qiyan (page does not exist)">Zhang Qiyan</a> (张起岩/張起巖), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ouyang_Xuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ouyang Xuan (page does not exist)">Ouyang Xuan</a> (欧阳玄/ 歐陽玄) <a href="/wiki/Li_Haowen" title="Li Haowen">Li Haowen</a> (李好文), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wang_Yi_(Song_dynasty)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wang Yi (Song dynasty) (page does not exist)">Wang Yi</a> (王沂) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yang_Zongduan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yang Zongduan (page does not exist)">Yang Zongduan</a> (楊宗端) were given responsibility for the project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChinaKnowledge_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChinaKnowledge-1">[1]</a></sup> with Woyuluntu (斡玉伦徒/斡玉倫徒), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tai_Buhua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tai Buhua (page does not exist)">Tai Buhua</a> ( 泰不华/泰不華), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yu_Wenzhuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yu Wenzhuan (page does not exist)">Yu Wenzhuan</a> (于文传/于文傳), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gong_Shidao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gong Shidao (page does not exist)">Gong Shidao</a> (贡师道/貢師道), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yu_Que&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yu Que (page does not exist)">Yu Que</a> (余阙/餘闕), <a href="/wiki/Jia_Lu" title="Jia Lu">Jia Lu</a> (贾鲁/賈魯) <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wei_Su_(1303-1372)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wei Su (1303-1372) (page does not exist)">Wei Su</a> (危素) and 23 others appointed as <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiographers</a>. Toktogan resigned in May 1344 to be replaced on the project by Prime Minister Alutu, even though the later was not familiar with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a>. The final book took only two and a half years to produce and was published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhejiang_Province" title="Zhejiang Province">Zhejiang Province</a> in 1346, the sixth year of the Zhizheng Era.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>History of Song</i> with its 496 chapters is the largest of the 24 Dynastic Histories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChinaKnowledge_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChinaKnowledge-1">[1]</a></sup> It contains 47 chapters of Imperial biographies, 162 chapters covering Song dynasty records (志/誌 <i>Zhì</i>), 32 chapters of tables (showing genealogy etc.) and 255 chapters of historical biographies. A work of enormous breadth, the History of Song contains more than 2,000 individual historical biographies, more than twice as many as the <a href="/wiki/Old_Book_of_Tang" title="Old Book of Tang">Old Book of Tang</a> that chronicles the history of the Early Tang dynasty. In the section of the book covering Song dynasty records there are fifteen separate categories <i>viz</i>: astronomy, the system of five phases known as <a href="/wiki/Wu_Xing" title="Wu Xing">Wu Xing</a>, the legal calendar (律历/律曆)， geography, rivers and water ways、<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confucian" title="Confucian">Confucian</a> rites, music, ceremonial weaponry and bodyguards (仪卫/儀衛), military dress (舆服/輿服), elections, government positions, consumer goods, the army and punishment together with art and culture. Altogether these chapters make up one third of the work. The historical biographies section of the <i>History of Song</i> is unsurpassed by any of the other Dynastic Histories; there are detailed descriptions of Song government structures from the central down to the local level. Sections covering consumer goods and the army are also well written with much more detail than found in the other Dynastic Histories. The fourteen chapters on consumer goods contain seven times the amount of information as the corresponding chapters of the Book of Tang. A total of seven chapters contain biographies of traitors and rebels including <a href="/wiki/Cai_Jing" title="Cai Jing">Cai Jing</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Huang_Qianshan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Huang Qianshan (page does not exist)">Huang Qianshan</a> (黃潛善), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Hui_(Song_dynasty)" title="Qin Hui (Song dynasty)">Qin Hui</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Bangchang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhang Bangchang (page does not exist)">Zhang Bangchang</a> (張邦昌) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Liu_Yu_(1073-1146)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Liu Yu (1073-1146) (page does not exist)">Liu Yu</a> (劉豫) whilst the four chapters on Confucian scholars feature individuals such as <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Dunyi" title="Zhou Dunyi">Zhou Dunyi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheng_Hao" title="Cheng Hao">Cheng Hao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheng_Yi_(philosopher)" title="Cheng Yi (philosopher)">Cheng Yi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Zai" title="Zhang Zai">Zhang Zai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Xi" title="Zhu Xi">Zhu Xi</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ideology behind the <i>History of Song</i> is that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-confucianism" title="Neo-confucianism">Neo-confucianism</a>, with coverage of the Confucian doctrines of loyalty, righteousness and ethics regarding the well-known scholars Zhou Dunyi, Cheng Hao, Cheng Yi, Zhang Zai and Zhu Xi amongst others. No less than 278 individuals feature in the section on loyalty and righteousness (忠義傳 <i>Zhōngyì Zhuàn</i>). <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Qian_Daxin" title="Qian Daxin">Qian Daxin</a> (錢大昕) once said: "The <i>History of Song</i> esteems neo-confucianism, especially the school of Zhu Xi". Its compilation follows the principles of Confucian life. Fine logic and language is used to convey <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a> whilst eschewing <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a>. The book's style is also highly regarded and considered a model example. <a href="/wiki/Wang_Anshi" title="Wang Anshi">Wang Anshi</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Xining_Reforms&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Xining Reforms (page does not exist)">Xining Reforms</a> (熙寧變法) are rejected by the <i>History of Song</i> whilst political reform campaigners including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lu_Huiqing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lu Huiqing (page does not exist)">Lu Huiqing</a> (呂惠卿), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zeng_Bu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zeng Bu (page does not exist)">Zeng Bu</a> (曾布) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Dun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhang Dun (page does not exist)">Zhang Dun</a> (章惇) feature in the section on traitors and rebels, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shi_Miyuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shi Miyuan (page does not exist)">Shi Miyuan</a> (史彌遠) however, despite his involvement in the suicide of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Ningzong_of_Song" title="Emperor Ningzong of Song">Emperor Ningzong of Song</a>'s eldest heir, does not feature in this section or indeed the entire <i>History of Song</i>. Famous general <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wang_Jianzai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wang Jianzai (page does not exist)">Wang Jianzai</a> (王堅在), regardless of his valiant combat record, is also omitted as are many other individuals involved in Mongol defeats by the Song.</p>
<p>Despite both the <i>History of Song</i> and the <i>History of Jin</i> being completed at the same time they are different in many ways. the <i>History of Song</i> records <a href="/wiki/Yue_Fei" title="Yue Fei">Yue Fei</a> emerging victorious from every battle with the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(1115%E2%80%931234)" title="Jin dynasty (1115–1234)">Jin dynasty</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> yet the <i>History of Jin</i> barely mentions <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taizu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Taizu of Jin">Emperor Taizu of Jin</a>'s capture of <a href="/wiki/Bozhou" title="Bozhou">Bozhou</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shunchangfu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shunchangfu (page does not exist)">Shunchangfu</a> (顺昌府), <a href="/wiki/Ruzhou" title="Ruzhou">Ruzhou</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Songzhou_(historical_prefecture_seated_in_Song_County)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Songzhou (historical prefecture seated in Song County) (page does not exist)">Songzhou</a> (嵩州) when Yue Fei and other commanders withdrew from the battle. Information in the <i>History of Song</i> regarding Yue Fei all comes from a work by his descendant <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yue_Ke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yue Ke (page does not exist)">Yue Ke</a>'s (岳珂) <i>Eguo Jintuo Zuibian</i> (鄂國金佗稡编 <i>literally: Record of the Jin in Hubei</i>), the reliability of which is questioned by some sources, for example whether the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Yancheng" title="Battle of Yancheng">Battle of Yancheng</a> (郾城之戰) really was a great victory for the Song<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and if the claim regarding Yue Fei's twelve gold medals is true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Furthermore, there remains the issue of whether Yu Fei's troops left the people unharmed as is sometimes claimed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Qing dynasty poet and historian <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Yi" title="Zhao Yi">Zhao Yi</a> (趙翼) covers this in his <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Twenty-two_Historical_Sketches&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Twenty-two Historical Sketches (page does not exist)">Twenty-two Historical Sketches</a> (二十二史劄記) under a section regarding observations on the Song and Jin armies (宋金用兵需參觀二史).</p>
<p>Because the <i>History of Song</i> was prepared in a hurry and is the work of many editing it contains a number of unavoidable errors and contradictions;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChinaKnowledge_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ChinaKnowledge-1">[1]</a></sup> for example an individual with two biographical entries such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Xijing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Li Xijing (page does not exist)">Li Xijing</a> (李熙靖) who appears in chapter 116 of the historical biographies section then again in chapter 222. The work also covers the Northern and Early Southern Song Dynasties in detail with only an outline of the later Southern Song dynasty. There are more biographies of individuals from the Northern Song period, for example the <i>Wenyuan Zhuan</i> (文苑傳) covers a total of 96 people of which only 11 are from the Southern Song era. The <i>History of Song</i> is also considered the most disordered of the 24 Dynastic Histories. Zhao Yi comments: “When the Yuan dynasty wrote the history of the overthrown Song they probably just arranged preexisting Song texts.” The Qing dynasty <i>Siku Quanshu</i> (四庫全書總目) collection of books says that the “main purpose of the <i>History of Song</i> is as a tribute to the Song emperors and their Confucianism. Attention is not paid to other matters so there are a great number of errors”. In 1977, Zhonghua Publishing issued a new version of the <i>History of Song</i> with corrections to the punctuation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_influence">Later influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the time of its publication, successive dynasties have produced commentaries on the <i>History of Song</i>. In 1546, <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> author <a href="/wiki/Wang_Zhu" title="Wang Zhu">Wang Zhu</a> (王洙) completed his 100 chapter <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Supplement_to_the_History_of_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Supplement to the History of Song (page does not exist)">Supplement to the History of Song</a></i> to be followed in 1561 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ke_Weiqi%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ke Weiqi's (page does not exist)">Ke Weiqi's</a> (柯維祺) 200 chapter <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Songshi_Xinbian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Songshi Xinbian (page does not exist)">Songshi Xinbian</a></i> (宋史新編) and the 250 chapter <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Songshi_Ji&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Songshi Ji (page does not exist)">Songshi Ji</a></i> (宋史記) written by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wang_Weijian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wang Weijian (page does not exist)">Wang Weijian</a> (王惟儉). During the Qing dynasty, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chen_Huangzhong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chen Huangzhong (page does not exist)">Chen Huangzhong</a> (陳黃中) wrote the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Songshi_Gao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Songshi Gao (page does not exist)">Songshi Gao</a></i> (宋史稿) running to 219 chapters whilst <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chen_Xinyuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chen Xinyuan (page does not exist)">Chen Xinyuan</a> (陸心源) produced the 40 chapter<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Songshi_Yi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Songshi Yi (page does not exist)">Songshi Yi</a></i> (宋史翼). In <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> <a href="/wiki/Jeongjo_of_Joseon" title="Jeongjo of Joseon">Li Suan</a> (李算) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> (1392-1910 CE) penned the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Songshi_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Songshi Qian (page does not exist)">Songshi Qian</a> (宋史筌) with 148 chapters. All these works correct some of the shortcomings of the <i>History of Song</i> but are no substitute for the original.</p>
<p>During the final years of the Qing <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong Emperor</a> (r. 1735-1796 CE), historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shao_Jinhan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shao Jinhan (page does not exist)">Shao Jinhan</a> (邵晉涵) along with <a href="/wiki/Qian_Daxin" title="Qian Daxin">Qian Daxin</a> (錢大昕) and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Xuecheng" title="Zhang Xuecheng">Zhang Xuecheng</a> (章學誠) worked hard to revamp the <i>History of Song</i>. Shao produced his <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biographical_sketch_of_the_Southern_Song&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biographical sketch of the Southern Song (page does not exist)">Biographical sketch of the Southern Song</a> (南都事略) then went back to work on the <i>History of Song</i> but died before it was completed.</p>

<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Yue Fei's biography in the <i>History of Song</i> quotes Yue Ke's version of events from his <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ewang_Xingshi_Biannian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ewang Xingshi Biannian (page does not exist)">Ewang Xingshi Biannian</a> (鄂王行實編年) regarding the "slaughter of the 5,000 enemy ". Chinese historian <a href="/wiki/Deng_Guangming" title="Deng Guangming">Deng Guangming</a> (鄧廣銘) in his <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biography_of_Yue_Fei&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biography of Yue Fei (page does not exist)">Biography of Yue Fei</a> (岳飛傳) claims that at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Yinchang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battle of Yinchang (page does not exist)">Battle of Yinchang</a> (潁昌決戰), which took place at modern day <a href="/wiki/Xuchang" title="Xuchang">Xuchang</a> City in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henan_Province" title="Henan Province">Henan Province</a>, only 500 of the enemy were killed. Wang Cengyu further argues in section three of chapter nine of his <i>Yue Fei and the Early Southern Song – Politics &amp; the Military</i> (岳飛和南宋前期政治與軍事研究)that the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Yancheng" title="Battle of Yancheng">Battle of Yancheng</a> (郾城之戰) was a decisive victory for the Song. On the other hand Wang also points out that even before he received the order from <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Gaozong_of_Song" title="Emperor Gaozong of Song">Emperor Gaozong of Song</a>, Yue Fei had already withdrawn his troops from the battle.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Hangzhou" title="Hangzhou">Hangzhou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yancheng" title="Yancheng">Yancheng</a> are separated by a distance of more than two thousand Chinese miles. <li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sanchao_Beimeng_Huibian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sanchao Beimeng Huibian (page does not exist)">Sanchao Beimeng Huibian</a></i> (三朝北盟會編) notes: When <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Shunfu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Han Shunfu (page does not exist)">Han Shunfu</a> (韓順夫) attacked Caocheng (曹成) he “often pitched camp, removed his armour and consorted with captive local women”. After Han’s defeat, Yue Fei was furious and had his soldiers murder all of Han’s relatives.</span></li>
