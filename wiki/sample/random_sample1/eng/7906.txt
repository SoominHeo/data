<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_Yeong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seong Young was born on March 15, 1940 in Yeongkwang <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeollanam-do" title="Jeollanam-do">Jeollanam-do</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Song graduated with a degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Literature" title="German Literature">German Literature</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hankuk_University_of_Foreign_Studies" title="Hankuk University of Foreign Studies">Hankuk University of Foreign Studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee1996_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee1996-3">[3]</a></sup> After his graduation in 1963, he took a teaching job. His literary debut came in 1967 with the publication of “Cock-fighting” in the journal Creation and Criticism (Changjakgwa bipyeong). His sobriquet was Mokdang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He died on October 14, 2016, aged 76.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Song_Yeong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Korea Literature Translation Institute summarizes his work:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Song Yeong’s fiction often unfolds in unusual settings through perspectives of unconventional characters. “The Teacher and the Crown Prince” (Seonsaenggwa hwangtaeja) and “The Day My Love Comes” take place in a prison, and “A Train on the Central Line” (Jungangseon gicha, ) narrates various events that occur inside a crowded train. For Song Yeong, these spaces offer unexpectedly accurate microcosms of the larger society; wild commotion and meaningless violence that the narrator observes, often with ironic detachment and terseness, raise questions about our lives in the real world. Often these questions lead to a critique of various forms of authority that flout common sense and fetter individual freedom. Typically, Song Yeong’s characters remain outside the network of relations that secure individuals’ social identities. “Cock-fighting” (Tugye, 1967) features as the protagonist a man who remains holed up in his own world and whose perception of the external reality is skewed to the extreme. Existential angst also marks the attitude of the main character to the world around him in “On the Steps”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
