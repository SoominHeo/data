<p>A <b>centrifugal fan</b> is a mechanical device for moving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>. The terms "blower" and "squirrel cage fan", (because it looks like a <a href="/wiki/Hamster_wheel" title="Hamster wheel">hamster wheel</a>), are frequently used as synonyms. These fans increase the speed and volume of an air stream with the rotating <a href="/wiki/Impeller" title="Impeller">impellers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fans_and_Blowers_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fans_and_Blowers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Centrifugal fans use the kinetic energy of the <a href="/wiki/Impeller" title="Impeller">impellers</a> to increase the volume of the air/gas stream. which in turn moves them against the resistance caused by ducts, dampers and other components. Centrifugal fans displace air radially, changing the direction (typically by 90°) of the airflow. They are sturdy, quiet, reliable, and capable of operating over a wide range of conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACMA_sourcebook_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACMA_sourcebook-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Centrifugal fans are constant displacement devices or constant volume devices, meaning that, at a constant fan speed, a centrifugal fan will move a relatively constant volume of air rather than a constant mass. This means that the air velocity in a system is fixed even though the mass flow rate through the fan is not.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="(July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Centrifugal fans are not positive displacement devices. Centrifugal fans have certain advantages and disadvantages when contrasted with positive-displacement blowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The centrifugal fan is one of the most widely used fans. Centrifugal fans are by far the most prevalent type of fan used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating,_Ventilation,_and_Air_Conditioning" title="Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning">HVAC</a> industry today. They are often cheaper than axial fans and simpler in construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-estar_air_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-estar_air-8">[8]</a></sup> They are used in transporting gas or materials and in ventilation system for buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_IITM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh_IITM-9">[9]</a></sup> They are also well-suited for <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> processes and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> control systems.</p>
<p>The centrifugal fan is a drum shape composed of a number of fan blades mounted around a hub. As shown in the animated figure, the hub turns on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driveshaft" title="Driveshaft">driveshaft</a> mounted in bearings in the fan housing. The gas enters from the side of the fan <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheel</a>, turns 90 degrees and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerate" title="Accelerate">accelerates</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">centrifugal force</a> as it flows over the fan blades and exits the fan housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest mention of centrifugal fans was in 1556 by Georg Pawer (Latin: <a href="/wiki/Georgius_Agricola" title="Georgius Agricola">Georgius Agricola</a>) in his book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Re_Metallica" title="De Re Metallica">De Re Metallica</a></i>, where he shows how such fans were used to ventilate mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Thereafter, centrifugal fans gradually fell into disuse. It wasn't until the early decades of the nineteenth century that interest in centrifugal fans revived. In 1815 the Marquis de Chabannes advocated the use of a centrifugal fan and took out a British patent in the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1827, Edwin A. Stevens of Bordentown, New Jersey, installed a fan for blowing air into the boilers of the steamship <i>North America</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Similarly, in 1832, the Swedish-American engineer <a href="/wiki/John_Ericsson" title="John Ericsson">John Ericsson</a> used a centrifugal fan as blower on the steamship <i>Corsair</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A centrifugal fan was invented by Russian military engineer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Sablukov" title="Alexander Sablukov">Alexander Sablukov</a> in 1832, and was used both in the Russian light industry (such as sugar making) and abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Main parts of a centrifugal fan are :</p>
<ol>
<li>Fan housing</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Impeller" title="Impeller">Impellers</a></li>
<li>Inlet and outlet ducts</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drive_shaft" title="Drive shaft">Drive shaft</a></li>
<li>Drive mechanism</li>
</ol>
<p>Other components used may include <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coupling" title="Coupling">couplings</a>, impeller locking device, fan discharge casing, shaft seal plates etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drive_mechanisms">Drive mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drive mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fan drive determines the speed of the fan wheel (impeller) and the extent to which this speed can be varied. There are three basic types of fan drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct">Direct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Direct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The fan wheel can be linked directly to the shaft of an <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a>. This means that the fan wheel speed is identical to the motor's <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotational</a> speed. With this type of fan drive mechanism, the fan speed cannot be varied unless the motor speed is adjustable. Air conditioning will then automatically provide faster speed because colder air is more dense.</p>
<p>Some electronics manufacturers have made centrifugal fans with external rotor motors (the stator is inside the rotor), and the rotor is directly mounted on the fan wheel (impeller).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Belt">Belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A set of <a href="/wiki/Sheave" title="Sheave">sheaves</a> is mounted on the motor shaft and the fan wheel shaft, and a belt transmits the mechanical energy from the motor to the fan.</p>

<p>Fan wheel speeds in belt-driven fans are fixed unless the belt(s) slip. Belt slippage can reduce the fan wheel speed by several hundred revolutions per minute (rpm).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Variable">Variable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Variable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Variable drive fans may use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic_coupling" title="Hydraulic coupling">hydraulic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_coupling" title="Magnetic coupling">magnetic couplings</a> (between the fan wheel shaft and the motor shaft) that allow variable speed. The fan speed controls are often integrated into automated systems to maintain the desired fan wheel speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternate method of varying the fan speed is by use of an electronic <a href="/wiki/Variable-frequency_drive" title="Variable-frequency drive">variable-speed drive</a> which controls the speed of the motor driving the fan. This offers better overall energy efficiency than mechanical couplings, especially at greatly-reduced speeds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bearings">Bearings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bearings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bearings are an important part of a fan. Sleeve-ring oil bearings are used extensively in fans. Some sleeve-ring bearings may be water-cooled. Water-cooled sleeve bearings are often used when hot gases are being moved by the fan. Heat is conducted through the shaft and into the oil which must be cooled to prevent overheating of the bearing.</p>
<p>Since lower-speed fans have bearings in hard-to-reach spots, grease-packed anti-friction bearings are used.</p>
<p>Many turbo blowers use either an <a href="/wiki/Air_bearing" title="Air bearing">air bearing</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">magnetic bearing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fan_dampers_and_vanes">Fan dampers and vanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fan dampers and vanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fan dampers are used to control gas flow into and out of the centrifugal fan. They may be installed on the inlet side or on the outlet side of the fan, or both. Dampers on the outlet side impose a flow resistance that is used to control gas flow. Dampers on the inlet side (inlet vanes) are designed to control gas flow by changing the amount of gas or air admitted to the fan inlet.</p>
<p>Inlet dampers (inlet vanes) reduce fan energy usage due to their ability to affect the airflow pattern into the fan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The fan wheel consists of a hub on which a number of fan blades are attached. The fan blades on the hub can be arranged in three different ways: forward-curved, backward-curved or radial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Forward-curved">Forward-curved</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Forward-curved">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Forward-curved blades, as in Figure 3(a), curve in the direction of the fan wheel's rotation. These are especially sensitive to particulates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Sensitive in what way? (April 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Forward-curved blades provide a low noise level and relatively small air flow with a high increase in static pressure. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebmpapst_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebmpapst-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Backward-curved">Backward-curved</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Backward-curved">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Backward-curved blades, as in Figure 3(b), curve against the direction of the fan wheel's rotation. Smaller blowers may have <b>backward-inclined</b> blades, which are straight, not curved. Larger backward-inclined/-curved blowers have blades whose backward curvatures mimic that of an airfoil cross section, but both designs provide good operating efficiency with relatively economical construction techniques. These types of blowers are designed to handle gas streams with low to moderate particulate loadings<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. They can be easily fitted with wear protection but certain blade curvatures can be prone to solids build-up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Backward curved wheels are often heavier than corresponding forward-curved equivalents, as they run at higher speeds and require stronger construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Backward curved fans can have a high range of specific speeds but are most often used for medium specific speed applications—high pressure, medium flow applications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Backward-curved fans are much more energy efficient than radial blade fans and so, for high power applications may be a suitable alternative to the lower cost radial bladed fan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Straight_radial">Straight radial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Straight radial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Radial blowers, as in Figure 3(c), have wheels whose blades extend straight out from the centre of the hub. Radial bladed wheels are often used on particulate-laden gas streams because they are the least sensitive to solid build-up on the blades, but they are often characterized by greater noise output. High speeds, low volumes, and high pressures are common with radial blowers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, and are often used in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_cleaner" title="Vacuum cleaner">vacuum cleaners</a>, pneumatic material conveying systems, and similar processes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_of_operation">Principles of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Principles of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The centrifugal fan uses the centrifugal power supplied from the rotation of impellers to increase the kinetic energy of air/gases. When the impellers rotate, the gas particles near the impellers are thrown-off from the impellers, then moves into the fan casing. As a result, the kinetic energy of gas is measured as pressure because of the system resistance offered by the casing and duct. The gas is then guided to the exit via outlet ducts. After the gas is thrown-off, the gas pressure in the middle region of the impellers decreases. The gas from the impeller eye rushes in to normalize this. This cycle repeats and therefore the gas can be continuously transferred.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_between_fans_and_blowers">Difference between fans and blowers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Difference between fans and blowers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The property that distinguishes a centrifugal fan from a blower is the pressure ratio it can achieve. In general, a blower can produce a higher pressure ratio. As per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="American Society of Mechanical Engineers">American Society of Mechanical Engineers</a> (ASME) the specific ratio - the ratio of the discharge pressure over the suction pressure – is used for defining the fans and blowers (Table 1).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratings">Ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ratings found in centrifugal fan performance tables and curves are based on standard air <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SCFM" title="SCFM">SCFM</a>. Fan manufacturers define standard air as clean, dry air with a <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of 0.075 pounds mass per cubic foot (1.2 kg/m³), with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> at sea level of 29.92 inches of mercury (101.325 kPa) and a <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of 70 °F (21 °C). Selecting a centrifugal fan to operate at conditions other than standard air requires adjustment to both static pressure and <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">power</a>.</p>
<p>At higher-than-standard elevation (<a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>) and higher-than-standard temperature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">air density</a> is lower than standard density. Air density corrections need to be taken into account for centrifugal fans that are specified for continuous operation at higher temperatures. The centrifugal fan will displace a constant volume of air in a given system regardless of the air density.</p>
<p>When a centrifugal fan is specified for a given CFM and static pressure at conditions other than standard, an air density correction factor must be applied to select the proper size fan to meet the new condition. Since 200 °F (93 °C) air weighs only 80% of 70 °F (21 °C) air, the centrifugal fan will create less pressure and require less power. To get the actual pressure required at 200 °F (93 °C), the designer would have to multiply the pressure at standard conditions by an air density correction factor of 1.25 (i.e., 1.0/0.8) to get the system to operate correctly. To get the actual power at 200 °F (93 °C), the designer would have to divide the power at standard conditions by the air density correction factor.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_Movement_and_Control_Association_.28AMCA.29">Air Movement and Control Association (AMCA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Air Movement and Control Association (AMCA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The centrifugal fan performance tables provide the fan RPM and power requirements for the given CFM and static pressure at standard air density. When the centrifugal fan performance is not at standard conditions, the performance must be converted to standard conditions before entering the performance tables. Centrifugal fans rated by the <a href="/wiki/Air_Movement_and_Control_Association" title="Air Movement and Control Association">Air Movement and Control Association</a> (AMCA) are tested in laboratories with test setups that simulate installations that are typical for that type of fan. Usually they are tested and rated as one of four standard installation types as designated in AMCA Standard 210.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>AMCA Standard 210 defines uniform methods for conducting laboratory tests on housed fans to determine airflow rate, pressure, power and efficiency, at a given speed of rotation. The purpose of AMCA Standard 210 is to define exact procedures and conditions of fan testing so that ratings provided by various manufacturers are on the same basis and may be compared. For this reason, fans must be rated in standardized SCFM.</p>

<p>Centrifugal fans suffer efficiency losses in both stationary and moving parts, increasing the energy input required for a given level of airflow performance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impeller_entry">Impeller entry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Impeller entry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flow at the intake and its turning from axial to radial direction causes losses at the intake. Friction and <a href="/wiki/Flow_separation" title="Flow separation">flow separation</a> cause impeller blade losses since there is change in <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">incidence angle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (April 2015)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These impeller blade losses are also included in the category.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leakage">Leakage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Leakage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leakage of some air and disturbance in the main flow field is caused due to the clearance provided between the rotating periphery of the impeller and the casing at the entry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impeller">Impeller</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Impeller">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Passage friction and flow separation causes impeller losses which are dependent on relative velocity, rate of diffusion and blade geometry. Impeller dynamic balancing usually is done on a precision balancing machine, because all energy of vibrational imbalance is lost (for example, this can easily amount to 50% of air-flow loss in poorly-maintained home AC units).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diffuser_and_volute">Diffuser and volute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Diffuser and volute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Friction and flow separation also causes losses in the <a href="/wiki/Diffuser_(thermodynamics)" title="Diffuser (thermodynamics)">diffuser</a>. Further losses due to incidence occur if the device is working beyond its design conditions. Flow from the impeller or diffuser expands in the <a href="/wiki/Volute_(pump)" title="Volute (pump)">volute</a>, which has a larger cross section leading to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddy</a>, which in turn reduces pressure head. Friction and flow separation losses also occur due the volute passage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disc_friction">Disc friction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_fan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Disc friction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Viscous <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> on the back surface of the impeller disc causes disc friction losses.</p>
