<p><i><b>Repatriation</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">송환</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">送還</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">Songhwan</span></i>) is a 2004 South Korean <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary film</a> that documents the lives of <a href="/wiki/Unconverted_long-term_prisoners" title="Unconverted long-term prisoners">North Korean spies who were captured</a> and imprisoned in the South for more than 30 years. They were finally set free in the 1990s when inter-Korean relations improved, and repatriated to the North.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was presented with the Freedom of Expression Award at the 2004 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Sundance_Film_Festival_award_winners#2004" title="List of Sundance Film Festival award winners">Sundance Film Festival</a>, the first time a Korean film has ever been presented with an award at the prestigious U.S. festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It also won Best Documentary Award at the 19th <a href="/wiki/Fribourg_International_Film_Festival" title="Fribourg International Film Festival">Fribourg International Film Festival</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Repatriation_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the "unconverted" North Korean spies, captured and imprisoned in South Korea, were released after more than 30 years, they moved to Bongchun-dong, filmmaker Kim Dong-won's village. Kim developed a close relationship with them which eventually led him to the <i>Repatriation</i> film project, which spanned 12 years and 800 hours of videotaping. The film documents their views on Korea's partition, their daily hardships as they attempt to adjust to South Korean society as well as their struggle for repatriation. Through this intimate portrayal, Kim offers a penetrating insight into the tragic consequences of the Cold War that still persist in Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
