<p>The <b>Shawnee</b> (<b>Shaawanwaki</b>, <b>Ša˙wano˙ki</b> and <b>Shaawanowi lenaweeki</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) are an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian-speaking</a> ethnic group indigenous to North America. In colonial times they were a semi-migratory Native American nation, primarily inhabiting areas of the Ohio Valley, extending from <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> eastward to <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Western_Maryland" title="Western Maryland">Western Maryland</a>; south to <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>; and westward to <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>Pushed west by European-American pressure, the Shawnee migrated to Missouri and Kansas, with some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Removal" title="Indian Removal">removed</a> to <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a> (Oklahoma) west of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> in the 1830s. Other Shawnee did not remove to Oklahoma until after the Civil War. Made up of different historical and kinship groups, today there are three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federally_recognized_tribes" title="Federally recognized tribes">federally recognized</a> Shawnee tribes, all headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absentee-Shawnee_Tribe_of_Indians_of_Oklahoma" title="Absentee-Shawnee Tribe of Indians of Oklahoma">Absentee-Shawnee Tribe of Indians of Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shawnee_Tribe_of_Oklahoma" title="Eastern Shawnee Tribe of Oklahoma">Eastern Shawnee Tribe of Oklahoma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_Tribe" title="Shawnee Tribe">Shawnee Tribe</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_language" title="Shawnee language">Shawnee language</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian language</a>, was spoken by 200 people in 2002, including over 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absentee_Shawnee" title="Absentee Shawnee">Absentee Shawnee</a> and 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loyal_Shawnee" title="Loyal Shawnee">Loyal Shawnee</a> speakers. The language is written in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>. It has a dictionary and portions of the Bible were translated into Shawnee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian peoples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proto-Algonquian_language" title="Proto-Algonquian language">Proto-Algonquian language</a></div>
<p>Some scholars believe that the Shawnee are descendants of the people of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_Americas" title="Pre-Columbian Americas">precontact</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Ancient_culture" title="Fort Ancient culture">Fort Ancient culture</a> of the Ohio region, although this is not universally accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Fort Ancient culture flourished from 1000 to 1650 CE among a people who predominantly inhabited lands along the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> in areas of southern <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> and western <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. They were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mound_builder_(people)" title="Mound builder (people)">mound builders</a>. Fort Ancient culture was once thought to have been an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a>. But, scholars now believe Fort Ancient culture developed independently and was descended from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hopewell_culture" title="Hopewell culture">Hopewell culture</a> (100 BCE—500 CE), also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mound_builder_(people)" title="Mound builder (people)">mound builder</a> people.</p>

<p>Uncertainty surrounds the fate of the Fort Ancient people. Most likely their society, like the <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a> to the south, was severely disrupted by waves of epidemics from new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> carried by the first Spanish explorers in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENCYCPREHIST_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ENCYCPREHIST-8">[8]</a></sup> After 1525 at <a href="/wiki/Madisonville_Site" title="Madisonville Site">Madisonville</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Type_site" title="Type site">type site</a>, the village's house sizes became smaller and fewer, with evidence showing the people changed from their previously "horticulture-centered, sedentary way of life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENCYCPREHIST_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ENCYCPREHIST-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a gap in the archaeological record between the most recent Fort Ancient sites and the oldest sites of the Shawnee. The latter were recorded by European (French and English) explorers as occupying this area at the time of encounter. Scholars generally accept that similarities in material culture, art, mythology, and Shawnee oral history linking them to the Fort Ancient peoples can be used to support the connection from Fort Ancient society and development as the historical Shawnee society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Shawnee traditionally considered the <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape</a> (or Delaware) of the East Coast mid-Atlantic region, who were also Algonquian speaking, as their "grandfathers." The <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian</a> nations of present-day Canada regarded the US Shawnee as their southernmost branch. Along the East Coast, the Algonquian-speaking tribes were mostly located in coastal areas, from Quebec to the Carolinas.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian languages</a> have words similar to the archaic <i>shawano</i> (now: <i>shaawanwa</i>) meaning "south". However, the stem <i>šawa-</i> does not mean "south" in Shawnee, but "moderate, warm (of weather)": See Voegelin "šawa (plus -ni, -te) MODERATE, WARM. Cp. šawani 'it is moderating...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In one Shawnee tale, "Sawage" (šaawaki) is the deity of the south wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Curtin translates Sawage as 'it thaws', referring to the warm weather of the south. šaawaki is attested as the spirit of the South, or the South Wind, in this account, in one of Voegelin's tales,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in a song collected by Voegelin.<i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Europeans reported encountering Shawnee over a widespread geographic area. One of the earliest mentions of the Shawnee may be a 1614 Dutch map showing some <i>Sawwanew</i> located just east of the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a>. Later 17th-century Dutch sources also place them in this general location. Accounts by French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorers</a> in the same century usually located the Shawnee along the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, where the French encountered them on forays from eastern Canada and the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Country" title="Illinois Country">Illinois Country</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanna_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanna-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>A Shawnee town might have from forty to one hundred bark-covered houses similar in construction to <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longhouses" title="Longhouses">longhouses</a>. Each village usually had a meeting house or council house, perhaps sixty to ninety feet long, where public deliberations took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleber1992_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kleber1992-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to one European legend, some Shawnee were descended from a party sent by Chief <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opechancanough" title="Opechancanough">Opechancanough</a>, ruler of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powhatan_Confederacy" title="Powhatan Confederacy">Powhatan Confederacy</a> 1618–1644, to settle in the <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a>. The party was led by his son, Sheewa-a-nee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Edward Bland, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorer</a> who accompanied <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Wood" title="Abraham Wood">Abraham Wood</a>'s expedition in 1650, wrote that in Opechancanough's day, there had been a falling-out between the <i>Chawan</i> <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">chief</a> and the <i>weroance</i> of the Powhatan (also a relative of Opechancanough's family). He said the latter had murdered the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Shawnee were "driven from <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> in the 1670s by the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> of Pennsylvania and New York, who claimed the Ohio valley as hunting ground to supply its fur trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleber1992_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kleber1992-16">[16]</a></sup> The explorers Batts and Fallam in 1671 reported that the Shawnee were contesting control of the Shenandoah Valley with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" title="Haudenosaunee">Haudenosaunee</a> Confederacy (Iroquois) in that year, and were losing.</p>
<p>Sometime before 1670, a group of Shawnee migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Savannah_River" title="Savannah River">Savannah River</a> area. The English based in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charles Town</a>, South Carolina were contacted by these Shawnee in 1674. They forged a long-lasting alliance. The Savannah River Shawnee were known to the Carolina English as "Savannah Indians". Around the same time, other Shawnee groups migrated to Florida, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, Pennsylvania, and other regions south and east of the Ohio country. <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Le_Moyne_d%27Iberville" title="Pierre Le Moyne d'Iberville">d'Iberville</a>, writing in his journal in 1699, describes the Shawnee (or as he spells them, <i>Chaouenons</i>) as "the single nation to fear, being spread out over Carolina and Virginia in the direction of the Mississippi."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Alan_Gallay" title="Alan Gallay">Alan Gallay</a> speculates that the Shawnee migrations of the middle to late 17th century were probably driven by the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Wars" title="Beaver Wars">Beaver Wars</a>, which began in the 1640s. The Shawnee became known for their widespread settlements, extending from Pennsylvania to <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> and to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S.)" title="Georgia (U.S.)">Georgia</a>. Among their known villages were <i><a href="/wiki/Indian_Old_Fields,_Kentucky" title="Indian Old Fields, Kentucky">Eskippakithiki</a></i> in Kentucky, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonnionto" title="Sonnionto">Sonnionto</a></i> (also known as Lower Shawneetown) in Ohio, <i>Chalakagay</i> near what is now <a href="/wiki/Sylacauga,_Alabama" title="Sylacauga, Alabama">Sylacauga, Alabama</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Chalahgawtha" title="Chalahgawtha">Chalahgawtha</a></i> at the site of present-day <a href="/wiki/Chillicothe,_Ohio" title="Chillicothe, Ohio">Chillicothe, Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Shawneetown,_Illinois" title="Old Shawneetown, Illinois">Old Shawneetown, Illinois</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suwanee,_Georgia" title="Suwanee, Georgia">Suwanee, Georgia</a>. Their language became a <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> for trade among numerous tribes. They became leaders among the tribes, initiating and sustaining pan-Indian resistance to European and Euro-American expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some Shawnee occupied areas in central Pennsylvania. Long without a chief, in 1714 they asked Carondawana, an <a href="/wiki/Oneida_people" title="Oneida people">Oneida</a> war chief of the Iroquois, to represent them to the Pennsylvania provincial council, which accepted the Shawnee choice. About 1727 Carondawana and his wife, a prominent interpreter known as <a href="/wiki/Madame_Montour" title="Madame Montour">Madame Montour</a>, settled at <a href="/wiki/Loyalsock_Creek#Village_of_Otstonwakin" title="Loyalsock Creek">Otstonwakin</a>, on the west bank at the confluence of <a href="/wiki/Loyalsock_Creek" title="Loyalsock Creek">Loyalsock Creek</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Branch_Susquehanna_River" title="West Branch Susquehanna River">West Branch Susquehanna River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time European-American settlers began to arrive in the <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a> (c. 1730) of Virginia, the Shawnee were the main residents of the northern part of the valley. They were claimed as tributaries by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" title="Haudenosaunee">Haudenosaunee</a></i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquois_Confederacy" title="Iroquois Confederacy">Six Nations</a> of the Iroquois, who had helped some of the <a href="/wiki/Tuscarora_people" title="Tuscarora people">Tuscarora people</a> from North Carolina resettle in the vicinity of what is now <a href="/wiki/Martinsburg,_West_Virginia" title="Martinsburg, West Virginia">Martinsburg, West Virginia</a>. Also at this time, <a href="/wiki/Seneca_people" title="Seneca people">Seneca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape</a> war parties from the north often fought pitched battles with pursuing bands of <a href="/wiki/Catawba_people" title="Catawba people">Catawba</a> from Virginia, who would overtake them in the Shawnee-inhabited regions of the Valley.</p>
<p>By the late 1730s pressure from colonial expansion produced repeated conflicts. Shawnee communities were affected by the <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a> in which furs were often traded to European traders for rum or brandy, leading to serious social problems related to <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_abuse" title="Alcohol abuse">alcohol abuse</a>. Several Shawnee communities in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pennsylvania" title="Province of Pennsylvania">Province of Pennsylvania</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Chartier" title="Peter Chartier">Peter Chartier</a>, a <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis" title="Métis">métis</a> trader, opposed the sale of alcohol in their communities, resulting in a conflict with colonial <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Gordon_(governor)" title="Patrick Gordon (governor)">Governor Patrick Gordon</a>. As a result, in 1745 some 400 Shawnee migrated from Pennsylvania to Ohio, Kentucky, Alabama and Illinois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warren-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to 1754, the Shawnee had a headquarters at Shawnee Springs at modern-day <a href="/wiki/Cross_Junction,_Virginia" title="Cross Junction, Virginia">Cross Junction, Virginia</a> near <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Virginia" title="Winchester, Virginia">Winchester</a>. The father of the later chief <a href="/wiki/Cornstalk" title="Cornstalk">Cornstalk</a> held his council there. Several other Shawnee villages were located in the northern <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a>: at <a href="/wiki/Moorefield,_West_Virginia" title="Moorefield, West Virginia">Moorefield, West Virginia</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/North_River_(South_Fork_Shenandoah_River)" title="North River (South Fork Shenandoah River)">North River</a>, and on the Potomac at <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_Maryland" title="Cumberland, Maryland">Cumberland, Maryland</a>. In 1753, the Shawnee on the <a href="/wiki/Scioto_River" title="Scioto River">Scioto River</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_country" title="Ohio country">Ohio country</a> sent messengers to those still in the Shenandoah Valley suggesting that they leave Virginia and cross the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alleghenies" title="Alleghenies">Alleghenies</a> to join the people further west, which they did the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The community known as <i>Shannoah</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lower_Shawneetown" title="Lower Shawneetown">Lower Shawneetown</a>) on the Ohio River reached a population of around 1,200 by 1750.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calloway_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Calloway-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ever since the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Wars" title="Beaver Wars">Beaver Wars</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" title="Haudenosaunee">Haudenosaunee</a> Confederacy ("Five Nations") had claimed the Ohio Country as their hunting ground by right of conquest, and treated the Shawnee and Lenape who resettled there as dependent tribes. Some independent Iroquois bands from various tribes also migrated westward, where they became known in Ohio as the <a href="/wiki/Mingo" title="Mingo">Mingo</a>. These three tribes—the Shawnee, the Delaware, and the Mingo— became closely associated with one another, despite the differences in their languages. The first two were Algonquian speaking and the third <a href="/wiki/Iroquoian_languages" title="Iroquoian languages">Iroquoian</a>.</p>
<p>After taking part in the first phase of the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a> (also known as "Braddock's War") as allies of the French,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> the Shawnee switched sides in 1758. They made formal peace with the British colonies at the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Easton" title="Treaty of Easton">Treaty of Easton</a>, which recognized the Allegheny Ridge (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Divide" title="Eastern Divide">Eastern Divide</a>) as their mutual border. This peace lasted only until <a href="/wiki/Pontiac%27s_War" title="Pontiac's War">Pontiac's War</a> erupted in 1763. Later that year, the Crown issued the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_1763" title="Proclamation of 1763">Proclamation of 1763</a>, legally confirming the 1758 border as the limits of British colonization, with the land beyond reserved for Native Americans. But, it had difficulty enforcing the boundary, as European colonists continued to move westward.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Fort_Stanwix" title="Treaty of Fort Stanwix">Treaty of Fort Stanwix</a> in 1768 extended that line westward, giving the British colonists a claim to what is now West Virginia and Kentucky. The Shawnee did not agree to this treaty: it was negotiated between British officials and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" title="Haudenosaunee">Haudenosaunee</a> Confederacy ("Six Nations"), who claimed sovereignty over the land, although Shawnee and other Native American tribes also hunted there.</p>
<p>After the Stanwix treaty, Anglo-Americans began pouring into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_River_Valley" title="Ohio River Valley">Ohio River Valley</a> for settlement. Violent incidents between settlers and Indians escalated into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunmore%27s_War" title="Dunmore's War">Dunmore's War</a> in 1774. British diplomats managed to isolate the Shawnee during the conflict: the Iroquois and the Lenape stayed neutral. The Shawnee faced the British colony of Virginia with only a few Mingo allies. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Dunmore" title="Lord Dunmore">Lord Dunmore</a>, royal governor of Virginia, launched a two-pronged invasion into the Ohio Country. The Shawnee chief <a href="/wiki/Cornstalk" title="Cornstalk">Cornstalk</a> attacked one wing but fought to a draw in the only major battle of the war, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Point_Pleasant" title="Battle of Point Pleasant">Battle of Point Pleasant</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Camp_Charlotte" title="Treaty of Camp Charlotte">Treaty of Camp Charlotte</a> ending this war (1774), Cornstalk and the Shawnee were compelled by the British to recognize the same Ohio River boundary as that established with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" title="Haudenosaunee">Haudenosaunee</a> Confederacy ("Six Nations") by the 1768 Fort Stanwix treaty. The Shawnee ceded all claims to the "hunting grounds" of West Virginia and Kentucky. Many other Shawnee leaders refused to recognize this boundary, however. A Shawnee party attacked <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Boone" title="Daniel Boone">Daniel Boone</a> in Kentucky in 1775.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Revolution">American Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: American Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the United States declared independence from the British crown in 1776, the Shawnee were divided. They did not support the American rebel cause, but Cornstalk led the minority who wished to remain neutral. The Shawnee north of the Ohio River were also unhappy about the American settlement of Kentucky. <a href="/wiki/Colin_G._Calloway" title="Colin G. Calloway">Colin Calloway</a> reports that most Shawnees allied with the British against the Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colin_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-colin-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>War leaders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Blackfish" title="Chief Blackfish">Chief Blackfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue_Jacket" title="Blue Jacket">Blue Jacket</a> joined <a href="/wiki/Dragging_Canoe" title="Dragging Canoe">Dragging Canoe</a> and a band of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherokee_people" title="Cherokee people">Cherokee people</a> along the lower Tennessee and <a href="/wiki/Chickamauga_Creek" title="Chickamauga Creek">Chickamauga Creek</a> against the colonists in that area. Some colonists called them <a href="/wiki/Chickamauga_Cherokee" title="Chickamauga Cherokee">Chickamauga</a> because they lived along that river at the time of what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee%E2%80%93American_wars" title="Cherokee–American wars">Cherokee–American wars</a>, during and after the American Revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colin_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-colin-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Shawnee later combined with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami_tribe" title="Miami tribe">Miami</a> into a great fighting force in the Ohio Valley, after the Revolution, during the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Indian_War" title="Northwest Indian War">Northwest Indian War</a> between the United States and a confederation of Native American tribes. After being defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fallen_Timbers" title="Battle of Fallen Timbers">Battle of Fallen Timbers</a> in 1794, most of the Shawnee bands signed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Greenville" title="Treaty of Greenville">Treaty of Greenville</a> the next year. They were forced to cede large parts of their homeland to the new United States. Other Shawnee groups rejected this treaty, migrating independently to <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> west of the Mississippi River, where they settled near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Girardeau" title="Cape Girardeau">Cape Girardeau</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tecumseh.27s_War_and_the_War_of_1812">Tecumseh's War and the War of 1812</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tecumseh's War and the War of 1812">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Great_Comet_of_1811" title="Great Comet of 1811">Great Comet of 1811</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1812_New_Madrid_earthquake" title="1812 New Madrid earthquake">1812 New Madrid earthquake</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tippecanoe" title="Battle of Tippecanoe">Battle of Tippecanoe</a></div>
<p>In the early 19th century, the Shawnee leader <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a> gained renown for organizing his namesake <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh%27s_Confederacy" title="Tecumseh's Confederacy">confederacy</a> to oppose American expansion in Native American lands. The resulting conflict came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh%27s_War" title="Tecumseh's War">Tecumseh's War</a>. The two principal adversaries in the conflict, chief Tecumseh and American politician <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harrison" title="William Henry Harrison">William Henry Harrison</a>, had both been junior participants in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fallen_Timbers" title="Battle of Fallen Timbers">Battle of Fallen Timbers</a> at the close of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Indian_Wars" title="Northwest Indian Wars">Northwest Indian Wars</a> in 1794. Tecumseh was not among the Native American signers of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Greenville" title="Treaty of Greenville">Treaty of Greenville</a>, which had ended the war, when the Shawnee and other Native Americans ceded much of their historic territory in present-day <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> to the United States. However, many Indian leaders in the region accepted the Greenville terms, and for the next ten years pan-tribal resistance to American <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> faded.</p>
<p>In September 1809 William Henry Harrison, then governor of the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Territory" title="Indiana Territory">Indiana Territory</a>, invited the <a href="/wiki/Potawatomi" title="Potawatomi">Potawatomi</a>, Lenape, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eel_River_tribe" title="Eel River tribe">Eel River people</a>, and the Miami to a meeting in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne,_Indiana" title="Fort Wayne, Indiana">Fort Wayne, Indiana</a>. In the negotiations, Harrison promised large subsidies and payments to the tribes if they would cede the lands he was asking for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-o201203_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-o201203-29">[29]</a></sup> After two weeks of negotiating, the Potawatomi leaders convinced the Miami to accept the treaty as reciprocity, because the Potawatomi had earlier accepted treaties less advantageous to them at the request of the Miami. Finally the tribes signed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Fort_Wayne_(1809)" title="Treaty of Fort Wayne (1809)">Treaty of Fort Wayne</a> on September 30, 1809, thereby selling the United States over 3,000,000 acres (approximately 12,000 km²), chiefly along the <a href="/wiki/Wabash_River" title="Wabash River">Wabash River</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Vincennes,_Indiana" title="Vincennes, Indiana">Vincennes, Indiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-o201203_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-o201203-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tecumseh was outraged by the Treaty of Fort Wayne, believing that American Indian land was owned in common by all tribes, an idea advocated in previous years by the Shawnee leader <a href="/wiki/Blue_Jacket" title="Blue Jacket">Blue Jacket</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_nation" title="Mohawk nation">Mohawk</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Brant" title="Joseph Brant">Joseph Brant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-o212_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-o212-30">[30]</a></sup> In response, Tecumseh began to expand on the teachings of his brother, known as <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">The Prophet</a>, who called for the tribes to return to their ancestral ways. He began to associate the teachings with the idea of a pan-tribal alliance. Tecumseh traveled widely, urging warriors to abandon the accommodationist chiefs and to join the resistance at Prophetstown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-o212_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-o212-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>In August 1810, Tecumseh led 400 armed warriors to confront Governor Harrison in Vincennes. Tecumseh demanded that Harrison nullify the Fort Wayne treaty, threatening to kill the chiefs who had signed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l164_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-l164-31">[31]</a></sup> Harrison refused, stating that the Miami were the owners of the land and could sell it if they so chose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l165_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-l165-32">[32]</a></sup> Tecumseh left peacefully, but warned Harrison that he would seek an alliance with the British unless the treaty was nullified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langguth.2C_p._166_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langguth.2C_p._166-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>In March the <a href="/wiki/Great_Comet_of_1811" title="Great Comet of 1811">Great Comet of 1811</a> appeared. During the next year, tensions between American colonists and Native Americans rose quickly. Four settlers were murdered on the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> and, in another incident, natives seized a boatload of supplies from a group of traders. Harrison summoned Tecumseh to Vincennes to explain the actions of his allies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langguth.2C_p._166_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langguth.2C_p._166-33">[33]</a></sup> In August 1811, the two leaders met, with Tecumseh assuring Harrison that the Shawnee intended to remain at peace with the United States.</p>
<p>Afterward Tecumseh traveled to the Southeast on a mission to recruit allies against the United States among the "<a href="/wiki/Five_Civilized_Tribes" title="Five Civilized Tribes">Five Civilized Tribes</a>." His name <i>Tekoomsē') meant "Shooting Star" or "Panther Across The Sky."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He told the <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw" title="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscogee_people" title="Muscogee people">Muscogee</a>, and many others that the comet of March 1811 had signaled his coming. He also said that the people would see a sign proving that the <a href="/wiki/Great_Spirit" title="Great Spirit">Great Spirit</a> had sent him.</i></p>
<p>While Tecumseh was traveling, both sides readied for the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tippecanoe" title="Battle of Tippecanoe">Battle of Tippecanoe</a>. Harrison assembled a small force of army regulars and <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> in preparation to combat the Native forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l168_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-l168-35">[35]</a></sup> On November 6, 1811, Harrison led this army of about 1,000 men to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophetstown,_Indiana" title="Prophetstown, Indiana">Prophetstown, Indiana</a>, hoping to disperse Tecumseh's confederacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Early next morning, forces under The Prophet prematurely attacked Harrison's army at the Tippecanoe River near the Wabash. Though outnumbered, Harrison repulsed the attack, forcing the Natives to retreat and abandon Prophetstown. Harrison's men burned the village and returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l169_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-l169-37">[37]</a></sup> This was the end of Tecumseh's dream of a united native alliance against the whites.</p>
<p>On December 11, 1811, the <a href="/wiki/1811%E2%80%9312_New_Madrid_earthquakes" title="1811–12 New Madrid earthquakes">New Madrid earthquake</a> shook the Muscogee lands and the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a>. While the interpretation of this event varied from tribe to tribe, they agreed that the powerful earthquake had to have meant something. The earthquake and its aftershocks helped the Tecumseh resistance movement as the Muscogee and other Native American tribes believed it was a sign that the Shawnee must be supported and that this was the sign Tecumseh had prophesied.</p>

<p>The Muscogee (Creek) who joined Tecumseh's confederation were known as the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sticks" title="Red Sticks">Red Sticks</a>. They were the more traditional part of the people, as their communities in the Upper Towns were more isolated from European-American settlement. They did not want to assimilate. The Red Sticks rose in resisting the Lower Creek, and the bands became involved in civil war, known as the <a href="/wiki/Creek_War" title="Creek War">Creek War</a>. This became part of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> when open conflict broke out between American soldiers and the Red Sticks of the Creek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>After Hull's surrender of Detroit during the War of 1812, General <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harrison" title="William Henry Harrison">William Henry Harrison</a> was given command of the U.S. Army of the Northwest. He set out to retake the city, then defended by the British Colonel <a href="/wiki/Henry_Procter_(British_Army_officer)" title="Henry Procter (British Army officer)">Henry Procter</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a>. A detachment of Harrison's army was defeated at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Frenchtown" title="Battle of Frenchtown">Frenchtown</a> along the <a href="/wiki/River_Raisin" title="River Raisin">River Raisin</a> on January 22, 1813. Procter left the prisoners with an inadequate guard; they could not prevent some of his Native American allies from attacking and killing perhaps as many as 60 Americans, many of whom were Kentucky militiamen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The Americans called the incident the "River Raisin Massacre." The defeat ended Harrison's campaign against Detroit, and the phrase "Remember the River Raisin!" became a rallying cry for the Americans.</p>
<p>In May 1813, Procter and Tecumseh set <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Fort_Meigs" title="Siege of Fort Meigs">siege to Fort Meigs</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. American reinforcements arriving during the siege were defeated by the Natives, but the fort held out. The Indians eventually began to disperse, forcing Procter and Tecumseh to return to Canada. Their second offensive in July against Fort Meigs also failed. To improve Indian morale, Procter and Tecumseh attempted to <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Stephenson" title="Battle of Fort Stephenson">storm Fort Stephenson</a>, a small American post on the <a href="/wiki/Sandusky_River" title="Sandusky River">Sandusky River</a>. After they were repulsed with serious losses, the Ohio campaign ended.</p>
<p>On Lake Erie, the American commander Captain <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Hazard_Perry" title="Oliver Hazard Perry">Oliver Hazard Perry</a> fought the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lake_Erie" title="Battle of Lake Erie">Battle of Lake Erie</a> on September 10, 1813. His decisive victory against the British ensured American control of the lake, improved American morale after a series of defeats, and compelled the British to fall back from Detroit. General Harrison launched another invasion of Upper Canada, which culminated in the U.S. victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Thames" title="Battle of the Thames">Battle of the Thames</a> on October 5, 1813. Tecumseh was killed there, and his death effectively ended the North American indigenous alliance with the British in the Detroit region. American control of Lake Erie meant the British could no longer provide essential military supplies to their aboriginal allies, who dropped out of the war. The Americans controlled the area during the remainder of the conflict.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Shawnee in Missouri became known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absentee_Shawnee" title="Absentee Shawnee">Absentee Shawnee</a>" after migrating from the United States into Mexico, in the <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">eastern part</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Texas" title="Spanish Texas">Spanish Texas</a>. They were joined in the migration by some Delaware. Although they were closely allied with the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a> led by <a href="/wiki/The_Bowl_(Cherokee_chief)" title="The Bowl (Cherokee chief)">The Bowl</a>, their chief John Linney remained neutral during the 1839 Cherokee War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In appreciation, in the late 1840s <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Texas" title="Republic of Texas">Texan</a> president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirabeau_Lamar" title="Mirabeau Lamar">Mirabeau Lamar</a> fully compensated the Shawnee for their improvements and crops at the time of forcing their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Removal" title="Indian Removal">removal</a> from Texas north to <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Territory" title="Arkansas Territory">Arkansas Territory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-43">[43]</a></sup> The Shawnee settled close to present-day <a href="/wiki/Shawnee,_Oklahoma" title="Shawnee, Oklahoma">Shawnee, Oklahoma</a>. They were joined by Shawnee pushed out of Kansas (see below), who shared their traditionalist views and beliefs.</p>
<p>In 1817, the Ohio Shawnee had signed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Fort_Meigs" title="Treaty of Fort Meigs">Treaty of Fort Meigs</a>, ceding their remaining lands in exchange for three reservations in <a href="/wiki/Wapakoneta,_Ohio" title="Wapakoneta, Ohio">Wapaughkonetta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River_(Auglaize_River)" title="Ottawa River (Auglaize River)">Hog Creek</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Lima,_Ohio" title="Lima, Ohio">Lima</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Lewistown,_Ohio" title="Lewistown, Ohio">Lewistown</a>, Ohio. They shared these lands with some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seneca_nation" title="Seneca nation">Seneca</a> who had migrated west from New York.</p>
<p>Missouri joined the Union in 1821. After the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_St._Louis_(1825)" title="Treaty of St. Louis (1825)">Treaty of St. Louis</a> in 1825, the 1,400 Missouri Shawnee were forcibly relocated from Cape Girardeau to southeastern <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, close to the <a href="/wiki/Neosho_River" title="Neosho River">Neosho River</a>.</p>
<p>During 1833, only Black Bob's band of Shawnee resisted removal. They settled in northeastern Kansas near <a href="/wiki/Olathe,_Kansas" title="Olathe, Kansas">Olathe</a> and along the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_River" title="Kansas River">Kansas (Kaw) River</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monticello_Township,_Kansas" title="Monticello Township, Kansas">Monticello</a> near <a href="/wiki/Shawnee,_Kansas" title="Shawnee, Kansas">Gum Springs</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_Methodist_Mission" title="Shawnee Methodist Mission">Shawnee Methodist Mission</a> was built nearby to minister to the tribe. About 200 of the Ohio Shawnee followed the <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">Tenskwatawa</a> and joined their Kansas brothers and sisters here in 1826.</p>
<p>The main body of Shawnee in Ohio followed <a href="/wiki/Black_Hoof" title="Black Hoof">Black Hoof</a>, who fought every effort to force the Shawnee to give up their homeland. After the death of Black Hoof, the remaining 400 Ohio Shawnee in Wapaughkonetta and Hog Creek surrendered their land and moved to the Shawnee Reserve in Kansas. In 1831, the Lewistown group of Seneca–Shawnee had left for the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a> (present-day Oklahoma).</p>
<p>In the 1853 Indian Appropriations Bill, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> appropriated $64,366 for treaty obligations to the Shawnee such as annuities, education, and other services. An additional $2,000 was appropriated for the Seneca and the Shawnee together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, Black Bob's band fled from Kansas and joined the "Absentee Shawnee" in Oklahoma to escape the war. After the Civil War, the Shawnee in Kansas were expelled and forced to move to northeastern Oklahoma. The Shawnee members of the former Lewistown group became known as the "Eastern Shawnee".</p>
<p>The former Kansas Shawnee became known as the "Loyal Shawnee" (some say this is because of their allegiance with the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> during the war; others say this is because they were the last group to leave their Ohio homelands). The latter group appeared to be regarded as part of the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a> Nation by the United States because they were also known as the "Cherokee Shawnee" and were settled on some of their land in Indian Territory.</p>
<p>In 2000 the "Loyal" or "Cherokee" Shawnee finally received federal recognition independent of the Cherokee Nation. They are now known as the "Shawnee Tribe". Today, most members of the three tribes of the Shawnee nation reside in Oklahoma.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_kinship_groups">Social and kinship groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Social and kinship groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before contact with Europeans, the Shawnee tribe had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matrilineal" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> system, by which descent and inheritance went through maternal lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry1855_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henry1855-45">[45]</a></sup> Their government is by kings, which they call sachema, and those reign by succession, but always of the mother's side: for instance, the children of him who is now king, will not succeed, but his brother, by the mother, or the children of his sister, whose sons (and after them, the children of her daughter,) will reign, for no woman inherits. The reason they render for this way of descent, is, that their issue may not be spurious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colette_s_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-colette_s-46">[46]</a></sup> The five divisions of tribes commonly known are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chalahgawtha" title="Chalahgawtha">Chillicothe</a></i> (Principal Place), Chalahgawtha, Chalaka, Chalakatha;</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Hathawekela" title="Hathawekela">Hathawekela</a></i>, Thawikila;</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kispoko" title="Kispoko">Kispoko</a></i>, Kispokotha, Kishpoko, Kishpokotha;</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mekoche" title="Mekoche">Mekoche</a></i>, Mequachake, Machachee, Maguck, Mackachack, etc.;</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pekowi" title="Pekowi">Pekowi</a></i>, Pekuwe, Piqua, Pekowitha.</li>
</ul>
<p>The war chiefs were hereditary and descended from their maternal line in the Kispoko division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleber1992_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kleber1992-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the five septs, the Shawnee are divided among six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clans" title="Clans">clans</a> or subdivisions according to kinship; each clan represented spiritual values and had a role in the overall confederacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voegelin_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-voegelin-47">[47]</a></sup> Each name group is common among each for the five divisions, and each Shawnee belongs to a clan or name group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voegelin_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-voegelin-47">[47]</a></sup> The six group names are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Pellewomhsoomi</i> (Turkey name group)—represents bird life,</li>
<li><i>Kkahkileewomhsoomi</i> (Turtle name group)—represents aquatic life,</li>
<li><i>Petekoθiteewomhsoomi</i> (Rounded-feet name group)—represents carnivorous animals such as the dog, wolf, or whose paws are ball-shaped or "rounded,"</li>
<li><i>Mseewiwomhsoomi</i> (Horse name group)—represents herbivorous animals such as the horse and deer,</li>
<li><i>θepatiiwomhsoomi</i> (Raccoon name group)—represents animals having paws which can rip and tear, such as those of a raccoon and bear.</li>
<li><i>Petakineeθiiwomhsoomi</i> (Rabbit name group)—represents a gentle and peaceful nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voegelin_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-voegelin-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Each division had a primary village where the chief of the division lived. This village was usually named after the division. By tradition, each Shawnee division and clan had certain roles it performed on behalf of the entire tribe. By the time these kinship elements were recorded in writing by European Americans, these strong social traditions were fading. They are poorly understood. Because of the scattering of the Shawnee people from the 17th century through the 19th century, the roles of the divisions changed.</p>
<p>Today the United States government recognizes three Shawnee tribes, all of which are located in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absentee-Shawnee_Tribe_of_Indians_of_Oklahoma" title="Absentee-Shawnee Tribe of Indians of Oklahoma">Absentee-Shawnee Tribe of Indians of Oklahoma</a>, consisting mainly of <i>Hathawekela</i>, <i>Kispokotha</i>, and <i>Pekuwe</i> divisions;</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shawnee_Tribe_of_Oklahoma" title="Eastern Shawnee Tribe of Oklahoma">Eastern Shawnee Tribe of Oklahoma</a>, mostly of the <i>Mekoche</i> division; and</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_Tribe" title="Shawnee Tribe">Shawnee Tribe</a>, formerly considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_Nation" title="Cherokee Nation">Cherokee Nation</a>, mostly of the <i>Chaalakatha</i> and <i>Mekoche</i> divisions.</li>
</ul>
<p>As of 2008, there were 7584 enrolled Shawnee, with most living in Oklahoma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State-recognized_tribes">State-recognized tribes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: State-recognized tribes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pekowi" title="Pekowi">Piqua Shawnee Tribe</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State-recognized_tribe" title="State-recognized tribe">state-recognized tribe</a> in Alabama, recognized by the Alabama Indian Affairs Commission under the Davis-Strong Act;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> in an honorary manner by Ohio in Ohio Senate Resolution 188, adopted February 26, 1991, and by the Ohio House of Representatives 119th General Assembly Resolution No. 83, adopted April 3, 1991;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watson_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-watson-50">[50]</a></sup> and Kentucky, by Governor's Proclamation dated August 13, 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The Piqua Shawnee tribe performed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Corn_Dance" title="Green Corn Dance">Green Corn Dance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Gap_National_Historical_Park" title="Cumberland Gap National Historical Park">Cumberland Gap National Historical Park</a> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unrecognized_groups_who_claim_Shawnee_descent">Unrecognized groups who claim Shawnee descent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Unrecognized groups who claim Shawnee descent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Self identified groups that consider themselves Shawnee reside in Ohio and other states:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Chickamauga Keetoowah Unami Wolf Band of Cherokee Delaware Shawnee of Ohio, West Virginia, and Virginia</li>
<li>East of the River Shawnee, Ohio<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>Kispoko Sept of Ohio Shawnee, Louisiana</li>
<li>Kispoko Sept of Ohio Shawnee (Hog Creek Reservation), Ohio</li>
<li>Lower Eastern Ohio Mekoce Shawnee, Ohio Letter of Intent to Petition 3/5/2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaanativearts_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaanativearts-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lower Eastern Ohio Mekojay Shawnee, Ohio</li>
<li>Morning Star Shawnee Nation, Ohio</li>
<li>Piqua Sept of Ohio Shawnee Indians, Ohio</li>
<li>Platform Reservation Remnant Band of the Shawnee Nation <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>Shawnee Nation Blue Creek Band, of Adams County, Ohio. Letter of Intent to Petition 8/5/1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaanativearts_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-aaanativearts-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li>Piqua Shawnee Tribe / Piqua Sept of Ohio Shawnee Tribe—Letter of Intent to Petition 04/16/1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaanativearts_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-aaanativearts-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ridgetop_Shawnee" title="Ridgetop Shawnee">Ridgetop Shawnee</a>, Kentucky. In 2009 and 2010, the State House of the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_General_Assembly" title="Kentucky General Assembly">Kentucky General Assembly</a> recognized the Ridgetop Shawnee Tribe of Indians by passing House Joint Resolutions 15 or HJR-15 and HJR-16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
<li>Southeastern Kentucky Shawnee, Kentucky</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Remnant_Band_of_the_Shawnee_Nation" title="United Remnant Band of the Shawnee Nation">United Remnant Band of the Shawnee Nation</a>, Ohio<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>United Tribe of Shawnee Indians, Kansas</li>
<li>Upper Kispoko Band of the Shawnee Nation, Indiana</li>
<li>Vinyard Indian Settlement of Shawnee Indians, Illinois</li>
<li>Youghiogaheny River Band Of Shawnee Indians, Maryland</li>
</ul>
<p>These bands are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federally_recognized_tribes" title="Federally recognized tribes">federally recognized</a>. Neither Ohio or Kentucky have formal process for recognition of tribes, but its legislature has acknowledged some groups in an honorary way by resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watson_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-watson-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_Shawnee">Notable Shawnee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawnee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notable Shawnee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Chartier" title="Peter Chartier">Peter Chartier</a> (1690–1759), also known as <i>Wacanackshina</i>, French-Canadian-Shawnee who opposed the sale of alcohol in Shawnee communities and fought on the side of the French in the French and Indian War.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cornstalk" title="Cornstalk">Cornstalk</a> (1720–1777), led the Shawnee in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunmore%27s_War" title="Dunmore's War">Dunmore's War</a> of 1774.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nonhelema" title="Nonhelema">Nonhelema</a> (1720–1786), sister of Cornstalk, helped compile the dictionary for the <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_language" title="Shawnee language">Shawnee language</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blue_Jacket" title="Blue Jacket">Blue Jacket</a> (1743–1810), also known as <i>Weyapiersenwah</i>, a leader in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Indian_War" title="Northwest Indian War">Northwest Indian War</a> and important predecessor to Tecumseh.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Drouillard" title="George Drouillard">George Drouillard</a> (1773–1810), scout on Lewis and Clark expedition</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Black_Hoof" title="Black Hoof">Black Hoof</a> (1740–1831), also known as <i>Catecahassa</i>, respected Shawnee chief who believed his people needed to adapt to European-American culture to survive.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiksika" title="Chiksika">Chiksika</a> (1760–1792), <a href="/wiki/Kispoko" title="Kispoko">Kispoko</a> war chief and older brother of Tecumseh</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a> (1768–1813), Shawnee leader; with his brother <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">Tenskwatawa</a> attempted to unite tribes west of the Appalachians against the expansion of European-American settlement.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">Tenskwatawa</a> (1775–1836), Shawnee prophet and younger brother of Tecumseh</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Black_Bob_(Shawnee_chief)" title="Black Bob (Shawnee chief)">Black Bob</a>, 19th-century leader and war chief in Ohio.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Link_Wray" title="Link Wray">Link Wray</a> (1929–2005), rock and roll guitarist, songwriter and vocalist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nas%27Naga" title="Nas'Naga">Nas'Naga</a> (1941–2012), novelist and poet in United States.</li>
</ul>
