<p><b>Matsutarō Shōriki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">正力 松太郎</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shōriki Matsutarō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, April 11, 1885 – October 9, 1969)</span> was a Japanese journalist and media mogul, also known as the father of <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball_in_Japan" title="Professional baseball in Japan">Japanese professional baseball</a>.</p>
<p>Shōriki owned the <i><a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Shimbun" title="Yomiuri Shimbun">Yomiuri Shimbun</a></i>, one of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_newspapers" title="Japanese newspapers">Japan's major daily newspapers</a>, and founded Japan's first commercial television station, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nippon_Television" title="Nippon Television">Nippon Television Network Corporation</a>. He was also elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Japan" title="House of Representatives of Japan">House of Representatives</a>, appointed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_House_of_Peers" title="Japanese House of Peers">House of Peers</a>, and was one of the most successful <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a> masters ever, reaching the extremely rare rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dan_rank" title="Dan rank">10th Dan</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shōriki was born in <a href="/wiki/Daimon,_Toyama" title="Daimon, Toyama">Daimon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyama_Prefecture" title="Toyama Prefecture">Toyama</a>. He graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_Police">Metropolitan Police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Metropolitan Police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shōriki joined the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Metropolitan_Police_Department" title="Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department">Metropolitan Police</a>, rising high in the ranks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup> He was dismissed from the police after the <a href="/wiki/Toranomon_Incident" title="Toranomon Incident">Toranomon Incident</a> of late 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yomiuri_Shimbun"><i>Yomiuri Shimbun</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yomiuri Shimbun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1924, with the help of a powerful investor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup> he bought <i><a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Shimbun" title="Yomiuri Shimbun">Yomiuri Shimbun</a></i>. Shōriki's innovations included improved news coverage and a full-page radio program guide. The emphasis of the paper shifted to broad news coverage aimed at readers in the Tokyo area. By 1941 it had the largest circulation of any daily newspaper in the Tokyo area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball">Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shōriki organized a Japanese baseball <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All_Star_Team" title="All Star Team">All-Star team</a> in <a href="/wiki/1934_in_baseball" title="1934 in baseball">1934</a> that matched up against an American All-Star team. While prior Japanese all-star contingents had disbanded, Shōriki went pro with this group, which eventually became known as the <a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Giants" title="Yomiuri Giants">Yomiuri Giants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shōriki survived an assassination attempt by right-wing nationalists for allowing foreigners (in this case, Americans) to play baseball in <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Jingu_Stadium" title="Meiji Jingu Stadium">Jingu Stadium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup> He received a 16-inch-long scar from a broadsword during the assassination attempt.</p>
<p>Shōriki became <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Professional_Baseball" title="Nippon Professional Baseball">Nippon Professional Baseball</a>'s (NPB) unofficial first commissioner in <a href="/wiki/1949_in_baseball" title="1949 in baseball">1949</a>. In <a href="/wiki/1950_in_baseball" title="1950 in baseball">1950</a>, Shōriki oversaw the realignment of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_League" title="Japanese Baseball League">Japanese Baseball League</a> into its present two-league structure and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Series" title="Japan Series">Japan Series</a>. One goal Shōriki did not accomplish was a true world series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_controversy">World War II controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: World War II controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shōriki was classified as a "Class A" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_criminal" title="War criminal">war criminal</a> after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, serving 21 months in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup> However, he was released in 1947 after it was determined that the accusations against him were mostly of an “ideological and political nature”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_power">Nuclear power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nuclear power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1956, Shōriki became chairman of the newly created <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="Japanese Atomic Energy Commission">Japanese Atomic Energy Commission</a>, and in May of that year was appointed head of the brand-new Science and Technology Agency, both under the cabinet of <a href="/wiki/Ichir%C5%8D_Hatoyama" title="Ichirō Hatoyama">Ichirō Hatoyama</a> with strong support behind the scenes from the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Tetsuo Arima, a professor specialising in media studies at <a href="/wiki/Waseda_University" title="Waseda University">Waseda University</a> in Tokyo, published an article that claimed Shōriki acted as an agent under the codenames of "podam" and "pojacpot-1" for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> to establish a pro-US nationwide commercial <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> network (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nippon_Television_Network_Corporation" title="Nippon Television Network Corporation">NTV</a>) and to introduce <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> plants using U.S. technologies across Japan. Arima's accusations were based on the findings of de-classified documents stored in the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">NARA</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shōriki is thus also now known as "the father of nuclear power."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shōriki died October 9, 1969, in <a href="/wiki/Atami,_Shizuoka" title="Atami, Shizuoka">Atami, Shizuoka</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributes">Tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsutar%C5%8D_Sh%C5%8Driki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1959_in_baseball" title="1959 in baseball">1959</a>, Shōriki was the first inductee into the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame">Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Matsutaro_Shoriki_Award" title="Matsutaro Shoriki Award">Matsutaro Shoriki Award</a> is given annually to the person who contributes the most to Japanese baseball.</p>
<p>The position of Chair of the Department of Asia, Oceania, and Africa at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts, Boston</a> is also named after Shōriki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
