<p>A <b>chauffeur</b> is a person employed to drive a passenger motor vehicle, especially a <a href="/wiki/Luxury_vehicle" title="Luxury vehicle">luxury vehicle</a> such as a large <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a>. A woman employed to drive a passenger motor vehicle is a chauffeuse.</p>
<p>Originally, such drivers were often personal employees of the vehicle owner, but now in many cases specialist chauffeur service companies or individual drivers provide both driver and <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_for_hire" title="Vehicle for hire">vehicle for hire</a>, although there are service companies that just provide the driver.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chauffeur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>chauffeur</i> comes from the French term for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoker_(occupation)" title="Stoker (occupation)">stoker</a> because the earliest automobiles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> like their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroad</a> and sea vessel counterparts, were <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam-powered</a> and required the driver to stoke the engine. Early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">petrol</a>/gasoline-powered motor cars, before the advent of electric ignition, were ignited by 'hot tubes' in the cylinder head which had to be pre-heated before the engine would start. Hence the term <i>chauffeur</i> which, in this context, means something like "heater-upper". The chauffeur would prime the hot tubes at the start of a journey, after which the natural compression cycle of the engine would keep them at the correct temperature. The chauffeur also maintained the car, including routine maintenance and cleaning, and had to be a skilled mechanic to deal with breakdowns and <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tyre</a> punctures en route, which were very common in the earliest years of the automobile.</p>
<p>Only the very wealthy could afford the first automobiles, and they generally employed chauffeurs rather than driving themselves. A 1906 article in <i>The New York Times</i> reported that "...the chauffeur problem to-day is one of the most serious that the automobilist has to deal with.", and complained that "...young men of no particular ability, who have been earning from $10 to $12 a week, are suddenly elevated to salaried positions paying from $25 to $50..." and recommended the re-training of existing <a href="/wiki/Coachman" title="Coachman">coach drivers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chauffeur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the term may refer to anybody who drives for a living, it usually implies a driver of an elegant passenger vehicle such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse-drawn_carriage" title="Horse-drawn carriage">horse-drawn carriage</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)">luxury sedan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coach_(vehicle)" title="Coach (vehicle)">motor coach</a>, or especially a <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a>; those who operate buses or non-passenger vehicles are generally referred to as "drivers". In some countries, particularly developing nations where a ready supply of labor ensures that even the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle classes</a> can afford <a href="/wiki/Domestic_worker" title="Domestic worker">domestic staff</a> and among the wealthy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the chauffeur may simply be called the "driver".</p>
<p>People currently sometimes employ chauffeurs full-time to drive themselves in their own personal vehicles, yet there are also professional services offering limousines or rental cars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> driven by chauffeurs. This is very similar to but more luxurious than taking a <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxicab</a>. A variety of benefits are cited for using chauffeurs, including convenience, productivity and time savings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and driving safety for businesspeople and seniors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_insurance" title="Vehicle insurance">Insurance</a> costs for luxury vehicles are often lower if the designated driver is a chauffeur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The legal requirements to be a chauffeur vary depending on the local jurisdiction and class of vehicle. In some cases, a simple permit is all that is required, but in others an additional professional license with certain minimum standards in areas such as: age, health, driving experience, criminal record, local geographic knowledge,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> training attended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chauffeur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to the minimum legal requirements, limousine companies often require their chauffeurs to undergo specific extra training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These courses may involve <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/evasive" title="wikt:evasive">evasive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Defensive_driving" title="Defensive driving">defensive driving techniques</a>, the proper methods to ensure safety in the most extreme conditions such as inclement weather, a flat tire at high speeds, or other exterior influences for loss of vehicular control, etc. Most companies also have their own courses as to what they expect from their chauffeurs. Chauffeurs may be taught proper <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a> for use when they are in presence of their <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Client" title="wikt:Client">clientele</a>. They may also be trained for services to the client beyond the car itself, such as for a personal <a href="/wiki/Valet" title="Valet">valet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bodyguard" title="Bodyguard">bodyguard</a>. Many companies and local licensing agencies currently require random <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_screening" title="Drug screening">drug screening</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> – in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> this was especially the case after professional <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> player <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Konstantinov" title="Vladimir Konstantinov">Vladimir Konstantinov</a>'s career-ending injuries when his recently hired chauffeur, Richard Gnida, already serving a license suspension for <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">drunken driving</a>, lost control of their limousine and crashed, seriously injuring Konstantinov and his other passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dress">Dress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chauffeur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many places (or at times in the past), proper physical presence is presented by the chauffeur at all times. This usually includes a well-groomed individual, conservatively dressed in a clean and crisply pressed black or dark <a href="/wiki/Suit_(clothing)" title="Suit (clothing)">suit</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tuxedo" title="Tuxedo">tuxedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dress_shirt" title="Dress shirt">dress shirt</a>, and appropriately matching tie, with black leather <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloves" title="Gloves">gloves</a> and freshly polished matching footwear. In some areas, such as Japan, white gloves are the norm. Some companies have complete uniforms for their chauffeurs, and some require that hats be worn as part of the uniform. Some companies do not keep strictly to this standard, and there is wide variation globally throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation_industry" title="Transportation industry">transportation industry</a>.</p>
