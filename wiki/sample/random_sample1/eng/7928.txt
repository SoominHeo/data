<p><b>Shawn Fanning</b> (born November 22, 1980) is an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" title="Computer programmer">computer programmer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Angel_investor" title="Angel investor">angel investor</a>. He developed <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a>, one of the first popular <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">peer-to-peer</a> ("P2P") file sharing platforms, in 1999. The popularity of Napster was widespread and Fanning was featured on the cover of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_magazine" title="Time magazine"><i>Time</i> magazine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shawn_Fanning_on_cover_of_October_2.2C_2000_issue_of_Time_magazine_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shawn_Fanning_on_cover_of_October_2.2C_2000_issue_of_Time_magazine-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site in its initial free P2P incarnation was shut down in 2001 after the company's unsuccessful appeal of court orders arising from its encouraging the illegal sharing of copyrighted material. A paid subscription version of the site followed, and was purchased by Rhapsody on December 1, 2011. Following his involvement with Napster, he joined, and invested in, a number of early-stage technology <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startup companies</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_career">Computer career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Computer career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Napster">Napster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Napster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 1, 1999, Fanning released a preliminary beta program of Napster and soon, hundreds of college students at Northeastern were trading music furiously.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="opinion (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snocap">Snocap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Snocap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Fanning was named to the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technology_Review" title="Technology Review">Technology Review</a> <a href="/wiki/TR35" title="TR35">TR100</a> as one of the top 100 innovators in the world under the age of 35.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2003, he opened a new company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snocap" title="Snocap">Snocap</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Mendelson" title="Jordan Mendelson">Jordan Mendelson</a> (Napster's Chief Architect), and <a href="/wiki/Ron_Conway" title="Ron Conway">Ron Conway</a>. The company aspired to be a legitimate marketplace for <a href="/wiki/Digital_media" title="Digital media">digital media</a>. However, their partners and the public did not respond well. Customer support was poor, and technical issues were numerous. One of their primary partners, <a href="/wiki/CD_Baby" title="CD Baby">CD Baby</a>, wrote a scathing account of their relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In late 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snocap" title="Snocap">Snocap</a> laid off 60% of its workforce. ValleyWag wrote an article that Fanning had long left Snocap and began to work on another venture, <a href="/wiki/Rupture_(social_networking)" title="Rupture (social networking)">Rupture</a>. The ValleyWag article stated that the failure was largely due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snocap" title="Snocap">Snocap</a>'s CEO Rusty Rueff and that of former CTO Dave Rowley, who "made a mess of engineering before he was fired".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snocap" title="Snocap">Snocap</a> was looking to sell itself and fast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2008, they found a buyer; <a href="/wiki/Imeem" title="Imeem">imeem</a> acquired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snocap" title="Snocap">Snocap</a> in a fire sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rupture">Rupture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rupture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rupture project was announced in 2007 with seed funding, and CrunchBase notes the date Shawn officially became CEO of Rupture was October 2, 2007.</p>
<p>In December 2006, Fanning developed <a href="/wiki/Rupture_(social_networking)" title="Rupture (social networking)">Rupture</a>, a social networking tool designed to handle the task of publishing gamers' individual profiles to a communal space and facilitating communication between <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i> players. Rupture was later acquired by <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a> for $15 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Fanning's career at Electronic Arts was short-lived as a round of layoffs in November 2009 included him and his team at Rupture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Path">Path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few months after Fanning was laid off from Electronic Arts, he started a new company called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Path.com" title="Path.com">Path.com</a>. In January 2010, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Morin" title="Dave Morin">Dave Morin</a> announced he was leaving <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, where he was a Senior Platform Manager, to join Fanning and become CEO at Path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airtime">Airtime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Airtime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011 Fanning reunited with Napster cofounder <a href="/wiki/Sean_Parker" title="Sean Parker">Sean Parker</a> to found <a href="/wiki/Airtime.com" title="Airtime.com">Airtime.com</a>. Some of the investors are <a href="/wiki/Ron_Conway" title="Ron Conway">Ron Conway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Arrington" title="Michael Arrington">Michael Arrington</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ashton_Kutcher" title="Ashton Kutcher">Ashton Kutcher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Fanning serves as CEO and Parker as executive chairman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Airtime launched in June 2012 at a disastrous public event where Parker and Fanning paid huge amounts of money to have celebrities present but the product repeatedly crashed and ultimately failed to work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Criticism was widespread and harsh on Twitter and other social media while numerous news outlets covered the fail with snarky comments such as CNET's "To launch his new start-up, Sean Parker should have spent less of his billions on celebrity guests and more of it on fixing his technology," <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shawn_Fanning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, Fanning appeared as a presenter at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="MTV Video Music Awards">MTV Video Music Awards</a>. He appeared wearing a <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a> T-shirt with "<a href="/wiki/For_Whom_the_Bell_Tolls_(Metallica_song)" title="For Whom the Bell Tolls (Metallica song)">For Whom the Bell Tolls</a>" playing in the background, and when asked where he got it, stated, "a friend of mine shared it with me." <a href="/wiki/Lars_Ulrich" title="Lars Ulrich">Lars Ulrich</a>'s reaction was shown as feigned boredom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2000, Fanning was featured on the cover of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_magazine" title="Time magazine">Time</a></i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Fanning had a <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearance</a>, playing himself, in the 2003 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Italian_Job_(2003_film)" title="The Italian Job (2003 film)">The Italian Job</a></i>. In the film, <a href="/wiki/Seth_Green" title="Seth Green">Seth Green</a>'s character Lyle accused Fanning of stealing Napster from him while he was taking a nap in their Northeastern University dorm room. Although other characters see this as mere bragging, a scene shows Fanning in fact creeping over Lyle's sleeping body and stealing a 3-1/2" floppy disk.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In early 2008, Fanning appeared in a <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> commercial directed by <a href="/wiki/Roman_Coppola" title="Roman Coppola">Roman Coppola</a>, in which he poked fun at his file-sharing past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fanning and his site Napster were the subject of <a href="/wiki/Alex_Winter" title="Alex Winter">Alex Winter</a>'s documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Downloaded_(film)" title="Downloaded (film)">Downloaded</a></i> in 2013.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
