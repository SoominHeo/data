<p>The <b>Mk/B53</b> was a high-yield <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_bunker_buster" title="Nuclear bunker buster">bunker buster</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear weapon</a> developed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. Deployed on <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a> bombers, the B53, with a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_yield" title="Nuclear weapon yield">yield</a> of 9 megatons, was the most powerful weapon in the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_and_the_United_States#United_States_strategic_nuclear_weapons_arsenal" title="Nuclear weapons and the United States">U.S. nuclear arsenal</a> after the last <a href="/wiki/B41_nuclear_bomb" title="B41 nuclear bomb">B41 nuclear bombs</a> were retired in 1976.</p>
<p>The B53 was the basis of the <b>W-53 warhead</b> carried by the <a href="/wiki/LGM-25C_Titan_II" title="LGM-25C Titan II">Titan II Missile</a>, which was decommissioned in 1987. Although not in active service for many years before 2010, fifty B53s were retained during that time as part of the "Hedge" portion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Enduring_Stockpile" title="Enduring Stockpile">Enduring Stockpile</a> until its complete dismantling in 2011. The last B53 was disassembled on 25 October 2011, a year ahead of schedule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>With its retirement, the largest bomb currently in service in the U.S. nuclear arsenal is the <a href="/wiki/B83_nuclear_bomb" title="B83 nuclear bomb">B83</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Variable_yield" title="Variable yield">maximum yield</a> of 1.2 megatons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The B53 was replaced in the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_bunker_buster" title="Nuclear bunker buster">bunker-busting</a> role by a variant of the two-stage <a href="/wiki/B61_nuclear_bomb" title="B61 nuclear bomb">B61 nuclear bomb</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B53_nuclear_bomb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HardtackOak.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="453" data-file-width="640" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/HardtackOak.JPG/220px-HardtackOak.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/HardtackOak.JPG/330px-HardtackOak.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/HardtackOak.JPG/440px-HardtackOak.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HardtackOak.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hardtack Oak nuclear weapon test.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Development of the weapon began in 1955 by <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a>, based on the earlier <a href="/wiki/Mark_21_nuclear_bomb" title="Mark 21 nuclear bomb">Mk 21</a> and <a href="/wiki/B46_nuclear_bomb" title="B46 nuclear bomb">Mk 46</a> weapons. In March 1958 the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a> issued a request for a new <b>Class C</b> (less than five tons, megaton-range) bomb to replace the earlier <a href="/wiki/B41_nuclear_bomb" title="B41 nuclear bomb">Mk 41</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen162_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-hansen162-2">[2]</a></sup> A revised version of the <b>Mk 46</b> became the <b>TX-53</b> in 1959. The development TX-53 warhead was apparently never tested, although an experimental TX-46 predecessor design was detonated 28 June 1958 as <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hardtack_I" title="Operation Hardtack I">Hardtack</a> Oak, which detonated at a yield of 8.9 <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">Megatons</a>.</p>
<p>The <b>Mk 53</b> entered production in 1962 and was built through June 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen162_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-hansen162-2">[2]</a></sup> About 340 bombs were built. It entered service aboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-47_Stratojet" title="B-47 Stratojet">B-47 Stratojet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" title="B-52 Stratofortress">B-52G Stratofortress</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucdatabook58_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-nucdatabook58-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-58_Hustler" title="B-58 Hustler">B-58 Hustler</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomber_aircraft" title="Bomber aircraft">bomber aircraft</a> in the mid-1960s. From 1968 it was redesignated <b>B53</b>.</p>
<p>Some early versions of the bomb were dismantled beginning in 1967. About 50 bomb and 54 <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan</a> warhead versions were in service through 1980. After the Titan II program ended, the remaining W-53s were retired in the late 1980s. The B53 was also intended to be retired in the 1980s, but 50 units remained in the active stockpile until the deployment of the <a href="/wiki/B61_nuclear_bomb" title="B61 nuclear bomb">B61-11</a> in 1997. At that point the obsolete B53s were slated for immediate disassembly; however, the process of disassembling the units was greatly hampered by safety concerns as well as a lack of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmw_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mmw-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2010 authorization was given to disassemble the 50 bombs at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pantex" title="Pantex">Pantex</a> plant in Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP2010-8">[8]</a></sup> The process of dismantling the last remaining B53 bomb in the stockpile commenced on 25 October 2011 and was completed soon afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications">Specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B53_nuclear_bomb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The B53 was 12 feet 4 inches (3.76 m) long with a diameter of 50 inches (4.17 ft; 1.27 m). It weighed 8,850 pounds (4,010 kg), including the 800-to-900 lb (360-to-410 kg) <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a> system and the honeycomb aluminum <a href="/wiki/Nose_cone" title="Nose cone">nose cone</a> to enable the bomb to survive <a href="/wiki/Laydown_delivery" title="Laydown delivery">laydown delivery</a>. It had five parachutes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucdatabook58_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-nucdatabook58-1">[1]</a></sup> one 5-foot (1.52 m) <a href="/wiki/Pilot_chute" title="Pilot chute">pilot chute</a>, one 16-foot (4.88 m) extractor chute, and three 48-foot (14.63 m) main chutes. Chute deployment depends on delivery mode, with the main chutes used only for laydown delivery. For free-fall delivery, the entire system was jettisoned.</p>
<p>The warhead of the B53 used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oralloy" title="Oralloy">oralloy</a> (highly enriched <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>) instead of <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> for fission, with a mix of <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>-6 <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuteride</a> fuel for fusion. The <a href="/wiki/Explosive_lens" title="Explosive lens">explosive lens</a> comprised a mixture of <a href="/wiki/RDX" title="RDX">RDX</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a>, which was not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insensitive_munitions" title="Insensitive munitions">insensitive</a>. Two variants were made: the <b>B53-Y1</b>, a "dirty" weapon using a U-238-encased secondary, and the <b>B53-Y2</b> "clean" version with a non-fissile (<a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>) secondary casing. Explosive yield was approximately nine <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">megatons</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B53_nuclear_bomb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was intended as a <a href="/wiki/Bunker_buster" title="Bunker buster">bunker buster</a> weapon, using a surface blast after laydown deployment to transmit a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> through the earth to collapse its target. Attacks against the Soviet deep underground leadership shelters in the <a href="/wiki/Chekhov,_Moscow_Oblast" title="Chekhov, Moscow Oblast">Chekhov</a>/<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sharapovo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sharapovo (page does not exist)">Sharapovo</a> area south of Moscow envisaged multiple B53/W53 exploding at ground level. It has since been supplanted in such roles by the earth-penetrating <a href="/wiki/B61_nuclear_bomb" title="B61 nuclear bomb">B61 Mod 11</a>, a bomb that penetrates the surface to deliver much more of its explosive energy into the ground, and therefore needs a much smaller yield to produce the same effects.</p>
<p>The B53 was intended to be retired in the 1980s, but 50 units remained in the active stockpile until the deployment of the B61-11 in 1997. At that point the obsolete B53s were slated for immediate disassembly; however, the process of disassembling the units was greatly hampered by safety concerns as well as a lack of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmw_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-mmw-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP2010_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-WP2010-8">[8]</a></sup> The last remaining B53 bomb began the disassembly processes on Tuesday, 25 October 2011 at the Energy Department’s <a href="/wiki/Pantex_Plant" title="Pantex Plant">Pantex Plant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-wired-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>An April 2014 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GAO" title="GAO">GAO</a> report notes that the <a href="/wiki/National_Nuclear_Security_Administration" title="National Nuclear Security Administration">NNSA</a> is retaining canned subassemblies (CSAs) " associated with a certain warhead indicated as excess in the 2012 Production and Planning Directive are being retained in an indeterminate state pending a senior-level government evaluation of their use in planetary defense against earthbound asteroids."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In its FY2015 budget request, the NNSA noted that the B53 component disassembly was "delayed", leading some observers to conclude they might be the warhead CSAs being retained for potential planetary defense purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="W53">W53</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B53_nuclear_bomb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: W53">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:W53_nuclear_bomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="239" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/W53_nuclear_bomb.jpg/220px-W53_nuclear_bomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/W53_nuclear_bomb.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:W53_nuclear_bomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
W53 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_package" title="Physics package">physics package</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>W53</b> warhead of the <a href="/wiki/LGM-25C_Titan_II" title="LGM-25C Titan II">Titan II</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBM</a> used the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_package" title="Physics package">physics package</a> as the B53, without the air drop-specific components like the parachute system and crushable structures in the nose and sides needed for lay-down delivery, reducing its mass to about 6,200 lb (2,800 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucdatabook59_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nucdatabook59-12">[12]</a></sup> The 8,140-pound (3,690 kg) Mark-6 re-entry vehicle containing the W53 warhead was about 123 inches (10.3 ft; 3.1 m) long, 7.5 feet (2.3 m) in diameter and was mounted atop a spacer which was 8.3 feet (2.5 m) in diameter at the missile interface (compared to the missile's core diameter of 10 feet [3.0 m]). With a yield of 9 <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">megatons</a>, it was the highest yield warhead ever deployed on a US missile. About 65 W53 warheads were constructed between December 1962 and December 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucdatabook59_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-nucdatabook59-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 September 1980 a fuel leak caused a Titan II to explode within its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_silo" title="Missile silo">silo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, throwing the W53 warhead some distance away. Due to the safety measures built into the weapon, it did not explode or release any radioactive material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> 52 active missiles were deployed in silos prior to the beginning of the retirement program in October 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucdatabook59_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-nucdatabook59-12">[12]</a></sup> With the retirement of the Titan fleet, disassembly of the W53 warheads was completed by about 1988.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B53_nuclear_bomb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg/220px-AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg/330px-AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg/440px-AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AtomicTestingMuseumB53nuclearbomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
B53 on display at the Atomic Testing Museum</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Assuming a detonation at optimum height, a 9 megaton blast would result in a fireball with an approximate 2.9 to 3.4 mi (4.7 to 5.5 km) diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The radiated heat would be sufficient to cause lethal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burn_(injury)" title="Burn (injury)">burns</a> to any unprotected person within a 20-mile (32 km) radius (1,250 sq mi or 3,200 km<sup>2</sup>). Blast effects would be sufficient to collapse most residential and industrial structures within a 9 mi (14 km) radius (254 sq mi or 660 km<sup>2</sup>); within 3.65 mi (5.87 km) (42 sq mi or 110 km<sup>2</sup>) virtually all above-ground structures would be destroyed and blast effects would inflict near 100% fatalities. Within 2.25 mi (3.62 km) a 500 rem dose of <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a> would be received by the average person, sufficient to cause a 50% to 90% casualty rate independent of thermal or blast effects at this distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
