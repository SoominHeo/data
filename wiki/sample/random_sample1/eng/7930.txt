<p>The <b>Schomburgk's deer</b> (<i>Rucervus schomburgki</i>) was a member of the family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervidae" title="Cervidae">Cervidae</a>. Native to <a href="/wiki/Central_Thailand" title="Central Thailand">central Thailand</a>, Schomburgk's deer was described by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Blyth" title="Edward Blyth">Edward Blyth</a> in 1863 and named after <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hermann_Schomburgk" title="Robert Hermann Schomburgk">Sir Robert H. Schomburgk</a>, who was the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> consul in <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> from 1857 to 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> It is thought to have gone extinct by 1938, but there is speculation that the deer might still exist.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburgk%27s_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cervus_schomburgki.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="725" data-file-width="697" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cervus_schomburgki.jpg/170px-Cervus_schomburgki.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cervus_schomburgki.jpg/255px-Cervus_schomburgki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cervus_schomburgki.jpg/340px-Cervus_schomburgki.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>This deer was a graceful species similar in appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Barasingha" title="Barasingha">barasingha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> The pelt was a dark brown with lighter underparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> The underside of the tail was white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> Males possessed basket-like antlers, upon which all the main tines branched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> This caused the deer to have up to 33 points on their antlers and the outer edge of the rack to be up to 35 inches (90 cm) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> Females had no antlers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburgk%27s_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schomburgk's deer inhabited swampy plains with long grass, cane, and shrubs in central Thailand, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River" title="Chao Phraya River">Chao Phraya River</a> valley near Bangkok.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> This deer avoided dense vegetation. They lived in herds that consisted of a single adult male, a few females, and their young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> However, during the flooding that occurred during the rainy season, the herds were forced together upon higher pieces of land which could turn into islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> This made them easy targets for hunters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction">Extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburgk%27s_deer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Commercial production of rice for export began in the late-19th century in Thailand, leading to the loss of nearly all grassland and swamp areas on which this deer depended. Intensive hunting pressure at the turn of the century restricted the species further until it became extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wild population of Schomburgk’s deer is thought to have died because of overhunting in 1932, with the last captive individual being killed in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup> The species was listed as extinct in the 2006 <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List of Threatened Species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-3">[3]</a></sup> However, some scientists consider this species to be still extant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Only one mounted specimen is known to be in existence, which currently resides in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_national_d%27Histoire_naturelle" title="Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle">Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle</a> after living in the zoo there until 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EllisNTB_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-EllisNTB-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Antler" title="Antler">antlers</a> were discovered in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_medicine" title="Chinese medicine">Chinese medicine</a> shop in <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>. Laurent Chazée, an <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomist</a> with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, later identified the antlers from a photograph he took as coming from Schomburgk's deer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
