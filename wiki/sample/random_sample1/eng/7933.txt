<p><b>Scholz's Star</b> (WISE designation <b>WISE 0720−0846</b> or fully <b>WISE J072003.20−084651.2</b>) is a dim <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary stellar system</a> about 17–23 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (5.1–7.2 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a>) from the Sun in the southern <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Monoceros" title="Monoceros">Monoceros</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_plane" title="Galactic plane">galactic plane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> It was discovered in 2013 by astronomer Ralf-Dieter Scholz. In 2015, Eric Mamajek and collaborators reported the system passed through the Solar System's <a href="/wiki/Oort_cloud" title="Oort cloud">Oort cloud</a> roughly 70,000 years ago <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup>, and dubbed it <b>Scholz's Star</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholz%27s_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary is a <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classification</a> of M<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000900000000000000♠</span>9<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>1</span> and has <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001860000000000000♠</span>86<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>2</span> <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass">Jupiter masses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The secondary is probably a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf#Spectral_class_T" title="Brown dwarf">T5 brown dwarf</a> with <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001650000000000000♠</span>65<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>12</span> Jupiter masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The system has 0.15 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The pair orbit at a distance of about 0.8 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a> (120,000,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometers</a>; 74,000,000 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> with a period of roughly 4 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgasser2015b_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burgasser2015b-6">[6]</a></sup> The system has an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of 18.3,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> and is estimated to be between 3 and 10 billion years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> With a parallax of 166 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_of_arc" title="Second of arc">mas</a> (0.166 arcseconds), there are about 80 known star systems closer to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recon2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recon2012-7">[7]</a></sup> It is a late discovery, as far as nearby stars go, because past efforts concentrated on high-proper-motion objects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_System_flyby">Solar System flyby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholz%27s_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar System flyby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Estimates indicate that the WISE 0720−0846 system passed about 52,000 astronomical units (0.25 parsecs; 0.82 light-years) from the Sun about 70,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily-2015-02-17_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-2015-02-17-5">[5]</a></sup> 98% of mathematical simulations of the star system's trajectory indicated it passed through the Solar System's Oort cloud, or within 120,000 AU (0.58 pc; 1.9 ly) of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-19"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> Comets <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbed</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Oort_cloud" title="Oort cloud">Oort cloud</a> would require roughly 2 million years to get to the inner Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-20"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup> At closest approach the system would have had an apparent magnitude of about 11.4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-4">[4]</a></sup> A star is expected to pass through the Oort Cloud every 100,000 years or so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-4">[4]</a></sup> An approach as close or closer than 52,000 AU is expected to occur about every 9 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-21"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholz%27s_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The star was first discovered to be a nearby one by astronomer Ralf-Dieter Scholz,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily-2015-02-17_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-2015-02-17-5">[5]</a></sup> announced on <a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a> in November 2013. Given the importance of the system having passed so close to the solar system in prehistorical times, Eric Mamajek and collaborators dubbed the system <b>Scholz's star</b> in their paper discussing the star's velocity and past trajectory<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mamajek2015_2-22"><a href="#cite_note-Mamajek2015-2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>
