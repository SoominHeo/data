<p>The <b>Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Research_library" title="Research library">research library</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a> (NYPL) and an archive repository for information on people of African descent worldwide. Located at 515 <a href="/wiki/Lenox_Avenue" title="Lenox Avenue">Malcolm X Boulevard</a> (Lenox Avenue) between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/135th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="135th Street (Manhattan)">West 135th</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/136th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="136th Street (Manhattan)">136th</a> Streets in the <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, it has, almost from its inception, been an integral part of the Harlem community. It is named for Afro-Puerto Rican scholar <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Alfonso_Schomburg" title="Arturo Alfonso Schomburg">Arturo Alfonso Schomburg</a>.</p>
<p>The resources of the Center are broken up into five divisions, the Art and Artifacts Division, the Jean Blackwell Hutson General Research and Reference Division, the Manuscripts, Archives and Rare Books Division, the Moving Image and Recorded Sound Division, and the Photographs and Prints Division.</p>
<p>In addition to research services, the center hosts readings, discussions, art exhibitions, and theatrical events. It is open to the general public.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="135th_Street_branch">135th Street branch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 135th Street branch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1901, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a> tentatively agreed to donate $5,200,000 (equivalent to $149,697,600 in 2016) to construct 65 <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_library" title="Carnegie library">branch libraries</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, with the requirement that the City provide the land and maintain the buildings once construction was complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Later in 1901 Carnegie formally signed a contract with the City of New York to transfer his donation to the city to then allow it to justify purchasing the land to house the libraries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/McKim,_Mead_%26_White" title="McKim, Mead &amp; White">McKim, Mead &amp; White</a> were chosen as the architects and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Follen_McKim" title="Charles Follen McKim">Charles Follen McKim</a> designed the three-story library building at 103 West 135th Street in the Italian Renaissance Palazzo mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At its opening on July 14, 1905, the library had 10,000 books<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYAN0714_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYAN0714-4">[4]</a></sup> and the librarian in charge was Gertrude Cohen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1920, <a href="/wiki/Ernestine_Rose_(librarian)" title="Ernestine Rose (librarian)">Ernestine Rose</a>, a white woman born in <a href="/wiki/Bridgehampton,_New_York" title="Bridgehampton, New York">Bridgehampton</a> in 1880, became the branch librarian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She quickly integrated the all-white library staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tibbets:_20-21_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tibbets:_20-21-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Allen_Latimer" title="Catherine Allen Latimer">Catherine Allen Latimer</a>, the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> librarian hired by the NYPL, was sent to work with Rose as was Roberta Bosely months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some time later <a href="/wiki/Sadie_Peterson_Delaney" title="Sadie Peterson Delaney">Sadie Peterson Delaney</a> became employed at the branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan31_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan31-9">[9]</a></sup> Together, they created a plan to assist integrating reading into the lives of the library attendees and cooperated with schools and social organizations in the community.</p>
<p>In 1921, the library hosted the first exhibition of African-American art in Harlem; it became an annual event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The library became a focal point to the burgeoning <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">Harlem Renaissance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tibbets:_20-21_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tibbets:_20-21-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1923, the 135th Street branch was the only branch in New York City employing Negroes as librarians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and consequently when <a href="/wiki/Regina_M._Anderson" title="Regina M. Anderson">Regina M. Anderson</a> was hired by the NYPL, she was sent to work at the 135th Street branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rose issued a report to the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a>, in 1923, which stated that requests for books about Negroes or written by Negroes had been increasing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and that the demand for professionally trained colored librarians was also.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In late 1924, Rose called a meeting, with attendees including <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Alfonso_Schomburg" title="Arturo Alfonso Schomburg">Arturo Alfonso Schomburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Weldon_Johnson" title="James Weldon Johnson">James Weldon Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Harrison" title="Hubert Harrison">Hubert Harrison</a>, that decided to focus on preserving rare books, and solicit donations to enhance its African-American collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On May 8, 1925, it began operating as the <i>Division of Negro Literature, History and Prints</i>, a division of the NYPL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1926, Schomburg was interested in selling his collection of African-American literature because he wanted it to be available to the general public,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but he wanted the collection to stay in Harlem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Rose and the National Urban League convinced the Carnegie Foundation to pay $10,000 to Schomburg and then donate the books to the library. In 1926, the center's collection won acclaim with the addition of Schomburg's personal collection. By donating his collection, Schomburg sought to show that black people had a history and a culture and thus were not inferior to other races.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup> About 5,000 objects in Schomburg's collection were donated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1929, Anderson was desirous of a promotion and enlisted the help of <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois" title="W. E. B. Du Bois">W. E. B. Du Bois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Francis_White" title="Walter Francis White">Walter Francis White</a> when she was being discriminated against by not being promoted. After letters of intervention on her behalf by Du Bois and White, and a boycott of the library by White, Anderson was promoted and transferred to the Rivington Street branch of the NYPL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1930, the Center had 18,000 volumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYAN0714_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYAN0714-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1932, Schomburg became the first curator of his collection, until his death in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1935, the Center developed a project to deliver books once a week to those handicapped severely enough that they could not make it to the library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Dr. <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_D._Reddick" title="Lawrence D. Reddick">Lawrence D. Reddick</a> became the second curator of the Schomburg Collection of Negro Literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> At the behest of Reddick, in October 1940, the entire Division of Negro History, Literature and Prints was renamed the Schomburg Collection of Negro History and Literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1942 Rose retired<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> after an extension was built onto the rear of the building, at a time when the library had 40,000 books. Dorothy Robinson Homer replaced her as Branch Librarian, after the Citizen's Committee of the 135th Street Branch Library specifically requested a Negro to replace Rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Countee_Cullen_branch">Countee Cullen branch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Countee Cullen branch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the extension was built, the library became known as the Countee Cullen Library,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and the 135th Street Library is still considered the original location of the Countee Cullen library by the New York Public Library,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> although that name is now only used for the extension itself on West 136th Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Homers created a room of books just for young adults and created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Negro_Theatre" title="American Negro Theatre">American Negro Theatre</a> in the basement that spawned the play <a href="/wiki/Anna_Lucasta_(play)" title="Anna Lucasta (play)">Anna Lucasta</a>, which was moved to Broadway. She kept the emphasis on building a community center for art, music and drama. She put on art exhibits that favored unknown, young artists of all races.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the outbreak of WWII, Homer started a program of monthly concert recitals in the auditorium to enhance public spirit, but the demand by performers and audience members to continue the practice made it permanent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hutson_tenure_.281948.E2.80.9380.29">Hutson tenure (1948–80)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hutson tenure (1948–80)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1948, Jean Blackwell, later <a href="/wiki/Jean_Blackwell_Hutson" title="Jean Blackwell Hutson">Jean Blackwell Hutson</a>, was named the director of the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In a 1966 speech, Hutson warned of the perilous status of the Schomburg collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, the Center began being supported by the privately funded Schomburg Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA19801011_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA19801011-35">[35]</a></sup> The next year, funds by New York City were allocated to renovate the building at 103 West 135th,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and it was renamed the building of the <i>Schomburg Collection for Research in Black Culture</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Simultaneously, the entire Schomburg collection was rounded up from various branch libraries and transferred to the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 1972, it was designated as one of NYPL's research libraries.</p>
<p>In 1973, a building on the west side of Lenox ave between 135th and 136th was bought so that it could be demolished and a new building could be constructed. The location was chosen due to its proximity to other community agencies and because it was the "scene of the Harlem Renaissance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 1978, the building on 135th Street between Lenox and 7th Avenues was entered into the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> (NRHP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 1979, it was formally listed in the NRHP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schomburg_Center">Schomburg Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Schomburg Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The entrance to the Center</div>
</div>

<p>In 1980, a new Schomburg Center was founded at 515 <a href="/wiki/Lenox_Avenue" title="Lenox Avenue">Lenox Avenue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA19801011_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-AA19801011-35">[35]</a></sup> In 1981, the original building on West 135th Street which held the Schomburg Collection was designated a New York City Landmark.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wray_tenure_.281981.E2.80.9383.29">Wray tenure (1981–83)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wray tenure (1981–83)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1981, Wendell L. Wray became the director of the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT19830310_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT19830310-42">[42]</a></sup> Protests began over Wray's decision to not hire an African-American man to head the Center's Manuscript, Archives, and Rare Books Division and instead hired Robert Morris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 1983, Wray resigned to pursue academic research<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT19830310_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT19830310-42">[42]</a></sup> and Catherine Hooker was named acting director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dodson_tenure_.281984.E2.80.932011.29">Dodson tenure (1984–2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dodson tenure (1984–2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Howard_Dodson" title="Howard Dodson">Howard Dodson</a> became the director in 1984, at a time when the Schomburg was primarily a cultural center visited by tourists and schoolchildren and its research facilities were known only to scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1984, the Schomburg's collection was at 5 million. In 1984 attendance was 40,000 a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT1117_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1117-45">[45]</a></sup> As early as 1984, the Schomburg was recognized as the most important institution in the world for collections of art and literature of people in Africa or its diaspora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In 1983, a scholars-in-residence program started at the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In 1986, an exhibit entitled <i>Give me your poor...</i> sparked controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In March 1987, a public funding campaign was started to raise money to renovate the old library and to enhance the new Center's housing and its functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1991, completion of additions to the Schomburg Center were completed. The new center on Malcolm X was expanded to include an auditorium and a connection to the old landmark building on 135th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The <i>Art and Artifacts Division</i> and the <i>Moving Image and Recorded Sound Division</i> were moved into the old landmark building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In 2000, the Schomburg Center held an exhibition titled "Lest We Forget: The Triumph Over Slavery", which later went on tour around the world for more than a decade under the sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Slave_Route_Project" title="The Slave Route Project">Slave Route Project</a>. In 2005, the center held an exhibition of letters, photographs and other material related to Malcolm X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2007, the building was renovated and expanded in an $11 million project. The Schomburg Center had 120,000 visitors a year, by 2010, and Dodson announced he would retire in early 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, the Schomburg Center was one of the sponsors of the <a href="/wiki/African_Burial_Ground_National_Monument" title="African Burial Ground National Monument">African Burial Ground National Monument</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muhammad_tenure_.282011.E2.80.93present.29">Muhammad tenure (2011–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Muhammad tenure (2011–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following Howard Dodson's announcement of his retirement in 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Khalil_Gibran_Muhammad" title="Khalil Gibran Muhammad">Khalil Gibran Muhammad</a>, great-grandson of <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Muhammad" title="Elijah Muhammad">Elijah Muhammad</a> and professor of history at <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">Indiana University</a>, was announced as Dodson's replacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT1117_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1117-45">[45]</a></sup> In the summer of 2011, Muhammad became the fifth director of the Schomburg. His stated goals were for the Schomburg to be a focal point for young adults and to collaborate with the local community, to not only reinforce its pride, but also for the Center to be a gateway for revealing the history of Black people worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA0915_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA0915-53">[53]</a></sup> In July, the Center began an exhibit of Malcolm X footage and prints entitled <i>Malcolm X: the Search for Truth</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Young_tenure_.28beginning_autumn_2016.29">Young tenure (beginning autumn 2016)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Young tenure (beginning autumn 2016)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 1, 2016, the New York Public Library announced that poet and academic <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Young_(poet)" title="Kevin Young (poet)">Kevin Young</a> would begin as the Director in the "late fall" of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schomburg_Center_for_Research_in_Black_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="4200" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg/220px-NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg/330px-NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg/440px-NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NYPL_Schomburg_logo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
NYPL Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture logo</div>
</div>

<p>The Schomburg Collection was considered, in 1998, as consisting of the rarest, and most useful, <a href="/wiki/Afrocentrism" title="Afrocentrism">Afrocentric</a> artifacts of any public library in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> At least as late of 2006, it is viewed as the most prestigious for African-American materials in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> As of 2010, the Collection stood at 10 million objects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup> The Center contains a signed, first edition of a book of poems by <a href="/wiki/Phillis_Wheatley" title="Phillis Wheatley">Phillis Wheatley</a>, archival material of <a href="/wiki/Melville_J._Herskovits" title="Melville J. Herskovits">Melville J. Herskovits</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Henrik_Clarke" title="John Henrik Clarke">John Henrik Clarke</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_Hansberry" title="Lorraine Hansberry">Lorraine Hansberry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">Nat King Cole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup> The collection includes the files, or papers of the <a href="/wiki/International_Labor_Defense" title="International Labor Defense">International Labor Defense</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Congress" title="Civil Rights Congress">Civil Rights Congress</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Negro_Congress" title="National Negro Congress">National Negro Congress</a>. It also includes the papers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Brown_(pianist)" title="Lawrence Brown (pianist)">Lawrence Brown</a> (1893-1973),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bunche" title="Ralph Bunche">Ralph Bunche</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Damas" title="Léon Damas">Léon Damas</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Pickens" title="William Pickens">William Pickens</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Rhodes_Revels" title="Hiram Rhodes Revels">Hiram Rhodes Revels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Cameron_White" title="Clarence Cameron White">Clarence Cameron White</a>. The files of the <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Brutus" title="Dennis Brutus">Dennis Brutus</a> Defense Committee (restricted).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The collection also includes manuscripts of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Crummell" title="Alexander Crummell">Alexander Crummell</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Edward_Bruce" title="John Edward Bruce">John Edward Bruce</a>, manuscripts of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">Slavery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">Abolitionism</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">West Indies</a>, and letters and unpublished manuscripts of <a href="/wiki/Langston_Hughes" title="Langston Hughes">Langston Hughes</a>. It includes some papers from <a href="/wiki/Christian_Fleetwood" title="Christian Fleetwood">Christian Fleetwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Robeson" title="Paul Robeson">Paul Robeson</a> (restricted),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Washington" title="Booker T. Washington">Booker T. Washington</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and Schomburg himself. It includes musical recordings, black and jazz periodicals, rare books and pamphlets, and tens of thousands of art objects. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The Center's collection includes documents signed by <a href="/wiki/Toussaint_Louverture" title="Toussaint Louverture">Toussaint Louverture</a> and a rare recording of a speech by <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Garvey" title="Marcus Garvey">Marcus Garvey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0419_19-7"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0419-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
