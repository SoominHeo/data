<p><b>Suni</b> (<i>Neotragus moschatus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a>. It occurs in dense underbrush from central <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> to <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=In_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="In South Africa (page does not exist)">in South Africa</a> .</p>
<p>Suni are around 12â17 inches (30â43Â cm) high at the shoulder and weigh 10â12 pounds (4.5â5.4Â kg). They are usually reddish brown, darker on their back than their sides and legs. The belly, chin, throat and insides of legs are white. The nostrils are prominent red, and there are black rings around the eyes and above the hooves. Males have horns 3â5 inches (8â13Â cm) long, that are ridged most of their length and curve backwards close to their heads. Females do not have horns. Suni can make weak barking and whistling sounds.</p>
<p>Suni feed on leaves, fungi, fruits and flowers, and need almost no free water. They are shy, most active at night, and sleep during the day in a shady, sheltered area. They are social but males defend a territory of about three hectares. They scent-mark the boundaries with secretions from their <a href="/wiki/Preorbital_gland" title="Preorbital gland">preorbital glands</a>. There may be an individual or communal dung pile on the periphery of the territory. A male usually takes one mate, but other females may share his territory. A single calf is born weighing about two pounds, after a <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> of 183 days.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">Lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, and other meat-eaters prey on suni. For protection, they are well <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflaged</a> in dry grass and keep very still. When a predator is almost on top of them, they spring out and bound away into the underbrush.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> of the suni is <i>Neotragus moschatus</i>. It is placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Neotragus" title="Neotragus">Neotragus</a></i> - along with <a href="/wiki/Bates%27s_pygmy_antelope" title="Bates's pygmy antelope">Bates's pygmy antelope</a> (<i>N. batesi</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_antelope" title="Royal antelope">royal antelope</a> (<i>N. pygmaeus</i>) - and in the family <a href="/wiki/Bovidae" title="Bovidae">Bovidae</a>. The common name "suni" (<small>pronouncedÂ </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/\ËsÃ¼nÄ\/</a></span>) is the name for this antelope in southeastern Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are identified, though these are sometimes considered to be independent species:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ut_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ut-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<li><i>N. m. kirchenpaueri</i> <small>(Pagenstecher, 1885)</small></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The suni is a small <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a>, but larger than the other two species of its genus. This antelope resembles Bates's pygmy antelope in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranium" title="Cranium">cranial</a> measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ut_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ut-4">[4]</a></sup> The suni stands 33â38 centimetres (13â15Â in) at the shoulder; the head-and-body length is typically between 57â62 centimetres (22â24Â in). Both sexes weigh between 4.5â7 kilograms (9.9â15.4Â lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castello_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-castello-5">[5]</a></sup> Horns are present only on males; <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in the suni is less marked than in Bates's pygmy antelope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ut_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ut-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_conservation">Threats and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Threats and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Populations of the suni have been notably reduced due to poaching, habitat loss and predation by dogs - especially in South Africa, where it is confined mainly to the northeastern <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>. Nevertheless, the antelope is known for its tolerance to heavy hunting pressure, and is listed as a species of Least Concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
