<p><b>Lutra</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otters</a>, one of seven in the subfamily Lutrinae.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus includes these recent species:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>L. lutra</i> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_otter" title="European otter">European otter</a></li>
<li><i>L. sumatrana</i> – <a href="/wiki/Hairy-nosed_otter" title="Hairy-nosed otter">hairy-nosed otter</a></li>
<li><i>L. nippon</i> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_otter" title="Japanese otter">Japanese otter</a> - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The genus most likely evolved in Asia during the late <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> epoch;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koepfli2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koepfli2008-1">[1]</a></sup> the oldest fossil belonging to the genus is of the species <i>L. palaeindica</i>, and dates from the late <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lariviere2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lariviere2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Lutra</i> species are semiaquatic mammals, so are well-adapted to both water and land. They prefer shallow, narrow areas of streams surrounded by mature trees and with rocks, especially where weirs reduce the flow of the water, as well as attract fishes. They seem to tolerate roads and residential and agricultural areas, but only moderate human interaction. They clearly avoid areas without vegetation cover and rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The otters' diets consist mainly of fish (hence, the aquatic environment). However, during the winter and in colder environments, fish consumption is significantly lower and the otters use other resources for their food supply. Their diets can consist of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a> (mainly frogs and pond turtles), bird predation (mainly anserine species), small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodents" title="Rodents">rodents</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a> such as water beetles, snails, and crayﬁsh. They have also feed on plants, specifically grasses. With this large diversity of prey and resources for their diets, otters are considered "opportunistic eaters".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some otters live in <a href="/wiki/Solitude" title="Solitude">solitude</a>, while others live in groups.</p>
