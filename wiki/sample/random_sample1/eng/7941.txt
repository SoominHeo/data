<p><b>Tokyo Metropolitan University</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">首都大学東京</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shuto Daigaku Tōkyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, often referred to as TMU, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Research_University" title="Public Research University">Public Research University</a> in Japan.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Metropolitan_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of Tokyo Metropolitan University was Prefectural Higher School, under the old system of education, established by <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Prefecture" title="Tokyo Prefecture">Tokyo Prefecture</a> in 1929 as the third public higher school. The School was modeled on <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a>, with three years of advanced course and four years of regular course.</p>
<p>The seven-year system had an advantage to assure the entrance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Seven_Universities" title="National Seven Universities">Imperial universities</a> at the age of Middle School, and the typical passway for academic elites before the end of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a> was to enter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_First_Middle_School" title="Tokyo First Middle School">Tokyo First Middle School</a>, proceeding First Higher School and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_Imperial_University" title="Tokyo Imperial University">Tokyo Imperial University</a>.</p>
<p>Since the jurisdiction control of Tokyo First Middle School and First Higher School were different, however, Tokyo First Middle School attempted to originally establish the prefectural higher school, whereas the other Middle Schools opposed to the said attempt. Prefectural Higher School was established in 1929 locating in the same site of Tokyo First Middle School, as a result of the opposition.</p>

<p>In 1932, Prefectural Higher School was relocated to 1–1–1 Yakumo, <a href="/wiki/Meguro" title="Meguro">Meguro</a> and became known one if the best higher schools with First Higher School. As the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_Metropolis" title="Tokyo Metropolis">Tokyo Metropolis</a> was enacted in 1943, Prefectural Higher School was renamed to Metropolitan Higher School.</p>
<p>After the reform of the educational system in 1949, Tokyo Metropolitan University (former) was established as a research university consisting of three faculties, namely Faculty of Humanities, Faculty of Science and Faculty of Technology; three years of advanced course was reorganised to Senior High School affiliated to Tokyo Metropolitan University, whereas four years of regular course was restructured to <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_college" title="Liberal arts college">Liberal arts college</a> and other faculties. Five Prefectural Colleges, namely Tokyo Prefectural College of Technology, Tokyo Prefectural College of Science, Tokyo Prefectural College of Machine Industry, Tokyo Prefectural College of Chemical Industry and Tokyo Prefectural College of Women were also merged to Tokyo Metropolitan University. In 1957, Faculty of Law and Economics was separated from Faculty of Humanities, and reorganised as Faculty of Law and Faculty of Economics in 1966.</p>
<p>As expanding its organisation, the university was relocated to 1–1–1 <a href="/wiki/Minami-%C5%8Dsawa_Station" title="Minami-ōsawa Station">Minami-Osawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hachi%C5%8Dji,_Tokyo" title="Hachiōji, Tokyo">Hachioji</a> in 1991.</p>
<p>The university signed the student exchange agreement with <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">University of Vienna</a> in 1997.</p>
<p>Tokyo Metropolitan University was reformed in 2005 by integrating three metropolitan universities and one junior college: Tokyo Metropolitan University <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">東京都立大学</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tōkyō Toritsu Daigaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, Tokyo Metropolitan Institute of Technology <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">東京都立科学技術大学</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, Tokyo Metropolitan University of Health Sciences <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">東京都立保健科学大学</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, and Tokyo Metropolitan college <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">東京都立短期大学</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, although its English name for the university has not changed since 1949.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Metropolitan_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following history includes the former institution of Tokyo Metropolitan University.</p>
<ul>
<li>1949 – Tokyo Metropolitan University was established with three faculties, namely Humanities, Science and Technology</li>
<li>1953 – Master's courses of Anthropology, Social Science, Natural Science and Technology were set up</li>
<li>1955 – Doctoral courses of Anthropology, Social Science, Natural Science and Technology were set up</li>
<li>1957 – Faculty of Law and Economics was set up</li>
<li>1966 – Faculty of Law and Economics was reorganised as Faculty of Law and Faculty of Economics</li>
<li>1977 – Centre of Metropolitan Study was set up</li>
<li>1994 – Centre of Metropolitan Study was reorganised as Institute of Metropolitan Study</li>
<li>1996 – Master's course of Metropolitan Science was set up</li>
<li>1998 – Doctoral course of Metropolitan Science was set up</li>
<li>1991 – The campus was transferred from Meguro to Minami-Osawa</li>
<li>2003 – Institute of Social Science launched to provide MBA course (<a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">Business school</a>)</li>
<li>2005 – Institute of Social Science launched to provide LLM course (<a href="/wiki/Law_school" title="Law school">Law school</a>)</li>
<li>2005 – Tokyo Metropolitan University was reformed with integrating three metropolitan universities and one junior college, which consists of 4 faculties, 7 divisions with 21 courses as well as 6 graduate schools (the organisation took over the former one) with 36 majors</li>
<li>2006 – Faculty of System Design opened the course of industrial art, and institutes were reorganised</li>
<li>2008 – Faculty of Urban Environment Sciences launched the course of nature- and culture- based tourism, and Graduate School of Urban Environmental Sciences launched the major of tourism science</li>
<li>2009 – Faculty of Urban Liberal Arts launched the course of economics</li>
<li>2010 – Graduate School of System Design launched the major of industrial art</li>
<li>2011 – Tokyo Metropolitan University (former) was closed</li>
<li>2014 – Bangkok Office was set up in <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>

</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="University_reform">University reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Metropolitan_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: University reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In later 1990s, Government and local municipalities facilitated to reform the administrative scheme and financial management in line with <a href="/wiki/Japanese_asset_price_bubble" title="Japanese asset price bubble">economic bubble burst</a> and financial difficulties due to Japan's progressive <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility" title="Sub-replacement fertility">low birthrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Population_ageing" title="Population ageing">longevity</a>. As a part of the said administrative and financial reform, social interest grew on restructuring national and public universities to <a href="/wiki/Independent_Administrative_Institution" title="Independent Administrative Institution">independent administrative agencies</a> with consolidating them. Tokyo Metropolitan University was also planned by <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Metropolitan_Government" title="Tokyo Metropolitan Government">Tokyo Metropolitan Government</a> to be consolidated with aforementioned three metropolitan universities and one junior college.</p>

<p>As a result of <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_gubernatorial_election,_2003" title="Tokyo gubernatorial election, 2003">Tokyo gubernatorial election in April 2003</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shintaro_Ishihara" title="Shintaro Ishihara">Shintaro Ishihara</a> was re-elected as Governor of Tokyo, holding up a promise "I will establish a revolutionary university", and consequently the original restructure plan was significantly and rapidly changed, in terms of the organisation of faculties, course structure, etc. During this process, many faculty staff left the university for making strong protest against the reform.</p>

<p>Although its small size and young history in contrast to <a href="/wiki/National_university" title="National university">national universities</a> and several leading <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private universities</a>, the university has been one of the most reputable institutions in Japan. According to the <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings">Times Higher Education World University Rankings</a>, it ranks 7th in 2014–2015 among 781 universities in Japan, behind renowned national universities, namely <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_University" title="Kyoto University">Kyoto University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology" title="Tokyo Institute of Technology">Tokyo Institute of Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University" title="Osaka University">Osaka University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tohoku_University" title="Tohoku University">Tohoku University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nagoya_University" title="Nagoya University">Nagoya University</a>. The university received the highest score of 100.0 for "citations.”</p>
<p>In 2012, Prof. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Masatake_Haruta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Masatake Haruta (page does not exist)">Masatake Haruta</a> was selected as a <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Reuters_Citation_Laureates" title="Thomson Reuters Citation Laureates">Thomson Reuters Citation Laureate</a> as a possible winner of the Nobel Prize for his "independent foundational discoveries of catalysis by gold."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
