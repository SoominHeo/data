<p>The <b>Sudd</b> (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_letter" title="Solar letter">s</a>-Sudd</span></i> or <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">al-Sudd</span></i>) is a vast <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamp</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>, formed by the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile" title="White Nile">White Nile</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_Nile" title="Mountain Nile">Baḥr al-Jabal</a></i> section. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> word <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">sudd</span></i> is derived from <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">sadd</span></i> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%B3%D8%AF" title="wikt:سد">سد</a></span>), meaning "barrier"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or "obstruction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term "the sudd" has come to refer to any large solid floating vegetation island or mat. The area which the swamp covers is one of the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> and the largest freshwater wetland in the Nile basin.</p>
<p>For many years the swamp, and especially its thicket of vegetation, proved an impenetrable barrier to navigation along the Nile. In 61 AD, a party of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> soldiers sent by the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a> proceeded up the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile" title="White Nile">White Nile</a> but were not able to get beyond the Sudd, which marked the limit of <a href="/wiki/Nile_exploration_by_Roman_emperor_Nero" title="Nile exploration by Roman emperor Nero">Roman penetration into equatorial Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For the same reasons in later times the <a href="/wiki/Nile#The_search_for_the_source_of_the_Nile" title="Nile">search for the source of the Nile</a> was particularly difficult; it eventually involved overland expeditions from the central African coast, so as to avoid having to travel through the Sudd.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sudd stretches from <a href="/wiki/Mongalla,_South_Sudan" title="Mongalla, South Sudan">Mongalla</a> to just outside the <a href="/wiki/Sobat_River" title="Sobat River">Sobat</a> confluence with the White Nile just upstream of Malakal as well as westwards along the <a href="/wiki/Bahr_el_Ghazal" title="Bahr el Ghazal">Bahr el Ghazal</a>. The shallow and flat inland delta lies between 5.5 and 9.5 degrees latitude North and covers an area of 500 kilometres (310 mi) south to north and 200 kilometres (120 mi) east to west between Mongalla in the south and <a href="/wiki/Malakal" title="Malakal">Malakal</a> in the north.</p>
<p>Its size is highly variable, averaging over 30,000 square kilometres (12,000 sq mi). During the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a> it may extend to over 130,000 square kilometres (50,000 sq mi), depending on the inflowing waters, with the discharge from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a> being the main control factor of flood levels and area inundation. Since the Sudd area consists of various meandering channels, lagoons, reed and <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> fields and loses half of its inflowing water through <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> in the permanent and seasonal floodplains, the complex hydrology has many primary and secondary effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A major feature of the area, even if not completed and not functional, is the <a href="/wiki/Jonglei_Canal" title="Jonglei Canal">Jonglei Canal</a>, planned to bypass waters from the Sudd to avoid evaporation losses and increase the amount of water discharged at the outlet of the Sudd.</p>
<p>From 1961 to 1963, a great increase in the inundated area occurred when the level of Lake Victoria rose and the outflow increased. The total area of the Sudd is related to the amount of water reaching Bor from Albert Nile and from torrents or seasonal watercourses that can add substantial amounts to the flow in the upstream end of the Sudd. During the 1960s increase in Lake Victoria discharge, where flows at Mongalla roughly doubled, the flows at Malakal at the northern end of the swamps increased by 1.5 times the previous average flow. As a consequence of these high flows, the areas of permanent swamp and seasonal floodplains have, taken together, increased to 2.5 times their former size. The swamps have increased the most, and the seasonal floodplain is 1.5 times its previous size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mefit_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mefit-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the southern inflow of the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile#In_South_Sudan" title="White Nile">Bahr al Jabal</a> ("Sea of the Mountain") at Mongalla, the defined riverbed successively widens into a floodplain, where the waters flow in meandering river stretches and various channels and lagoons throughout the dry season. With rising water levels it expands over the semi-flooded grasslands during the flood season.</p>
<p>Slightly downstream of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bor,_Sudan" title="Bor, Sudan">Bor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bahr_el_Zeraf" title="Bahr el Zeraf">Bahr el Zeraf</a> ("Sea of the <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">Giraffes</a>") river branches off the Bahr al Jabal to the east, diverting part of the flow, and again joins the Bahr al Jabal just before reaching Malakal. During the course of its flow, the Bahr al Jabal passes <a href="/wiki/Lake_No" title="Lake No">Lake No</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahr_el_Ghazal_(river)" title="Bahr el Ghazal (river)">Bahr el Ghazal</a> ("Sea of the <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">Gazelles</a>") connects to the Bahr al Jabal, contributing an inflow with seasonal variation. At Malakal, the Sobat River joins into the system. The combined flows then stream to the north as the White Nile in a defined bed, joining with the Blue Nile waters at Khartoum to form the main Nile.</p>
<p>Sudd was designated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramsar_Wetland_of_International_Importance" title="Ramsar Wetland of International Importance">Ramsar Wetland of International Importance</a> on 5 June 2006. An area of 57,000 square kilometres (22,000 sq mi) was designated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrologically the Sudd plays an important role in storing floodwaters and trapping sediments from the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile#In_South_Sudan" title="White Nile">Bahr al Jabal</a>. Roughly 55 percent of water entering the area is lost to evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baecher_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-baecher-6">[6]</a></sup> Water levels fluctuate by up to 1.5 metres, depending on the intensity of seasonal flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mefit_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mefit-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The region receives less rainfall (typically between 55 and 65 centimetres per year) than neighbouring areas at the same latitude. <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">Orographic lifting</a> on the eastern and western sides of the Sudd contribute to that condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geomorphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The morphology of the area is defined by the channel and lagoon system of the permanent Sudd swamps, the adjacent flood plains and the surrounding flat terrain. The Bahr al Jabal runs to the north-northwest and therefore in an angle to the gradient of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_plain" title="Flood plain">flood plain</a>, which slopes down to the north, while north of Juba the river flows in an incised trough. The banks of this trough decrease in height from south to north with the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile#In_South_Sudan" title="White Nile">Bahr al Jabal</a> approaching Bor and ending in the Sudd flood plain just north of <a href="/wiki/Bor,_South_Sudan" title="Bor, South Sudan">Bor</a> on the eastern bank and towards Shambe on the western bank. In the southern part, the river meanders from side to side in the restraining trough in one or more channels, but further north the swamp is not limited by higher ground and the system of river channels becomes increasingly complex. The characteristics of the river with its network of channels and lagoons are distinguishable in satellite imagery and digital elevation models.</p>
<p>The geology of the area is defined by heavy clay soils, highly impermeable with a top layer of "black cotton" <a href="/wiki/Vertisol" title="Vertisol">vertisol</a> of approximately 500 mm on average. Sandy soils are found only at depths of approximately 30 metres (98 ft) and below, as determined by well drilling profiles (Petersen, 2008). This indicates a very limited groundwater influence on the area's hydrology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinka" title="Dinka">Dinka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuer_people" title="Nuer people">Nuer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shilluk_people" title="Shilluk people">Shilluk</a> pastoralists use the Sudd and the surrounding areas extensively. Livestock and rain-fed agriculture are the dominant means of support for the largely rural population for which the seasonal flooded grasslands along the Sudd provides valuable grazing lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baecher_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-baecher-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation_and_ecosystem">Vegetation and ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vegetation and ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vegetation cover of the area can generally be classified in five categories which depend on the elevation of the area above river flood level: the lakes and rivers themselves, the floating plant life of the swamp, river-flooded grasslands (Toic), rain-flooded grasslands, and wooded grasslands on the fringes. Grassland and woodland areas have been cultivated by local populations.</p>
<p>The density of the grasslands along the Sudd changes with the season, with tall grass in the rainy season and short dry grass in the dry season, when frequent fires also occur. The fluvial area is mostly overgrown with vegetation, with some main and side channels as well as lagoons of open water. The vegetation distribution is described in further detail in Sutcliffe (1974) and Petersen (2007). The main species are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phragmites_communis" title="Phragmites communis">Phragmites communis</a></i> (shallow flooded, buried roots)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Echinochloa" title="Echinochloa">Echinochloa</a> pyramidalis</i> (shallow flooded, buried roots)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Oryza_barthii" title="Oryza barthii">Oryza barthii</a></i> (shallow flooded, buried roots)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Echinochloa_stagnina" title="Echinochloa stagnina">Echinochloa stagnina</a></i> (deep flooded, superficial/floating roots)</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vossia_(plant)" title="Vossia (plant)">Vossia cuspidata</a></i> (deep flooded, superficial/floating roots)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Cyperus_papyrus" title="Cyperus papyrus">Cyperus papyrus</a></i> (deep flooded, superficial/floating roots)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Typha_domingensis" title="Typha domingensis">Typha domingensis</a></i></li>
</ul>
<p>The first three species are anchored so their distribution is limited to the depth of flooding. For the last species their root system needs to be permanently in water or saturated soil, which is a good indicator of flood patterns. <i>P. communis</i>, <i>E. pyramidalis</i> and <i>O. barthii</i> for example dominate only in areas where the depth of flooding does not exceed 130 cm over a period of ten years or 118 cm for one month in the year.</p>
<p>Floating vegetation of <i>C. papyrus</i> had caused blockages in the Sudd swamps on a number of occasions between 1879 and 1900, when the plants were torn out by increased flooding. <i>C. papyrus</i> needs saturated conditions and can tolerate flooding that is not more than 150 cm deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the matted vegetation breaks free of its moorings, it forms floating islands of vegetation up to 30 km in length. Such islands, in varying stages of decomposition, eventually break up.</p>
<p>The sluggish waters are host to a large population of <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a> that cause <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a>.</p>
<p>The early explorers searching for the <a href="/wiki/Nile#The_search_for_the_source_of_the_Nile" title="Nile">source of the Nile</a> experienced considerable difficulties, sometimes taking months to get through the Sudd. In <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_White_Nile&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The White Nile (page does not exist)">The White Nile</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moorehead" title="Alan Moorehead">Alan Moorehead</a> says of the Sudd, "there is no more formidable swamp in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sudd provides food and water to large populations of migrating birds; over 400 species of bird are found here, including <a href="/wiki/Shoebill" title="Shoebill">shoebills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_white_pelican" title="Great white pelican">great white pelicans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Black_crowned_crane" title="Black crowned crane">black crowned cranes</a>. As the surrounding landscape is a large swath of dry <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> across Africa, the swamp is also a haven for migrating animals, especially <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelopes</a>, such as the endangered <a href="/wiki/Nile_lechwe" title="Nile lechwe">Nile lechwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korrigum" title="Korrigum">tiang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reedbuck" title="Reedbuck">reedbuck</a>, and the world's largest population of white-eared <a href="/wiki/Kob" title="Kob">kob</a>, estimated at around 1.2 million—an animal population comparable to Tanzania's better-known <a href="/wiki/Serengeti_National_Park" title="Serengeti National Park">Serengeti National Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furniss2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Furniss2010-11">[11]</a></sup> The shallow water is frequented by <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamuses</a>. In more upland areas the Sudd was known as an historic <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">endangered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painted_hunting_dog" title="Painted hunting dog">painted hunting dog</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lycaon_pictus" title="Lycaon pictus">Lycaon pictus</a></i>, which however may have been exterminated in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_preservation">Threats and preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Threats and preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The long-running <a href="/wiki/Second_Sudanese_Civil_War" title="Second Sudanese Civil War">civil war in Southern Sudan</a> seriously disrupted conservation efforts in the Sudd, especially as the widespread availability of weapons has encouraged wildlife poaching, including of elephants. There are three game reserves: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zeraf_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zeraf Island (page does not exist)">Zeraf Island</a> between the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zeraf_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zeraf River (page does not exist)">Zeraf River</a> and the Nile, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shambe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shambe (page does not exist)">Shambe</a> Reserve, and <a href="/wiki/Mongalla_Game_Reserve" title="Mongalla Game Reserve">Mongalla Game Reserve</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jonglei_diversion_canal">Jonglei diversion canal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jonglei diversion canal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jonglei_Canal" title="Jonglei Canal">Jonglei Canal</a></div>
<p>Because of the Sudd swamp, the water from the southwestern tributaries (the Bahr el Ghazal system) for all practical purposes does not reach the main river and is lost through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a>.</p>
<p>Sir William Garstin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undersecretary_of_State_of_Public_Works_of_Egypt" title="Undersecretary of State of Public Works of Egypt">Undersecretary of State of Public Works of Egypt</a>, created the first detailed proposal for digging a canal east of the Sudd in 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By bypassing the swamps, evaporation of the Nile's water would vastly decrease, allowing an increase in the area of cultivatable land in Egypt by 2,000,000 acres (8,100 km<sup>2</sup>).</p>
<p>Hydrogeologists in the 1930s proposed digging a <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a> east of the Sudd to divert water from the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile#In_South_Sudan" title="White Nile">Bahr al Jabal</a> above the Sudd to a point farther down the White Nile, bypassing the swamps and carrying the White Nile's water directly to the main channel of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Jonglei canal scheme was first studied by the government of Egypt in 1946 and plans were developed in 1954-59. Construction work on the canal began in 1978 but the outbreak of political instability in Sudan has held up work for many years. By 1984 when the <a href="/wiki/Sudan_People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="Sudan People's Liberation Army">Southern Sudanese rebels (SPLA)</a> brought the works to a halt, 240 km of the canal of a total of 360 km had been excavated. The rusting remains of the giant German-built excavation machine—variously nicknamed either "Sarah" or "Lucy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>—are visible on a <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a> image at the south end of the canal: <a class="external text" href="http://goo.gl/maps/tV5lK" rel="nofollow">link</a>. It was destroyed by a missile. When peace was restored in 2000, speculation grew about a restart of the project. However, on 21 February 2008, the Sudanese government said the revival of the project was not a priority. Nevertheless, in 2008, Sudan and Egypt agreed to restart the project and finish the canal in 24 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahmad_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ahmad-16">[16]</a></sup> South Sudan gained independence in 2011.</p>
<p>It is estimated that the Jonglei canal project would produce 3.5-4.8 x 10<sup>9</sup> m<sup>3</sup> of water per year (equal to a mean annual discharge of 110–152 m<sup>3</sup>/s (3,883-5,368 ft<sup>3</sup>/s), an increase of around five to seven per cent of Egypt's current water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furniss2010_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Furniss2010-11">[11]</a></sup> The canal's benefits would be shared by Egypt and Sudan, with the expected damage falling on <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahmad_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ahmad-16">[16]</a></sup> The complex environmental and social issues involved, including the collapse of fisheries, drying of grazing lands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> a drop of groundwater levels, and a reduction of rainfall in the region<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> may limit the scope of the project in practical terms. The draining of the Sudd is likely to have environmental effects comparable to the drying of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Chad" title="Lake Chad">Lake Chad</a> or the draining of the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>.</p>
