<p><b>Nymphaeaceae</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a family of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a>, commonly called <b>water lilies</b>. They live as <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomatous</a> aquatic herbs in temperate and <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical climates</a> around the world. The family contains five genera with about 70 known species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christenhusz-Byng2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christenhusz-Byng2016-2">[2]</a></sup> Water lilies are rooted in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> in bodies of water, with <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> floating on or emergent from the surface. The leaves are round, with a radial notch in <i><a href="/wiki/Nymphaea" title="Nymphaea">Nymphaea</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nuphar" title="Nuphar">Nuphar</a></i>, but fully circular in <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_(plant)" title="Victoria (plant)">Victoria</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Euryale" title="Euryale">Euryale</a></i>.</p>
<p>Water lilies are a well studied <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of plants because their large flowers with multiple unspecialized parts were initially considered to represent the floral pattern of the earliest flowering plants, and later genetic studies confirmed their evolutionary position as <a href="/wiki/Basal_angiosperms" title="Basal angiosperms">basal angiosperms</a>. Analyses of floral morphology and molecular characteristics and comparisons with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" title="Sister taxon">sister taxon</a>, the family <a href="/wiki/Cabombaceae" title="Cabombaceae">Cabombaceae</a>, indicate, however, that the flowers of extant water lilies with the most floral parts are more derived than the genera with fewer floral parts. Genera with more floral parts, <i>Nuphar</i>, <i>Nymphaea</i>, <i>Victoria</i>, have a <a href="/wiki/Pollination_syndrome#Beetle_pollination_.28cantharophily.29" title="Pollination syndrome">beetle</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollination_syndrome" title="Pollination syndrome">pollination syndrome</a>, while genera with fewer parts are pollinated by <a href="/wiki/Pollination_syndrome#Fly_pollination_.28myophily_and_sapromyophily.29" title="Pollination syndrome">flies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pollination_syndrome#Bee_pollination_.28melittophily.29" title="Pollination syndrome">bees</a>, or are self- or <a href="/wiki/Anemophily" title="Anemophily">wind-pollinated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Non-molecular_1999.2C_pp._28-46_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Non-molecular_1999.2C_pp._28-46-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus, the large number of relatively unspecialized floral organs in the Nymphaeaceae is not an ancestral condition for the clade.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">Horticulturally</a>, water lilies have been <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridized</a> for temperate gardens since the 19th century, and the hybrids are divided into three groups: hardy, night-blooming tropical, and day-blooming tropical water lilies. Hardy water lilies are hybrids of <i>Nymphaea</i> species from the subgenus <i>Castalia</i>; night-blooming tropical water lilies are developed from the subgenus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lotos(subgenus)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lotos(subgenus) (page does not exist)">Lotos</a></i>; and the day-blooming tropical plants arise from hybridization of plants of the subgenus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brachyceras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brachyceras (page does not exist)">Brachyceras</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Nymphaeaceae are aquatic, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhizomatous" title="Rhizomatous">rhizomatous</a> herbs. The family is further characterized by scattered vascular bundles in the stems, and frequent presence of <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, usually with distinct, stellate-branched <a href="/wiki/Sclereid" title="Sclereid">sclereids</a> projecting into the air canals. Hairs are simple, usually producing <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a> (slime). Leaves are alternate and spiral, opposite or occasionally whorled, simple, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peltate" title="Peltate">peltate</a> or nearly so, entire to toothed or dissected, short to long <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petiolate)</a>, with blade submerged, floating or emergent, with palmate to pinnate venation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stipules" title="Stipules">Stipules</a> are either present or absent. Flowers are solitary, bisexual, radial, with a long pedicel and usually floating or raised above the surface of the water, with girdling vascular bundles in <a href="/wiki/Receptacle_(botany)" title="Receptacle (botany)">receptacle</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">Sepals</a> are 4-12, distinct to <a href="/wiki/Connation" title="Connation">connate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imbricate" title="Imbricate">imbricate</a>, and often petal-like. Petals lacks or 8 to numerous, inconspicuous to showy, often intergrading with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stamens" title="Stamens">stamens</a>. Stamens are 3 to numerous, the innermost sometimes represented by staminodes. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filament_(botany)" title="Filament (botany)">Filaments</a> are distinct, free or <a href="/wiki/Adnation" title="Adnation">adnate</a> to petaloid staminodes, slender and well differentiated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthers" title="Anthers">anthers</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_shape" title="Leaf shape">laminar</a> and poorly differentiated from anthers; pollen grains usually monosulcate or lacking apertures. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpels" title="Carpels">Carpels</a> are 3 to numerous, distinct or connate. Fruit is an aggregate of nuts, a berry, or an irregularly dehiscent fleshy capsule. Seeds are often arillate, more or less lacking <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphaeaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nymphaeaceae has been investigated systematically for decades because botanists considered their floral morphology to represent one of the earliest groups of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperms" title="Angiosperms">angiosperms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Non-molecular_1999.2C_pp._28-46_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Non-molecular_1999.2C_pp._28-46-3">[3]</a></sup> Modern genetic analyses by the <a href="/wiki/Angiosperm_Phylogeny_Group" title="Angiosperm Phylogeny Group">Angiosperm Phylogeny Group</a> researchers has confirmed its <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> position among flowering plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apgiii_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-apgiii-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Linnean_Society_of_London_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Linnean_Society_of_London-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horticulture_Week_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horticulture_Week-7">[7]</a></sup> In addition, the Nymphaeaceae are more genetically diverse and geographically dispersed than other basal angiosperms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nymphaeaceae is placed in the order <a href="/wiki/APG_III_system" title="APG III system">Nymphaeales</a>, which is the second diverging group of angiosperms after <i><a href="/wiki/Amborella" title="Amborella">Amborella</a></i> in the most widely accepted flowering plant classification system, <a href="/wiki/APG_III_system" title="APG III system">APG III system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apgiii_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-apgiii-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Linnean_Society_of_London_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Linnean_Society_of_London-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horticulture_Week_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horticulture_Week-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nymphaeaceae is a small family of three to six genera: <i><a href="/wiki/Barclaya" title="Barclaya">Barclaya</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Euryale_ferox" title="Euryale ferox">Euryale</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nuphar" title="Nuphar">Nuphar</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nymphaea" title="Nymphaea">Nymphaea</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ondinea" title="Ondinea">Ondinea</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_(plant)" title="Victoria (plant)">Victoria</a></i>. The genus <i>Barclaya</i> is sometimes given rank as its own family, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barclayaceae" title="Barclayaceae">Barclayaceae</a>, on the basis of an extended <a href="/wiki/Perianth" title="Perianth">perianth</a> tube (combined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sepals" title="Sepals">sepals</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petals" title="Petals">petals</a>) arising from the top of the <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovary</a> and by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stamens" title="Stamens">stamens</a> that are joined in the base. However, molecular phylogenetic work includes it in Nymphaeaceae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The genus <i>Ondinea</i> has recently been shown to be a morphologically aberrant species of <i>Nymphaea</i>, and is now included in this genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The genera <i>Euryale</i>, of far east Asia, and <i>Victoria</i>, from South America, are closely related despite their geographic distance, but their relationship toward <i>Nymphaea</i> need further studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nelumbo_nucifera" title="Nelumbo nucifera">sacred lotus</a> was once thought to be a water lily, but is now recognized to be a highly modified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eudicot" title="Eudicot">eudicot</a> in its own family <a href="/wiki/Nelumbonaceae" title="Nelumbonaceae">Nelumbonaceae</a> of the order <a href="/wiki/Proteales" title="Proteales">Proteales</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_invasive_species">As invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphaeaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The beautiful nature of water lilies has led to their widespread use as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornamental_plants" title="Ornamental plants">ornamental plants</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nymphaea_mexicana" title="Nymphaea mexicana">Mexican water lily</a>, native to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf Coast</a> of North America, is planted throughout the continent. It has escaped from cultivation and become invasive in some areas, such as California's <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a>. It can infest slow-moving bodies of water and is difficult to eradicate. Populations can be controlled by cutting top growth. Herbicides can also be used to control populations using <a href="/wiki/Glyphosate" title="Glyphosate">glyphosate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluridone" title="Fluridone">fluridone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">Symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nymphaeaceae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nymphaea_nouchali" title="Nymphaea nouchali">white water lily</a> is the <a href="/wiki/National_emblem" title="National emblem">national flower</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and state flower for <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nymphaea_nouchali" title="Nymphaea nouchali">blue waterlily</a> is the <a href="/wiki/National_emblem" title="National emblem">national flower</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. It is also the birth flower for July.</p>
<p>Lily pads, also known as <i><a href="/wiki/Seeblatt" title="Seeblatt">Seeblätter</a></i>, are a charge in Northern European heraldry, often coloured <a href="/wiki/Gules" title="Gules">red (gules)</a>, and appear on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Friesland" title="Flag of Friesland">flag of Friesland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Denmark" title="Coat of arms of Denmark">coat of arms of Denmark</a> (in the latter case often replaced by red <a href="/wiki/Heart_(symbol)" title="Heart (symbol)">hearts</a>).</p>
<p>The water lily has a special place in <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a> and Tamil poetics, where it is considered symbolic of the grief of separation; it is considered to evoke imagery of the sunset, the seashore, and the shark.</p>
