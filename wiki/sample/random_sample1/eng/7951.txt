<p><b>Sumatra</b> (<a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a>: <span lang="id" xml:lang="id"><i>Sumatera</i></span>) is a large island in western <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> that is part of the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Islands" title="Sunda Islands">Sunda Islands</a>. It is the largest island that is entirely in Indonesia (two larger islands, <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, are shared between Indonesia and other countries) and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">sixth-largest island</a> in the world at 473,481 km<sup>2</sup> (including adjacent islands such as the Riau Islands and Bangka Belitung Islands).</p>
<p>Sumatra is an elongated landmass spanning a diagonal northwest-southeast axis. The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> borders the west, northwest, and southwest sides of Sumatra with the island chain of <a href="/wiki/Simeulue" title="Simeulue">Simeulue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nias" title="Nias">Nias</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mentawai_Islands" title="Mentawai Islands">Mentawai</a> bordering the southwestern coast. On the northeast side the narrow <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Strait of Malacca</a> separates the island from the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>, an extension of the Eurasian continent. On the southeast the narrow <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a> separates Sumatra from <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>. The northern tip of Sumatra borders the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a>, while on the lower eastern side are the islands of Bangka and Belitung, <a href="/wiki/Karimata_Strait" title="Karimata Strait">Karimata Strait</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Java_Sea" title="Java Sea">Java Sea</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bukit_Barisan" title="Bukit Barisan">Bukit Barisan</a> mountains, which contain several active volcanoes, form the backbone of the island, while the northeast sides are outlying lowlands with swamps, mangrove and complex river systems. The <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> crosses the island at its center on <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riau" title="Riau">Riau</a> provinces. The climate of the island is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a>, hot and humid with lush <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_rain_forest" title="Tropical rain forest">tropical rain forest</a> once dominating the landscape.</p>
<p>Sumatra has a wide range of plant and animal species but has lost almost 50% of its tropical rainforest in the last 35 years, and many species are <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_ground_cuckoo" title="Sumatran ground cuckoo">Sumatran ground cuckoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_elephant" title="Sumatran elephant">Sumatran elephant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_rhinoceros" title="Sumatran rhinoceros">Sumatran rhinoceros</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_orangutan" title="Sumatran orangutan">Sumatran orangutan</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> on the island has also resulted in serious haze over neighbouring countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/2013_Southeast_Asian_haze" title="2013 Southeast Asian haze">2013 Southeast Asian haze</a> causing considerable tensions with affected countries such as <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sumatra was known in ancient times by the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> names of <i>Swarnadwīpa</i> ("Island of Gold") and <i>Swarnabhūmi</i> ("Land of Gold"), because of the gold deposits of the island's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_(geography)" title="Highland (geography)">highland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first word mentioning the name of <i>Sumatra</i> was the name of <a href="/wiki/Srivijaya" title="Srivijaya">Srivijayan</a> <i>Haji</i> (king) <i>Sumatrabhumi</i> ("King of the land of Sumatra"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MUNOZ_175_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MUNOZ_175-3">[3]</a></sup> who sent an envoy to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in 1017. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> geographers referred to the island as <i>Lamri</i> (Lamuri, Lambri or Ramni) in the tenth through thirteenth centuries, in reference to a kingdom near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Banda_Aceh" title="Banda Aceh">Banda Aceh</a> which was the first landfall for traders.</p>
<p>Late in the 14th century the name Sumatra became popular in reference to the kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasai" title="Pasai">Samudra Pasai</a>, which was a rising power until it was replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Aceh" title="Sultanate of Aceh">Sultanate of Aceh</a>. Sultan Alauddin Shah of Aceh, on letters written in 1602 addressed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, referred to himself as "king of Aceh and Samudra".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sneddon_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sneddon-4">[4]</a></sup> The word itself is from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> "<i><a href="/wiki/Samudra" title="Samudra">Samudra</a></i>", (समुद्र), meaning "<i>gathering together of waters, sea or ocean</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>European writers in the 19th century found that the indigenous inhabitants did not have a name for the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Melayu_Kingdom" title="Melayu Kingdom">Melayu Kingdom</a> was absorbed by Srivijaya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:79–80</sup></p>
<p>Srivijaya was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> monarchy centred in what is now <a href="/wiki/Palembang" title="Palembang">Palembang</a>. Dominating the region through trade and conquest throughout the 7th to 9th centuries, the empire helped spread the <a href="/wiki/Malays_(ethnic_group)" title="Malays (ethnic group)">Malay</a> culture throughout <a href="/wiki/Nusantara" title="Nusantara">Nusantara</a>. The empire was a <a href="/wiki/Thalassocracy" title="Thalassocracy">thalassocracy</a> or maritime power that extended its influence from island to island. Palembang was a center for scholarly learning, and it was there the Chinese Buddhist pilgrim <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Ching_(monk)" title="I Ching (monk)">I Ching</a> studied Sanskrit in 671 CE before departing for India. On his journey to China, he spent four years in Palembang translating Buddhist texts and writing two manuscripts.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Srivijayan" title="Srivijayan">Srivijayan</a> influence waned in the 11th century after it was defeated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chola" title="Chola">Chola</a> Empire of southern India. At the same time, Islam made its way to Sumatra through <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">Indian</a> traders in the 6th and 7th centuries AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> By the late 13th century, the monarch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasai" title="Pasai">Samudra</a> kingdom had converted to Islam. <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> visited the island in 1292, and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a> visited twice during 1345–1346. Samudra was succeeded by the powerful <a href="/wiki/Aceh_Sultanate" title="Aceh Sultanate">Aceh Sultanate</a>, which survived to the 20th century. With the coming of the Dutch, the many Sumatran princely states gradually fell under their control. Aceh, in the north, was the major obstacle, as the Dutch were involved in the long and costly <a href="/wiki/Aceh_War" title="Aceh War">Aceh War</a> (1873–1903).</p>
<p>Sumatra came under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> and became a major producer of pepper, rubber, and oil. In the early and mid-twentieth century, Sumatran academics and leaders were important figures in <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Revolution" title="Indonesian National Revolution">Indonesia's independence movements</a>, such as: <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Hatta" title="Mohammad Hatta">Mohammad Hatta</a> (the first vice-president) and <a href="/wiki/Sutan_Sjahrir" title="Sutan Sjahrir">Sutan Sjahrir</a> (the first prime minister).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Free_Aceh_Movement" title="Free Aceh Movement">Free Aceh Movement</a> fought against Indonesian government forces in the <a href="/wiki/Insurgency_in_Aceh" title="Insurgency in Aceh">Aceh Insurgency</a> from 1976 to 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Security crackdowns in 2001 and 2002 resulted in several thousand civilian deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sumatra_Volcanoes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="527" data-file-width="600" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sumatra_Volcanoes.png/220px-Sumatra_Volcanoes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sumatra_Volcanoes.png/330px-Sumatra_Volcanoes.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sumatra_Volcanoes.png/440px-Sumatra_Volcanoes.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sumatra_Volcanoes.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of geological formation of Sumatra island</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The longest axis of the island runs approximately 1,790 km (1,110 mi) northwest–southeast, crossing the equator near the centre. At its widest point, the island spans 435 km (270 mi). The interior of the island is dominated by two geographical regions: the <a href="/wiki/Barisan_Mountains" title="Barisan Mountains">Barisan Mountains</a> in the west and swampy plains in the east. Sumatra is the closest Indonesian island to mainland Asia.</p>
<p>To the southeast is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_(island)" title="Java (island)">Java</a>, separated by the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a>. To the north is the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a> (located on the Asian mainland), separated by the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Strait of Malacca</a>. To the east is <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, across the <a href="/wiki/Karimata_Strait" title="Karimata Strait">Karimata Strait</a>. West of the island is the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.</p>
<p>The backbone of the island is the Barisan Mountain chain, with the active volcano <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kerinci" title="Mount Kerinci">Mount Kerinci</a> as the highest point at 3,805 m (12,467 ft), located at about the midpoint of the range. The volcanic activity of this region endowed the region with fertile land and beautiful sceneries, for instance around <a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">Lake Toba</a>. It also contains deposits of coal and gold. The volcanic activity stems from Sumatra's location on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Ring_of_Fire" title="Pacific Ring of Fire">Pacific Ring of Fire</a>—which is also the reason why Sumatra has had some of the most powerful earthquakes ever recorded: in <a href="/wiki/1797_Sumatra_earthquake" title="1797 Sumatra earthquake">1797</a>, <a href="/wiki/1833_Sumatra_earthquake" title="1833 Sumatra earthquake">1833</a>, <a href="/wiki/1861_Sumatra_earthquake" title="1861 Sumatra earthquake">1861</a>, <a href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami">2004</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_Nias%E2%80%93Simeulue_earthquake" title="2005 Nias–Simeulue earthquake">2005</a>, and <a href="/wiki/September_2007_Sumatra_earthquakes" title="September 2007 Sumatra earthquakes">2007</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Sumatran_fault" title="Great Sumatran fault">Great Sumatran fault</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Strike-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">strike-slip</a> fault), and the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_megathrust" title="Sunda megathrust">Sunda megathrust</a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">subduction zone</a>), run the entire length of the island along its west coast. On 26 December 2004, the western coast and islands of Sumatra, particularly <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a> province, were struck by a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">Indian Ocean earthquake</a>. This was the longest earthquake recorded, lasting between 500 and 600 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> More than 170,000 Indonesians were killed, primarily in Aceh. Other recent earthquakes to strike Sumatra include the <a href="/wiki/2005_Nias%E2%80%93Simeulue_earthquake" title="2005 Nias–Simeulue earthquake">2005 Nias–Simeulue earthquake</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/October_2010_Sumatra_earthquake" title="October 2010 Sumatra earthquake">October 2010 Sumatra earthquake</a>.</p>

<p>To the east, big rivers carry silt from the mountains, forming the vast lowland interspersed by swamps. Even if mostly unsuitable for farming, the area is currently of great economic importance for Indonesia. It produces oil from both above and below the soil – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_palm" title="Oil palm">palm oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>.</p>
<p>Sumatra is the largest producer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_coffee" title="Indonesian coffee">Indonesian coffee</a>. Small-holders grow Arabica coffee (<i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">Coffea arabica</a></i>) in the highlands, while Robusta (<i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_canephora" title="Coffea canephora">Coffea canephora</a></i>) is found in the lowlands. Arabica coffee from the regions of Gayo, Lintong and Sidikilang is typically processed using the <a href="/wiki/Giling_Basah" title="Giling Basah">Giling Basah</a> (wet hulling) technique, which gives it a heavy body and low acidity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of Sumatra used to be covered by <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a>, but economic development coupled with corruption and <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging" title="Illegal logging">illegal logging</a> has severely threatened its existence. Even designated conservation areas have not been spared from this destruction.</p>
<p>The island is the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_highest_point" title="List of islands by highest point">fifth highest island</a>, and the third highest in the Indonesian archipelago.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Batang_Hari_River" title="Batang Hari River">Batang Hari River</a> is the longest river in the island with its source in the <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_Highlands" title="Minangkabau Highlands">Minangkabau Highlands</a>, stretching around 800 kilometres eastward to its estuary in the <a href="/wiki/Jambi" title="Jambi">Jambi</a> lowlands.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sumatran_tiger.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="330" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Sumatran_tiger.jpg/220px-Sumatran_tiger.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/31/Sumatran_tiger.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sumatran_tiger.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg/170px-Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg/255px-Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg/340px-Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Rafflesia_arnoldii" title="Rafflesia arnoldii">Rafflesia arnoldii</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_Indonesia" title="List of national parks of Indonesia">List of national parks of Indonesia</a></div>
<p>Sumatra supports a wide range of vegetation types which are home to a rich variety of species, including 17 endemic genera of plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitten1999_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten1999-15">[15]</a></sup> Unique species include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumatran_pine" title="Sumatran pine">Sumatran pine</a> which dominates the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tropical_pine_forests" title="Sumatran tropical pine forests">Sumatran tropical pine forests</a> of the higher mountainsides in the north of the island and rainforest plants such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rafflesia_arnoldii" title="Rafflesia arnoldii">Rafflesia arnoldii</a></i> (the world's largest individual flower), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_arum" title="Titan arum">titan arum</a> (the world's largest unbranched <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a>).</p>
<p>The island is home to 201 mammal species and 580 bird species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_ground_cuckoo" title="Sumatran ground cuckoo">Sumatran ground cuckoo</a>. There are 9 endemic mammal species on mainland Sumatra and 14 more endemic to the nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mentawai_Islands" title="Mentawai Islands">Mentawai Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitten1999_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten1999-15">[15]</a></sup> There are about 300 freshwater fish species in Sumatra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_rhinoceros" title="Sumatran rhinoceros">Sumatran rhinoceros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_elephant" title="Sumatran elephant">Sumatran elephant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_ground_cuckoo" title="Sumatran ground cuckoo">Sumatran ground cuckoo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_orangutan" title="Sumatran orangutan">Sumatran orangutan</a> are all critically endangered, indicating the highest level of threat to their survival. In October 2008, the Indonesian government announced a plan to protect Sumatra's remaining forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island includes more than 10 national parks, including 3 which are listed as the <i><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Rainforest_Heritage_of_Sumatra" title="Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra">Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra</a></i> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> – <a href="/wiki/Gunung_Leuser_National_Park" title="Gunung Leuser National Park">Gunung Leuser National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerinci_Seblat_National_Park" title="Kerinci Seblat National Park">Kerinci Seblat National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bukit_Barisan_Selatan_National_Park" title="Bukit Barisan Selatan National Park">Bukit Barisan Selatan National Park</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Berbak_National_Park" title="Berbak National Park">Berbak National Park</a> is one of three national parks in Indonesia listed as a <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> of international importance under the <a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Convention" title="Ramsar Convention">Ramsar Convention</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Minangprocession.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1054" data-file-width="700" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Minangprocession.jpg/170px-Minangprocession.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Minangprocession.jpg/255px-Minangprocession.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Minangprocession.jpg/340px-Minangprocession.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Minangprocession.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Minangkabau women carrying platters of food to a ceremony</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sumatra is not particularly densely populated, with just over 100 people per km<sup>2</sup> – more than 50 million people in total. Because of its great extent, it is nonetheless the fourth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_population" title="List of islands by population">most populous island in the world</a>. The most populous regions include most of North Sumatra and central highlands in West Sumatra, while the major urban centers are <a href="/wiki/Medan" title="Medan">Medan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palembang" title="Palembang">Palembang</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_groups">Ethnic groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ethnic groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The people of Sumatra are multi-lingual, multi-diverse and multi-religious. Most of these groups share many similar traditions and the different tongues are closely related. <a href="/wiki/Malay_Indonesian" title="Malay Indonesian">Ethnic Malays</a> dominate most of the eastern coast in the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Riau" title="Riau">Riau</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bangka-Belitung" title="Bangka-Belitung">Bangka-Belitung</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Sumatra" title="South Sumatra">South Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riau_Islands" title="Riau Islands">Riau Islands</a>, northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Lampung" title="Lampung">Lampung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bengkulu" title="Bengkulu">Bengkulu</a> and in the easternmost parts of <a href="/wiki/North_Sumatra" title="North Sumatra">North Sumatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a>, while people in the southern and central interior speak languages related to <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lembak_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lembak people (page does not exist)">Lembak</a> (that speak <a href="/wiki/Col_language" title="Col language">Col language</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerinci_people" title="Kerinci people">Kerinci</a> (that speak <a href="/wiki/Kerinci_language" title="Kerinci language">Kerinci language</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minangkabau</a> people (that speak <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_language" title="Minangkabau language">Minangkabau</a>). The highlands of northern Sumatra is inhabited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batak_people" title="Batak people">Bataknese</a>, the northernmost coast is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Acehnese_people" title="Acehnese people">Acehnese</a>, while southernmost coast is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Ethnic Javanese</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesian" title="Chinese Indonesian">Chinese</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamil_Indonesian" title="Tamil Indonesian">Tamil</a> minorities are present in urban centres.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are over 52 <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Indonesia" title="Languages of Indonesia">languages</a> spoken, all of them (except Chinese and Indians) belong to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Nuclear Malayo-Polynesian languages">Nuclear Malayo-Polynesian</a> sub-branch of <a href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Malayo-Polynesian languages">Malayo-Polynesian</a> which in turn is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian</a> language family. Within Nuclear Malayo-Polynesian, they were divided into several sub-branches that is <a href="/wiki/Chamic_languages" title="Chamic languages">Chamic</a> (which are represented by <a href="/wiki/Acehnese_language" title="Acehnese language">Acehnese</a> in which its closest relatives are languages spoken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cham_people" title="Cham people">Ethnic Chams</a> in Cambodia and Vietnam), <a href="/wiki/Malayic_languages" title="Malayic languages">Malayic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_language" title="Minangkabau language">Minangkabau</a> and other closely related languages), <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Sumatran_languages" title="Northwest Sumatran languages">Northwest Sumatran</a> (<a href="/wiki/Batak_languages" title="Batak languages">Batak languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gayo_language" title="Gayo language">Gayo</a> and others), <a href="/wiki/Lampung_language" title="Lampung language">Lampungic</a> (includes Proper Lampung and <a href="/wiki/Komering_language" title="Komering language">Komering</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bornean_languages" title="Bornean languages">Bornean</a> (represented by <a href="/wiki/Rejangese_language" title="Rejangese language">Rejang</a> in which its closest linguistic relatives are <a href="/wiki/Bukar_Sadong_language" title="Bukar Sadong language">Bukar Sadong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_Dayak_languages" title="Land Dayak languages">Land Dayak</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/West_Kalimantan" title="West Kalimantan">West Kalimantan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>)). Northwest Sumatran and Lampungic branches are endemic to the island. Like all parts of Indonesia, <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> (which was based on Riau Malay) is the official language and the main Lingua franca. Although Sumatra has its own local Lingua franca, <a href="/wiki/Malayan_languages" title="Malayan languages">variants of Malay</a> like Medan Malay and <a href="/wiki/Musi_language" title="Musi language">Palembang Malay</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> are popular in North and South Sumatra, especially in urban areas. Minangkabau (Padang dialect)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> is popular in West Sumatra, some parts of North Sumatra, Bengkulu, Jambi and Riau (especially in <a href="/wiki/Pekanbaru" title="Pekanbaru">Pekanbaru</a> and areas bordered with West Sumatra) while Acehnese is also used as an inter-ethnic means of communication in some parts of Aceh province.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meuseujid_Raya.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1248" data-file-width="1664" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Meuseujid_Raya.JPG/220px-Meuseujid_Raya.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Meuseujid_Raya.JPG/330px-Meuseujid_Raya.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Meuseujid_Raya.JPG/440px-Meuseujid_Raya.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meuseujid_Raya.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Baiturrahman_Grand_Mosque" title="Baiturrahman Grand Mosque">Baiturrahman Grand Mosque</a> in <a href="/wiki/Banda_Aceh" title="Banda Aceh">Banda Aceh</a></div>
</div>

<p>A majority of people in Sumatra are Muslims (87%), while 10% are Christians, 2% are Buddhist and 1% are Hindu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Most central Bataks are Protestant Christians, a faith introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhenish_Missionary_Society" title="Rhenish Missionary Society">Rhenish Missionary Society</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_transport">Rail transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rail transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several unconnected railway networks built during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Netherlands_East_Indies" title="Netherlands East Indies">Netherlands East Indies</a> exist in Sumatra, such as the ones connecting <a href="/wiki/Banda_Aceh" title="Banda Aceh">Banda Aceh</a>-<a href="/wiki/Lhokseumawe" title="Lhokseumawe">Lhokseumawe</a>-<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Besitang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Besitang (page does not exist)">Besitang</a>-<a href="/wiki/Medan" title="Medan">Medan</a>-Tebingtinggi-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pematang_Siantar" title="Pematang Siantar">Pematang Siantar</a>-Rantau Prapat in Northern Sumatra (the Banda Aceh-Besitang section was closed in 1971, but is currently being rebuilt).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Padang,_Indonesia" title="Padang, Indonesia">Padang</a>-<a href="/wiki/Solok" title="Solok">Solok</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bukittinggi" title="Bukittinggi">Bukittinggi</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bandar_Lampung" title="Bandar Lampung">Bandar Lampung</a>-<a href="/wiki/Palembang" title="Palembang">Palembang</a>-Lahat-Lubuk Linggau in Southern Sumatra.</p>
