<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sous-vide</h1>

<p><b>Sous-vide</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; French for ‘under vacuum’)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dict_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dict-1">[1]</a></sup> is a method of <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a> in which food is sealed in a vacuum-sealed plastic pouch then placed in a water bath or in a temperature-controlled steam environment for longer than normal cooking times (usually 1 to 7 hours, up to 48 or more in some select cases) at an accurately regulated temperature much lower than normally used for cooking, typically around 55 to 60 °C (131 to 140 °F) for meat and higher for vegetables. The intent is to cook the item evenly, ensuring that the inside is properly cooked without overcooking the outside, and retain moisture.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sous-vide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The method was first described by Sir <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Thompson" title="Benjamin Thompson">Benjamin Thompson</a>, Count Rumford in 1799 (although he used air as the heat transfer medium).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medellitin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medellitin-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was re-discovered by American and French engineers in the mid-1960s and developed into an industrial food preservation method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hesser_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hesser-4">[4]</a></sup> The method was adopted by Georges Pralus in 1974 for the <a href="/wiki/Troisgros_family" title="Troisgros family">Restaurant Troisgros</a> (of Pierre and Michel Troisgros) in <a href="/wiki/Roanne" title="Roanne">Roanne</a>, France. He discovered that when <a href="/wiki/Foie_gras" title="Foie gras">foie gras</a> was cooked in this manner, it kept its original appearance, did not lose excess amounts of fat, and had better texture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hesser_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hesser-4">[4]</a></sup> Another pioneer in sous-vide is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bruno_Goussault&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bruno Goussault (page does not exist)">Bruno Goussault</a>, who further researched the effects of temperature on various foods and became well known for training top chefs in the method. As chief scientist of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria, Virginia</a>-based food manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_Solutions" title="Cuisine Solutions">Cuisine Solutions</a>, Goussault developed the parameters of cooking times and temperatures for various foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hesser_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hesser-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Essential_features">Essential features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sous-vide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Essential features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As may also be done in traditional <a href="/wiki/Poaching_(cooking)" title="Poaching (cooking)">poaching</a>, sealing the food in sturdy plastic bags keeps in juices and aroma that otherwise would be lost in the process.</p>
<p>Placing the food in a water bath, with the temperature having been set at the desired final cooking temperature of the food, avoids overcooking, because the food cannot get hotter than the bath it is in, as in <a href="/wiki/Bain-marie" title="Bain-marie">bain-marie</a>. In conventional high-heat cooking, such as oven <a href="/wiki/Roasting" title="Roasting">roasting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilling</a>, the food is exposed to heat levels that are much higher than the desired internal cooking temperature; the food must be removed from the high heat prior to its reaching the desired cooking temperature. If the food is removed from the heat too late, it becomes overcooked, and if it is removed too early, it is under-cooked. As a result of precise temperature control of the bath and the fact that the bath temperature is the same as the target cooking temperature, very precise control of cooking can be achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup> Additionally, temperature, and thus cooking, can be very even throughout the food in sous-vide cooking, even with irregularly shaped or very thick items, given enough time.</p>
<p>The use of temperatures much lower than for conventional cooking is an equally essential feature of sous-vide, resulting in much higher succulence: at these lower temperatures, cell walls in the food do not burst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hesser_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hesser-4">[4]</a></sup> In the case of meat cooking, tough <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> in connective tissue can be hydrolysed into gelatin, without heating the meat’s <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> high enough that they denature to a degree that the texture toughens and moisture is wrung out of the meat. In contrast, with the cooking of vegetables, where extreme tenderness or softness is seen as undesirably overcooked, the ability of the sous-vide technique to cook vegetables at a temperature below the boiling point of water allows vegetables to be thoroughly cooked (and <a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">pasteurized</a>, if necessary) while maintaining a firm or somewhat crisp texture. While the cell walls will generally not burst, the de-polymerization of the pectic polysaccharides that connect the vegetable cells together and/or the gelatinisation of starch in the vegetable can be achieved without overcooking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>From a culinary viewpoint, the exclusion of air is secondary, but this has practical importance: it allows cooked food to be stored, still sealed and refrigerated, for considerable times, which is especially useful for the catering industry, and it excludes oxygen from food that requires long cooking and is susceptible to oxidation, e.g., fat on meat, which may become <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">rancid</a> with prolonged exposure to air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sous-vide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The degree of accuracy and constancy of cooking temperature required varies with the food cooked. In some cases it is not critical; a 15-millimeter-thick (0.6 in) piece of fish will cook in 17 to 18 minutes at any temperature from 44 °C (111 °F) to 61 °C (142 °F);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup> such food can be cooked in a switched-off slow cooker filled with hot water and a thermometer. But for an egg, which has proteins that denature at different temperatures, it is much more critical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egg_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-egg-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cooking times for normal cooking are determined by when the center of the cooked item reaches a few degrees below the targeted temperature. Then heating should be stopped immediately; while resting the food, residual heat will continue to cook it for a while. If the heating continues, the food will be overcooked. Sous-vide cooking continues until the center of the food has reached its target temperature; if it continues after this, the food will not be overcooked, and it will not cook more after it stops being heated. The time taken for the center of food to reach the target temperature depends on the initial temperature, the thickness and shape of the food, and the temperature of the bath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>One limitation of sous-vide cooking is the fact that browning (<a href="/wiki/Maillard_reaction" title="Maillard reaction">Maillard reactions</a>) happens at much higher temperatures (above the boiling point of water). The flavors and ‘crust’ texture developed by browning are generally seen as very desirable in the cooking of certain types of meat, such as a <a href="/wiki/Steak" title="Steak">steak</a>. The flavors and texture produced by browning cannot be obtained with only the sous-vide technique. In many cases, meats and other foods cooked with the sous-vide technique will be browned before and/or after being placed in the water bath, using techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilling</a> or <a href="/wiki/Searing" title="Searing">searing</a> on an extremely hot pan. This secondary browning is done briefly, and sometimes at higher heat than normally used, so as to affect only the surface of the food and to avoid overcooking the interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup> Similarly, the skin of fish can be cooked at high temperatures after the sous-vide to make the skin crisp.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sous-vide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Food safety is a function of both time and temperature; a temperature usually considered insufficient to render food safe may be perfectly safe if maintained for long enough. Some sous-vide fish recipes, for example, are cooked below 55 °C (131 °F). However, people with <a href="/wiki/Immunodeficiency" title="Immunodeficiency">compromised immunity</a> should never eat food that has not been properly pasteurized. Women eating food cooked <i>sous-vide</i> while <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnant</a> expose themselves and/or their unborn children to risk and thus may choose to avoid unpasteurized recipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_botulinum" title="Clostridium botulinum">Clostridium botulinum</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> can grow in food <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">in the absence of</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and produce the deadly <a href="/wiki/Botulinum_toxin" title="Botulinum toxin">botulinum toxin</a>, so sous-vide cooking must be performed under carefully controlled conditions to avoid <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a> poisoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyyti.C3.A4-Trees_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hyyti.C3.A4-Trees-7">[7]</a></sup> Generally speaking, food that is heated and served within four hours is considered safe, but meat that is cooked for longer to tenderize must reach a temperature of at least 55 °C (131 °F) within four hours and then be kept there for sufficient time, in order to pasteurize the meat.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">Pasteurization</a> kills the botulism <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, but the possibility of hardy botulism <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a> surviving and reactivating once cool remains a concern as with many preserved foods, however processed. For that reason, Baldwin’s treatise specifies precise chilling requirements for ‘cook-chill’, so that the botulism spores do not have the opportunity to grow or propagate. Pasteurised food can then be stored for up to two weeks at around 3 °C (37 °F) sealed within the vacuum pack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The plastic used must not leach <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruptors</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Plasticizer" title="Plasticizer">plasticizers</a> used in plastics have these properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paleo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paleo-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chow_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chow-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sous-vide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The sous-vide method is used in many high end gourmet restaurants by chefs such as <a href="/wiki/Heston_Blumenthal" title="Heston Blumenthal">Heston Blumenthal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blumenthal_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blumenthal-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bocuse" title="Paul Bocuse">Paul Bocuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Carlson" title="Michael Carlson">Michael Carlson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huges_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huges-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Keller" title="Thomas Keller">Thomas Keller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grant_Achatz" title="Grant Achatz">Grant Achatz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferran_Adri%C3%A0" title="Ferran Adrià">Ferran Adrià</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%ABl_Robuchon" title="Joël Robuchon">Joël Robuchon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Rochat" title="Philippe Rochat">Philippe Rochat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Stratta" title="Alessandro Stratta">Alessandro Stratta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Trotter" title="Charlie Trotter">Charlie Trotter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Mina" title="Michael Mina">Michael Mina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joan_Roca" title="Joan Roca">Joan Roca</a> and others. <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> also uses this method for meals served on their trains, including <a href="/wiki/Acela_Express" title="Acela Express">Acela Express</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amtrak_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amtrak-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sous-vide has become a common feature on television cooking shows. It has also been used to quickly produce significant quantities of meals for hurricane evacuees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagon_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagon-13">[13]</a></sup> Non-professional cooks are also beginning to use sous-vide cooking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moskin_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moskin-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, enthusiasts used laboratory-grade <a href="/wiki/Thermal_immersion_circulator" title="Thermal immersion circulator">thermal immersion circulators</a>, often bought used on eBay, which required very careful cleaning and even then were not recommended for kitchen use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Beginning in 2008, Auber Instruments and Fresh Meals Solutions made available comparatively inexpensive yet highly accurate <a href="/wiki/PID_controller" title="PID controller">PID controllers</a> with attached thermocouple probes that could be used to control commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_cookers" title="Rice cookers">rice cookers</a>, slow cookers, electric stock pots, and similar apparatuses. In late 2009 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sous_Vide_Supreme" title="Sous Vide Supreme">Sous Vide Supreme</a> sous-vide machine (which does not circulate the cooking water) and Addélice swid® immersion circulator, both intended for home use and less expensive than laboratory-standard equipment at a few hundred US dollars, went on sale.</p>
<p>More expensive but more universally usable solutions are available in the form of <a href="/wiki/Combi_steamer" title="Combi steamer">combi steamers</a>. Instead of water baths, they feature tightly-controlled steam generation systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V-Zug_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-V-Zug-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2013, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anova_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anova (company) (page does not exist)">Anova</a> released a smaller sous-vide circulator, controllable via mobile app, at an affordable price that made it more accessible for home cooks. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Serious_Eats_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Serious_Eats-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is possible to duplicate some effects of sous-vide techniques <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lopez-Alt_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lopez-Alt-17">[17]</a></sup> through the use of a beer cooler filled with warm water, checked with an accurate thermometer, and <a href="/wiki/Ziploc" title="Ziploc">ziploc</a> bags with the air removed to package the food for cooking. However, the heat loss involved in this technique makes it unfeasible for long-term (4+ hours) cooking.</p>
