<p>The <b>fishing industry</b> includes any industry or activity concerned with taking, culturing, processing, preserving, storing, transporting, marketing or selling fish or fish products. It is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> as including <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">subsistence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishing</a>, and the harvesting, <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">processing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fish_marketing" title="Fish marketing">marketing</a> sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The commercial activity is aimed at the delivery of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> products for human consumption or as input factors in other industrial processes. Directly or indirectly, the livelihood of over 500 million people in developing countries depends on fisheries and aquaculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>There are three principal industry sectors:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>The commercial sector</b>: comprises enterprises and individuals associated with wild-catch or aquaculture resources and the various transformations of those resources into products for sale. It is also referred to as the "seafood industry", although non-food items such as pearls are included among its products.</li>
<li><b>The traditional sector</b>: comprises enterprises and individuals associated with fisheries resources from which aboriginal people derive products in accordance with their traditions.</li>
<li><b>The recreational sector</b>: comprises enterprises and individuals associated for the purpose of recreation, sport or sustenance with fisheries resources from which products are derived that are not for sale.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_sector">Commercial sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The commercial sector of the fishing industry comprises the following chain:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">Commercial fishing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">fish farming</a> which produce the fish</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">Fish processing</a> which produce the <a href="/wiki/Fish_products" title="Fish products">fish products</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fish_marketing" title="Fish marketing">Marketing</a> of the fish products</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_production">World production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_fish_production" title="World fish production">World fish production</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry_by_country" title="Fishing industry by country">Fishing industry by country</a></div>
<p>Fish are harvested by <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_by_country" title="Fishing by country">world harvest</a> in 2005 consisted of 93.3 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> captured by <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fishing</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries_of_the_world" title="Wild fisheries of the world">wild fisheries</a>, plus 48.1 million tonnes produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">fish farms</a>. In addition, 1.3 million tons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_plants" title="Aquatic plants">aquatic plants</a> (<a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> etc.) were captured in wild fisheries and 14.8 million tons were produced by <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO:_Fisheries_and_Aquaculture_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO:_Fisheries_and_Aquaculture-5">[5]</a></sup> The number of individual fish caught in the wild has been estimated at 0.97-2.7 trillion per year (not counting fish farms or marine invertebrates).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mood_and_Brooke.2C_2010_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mood_and_Brooke.2C_2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_fishing">Commercial fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">Commercial fishing</a></div>
<p>The top producing countries were, in order, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (excluding <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>), <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. Those countries accounted for more than half of the world's production; China alone accounted for a third of the world's production.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_farming">Fish farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fish farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mariculture" title="Mariculture">Mariculture</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">Fish farm</a></div>
<p>Aquaculture is the cultivation of <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic</a> organisms. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, aquaculture, also known as aquafarming, is the cultivation of aquatic populations under controlled conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericanHeritageDef_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanHeritageDef-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mariculture" title="Mariculture">Mariculture</a> refers to aquaculture practiced in marine environments. Particular kinds of aquaculture include <a href="/wiki/Algaculture" title="Algaculture">algaculture</a> (the production of <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a>/<a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>); <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farming</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrimp_farm" title="Shrimp farm">shrimp farming</a>, shellfish farming, and the growing of <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">cultured pearls</a>.</p>
<p>Fish farming involves raising fish commercially in tanks or enclosed pools, usually for food. Fish species raised by fish farms include <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tilapia" title="Tilapia">tilapia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>. Increasing demands on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries_of_the_world" title="Wild fisheries of the world">wild fisheries</a> by commercial fishing operations have caused widespread <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>. Fish farming offers an alternative solution to the increasing <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> <a href="/wiki/Demand" title="Demand">demand</a> for <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and fish <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_processing">Fish processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fish processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">Fish processing</a></div>
<p>Fish processing is the processing of fish delivered by commercial fisheries and fish farms. The larger fish processing companies have their own fishing fleets and independent fisheries. The products of the industry are usually sold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesale" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grocery_chain" title="Grocery chain">grocery chains</a> or to intermediaries.</p>
<p>Fish processing can be subdivided into two categories: fish handling (the initial processing of raw fish) and fish products manufacturing. Aspects of fish processing occur on <a href="/wiki/Fishing_vessel" title="Fishing vessel">fishing vessels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_processing_vessel" title="Fish processing vessel">fish processing vessels</a>, and at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_processing_plant" title="Fish processing plant">fish processing plants</a>.</p>
<p>Another natural subdivision is into primary processing involved in the filleting and freezing of fresh fish for onward distribution to fresh fish retail and catering outlets, and the secondary processing that produces chilled, frozen and canned products for the retail and catering trades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_products">Fish products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fish products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fish_products" title="Fish products">Fish products</a></div>
<p>Fisheries are estimated to currently provide 16% of the world population's <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>. The flesh of many fish are primarily valued as a source of food; there are many edible species of fish. Other marine life taken as food includes <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustaceans" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_cucumber_(food)" title="Sea cucumber (food)">sea cucumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">roe</a>.</p>
<p>Fish and other marine life can also be used for many other uses: <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nacre" title="Nacre">mother-of-pearl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharkskin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rajiformes" title="Rajiformes">rayskin</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_horse" title="Sea horse">Sea horses</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_fish" title="Star fish">star fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holothuroidea" title="Holothuroidea">sea cucumber</a> are used in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional Chinese medicine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tyrian_purple" title="Tyrian purple">Tyrian purple</a> is a pigment made from marine snails, <a href="/wiki/Sepia_(color)" title="Sepia (color)">sepia</a> is a pigment made from the inky secretions of <a href="/wiki/Cuttlefish" title="Cuttlefish">cuttlefish</a>. <a href="/wiki/Animal_glue" title="Animal glue">Fish glue</a> has long been valued for its use in all manner of products. <a href="/wiki/Isinglass" title="Isinglass">Isinglass</a> is used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarification_(wine)" title="Clarification (wine)">clarification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fish_emulsion" title="Fish emulsion">Fish emulsion</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a> that is produced from the fluid remains of fish processed for <a href="/wiki/Fish_oil" title="Fish oil">fish oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_meal" title="Fish meal">fish meal</a>.</p>
<p>In the industry the term <i><a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> products</i> is often used instead of <i>fish products</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_marketing">Fish marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fish marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fish_marketing" title="Fish marketing">Fish marketing</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fish_market" title="Fish market">Fish markets</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marketplace" title="Marketplace">marketplace</a> used for the <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> in and sale of fish and other <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a>. They can be dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesale" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> trade between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishermen" title="Fishermen">fishermen</a> and fish <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchants</a>, or to the sale of seafood to individual <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a>, or to both. Retail fish markets, a type of <a href="/wiki/Wet_market" title="Wet market">wet market</a>, often sell <a href="/wiki/Street_food" title="Street food">street food</a> as well.</p>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a> are sold frozen and are <a href="/wiki/Shrimp_marketing" title="Shrimp marketing">marketed</a> in different categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_food_fish_trade" title="Live food fish trade">live food fish trade</a> is a global system that links fishing communities with markets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_sector">Traditional sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Traditional sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">Artisan fishing</a></div>
<p>The traditional fishing industry, or artisan fishing, are terms used to describe small scale <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence" title="Subsistence">subsistence</a> fishing practises, particularly using traditional techniques such as rod and tackle, arrows and harpoons, throw nets and drag nets, etc. It does not usually cover the concept of fishing for sport, and might be used when talking about the pressures between large scale modern commercial fishing practises and traditional methods, or when aid programs are targeted specifically at fishing at or near subsistence levels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreational_sector">Recreational sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Recreational sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">Recreational fishing</a></div>
<p>The recreational fishing industry consists of enterprises such as the manufacture and retailing of <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tackle" title="Fishing tackle">fishing tackle</a> and apparel, the payment of license fees to regulatory authorities, fishing books and magazines, the design and building of recreational fishing boats, and the provision of accommodation, fishing boats for charter, and guided fishing adventures.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_disputes">International disputes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: International disputes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ocean covers 71% of the earth's surface and 80% of the value of exploited marine resources are attributed to the fishing industry. The fishing industry has provoked various international disputes as wild fish capture rose to a peak about the turn of the century, and has since started a gradual decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Iceland, Japan, and Portugal are the greatest consumers of seafood per capita in the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes_in_the_Americas">Disputes in the Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Disputes in the Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> are countries with high fish consumption, and therefore had troubles regarding fish industry. In 1947, Chile and Peru first adopted the 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_miles" title="Nautical miles">nautical miles</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">Exclusive Economic Zone</a> for their shore, and in 1982, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN" title="UN">UN</a> formally adopted this term. In the 2000s, Chile and Peru suffered serious fish crisis because of excessive fishing and lack of proper regulations, and now political power play in the area is rekindled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> From the late 1950s, offshore bottom trawlers began exploiting the deeper part, leading to a large catch increase and a strong decline in the underlying biomass. The stock collapsed to extremely low levels in the early 1990s and this is a well-known example of non-excludable, non-rivalrous <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public good</a> in economics, causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-rider" title="Free-rider">free-rider</a> problems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes_in_Europe">Disputes in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Disputes in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> is one of the largest consumers in the world and in 1972, a dispute occurred between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> and Iceland because of Iceland’s announcement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">Exclusive Economic Zone</a> (EEZ) to reduce overfishing. This dispute is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cod_War" title="Cod War">Cod War</a>, direct confrontations between Icelandic patrol vessels and British warships. Nowadays in Europe in general, countries are searching for a way to recover fishing industry. Overfishing of <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> fisheries is costing 3.2 billion euros a year and 100,000 jobs according to a report. So Europe is constantly looking for some collective actions to prevent overfishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_article_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_article-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes_in_Asia">Disputes in Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishing_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Disputes in Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> are some of the greatest consumers of fish, and have some disputes over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">Exclusive Economic Zone</a>. In 2011, due to a serious earthquake, the nuclear power facility in <a href="/wiki/Fukushima,_Fukushima" title="Fukushima, Fukushima">Fukushima</a> was damaged. Ever since, huge amount of contaminated water leaked and is entering the oceans. <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Electric_Power_Company" title="Tokyo Electric Power Company">Tokyo Electric Power Company</a> (Tepco) admitted that around 300 tonnes of highly radioactive water had leaked from a storage tank on the site. In the <a href="/wiki/Kuroshio_Current" title="Kuroshio Current">Kuroshio Current</a>, the sea near Fukushima, about 11 countries catch fish. Not only the surrounding countries such as Japan, Korea and China, but also the countries like <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> have boats in the Kuroshio Current. In September 2013, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> banned all fish imports from eight Japanese prefectures, concerning radioactive water leak from the Fukushima nuclear plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_article_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_article-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
