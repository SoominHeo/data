<p><b>Sodium hydroxide</b> (<b>NaOH</b>), also known as <b>lye</b> and <b>caustic soda</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msd_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msd-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msd2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-msd2-2">[2]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a>. It is a white solid and highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">caustic</a> metallic <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> which is available in pellets, flakes, granules, and as prepared solutions at different concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Sodium hydroxide forms an approximately 50% (by mass) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_solution" title="Saturated solution">saturated solution</a> with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is soluble in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deliquescent" title="Deliquescent">deliquescent</a> and readily absorbs <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>.</p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is used in many industries, mostly as a strong <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical</a> <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">base</a> in the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">pulp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soaps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/Drain_cleaner" title="Drain cleaner">drain cleaner</a>. Worldwide production in 2004 was approximately 60 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>, while demand was 51 million tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pure sodium hydroxide is a whitish solid, sold in pellets, flakes, and granular form, as well as in solution. It is highly soluble in water, with a lower solubility in <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, but is insoluble in ether and other non-polar solvents.</p>
<p>Similar to the hydration of sulfuric acid, <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolution</a> of solid sodium hydroxide in water is a highly <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> in which a large amount of heat is liberated, posing a threat to safety through the possibility of splashing. The resulting solution is usually colourless and odorless. As with other alkaline solutions, it feels slippery when it comes in contact with skin.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_acids">Reaction with acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaction with acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sodium hydroxide reacts with protic acids to produce water and the corresponding salts. For example, when sodium hydroxide reacts with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> is formed:</p>
<dl>
<dd><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NaOH" title="NaOH">NaOH</a>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous" title="Aqueous">aq</a>) + <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">HCl</a>(aq) → <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">NaCl</a>(aq) + <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>(<a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">l</a>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>In general, such <a href="/wiki/Neutralization_(chemistry)" title="Neutralization (chemistry)">neutralization</a> reactions are represented by one simple net ionic equation:</p>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">OH<sup>−</sup></a>(aq) + <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">H<sup>+</sup></a>(aq) → <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>(l)</dd>
</dl>
<p>This type of reaction with a strong acid releases heat, and hence is <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a>. Such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction" title="Acid-base reaction">acid-base reactions</a> can also be used for <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titrations</a>. However, sodium hydroxide is not used as a <a href="/wiki/Primary_standard" title="Primary standard">primary standard</a> because it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a> and absorbs <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from air.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_acidic_oxides">Reaction with acidic oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reaction with acidic oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sodium hydroxide also reacts with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_anhydride" title="Acid anhydride">acidic oxides</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>. Such reactions are often used to "<a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrub</a>" harmful acidic gases (like SO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>S) produced in the burning of coal and thus prevent their release into the atmosphere. For example,</p>
<dl>
<dd>2 NaOH + <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">SO<sub>2</sub></a> → <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfite" title="Sodium sulfite">Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub></a> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_amphoteric_metals_and_oxides">Reaction with amphoteric metals and oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reaction with amphoteric metals and oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Glass reacts slowly with aqueous sodium hydroxide solutions at ambient temperatures to form soluble <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>. Because of this, glass joints and <a href="/wiki/Stopcock" title="Stopcock">stopcocks</a> exposed to sodium hydroxide have a tendency to "freeze". <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_flask" title="Laboratory flask">Flasks</a> and glass-lined <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reactor" title="Chemical reactor">chemical reactors</a> are damaged by long exposure to hot sodium hydroxide, which also frosts the glass. Sodium hydroxide does not attack <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> since iron does not have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> properties (i.e., it only dissolves in acid, not base). A few <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a>, however, may react vigorously with sodium hydroxide.</p>
<p>In 1986, an aluminium <a href="/wiki/Tank_truck" title="Tank truck">road tanker</a> in the UK was mistakenly used to transport 25% sodium hydroxide solution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> causing pressurization of the contents and damage to the tanker. The pressurization was due to the hydrogen gas which is produced in the reaction between sodium hydroxide and aluminium:</p>
<dl>
<dd>2 Al + 2 NaOH + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 NaAl(OH)<sub>4</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitant">Precipitant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Precipitant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unlike sodium hydroxide, the hydroxides of most transition metals are insoluble, and therefore sodium hydroxide can be used to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a> transition metal hydroxides. The following colours are observed: blue-copper, green-iron(II), yellow/brown-iron(III). Zinc and lead salts dissolve in excess sodium hydroxide to give a clear solution of Na<sub>2</sub>ZnO<sub>2</sub> or Na<sub>2</sub>PbO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">Aluminium hydroxide</a> is used as a gelatinous <a href="/wiki/Flocculation#Flocculants" title="Flocculation">flocculant</a> to filter out particulate matter in <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a>. Aluminium hydroxide is prepared at the treatment plant from <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_sulfate" title="Aluminium sulfate">aluminium sulfate</a> by reacting it with sodium hydroxide or carbonate.</p>
<dl>
<dd>Al<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 6 NaOH → 2 Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub></dd>
<dd>Al<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 6 NaHCO<sub>3</sub> → 2 Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> + 6 CO<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Saponification">Saponification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Saponification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sodium hydroxide can be used for the base-driven <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> (as in <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">saponification</a>), <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a>. However, the limited solubility of sodium hydroxide in organic solvents means that the more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soluble" title="Soluble">soluble</a> <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> (KOH) is often preferred. Touching sodium hydroxide solution with the bare hands, while not recommended, produces a slippery feeling. This happens because the oils of the hand are converted to soap.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is industrially produced as a 50% solution by variations of the electrolytic <a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">chloralkali process</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorine_gas" title="Chlorine gas">Chlorine gas</a> is also produced in this process. Solid sodium hydroxide is obtained from this solution by the evaporation of water. Solid sodium hydroxide is most commonly sold as flakes, <a href="/wiki/Prill" title="Prill">prills</a>, and cast blocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, world production was estimated at 60 million dry metric tonnes of sodium hydroxide, and demand was estimated at 51 million tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1998, total world production was around 45 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. North America and Asia each contributed around 14 million tonnes, while Europe produced around 10 million tonnes. In the United States, the major producer of sodium hydroxide is the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical Company</a>, which has annual production around 3.7 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> from sites at <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_Texas" title="Freeport, Texas">Freeport</a>, Texas, and <a href="/wiki/Plaquemine,_Louisiana" title="Plaquemine, Louisiana">Plaquemine</a>, Louisiana. Other major US producers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxychem" title="Oxychem">Oxychem</a>, <a href="/wiki/PPG_Industries" title="PPG Industries">PPG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olin_Corporation" title="Olin Corporation">Olin</a>, Pioneer Companies, Inc. (PIONA, which was purchased by Olin), and <a href="/wiki/Formosa_Plastics_Group" title="Formosa Plastics Group">Formosa</a>. All of these companies use the <a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">chloralkali process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, sodium hydroxide was produced by treating <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metathesis_reaction" title="Metathesis reaction">metathesis reaction</a>. (Sodium hydroxide is soluble while calcium carbonate is not.) This process was called causticizing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>(aq) + Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(s) → CaCO<sub>3</sub> ↓ + 2 NaOH(aq)</dd>
</dl>
<p>This process was superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Solvay_process" title="Solvay process">Solvay process</a> in the late 19th century, which was in turn supplanted by the <a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">chloralkali process</a> which we use today.</p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is also produced by combining pure sodium metal with water. The byproducts are hydrogen gas and heat, often resulting in a flame, making this a common demonstration of the reactivity of alkali metals in academic environments; however, it is not commercially viable, as the isolation of sodium metal is typically performed by reduction or electrolysis of sodium compounds including sodium hydroxide.</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>For further information in historical production, see <a href="/wiki/Alkali_manufacture" title="Alkali manufacture">alkali manufacture</a>.</i></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lye.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="440" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1c/Lye.jpg/220px-Lye.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1c/Lye.jpg/330px-Lye.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/1c/Lye.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lye.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Canister of sodium hydroxide.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is a popular strong <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> used in industry. Around 56% of sodium hydroxide produced is used by industry, 25% of which is used in the paper industry. Sodium hydroxide is also used in the manufacture of sodium salts and detergents, pH regulation, and organic synthesis. It is used in the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_process" title="Bayer process">Bayer process</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-12">[12]</a></sup> In bulk, it is most often handled as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous" title="Aqueous">aqueous</a> <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> since solutions are cheaper and easier to handle.</p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is used in many scenarios where it is desirable to increase the <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> of a mixture, or to neutralize acids.</p>
<p>For example, in the petroleum industry, sodium hydroxide is used as an additive in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drilling_mud" title="Drilling mud">drilling mud</a> to increase <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bentonite" title="Bentonite">bentonite</a> mud systems, to increase the mud <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, and to neutralize any <a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gas</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>) which may be encountered in the <a href="/wiki/Geological_formation" title="Geological formation">geological formation</a> as drilling progresses.</p>
<p>Poor quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> can be treated with sodium hydroxide to remove <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfurous</a> impurities in a process known as <i>caustic washing</i>. As above, sodium hydroxide reacts with weak acids such as hydrogen sulfide and mercaptans to yield non-volatile sodium salts, which can be removed. The waste which is formed is toxic and difficult to deal with, and the process is banned in many countries because of this. In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Trafigura" title="Trafigura">Trafigura</a> used the process and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006_C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire_toxic_waste_dump" title="2006 Côte d'Ivoire toxic waste dump">dumped the waste in Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization" title="Hydrodesulfurization">Hydrodesulfurization</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_pulping">Chemical pulping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chemical pulping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)" title="Pulp (paper)">Pulp (paper)</a></div>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is also widely used in pulping of wood for making paper or regenerated fibers. Along with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfide" title="Sodium sulfide">sodium sulfide</a>, sodium hydroxide is a key component of the white liquor solution used to separate <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fibers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">kraft process</a>. It also plays a key role in several later stages of the process of <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">bleaching the brown pulp</a> resulting from the pulping process. These stages include <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> delignification, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidative</a> extraction, and simple extraction, all of which require a strong alkaline environment with a pH &gt; 10.5 at the end of the stages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tissue_digestion">Tissue digestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tissue digestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a similar fashion, sodium hydroxide is used to digest tissues, as in a process that was used with farm animals at one time. This process involved placing a carcass into a sealed chamber, then adding a mixture of sodium hydroxide and water (which breaks the chemical bonds that keep the flesh intact). This eventually turns the body into a liquid with coffee-like appearance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ayres_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ayres-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carcassdisposal_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-carcassdisposal-21">[21]</a></sup> and the only solid that remains are bone hulls, which could be crushed between one's fingertips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roach_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roach-22">[22]</a></sup> Sodium hydroxide is frequently used in the process of decomposing <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">roadkill</a> dumped in landfills by animal disposal contractors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carcassdisposal_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-carcassdisposal-21">[21]</a></sup> Due to its low cost and availability, it has been used to dispose of corpses by criminals. Italian <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killer</a> <a href="/wiki/Leonarda_Cianciulli" title="Leonarda Cianciulli">Leonarda Cianciulli</a> used this chemical to turn dead bodies into soap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In Mexico, a man who worked for drug cartels admitted disposing of over 300 bodies with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Sodium hydroxide is a dangerous chemical due to its ability to hydrolyze protein. If a dilute solution is spilled on the skin, burns may result if the area is not washed thoroughly and for several minutes with running water. Splashes in the eye can be more serious and can lead to blindness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This danger is often overlooked.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolving_amphoteric_metals_and_compounds">Dissolving amphoteric metals and compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dissolving amphoteric metals and compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strong bases attack <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>. Sodium hydroxide reacts with aluminium and water to release hydrogen gas. The aluminium takes the oxygen atom from sodium hydroxide, which in turn takes the oxygen atom from the water, and releases the two hydrogen atoms, The reaction thus produces <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminate" title="Sodium aluminate">sodium aluminate</a>. In this reaction, sodium hydroxide acts as an agent to make the solution alkaline, which aluminium can dissolve in. This reaction can be useful in etching, removing anodizing, or converting a polished surface to a satin-like finish, but without further <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Anodizing" title="Anodizing">anodizing</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alodining" title="Alodining">alodining</a> the surface may become degraded, either under normal use or in severe atmospheric conditions.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_process" title="Bayer process">Bayer process</a>, sodium hydroxide is used in the refining of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> containing ores (<a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>) to produce alumina (aluminium oxide) which is the raw material used to produce <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> metal via the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolytic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall-H%C3%A9roult_process" title="Hall-Héroult process">Hall-Héroult process</a>. Since the alumina is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>, it dissolves in the sodium hydroxide, leaving impurities less soluble at high pH such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_oxides" title="Iron oxides">iron oxides</a> behind in the form of a highly alkaline <a href="/wiki/Red_mud" title="Red mud">red mud</a>.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ajka_alumina_plant_accident" title="Ajka alumina plant accident">Ajka alumina plant accident</a></div>
<p>Other amphoteric metals are zinc and lead which dissolve in concentrated sodium hydroxide solutions to give <a href="/wiki/Sodium_zincate" title="Sodium zincate">sodium zincate</a> and sodium plumbate respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Esterification_and_transesterification_reagent">Esterification and transesterification reagent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Esterification and transesterification reagent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is traditionally used in soap making (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_process" title="Cold process">cold process</a> soap, <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">saponification</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morfit_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morfit-26">[26]</a></sup> It was made in the nineteenth century for a hard surface rather than liquid product because it was easier to store and transport.</p>
<p>For the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>, sodium hydroxide is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a> of methanol and triglycerides. This only works with <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> sodium hydroxide, because combined with water the fat would turn into <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, which would be tainted with <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. NaOH is used more often than <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> because it is cheaper and a smaller quantity is needed.</p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is also being used experimentally in a new technology to create synthetic <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_preparation">Food preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Food preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food uses of sodium hydroxide include washing or chemical peeling of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruits" title="Fruits">fruits</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetables" title="Vegetables">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cocoa_mass" title="Cocoa mass">cocoa</a> processing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caramel_coloring" title="Caramel coloring">caramel coloring</a> production, <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> scalding, <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drink</a> processing, and thickening <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">Olives</a> are often soaked in sodium hydroxide for softening; <a href="/wiki/Pretzel" title="Pretzel">Pretzels</a> and German <a href="/wiki/Lye_roll" title="Lye roll">lye rolls</a> are glazed with a sodium hydroxide solution before baking to make them crisp. Owing to the difficulty in obtaining food grade sodium hydroxide in small quantities for home use, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> is often used in place of sodium hydroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hominy_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hominy-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Specific foods processed with sodium hydroxide include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> dish known as <a href="/wiki/Lutefisk" title="Lutefisk">lutefisk</a> (from <i>lutfisk</i>, "lye fish").</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hominy" title="Hominy">Hominy</a> is dried <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> (corn) kernels reconstituted by soaking in <a href="/wiki/Lye" title="Lye">lye</a>-water. These expand considerably in size and may be further processed by frying to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn_nuts" title="Corn nuts">corn nuts</a> or by drying and grinding to make <a href="/wiki/Grits" title="Grits">grits</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nixtamal" title="Nixtamal">Nixtamal</a> is similar, but uses <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a> instead of sodium hydroxide.</li>
<li>Sodium hydroxide is also the chemical that causes gelling of egg whites in the production of <a href="/wiki/Century_egg" title="Century egg">Century eggs</a>.</li>
<li>German <a href="/wiki/Pretzel" title="Pretzel">pretzels</a> are poached in a boiling <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> solution or cold sodium hydroxide solution before baking, which contributes to their unique crust.</li>
<li>Lye-water is an essential ingredient in the crust of the traditional baked Chinese moon cakes.</li>
<li>Most yellow coloured <a href="/wiki/Chinese_noodles" title="Chinese noodles">Chinese noodles</a> are made with <a href="/wiki/Lye" title="Lye">lye</a>-water but are commonly mistaken for containing egg.</li>
<li>Some methods of preparing olives involve subjecting them to a lye-based brine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Filipino dessert (<i>kakanin</i>) called <i><a href="/wiki/Kutsinta" title="Kutsinta">kutsinta</a></i> uses a small quantity of lye water to help give the rice flour batter a jelly like consistency. A similar process is also used in the kakanin known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitsi-pitsi" title="Pitsi-pitsi">pitsi-pitsi</a></i> or <i>pichi-pichi</i> except that the mixture uses grated <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> instead of rice flour.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaning_agent">Cleaning agent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cleaning agent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cleaning_agent" title="Cleaning agent">Cleaning agent</a></div>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is frequently used as an industrial <a href="/wiki/Cleaning_agent" title="Cleaning agent">cleaning agent</a> where it is often called "caustic". It is added to water, heated, and then used to clean process equipment, storage tanks, etc. It can dissolve <a href="/wiki/Grease_(lubricant)" title="Grease (lubricant)">grease</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oils" title="Oils">oils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>-based deposits. It is also used for cleaning waste discharge pipes under sinks and drains in domestic properties. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfactants" title="Surfactants">Surfactants</a> can be added to the sodium hydroxide solution in order to stabilize dissolved substances and thus prevent redeposition. A sodium hydroxide soak solution is used as a powerful degreaser on <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> and glass bakeware. It is also a common ingredient in oven cleaners.</p>
<p>A common use of sodium hydroxide is in the production of <a href="/wiki/Parts_washer" title="Parts washer">parts washer</a> <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a>. Parts washer detergents based on sodium hydroxide are some of the most aggressive parts washer cleaning chemicals. The sodium hydroxide-based detergents include surfactants, rust inhibitors and defoamers. A parts washer heats water and the detergent in a closed cabinet and then sprays the heated sodium hydroxide and hot water at pressure against dirty parts for degreasing applications. Sodium hydroxide used in this manner replaced many solvent-based systems in the early 1990s<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> when <a href="/wiki/1,1,1-Trichloroethane" title="1,1,1-Trichloroethane">trichloroethane</a> was outlawed by the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>. Water and sodium hydroxide detergent-based parts washers are considered to be an environmental improvement over the solvent-based cleaning methods.</p>

<p>Sodium hydroxide is used in the home as a type of <a href="/wiki/Drain_cleaner#Alkaline_drain_openers" title="Drain cleaner">drain opener</a> to unblock clogged drains, usually in the form of a dry crystal or as a thick liquid gel. The alkali dissolves <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">greases</a> to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_soluble" title="Water soluble">water soluble</a> <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a>. It also <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzes</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> such as those found in <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a> which may block water pipes. These reactions are sped by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">heat generated</a> when sodium hydroxide and the other chemical components of the cleaner dissolve in water. Such <a href="/wiki/Drain_cleaner#Alkaline_drain_openers" title="Drain cleaner">alkaline drain cleaners</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Drain_cleaner#Acidic_drain_openers" title="Drain cleaner">acidic versions</a> are highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">corrosive</a> and should be handled with great caution.</p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is used in some <a href="/wiki/Relaxer" title="Relaxer">relaxers</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straighten_hair" title="Straighten hair">straighten hair</a>. However, because of the high incidence and intensity of chemical burns, manufacturers of chemical relaxers use other alkaline chemicals in preparations available to average consumers. Sodium hydroxide relaxers are still available, but they are used mostly by professionals.</p>
<p>A solution of sodium hydroxide in water was traditionally used as the most common paint stripper on wooden objects. Its use has become less common, because it can damage the wood surface, raising the grain and staining the colour.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_treatment">Water treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Water treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is sometimes used during <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">water purification</a> to raise the pH of water supplies. Increased pH makes the water less corrosive to plumbing and reduces the amount of lead, copper and other toxic metals that can dissolve into drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_uses">Historical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Historical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium hydroxide has been used for detection of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">carbon monoxide poisoning</a>, with blood samples of such patients turning to a <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a> color upon the addition of a few drops of sodium hydroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Today, carbon monoxide poisoning can be detected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO_oximetry" title="CO oximetry">CO oximetry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Cement_mixes.2C_mortars.2C_concrete.2C_grouts">In Cement mixes, mortars, concrete, grouts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: In Cement mixes, mortars, concrete, grouts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is used in some cement mix plasticisers. This helps Homogenise cement mixes, preventing segregation of sands and cement, decreases the amount of water required in a mix and increases workability of the cement product, be it mortar, render or concrete.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Summer_-winter_heat_storage">Summer -winter heat storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Summer -winter heat storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EMPA" title="EMPA">EMPA researchers</a> are experimenting with concentrated sodium hydroxide (NaOH) as the thermal storage or seasonal reservoir medium for domestic <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">space-heating</a>. If water is added to solid or concentrated sodium hydroxide (NaOH), heat is released. The dilution is <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_process" title="Exothermic process">exothermic</a> - chemical energy is released in the form of heat. Conversely, by applying heat energy into a dilute sodium hydroxide solution the water will evaporate so that the solution become more concentrated and thus store the supplied heat as <a href="/wiki/Endothermic_process" title="Endothermic process">latent chemical energy</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>]</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1466" data-file-width="1166" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png/220px-Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png/330px-Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png/440px-Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sodium_hydroxide_burn.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">Chemical burns</a> caused by sodium hydroxide solution photographed 44 hours after exposure.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">corrosive</a> <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a>, drops of sodium hydroxide solutions can readily decompose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">living tissues</a> via <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis#Esters_and_amides" title="Hydrolysis">amide hydrolysis and ester hydrolysis</a>, which consequently cause <a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">chemical burns</a> and may induce permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a> upon contact with eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msd_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-msd-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msd2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-msd2-2">[2]</a></sup> Solid alkali can also express its corrosive nature if there is water, such as water vapor. Thus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protective_equipment" title="Protective equipment">protective equipment</a>, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber_gloves" title="Rubber gloves">rubber gloves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_clothing" title="Safety clothing">safety clothing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eye_protection" title="Eye protection">eye protection</a>, should always be used when handling this chemical or its solutions. The standard first aid measures for alkali spills on the skin is, as for other corrosives, irrigation with large quantities of water. Washing is continued for at least ten to fifteen minutes.</p>
<p>Moreover, <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolution</a> of sodium hydroxide is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>, and the resulting heat may cause heat burns or ignite flammables. It also produces heat when reacted with acids.</p>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is corrosive to several metals, like <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> which reacts with the alkali to produce flammable <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas on contact:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>2 Al + 6 NaOH → 3 H<sub>2</sub> + 2 Na<sub>3</sub>AlO<sub><b>3</b></sub></dd>
<dd>2 Al + 2 NaOH + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O → 3 H<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaAlO<sub><b>2</b></sub></dd>
<dd>2 Al + 2 NaOH + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O → 3 H<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaAl(OH)<sub><b>4</b></sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>Sodium hydroxide is also mildly corrosive to <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, which can cause damage to <a href="/wiki/Glazing_(window)" title="Glazing (window)">glazing</a> or cause <a href="/wiki/Ground_glass_joint" title="Ground glass joint">ground glass joints</a> to bind. Careful storage is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sodium hydroxide was first prepared by soap makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> A procedure for making sodium hydroxide appeared as part of a recipe for making soap in an Arab book of the late 13th century: <i>Al-mukhtara` fi funun min al-suna`</i> (Inventions from the Various Industrial Arts), which was compiled by al-Muzaffar Yusuf ibn `Umar ibn `Ali ibn Rasul (d. 1295), a king of Yemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The recipe called for passing water repeatedly through a mixture of <i><a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a></i> (Arabic: <i>al-qily</i>, where <i>qily</i> is ash from <a href="/wiki/Glasswort" title="Glasswort">saltwort</a> plants, which are rich in sodium ; hence <i>alkali</i> was impure <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and quicklime (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, CaO), whereby a solution of sodium hydroxide was obtained. European soap makers also followed this recipe. When in 1791 the French chemist and surgeon <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Leblanc" title="Nicolas Leblanc">Nicolas Leblanc</a> (1742–1806) patented a <a href="/wiki/Leblanc_process" title="Leblanc process">process for mass-producing sodium carbonate</a>, natural "soda ash" (impure sodium carbonate that was obtained from the ashes of plants that are rich in sodium)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> was replaced by this artificial version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> However, by the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">electrolysis of sodium chloride</a> had become the primary method for producing sodium hydroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
