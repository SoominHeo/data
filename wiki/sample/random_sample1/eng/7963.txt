<p><b>Suseok</b> (수석), also called <b>viewing stones</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> term for small naturally occurring or shaped <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> which are traditionally valued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Such stones are similar to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_scholar%27s_rocks" title="Chinese scholar's rocks">Chinese scholar's rocks</a> and Japanese <a href="/wiki/Suiseki" title="Suiseki">suiseki</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brokaw73_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-brokaw73-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Suseok</i> can be any color. There are a wide variety of sizes. <i>Suseok</i> can weigh hundreds of pounds or less than one pound. The term also means stones which are placed in traditional Korean gardens.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suseok&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Suseok</i> began as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Votive_art&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Votive art (page does not exist)">votive art</a> over 3000 years ago, and began to be seen as worthy of scholars a thousand years ago. The art usually works on three scales: large installations of monumental shaped stones as ornamental gates; medium-sized shaped stones for landscape decoration within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_gardens" title="Korean gardens">Korean gardens</a>; and the smaller shaped stones for scholar's tables which is the most important.</p>
<p>Chinese scholar's rocks influenced the development of <i>suseok</i> in Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brokaw73_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-brokaw73-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suseok&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early on important sites within landscape were marked with shaped stones, much as those distance markers on post roads. Burial sites were as well given permanent marking by large scale tumulus or mounds, often surrounded by anthropomorphic shaped stones much akin to that of <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> or <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>' memory markers. The shamanistic belief of nature being alive, and large scaled elements of nature having souls, has led to the continued use of massive sculpted stone in natural forms throughout Korean traditional entranceways as the firstgrowth cedarwood for gates is now rare.</p>
<p>As Confucian scholarship ascended into the golden age of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon</a> dynasty, scholar rocks became an essential fixture of the writing tables of the <a href="/wiki/Yangban" title="Yangban">yangban</a> class of scholars, and a brilliant example of <a href="/wiki/Confucian_art" title="Confucian art">Confucian art</a>. The repeated Japanese invasions of Korea, culminating with the 50 year occupation period from 1895 to 1945, saw great theft of Korean scholar stones as well as theft of larger pieces taken to Japan from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_gardens" title="Korean gardens">Korean gardens</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seon_garden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seon garden (page does not exist)">Seon garden</a> and put into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zen_garden" title="Zen garden">Zen garden</a> without reference to origins. Recreating a historical approach to Korean scholar stones is difficult, but it is being done.</p>
<p>Smaller ceramic versions of scholar's rocks have been seen cast in celadon, and used as brush-holders, and as well as water droppers for scholar's <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphy</a> - particularly in the shape of small mountains.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genres_of_Korean_stone_art">Genres of Korean stone art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suseok&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genres of Korean stone art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>mountain view (horizontal and vertical)</li>
<li>shaped jade mountains</li>
<li>shaped rock crystal mountains</li>
<li>abstract shape</li>
<li>overhanging shapes</li>
<li>organic mineral shapes (calcites, pyrites)</li>
<li>stalactite and stalagmite stelae</li>
<li>shamanistic shape</li>
<li>single stone buddhas</li>
<li>multiple stone buddhas</li>
<li>astrological year figures (dragon, snake, monkey etc.)</li>
<li>tree and house shapes</li>
<li>fossilized fish</li>
<li>fossilized insects</li>
<li>stalactite and stalagmite stelae</li>
<li>enhanced coloured stones</li>
</ul>
