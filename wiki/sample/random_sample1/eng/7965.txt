<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Suessiones</h1>

<p>The <b>Suessiones</b> were a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgic" title="Belgic">Belgic</a> tribe of western <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Belgica" title="Gallia Belgica">Gallia Belgica</a> in the 1st century BC, inhabiting the region between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oise_River" title="Oise River">Oise</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marne_River" title="Marne River">Marne</a>, around the present-day city of <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">Soissons</a>. They were conquered in 57 BC by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> apparently gives their name as 'Suaeuconi'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Caesar recounts in his <i><a href="/wiki/Gallic_Wars" title="Gallic Wars">Gallic Wars</a></i> that in 57 BC the Suessiones were ruled by <a href="/wiki/Galba_(Suessiones)" title="Galba (Suessiones)">Galba</a>, and that in living memory of that time their king <a href="/wiki/Diviciacus_(Suessiones)" title="Diviciacus (Suessiones)">Diviciacus</a> had exercised sovereignty over most of the Belgians and even parts of <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">Britain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coinage minted by Belgic Gauls first appeared in Britain in the mid-2nd century BC with the coinage now categorized as the "Gallo-Belgic A" type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Coins associated with King Diviciacus of the Suessiones, issued near or between 90 and 60 BC, have been categorized as "Gallo-Belgic C." Finds of this issue of coin extend from Sussex to the Wash, with a concentration of finds near Kent. A later issue of coin, "Gallo-Belgic F" (c. 60-50 BC), has concentrated finds near Paris, throughout the lands of the Suessiones, and the southern, coastal areas of Britain. These finds lead scholars to suggest that the Suessiones had significant trade and migration into Britain during the 2nd and 1st centuries prior to Roman conquest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Caesar describes the Belgae as going to Britain looking for booty: "The inland part of Britain is inhabited by tribes declared in their own tradition to be indigenous to the island, the maritime part by tribes that migrated at an earlier time from Belgium to seek booty by invasion. ..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Caesar mentions that their capital was <i>Noviodunum</i> ("new hill-fort"), the present-day city of <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">Soissons</a>. Soissons was the capital city of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Soissons" title="Kingdom of Soissons">Kingdom of Soissons</a> from 511 to 613. Soissons was the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> <a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">Prince</a> <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> in the year 747, son of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pippin_the_Short" title="Pippin the Short">Pippin the Short</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bertrada_of_Laon" title="Bertrada of Laon">Bertrada of Laon</a>. It is today the capital of the <i>département</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Aisne" title="Aisne">Aisne</a>, in the northern part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champagne,_France" title="Champagne, France">Champagne</a>.</p>
<p>The region is still commonly referred to as the Soissonnais and people of the region are called <i>Soissonaires</i>.</p>
