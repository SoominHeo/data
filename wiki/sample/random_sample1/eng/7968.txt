<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Demand factor</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_power_industry" title="Electrical power industry">electrical power industry</a>, the term <b>demand factor</b> is used to refer to the fractional amount of some quantity being used relative to the maximum amount that could be used by the same system. The demand factor is always less than or equal to one. As the amount of demand is a time dependent quantity so is the demand factor.</p>

<p>The demand factor is often implicitly averaged over time when the time period of demand is understood by the context.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_engineering">Electrical engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demand_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Electrical engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> the demand factor is taken as a time independent quantity where the numerator is taken as the maximum demand in the specified time period instead of the averaged or instantaneous demand. the possible</p>

<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mfrac>
<mtext>Maximum load in given time period</mtext>
<mtext>Maximum possible load</mtext>
</mfrac>
</mrow>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle f_{Demand}={\frac {\text{Maximum load in given time period}}{\text{Maximum possible load}}}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="f_{{Demand}}={\frac  {{\text{Maximum load in given time period}}}{{\text{Maximum possible load}}}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-math-element" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f66316b1bac0cd4a4aa36fc337309cd0845dba17" style="vertical-align: -2.338ex; width:47.608ex; height:6.009ex;"/></span></p>
<p>This is the peak in the <a href="/wiki/Load_profile" title="Load profile">load profile</a> divided by the full load of the device.</p>
<p>Example: If a residence has equipment which would draw 6,000 W when all equipment was drawing a full load draw a maximum of 3,000 W in a specified time, then the demand factor = 3,000 W / 6,000 W = 0.5</p>
<p><br/>
This quantity is relevant when trying to establish the amount of load a system should be rated for. In the above example it would be unlikely that the system would be rated to 6,000 W even though there may be a slight possibility that this amount of power can be drawn. This is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Load_factor_(electrical)" title="Load factor (electrical)">load factor</a> which is the average load divided by the peak load in a specified time period.</p>
