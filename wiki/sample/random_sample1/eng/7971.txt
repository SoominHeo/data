<p>A <b>veterinary physician</b>, colloquially called a <b>vet</b>, shortened from <b>veterinarian</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a>) or <b>veterinary surgeon</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>), is a professional who practices <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_medicine" title="Veterinary medicine">veterinary medicine</a> by treating disease, disorder, and injury in animals.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many countries, the local nomenclature for a veterinarian is a regulated and protected term, meaning that members of the public without the prerequisite qualifications and/or licensure are not able to use the title. In many cases, the activities that may be undertaken by a veterinarian (such as treatment of illness or surgery in animals) are restricted only to those professionals who are registered as a veterinarian. For instance, in the United Kingdom, as in other jurisdictions, animal treatment may only be performed by registered veterinary physicians (with a few designated exceptions, such as <a href="/wiki/Paraveterinary_workers" title="Paraveterinary workers">paraveterinary workers</a>), and it is illegal for any person who is not registered to call themselves a veterinarian or prescribe any treatment.</p>
<p>Most veterinary physicians work in clinical settings, treating animals directly. These veterinarians may be involved in a general practice, treating animals of all types; they may be specialized in a specific group of animals such as <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">companion animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoological_medicine" title="Zoological medicine">zoo animals</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equine" title="Equine">equines</a>; or may specialize in a narrow medical discipline such as <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_surgery" title="Veterinary surgery">surgery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Internal_medicine" title="Internal medicine">internal medicine</a>. As with other healthcare professionals, veterinarians face ethical decisions about the care of their patients. Current debates within the profession include the ethics of certain procedures believed to be purely cosmetic or unnecessary for behavioral issues, such as <a href="/wiki/Onychectomy" title="Onychectomy">declawing</a> of cats, <a href="/wiki/Docking_(dog)" title="Docking (dog)">docking of tails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cropping_(animal)" title="Cropping (animal)">cropping of ears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devocalization" title="Devocalization">debarking</a> on dogs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_nomenclature">Etymology and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word veterinary comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i>veterinae</i> meaning "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_animals" title="Working animals">working animals</a>". "Veterinarian" was first used in print by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Browne" title="Thomas Browne">Thomas Browne</a> in 1646.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary_Medicine#History" title="Veterinary Medicine">History of veterinary profession</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bourgelat.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="422" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Bourgelat.png/170px-Bourgelat.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Bourgelat.png/255px-Bourgelat.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Bourgelat.png/340px-Bourgelat.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bourgelat.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Claude_Bourgelat" title="Claude Bourgelat">Claude Bourgelat</a> established the earliest veterinary college in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> in 1761.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first veterinary college was founded in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, France in 1762 by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Bourgelat" title="Claude Bourgelat">Claude Bourgelat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Lupton, after observing the devastation being caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_plague" title="Cattle plague">cattle plague</a> to the French herds, Bourgelat devoted his time to seeking out a remedy. This resulted in his founding a veterinary college in Lyon in 1761, from which establishment he dispatched students to combat the disease; in a short time, the plague was stayed and the health of stock restored, through the assistance rendered to agriculture by veterinary science and art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Odiham_society_minute_book.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="220" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/da/Odiham_society_minute_book.jpg" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Odiham_society_minute_book.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Odiham_Agricultural_Society" title="Odiham Agricultural Society">Odiham Agricultural Society</a> helped establish the veterinary profession in England.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Odiham_Agricultural_Society" title="Odiham Agricultural Society">Odiham Agricultural Society</a> was founded in 1783 in England to promote <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugh_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-4">[4]</a></sup> and played an important role in the foundation of the veterinary profession in Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A 1785 Society meeting resolved to "promote the study of Farriery upon rational scientific principles."</p>
<p>The professionalization of the veterinary trade was finally achieved in 1790, through the campaigning of <a href="/wiki/Granville_Penn" title="Granville Penn">Granville Penn</a>, who persuaded the Frenchman, Benoit Vial de St. Bel to accept the professorship of the newly established Veterinary College in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugh_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Veterinary_Surgeons" title="Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons">Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons</a> was established by <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a> in 1844.</p>
<p>Veterinary science came of age in the late 19th century, with notable contributions from Sir John McFadyean, credited by many as having been the founder of modern Veterinary research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles_and_responsibilities">Roles and responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roles and responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Picture_468.jpg"><img alt="Picture 468.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Picture_468.jpg/220px-Picture_468.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Picture_468.jpg/330px-Picture_468.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Picture_468.jpg/440px-Picture_468.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Picture_468.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Veterinarians treat disease, disorder or injury in animals, which includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diagnose" title="Diagnose">diagnosis</a>, treatment and aftercare. The scope of practice, specialty and experience of the individual veterinarian will dictate exactly what interventions they perform, but most will perform surgery (of differing complexity).</p>
<p>Unlike in human medicine, veterinarians must rely primarily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinical_signs" title="Clinical signs">clinical signs</a>, as animals are unable to vocalize <a href="/wiki/Symptom" title="Symptom">symptoms</a> as a human would. In some cases, owners may be able to provide a <a href="/wiki/Medical_history" title="Medical history">medical history</a> and the veterinarian can combine this information along with observations, and the results of pertinent diagnostic tests such as <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_computed_tomography" title="X-ray computed tomography">CT scans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">blood tests</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urinalysis" title="Urinalysis">urinalysis</a> and others.</p>
<p>Veterinarians must consider the appropriateness of <a href="/wiki/Animal_euthanasia" title="Animal euthanasia">euthanasia</a> ("putting to sleep") if a condition is likely to leave the animal in pain or with a poor quality of life, or if treatment of a condition is likely to cause more harm to the patient than good, or if the patient is unlikely to survive any treatment regimen. Additionally, there are scenarios where euthanasia is considered due to the constrains of the client's finances.</p>
<p>As with human medicine, much veterinary work is concerned with <a href="/wiki/Preventive_healthcare" title="Preventive healthcare">prophylactic treatment</a>, in order to prevent problems occurring in the future. Common interventions include <a href="/wiki/Vaccination" title="Vaccination">vaccination</a> against common animal illnesses, such as <a href="/wiki/Canine_distemper" title="Canine distemper">distemper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rabies" title="Rabies">rabies</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_prophylaxis" title="Dental prophylaxis">dental prophylaxis</a> to prevent or inhibit dental disease. This may also involve owner education so as to avoid future medical or behavioral issues.</p>
<p>Additionally veterinarians have important roles in public health and the prevention of zoonoses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment">Employment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Employment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The majority of veterinarians are employed in private practice treating animals (75% of vets in the United States, according to the American Veterinary Medical Association).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Small animal veterinarians typically work in veterinary clinics, veterinary hospitals, or both. Large animal veterinarians often spend more time travelling to see their patients at the primary facilities which house them, such as zoos or farms.</p>
<p>Other employers include charities treating animals, colleges of veterinary medicine, research laboratories, animal food companies, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_companies" title="Pharmaceutical companies">pharmaceutical companies</a>. In many countries, the government may also be a major employer of veterinarians, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> or the <a href="/wiki/State_Veterinary_Service" title="State Veterinary Service">State Veterinary Service</a> in the United Kingdom. State and local governments also employ veterinarians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Focus_of_practice">Focus of practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Focus of practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Veterinarians and their practices may be specialized in certain areas of veterinary medicine. Areas of focus include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Exotic_animal_veterinarian" title="Exotic animal veterinarian">Exotic animal veterinarian</a> - Generally considered to include <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, exotic birds such as <a href="/wiki/Parrot" title="Parrot">parrots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cockatoo" title="Cockatoo">cockatoos</a>, and small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ferret" title="Ferret">ferrets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinchilla" title="Chinchilla">chinchillas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Degu" title="Degu">degus</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservation_medicine" title="Conservation medicine">Conservation medicine</a> - The study of the relationship between animal and human health and environmental information.</li>
<li>Small animal practice - Usually <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_animals" title="Companion animals">companion animals</a>/household pets such as <a href="/wiki/Hamster" title="Hamster">hamsters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerbil" title="Gerbil">gerbils</a>. Some practices are canine-only or feline-only practices.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laboratory_animal" title="Laboratory animal">Laboratory animal</a> practice - Some veterinarians work in a <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> or industrial laboratory and are responsible for the care and treatment of laboratory animals of any species (often involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bovine" title="Bovine">bovines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porcine" title="Porcine">porcine</a> species, felines, canines, <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, and even exotic animals). Their responsibility is not only for the health and well being of the animals, but also for enforcing humane and ethical treatment of the animals in the facility.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Large animal practice - Usually referring to veterinarians that work with, variously, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> and other large farm animals, as well as <a href="/wiki/Equus_(genus)" title="Equus (genus)">equine species</a> and large reptiles.</li>
<li>Equine medicine - Some veterinarians are specialists in equine medicine. Horses are different in <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">husbandry</a> to other domestic species. Specialization in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equine" title="Equine">equine</a> veterinary practice is something that is normally developed after qualification, even if students do have some interest before graduation.</li>
<li>Food animal medicine - Some veterinarians deal exclusively or primary with animals raised for food (such as <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>). Livestock practitioners may deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep">ovine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>), <a href="/wiki/Bovinae" title="Bovinae">bovine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Suidae" title="Suidae">porcine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">swine</a>) species; such veterinarians deal with management of <a href="/wiki/Herd" title="Herd">herds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, and minor field surgery. <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">Dairy</a> medicine practice focuses on dairy animals. <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">Poultry</a> medicine practice focuses on the health of flocks of poultry; the field often involves extensive training in pathology, epidemiology, and nutrition of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. The veterinarian treats the flock and not the individual animals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">Food safety</a> practice - Veterinarians are employed by both the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government agencies</a> to advise on and monitor the handling, preparation, and storage of food in ways that prevent <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">foodborne illness</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zoological_medicine" title="Zoological medicine">Wildlife medicine</a> - A relatively recent branch of veterinary medicine, focusing on <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>. Wildlife medicine veterinarians may work with <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologists</a> and conservation medicine practitioners and may also be called out to treat marine species such as <a href="/wiki/Sea_otter" title="Sea otter">sea otters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a> after a natural disaster or <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spill</a>.</li>
<li>Aquatic medicine - mostly refers to veterinary care of fish in aquaculture (like salmon, cod, among other species), but can also include care of aquatic mammals. For certain countries with high economic income from aquaculture, this is an important part of the veterinary field (like Norway, Chile). Other countries (particularly those who are landlocked), might have little or no emphasis on aquatic medicine.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_specialties">Veterinary specialties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Veterinary specialties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_specialties" title="Veterinary specialties">Veterinary specialties</a></div>
<p>Veterinary specialists are in the minority compared to general practice veterinarians, and tend to be based at points of referral, such as veterinary schools or larger animal hospitals. Unlike human medicine, veterinary specialties often combine both the surgical and medical aspects of a biological system.</p>
<p>Veterinary specialties are accredited in North America by the AVMA through the American Board of Veterinary Specialties, in Europe by the European Board of Veterinary Specialisation and in Australasia by the Australasian Veterinary Boards Council. While some veterinarians may have areas of interest outside of recognized specialties, they are not legally specialists.</p>
<p>Specialties can cover general topics such as <a href="/wiki/Anesthesiology" title="Anesthesiology">anesthesiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_surgery" title="Veterinary surgery">surgery</a>, as well as organ system focus such as <a href="/wiki/Cardiology" title="Cardiology">cardiology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatology</a>. A full list can be seen at <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_specialties" title="Veterinary specialties">veterinary specialties</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_vs_Stationary_Practice">Mobile vs Stationary Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mobile vs Stationary Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of the advantages of operating a mobile veterinary practice over a standard practice are the start-up and operating costs. Running a mobile practice is much less expensive than opening a brick and mortar location. A traditional physical location practice can cost upwards of $1,000,000 or more. A mobile vet can operate as low as $3000 for a box in an SUV to around $250,000 for a fully equipped custom built chassis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The advantages for the pet owner are less stress to their loved ones, less risk of disease transmission and convenience for having more than one pet all at close to the same cost as a clinic. Having to harness up or put a pet in a carrier to transport them to the clinic can be stressful to the animal. A 2015 study published in the Journal of American Veterinary Medical Association proved that blood pressure readings, pulse rates and body temperature rates were increased by 11-16% when those readings were done in the clinic versus in the home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salary">Salary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Salary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mean salary for new graduates in the United States during 2010 was <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>48,674, including nearly 50% going on to advanced study programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avma1yr_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-avma1yr-13">[13]</a></sup> Those not continuing their studies made US$67,359 at first,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avma1yr_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-avma1yr-13">[13]</a></sup> whereas veterinarians in the United Kingdom earned slightly less with new graduate wages at an average of £25,000.</p>
<p>The average income for private practice in the United States rose from $105,510 in 2005 to $115,447 in 2007. These increased values exceed those of public practice including <a href="/wiki/Uniformed_services" title="Uniformed services">uniformed services</a> and government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In Australia, the profession wide average income was $67,000 in 2011 and this has declined compared to other professions for the past 30 years whilst graduate unemployment has doubled between 2006 and 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_regulation">Education and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In order to practice, vets must complete both an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_medicine" title="Veterinary medicine">veterinary medicine</a>, and in most cases must be registered with the relevant governing body for their jurisdiction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_science_degrees">Veterinary science degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Veterinary science degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Degrees in veterinary medicine culminate in the award of a veterinary science degree, although the title varies by region. For instance, in North America, graduates will receive a Doctor of Veterinary Medicine (Doctor of Veterinary Medicine or Veterinariae Medicinae Doctor; DVM or VMD), whereas in the United Kingdom or India they would be awarded a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Veterinary_Science" title="Bachelor of Veterinary Science">Bachelor's degree in Veterinary Science, Surgery or Medicine</a> (BVS, BVSc, BVetMed or BVMS), and in Ireland graduates receive a Medicinae Veterinariae Baccalaureus (MVB). In continental Europe, the degree of Doctor Medicinae Veterinariae (DMV, DrMedVet, Dr. med. vet.) or Doctor Veterinariae Medicinae (DVM, DrVetMed, Dr. vet. med.) is granted.</p>
<p>The award of a bachelor's degree was previously commonplace in the United States, but the degree name and academic standards were upgraded to match the 'doctor' title used by graduates.</p>
<p>Comparatively few universities have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary_schools" title="Veterinary schools">veterinary schools</a> that offer degrees which are accredited to qualify the graduates as registered vets. For example, there are 30 in the United States, 5 in Canada, and 8 in the United Kingdom (3 of which offer degrees accredited by the <a href="/wiki/American_Veterinary_Medical_Association" title="American Veterinary Medical Association">American Veterinary Medical Association</a> (AVMA)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA_Accredited_Veterinary_Colleges_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA_Accredited_Veterinary_Colleges-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to this scarcity of places for veterinary degrees, admission to veterinary school is competitive and requires extensive preparation. In the United States in 2007, approximately 5,750 applicants competed for the 2,650 seats in the 28 accredited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary_school" title="Veterinary school">veterinary schools</a>, with an acceptance rate of 46%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>With competitive admission, many schools may place heavy emphasis and consideration on a candidate's veterinary and animal experience. Formal experience is a particular advantage to the applicant, often consisting of work with veterinarians or scientists in clinics, agribusiness, research, or some area of health science. Less formal experience is also helpful for the applicant to have, and this includes working with animals on a farm or ranch or at a stable or animal shelter and basic overall animal exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, approximately 80% of admitted students are female. In the early history of veterinary medicine of the United States, most veterinarians were males. However, in the 1990s this ratio reached parity, and now it has been reversed.</p>
<p>Preveterinary courses should emphasize the sciences. Most veterinary schools typically require applicants to have taken one year equivalent classes in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">general biology</a>; and one semester of vertebrate embryology and biochemistry. Usually, the minimal mathematics requirement is college level calculus. Individual schools might require introduction to animal science, livestock judging, animal nutrition, cell biology, and genetics. However, due the limited availability of these courses, many schools have removed these requirements to widen the pool of possible applicants.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Registration_and_licensing">Registration and licensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Registration and licensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following academic education, most countries require a vet to be registered with the relevant governing body, and to maintain this license to practice.</p>
<p>Dependent on where the vet practices (or wishes to practice), they may have to complete an examination or test in order to complete this registration. For instance, in the United States, a prospective vet must receive a passing grade on a national board examination, the North America Veterinary Licensing Exam. This exam must be completed over the course of eight hours, and consists of 360 multiple-choice questions, covering all aspects of veterinary medicine, as well as visual material designed to test diagnostic skills.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postgraduate_study">Postgraduate study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Postgraduate study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The percentage electing to undertake further study following registration in the United States has increased from 36.8% to 39.9% in 2008. About 25% of those or about 9% of graduates were accepted into traditional academic internships. (2008 -696 graduates accepted a position in advanced study, 89.2% (621) accepted an internship (private practice, 74.5%; academic, 25.3%; and other internship, 0.2%). An additional 6.0% (42) accepted a residency). Approximately 9% of veterinarians eventually board certify in one of 40 distinct specialties from 22<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> specialty organizations recognized by the AVMA American Board of Veterinary Specialties (ABVS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curriculum_comparison_with_human_medicine">Curriculum comparison with human medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Curriculum comparison with human medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first two-year curriculum in both veterinary and human medical schools are very similar in the course names, but at certain subjects relatively different in content. Generally, the more basal the field of science is (for example: biochemistry, biophysics, cell biology etc.), the more similar it is. Later on when the courses get more clinically oriented, more significant differences arise. Where some things are completely different, and other things are about the same. Considering the courses, the first two-year curriculum usually include biochemistry, physiology, histology, anatomy, pharmacology, microbiology, epidemiology, pathology and hematology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cvm.msu.edu_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-cvm.msu.edu-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some veterinary school uses the same biochemistry, histology, and microbiology books as human medicine students; however, the course content is greatly supplemented to include the varied animal diseases and species specific differences. Many veterinarians were trained in pharmacology using the same text books as human physicians. As the specialty of veterinary pharmacology develop, more schools are using pharmacology textbooks written specifically for veterinarians. Veterinary physiology, anatomy, and histology is complex, as physiology often varies among species. Microbiology and virology of animals share the same foundation as human microbiology, but with grossly different disease manifestation and presentations. Epidemiology is focused on herd health and prevention of herd borne diseases, and foreign animal diseases. Pathology, like microbiology and histology, is very diverse and encompasses many species and organ systems. Most veterinary school have courses in small animal and also large animal nutrition, often taken as electives in the clinical years or as part of the core curriculum in the first two years.</p>
<p>The last two year curriculum of the two fields are similar only in their clinical emphasis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cvm.msu.edu_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-cvm.msu.edu-23">[23]</a></sup> A veterinary student must be well prepared to be a fully functional animal physician on the day of graduation, competent in both surgery and medicine. The graduating veterinarian must be able to pass medical board examination and be prepare to enter clinical practice on the day of graduation, while most human medical doctors in the United States complete 3 to 5 years of post-doctoral residency before practicing medicine independently, usually in a very narrow and focused specialty. Many veterinarians do also complete a post-doctoral residency, but it is not nearly as common as it is in human medicine.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_human_medicine">Impact on human medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Impact on human medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some veterinarians pursue post-graduate training and enter research careers and have contributed to advances in many human and veterinary medical fields, including <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a>. Research veterinarians were the first to isolate <a href="/wiki/Oncovirus" title="Oncovirus">oncoviruses</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> species, <i><a href="/wiki/Brucella" title="Brucella">Brucella</a></i> species, and various other <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogenic</a> agents. Veterinarians were in the forefront in the effort to suppress <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a> in the United States. Veterinarians identified the <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a> disease-causing agent, produced an <a href="/wiki/Anticoagulant" title="Anticoagulant">anticoagulant</a> used to treat human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, and developed <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgical</a> techniques for <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hip_replacement" title="Hip replacement">hip-joint replacement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limb_(anatomy)" title="Limb (anatomy)">limb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_transplant" title="Organ transplant">transplants</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_malpractice">Veterinary malpractice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Veterinary malpractice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most states in the US allow for malpractice lawsuit in case of death or injury to an animal from professional negligence. Usually the penalty is not greater than the value of the animal. For that reason, malpractice insurance for veterinarians is usually under $500 a year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some states allow for punitive penalty, loss of companionship, and suffering into the award, likely increasing the cost of veterinary malpractice insurance and the cost of veterinary care. Most veterinarians carry much higher cost business, worker's compensation, and facility insurance to protect their clients and workers from injury inflicted by animals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_physician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Concerns about the role of veterinary physicians in helping health threats survive and spread have been raised by several commentators, particularly with respect to pedigree dogs. Koharik Arman (2007) reached the following conclusion for example: "Veterinarians also bear some responsibility for the welfare situation of purebred dogs. In fact, the veterinary profession has facilitated the evolution of purebred dogs. 'Breeds' that would not normally be sustainable are propagated by the compliance of veterinarians to breeder wishes." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This finding was echoed by Sir Patrick Bateson in his Independent Review of Dog Breeding following the broadcast of the BBC documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Pedigree_Dogs_Exposed" title="Pedigree Dogs Exposed">Pedigree Dogs Exposed</a></i>: "It's only the ready availability of modern veterinary medicine that has permitted some conditions…to become widespread."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
