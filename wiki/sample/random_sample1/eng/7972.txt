<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Veterinary medicine</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Veterinary medicine</b> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> that deals with the prevention, diagnosis and treatment of disease, disorder and injury in non-human <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. The scope of veterinary medicine is wide, covering all animal species, both <a href="/wiki/List_of_domesticated_animals" title="List of domesticated animals">domesticated</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wild</a>, with a wide range of conditions which can affect different species.</p>
<p>Veterinary medicine is widely practiced, both with and without professional supervision. Professional care is most often led by a <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_physician" title="Veterinary physician">veterinary physician</a> (also known as a vet, veterinary surgeon or veterinarian), but also by <a href="/wiki/Paraveterinary_workers" title="Paraveterinary workers">paraveterinary workers</a> such as veterinary nurses or technicians. This can be augmented by other paraprofessionals with specific specialisms such as animal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiotherapy" title="Physiotherapy">physiotherapy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>, and species relevant roles such as <a href="/wiki/Farrier" title="Farrier">farriers</a>.</p>
<p>Veterinary science helps human health through the monitoring and control of <a href="/wiki/Zoonosis" title="Zoonosis">zoonotic disease</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious disease</a> transmitted from non-human animals to humans), food safety, and indirectly through human applications from basic medical research. They also help to maintain food supply through livestock health monitoring and treatment, and mental health by keeping pets healthy and long living. Veterinary scientists often collaborate with <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiologists</a>, and other health or natural scientists depending on type of work. Ethically, veterinarians are usually obliged to look after animal welfare.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Premodern_era">Premodern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Premodern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="848" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg/300px-Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg/450px-Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg/600px-Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shalihotra_manuscript_pages.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Shalihotra" manuscript pages</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <i>Papyrus of Kahun</i> (1900 BCE) and <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedic literature</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">ancient India</a> offer one of the first written records of veterinary medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThrusfield20072_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThrusfield20072-1">[1]</a></sup> (See also <a href="/wiki/Shalihotra" title="Shalihotra">Shalihotra</a>) ( <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>) First Buddhist Emperor of India <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edicts_of_Asoka" title="Edicts of Asoka">edicts of Asoka</a> reads: "Everywhere King Piyadasi (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asoka" title="Asoka">Asoka</a>) made two kinds of medicine (चिकित्सा) available, medicine for people and medicine for animals. Where there were no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbal_medicine" title="Herbal medicine">healing herbs</a> for people and animals, he ordered that they be bought and planted."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFinger200112_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFinger200112-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first attempts to organize and regulate the practice of treating animals tended to focus on horses because of their economic significance. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> from around 475 CE, <a href="/wiki/Farrier" title="Farrier">farriers</a> combined their work in <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe" title="Horseshoe">horseshoeing</a> with the more general task of "horse doctoring". In 1356, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_London" title="Lord Mayor of London">Lord Mayor of London</a>, concerned at the poor standard of care given to horses in the city, requested that all farriers operating within a seven-mile radius of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> form a "fellowship" to regulate and improve their practices. This ultimately led to the establishment of the Worshipful Company of Farriers in 1674.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Ruini" title="Carlo Ruini">Carlo Ruini</a>'s book <i>Anatomia del Cavallo,</i> (<i>Anatomy of the Horse</i>) was published in 1598. It was the first comprehensive treatise on the anatomy of a non-human species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment_of_profession">Establishment of profession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Establishment of profession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bourgelat.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="422" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Bourgelat.png/200px-Bourgelat.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Bourgelat.png/300px-Bourgelat.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Bourgelat.png/400px-Bourgelat.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bourgelat.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Claude_Bourgelat" title="Claude Bourgelat">Claude Bourgelat</a> established the earliest veterinary college in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> in 1762.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first veterinary college was founded in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, France in 1762 by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Bourgelat" title="Claude Bourgelat">Claude Bourgelat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to Lupton, after observing the devastation being caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_plague" title="Cattle plague">cattle plague</a> to the French herds, Bourgelat devoted his time to seeking out a remedy. This resulted in his founding a veterinary college in Lyon in 1761, from which establishment he dispatched students to combat the disease; in a short time, the plague was stayed and the health of stock restored, through the assistance rendered to agriculture by veterinary science and art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Odiham_Agricultural_Society" title="Odiham Agricultural Society">Odiham Agricultural Society</a> was founded in 1783 in England to promote <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugh_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-7">[7]</a></sup> and played an important role in the foundation of the veterinary profession in Britain. A founding member, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burgess_(bishop)" title="Thomas Burgess (bishop)">Thomas Burgess</a>, began to take up the cause of animal welfare and campaign for the more humane treatment of sick animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A 1785 Society meeting resolved to "promote the study of Farriery upon rational scientific principles.”</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Odiham_society_minute_book.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="220" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/da/Odiham_society_minute_book.jpg" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Odiham_society_minute_book.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Minutes taken at the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Odiham_Agricultural_Society" title="Odiham Agricultural Society">Odiham Agricultural Society</a>, which went on to play a pivotal role in the establishment of the veterinary profession in England.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The physician James Clark wrote a treatise entitled <i>Prevention of Disease</i> in which he argued for the professionalization of the veterinary trade, and the establishment of veterinary colleges. This was finally achieved in 1790, through the campaigning of <a href="/wiki/Granville_Penn" title="Granville Penn">Granville Penn</a>, who persuaded the Frenchman, Benoit Vial de St. Bel to accept the professorship of the newly established Veterinary College in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugh_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Veterinary_Surgeons" title="Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons">Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons</a> was established by <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a> in 1844. Veterinary science came of age in the late 19th century, with notable contributions from Sir John McFadyean, credited by many as having been the founder of modern Veterinary research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, the first schools were established in the early 19th century in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>. In 1879, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iowa_Agricultural_College" title="Iowa Agricultural College">Iowa Agricultural College</a> became the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_grant_college" title="Land grant college">land grant college</a> to establish a school of veterinary medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Saueressig" title="Suzanne Saueressig">Suzanne Saueressig</a> became the first accredited female veterinarian in Missouri in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_workers">Veterinary workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Veterinary workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_physicians">Veterinary physicians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Veterinary physicians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_physician" title="Veterinary physician">Veterinary physician</a></div>
<p>Veterinary care and management is usually led by a veterinary physician (usually called a vet, veterinary surgeon or veterinarian). This role is the equivalent of a doctor in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_medicine" title="Human medicine">human medicine</a>, and usually involves post-graduate study and qualification.</p>
<p>In many countries, the local nomenclature for a vet is a protected term, meaning that people without the prerequisite qualifications and/or registration are not able to use the title, and in many cases, the activities that may be undertaken by a vet (such as animal treatment or surgery) are restricted only to those people who are registered as vet. For instance, in the United Kingdom, as in other jurisdictions, animal treatment may only be performed by registered vets (with a few designated exceptions, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paraveterinary_worker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paraveterinary worker (page does not exist)">paraveterinary workers</a>), and it is illegal for any person who is not registered to call themselves a vet or perform any treatment.</p>
<p>Most vets work in clinical settings, treating animals directly. These vets may be involved in a general practice, treating animals of all types; may be specialized in a specific group of animals such as <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">companion animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, laboratory animals, <a href="/wiki/Zoological_medicine" title="Zoological medicine">zoo animals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>; or may specialize in a narrow medical discipline such as <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_surgery" title="Veterinary surgery">surgery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatology</a>, laboratory animal medicine, or <a href="/wiki/Internal_medicine" title="Internal medicine">internal medicine</a>.</p>
<p>As with healthcare professionals, vets face ethical decisions about the care of their patients. Current debates within the profession include the ethics of purely cosmetic procedures on animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Onychectomy" title="Onychectomy">declawing</a> of cats, <a href="/wiki/Docking_(dog)" title="Docking (dog)">docking of tails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cropping_(animal)" title="Cropping (animal)">cropping of ears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devocalization" title="Devocalization">debarking</a> on dogs.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paraveterinary_workers" title="Paraveterinary workers">Paraveterinary workers</a></div>
<p>Paraveterinary workers, including veterinary nurses, technicians and assistants, either assist vets in their work, or may work within their own scope of practice, depending on skills and qualifications, including in some cases, performing minor surgery.</p>
<p>The role of paraveterinary workers is less homogeneous globally than that of a vet, and qualification levels, and the associated skill mix, vary widely.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allied_professions">Allied professions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Allied professions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of professions exist within the scope of veterinary medicine, but which may not necessarily be performed by vets or veterinary nurses. This includes those performing roles which are also found in human medicine, such as practitioners dealing with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musculoskeletal" title="Musculoskeletal">musculoskeletal</a> disorders, including <a href="/wiki/Osteopathy" title="Osteopathy">osteopaths</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiropractor" title="Chiropractor">chiropractors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiotherapist" title="Physiotherapist">physiotherapists</a>.</p>
<p>There are also roles which are specific to animals, but which have parallels in human society, such as animal grooming and animal massage.</p>
<p>Some roles are specific to a species or group of animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Farrier" title="Farrier">farriers</a>, who are involved in the shoeing of horses, and in many cases have a major role to play in ensuring the medical fitness of the <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_Research">Veterinary Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Veterinary Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Veterinary research includes research on prevention, control, diagnosis, and treatment of diseases of animals and on the basic biology, welfare, and care of animals. Veterinary research transcends species boundaries and includes the study of spontaneously occurring and experimentally induced models of both human and animal disease and research at human-animal interfaces, such as food safety, wildlife and ecosystem health, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoonotic_disease" title="Zoonotic disease">zoonotic diseases</a>, and public policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_veterinary_research">Clinical veterinary research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veterinary_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Clinical veterinary research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As in medicine, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trials" title="Randomized controlled trials">randomized controlled trials</a> are fundamental also in veterinary medicine to establish the effectiveness of a treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, clinical veterinary research is far behind human medical research, with fewer randomized controlled trials, that have a lower quality and that are mostly focused on research animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Possible improvement consists in creation of network for inclusion of private veterinary practices in randomized controlled trials.</p>
