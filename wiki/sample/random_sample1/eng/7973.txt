<p><b>Susan Jacks</b> (born <b>Susan Pesklevits</b>, 19 August 1948, <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singer%E2%80%93songwriter" title="Singer–songwriter">singer–songwriter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">producer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_Jacks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Susan Jacks was born to a family of eight children in Saskatoon, Saskatchewan. Her family moved to British Columbia when she was 9, settling in the Fraser valley community of Haney. Jacks began her professional career at 15 when she was asked to be a regular performer on the national <a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">Canadian television show</a>, <i>Music Hop</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> She also appeared on several other national television shows and regularly did live performances in the British Columbia and Alberta areas. She recorded her first singles with two other well known Vancouver performers, Howie Vickers and Tom Northcott, under the name of "The Eternal Triangle"</p>
<p>In 1966, 18-year-old Susan Pesklevits asked <a href="/wiki/Terry_Jacks" title="Terry Jacks">Terry Jacks</a> to accompany her on guitar for an upcoming appearance. Susan performed a small number of dates with Terry accompanying her on guitar. They decided to add Craig McCaw on lead guitar and, although she continued to make various solo television appearances, Susan made the decision to stop performing live as a solo artist, left the "Eternal Triangle" and dedicated her time to the newly formed trio. The trio used the name "Powerline" for a number of months before settling on the name "The Poppy Family". Susan and Terry married in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> Craig McCaw introduced Satwant Singh to the group and he soon joined the trio on tablas.</p>
<p>The group began recording as "The Poppy Family featuring Susan Jacks" and had a number of <a href="/wiki/Hit_record" title="Hit record">hits</a> from 1968 through the early 1970s. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Which_Way_You_Goin%27_Billy%3F" title="Which Way You Goin' Billy?">Which Way You Goin' Billy?</a>", originally called "Which Way You Goin' Buddy?", was written from a male perspective but was re-written at Susan's suggestion to be sung by a female. Susan's brother Billy's name was chosen to replace "Buddy" and the song went on to sell nearly 4 million copies worldwide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> hitting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chart-topper" title="Chart-topper">No. 1</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, No. 1 in <i><a href="/wiki/Cashbox_(magazine)" title="Cashbox (magazine)">Cashbox</a></i> and No. 2 in <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> in the United States. "That's Where I Went Wrong" and "Where Evil Grows' also charted well on Billboard. They consequently followed up with numerous hits in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Terry released Satwant Singh and Craig McCaw from the group in 1970 and, although the name Poppy Family was still used, Susan essentially became a solo artist, with the exception of one or two duets with Terry. She often performed Poppy Family hits on numerous television shows including "Rollin' On The River", "The Bobby Darin Show" and "The George Kirby Special".</p>
<p>In 1972, the Poppy Family name was dropped and Susan and Terry worked together in the studio to record their solo albums. Susan's album was titled "I Thought of You Again" and Terry's titled <i>Seasons in the Sun</i>. Both albums were released in 1973 after Susan left the marriage. The title song on Susan's album earned her a nomination for Canadian Female Vocalist of the Year. Susan continued to have hits and other Juno nominations in Canada with songs including "I Thought of You Again", "Anna Marie", "Forever", "All The Tea in China", "Evergreen", "You Don't Know What Love Is", "I Want You To Love Me", "Build A Tower", "Love Has No Pride", and "Another Woman's Man". With the release of her <i>Dream</i> album in 1975, Susan was nominated for her single "Anna Marie". Her 1980 <i>Ghosts</i> album garnered her a Juno nomination with her single "All The Tea in China". In 1982 her <i>Forever</i> album was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As a solo artist, she appeared regularly on tour as well as television shows including "The Bobby Vinton Show", the Allan Thicke Show and numerous talk shows. She was also featured in a special called "Caught in the Act" and in a Christmas special at Whistler Mountain.</p>
<p>In 1977, Susan met <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">Canadian Football League</a> player <a href="/wiki/Ted_Dushinski" title="Ted Dushinski">Ted Dushinski</a>. They married in 1980 and had a son, Thad. The family moved to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a> in 1983 where she <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">recorded</a> the <a href="/wiki/Juno_Award" title="Juno Award">Juno</a>-nominated <a href="/wiki/Song" title="Song">song</a>, "Another Woman's Man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> She was voted the best new country artist in Oklahoma for her song "Tall Dark Stranger". She became a staff <a href="/wiki/Songwriter" title="Songwriter">songwriter</a> for a Nashville publishing company and has had several songs recorded, including a children's song on a Grammy nominated album entitled "Snuggle Up". Susan would later co-write and sing "Looking For Love" for the movie "The Last Chance Cafe".</p>
<p>In 2004, Jacks returned to Canada when her husband was diagnosed with lung cancer. Ted died on October 24, 2005. Upon returning, she was told she was in kidney failure and performed on a limited basis as her strength deteriorated. In 2010, Susan received a kidney transplant when her brother Billy donated a kidney to her. A few months after the transplant, Jacks was again on stage and on 17 April 2011 performed a benefit concert for the Kidney Foundation of Canada to raise awareness of the need for organ donation.</p>
<p>Jacks was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/BC_Entertainment_Hall_of_Fame" title="BC Entertainment Hall of Fame">BC Entertainment Hall of Fame</a> on 27 June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> She continues to perform live concerts and will be releasing a new CD in 2016. Susan's album "Dream", originally released in 1976 but abruptly removed from the market, was re-released in 2015.</p>
