<p>A <b>zygote</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ζυγωτός <i>zygōtos</i> "joined" or "yoked", from ζυγοῦν <i>zygoun</i> "to join" or "to yoke"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> formed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> event between two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gametes" title="Gametes">gametes</a>. The zygote's <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> is a combination of the DNA in each gamete, and contains all of the genetic information necessary to form a new individual. In multicellular organisms, the zygote is the earliest developmental stage. In single-celled organisms, the zygote can divide asexually by <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> to produce identical offspring.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oscar_Hertwig" title="Oscar Hertwig">Oscar Hertwig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hertwig" title="Richard Hertwig">Richard Hertwig</a> made some of the first discoveries on animal zygote formation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungi">Fungi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zygote&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fungi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In fungi, the sexual fusion of haploid cells is called <a href="/wiki/Karyogamy" title="Karyogamy">karyogamy</a>. The result of karyogamy is a diploid cell called a zygote or zygospore. This cell may then enter meiosis or mitosis depending on the life cycle of the species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zygote&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In plants, the zygote may be <a href="/wiki/Ploidy#Polyploidy" title="Ploidy">polyploid</a> if fertilization occurs between meiotically unreduced gametes.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Embryophyte" title="Embryophyte">land plants</a>, the zygote is formed within a chamber called the <a href="/wiki/Archegonium" title="Archegonium">archegonium</a>. In seedless plants, the archegonium is usually flask-shaped, with a long hollow neck through which the sperm cell enters. As the zygote divides and grows, it does so inside the archegonium.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans">Humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zygote&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In human fertilization, a release ovum (a haploid secondary oocyte with replicate chromosome copies) and a haploid<a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> cell (<a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> gamete)—combine to form a single <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">2n diploid</a> cell called the zygote. Once the single sperm enters the oocyte, it completes the division of the second meiosis forming a haploid daughter with only 23 chromosomes, almost all of the cytoplasm, and the sperm in its own pronucleus. The other product of meiosis is the second polar body with only chromosomes but no ability to replicate or survive. In the fertilized daughter, DNA is then replicated in the two separate pronuclei derived from the sperm and ovum, making the zygote's chromosome number temporarily <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">4n diploid</a>. After approximately 30 hours from the time of fertilization, fusion of the pronuclei and immediate mitotic division produce two <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">2n diploid</a> daughter cells called <a href="/wiki/Blastomere" title="Blastomere">blastomeres</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between the stages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Implantation_(human_embryo)" title="Implantation (human embryo)">implantation</a>, the developing human is a <i>preimplantation conceptus</i>. There is some dispute about whether this conceptus should no longer be referred to as an embryo, but should now be referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Proembryo#Preembryo_in_context_of_human_development" title="Proembryo">proembryo</a>, which is terminology that traditionally has been used to refer to plant life. Some ethicist and legal scholars make the argument that it is incorrect to call the conceptus an <i>embryo</i>, because it will later differentiate into both intraembryonic and extraembryonic tissues,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and can even split to produce multiple embryos (identical twins), while others have pointed out that, as so-called extraembryonic tissues are really parts of the embryo's body that are no longer utilized after birth, much as milk teeth fall out after childhood, and as the process of the embryo splitting to form identical twins leaves the original intact, while generating a new embryo, rendering it no different from the process of cloning an adult human, these arguments against the individuality of the zygote and preimplantation embryo are invalid. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the National Institute of Health has made the determination that the traditional classification of pre-implantation embryo is still correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After fertilization, the conceptus travels down the <a href="/wiki/Oviduct" title="Oviduct">oviduct</a> towards the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> while continuing to <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">divide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitotically</a> without actually increasing in size, in a process called <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(embryo)" title="Cleavage (embryo)">cleavage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After four divisions, the conceptus consists of 16 blastomeres, and it is known as the <a href="/wiki/Morula" title="Morula">morula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Through the processes of compaction, cell division, and blastulation, the conceptus takes the form of the <a href="/wiki/Blastocyst" title="Blastocyst">blastocyst</a> by the fifth day of development, just as it approaches the site of implantation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When the blastocyst hatches from the <a href="/wiki/Zona_pellucida" title="Zona pellucida">zona pellucida</a>, it can implant in the endometrial lining of the uterus and begin the embryonic stage of development.</p>
<p>The human zygote has been genetically edited in experiments designed to cure inherited diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_species">In other species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zygote&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In other species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Chlamydomonas zygote that contains chloroplast DNA (cpDNA) from both parents, such cells generally are rare since normally cpDNA is inherited uniparental from the mt+ mating type parent.These rare biparental zygotes allowed mapping of chloroplast genes by recombination.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_protozoa">In protozoa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zygote&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In protozoa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Amoeba" title="Amoeba">amoeba</a>, reproduction occurs by cell division of the parent cell: first the nucleus of the parent divides into two and then the cell membrane also cleaves, becoming two "daughter" Amoebae.</p>
