<p>In <a href="/wiki/Polymer_chemistry" title="Polymer chemistry">polymer chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, <b>resin</b> is a "solid or highly viscous substance" of plant or synthetic origin that is typically convertible into <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They are often mixtures of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>, principally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terpenes" title="Terpenes">terpenes</a>. Many plants, particularly woody plants, produce resin in response to injury. The resin acts as a bandage protecting the plant from invading insects and pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_natural_resins_and_related_materials">Examples of natural resins and related materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples of natural resins and related materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plants secrete resins and <a href="/wiki/Rosin" title="Rosin">rosins</a> for their protective benefits. They confound a wide range of herbivores, insects, and pathogens, while the volatile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_phenol" title="Natural phenol">phenolic compounds</a> may attract benefactors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasitoids" title="Parasitoids">parasitoids</a> or predators of the herbivores that attack the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resins">Resins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Resins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The resin produced by most plants is composed mainly of terpenes and derivatives. The most common terpenes in resin are the bicyclic terpenes <a href="/wiki/Alpha-Pinene" title="Alpha-Pinene">alpha-pinene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinene" title="Pinene">beta-pinene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carene" title="Carene">delta-3 carene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sabinene" title="Sabinene">sabinene</a>, the monocyclic terpenes <a href="/wiki/Limonene" title="Limonene">limonene</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terpinolene" title="Terpinolene">terpinolene</a>, and smaller amounts of the tricyclic <a href="/wiki/Sesquiterpene" title="Sesquiterpene">sesquiterpenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longifolene" title="Longifolene">longifolene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllene" title="Caryophyllene">caryophyllene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadinene" title="Cadinene">delta-cadinene</a>. Some resins also contain a high proportion of <a href="/wiki/Resin_acid" title="Resin acid">resin acids</a>. The individual components of resin can be separated by <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a>. Rosins on the other hand are less volatile and consist, inter alia, of diterpenes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Notable examples of plant resins include <a href="/wiki/Amber" title="Amber">amber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balm_of_Gilead" title="Balm of Gilead">Balm of Gilead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balsam" title="Copal">copal</a> from trees of <i><a href="/wiki/Protium_(plant)" title="Protium (plant)">Protium</a> copal</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hymenaea_courbaril" title="Hymenaea courbaril">Hymenaea courbaril</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Dammar_gum" title="Dammar gum">dammar gum</a> from trees of the family <a href="/wiki/Dipterocarpaceae" title="Dipterocarpaceae">Dipterocarpaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_blood" title="Dragon's blood">Dragon's blood</a> from the dragon trees (<i><a href="/wiki/Dracaena_(plant)" title="Dracaena (plant)">Dracaena</a></i> species), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elemi" title="Elemi">elemi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankincense" title="Frankincense">frankincense</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Boswellia_sacra" title="Boswellia sacra">Boswellia sacra</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Galbanum" title="Galbanum">galbanum</a> from <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ferula_gummosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ferula trees of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Guaiacum" title="Guaiacum">Guaiacum</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Kauri_gum" title="Kauri gum">kauri gum</a> from trees of <i><a href="/wiki/Agathis_australis" title="Agathis australis">Agathis australis</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Hashish" title="Hashish">hashish</a> (Cannabis resin) from <i><a href="/wiki/Cannabis_indica" title="Cannabis indica">Cannabis <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_resins">Fossil resins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil resins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amber" title="Amber">Amber</a> is <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> resin (also called resinite) from coniferous and other tree species. Copal, kauri gum, dammar and other resins may also be found as subfossil deposits. Subfossil copal can be distinguished from genuine fossil amber because it becomes tacky when a drop of a solvent such as <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> is placed on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> African <a href="/wiki/Copal" title="Copal">copal</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gum-digger" title="Gum-digger">kauri gum</a> of New Zealand are also procured in a semi-fossil condition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rosin">Rosin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rosin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rosin" title="Rosin">Rosin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Araucaria_Resin.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Extremely viscous resin extruding from the trunk of a mature <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria_columnaris" title="Araucaria columnaris">Araucaria columnaris</a></i>.</div>

<p>Solidified resin from which the <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpene</a> components have been removed by distillation is known as <a href="/wiki/Rosin" title="Rosin">rosin</a>. Typical rosin is a transparent or translucent mass, with a vitreous fracture and a faintly yellow or brown colour, non-odorous or having only a slight turpentine odor and taste. Rosin is insoluble in water, mostly soluble in alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> and hot fatty oils, and softens and melts under the influence of heat, and burns with a bright but smoky flame.</p>
<p>Rosin consists of a complex mixture of different substances including organic acids named the <a href="/wiki/Resin_acid" title="Resin acid">resin acids</a>. These are closely related to the terpenes, and derive from them through partial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>. Resin acids can be dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Resin_soap" title="Resin soap">resin soaps</a>, from which the purified resin acids are regenerated by treatment with acids. Examples of resin acids are <a href="/wiki/Abietic_acid" title="Abietic acid">abietic acid</a> (sylvic acid), C<sub>20</sub>H<sub>30</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Plicatic_acid" title="Plicatic acid">plicatic acid</a> contained in cedar, and <a href="/wiki/Pimaric_acid" title="Pimaric acid">pimaric acid</a>, C<sub>20</sub>H<sub>30</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Galipot" title="Galipot">galipot</a> resin. Abietic acid can also be extracted from rosin by means of hot alcohol; it crystallizes in leaflets, and on oxidation yields <a href="/wiki/Trimellitic_acid" title="Trimellitic acid">trimellitic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isophthalic_acid" title="Isophthalic acid">isophthalic acid</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Terebic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Terebic acid (page does not exist)">terebic acid</a>. Pimaric acid closely resembles abietic acid into which it passes when distilled in a vacuum; it has been supposed to consist of three isomers.</p>
<p>Rosin is obtained from <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> and some other <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Plant resins are generally produced as stem secretions, but in some Central and South American species such as <i>Euphorbia dalechampia</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Clusia" title="Clusia">Clusia</a></i> species they are produced as pollination rewards, and used by some <a href="/wiki/Stingless_bee" title="Stingless bee">stingless bee</a> species to construct their nests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Propolis" title="Propolis">Propolis</a>, consisting largely of resins collected from plants such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poplars" title="Poplars">poplars</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifers" title="Conifers">conifers</a>, is used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honey_bees" title="Honey bees">honey bees</a> to seal gaps in their hives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finstrom_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Finstrom-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum-_and_insect-derived_resins">Petroleum- and insect-derived resins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Petroleum- and insect-derived resins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shellac" title="Shellac">Shellac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lacquer" title="Lacquer">lacquer</a> are examples of insect-derived resins.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphaltite" title="Asphaltite">Asphaltite</a> and Utah resin are petroleum bitumens, not a product secreted by plants, although it was ultimately derived from plants.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_etymology">History and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg/220px-Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg/330px-Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg/440px-Resin_on_Almond_tree.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Plant resins have a very long history that was documented in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> by <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a>, in ancient Rome by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, and especially in the resins known as <a href="/wiki/Frankincense" title="Frankincense">frankincense</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myrrh" title="Myrrh">myrrh</a>, prized in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ancegy_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ancegy-9">[9]</a></sup> These were highly prized substances, and required as <a href="/wiki/Incense" title="Incense">incense</a> in some religious rites.</p>
<p>The word <i>resin</i> comes from French <i>resine</i>, from Latin <i>resina</i> "resin", which either derives from or is a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> of the Greek <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ῥητίνη</span> <i>rhētinē</i> "resin of the pine", of unknown earlier origin, though probably non-<a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word "resin" has been applied in the modern world to nearly any component of a liquid that will set into a hard <a href="/wiki/Lacquer" title="Lacquer">lacquer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel</a>-like finish. An example is nail polish. Certain "casting resins" and <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_resin" title="Synthetic resin">synthetic resins</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoxy_resin" title="Epoxy resin">epoxy resin</a>) have also been given the name "resin."</p>
<p>Some resins when soft are known as 'oleoresins', and when containing <a href="/wiki/Benzoic_acid" title="Benzoic acid">benzoic acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cinnamic_acid" title="Cinnamic acid">cinnamic acid</a> they are called balsams. Oleoresins are naturally occurring mixtures of an oil and a resin; they can be extracted from various plants. Other resinous products in their natural condition are a mix with <a href="/wiki/Natural_gum" title="Natural gum">gum</a> or mucilaginous substances and known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gum_resin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gum resin (page does not exist)">gum resins</a>. Several natural resins are used as ingredients in perfumes, e.g., balsams of Peru and tolu, elemi, styrax, and certain turpentines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-resinous_exudates">Non-resinous exudates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-resinous exudates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other liquid compounds found inside plants or exuded by plants, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sap" title="Plant sap">sap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a>, are sometimes confused with resin, but are not the same. Saps, in particular, serve a nutritive function that resins do not.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_resins">Plant resins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Plant resins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Plant resins are valued for the production of <a href="/wiki/Varnish" title="Varnish">varnishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a>, and food <a href="/wiki/Glazing_agent" title="Glazing agent">glazing agents</a>. They are also prized as raw materials for the <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">synthesis of other organic compounds</a> and provide constituents of <a href="/wiki/Incense" title="Incense">incense</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfume</a>.</p>

<p>The hard transparent resins, such as the copals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dammar" title="Dammar">dammars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mastic_(plant_resin)" title="Mastic (plant resin)">mastic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sandarac" title="Sandarac">sandarac</a>, are principally used for varnishes and adhesives, while the softer odoriferous oleo-resins (<a href="/wiki/Frankincense" title="Frankincense">frankincense</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elemi" title="Elemi">elemi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turpentine" title="Turpentine">turpentine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copaiba" title="Copaiba">copaiba</a>), and gum resins containing essential oils (<a href="/wiki/Ammoniacum" title="Ammoniacum">ammoniacum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asafoetida" title="Asafoetida">asafoetida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gamboge" title="Gamboge">gamboge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myrrh" title="Myrrh">myrrh</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scammony" title="Scammony">scammony</a>) are more used for therapeutic purposes and <a href="/wiki/Incense" title="Incense">incense</a>. The resin of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleppo_Pine" title="Aleppo Pine">Aleppo Pine</a> is used to flavour <a href="/wiki/Retsina" title="Retsina">retsina</a>, a Greek <a href="/wiki/Resinated_wine" title="Resinated wine">resinated wine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_resins">Synthetic resins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Synthetic resins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many materials are produced via the conversion of synthetic resins to solids. Important examples are <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A_diglycidyl_ether" title="Bisphenol A diglycidyl ether">bisphenol A diglycidyl ether</a>, which is a resin converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoxy_glue" title="Epoxy glue">epoxy glue</a> upon the addition of a hardener. Silicones are often prepared from silicone resins via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Room_temperature_vulcanization" title="Room temperature vulcanization">room temperature vulcanization</a>.</p>
