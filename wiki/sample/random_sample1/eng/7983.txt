<p><b>Sucralose</b> is an artificial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweetener" title="Sweetener">sweetener</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitute" title="Sugar substitute">sugar substitute</a>. The majority of ingested sucralose is not broken down by the body, so it is noncaloric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, it is also known under the <a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E number</a> <b>E955</b>. Sucralose is about 320 to 1,000 times <a href="/wiki/Sweetness" title="Sweetness">sweeter</a> than <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-5">[5]</a></sup> three times as sweet as <a href="/wiki/Aspartame" title="Aspartame">aspartame</a>, twice as sweet as <a href="/wiki/Saccharin" title="Saccharin">saccharin</a> and three times as sweet as <a href="/wiki/Acesulfame_potassium" title="Acesulfame potassium">acesulfame potassium</a>. It is stable under heat and over a broad range of <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> conditions. Therefore, it can be used in baking or in products that require a longer shelf life. The commercial success of sucralose-based products stems from its favorable comparison to other low-calorie sweeteners in terms of taste, stability, and safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Common brand names of sucralose-based sweeteners are <a href="/wiki/Splenda" title="Splenda">Splenda</a>, Zerocal, Sukrana, SucraPlus, Candys, Cukren, and Nevella. Canderel Yellow also contains Sucralose, but the original Canderel and Green Canderel do not.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sucralose was discovered in 1976 by scientists from <a href="/wiki/Tate_%26_Lyle" title="Tate &amp; Lyle">Tate &amp; Lyle</a>, working with researchers Leslie Hough and Shashikant Phadnis at <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_College" title="Queen Elizabeth College">Queen Elizabeth College</a> (now part of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While researching ways to use <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and its synthetic derivatives for industrial use, Phadnis was told to "test" a chlorinated sugar compound. Phadnis thought Hough asked him to "taste" it, so he did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gratzer2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gratzer2002-7">[7]</a></sup> He found the compound to be exceptionally sweet.</p>
<p>Tate &amp; Lyle patented the substance in 1976; as of 2008, the only remaining patents concern specific manufacturing processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sucralose was first approved for use in Canada in 1991. Subsequent approvals came in Australia in 1993, in New Zealand in 1996, in the United States in 1998, and in the European Union in 2004. By 2008, it had been approved in over 80 countries, including Mexico, Brazil, China, India, and Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2006, the US <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> amended the regulations for foods to include sucralose as a "non-nutritive sweetener" in food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In May 2008, Fusion Nutraceuticals launched a generic product to the market, using Tate &amp; Lyle patents.</p>
<p>In April 2015 PepsiCo announced that it would be moving from <a href="/wiki/Aspartame" title="Aspartame">aspartame</a> to sucralose for most of its diet drinks in the US,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> due to sales of Diet Pepsi falling by more than 5% in the US. PepsiCo says its decision is a commercial one - responding to consumer preferences.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sucralose is manufactured by the selective <a href="/wiki/Halogenation" title="Halogenation">chlorination</a> of sucrose in a multistep <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">synthesis</a>, which substitutes three of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl groups</a> of sucrose with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> atoms. This chlorination is achieved by selective <a href="/wiki/Protecting_group" title="Protecting group">protection</a> of a primary alcohol group, followed by chlorination of the partially <a href="/wiki/Acetylation" title="Acetylation">acetylated</a> sugar with excess chlorinating agent, and then by removal of the acetyl groups to give the desired sucralose product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A company realignment announced April 21, 2015 moved sucralose production from Tate &amp; Lyle's plant on Jurong Island, Singapore to the recently reopened plant in <a href="/wiki/McIntosh,_Alabama" title="McIntosh, Alabama">McIntosh, Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_uses">Product uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Product uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sucralose is found in many food and beverage products, used because it is a no-calorie sweetener, does not promote <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_cavities" title="Dental cavities">dental cavities</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> is as safe for consumption by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetic" title="Diabetic">diabetics</a> and nondiabetics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and does not affect insulin levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Sucralose is used as a replacement for, or in combination with, other artificial or natural sweeteners such as <a href="/wiki/Aspartame" title="Aspartame">aspartame</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acesulfame_potassium" title="Acesulfame potassium">acesulfame potassium</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a>. Sucralose is used in products such as <a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">candy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breakfast" title="Breakfast">breakfast</a> bars and <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a>. It is also used in canned fruits wherein water and sucralose take the place of much higher calorie <a href="/wiki/Corn_syrup" title="Corn syrup">corn syrup</a>-based additives. Sucralose mixed with <a href="/wiki/Maltodextrin" title="Maltodextrin">maltodextrin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">dextrose</a> (both made from corn) as bulking agents is sold internationally by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McNeil_Nutritionals" title="McNeil Nutritionals">McNeil Nutritionals</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Splenda" title="Splenda">Splenda</a> brand name. In the United States and Canada, this blend is increasingly found in restaurants, in yellow packets, in contrast to the blue packets commonly used by aspartame and the pink packets used by those containing saccharin sweeteners; in Canada, though, yellow packets are also associated with the SugarTwin brand of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclamate" title="Cyclamate">cyclamate</a> sweetener.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cooking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sucralose is a highly heat-stable noncaloric sweetener, allowing it to be used in many recipes with little or no <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>. It is available in a granulated form that allows for same-volume substitution with sugar. This mix of granulated sucralose includes fillers, all of which rapidly dissolve in liquids. While the granulated sucralose provides apparent volume-for-volume sweetness, the texture in baked products may be noticeably different. Sucralose is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a>, which can lead to baked goods that are noticeably drier and manifest a less dense texture than those made with sucrose. Unlike sucrose, which melts when baked at high temperatures, sucralose maintains its granular structure when subjected to dry, high heat (e.g., in a 350 °F or 180 °C oven). Furthermore, in its pure state, sucralose begins to decompose at 119 °C or 246 °F.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Thus, in some baking recipes, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A8me_br%C3%BBl%C3%A9e" title="Crème brûlée">crème brûlée</a></i>, which require sugar sprinkled on top to partially or fully melt and crystallize, substituting sucralose will not result in the same surface texture, crispness, or crystalline structure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging_and_storage">Packaging and storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Packaging and storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pure sucralose is sold in bulk, but not in quantities suitable for individual use, although some highly concentrated sucralose–water blends are available online. These concentrates contain one part sucralose for each two parts water. A quarter teaspoon of concentrate substitutes for one cup of sugar. Pure, dry sucralose undergoes some decomposition at elevated temperatures. In solution or blended with maltodextrin, it is slightly more stable. Most products containing sucralose add fillers and additional sweetener to bring the product to the approximate volume and texture of an equivalent amount of sugar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_caloric_content">Effect on caloric content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effect on caloric content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though sucralose contains no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilocalorie" title="Kilocalorie">calories</a>, products that contain fillers, such as maltodextrin and/or <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">dextrose</a>, add about 2–4 <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a> per teaspoon or individual packet, depending on the product, the fillers used, brand, and the intended use of the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="US Food and Drug Administration">US Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) allows for any product containing fewer than five calories per serving to be labeled as "zero calories".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health.2C_safety.2C_and_regulation">Health, safety, and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Health, safety, and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sucralose has been accepted as safe by several national and international food safety regulatory bodies, including the FDA, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Joint_FAO/WHO_Expert_Committee_Report_on_Food_Additives" title="The Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee Report on Food Additives">The Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee Report on Food Additives</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Committee_on_Food" title="Scientific Committee on Food">Scientific Committee on Food</a>, Health Protection Branch of <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Welfare_Canada" title="Health and Welfare Canada">Health and Welfare Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Food_Standards_Australia_New_Zealand" title="Food Standards Australia New Zealand">Food Standards Australia New Zealand</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Diabetes_Association" title="Canadian Diabetes Association">Canadian Diabetes Association</a>, the amount of sucralose that can be consumed over a person's lifetime without any adverse effects is 9 mg per kg of body weight per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"In determining the safety of sucralose, the FDA reviewed data from more than 110 studies in humans and animals. Many of the studies were designed to identify possible toxic effects, including carcinogenic, reproductive, and neurological effects. No such effects were found, and FDA's approval is based on the finding that sucralose is safe for human consumption." For example, McNeil Nutritional LLC studies submitted as part of its U.S. FDA Food Additive Petition 7A3987 indicated that "in the 2-year rodent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioassays" title="Bioassays">bioassays</a> ... there was no evidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a> activity for either sucralose or its hydrolysis products ..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_of_consumption">Safety of consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Safety of consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Results from over a hundred animal and clinical studies in the FDA approval process unanimously indicated a lack of risk associated with sucralose consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kon_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-kon-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> When the estimated daily intake (EDI) is compared to the intake at which adverse effects are seen (known as the highest no adverse effects limit, or HNEL) at 1500 mg/kg BW/day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baird_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baird-29">[29]</a></sup> a large margin of safety exists. The bulk of sucralose ingested is not absorbed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal" title="Gastrointestinal">gastrointestinal</a> (GI) tract and is directly excreted in the feces, while 11–27% of it is absorbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-5">[5]</a></sup> The amount absorbed from the GI tract is largely removed from the blood stream by the kidneys and eliminated in the urine, with 20–30% of the absorbed sucralose being metabolized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-5">[5]</a></sup> This means that only between 2–8% of sucralose consumed is metabolized, on average.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_effects_on_health">Potential effects on health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Potential effects on health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Despite being generally recognized as safe by regulatory agencies based on information from a multitude of large-scale and clinical studies, sucralose has been the subject of a few small-scale or animal studies attempting to determine yet-unknown effects on illnesses such as <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> animal study funded by the <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Association" title="Sugar Association">Sugar Association</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> found evidence that doses of Splenda between 100 and 1000 mg/kg BW/day, containing sucralose at 1.1 to 11 mg/kg BW/day, fed to rats reduced <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">fecal microflora</a>, increased the pH level in the intestines, contributed to increases in body weight, and increased levels of <a href="/wiki/P-glycoprotein" title="P-glycoprotein">P-glycoprotein</a> (P-gp).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> These effects have not been reported in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-5">[5]</a></sup> An expert panel, including scientists from Duke University, <a href="/wiki/Rutgers_University" title="Rutgers University">Rutgers University</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Medical_College" title="New York Medical College">New York Medical College</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Public_Health" title="Harvard School of Public Health">Harvard School of Public Health</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> reported in <i>Regulatory Toxicology and Pharmacology</i> that the Duke study was "not scientifically rigorous and is deficient in several critical areas that preclude reliable interpretation of the study results".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Science_in_the_Public_Interest" title="Center for Science in the Public Interest">Center for Science in the Public Interest</a>, a consumer advocacy group for food products, downgraded sucralose from "Safe" to "Caution" in June 2013, citing an unpublished study linking sucralose consumption with leukemia risk in rats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The study has been criticized as being poorly executed and reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The study was finally published on January 29 in the peer-reviewed International Journal of Occupational and Environmental Health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> One study linked large doses of sucralose, equivalent to 11,450 packets (136 g) per day in a person (i.e., ~360 times the <a href="/wiki/Acceptable_daily_intake" title="Acceptable daily intake">ADI</a> of 5 mg/kg BW/day), to DNA damage in mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Sucralose has not shown any DNA-damaging properties in DNA repair assays at normal consumption levels, and no evidence of carcinogenicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilot_study" title="Pilot study">small-scale study</a> of 17 obese test subjects, sucralose was found to affect glycemic and insulin responses, leading to an increase in peak plasma glucose concentration and insulin secretion rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In a 2014 study, sucralose limited the growth of <a href="/wiki/Bacteroides" title="Bacteroides">Bacteroides</a> bacteria <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rettig_2014_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rettig_2014-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sucralose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to one study, sucralose is digestible by a number of microorganisms and is broken down once released into the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, measurements by the Swedish Environmental Research Institute have shown <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> has little effect on sucralose, which is present in wastewater effluents at levels of several μg/l (ppb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> No ecotoxicological effects are known at such levels, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Swedish Environmental Protection Agency">Swedish Environmental Protection Agency</a> warns a continuous increase in levels may occur if the compound is only slowly degraded in nature. When heated to very high temperatures (over 350 °C or 662 °F) in metal containers, sucralose can produce in the resulting smoke <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">polychlorinated dibenzo-p-dioxins</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutant" title="Persistent organic pollutant">persistent organic pollutants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sucralose has been detected in natural waters. Studies indicate that this has virtually no impact on the early life development of certain animal species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25120258_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25120258-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
