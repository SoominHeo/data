<p>The <b>Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira</b> (<small>pronounced: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[ˈsupɛɾ ˈtasɐ ˈkɐ̃didu dɨ oliˈvɐjɾɐ]</a></span>; English: Cândido de Oliveira Super Cup, or Portuguese Super Cup) is an annual Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> match contested by the champions of the previous <a href="/wiki/Primeira_Liga" title="Primeira Liga">Primeira Liga</a> season and the holders of the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%A7a_de_Portugal" title="Taça de Portugal">Taça de Portugal</a>. If the Primeira Liga champions also win the Taça de Portugal (i.e. achieve the <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)" title="Double (association football)">double</a>, Portuguese: <i>dobradinha</i>), then they play against the runners-up of the Taça de Portugal.</p>
<p>The Supertaça is organised by the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Football_Federation" title="Portuguese Football Federation">Portuguese Football Federation</a> since its creation in 1979, and is usually played in August, just before the start of the domestic season. The trophy is named after former player, coach, and sports journalist <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="Cândido de Oliveira">Cândido de Oliveira</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1943–44 season, the Super Cup was created for a special game between <a href="/wiki/1943%E2%80%9344_Primeira_Divis%C3%A3o" title="1943–44 Primeira Divisão">Primeira Divisão</a> champions <a href="/wiki/Sporting_Clube_de_Portugal" title="Sporting Clube de Portugal">Sporting CP</a> and <a href="/wiki/1943%E2%80%9344_Ta%C3%A7a_de_Portugal" title="1943–44 Taça de Portugal">Taça de Portugal</a> winners <a href="/wiki/S.L._Benfica" title="S.L. Benfica">Benfica</a>, on occasion of the inauguration of the <a href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_Nacional" title="Estádio Nacional">Estádio Nacional</a>. The commissioned trophy was named <i>Taça Império</i> – not to be mistaken with <i>Taça do Império</i>, the first incarnation of the Taça de Portugal. After the game, it was decided that the competition was to be continued, but it was later canceled. The second incarnation came 20 years later when Casa da Imprensa (The Press House) instituted a trophy, the <i>Taça de Ouro da Imprensa</i> to be challenged between the national champions and the cup winners.</p>

<p>The Super Cup started unofficially in 1978–79 with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_derby" title="Local derby">local derby</a> between <a href="/wiki/Boavista_F.C." title="Boavista F.C.">Boavista</a> (Taça de Portugal holders) and <a href="/wiki/FC_Porto" title="FC Porto">Porto</a> (<a href="/wiki/1978%E2%80%9379_Primeira_Divis%C3%A3o" title="1978–79 Primeira Divisão">league champions</a>) that ended with a 2–1 victory for Boavista. The following season, another derby occurred between Benfica and Sporting, which constituted the second unofficial Super Cup and the first played over two legs (home and away). With the success of both unofficial Super Cup editions, the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Football_Federation" title="Portuguese Football Federation">Portuguese Football Federation</a> (FPF) decided to uphold the competition on a yearly basis in a two-legged format. The first official edition took place in <a href="/wiki/1981_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="1981 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">the 1981–82 season</a>, already under the current name.</p>
<p>The rules stated that two matches were played and that the aggregate result would determine the winner. If a draw occurred, then a replay of the match should be played in a neutral ground. This occurred six times — <a href="/wiki/1984_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="1984 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">1984</a>, <a href="/wiki/1991_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="1991 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">1991</a>, <a href="/wiki/1993_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="1993 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">1993</a>, <a href="/wiki/1994_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="1994 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">1994</a>, <a href="/wiki/1995_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="1995 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">1995</a> and <a href="/wiki/2000_Superta%C3%A7a_C%C3%A2ndido_de_Oliveira" title="2000 Supertaça Cândido de Oliveira">2000</a> — with the replay of the 1984 edition being contested again in two legs.</p>
<p>Because interest in the Super Cup was waning and in order to reduce the crowded football calendar, in the 2000–01 season, the FPF decided to abolish the two-legged format and replay match and use a single match played in a neutral ground to determine the winner.</p>
