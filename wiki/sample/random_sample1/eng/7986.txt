<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Horizontal branch</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>horizontal branch</b> (<b>HB</b>) is a stage of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a> that immediately follows the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_giant_branch" title="Red giant branch">red giant branch</a> in stars whose masses are similar to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s. Horizontal-branch stars are powered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helium_fusion" title="Helium fusion">helium fusion</a> in the core (via the <a href="/wiki/Triple-alpha_process" title="Triple-alpha process">triple-alpha process</a>) and by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fusion" title="Hydrogen fusion">hydrogen fusion</a> (via the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a>) in a shell surrounding the core. The onset of core helium fusion at the tip of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_giant_branch" title="Red giant branch">red giant branch</a> causes substantial changes in <a href="/wiki/Star#Structure" title="Star">stellar structure</a>, resulting in an overall reduction in <a href="/wiki/Luminosity#In_astronomy" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a>, some contraction of the stellar envelope, and the surface reaching higher temperatures.</p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizontal_branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horizontal branch stars were discovered with the first deep photographic <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometric</a> studies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">globular clusters</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and were notable for being absent from all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_clusters" title="Open clusters">open clusters</a> that had been studied up to that time. The horizontal branch is so named because in low-<a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a> star collections like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">globular clusters</a>, HB stars lie along a roughly horizontal line in a <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a> (CMD).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizontal_branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> stars with masses up to 2.3 times the mass of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_fusion" title="Thermonuclear fusion">thermonuclear fusion</a> of hydrogen (bearing the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-p_chain" title="P-p chain">p-p chain</a>) at the core will steadily build up a concentration of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> at a rate primarily determined by the mass of the <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>. In due course, the helium-enriched core becomes unable to sustain <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> of hydrogen and that fusion process migrates outward to a shell. The core becomes a region of <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_matter" title="Degenerate matter">degenerate matter</a> that does not contribute to the generation of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. It continues to grow and increase in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fusion" title="Hydrogen fusion">hydrogen fusion</a> in the shell contributes more <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karttunen_oja2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-karttunen_oja2007-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stars initially between about 2.3 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub> and 8 <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub> have larger helium cores that do not become degenerate. Instead their cores reach the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schoenberg-Chandrasekhar_limit" title="Schoenberg-Chandrasekhar limit">Schoenberg-Chandrasekhar mass</a> at which they are no longer in hydrostatic or thermal equilibrium. They then contract and heat up, which triggers helium fusion before the core becomes degenerate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salaris_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-salaris-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>If the <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> has more than about 0.5 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the core eventually reaches the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> necessary for the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">fusion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> into carbon through the <a href="/wiki/Triple-alpha_process" title="Triple-alpha process">triple-alpha process</a>. The initiation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helium_fusion" title="Helium fusion">helium fusion</a> begins across the core region, which will cause an immediate <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> rise and a rapid increase in the rate of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">fusion</a>. Within a few seconds the core becomes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-degenerate" title="Non-degenerate">non-degenerate</a> and quickly expands, producing an event called <a href="/wiki/Helium_flash" title="Helium flash">helium flash</a>. Non-degenerate cores initiate fusion more smoothly, without a flash. The output of this event is absorbed by the layers of <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> above, so the effects are not seen from the exterior of the star. The star now changes to a new <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> state, and its evolutionary path switches from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_giant_branch" title="Red giant branch">red giant branch</a> (RGB) onto the horizontal branch of the <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a>. This term means that the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> will stay relatively stable while the <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a> increases, and the star migrates horizontally across the <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">H–R diagram</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karttunen_oja2007_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-karttunen_oja2007-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stars with an initial mass close to the sun dip down to the red end of the horizontal branch when core helium burning starts, but show only a small increase in temperature before core helium is exhausted. More massive stars spend an extended time on the horizontal branch and show a larger increase in temperature as they burn helium in the core. The shape of the horizontal branch is due both to the movement of individual stars bluewards as they age, and to the temperature of stars with different masses when they reach the horizontal branch. There are further variations, both in luminosity and temperature, due to metallicity and helium content.</p>
<p>Although the horizontal branch is named because it consists largely of stars with approximately the same absolute magnitude across a range of temperatures, lying in a horizontal bar on a color–magnitude diagrams, the branch is far from horizontal at the blue end. The horizontal branch ends in a "blue tail" with hotter stars having lower luminosity, occasionally with a "blue hook" of extremely hot stars. It is also not horizontal when plotted by bolometric luminosity, with hotter horizontal branch stars being less luminous than cooler ones.</p>
<p>The hottest horizontal-branch stars, referred to as extreme horizontal branch, have temperatures of 20,000–30,000K. This is far beyond what would be expected for a normal core helium burning star. Theories to explain these stars include binary interactions, and "late thermal pulses", where a thermal pulse that <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">Asymptotic giant branch</a> (AGB) stars experience regularly, occurs after fusion has ceased and the star has entered the superwind phase. These stars are "born again" with unusual properties. Despite the bizarre-sounding process, this is expected to occur for 10% or more of post-AGB stars, although it is thought that only particularly late thermal pulses create extreme horizontal-branch stars, after the planetary nebular phase and when the central star is already cooling towards a white dwarf.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_RR_Lyrae_.22gap.22_and_horizontal-branch_morphology">The RR Lyrae "gap" and horizontal-branch morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizontal_branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: The RR Lyrae "gap" and horizontal-branch morphology'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="673" data-file-width="796" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg/260px-M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg/390px-M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg/520px-M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M3_color_magnitude_diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Color–magnitude diagram for the globular cluster <a href="/wiki/Messier_3" title="Messier 3">M3</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">Globular cluster</a> CMDs generally show horizontal branches that have a prominent gap in the HB. This gap in the CMD incorrectly suggests that the <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">cluster</a> has no <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> in this region of its CMD. The gap occurs at the <a href="/wiki/Instability_strip" title="Instability strip">instability strip</a>, so many stars in this region pulsate. These pulsating horizontal-branch stars are known as <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">RR Lyrae variable</a> stars and they are obviously variable in <a href="/wiki/Brightness" title="Brightness">brightness</a> with periods of up to 1.2 days .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It requires an extended observing program to establish the star's true (that is, averaged over a full period) <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star#Classification" title="Star">color</a>. Such a program is usually beyond the scope of an investigation of a cluster's color–magnitude diagram. Because of this, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_stars" title="Variable stars">variable stars</a> are noted in tables of a cluster's stellar content from such an investigation, these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_stars" title="Variable stars">variable stars</a> are not included in the graphic presentation of the cluster CMD because data adequate to plot them correctly are unavailable. This omission often results in the <i>RR Lyrae gap</i> seen in many published <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a> CMDs.</p>
<p>Different <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular clusters</a> often display different HB <i>morphologies</i>, by which is meant that the relative proportions of HB stars existing blue of the RR Lyr gap, within the gap, and to the red of the gap varies sharply from cluster to cluster. The underlying cause of different HB morphologies is a long-standing problem in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_astrophysics" title="Stellar astrophysics">stellar astrophysics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Star#Chemical_composition" title="Star">Chemical composition</a> is one factor (usually in the sense that more metal-poor clusters have bluer HBs), but other stellar properties like <a href="/wiki/Star#Age" title="Star">age</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star#Rotation" title="Star">rotation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star#Chemical_composition" title="Star">helium content</a> have also been suggested as affecting HB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphology_(astronomy)" title="Morphology (astronomy)">morphology</a>. This has sometimes been called the "Second Parameter Problem" for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">globular clusters</a>, because there exist pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">globular clusters</a> which seem to have the same <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a> yet have very different HB morphologies; one such pair is <a href="/wiki/NGC_288" title="NGC 288">NGC 288</a> (which has a very blue HB) and <a href="/wiki/NGC_362" title="NGC 362">NGC 362</a> (which has a rather red HB). The label "second parameter" acknowledges that some unknown physical effect is responsible for HB morphology differences in clusters that seem otherwise identical.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_the_red_clump">Relationship to the red clump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizontal_branch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationship to the red clump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evolutionary_track_1m.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="1166" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Evolutionary_track_1m.svg/220px-Evolutionary_track_1m.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Evolutionary_track_1m.svg/330px-Evolutionary_track_1m.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Evolutionary_track_1m.svg/440px-Evolutionary_track_1m.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evolutionary_track_1m.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The evolutionary track of a sun-like star, showing the horizontal branch and red clump region</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A related class of stars is the <i>clump giants</i>, those belonging to the so-called <a href="/wiki/Red_clump" title="Red clump">red clump</a>, which are the relatively <a href="/wiki/Star#Age" title="Star">younger</a> (and hence <a href="/wiki/Star#Mass" title="Star">more massive</a>) and usually more <a href="/wiki/Star#Chemical_composition" title="Star">metal-rich</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_I" title="Population I">population I</a> counterparts to HB stars (which belong to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_II" title="Population II">population II</a>). Both HB stars and clump giants are fusing <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> in their cores, but differences in the <a href="/wiki/Star#Structure" title="Star">structure</a> of their outer layers result in the different types of stars having different radii, <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature#Star" title="Effective temperature">effective temperatures</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Star#Classification" title="Star">color</a>. Since <a href="/wiki/Star#Classification" title="Star">color index</a> is the horizontal coordinate in a <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a>, the different types of star appear in different parts of the CMD despite their common <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> source. In effect, the red clump represents one extreme of horizontal-branch morphology: all the stars are at the red end of the horizontal branch, and may be difficult to distinguish from stars ascending the red giant branch for the first time.</p>
