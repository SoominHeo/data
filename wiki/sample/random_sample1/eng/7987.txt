<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blister agent</h1>

<p>A <b>blister agent</b>, or <b>vesicant</b>, is a chemical compound that causes severe skin, eye and mucosal pain and irritation. They are named for their ability to cause severe <a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">chemical burns</a>, resulting in painful water <a href="/wiki/Blister" title="Blister">blisters</a> on the bodies of those affected. Although the term is often used in connection with large-scale burns caused by chemical spills or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare_agent" title="Chemical warfare agent">chemical warfare agents</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> some naturally occurring substances such as <a href="/wiki/Cantharidin" title="Cantharidin">cantharidin</a> are also blister-producing agents (vesicants).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Furanocoumarin" title="Furanocoumarin">Furanocoumarin</a>, another naturally occurring substance, causes vesicant-like effects indirectly, for example, by increasing skin photosensitivity greatly. Vesicants have medical uses including wart removal but can be fatal if even small amounts are ingested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blister_agents_used_in_warfare">Blister agents used in warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blister_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Blister agents used in warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most blister agents fall into one of three groups:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mustard_gas" title="Mustard gas">Sulfur mustards</a> – A family of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>-based agents, including mustard gas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_mustard" title="Nitrogen mustard">Nitrogen mustards</a> – A family of agents similar to the sulfur mustards, but based on <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> instead of sulfur.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lewisite" title="Lewisite">Lewisite</a> – An early blister agent that was developed, but not used, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. It was effectively rendered obsolete with the development of <a href="/wiki/Dimercaprol" title="Dimercaprol">British anti-Lewisite</a> in the 1940s.</li>
</ul>
<p>Occasionally, <a href="/wiki/Phosgene_oxime" title="Phosgene oxime">phosgene oxime</a> is included among the blister agents, although it is more properly termed a <a href="/wiki/Nettle_agent" title="Nettle agent">nettle agent</a> (urticant).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_blister_agents">Effects of blister agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blister_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects of blister agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Exposure to a weaponized blister agent can cause a number of life-threatening symptoms, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Severe skin, eye and mucosal pain and irritation</li>
<li>Skin <a href="/wiki/Erythema" title="Erythema">erythema</a> with large fluid <a href="/wiki/Blister" title="Blister">blisters</a> that heal slowly and may become infected</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tears" title="Tears">Tearing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conjunctivitis" title="Conjunctivitis">conjunctivitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">corneal</a> damage</li>
<li>Mild <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_distress" title="Respiratory distress">respiratory distress</a> to marked airway damage</li>
</ul>
<p>All blister agents currently known are heavier than air, and are readily absorbed through the eyes, lungs, and skin. Effects of the two mustard agents are typically delayed: exposure to vapors becomes evident in 4 to 6 hours, and skin exposure in 2 to 48 hours. The effects of <a href="/wiki/Lewisite" title="Lewisite">Lewisite</a> are immediate.</p>
