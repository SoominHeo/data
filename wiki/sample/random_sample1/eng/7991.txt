<p>A <b>patron saint</b>, <b>patroness saint</b>, <b>patron hallow</b> or <b>heavenly protector</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> who in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</a> is regarded as the heavenly <a href="/wiki/Advocacy" title="Advocacy">advocate</a> of a nation, place, craft, activity, class, clan, family or person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Catholics believe that patron saints, having already transcended to the metaphysical, are able to intercede effectively for the needs of their special charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patron_saint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Saints often become the patrons of places where they were born or had been active. However, there were cases in Medieval Europe where a city which grew to prominence and obtained for its cathedral the remains or some <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a> of a famous saint who had lived and was buried elsewhere, thus making him or her the city's patron saint – such a practice conferred considerable prestige on the city concerned. In <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, Spanish and Portuguese explorers often named a location for the saint on whose feast or commemoration they first visited the place, with that saint naturally becoming the area's patron.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Professions sometimes have a patron saint owing to that individual being involved somewhat with it, although some of the connections were tenuous. Lacking such a saint, an occupation would have a patron whose acts or miracles in some way recall the profession. For example, when the previously unknown profession of <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a> appeared in the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Veronica" title="Saint Veronica">Saint Veronica</a> was made its patron, owing to how her <a href="/wiki/Veil_of_Veronica" title="Veil of Veronica">veil</a> miraculously received the imprint of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Face" title="Holy Face">face</a> after she wiped the blood and sweat off. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denominations">Denominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patron_saint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Denominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">veneration</a> or "commemoration" and recognition of patron saints or saints in general is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholicism" title="Eastern Catholicism">Eastern Catholicism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>.</p>
<p>It is, however, generally discouraged in branches of <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>, where the practice is considered a form of <a href="/wiki/Idolatry" title="Idolatry">idolatry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
