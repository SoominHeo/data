<p>The <b>Sukhoi Su-37</b> (<a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a>: <b>Flanker-F</b>, unofficially nicknamed <b>Terminator</b>) is an experimental single-seat, <a href="/wiki/Supermaneuverability" title="Supermaneuverability">supermaneuverable</a> <a href="/wiki/Multirole_combat_aircraft" title="Multirole combat aircraft">multirole jet fighter</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi" title="Sukhoi">Sukhoi</a>. A further development of the original <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-27" title="Sukhoi Su-27">Su-27 "Flanker"</a>, it was modified from the first-generation <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-35" title="Sukhoi Su-35">Su-35</a> (formerly "T10M") prototypes. The Su-37 features an upgraded <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionic suite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">fire-control system</a> but its most notable additions are the <a href="/wiki/Thrust_vectoring" title="Thrust vectoring">thrust vectoring</a> nozzles; only two prototypes were converted.</p>
<p>During the Su-35 flight test programme, active controls during <a href="/wiki/Dogfight" title="Dogfight">dogfighting</a> maneuvers could not be attained; thus Sukhoi explored the application of thrust-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vectoring_nozzle" title="Vectoring nozzle">vectoring nozzles</a> to give fighters better dogfighting attributes. The first Su-37, converted from the eleventh Su-35, made its <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a> in April 1996 at Zhukovsky. It was joined by a second prototype in 1998. Throughout the program, the Su-37 publicly demonstrated its attributes at numerous <a href="/wiki/Air_show" title="Air show">air shows</a>, performing maneuvers impossible to complete with normal flight control methods, among which was a <a href="/wiki/Kulbit" title="Kulbit">360° somersault</a>. Despite its potential advantages, the Su-37 did not enter production; it has instead remained as a technology demonstrator for updated Su-27 family aircraft such as the export <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-30" title="Sukhoi Su-30">Su-30</a> and the modernized Su-35.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sukhoi_Su-37&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest records on <a href="/wiki/Thrust_vectoring" title="Thrust vectoring">thrust vectoring</a> technology research within Sukhoi began in 1983. The <a href="/wiki/OKB" title="OKB">design bureau</a> studied two-dimensional (2D) vector nozzle, which was believed by the West to be the best way of controlling thrust. Sukhoi modified an Su-27UB-PS twin-seater with a 2D nozzle to verify the feasibility of this kind of nozzle. However, the design bureau's General Designer, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Simonov" title="Mikhail Simonov">Mikhail Simonov</a>, believed asymmetrical nozzles (3D) to be more suitable. At the request of Sukhoi, experiments on both types of nozzles were carried out by the Siberian Aeronautical Research Institute (SibNIA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._63_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._63-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, Sukhoi was busy with the T10M (later renamed <i>Su-35</i> in 1993) program, which was a comprehensive upgrade over the Su-27. The T10M would incorporate aerodynamic modifications, improved avionics and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armament" title="Armament">armament</a>, and have a better <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">propulsion system</a>, designed to give it <a href="/wiki/Supermaneuverability" title="Supermaneuverability">greatly enhanced agility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eden-4">[4]</a></sup> The first Su-27M prototype (T-10S-70) made its maiden flight on 28 June 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._62_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._62-5">[5]</a></sup> Changes from the Su-27 include canards, upgraded engines, new radar, and a digital <a href="/wiki/Fly-by-wire" title="Fly-by-wire">fly-by-wire</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_flight_control_system" title="Aircraft flight control system">flight-control system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_mil_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson_mil-6">[6]</a></sup> Later Su-35 prototypes featured <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpits</a> and modified <a href="/wiki/Vertical_stabilizer" title="Vertical stabilizer">vertical stabilizers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eden-4">[4]</a></sup> The Su-35's construction made significant use of <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composites</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Aluminium-lithium_alloy" title="Aluminium-lithium alloy">aluminium-lithium</a> (Al-Li) alloy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The aircraft, like the Su-27, could perform the previously unattainable "<a href="/wiki/Pugachev%27s_Cobra" title="Pugachev's Cobra">Pugachev's Cobra</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Tailslide" title="Tailslide">tailslide</a>, but during these low-speed maneuvers, active controls could not be achieved because the <a href="/wiki/Flight_control_surfaces" title="Flight control surfaces">flight control surfaces</a> were ineffective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._63_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._63-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1995, the eleventh T10M prototype, T10M-11, was delivered to Sukhoi's experimental workshop to be outfitted with exclusive systems. Built by <a href="/wiki/Komsomolsk-on-Amur_Aircraft_Production_Association" title="Komsomolsk-on-Amur Aircraft Production Association">KnAAPO</a>, its structure has increased carbon-fibre and Al-Li content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globalsecurity.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Globalsecurity.com-9">[9]</a></sup> Installed was the 2D thrust-vectoring <a href="/wiki/Saturn_AL-31" title="Saturn AL-31">Lyulka AL-31FP</a>, an interim measure pending the availability of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_AL-31" title="Saturn AL-31">AL-37FU</a> (<i>Forsazh Upravleniye</i>, "afterburner-controlled").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eden-4">[4]</a></sup> The 3D thrust-vectoring <a href="/wiki/Saturn_AL-31" title="Saturn AL-31">Lyulka AL-37FU</a> was still in development. The Al-31FP, in fact, is a hybrid version combining the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_AL-31" title="Saturn AL-31">Al-31F</a> and the vectoring nozzle of the Al-37FU. Being fitted with a 2D vector nozzle, the Al-31FP is only variable in pitch, plus or minus 15°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._64_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._64-10">[10]</a></sup> The engine not only incorporates a new-generation 2D TVC nozzle but also is resistant to engine <a href="/wiki/Compressor_stall" title="Compressor stall">surge</a> even during inverted and flat spins, giving better reliability and maneuverability even when the <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a> is 180°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">fire-control system</a> was also improved. An upgraded <a href="/wiki/Bars_radar" title="Bars radar">N-011M BARS</a> <a href="/wiki/Passive_electronically_scanned_array" title="Passive electronically scanned array">Passive electronically scanned array</a> radar was fitted. It can capable of tracking 15 aerial targets and guiding four <a href="/wiki/Air-to-air_missile" title="Air-to-air missile">air-to-air missiles</a>. In the aircraft's tail sting is an N-012 rearward facing radar, which has a 120° view horizontally and in elevation. The Su-37 also features an upgraded <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare_support_measures" title="Electronic warfare support measures">electronic warfare support measures</a> package.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It can carry <a href="/wiki/Air-to-air_missile" title="Air-to-air missile">air-to-air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air-to-ground_weaponry" title="Air-to-ground weaponry">air-to-surface weapons</a> on 12 <a href="/wiki/Hardpoint" title="Hardpoint">hardpoints</a>. The vast range of weapons, of which the Su-37 can carry 8,000 kg (17,600 lb), is supplemented by the 150-round 30 mm <a href="/wiki/Gryazev-Shipunov_GSh-301" title="Gryazev-Shipunov GSh-301">GSh-301</a> cannon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._148_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._148-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instead of traditional analogue instruments, the cockpit has four T-form <a href="/wiki/Thales_Group" title="Thales Group">Sextan Avionique</a> (Thales) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCD" title="LCD">LCD</a> <a href="/wiki/Multi-function_display" title="Multi-function display">multi-function displays</a> (MFD). These display air data/navigation, systems status, weapons/systems selection and tactical situation information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._65_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._65-14">[14]</a></sup> The pilot, who is provided with a <a href="/wiki/Head-up_display" title="Head-up display">head-up display</a> (HUD), sits on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-36DM" title="K-36DM">K-36DM</a> ejection seat inclined 30° to help counter the effects of high g-forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._65_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._65-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su-37_Sci-fi_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Su-37_Sci-fi-16">[16]</a></sup> The pilot steers with a <a href="/wiki/Side-stick" title="Side-stick">side-stick</a> and pressure-sensing throttles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._65_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._65-14">[14]</a></sup> The two-grip flying control configuration was designed to prevent the pilot from losing contact with the controls when the aircraft is engaged in fast vectored-thrust maneuvers. Both the fixed throttle and the side-stick controller provide secure points for the pilot to brace his hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su-37_Sci-fi_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Su-37_Sci-fi-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Painted in a disruptive sand and brown scheme, the aircraft was given the code <i>711 Blue</i>, later changed to <i>711 White</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._66_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._66-18">[18]</a></sup> Its maiden flight occurred at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramenskoye_Airport" title="Ramenskoye Airport">Zhukovsky</a> on 2 April 1996, with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yevgeni_Ivanovich_Frolov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yevgeni Ivanovich Frolov (page does not exist)">Yevgeni Frolov</a> at the controls. The nozzle was locked fully aft throughout the flight, and it was not until the sixth flight that tests on the thrust-vector nozzles commenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su-37_Sci-fi_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Su-37_Sci-fi-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI_Su-37_thrust_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI_Su-37_thrust-19">[19]</a></sup> Frolov was joined by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Igor_Votintsev&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Igor Votintsev (page does not exist)">Igor Votintsev</a>, and for the next twelve days, the two had amassed twelve flights between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._66_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._66-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sukhoi_Su-37&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

A modernized Su-35Ѕ performing at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAKS_(air_show)" title="MAKS (air show)">Moscow International Aviation and Space Show</a> in 2009. <p>The 711 Blue was demonstrated to the press at Zhukovsky in spring 1996, by which time it was re-designated <i>Su-37</i>. Its debut to the general public came in September that year at the Farnborough Airshow, piloted by Sukhoi test pilot Yevgeni Frolov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eden-4">[4]</a></sup> A big talking point of the show was its performance of the "<a href="/wiki/Pugachev%27s_Cobra" title="Pugachev's Cobra">Super Cobra</a>", when the aircraft pitched up to 180°, literally flying tail first, albeit for only a few seconds; this maneuver evolved into the 360° "<a href="/wiki/Kulbit" title="Kulbit">Frolov Chakra</a>" somersault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globalsecurity.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Globalsecurity.com-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._66_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._66-18">[18]</a></sup> The following year, the aircraft arrived on the fifth and last day of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Air_Show" title="Paris Air Show">Paris Air Show</a> to perform five aerial displays, the fourth of which was marred by a minor incident when the emergency undercarriage extension handle was moved, preventing gear retraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It appeared at that year's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAKS_(air_show)" title="MAKS (air show)">Moscow International Aviation and Space Show</a> and Dubai <a href="/wiki/International_Defence_Exhibition" title="International Defence Exhibition">International Defence Exhibition</a>, as well as the 1998 <a href="/wiki/FIDAE" title="FIDAE">FIDAE</a> air show in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>; for the latter two the Su-37 appeared under the enigmatic designation, "Su-37MR".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, there was a report about a second Su-37 (T10M-12) which was similarly converted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p34-37_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p34-37-1">[1]</a></sup> before making its first flight on 23 March the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> But this aircraft, No.712, was never converted to the Su-37 specifications; it was used in Aktubinsk for weapon and radar tests. Later, the No.712 (with the No.703 and all three serial produced planes, bort No. 86, 87, and 88) added to the Russkie Vityazi demo team, repainted. Flight tests and demonstrations at air shows continued until 2000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2001, 711 was outfitted with AL-37FU engines and updated fly-by-wire flight control system and avionics. In December 2002, 711 crashed during a ferry flight, effectively bringing an end to the program; the fault was later traced to structurally damaged right horizontal stabilizer, caused by the extensive load during high g post-stall tight turns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Su-37 did not reach the production stage, although by mid-2000, Sukhoi introduced several modernizations of the Su-27, such as <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-30MKI" title="Sukhoi Su-30MKI">Su-30MKI</a> and second generation Su-35, which feature similar improvements such as improved avionics and radar systems, and <a href="/wiki/Thrust_vectoring" title="Thrust vectoring">thrust vectoring</a> engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su-35_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Su-35-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/R-37_(missile)" title="R-37 (missile)">Vympel R-37M</a> 200 km<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kh-29" title="Kh-29">Kh-29</a>T/L</li>

</li>
<li><b>Bombs:</b><br/>
