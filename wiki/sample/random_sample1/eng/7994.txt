<p><b>Sukjeongmun</b> (Hangul 숙정문, Hanja 肅靖門; also known as North Gate) is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eight_Gates_of_Seoul" title="Eight Gates of Seoul">The Eight Gates of Seoul</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fortress_Wall_of_Seoul" title="Fortress Wall of Seoul">Fortress Wall</a> of <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, which surrounded the city in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>. The gate is also known as Bukdaemun (북대문, “North Big Gate”). It was built north of Seoul behind Gyeongbokgung Place. It was rarely used. It was only used in ceremonious and symbolic functions. To access it requires identification like passport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sukjeongmun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sukjeongmun was originally built in 1396,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and was originally called Sukcheongmun (肅淸門), but its name was modified slightly to its current name (肅靖門) in the early 16th century. Being situated so close to the <a href="/wiki/Gyeongbokgung" title="Gyeongbokgung">Royal Palace</a> of the Joseon Dynasty, it was rarely used for receiving visitors, and had more of a ceremonious function. The original wooden gatehouse over the gate was destroyed by fire, and the current gatehouse dates from 1976.</p>
<p>The name Sukjeongmun means literally “Rule Solemnly Gate.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is one of the Four Great Gates (사대문) in the Fortress Wall of Seoul.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Gate_today">The Gate today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sukjeongmun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Gate today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the infiltration of North Korean agents during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_House_Raid" title="Blue House Raid">Blue House Raid</a> in 1968, both the gate and the surrounding area were closed off for security reasons. They were opened again for public touring by 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the area is still a highly secured area, patrolled by South Korean Army soldiers. Visiting Sukjeongmun today (2012) requires identification and issuing of a pass. Pictures of army soldiers or pictures facing south (toward the Presidential <a href="/wiki/Blue_House" title="Blue House">residence</a>) are not allowed at the gate or while hiking along the northern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Fortress_Wall_of_Seoul" title="Fortress Wall of Seoul">Fortress Wall</a>.</p>
