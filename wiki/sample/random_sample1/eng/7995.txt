<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cyanoacrylate</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Cyanoacrylates</b> are a family of strong fast-acting <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a> with industrial, medical, and household uses. Cyanoacrylate adhesives have a short <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a> if not used, about one year from manufacture if unopened, one month once opened. They have some <a href="#Toxicity">minor toxicity</a>.</p>
<p>Cyanoacrylates include <a href="/wiki/Methyl_cyanoacrylate" title="Methyl cyanoacrylate">methyl 2-cyanoacrylate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_cyanoacrylate" title="Ethyl cyanoacrylate">ethyl-2-cyanoacrylate</a> (commonly sold under trade names such as "Super Glue" and "Krazy Glue"), <a href="/wiki/Butyl_cyanoacrylate" title="Butyl cyanoacrylate">n-butyl cyanoacrylate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-octyl_cyanoacrylate" title="2-octyl cyanoacrylate">2-octyl cyanoacrylate</a> (used in medical, veterinary and first aid applications). Octyl cyanoacrylate was developed to address toxicity concerns and to reduce skin irritation and allergic response. Cyanoacrylate adhesives are sometimes known generically as <b>instant glues</b>, <b>power glues</b> or <b>superglues</b> (although "Super Glue" is a trade name).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-1">[1]</a></sup> The abbreviation "CA" is commonly used for industrial grades.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:181px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harry_Wesley_Coover_Jr.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="197" data-file-width="179" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/Harry_Wesley_Coover_Jr.png" width="179"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harry_Wesley_Coover_Jr.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Harry Wesley Coover Jr. shortly before being awarded the National Medal of Technology and Innovation by US President Barack Obama in 2010</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The original patent for cyanoacrylate was filed in 1942 by Goodrich Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as an outgrowth of a search for materials suitable for clear plastic <a href="/wiki/Sight_(device)" title="Sight (device)">gun sights</a> for <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">the war</a> effort. In 1942, a team of scientists headed by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Coover" title="Harry Coover">Harry Coover</a> Jr. stumbled upon a formulation that stuck to everything with which it came in contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The team quickly rejected the substance for the wartime application, but in 1951, while working as researchers for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" title="Eastman Kodak">Eastman Kodak</a>, Coover and a colleague, Fred Joyner, rediscovered cyanoacrylates. The two realized the true commercial potential, and a form of the adhesive was first sold in 1958 under the title "Eastman #910" (later "Eastman 910").</p>
<p>During the 1960s, Eastman Kodak sold cyanoacrylate to <a href="/wiki/Loctite" title="Loctite">Loctite</a>, which in turn repackaged and distributed it under a different brand name "Loctite Quick Set 404". In 1971 Loctite developed its own manufacturing technology and introduced its own line of cyanoacrylate, called "Super Bonder". Loctite quickly gained market share, and by the late 1970s it was believed to have exceeded Eastman Kodak's share in the North American industrial cyanoacrylate market. National Starch and Chemical Company purchased Eastman Kodak’s cyanoacrylate business and combined it with several acquisitions made throughout the 1970s forming Permabond. Other manufacturers of cyanoacrylate include LePage (a Canadian company acquired by <a href="/wiki/Henkel" title="Henkel">Henkel</a> in 1996), the Permabond Division of National Starch and Chemical, Inc., which was a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a>. Together, Loctite, Eastman and Permabond accounted for approximately 75% of the industrial cyanoacrylate market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> Permabond continued to manufacture the original 910 formula.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In its liquid form, cyanoacrylate consists of <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomers</a> of cyanoacrylate molecules. Methyl-2-cyanoacrylate (CH<sub>2</sub>=C(CN)COOCH<sub>3</sub> or C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NO<sub>2</sub>) has a molecular weight equal to 111.1, a <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flashpoint</a> of 79 °C, and a density of 1.1 g/ml.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Ethyl 2-cyanoacrylate (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>7</sub>NO<sub>2</sub>) has a molecular weight equal to 125 and a flashpoint of &gt;75 °C. To facilitate easy handling, a cyanoacrylate adhesive is frequently formulated with an ingredient such as <a href="/wiki/Fumed_silica" title="Fumed silica">fumed silica</a> to make it more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscous" title="Viscous">viscous</a> or gel-like. More recently, formulations are available with additives to increase shear strength, creating a more impact resistant bond. Such additives may include rubber, as in Loctite's <i>Ultra Gel</i>, or others which are not specified.</p>
<p>In general, cyanoacrylate is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acryl_group" title="Acryl group">acrylic</a> <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a> that rapidly <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymerises</a> in the presence of water (specifically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxide_ion" title="Hydroxide ion">hydroxide ions</a>), forming long, strong chains, joining the bonded surfaces together. Because the presence of moisture causes the glue to set, exposure to normal levels of humidity in the air causes a thin skin to start to form within seconds, which very greatly slows the reaction. Because of this cyanoacrylate is applied thinly, to ensure that the reaction proceeds rapidly for bonding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The reaction with moisture can cause a container of glue which has been opened and resealed to become unusable more quickly than if never opened. To minimise this reduction <a href="#Shelf_life">in shelf life</a> cyanoacrylate, once opened, can be stored in an airtight container with a package of <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a> desiccant. Another technique is to insert a hypodermic needle into the opening of a tube. After using the glue, residual glue soon clogs the needle, keeping moisture out. The clog is removed by heating the needle (e.g. with a lighter) before use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The polymerisation is also temperature-dependant: storage at zero degrees or below stops it, so keeping it in the freezer is also effective.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviours">Behaviours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanoacrylates are mainly used as adhesives. They require some care and knowledge for effective use: they do not bond some materials; <a href="#Shelf_life">their shelf life</a> at room temperature is about 12 months unopened and one month once opened; they do not fill spaces, unlike epoxies, and a very thin layer bonds more effectively than a thicker one that does not cure properly; they bond many substances, including human skin and tissues. They have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> reaction to natural fibres: cotton, wool, leather, see <a href="/wiki/Cyanoacrylate#cotton" title="Cyanoacrylate">reaction with cotton</a> below.</p>
<p>Cyanoacrylate glue has a low <a href="/wiki/Shearing_(physics)" title="Shearing (physics)">shearing</a> strength, which has led to its use as a temporary adhesive in cases where the piece needs to be sheared off later. Common examples include mounting a workpiece to a sacrificial glue block on a <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">lathe</a>, and tightening pins and bolts. It is also used in conjunction with another, slower, but more resilient adhesive as way of rapidly forming a joint, which then holds the pieces in the appropriate configuration until the second adhesive has set.</p>
<p>Cyanoacrylate is not resistant to friction if applied to a smooth surface. Using either sugar or sandpaper can remove a good amount of cyanoacrylate from a user's fingertips.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanoacrylates are used to assemble prototype <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Wire_wrap" title="Wire wrap">wire wrap</a>), flying <a href="/wiki/Model_aircraft" title="Model aircraft">model aircraft</a>, and as retention dressings for <a href="/wiki/Nut_(hardware)" title="Nut (hardware)">nuts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Screw#bolt" title="Screw">bolts</a>. Their effectiveness in bonding metal and general versatility have made them popular among <a href="/wiki/Scale_model" title="Scale model">modeling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miniature_figure_(gaming)" title="Miniature figure (gaming)">miniatures</a> hobbyists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquaria">Aquaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aquaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanoacrylate glue's ability to resist water has made it popular with <a href="/wiki/Marine_aquarium" title="Marine aquarium">marine aquarium</a> hobbyists for <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(reproduction)" title="Fragmentation (reproduction)">fragging</a> corals. The cut branches of <a href="/wiki/Scleractinia" title="Scleractinia">hard corals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Acropora" title="Acropora">Acropora</a> can be glued to a piece of <a href="/wiki/Live_rock" title="Live rock">live rock</a> (harvested reef coral) or <a href="/wiki/Milliput" title="Milliput">Milliput</a> (epoxy putty) to allow the new frag to grow out. It is safe to use directly in the tank, unlike silicone, which must be cured to be safe. However, as a class of adhesives, traditional cyanoacrylates are classified as having "weak" resistance to both moisture and heat<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> although the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Phthalic_anhydride" title="Phthalic anhydride">phthalic anhydride</a> reportedly counteracts both of these characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding_smooth_surfaces">Bonding smooth surfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bonding smooth surfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most standard cyanoacrylate adhesives do not bond well with smooth glass, although they can be used as a quick, temporary bond prior to application of an epoxy or cyanoacrylate specifically formulated for use on glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Adhesion#Mechanical_adhesion" title="Adhesion">mechanical adhesive</a> bond may be formed around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_fibre" title="Glass fibre">glass fibre</a> mat or tissue to reinforce joints or to fabricate small parts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filler">Filler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Filler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When added to baking soda (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>), cyanoacrylate glue forms a hard, lightweight adhesive filler (baking soda is first used to fill a gap then the adhesive is dropped onto the baking soda). This works well with porous materials that the glue does not work well with alone. This method is sometimes used by aircraft modelers to assemble or repair polystyrene foam parts. It is also used to repair small nicks in the leading edge of composite <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a> blades on light aircraft. The reaction between cyanoacrylate and baking soda is very <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a> (heat-producing) and also produces noxious vapors. See <a href="/wiki/Cyanoacrylate#Reaction_with_cotton_and_wool" title="Cyanoacrylate">Reaction with cotton and wool</a> below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forensics">Forensics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Forensics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanoacrylate is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic" title="Forensic">forensic</a> tool to capture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latent_fingerprint" title="Latent fingerprint">latent fingerprints</a> on non-porous surfaces like glass, plastic, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Cyanoacrylate is warmed to produce fumes that react with the invisible fingerprint residues and atmospheric moisture to form a white polymer (polycyanoacrylate) on the fingerprint ridges. The ridges can then be recorded. The developed fingerprints are, on most surfaces (except on white plastic or similar), visible to the naked eye. Invisible or poorly visible prints can be further enhanced by applying a luminescent or non-luminescent stain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Woodworking">Woodworking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Woodworking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thin CA glue has application in woodworking.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It can be used as a fast-drying, glossy finish. The use of oil (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiled_linseed_oil" title="Boiled linseed oil">boiled linseed oil</a>) may be used to control the rate at which the CA cures. CA glue is also used in combination with sawdust (from a saw or sanding) to fill voids and cracks. These repair methods are used on piano soundboards, wood instruments, and wood furniture. CA glue is also used in the finishing of pen blanks that have been turned on a lathe by applying multiple thin layers to build up a hard, clear finish that can then be sanded and polished to a very glossy finish.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>CA glue was in <b>veterinary</b> use for mending bone, hide, and tortoise shell by the early 1970s or before. <a href="/wiki/Harry_Coover" title="Harry Coover">Harry Coover</a> said in 1966 that a CA spray was used in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> to reduce bleeding in wounded soldiers until they could be brought to a hospital. <i>n</i>-<a href="/wiki/Butyl_cyanoacrylate" title="Butyl cyanoacrylate">Butyl cyanoacrylate</a> has been used medically since the 1970s. In the US, due to its potential to irritate the skin, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> did not approve its use as a medical adhesive until 1998 with <a href="/wiki/2-Octyl_cyanoacrylate" title="2-Octyl cyanoacrylate">Dermabond</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Studies confirm that cyanoacrylate is safer and more functional for wound closure than traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suturing" title="Suturing">suturing</a> (stitches).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Non-suture_Closure_of_Wounds_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Non-suture_Closure_of_Wounds-11">[11]</a></sup> The adhesive is superior in time required to close wounds, incidence of infection (suture canals through the skin's epidermal, dermal, and subcutaneous fat layers introduce extra routes of contamination),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Non-suture_Closure_of_Wounds_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Non-suture_Closure_of_Wounds-11">[11]</a></sup> and final cosmetic appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Rock_climbing" title="Rock climbing">rock climbers</a> use cyanoacrylate to repair damage to the skin on their fingertips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Similarly, stringed-instrument players can form protective finger caps (in addition to calluses) with cyanoacrylates. While the glue is not very toxic and wears off quickly with shed skin, applying large quantities of glue and its fumes directly to the skin can cause chemical burns.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>While standard "superglue" is 100% ethyl cyanoacrylate, many custom formulations (<i>e.g.,</i> 91% ECA, 9% <a href="/wiki/Poly(methyl_methacrylate)" title="Poly(methyl methacrylate)">poly(methyl methacrylate)</a>, &lt;0.5% <a href="/wiki/Hydroquinone" title="Hydroquinone">hydroquinone</a>, and a small amount of organic <a href="/wiki/Sulfonic_acid" title="Sulfonic acid">sulfonic acid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and variations on the compound <i>n</i>-butyl-cyanoacrylate for medical applications<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Non-suture_Closure_of_Wounds_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Non-suture_Closure_of_Wounds-11">[11]</a></sup>) have come to be used for specific applications. There are 3 cyanoacrylate compounds currently available as topical skin adhesives. 2-Octyl-cyanoacrylate is marketed as Dermabond and SurgiSeal. <a href="/wiki/Butyl_cyanoacrylate" title="Butyl cyanoacrylate">n-2-Butyl-cyanoacrylate</a> is marketed as Histoacryl, Indermil, GluStitch, GluSeal, PeriAcryl, and LiquiBand. The compound 2-Ethyl-cyanoacrylate is available as Epiglu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archery">Archery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Archery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanoacrylate is used in <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archery</a> to glue <a href="/wiki/Fletching" title="Fletching">fletching</a> to arrow shafts. Some special fletching glues are primarily cyanoacrylate repackaged in special fletching glue kits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Such tubes often have a long thin metal nozzle for improved precision in applying the glue to the base of the fletching and to ensure secure bonding to the arrow shaft.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmetics">Cosmetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cosmetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanoacrylate is used in the cosmetology/beauty industry as a "nail glue" for some artificial nail enhancements such as nail tips and nail wraps, and is sometimes mistaken for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eye_drops" title="Eye drops">eye drops</a> causing accidental injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham2001_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Needham2001-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_issues">Safety issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safety issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skin_injuries">Skin injuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Skin injuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CA adhesives may adhere to body parts, and injuries may occur when parts of the skin are torn off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clarke2011_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke2011-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Without force, however, the glue will spontaneously separate from the skin in time (up to four days). Separation can be accelerated by applying vegetable oil near, on, and around the glue. In the case of glued eyelids, a doctor should be consulted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fumes from CA are a vaporized form of the cyanoacrylate monomer that irritate sensitive membranes in the eyes, nose, and throat. They are immediately polymerized by the moisture in the membranes and become inert. These risks can be minimized by using CA in well ventilated areas. About 5% of the population can become sensitized to CA fumes after repeated exposure, resulting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flu-like_symptoms" title="Flu-like symptoms">flu-like symptoms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> CA may also be a skin irritant, causing an allergic skin reaction. The <a href="/wiki/American_Conference_of_Governmental_Industrial_Hygienists" title="American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists">ACGIH</a> assign a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threshold_Limit_Value" title="Threshold Limit Value">Threshold Limit Value</a> exposure limit of 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">parts per billion</a>. On rare occasions, inhalation may trigger <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>. There is no singular measurement of toxicity for all cyanoacrylate adhesives because of the large number of adhesives that contain various cyanoacrylate formulations.</p>
<p>The United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Safety_Executive" title="Health and Safety Executive">Health and Safety Executive</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Toxicology_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="United States National Toxicology Program (page does not exist)">United States National Toxicology Program</a> have concluded that the use of ethyl cyanoacrylate is safe and that additional study is unnecessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The compound 2-octyl cyanoacrylate degrades much more slowly due to its longer organic backbone and the adhesive does not reach the threshold of tissue toxicity. Due to the toxicity issues of ethyl cyanoacrylate, the use of 2-octyl cyanoacrylate for sutures is preferred.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_cotton_and_wool">Reaction with cotton and wool <span id="cotton"></span> <span id="wool"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reaction with cotton and wool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Applying cyanoacrylate to some natural materials such as <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cotton_swab" title="Cotton swab">cotton swabs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotton_ball" title="Cotton ball">cotton balls</a>, and certain yarns or fabrics) results in a powerful, rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> reaction. The heat released may cause serious burns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> ignite the cotton product, or release irritating white smoke. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_Safety_Data_Sheet" title="Material Safety Data Sheet">Material Safety Data Sheets</a> for cyanoacrylate instruct users not to wear cotton or wool clothing, especially cotton gloves, when applying or handling cyanoacrylates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvents_and_debonders">Solvents and debonders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Solvents and debonders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">Acetone</a>, commonly found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nail_polish_remover" title="Nail polish remover">nail polish remover</a>, is a widely available solvent capable of softening cured cyanoacrylate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Other solvents include <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylene_chloride" title="Methylene chloride">methylene chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Butyrolactone" title="Gamma-Butyrolactone">gamma-Butyrolactone</a> may also be used to remove cured cyanoacrylate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Commercial debonders are also available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shelf_life">Shelf life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanoacrylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Shelf life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CA adhesives have a short shelf life. Date-stamped containers help to ensure that the adhesive is still viable. One manufacturer supplies the following information and advice:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When kept unopened in a cool, dry location such as a refrigerator at a temperature of about 55 °F (13 °C), the shelf life of cyanoacrylate will be extended from about one year from manufacture to at least 15 months. If the adhesive is to be used within six months, it is not necessary to refrigerate it. Cyanoacrylates are moisture-sensitive, and moving from a cool to a hot location will create condensation; after removing from the refrigerator, it is best to let the adhesive reach room temperature before opening. After opening, it should be used within 30 days. Open containers should not be refrigerated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another manufacturer says that the maximum shelf life of 12 months is obtained for some of their cyanoacrylates if the original containers are stored at 35 to 40 °F (2 to 4 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> User forums and some manufacturers say that an almost unlimited shelf life is attainable by storing unopened at −4 °F (−20 °C), the typical temperature of a domestic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezer" title="Freezer">freezer</a>, and allowing the contents to reach room temperature before use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Rechilling an opened container may cause moisture from the air to condense in the container; however, reports from hobbyists suggest that storing CA in a freezer can preserve opened cyanoacrylate indefinitely.</p>
<p>As cyanoacrylates age, they polymerize, become thicker, and cure more slowly. They can be thinned with a cyanoacrylate of the same chemical composition with lower viscosity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Storing cyanoacrylates below 0 °F (−18 °C) will nearly stop the polymerization process and prevent aging.</p>
