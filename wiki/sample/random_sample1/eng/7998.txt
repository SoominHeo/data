<p><b>Xun Yi</b> (died 274),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RDC_death_year_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-RDC_death_year-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> <b>Jingqian</b>, was an official of the state of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period of China. After the fall of Wei, he continued serving under the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265–420)">Jin dynasty</a>, which replaced Wei in 265. He was the sixth son of <a href="/wiki/Xun_Yu" title="Xun Yu">Xun Yu</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_background_and_early_life">Family background and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xun_Yi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family background and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Xun Yi's <a href="/wiki/Ancestral_home_(Chinese)" title="Ancestral home (Chinese)">ancestral home</a> was in Yingchuan Commandery (穎川郡; around present-day <a href="/wiki/Xuchang" title="Xuchang">Xuchang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a>). He was born in the influential <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Xun_family_of_Yingchuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Xun family of Yingchuan (page does not exist)">Xun family</a> as the sixth son of <a href="/wiki/Xun_Yu" title="Xun Yu">Xun Yu</a>, a prominent statesman of the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Han_dynasty" title="Eastern Han dynasty">Eastern Han dynasty</a> and an adviser to the warlord <a href="/wiki/Cao_Cao" title="Cao Cao">Cao Cao</a>. When he was still young, his brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Chen_Qun" title="Chen Qun">Chen Qun</a> already regarded him highly. Before he reached adolescence, he was already known for his <a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">filial piety</a>, and for being knowledgeable, insightful and meticulous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_in_Cao_Wei">Service in Cao Wei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xun_Yi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Service in Cao Wei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to his father's past contributions, Xun Yi was given an appointment as a Palace Gentleman (中郎) in the state of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Sima_Yi" title="Sima Yi">Sima Yi</a> was the regent of Wei, he felt that Xun Yi was a rare talent and once remarked, "<a href="/wiki/Xun_Yu" title="Xun Yu">Chief Imperial Secretary Xun</a>'s son is comparable to <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Huan" title="Yuan Huan">Yaoqing</a>'s son Yuan Kan (袁侃)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Xun Yi was later promoted to a Gentleman of Scattered Cavalry (散騎侍郎) and then to a Palace Attendant (侍中).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Xun Yi was a tutor to the third Wei emperor, <a href="/wiki/Cao_Fang" title="Cao Fang">Cao Fang</a>. He was also commissioned as a Cavalry Commandant (騎都尉) and awarded the title of a Secondary Marquis (關內侯). He studied the <i><a href="/wiki/I_Ching" title="I Ching">Yijing</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Zhong_Hui" title="Zhong Hui">Zhong Hui</a> and had philosophical debates with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sima_Jun_(Jin_dynasty_prince)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sima Jun (Jin dynasty prince) (page does not exist)">Sima Jun</a> on the Confucian values <i><a href="/wiki/Ren_(Confucianism)" title="Ren (Confucianism)">ren</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">xiao</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the regent <a href="/wiki/Cao_Shuang" title="Cao Shuang">Cao Shuang</a> was in power from 239 to 249,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the official <a href="/wiki/He_Yan" title="He Yan">He Yan</a> and others wanted to harm <a href="/wiki/Fu_Jia" title="Fu Jia">Fu Jia</a>, but Xun Yi saved him. After the regent <a href="/wiki/Sima_Shi" title="Sima Shi">Sima Shi</a> deposed Cao Fang and replaced him with <a href="/wiki/Cao_Mao" title="Cao Mao">Cao Mao</a> as the emperor of Wei in 254,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_76_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_76-7">[7]</a></sup> Xun Yi advised Sima to use the opportunity to announce the new emperor and see how his potential political rivals would react. In the same year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_76_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_76-7">[7]</a></sup> the generals <a href="/wiki/Guanqiu_Jian" title="Guanqiu Jian">Guanqiu Jian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wen_Qin" title="Wen Qin">Wen Qin</a>, who opposed Sima Shi's act of changing the emperor, started <a href="/wiki/Guanqiu_Jian_and_Wen_Qin%27s_Rebellion" title="Guanqiu Jian and Wen Qin's Rebellion">a rebellion</a> in Shouchun Commandery (壽春郡; around present-day <a href="/wiki/Shou_County" title="Shou County">Shou County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">Anhui</a>). Xun Yi assisted Sima Shi in suppressing the rebellion. As a reward for his efforts, he was enfeoffed as the Marquis of Wansui Village (萬歲亭侯) and given 400 taxable households to form his marquisate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following Sima Shi's death in 255, his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhao" title="Sima Zhao">Sima Zhao</a> became the new regent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_76_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_76-7">[7]</a></sup> Xun Yi was promoted to a Master of Writing (尚書). Between 257 and 258,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_77_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_77-9">[9]</a></sup> when Sima Zhao was on a campaign to suppress <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Dan%27s_Rebellion" title="Zhuge Dan's Rebellion">a rebellion</a> by <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Dan" title="Zhuge Dan">Zhuge Dan</a>, he left Xun Yi behind to guard the imperial capital <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a> in his absence. In 260,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_77_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_77-9">[9]</a></sup> after his maternal nephew <a href="/wiki/Chen_Tai" title="Chen Tai">Chen Tai</a> died, Xun Yi replaced him as a Supervisor (僕射) and took charge of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Departments_and_Six_Ministries" title="Three Departments and Six Ministries">Ministry of Personnel</a>. After he took over the ministry, he implemented more stringent practices to ensure that officials were carefully selected and appointed. During the Xianxi era (264-265) in the reign of the last Wei emperor <a href="/wiki/Cao_Huan" title="Cao Huan">Cao Huan</a>, Xun Yi served as <a href="/wiki/Three_Ducal_Ministers" title="Three Ducal Ministers">Minister of Works</a> and was promoted from a village marquis to a district marquis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Xun Yi was known for his filial piety, which he maintained even when he was already in his 60s. When his mother died, he left office to perform <a href="/wiki/Filial_mourning_(East_Asia)" title="Filial mourning (East Asia)">filial mourning</a> and displayed such deep sorrow over her death that he earned praise from his contemporaries for his filial piety. Sima Zhao also provided escorts for Xun Yi when he travelled around. In 265,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_79_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_79-11">[11]</a></sup> after the Cao Wei state <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Shu_by_Wei" title="Conquest of Shu by Wei">conquered</a> one of its rival states, <a href="/wiki/Shu_Han" title="Shu Han">Shu Han</a>, it wanted to restore the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_nobility#Male_aristocracy" title="Chinese nobility">five-tiered nobility hierarchy</a> system so it put Xun Yi in charge of the process. Xun Yi proposed to the Wei imperial court to allow <a href="/wiki/Yang_Hu" title="Yang Hu">Yang Hu</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ren_Kai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ren Kai (page does not exist)">Ren Kai</a> (任愷), <a href="/wiki/Geng_Jun" title="Geng Jun">Geng Jun</a> (庚峻), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ying_Zhen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ying Zhen (page does not exist)">Ying Zhen</a> (應貞) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kong_Hao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kong Hao (page does not exist)">Kong Hao</a> (孔顥) to assist him, and they collectively drafted a set of rules governing imperial protocol and etiquette. Xun Yi was also promoted from a district marquis to a county marquis under the title "Marquis of Linhuai" (臨淮侯).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_under_the_Jin_dynasty">Service under the Jin dynasty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xun_Yi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Service under the Jin dynasty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Xun Yi continued serving under the government of the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265–420)">Jin dynasty</a>, which replaced the state of Cao Wei in 265.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ_79_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ_79-11">[11]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Wu of Jin">Sima Yan (Emperor Wu)</a> was enthroned as the first Jin emperor, he promoted Xun Yi from a county marquis to a duke under the title "Duke of Linhuai" (臨淮公), with 1,800 taxable households in his dukedom. Emperor Wu also appointed Xun Yi as <a href="/wiki/Three_Ducal_Ministers" title="Three Ducal Ministers">Minister over the Masses</a>. Later, Xun Yi was concurrently appointed as a Palace Attendant and promoted to Grand Marshal (太尉), putting him in charge of military affairs and in command of 100 of the emperor's close guards. Shortly after, Xun Yi was given an additional appointment as acting Grand Tutor to the Crown Prince (太子太傅).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Xun Yi died in 274<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> during the Taishi era (265–274) of Emperor Wu's reign. Before his death, he had been tasked with arranging the music for two dance pieces, <i>Zhengde</i> (正德) and <i>Dayu</i> (大豫). Emperor Wu held a grand state funeral for Xun Yi and ordered the crown prince <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Hui_of_Jin" title="Emperor Hui of Jin">Sima Zhong</a> to pay respects at the funeral. He also honoured Xun Yi with the <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous name</a> "Kang" (康), hence Xun Yi was formally known as "Duke Kang of Linhuai" (臨淮康公). Xun Yi's family members were given two million coins to build a house because Xun Yi and his family had no fixed residence when he was still living. In the early Xianning era (275–280) of Emperor Wu's reign, the emperor issued an imperial edict to honour his subjects who had rendered meritorious service. Xun Yi, as one of those subjects named in the edict, was enshrined in the imperial <a href="/wiki/Ancestral_shrine" title="Ancestral shrine">ancestral temple</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Xun Yi was very familiar with the rules of decorum and propriety, having read and known by heart the contents of books such as <i><a href="/wiki/Etiquette_and_Ceremonial" title="Etiquette and Ceremonial">Etiquette and Ceremonial</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Rites" title="Book of Rites">Book of Rites</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rites_of_Zhou" title="Rites of Zhou">Rites of Zhou</a></i>. Although his moral character was considered generally good, it was nonetheless tarnished by his obsequious behaviour towards, and association with, <a href="/wiki/Jia_Chong" title="Jia Chong">Jia Chong</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Xun_Xu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Xun Xu (page does not exist)">Xun Xu</a> (荀勗). When it was time for the crown prince <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Hui_of_Jin" title="Emperor Hui of Jin">Sima Zhong</a> to marry, Xun Yi nominated <a href="/wiki/Jia_Nanfeng" title="Jia Nanfeng">Jia Chong's daughter</a> to be the prince's consort. He was scorned by others for doing that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession">Succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xun_Yi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Xun Yi had no son when he died so he had no one to inherit his peerage and dukedom. Sometime in the late 380s, Xun Xu (荀序), a great-great-grandnephew of Xun Yi, inherited the peerage as the "Duke of Linhuai". After Xun Xu's death, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xiaowu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Xiaowu of Jin">Emperor Xiaowu</a> (r. 372–396) designated Xun Xu's son, Xun Heng (荀恆), as the new Duke of Linhuai. The peerage was later passed on to Xun Heng's son, Xun Longfu (荀龍符), and finally abolished in 420<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> when <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Liu_Song" title="Emperor Wu of Liu Song">Liu Yu</a> ended the Jin dynasty and founded the <a href="/wiki/Liu_Song_dynasty" title="Liu Song dynasty">Liu Song dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
