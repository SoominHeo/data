<p><b>Horse training</b> refers to a variety of practices that teach <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> to perform certain behaviors when asked to do so by humans. Horses are trained to be manageable by humans for everyday care as well as for <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">equestrian</a> activities from <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse racing</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Therapeutic_horseback_riding" title="Therapeutic horseback riding">therapeutic horseback riding</a> for people with disabilities.</p>
<p>Historically, horses were trained for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_horse" title="War horse">warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> work, sport and transport. Today, most horse training is geared toward making horses useful for a variety of recreational and sporting equestrian pursuits. Horses are also trained for specialized jobs from movie stunt work to police and crowd control activities, circus entertainment, and equine-assisted psychotherapy.</p>
<p>There is tremendous controversy over various methods of horse training and even some of the words used to describe these methods. Some techniques are considered cruel, other methods are considered gentler and more humane. However, it is beyond the scope of this article to go into the details of various training methodology, so general, basic principles are described below. The <i>see also</i> section of this article provides links to more specific information about various schools and techniques of horse training.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals">Goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trakehner_Reithengst,_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="359" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Trakehner_Reithengst%2C_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg/220px-Trakehner_Reithengst%2C_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Trakehner_Reithengst%2C_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg/330px-Trakehner_Reithengst%2C_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/Trakehner_Reithengst%2C_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trakehner_Reithengst,_Auktion_Neum%C3%BCnster_2004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Effective communication and harmony between horse and rider are among the goals of proper training</div>

<p>The range of training techniques and training goals is large, but basic <a href="/wiki/Animal_training" title="Animal training">animal training</a> concepts apply to all forms of horse training. The initial goal of most types of training is to create a horse that is safe for humans to handle (under most circumstances) and able to perform a useful task for the benefit of humans</p>
<p>A few specific considerations and some basic knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Horse_behavior" title="Horse behavior">horse behavior</a> helps a horse trainer be effective no matter what school or discipline is chosen:</p>
<ul>
<li>Safety is paramount: Horses are much larger and stronger than humans, so must be taught behavior that will not injure people.</li>
<li>Horses, like other animals, differ in brain structure from humans and thus do not have the same type of thinking and reasoning ability as human beings. Thus, the human has the responsibility to think about how to use the <a href="/wiki/Horse_behavior" title="Horse behavior">psychology of the horse</a> to lead the animal into an understanding of the goals of the <a href="/wiki/Horse_trainer" title="Horse trainer">human trainer</a>.</li>
<li>Horses are social herd animals and, when properly handled, can learn to follow and respect a human leader.</li>
<li>Horses, as prey animals, have an inborn <a href="/wiki/Fight-or-flight_response" title="Fight-or-flight response">fight or flight</a> instinct that has to be adapted to human needs. Horses need to be taught to rely upon humans to determine when fear or flight is an appropriate response to new stimuli and not to react by <a href="/wiki/Instinct" title="Instinct">instinct</a> alone.</li>
<li>Like most animals, a young horse will more easily adapt to human expectations than an older one, so human handling of the horse from a very early age is generally advised.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stages">Stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Regardless of the goal of training, most horses go through a predictable series of steps on their way to being "finished" animals for a given discipline.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_of_foals_and_younger_horses">Training of foals and younger horses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Training of foals and younger horses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most young domesticated horses are handled at birth or within the first few days of life, though some are only handled for the first time when they are weaned from their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mare_(horse)" title="Mare (horse)">mothers, or <i>dams.</i></a> Advocates of handling <a href="/wiki/Foal" title="Foal">foals</a> from birth sometimes use the concept of <a href="/wiki/Imprinting_(psychology)" title="Imprinting (psychology)">imprinting</a> to introduce a foal within its first few days and weeks of life to many of the activities they will see throughout their lives. Within a few hours of birth, a foal being imprinted will have a human touch it all over, pick up its feet, and introduce it to human touch and voice.</p>
<p>Others may leave a foal alone for its first few hours or days, arguing that it is more important to allow the foal to bond with its dam. However, even people who do not advocate imprinting often still place value on handling a foal a great deal while it is still nursing and too small to easily overpower a human. By doing so, the foal ideally will learn that humans will not harm it, but also that humans must be respected.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg/220px-Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg/330px-Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg/440px-Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Julgamento_%C3%81rabe_REFON.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Horses too young to be ridden are trained to accept a halter, taught basic skills, manners, and to become accustomed to human activity. Some, like this <a href="/wiki/Yearling_(horse)" title="Yearling (horse)">yearling</a>, are shown in conformation classes.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While a foal is far too young to be ridden, it is still able to learn skills it will need later in life. By the end of a foal's first year, it should be <i>halter-broke</i>, meaning that it allows a <a href="/wiki/Halter" title="Halter">halter</a> placed upon its head and has been taught to be led by a human at a walk and trot, to stop on command and to stand tied.</p>
<p>The young horse needs to be calm for basic <a href="/wiki/Horse_grooming" title="Horse grooming">grooming</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian">veterinary</a> care such as <a href="/wiki/Vaccination" title="Vaccination">vaccinations</a> and de-worming. A foal needs regular <a href="/wiki/Horse_hoof" title="Horse hoof">hoof</a> care and can be taught to stand while having its feet picked up and trimmed by a <a href="/wiki/Farrier" title="Farrier">farrier</a>. Ideally a young horse should learn all the basic skills it will need throughout its life, including: being caught from a field, loaded into a <a href="/wiki/Horse_trailer" title="Horse trailer">horse trailer</a>, and not to fear flapping or noisy objects. It also can be exposed to the noise and commotion of ordinary human activity, including seeing motor vehicles, hearing radios, and so on. More advanced skills sometimes taught in the first year include learning to accept <a href="/wiki/Horse_blanket" title="Horse blanket">blankets</a> placed on it, to be trimmed with electric clippers, and to be given a bath with water from a hose. The foal may learn basic voice commands for starting and stopping, and sometimes will learn to square its feet up for showing in <a href="/wiki/Halter_(horse_show)" title="Halter (horse show)">in-hand or conformation</a> classes. If these tasks are completed, the young horse will have no fear of things placed on its back, around its belly or in its mouth.</p>
<p>Some people, whether through philosophy or simply due to being pressed for time, do not handle foals significantly while they are still nursing, but wait until the foal is <a href="/wiki/Weanling" title="Weanling">weaned</a> from its dam to begin halter breaking and the other tasks of training a horse in its first year. The argument for gentling and halter-breaking at weaning is that the young horse, in crisis from being separated from its dam, will more readily bond with a human at weaning than at a later point in its life. Sometimes the tasks of basic gentling are not completed within the first year but continue when the horse is a <a href="/wiki/Yearling_(horse)" title="Yearling (horse)">yearling</a>. Yearlings are larger and more unpredictable than weanlings, plus often are easily distracted, in part due to the first signs of sexual maturity. However, they also are still highly impressionable, and though very quick and agile, are not at their full adult strength.</p>
<p>Rarer, but not uncommon even in the modern world, is the practice of leaving young horses completely unhandled until they are old enough to be ridden, usually between the age of two and four, and completing all ground training as well as training for riding at the same time. However, waiting until a horse is full grown to begin training is often far riskier for humans and requires considerably more skill to avoid injury.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_training">Ground training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ground training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wakira3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="218" data-file-width="286" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Wakira3.jpg/220px-Wakira3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/da/Wakira3.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wakira3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A young horse in Europe being longed with a surcingle and side reins</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After a young horse is taught to lead and other basic skills, various tasks can be introduced to the horse as it matures while it is still too young to be ridden. Some schools of training do a great deal of work with young horses during their yearling and two-year-old years to prepare them for riding, others merely reinforce the basic lessons taught to the horse as a foal and simply keep the horse accustomed to the presence of humans. Many times, a young horse did not have all necessary basic skills described above taught to it as a foal and its "adolescent" years are spent learning or re-learning basic lessons.</p>
<p>Several ground training techniques are commonly introduced to a young horse some time after it is a year old, but prior to being ridden. All horses usually have some or all of this ground work done prior to being ridden, though the time spent can range from hours to months. While a <a href="/wiki/Foal" title="Foal">foal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Yearling_(horse)" title="Yearling (horse)">yearling</a> can be introduced to a small amount of ground work, a young horse's bones and joints are quite soft and fragile. So, to prevent joint and <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a> injury, intense work, particularly intense work in a confined circle (such as advanced roundpenning or <a href="/wiki/Longeing" title="Longeing">longeing</a>), should wait until the horse is at least two years old. Common ground training techniques include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Liberty</i> work, sometimes called <i><a href="/wiki/Longeing" title="Longeing">free longeing</a></i>, <i>round pen work</i> or <i>roundpenning,</i> but regardless of terminology, is the process of working a loose horse in a small area (usually a <a href="/wiki/Round_pen" title="Round pen">round pen</a> 40–60 feet/15–20 meters in diameter) with the handler holding only a long whip or a rope lariat, teaching the horse to respond to the voice and body language of the handler as he or she asks the horse to move faster or slower, to change direction, and to stop.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Longeing" title="Longeing">Longeing</a></i> (Lungeing- UK), pronounced "lungeing", the training of a young horse to move in circles at the end of a long rope or line, usually about 25 to 30 feet long.</li>
<li><i>Desensitization,</i> sometimes called <i><a href="/wiki/Sacking_out" title="Sacking out">sacking out</a></i>, the process of introducing a horse to flapping objects such as blankets, teaching the horse to allow itself to be touched by an object and not to fear things that people move about a horse.</li>
<li>Introduction to a <a href="/wiki/Saddle" title="Saddle">saddle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bridle" title="Bridle">bridle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Horse_harness" title="Horse harness">harness</a>, without actually getting on the horse or hooking up a cart.</li>
<li>Ground driving, also called long-lining, teaching a young horse to move forward with a person walking behind it, a precursor to both <a href="/wiki/Driving_(horse)" title="Driving (horse)">harness driving</a> and having <a href="/wiki/Rein" title="Rein">reins</a> used by a mounted rider.</li>
<li><i>Bitting</i>, the process of accustoming a horse to a <a href="/wiki/Bit_(horse)" title="Bit (horse)">bit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bridle" title="Bridle">bridle</a>, sometimes with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Side_reins" title="Side reins">side reins</a> that attach to a saddle, harness, or <a href="/wiki/Surcingle" title="Surcingle">surcingle</a> (a wide leather or nylon band that goes around the horse's barrel) and accustom the horse to the feel of pressure on the bit.</li>
</ul>
<p>A horse is not ready to be ridden until it is accustomed to all the equipment that it needs to wear and is responsive to basic voice, and usually rein, commands to start, stop, turn and change <a href="/wiki/Horse_gait" title="Horse gait">gaits</a>.</p>
<p>For some disciplines, ground work is also used to develop specific types of muscling as well as to instill certain behaviors. When ground work incorporates both mental and muscular development, it may take considerably longer for the horse to be ready to be ridden, but advocates of these methods maintain that the additional time on the ground allows the horse to advance more quickly or with better manners once under saddle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Backing.22_or_riding_the_young_horse">"Backing" or riding the young horse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: "Backing" or riding the young horse'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1668" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg/220px-Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg/330px-Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg/440px-Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lipizzaner_DSC02443.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A young <a href="/wiki/Lipizzan" title="Lipizzan">Lipizzan</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Riding_School" title="Spanish Riding School">Spanish Riding School</a> in training equipment, wearing <a href="/wiki/Saddle" title="Saddle">saddle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridle" title="Bridle">bridle</a> and longeing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavesson" title="Cavesson">cavesson</a> so that it may be <a href="/wiki/Longeing" title="Longeing">longed</a> prior to being ridden.</div>
</div>

<p>The age that horses are first ridden, or "backed" (UK) varies considerably by breed and discipline. Many <a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred" title="Thoroughbred">Thoroughbred</a> race horses have small, light riders on their backs as early as the fall of their yearling year. Most stock horse breeds, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Quarter_Horse" title="American Quarter Horse">American Quarter Horse</a>, are ridden at the age of two. Most horses used in harness have a cart first put behind them at age two, and even some horses not ridden until age three will be trained to pull a light cart at two, in order to learn better discipline and to help develop stronger muscles with less stress. The vast majority of horses across disciplines and throughout the world are first put under saddle at the age of three. However, some slower-maturing breeds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lipizzan" title="Lipizzan">Lipizzan</a>, are not ridden until the age of four.</p>
<p>The act of getting on a horse for the first time goes by many names, including <i>backing, breaking, mounting,</i> and simply <i>riding.</i> There are many techniques for introducing the young horse to a rider or to a harness and cart for driving, but the end goal of all methods is to have the horse calmly and quietly allow a rider on its back or behind it in a cart and to respond to basic commands to go forward, change <a href="/wiki/Horse_gait" title="Horse gait">gaits</a> and speed, stop, turn and back up.</p>
<p>Ideally, a young horse will have no fear of humans and view being ridden as simply one more new lesson. A properly handled young horse that had adequate ground work will seldom buck, rear, or run away when it is ridden, even for the very first time.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="847" data-file-width="1005" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg/220px-Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg/330px-Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg/440px-Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sliding_Stop_Flachsberg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Discipline-specific training can take years to perfect.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Horses that have never been taught to be ridden can learn at any age, though it may take somewhat longer to teach an older horse. An older horse that is used to humans but has no prior bad habits is easier to put under saddle than is a completely <a href="/wiki/Feral_horse" title="Feral horse">feral horse</a> caught "wild" off the <a href="/wiki/Open_range" title="Open range">open range</a> as an adult. However, an adult feral horse may be easier to train than a domesticated animal that has previously learned to treat humans with disrespect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_for_a_specific_discipline">Training for a specific discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Training for a specific discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many horse training philosophies and techniques and details are far too extensive to describe in a single article. Also, horses have different conformation, athletic potential, temperaments and personalities, all of which may influence what techniques are used. For further information on horse training and specific disciplines, see the Bibliography and the articles below, which cover many of the concepts and different schools of thought on training and handling horses.</p>
