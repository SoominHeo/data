<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Drinking establishment</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>A <b>drinking establishment</b> is a business whose primary function is the serving of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> for consumption on the premises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Some establishments may also serve food, or have entertainment, but their main purpose is to serve alcoholic beverages. There are different types of drinking establishment ranging from seedy bars or nightclubs, sometimes termed "dive bars", to 5,000 seat beer halls and elegant places of entertainment for the elite. A public house, informally known as a "pub", is an establishment licensed to serve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic drinks</a> for consumption <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Licensing_laws_of_the_United_Kingdom#On-licence" title="Licensing laws of the United Kingdom">on the premises</a> in countries and regions of British influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_pub_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_pub-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> Although the terms are increasingly used to refer to the same thing, there is a difference between pubs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">bars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inn" title="Inn">inns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tavern" title="Tavern">taverns</a> and lounges where alcohol is served commercially. A tavern or pot-house is, loosely, a place of <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> where people gather to drink <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> and, more than likely, also be served <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, though not licensed to put up guests. The word derives from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Taberna" title="Taberna">taberna</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>ταβέρνα</i>/<a href="/wiki/Taverna" title="Taverna">taverna</a>.</p>
<p>A brewpub is a <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pub</a> or <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> that brews <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> on the premises. A beer hall (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Bierpalast, Bierstube</i></span>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pub</a> that specializes in <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>. An Izakaya is a type of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">drinking establishment</a> which also serves food to accompany the drinks. A speakeasy is an establishment that illegally sells alcoholic beverages.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bar">Bar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">Bar (establishment)</a></div>
<p>Types of bars range from seedy bars or nightclubs, sometimes termed "dive bars",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> to elegant places of entertainment for the elite. Many bars have a <a href="/wiki/Happy_hour" title="Happy hour">happy hour</a> to encourage off-peak patronage. Bars that fill to capacity sometimes implement a <a href="/wiki/Cover_charge" title="Cover charge">cover charge</a> during their peak hours. Such bars often feature entertainment, which may be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_band" title="Musical band">live band</a> or a popular <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a>. Bars provide <a href="/wiki/Bar_stool" title="Bar stool">stools</a> or chairs that are placed at tables or counters for their patrons. Some bars have entertainment on a stage, such as a live band, comedians, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go-Go_dancing" title="Go-Go dancing">go-go dancers</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Striptease" title="Striptease">strippers</a>.</p>
<p>The term "bar" is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(counter)" title="Bar (counter)">specialized counter</a> on which drinks are served. The "back bar" is a set of shelves of glasses and bottles behind that counter. In some establishments, the back bar is elaborately decorated with woodwork, etched glass, mirrors, and lights.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pub">Pub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">Pub</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pubbsm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Pubbsm.jpg/220px-Pubbsm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Pubbsm.jpg/330px-Pubbsm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Pubbsm.jpg/440px-Pubbsm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pubbsm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The interior of a typical English pub</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A public house, informally known as a "pub", is an establishment licensed to serve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic drinks</a> for consumption <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Licensing_laws_of_the_United_Kingdom#On-licence" title="Licensing laws of the United Kingdom">on the premises</a> in countries and regions of British influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_pub_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_pub-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> Although the terms are increasingly used to refer to the same thing, there is a definite difference between pubs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">bars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inn" title="Inn">inns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tavern" title="Tavern">taverns</a> and lounges where alcohol is served commercially. A pub that offers <a href="/wiki/Lodging" title="Lodging">lodging</a> may be called an <a href="/wiki/Inn" title="Inn">inn</a> or (more recently) <a href="/wiki/Hotel" title="Hotel">hotel</a> in the United Kingdom. Today, many pubs in the UK, Canada and Australia with the word "inn" or "hotel" in their names no longer offer accommodation, and in some cases have never done so. Some pubs bear the name of "hotel" because they are in countries where stringent anti-drinking laws were once in force. In Scotland until 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> only hotels could serve alcohol on Sundays. In Wales, an 1881 Act applied the same law until 1961 when local polls could lift such a ban in a district and in 1996 the last ban was lifted in <a href="/wiki/Dwyfor" title="Dwyfor">Dwyfor</a>. The need for such polls was removed by the Welsh Assembly in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are approximately 53,500<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Beer_and_Pub_Association_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_Beer_and_Pub_Association-8">[8]</a></sup> public houses in the United Kingdom. In many places, especially in villages, a pub can be the focal point of the community, so there is concern that more pubs are closing down than new ones opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The history of pubs can be traced back to Roman <a href="/wiki/Tavern" title="Tavern">taverns</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_British_Pub_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Great_British_Pub-10">[10]</a></sup> through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> alehouse, to the development of the modern generally prevailing <a href="/wiki/Tied_house" title="Tied house">tied house</a> system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tavern">Tavern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tavern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tavern" title="Tavern">Tavern</a></div>
<p>A tavern or pot-house is, loosely, a place of <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> where people gather to drink <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> and, more than likely, also be served <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, though not licensed to put up guests. The word derives from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Taberna" title="Taberna">taberna</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>ταβέρνα</i>/<a href="/wiki/Taverna" title="Taverna">taverna</a>, whose original meaning was a <a href="/wiki/Shed" title="Shed">shed</a> or <a href="/wiki/Workshop" title="Workshop">workshop</a>. The distinction of a tavern from an <a href="/wiki/Inn" title="Inn">inn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">bar</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">pub</a> varies by location, in some places being identical and in others being distinguished by traditions or by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal" title="Legal">legal</a> <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">license</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> England, a tavern was distinguished from a public ale house by dint of being run as a private enterprise, where drinkers were "guests" rather than members of the public.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brewpub">Brewpub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brewpub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewpub" title="Brewpub">Brewpub</a></div>
<p>A brewpub is a <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pub</a> or <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> that brews <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> on the premises. Some brewpubs, such as those in Germany, have been brewing traditionally on the premises for hundreds of years. Others, such as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Les_3_Brasseurs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Les 3 Brasseurs (page does not exist)">Les 3 Brasseurs</a> chain in France and Canada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup> and the various chains in North America, are modern restaurants.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beer_hall">Beer hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Beer hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beer_hall" title="Beer hall">Beer hall</a></div>
<p>A beer hall (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Bierpalast, Bierstube</i></span>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pub</a> that specializes in <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>. Bavaria's capital <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> is the city most associated with beer halls; almost every <a href="/wiki/Brewery" title="Brewery">brewery</a> in Munich operates a beer hall. The largest beer hall was the 5,000-seat <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Math%C3%A4ser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mathäser (page does not exist)">Mathäser</a> near the München <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hauptbahnhof" title="Hauptbahnhof">Hauptbahnhof</a> (Munich central train station) which has since been converted into a film theatre.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Izakaya">Izakaya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Izakaya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Izakaya" title="Izakaya">Izakaya</a></div>
<p>An <i>izakaya <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">居酒屋</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span></i> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">drinking establishment</a> which also serves food to accompany the drinks. The food is usually more substantial than that offered in other types of drinking establishments in Japan such as bars or <a href="/wiki/Host_and_hostess_clubs#Snack_bars" title="Host and hostess clubs">snack bars</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beer_garden">Beer garden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Beer garden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beer_garden" title="Beer garden">Beer garden</a></div>
<p>A beer garden' (a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">loan translation</a> from the German <i>Biergarten</i>) is an outdoor area in which beer, other drinks, and local food are served (see <a href="/wiki/German_cuisine" title="German cuisine">German cuisine</a>). Beer gardens originated in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Germany" title="Southern Germany">Southern Germany</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>) and are most common there. They are usually attached to a <a href="/wiki/Beer_hall" title="Beer hall">beer hall</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">pub</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The term "beer garden" (<i>Biergarten</i>) has become a generic term for open-air establishments where beer is served. Many countries have such establishments. The characteristics of a traditional beer garden include trees, wooden benches, a gravel bed, and freshly prepared meals. Some modern beer gardens use plastic chairs, fast food, and other variations of the traditional beer garden. The largest traditional beer garden in the world is the <a href="/wiki/Hirschgarten" title="Hirschgarten">Hirschgarten</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, which seats 8,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speakeasy">Speakeasy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drinking_establishment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Speakeasy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speakeasy" title="Speakeasy">Speakeasy</a></div>
<p>A speakeasy, also called a <i>blind pig</i> or <i>blind tiger</i>, is an establishment that illegally sells <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>. Such establishments came into prominence in the United States during the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition era</a> (1920–1933, longer in some states). During that time, the sale, manufacture, and transportation (<a href="/wiki/Rum-running" title="Rum-running">bootlegging</a>) of alcoholic beverages was illegal throughout the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Speakeasies largely disappeared after Prohibition was ended in 1933, and the term is now used to describe some <a href="/wiki/Retro_style" title="Retro style">retro style</a> bars. Different names for speakeasies were created. The terms "blind pig" and "blind tiger" originated in the United States in the 19th century. These terms were applied to lower-class establishments that sold alcoholic beverages illegally, and they are still in use today. The operator of an establishment (such as a saloon or bar) would charge customers to see an attraction (such as an animal) and then serve a "complimentary" alcoholic beverage, thus circumventing the law. "Blind tiger" also referred to illegal drinking establishment in which the seller's identity was concealed. A drawer would open up in a wall, the patron drops in change, and then a drink is placed in the drawer.</p>
<p>Speakeasies were numerous and popular during the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> years. Some of them were operated by people who were part of <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a>. Even though police and agents of the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Prohibition" title="Bureau of Prohibition">Bureau of Prohibition</a> would often raid them and arrest their owners and patrons, they were so <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profitable</a> that they continued to flourish. The poor quality <a href="/wiki/Rum-running" title="Rum-running">bootleg</a> liquor sold in speakeasies was responsible for a shift away from 19th century 'classic' cocktails, that celebrated the raw taste of the liquor (such as the Gin Cocktail, made with Genever (sweet) gin), to new cocktails aimed at masking the taste of rough <a href="/wiki/Moonshine" title="Moonshine">moonshine</a>.</p>
