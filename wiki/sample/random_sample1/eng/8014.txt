<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Spiracle</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Spiracles</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/aɪər/ 'ire' in 'fire'">aɪər</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) are openings on the surface of some <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, which usually lead to <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory systems</a>.</p>

<p>The spiracle is a small hole behind each eye that opens to the mouth in some <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>. In the primitive <a href="/wiki/Agnatha" title="Agnatha">jawless fish</a> the first gill opening immediately behind the mouth is essentially similar to the other gill opening. With the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Jaw" title="Jaw">jaw</a> in the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnathostomes" title="Gnathostomes">jawed vertebrates</a>, this gill slit was "caught" between the forward gill-rod (now functioning as the jaw) and the next rod, the <a href="/wiki/Hyomandibula" title="Hyomandibula">hyomandibular bone</a>, supporting the jaw hinge and anchoring the jaw to the <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a> proper. The gill opening was closed off from below, the remaining opening was small and hole-like, and is termed a "spiracle". The spiracle is still found in all <a href="/wiki/Chondrichthyes" title="Chondrichthyes">cartilaginous fish</a> except <a href="/wiki/Requiem_shark" title="Requiem shark">requiem sharks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hammerhead_shark" title="Hammerhead shark">hammerhead sharks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chimaera" title="Chimaera">chimaeras</a>, and is found in some primitive <a href="/wiki/Osteichthyes" title="Osteichthyes">bony fishes</a>, the (<a href="/wiki/Coelacanth" title="Coelacanth">coelacanth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sturgeon" title="Sturgeon">sturgeon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acipenseriformes" title="Acipenseriformes">paddlefish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bichir" title="Bichir">bichirs</a>). It is also seen as an <a href="/wiki/Otic_notch" title="Otic notch">otic notch</a> in the skull of the extinct <a href="/wiki/Labyrinthodontia" title="Labyrinthodontia">labyrinthodonts</a>, and is thought to be associated with the <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a> opening in <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">amniotes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthropods">Arthropods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spiracle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arthropods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> and some more derived <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> have spiracles on their <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeletons</a> to allow air to enter the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrate_trachea" title="Invertebrate trachea">trachea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system_of_insects" title="Respiratory system of insects">respiratory system of insects</a>, the tracheal tubes primarily deliver <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> directly into the animals' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissues</a>. The spiracles can be opened and closed in an efficient manner to reduce water loss. This is done by contracting closer muscles surrounding the spiracle. In order to open, the muscle relaxes. The closer muscle is controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> but can also react to localized chemical stimuli. Several aquatic insects have similar or alternative closing methods to prevent water from entering the trachea. The timing and duration of spiracle closures can affect the respiratory rates of the organism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Spiracles may also be surrounded by hairs to minimize bulk air movement around the opening, and thus minimize water loss.</p>
