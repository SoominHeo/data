<p><b>Artamidae</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds found in Australia, the Indo-Pacific region, and Southern Asia. It includes 23 extant species in four genera and two subfamilies: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artaminae" title="Artaminae">Artaminae</a> (with only one genus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artamus" title="Artamus">woodswallows</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Cracticinae" title="Cracticinae">Cracticinae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Currawong" title="Currawong">currawongs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butcherbird" title="Butcherbird">butcherbirds</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_magpie" title="Australian magpie">Australian magpie</a>). Artamids used to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>, containing only the woodswallows, but it was expanded to include the family Cracticidae in 1994. Some authors, however, still treat the two as separate families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christidis_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christidis-1">[1]</a></sup> Some species, known for their beautiful song, are in this family. Their feeding habits vary from the harmless nector sucking of the woodswallows to the fearless predation on small birds from the pied currawong.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Artamids are part of the <a href="/wiki/Malaconotoidea" title="Malaconotoidea">Malaconotoidea</a> <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">superfamily</a>, consisting of a vast diversity of omnivores and carnivorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songbirds" title="Songbirds">songbirds</a> wide spread through Australasia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Artamids has been divided over time into two subfamilies. With little studies and dispute on the inclusion of Cracticidae to the Artamidae family, it appears they have been placed in this respective joint position due to lack of evidence or knowledge. Jerome Fuchs and colleagues extensively analysed both the mitochondrial and nuclear DNA of the Artamid family. The results suggested that the group may have existed in Australasia for 33.7 to 45 million years, dating back to the late <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fuchs_2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs_2012-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Artamid species occur throughout Australasia with most species occurring in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4">[4]</a></sup> The social interactions of Artamids vary from the solitary <a href="/wiki/Black_butcherbird" title="Black butcherbird">black butcherbird</a>, who lives alone or in a single pair, to the <a href="/wiki/White-breasted_woodswallow" title="White-breasted woodswallow">white-breasted woodswallow</a> who lives in flocks or loose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonies" title="Colonies">colonies</a>. While some species are sedentary, staying close to suburbia and ample food sources, others are migratory or even nomadic like the <a href="/wiki/Masked_woodswallow" title="Masked woodswallow">Masked woodswallow</a>, moving around in response to changes in climate such as rainfall or temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burnie12_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burnie12-5">[5]</a></sup> Their range of habitats varies between species but most will adapt to rain forests, woodlands, coastal scrubs (swallows), water courses, playing fields, pastoral lands and paperbark mangroves (butcher birds). One of their greatest abilities is to adapt to urban landscapes where they contend with fragmented and degraded remnants of native vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis13_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis13-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3157" data-file-width="3444" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG/220px-Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG/330px-Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG/440px-Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aus_Magpie_morphology.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Magpie" title="Australian Magpie">Australian Magpie</a> showing its plumage.</div>
</div>

<p>Artamids are a diverse family possessing a notable variation in size and shape. Ranging in size from the short stocky <a href="/wiki/Fiji_woodswallow" title="Fiji woodswallow">Fiji woodswallow</a> <i>Artamus mentalis</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Ashy_woodswallow" title="Ashy woodswallow">Ashy woodswallow</a> <i>Artamus fuscus</i>, both of which are around 19 cm in length and weigh about 40 g, to the larger built <a href="/wiki/Grey_currawong" title="Grey currawong">grey currawong</a> <i>Strepera versicolour</i>, which measures up to 50 cm and weighs up to 440 g. The beaks of artamids are strong and robust, sometimes known as a generalist beak. Alike <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcons</a>, some of the Subfamily <a href="/wiki/Cracticinae" title="Cracticinae">Cracticinae</a> possess a sharp projection along the upper mandible, with a corresponding notch on the lower mandible. This hook like tooth is used to catch and fatally sever insects, lizards and small mammals apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A distinguishing trait of artamids (all passerines) is that it possesses an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anisodactyl" title="Anisodactyl">anisodactyl</a> foot arrangement. This means that three toes are directed forward and one toe directed backward. This enables them to perch on vertical objects like tree branches and power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the possession of a <a href="/wiki/Syrinx_(bird_anatomy)" title="Syrinx (bird anatomy)">syrinx</a>, Artamids are able to create subtle songs, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Pied_butcherbird" title="Pied butcherbird">pied butcherbird</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pied_currawong" title="Pied currawong">pied currawong</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_magpie" title="Australian magpie">Australian magpie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoB_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoB-9">[9]</a></sup> Another unique trait, among other perching birds, is that some woodswallows possess special feathers called powder down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The tips of the barbules on powder down feathers disintegrate, forming fine particles of <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a>, which appear as a powder, or "feather dust", among the feathers and spread when preening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the Artamids is relatively dull with most birds using a combination of greys, earthy browns, blacks and patches of whites. There is seldom-<a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in plumage, when it occurs the males are brighter and the females appear dull or resemble juveniles. In many species juveniles have a distinctly duller plumage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gluckman14_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gluckman14-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Notarium" title="Notarium">notarium</a>, being fused vertebra of the shoulder in birds that helps brace the chest against the forces generated by the wings, is a distinctive osteological trait that has evolved repeatedly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passerines" title="Passerines">passerines</a> including the Artamadae family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James09_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-James09-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg"><img alt="Black faced woodswallow.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg/220px-Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg/330px-Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg/440px-Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_faced_woodswallow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>

<p>Members of the Artamidae, especially the woodswallows, have been known to cluster together during the night and day. Accounts have appeared in literature from the earliest days of ornithological documentation in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood70_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood70-14">[14]</a></sup> The habit of clustering is believed to serve two purposes being the retention of body heat during the cooler weather and a social form of camouflage. Another unusually behaviour by an Artamid is the swooping of humans by magpies. While there is not much information on this behaviour, previous studies have suggested that Magpie attacks on humans may be strongly influenced by hormone levels. However, recent investigations indicate that the stress hormone corticosterone may cause magpie aggression and swooping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warne_2010_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warne_2010-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All are omnivorous to some degree: the butcherbirds mostly eat meat; Australian magpies usually forage through short grass looking for worms and other small creatures; currawongs are true omnivores, taking fruit, grain, meat, insects, eggs and nestlings and woodswallows feed on insects and nectar. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Most are opportunistic feeders, such as the woodswallows, taking advantage of the flowering plants such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silky_oak" title="Silky oak">silky oak</a> <i>Grevillea robusta</i>, box mistletoe <i>Amyema miquelii</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the long flowering stalks of <i>Xanthorrhoea spp</i> or insects such as cockroaches or spiders eaten by the <a href="/wiki/Black_butcherbird" title="Black butcherbird">black butcherbird</a>. Bigger species like the grey currawong preys on many vertebrates, including frogs, lizards such as skinks and juveniles of smaller birds such as the native <a href="/wiki/Eastern_spinebill" title="Eastern spinebill">eastern spinebill</a> <i>Acanthorhynchus tenuirostris</i> or the introduced <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a> <i>Passer domesticus</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_1984_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_1984-18">[18]</a></sup> The evolution of vertical feeding zones (feeding strategies subdividing into vertical space, e.g. upper, mid and ground canopy) is noted in the artamid family. While species will sometimes overlap vertical terrains most woodswallows will use the upper canopy, feeding on insects and pollen, while magpies and currawongs tend to use the ground or swoop down from the mid story pouncing on their food. Being accomplished in extractive foraging is another trait of the artamid. While being opportunistic feeders they are very methodical forages, often following a set routine. ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan2004_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan2004-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_loss">Habitat loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Habitat loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill,_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2951" data-file-width="2961" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill%2C_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG/220px-Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill%2C_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill%2C_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG/330px-Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill%2C_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill%2C_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG/440px-Australian_magpie_subject_to_roadkill%2C_a_threat_to_ArtamidaeJPG.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>With developments occurring more regularly the most critical threat to the artamids is <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a>. This loss of habitat reduces vegetation corridors (connective parcels of vegetation) that are used for feeding, breeding and safely travelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> After fragmentation, habitats are often too small or are limited by the construction of roads. These barriers that cause populations stresses and species can become vulnerable to localised extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Habitat loss and fragmentation can force species into urbanised habitats that impose their own dangers such as cars. Roadkill is considered to significantly contribute to the population decline of many species, especially opportunistic feeders like the Artimade. Often unconsciously swooping down on an insects with out seeing an on coming car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO_2008_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO_2008-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_species">Introduced species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artamidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Introduced species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Australia introduced species have caused the greatest number of extinctions. Exotic feral animals such as cats can have a negative effect on the artamids. Ground foraging species such as the magpie can often fall prey to an unsuspecting cat in urban environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
