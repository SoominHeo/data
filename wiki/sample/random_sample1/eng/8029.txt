<p>The <b>comma</b> ( , ) is a <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a> mark that appears in several variants in various languages. It has the same shape as an <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">apostrophe</a> or single closing <a href="/wiki/Quotation_mark" title="Quotation mark">quotation mark</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a>, but it differs from them in being placed on the baseline of the text. Some typefaces render it as a small line, slightly curved or straight but inclined from the vertical, or with the appearance of a small, filled-in number nine.</p>
<p>The comma is used in many contexts and languages, mainly for separating parts of a <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentence</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clauses</a>, and items in lists, particularly when there are three or more items listed. The word <i>comma</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CF%8C%CE%BC%CE%BC%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:κόμμα">κόμμα</a></span> (<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>kómma</i></span>), which originally meant a cut-off piece; specifically, in grammar, a short clause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A comma-shaped mark is used as a <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">diacritic</a> in several writing systems, and is considered distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Cedilla" title="Cedilla">cedilla</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Rough_breathing" title="Rough breathing">rough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smooth_breathing" title="Smooth breathing">smooth breathings</a> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἁ, ἀ</span>) appear above the letter in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, and the <a href="#Diacritical_usage">comma diacritic</a> appears below the letter in <a href="/wiki/Latvian_orthography" title="Latvian orthography">Latvian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_orthography" title="Romanian orthography">Romanian</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livonian_orthography" title="Livonian orthography">Livonian</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 3rd century BC, <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes_of_Byzantium" title="Aristophanes of Byzantium">Aristophanes of Byzantium</a> invented a system of single <a href="/wiki/Interpunct" title="Interpunct">dots</a> (<i>distinctiones</i>) that separated verses (colometry) and indicated the amount of breath needed to complete each fragment of text when <a href="/wiki/Reading_(process)" title="Reading (process)">reading</a> aloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The different lengths were signified by a dot at the bottom, middle, or top of the line. For a short passage (a <i>komma</i>), a <i>media distinctio</i> dot was placed mid-level ( <b>·</b> ). This is the origin of the concept of a comma, although the name came to be used for the mark itself instead of the clause it separated.</p>
<p>The mark used today is descended from a diagonal <a href="/wiki/Slash_(punctuation)" title="Slash (punctuation)">slash</a>, or <i>virgula suspensiva</i> ( <b>/</b> ), used from the 13th to 17th centuries to represent a pause. The modern comma was first used by <a href="/wiki/Aldus_Manutius" title="Aldus Manutius">Aldus Manutius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_in_English">Uses in English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses in English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, the comma shows that the words immediately before the comma are less closely or exclusively linked <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammatically</a> to those immediately after the comma than they might be otherwise. The comma may perform a number of functions in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> writing. It is used in generally similar ways in other languages, particularly European ones, although the rules on comma usage – and their rigidity – vary from language to language.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_lists">In lists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In lists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas are placed between items in lists, as in <i>They own a cat, a dog, two rabbits, and seven mice.</i> Some English style guides recommend that a comma be used before the final <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> (<i>and</i>, <i>or</i>, <i>nor</i>) in a list of more than two elements. A comma used in such a position is variously called a <a href="/wiki/Serial_comma" title="Serial comma">serial comma</a>, an Oxford comma, or a Harvard comma (after the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">Oxford University Press</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Press" title="Harvard University Press">Harvard University Press</a>, both prominent advocates of this style). Such use of a comma sometimes prevents ambiguity:</p>
<ul>
<li>The sentence <i>I spoke to the boys, Sam and Tom</i> could mean either <i>I spoke to the boys and Sam and Tom</i> (I spoke to more than three people) or <i>I spoke to the boys, who are Sam and Tom</i> (I spoke to two people);</li>
<li><i>I spoke to the boys, Sam, and Tom</i> – must be <i>the boys and Sam and Tom</i> (I spoke to more than three people).</li>
</ul>
<p>The serial comma does not eliminate all confusion. Consider the following sentence:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>I thank my mother, Anne Smith, and Thomas.</i> This could mean either <i>my mother and Anne Smith and Thomas</i> (three people) or <i>my mother, who is Anne Smith; and Thomas</i> (two people). This sentence might be recast as "my mother (Anne Smith) and Thomas" for clarity.</li>
<li><i>I thank my mother, Anne Smith and Thomas.</i> Because the comma after "mother" is conventionally used to prepare the reader for an <a href="/wiki/Apposition" title="Apposition">apposite phrase</a> – that is, a renaming of or further information about a noun – this construction suggests that my mother's name is "Anne Smith and Thomas". Compare "I thank my friend, Smith and Wesson," in which the ambiguity is obvious.</li>
</ul>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_thumb" title="Rule of thumb">rule of thumb</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a> Style Guide</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> suggests that straightforward lists (he ate ham, eggs and chips) do not need a comma before the final "and", but sometimes it can help the reader (he ate cereal, kippers, bacon, eggs, toast and marmalade, and tea). <i>The Chicago Manual of Style</i>, and other academic writing guides, require the "serial comma": all lists must have a comma before the "and" prefacing the last item in a series.</p>
<p>If the individual items of a list are long, complex, affixed with description, or themselves contain commas, <a href="/wiki/Semicolon" title="Semicolon">semicolons</a> may be preferred as separators, and the list may be introduced with a <a href="/wiki/Colon_(punctuation)" title="Colon (punctuation)">colon</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation_of_clauses">Separation of clauses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Separation of clauses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas are often used to separate <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clauses</a>. In English, a comma is used to separate a <a href="/wiki/Dependent_clause" title="Dependent clause">dependent clause</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Independent_clause" title="Independent clause">independent clause</a> if the dependent clause comes first: <i>After I fed the cat, I brushed my clothes.</i> (Compare this with <i>I brushed my clothes after I fed the cat.</i>) A <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">relative clause</a> takes commas if it is non-<a href="/wiki/Restrictiveness" title="Restrictiveness">restrictive</a>, as in <i>I cut down all the trees, which were over six feet tall.</i> (Without the comma, this would mean that only those trees over six feet tall were cut down.)</p>
<p>Some style guides prescribe that two <a href="/wiki/Independent_clause" title="Independent clause">independent clauses</a> joined by a coordinating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> (<i>for</i>, <i>and</i>, <i>nor</i>, <i>but</i>, <i>or</i>, <i>yet</i>, <i>so</i>) must be separated by a comma placed before the conjunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler3rev1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler3rev1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-getitwriteonline1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-getitwriteonline1-8">[8]</a></sup> In the following sentences, where the second clause is independent (because it can stand alone as a sentence), the comma is considered by those guides to be necessary:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Mary walked to the party, but she was unable to walk home.</i></li>
<li><i>Designer clothes are silly, and I can't afford them anyway.</i></li>
<li><i>Don't push that button, or twelve tons of high explosives will go off right under our feet!</i></li>
</ul>
<p>In the following sentences, where the second half of the sentence is not an independent clause (because it does not contain an explicit <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a>), those guides prescribe that the comma be omitted:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Mary walked to the party but was unable to walk home.</i></li>
<li><i>I think designer clothes are silly and can't afford them anyway.</i></li>
</ul>
<p>However, such guides permit the comma to be omitted if the second independent clause is very short, typically when the second independent clause is an <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler3rev1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler3rev1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-getitwriteonline1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-getitwriteonline1-8">[8]</a></sup> as in:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Sit down and shut up.</i></li>
</ul>
<p>The above guidance is not universally accepted or applied. Long coordinating clauses are nonetheless usually separated by commas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>She had very little to live on, but she would never have dreamed of taking what was not hers.</i></li>
</ul>
<p>In some languages, such as <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, stricter rules apply on comma usage between clauses, with dependent clauses always being set off with commas, and commas being generally proscribed before certain coordinating conjunctions.</p>
<p>The joining of two independent sentences with a comma and no conjunction (as in "It is nearly half past five, we cannot reach town before dark.") is known as a <a href="/wiki/Comma_splice" title="Comma splice">comma splice</a> and is sometimes considered an error in English;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in most cases a semicolon should be used instead. A comma splice should not be confused, though, with <a href="/wiki/Asyndeton" title="Asyndeton">asyndeton</a>, a literary device used for a specific effect in which coordinating conjunctions are purposely omitted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certain_adverbs">Certain adverbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Certain adverbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas are always used to set off certain <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverbs</a> at the beginning of a sentence, including <i>however</i>, <i>in fact</i>, <i>therefore</i>, <i>nevertheless</i>, <i>moreover</i>, <i>furthermore</i>, and <i>still</i>.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Therefore, a comma would be appropriate in this sentence.</i></li>
<li><i>Nevertheless, I will not use one.</i></li>
</ul>
<p>If these adverbs appear in the middle of a sentence, they are followed and preceded by a comma. As in the second of the two below examples, if the two sentences are separated by a semicolon and the second sentence starts with an adverb, then it is preceded by a semicolon and followed by a comma.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>In this sentence, furthermore, commas would also be called for.</i></li>
<li><i>This sentence is similar; however, a semicolon is necessary as well.</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Using commas to offset certain adverbs is optional, including <i>then</i>, <i>so</i>, <i>yet</i>, <i>instead</i>, and <i>too</i> (meaning <i>also</i>).</p>
<ul>
<li><i>So, that's it for this rule.</i> or</li>
<li><i>So that's it for this rule.</i></li>
<li><i>A comma would be appropriate in this sentence, too.</i> or</li>
<li><i>A comma would be appropriate in this sentence too.</i></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parenthetical_phrases">Parenthetical phrases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parenthetical phrases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Parenthetical_phrase"></span> Commas are often used to enclose <a href="/wiki/Parenthesis_(rhetoric)" title="Parenthesis (rhetoric)">parenthetical</a> words and phrases within a sentence (i.e., information that is not essential to the meaning of the sentence). Such phrases are both preceded and followed by a comma, unless that would result in a doubling of punctuation marks, or the parenthetical is at the start or end of the sentence. The following are examples of types of parenthetical phrases:</p>
<ul>
<li>Introductory phrase: Once upon a time, my father ate a muffin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Interjection: My father ate the muffin, gosh darn it!</li>
<li>Aside: My father, if you don't mind me telling you this, ate the muffin.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Apposition" title="Apposition">Appositive</a>: My father, a jaded and bitter man, ate the muffin.</li>
<li>Absolute phrase: My father, his eyes flashing with rage, ate the muffin.</li>
<li>Free modifier: My father, chewing with unbridled fury, ate the muffin.</li>
<li>Resumptive modifier: My father ate the muffin, a muffin which no man had yet chewed.</li>
<li>Summative modifier: My father ate the muffin, a feat which no man had attempted.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Between_adjectives">Between adjectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Between adjectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A comma is used to separate <i>coordinate adjectives</i> (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Adjective#Adjective_order" title="Adjective">adjectives</a> that directly and equally modify the following noun). Adjectives are considered coordinate if the meaning would be the same if their order were reversed or if <i>and</i> were placed between them. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The dull, incessant droning</i> but <i>the cute little cottage.</i></li>
<li><i>The devious lazy red frog</i> suggests there are lazy red frogs (one of which is devious), while <i>the devious, lazy red frog</i> does not carry this connotation.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_quotations">Before quotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Before quotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By some writers, a comma is used to set off quoted material that is the grammatical object of an active verb of speaking or writing, as in <i>Mr. Kershner says, "You should know how to use a comma."</i> Quotations that follow and support an assertion should be set off by a <a href="/wiki/Colon_(punctuation)" title="Colon (punctuation)">colon</a> rather than a comma.</p>
<p>Other writers do not put a comma before quotations unless one would occur anyway. Thus they would write <i>Mr. Kershner says "You should know how to use a comma."</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_dates">In dates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In dates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Month_day.2C_year">Month day, year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Month day, year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When a date is written as a month followed by a day followed by a year, a comma separates the day from the year: December 19, 1941. This style is common in American English. The comma is used to avoid confusing consecutive numbers: December 19 1941. Most style manuals, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/AP_Stylebook" title="AP Stylebook">AP Stylebook</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> also recommend that the year be treated as a parenthetical, requiring a second comma after it: <i>"Feb. 14, 1987, was the target date."</i></p>
<p>If just month and year are given, no commas are used:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> "Her daughter April may return in June 2009 for the reunion."</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Day_month_year">Day month year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Day month year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When the day precedes the month, the month name separates the numeric day and year, so commas are not necessary to separate them: "The <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Alexandria_(1941)" title="Raid on Alexandria (1941)">Raid on Alexandria</a> was carried out on 19 December 1941."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_geographical_names">In geographical names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In geographical names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas are used to separate parts of geographical references, such as city and state (<i>Dallas, Texas</i>) or city and country (<i>Kampala, Uganda</i>). Additionally, most style manuals, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and the <i>AP Stylebook</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> recommend that the second element be treated as a parenthetical, requiring a second comma after: <i>"The plane landed in Kampala, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, that evening."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> recommend writing addresses without any punctuation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_numbers">In numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Decimal_mark" title="Decimal mark">Decimal mark</a></div>
<p>In representing large numbers, from the right side to the left, English texts usually use commas to separate each group of three digits in front of the decimal. This is almost always done for numbers of six or more digits and often for five or four digits but not in front of the number itself. However, in much of Europe, Southern Africa and Latin America, <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">periods</a> or spaces are used instead; the comma is used as a decimal separator, equivalent to the use in English of the <a href="/wiki/Decimal_mark" title="Decimal mark">decimal point</a>. In India, the groups are two digits, except for the rightmost group. However the comma may not be used for this purpose at all in some styles, e.g. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI#SI_writing_style" title="SI">SI writing style</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> a space may be used to separate groups of three digits instead.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_names">In names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas are used when writing names that are presented surname first, generally in instances of alphabetization by surname: <i>Smith, John.</i> They are also used before many titles that follow a name: <i>John Smith, Ph.D.</i></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:27%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9; padding:8px;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"The big final rule for the comma is one that you won't find in any books by grammarians ... don't use commas like a stupid person."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"> <a href="/wiki/Lynne_Truss" title="Lynne Truss">Lynne Truss</a>, <i>Eats, Shoots, and Leaves</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Similarly in lists that are presented with an inversion: <i>...; socks, green: 3 pairs; socks, red: 2 pairs; tie, regimental: 1</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ellipsis">Ellipsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ellipsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas may be used to indicate that a word has been omitted, as in <i>The cat was white; the dog, brown.</i> (Here the comma replaces <i>was</i>.)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocative">Vocative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Vocative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commas are placed before, after, or around a noun or pronoun used independently in speaking to some person, place or thing:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>I hope, John, that you will read this.</i></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Between_the_subject_and_predicate">Between the subject and predicate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Between the subject and predicate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his 1785 essay <i>On Punctuation</i>, Joseph Robertson advocated a comma between the subject and predicate of long sentences for clarity; however, this usage is regarded as an error in modern times.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The good taste of the present age, has not allowed us to neglect the cultivation of the English language.</i></li>
<li><i>Whoever is capable of forgetting a benefit, is an enemy to society.</i></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_American_and_British_usage">Differences between American and British usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Differences between American and British usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The comma and the <a href="/wiki/Quotation_mark" title="Quotation mark">quotation mark</a> pairing can be used in several ways. In America, the comma is commonly included inside a quotation mark:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APA_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-APA-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilbersonChicago14_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-WilbersonChicago14-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>My mother gave me the nickname "Bobby Bobby Bob Bob Boy," which really made me angry.</li>
</ul>
<p>In Great Britain and many other parts of the world, punctuation is usually placed within quotation marks only if it is part of what is being quoted or referred to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APA_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-APA-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilbersonChicago14_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-WilbersonChicago14-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSF_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSF-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>My mother gave me the nickname "Bobby Bobby Bob Bob Boy", which really made me angry.</li>
</ul>
<p>There is also some difference regarding the use of the <a href="/wiki/Serial_comma" title="Serial comma">serial comma</a>, which is an optional comma placed before the coordinating conjunction in a list of three or more items:</p>
<ul>
<li>They served apples, peaches, and bananas. (serial comma used)</li>
<li>We cleaned up cores, pits and skins. (serial comma omitted)</li>
</ul>
<p>The serial comma is also known as the Oxford comma, Harvard comma, or series comma. It is sometimes perceived as overly careful or an Americanism<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but its usage occurs within both American and British English. It is called the Oxford comma because of its long history of use by Oxford University Press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxfordD_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordD-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Opinions among writers and editors differ on whether to use the serial comma. A majority of American style guides mandate use of the serial comma, including <i>The Chicago Manual of Style</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Strunk,_Jr." title="William Strunk, Jr.">Strunk</a> and <a href="/wiki/E._B._White" title="E. B. White">White</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elements_of_Style" title="The Elements of Style">The Elements of Style</a></i>, and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Government_Printing_Office" title="U.S. Government Printing Office">U.S. Government Printing Office</a> Style Manual</i>. The <i>AP Stylebook</i> for journalistic writing advises against it. It is used less often in British English, but some British style guides require it, including the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">Oxford University Press</a> style manual and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Watson_Fowler" title="Henry Watson Fowler">Fowler</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_English_Usage" title="Modern English Usage">Modern English Usage</a></i>. Some writers of British English use it only where necessary to avoid ambiguity.</p>
<p>Barbara Child advises that "it is a good idea to put a comma before the last item in a series", but claims that in America there is a trend toward a decreased use of the comma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This is reinforced by an article by <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Samuelson" title="Robert J. Samuelson">Robert J. Samuelson</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comma_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comma-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lynne_Truss" title="Lynne Truss">Lynne Truss</a> says that this is equally true in the UK, where it has been a slow, steady trend for at least a century:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Nowadays... A passage peppered with commas—which in the past would have indicated painstaking and authoritative editorial attention—smacks simply of no backbone. People who put in all the commas betray themselves as moral weaklings with empty lives and out-of-date reference books. (Truss, 2004, p. 97–98)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In his 1963 book <i>Of Spies and Stratagems</i>, Stanley P. Lovell recalls that, during the Second World War, the British carried the comma over into abbreviations. Specifically, "Special Operations, Executive" was written "S.O.,E.". Nowadays, even the <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">full stops</a> are frequently discarded.</p>
<p>According to <i>New Hart's Rules</i>, "house style will dictate" whether to use the serial comma, and "The general rule is that one style or the other should be used consistently." No association with region or dialect is suggested, other than that its use has been strongly advocated by Oxford University Press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_languages">In other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: In other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Punctuation has been added to many languages which originally developed without it, including a number of different comma forms. European languages like German, French, Italian, Spanish, and Portuguese use the same comma as English with similar spacing, though usage may be somewhat different. For instance, in Standard German, subordinate clauses are always preceded by commas.</p>
<p><span id="Greek_comma"></span><span id="komma"></span> <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Modern Greek</a> uses the same <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> comma for its <i>kómma</i> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">κόμμα</span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicky_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicky-28">[28]</a></sup> and it is officially <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Greek" title="Romanization of Greek">romanized</a> as a Latin comma,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elot_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-elot-29">[29]</a></sup> but it has additional roles owing to its conflation with the former <a href="/wiki/Hypodiastole" title="Hypodiastole">hypodiastole</a>, a curved <a href="/wiki/Interpunct" title="Interpunct">interpunct</a> used to disambiguate certain homonyms. The comma therefore functions as a <a href="/wiki/Silent_letter" title="Silent letter">silent letter</a> in a handful of Greek words, principally distinguishing <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%8C,%CF%84%CE%B9" title="wikt:ό,τι">ό,τι</a></span> (<i>ó,ti</i>, "whatever") from <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%8C%CF%84%CE%B9" title="wikt:ότι">ότι</a></span> (<i>óti</i>, "that").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicky_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-nicky-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <b>enumeration</b> or <b>ideographic comma</b>—<span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+3001</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">、</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">IDEOGRAPHIC COMMA</span>—is used in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_punctuation" title="Chinese punctuation">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_punctuation" title="Japanese punctuation">Japanese punctuation</a>. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, this comma (<small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_characters" title="Traditional characters">t</a></small> <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A0%93%E8%99%9F" title="wikt:頓號">頓號</a></span>, <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_characters" title="Simplified characters">s</a></small> <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A1%BF%E5%8F%B7" title="wikt:顿号">顿号</a></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">p</a></small> <i>dùnhào</i>) is usually used only to separate items in lists, while in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> it is the more common form of comma (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AA%AD%E7%82%B9" title="wikt:読点">読点</a></span>, <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C5%8Dmaji" title="Rōmaji">r</a></small> <i>tōten</i>, <small><abbr title="Literally">lit.</abbr></small> "reading mark"). In documents that mix <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_script" title="Japanese script">Japanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin scripts</a>, the <b>full-width comma</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+FF0C</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">，</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">FULLWIDTH COMMA</span>) is used; this is the standard form of comma (<small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_characters" title="Traditional characters">t</a></small> <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%80%97%E8%99%9F" title="wikt:逗號">逗號</a></span>, <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_characters" title="Simplified characters">s</a></small> <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%80%97%E5%8F%B7" title="wikt:逗号">逗号</a></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">p</a></small> <i>dòuhào</i>) in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Since East Asian typography permits commas to join clauses dealing with certain topics or lines of thought, commas may separate subjects and predicates and constructions that would be considered a "<a href="/wiki/Comma_splice" title="Comma splice">comma splice</a>" in English are acceptable and commonly encountered.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Korean_punctuation" title="Korean punctuation">Korean punctuation</a> uses both commas and <a href="/wiki/Interpunct" title="Interpunct">interpuncts</a> for lists.</p>
<p>The comma in the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">Arabic script</a> (used by <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, etc.) is inverted, upside-down: '<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">،</span>' (<span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+060C</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">،</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">ARABIC COMMA</span>), in order to distinguish it from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_diacritic" title="Arabic diacritic">Arabic diacritic</a> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">ḍammah</span></i> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">ُ</span> ), representing the vowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span>, which is similarly comma-shaped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbiFar.C3.A8s2001_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-AbiFar.C3.A8s2001-30">[30]</a></sup> In Arabic texts, Western-styled comma (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">٫</span>) is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decimal_point" title="Decimal point">decimal point</a>.</p>
<p>Reversed comma (<span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+2E41</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">⹁</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">REVERSED COMMA</span>) is used in Sindhi when written in Arabic script. It is different from the standard Arabic comma.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_script" title="Hebrew script">Hebrew script</a> is also written from right to left. However, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_punctuation" title="Hebrew punctuation">Hebrew punctuation</a> includes only a regular comma (<span lang="he" xml:lang="he">,</span>).</p>
<p>Dravidian languages such as Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, and Malayalam also use the punctuation mark in similar usage to that of European languages with similar spacing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ta.wiki_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ta.wiki-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computing">Computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the common <a href="/wiki/Character_(computing)" title="Character (computing)">character</a> encoding systems <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> and <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a>, character 44 (<a href="/wiki/Hexadecimal" title="Hexadecimal">0x2C</a>) corresponds to the comma symbol. The <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> <a href="/wiki/Numeric_character_reference" title="Numeric character reference">numeric character reference</a> is <code>&amp;#44;</code>.</p>
<p>In many computer languages commas are used as a field delimiter to separate arguments to a <a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">function</a>, to separate elements in a <a href="/wiki/Array_data_type" title="Array data type">list</a>, and to perform data designation on multiple variables at once.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C programming language</a> the comma symbol is an <a href="/wiki/Comma_operator" title="Comma operator">operator</a> which evaluates its first <a href="/wiki/Parameter_(computer_programming)" title="Parameter (computer programming)">argument</a> (which may have side-effects) and then returns the value of its evaluated second argument. This is useful in <i>for</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statement_(programming)" title="Statement (programming)">statements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macro_(computer_science)" title="Macro (computer science)">macros</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Smalltalk" title="Smalltalk">Smalltalk</a>, the comma operator is used to <a href="/wiki/Concatenation" title="Concatenation">concatenate</a> collections, including strings.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Prolog" title="Prolog">Prolog</a>, the comma is used to denote <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_Conjunction" title="Logical Conjunction">Logical Conjunction</a> ("and").</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Comma-separated_values" title="Comma-separated values">comma-separated values</a> (CSV) format is very commonly used in exchanging text data between database and spreadsheet formats.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diacritical_usage">Diacritical usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Diacritical usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/S-comma" title="S-comma">S-comma</a> and <a href="/wiki/T-comma" title="T-comma">T-comma</a></div>
<p>The comma is used as a <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">diacritic</a> mark in <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> under the <i>s</i> (<i>Ș</i>, <i>ș</i>), and under the <i>t</i> (<i>Ț</i>, <i>ț</i>). A <a href="/wiki/Cedilla" title="Cedilla">cedilla</a> is occasionally used instead of it (notably in the <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> glyph names), but this is technically incorrect. The symbol <i>d̦</i> (<a href="/wiki/D-comma" title="D-comma"><i>d</i> with comma below</a>) was used as part of the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_alphabet" title="Romanian alphabet">Romanian transitional alphabet</a> (19th century) to indicate the sounds denoted by the Latin letter <i><a href="/wiki/Z" title="Z">z</a></i> or letters <i>dz</i>, where derived from a <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dze" title="Dze">ѕ</a></i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dz/</span>). The comma and the cedilla are both derivative of a small cursive <i>z</i> (ʒ) placed below the letter. From this standpoint alone, <i>ș</i>, <i>ț</i>, and <i>d̦</i> could potentially be regarded as stand-ins for <i>sz</i>, <i>tz</i>, and <i>dz</i> respectively.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>, the comma is used on the letters <i>ģ</i>, <i>ķ</i>, <i>ļ</i>, <i>ņ</i>, and historically also <i>ŗ</i>, to indicate <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">palatalization</a>. Because the lowercase letter <i>g</i> has a <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">descender</a>, the comma is rotated 180° and placed over the letter. Although their <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">Adobe</a> <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyph</a> names are commas, their names in the <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> Standard are <i>g</i>, <i>k</i>, <i>l</i>, <i>n</i>, and <i>r</i> with a <a href="/wiki/Cedilla" title="Cedilla">cedilla</a>. They were introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> standard before 1992, and their name cannot be altered.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>, the diacritic in the characters <i>ď</i>, <i>ť</i>, and <i>ľ</i> resembles a superscript comma, but it is used instead of a <a href="/wiki/Caron" title="Caron">caron</a> because the letter has an <a href="/wiki/Ascender_(typography)" title="Ascender (typography)">ascender</a>. Other ascender letters with carons, such as letters <i>ȟ</i> (used in <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Kalo_language" title="Finnish Kalo language">Finnish Romani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lakota_language" title="Lakota language">Lakota</a>) and <i>ǩ</i> (used in <a href="/wiki/Skolt_Sami_language" title="Skolt Sami language">Skolt Sami</a>), did not modify their carons to superscript commas.</p>
