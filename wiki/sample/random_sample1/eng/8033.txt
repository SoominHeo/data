<p>Shruti or Shruthi (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a>: <span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">श्रुति</span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAST" title="IAST">IAST</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śruti</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Sanskrit" title="Help:IPA for Sanskrit">IPA for Sanskrit</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Sanskrit" title="Help:IPA for Sanskrit">[ʃrut̪i]</a></span>) in Sanskrit means "that which is heard" and refers to the body of most authoritative, ancient <a href="/wiki/Religious_text" title="Religious text">religious texts</a> comprising the central canon of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jameslochtefeld_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jameslochtefeld-1">[1]</a></sup> It includes the four <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> including its four types of embedded texts - the <a href="/wiki/Samhita" title="Samhita">Samhitas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brahmana" title="Brahmana">Brahmanas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aranyaka" title="Aranyaka">Aranyakas</a> and the early <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydof_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wendydof-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Śruti</i>s have been variously described as a revelation through <i>anubhava</i> (direct experience),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or of primordial origins realized by ancient <a href="/wiki/Rishi" title="Rishi">Rishis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jameslochtefeld_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-jameslochtefeld-1">[1]</a></sup> In Hindu tradition, they have been referred to as <i>apauruṣeya</i> (authorless).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <i>Śruti</i> texts themselves assert that they were skillfully created by <a href="/wiki/Rishi" title="Rishi">Rishis</a> (sages), after inspired creativity, just as a carpenter builds a chariot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scharfe_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-scharfe-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>All six <a href="/wiki/Hindu_philosophy" title="Hindu philosophy">orthodox schools of Hinduism</a> accept the authority of <i>śruti</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elisa_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-elisa-8">[note 1]</a></sup> but many scholars in these schools denied that the <i>śruti</i>s are divine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royperrett_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-royperrett-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/%C4%80stika_and_n%C4%81stika" title="Āstika and nāstika">Nāstika</a></i> (heterodox) philosophies such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Charvaka" title="Charvaka">Cārvāka</a></i>s did not accept the authority of the <i>śruti</i>s and considered them to be flawed human works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richardhayes_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-richardhayes-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ma_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ma-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shruti (<i>Śruti</i>) differs from other sources of Hindu philosophy, particularly <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sm%E1%B9%9Bti" title="Smṛti">smṛti</a></i> “which is remembered” or textual material. These works span much of the history of Hinduism, beginning with the earliest known texts and ending in the early historical period with the later Upanishads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Of the <i>śruti</i>s, the Upanishads alone are widely known, and the central ideas of the Upanishadic <i>śruti</i>s are at the spiritual core of Hindus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydoniger_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-wendydoniger-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patrickolivelle_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-patrickolivelle-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%9Aruti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sanskrit word "<span lang="sa" xml:lang="sa">श्रुति</span>" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAST" title="IAST">IAST</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Śruti</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Sanskrit" title="Help:IPA for Sanskrit">IPA for Sanskrit</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Sanskrit" title="Help:IPA for Sanskrit">[ɕrut̪i]</a></span>) has multiple meanings depending on context. It means "hearing, listening", a call to "listen to a speech", any form of communication that is aggregate of sounds (news, report, rumor, noise, hearsay).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monierwilliams_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-monierwilliams-16">[15]</a></sup> The word is also found in ancient geometry texts of India, where it means "the diagonal of a tetragon or hypotenuse of a triangle",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monierwilliams_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-monierwilliams-16">[15]</a></sup> and is a synonym of <i>karna</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> The word <i>śruti</i> is also found in ancient Indian music literature, where it means "a particular division of the octave, a quarter tone or interval" out of twenty-two enumerated major tones, minor tones, and semitones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monierwilliams_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-monierwilliams-16">[15]</a></sup> In music, it refers the <a href="/wiki/Shruti_(music)" title="Shruti (music)">smallest measure of sound</a> a human being can detect, and the set of twenty-two <i>śruti</i> and forty four <i>half Shruti</i>, stretching from about 250 Hz to 500 Hz, is called the <i>Shruti octave</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In scholarly works on Hinduism, <i>śruti</i> refers to ancient Vedic texts from India. Monier-Williams<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monierwilliams_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-monierwilliams-16">[15]</a></sup> traces the contextual history of this meaning of <i>śruti</i> as, "which has been heard or communicated from the beginning, sacred knowledge that was only heard and verbally transmitted from generation to generation, the Veda, from earliest <a href="/wiki/Rishi" title="Rishi">Rishis</a> (sages) in Vedic tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jameslochtefeld_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-jameslochtefeld-1">[1]</a></sup> In scholarly literature, <i>Śruti</i> is also spelled as Shruti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_.C5.9Bruti_and_sm.E1.B9.9Bti">Distinction between śruti and smṛti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%9Aruti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distinction between śruti and smṛti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smriti literally "that which is remembered," refers to a body of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> texts usually attributed to an author, traditionally written down but constantly revised, in contrast to Śrutis (the Vedic literature) considered authorless, that were transmitted verbally across the generations and fixed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydof_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-wendydof-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Smriti</i> is a derivative secondary work and is considered less authoritative than <i>Sruti</i> in Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> <i>Sruti</i> are fixed and its originals preserved better, while each Smriti text exists in many versions, with many different readings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydof_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-wendydof-2">[2]</a></sup> Smritis were considered fluid and freely rewritten by anyone in ancient and medieval Hindu tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydof_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-wendydof-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both śrutis and smṛtis represent categories of texts of different traditions of <a href="/wiki/Hindu_philosophy" title="Hindu philosophy">Hindu philosophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> According to Gokul Narang, the Sruti are asserted to be of divine origin in the mythologies of the <a href="/wiki/Puranas" title="Puranas">Puranas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> In contrast, states Roy Perrett, ancient and medieval Hindu philosophers have denied that śruti are divine, authored by God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royperrett_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-royperrett-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81" title="Mīmāṃsā">Mīmāṃsā</a> tradition, famous in Hindu tradition for its Sruti exegetical contributions, radically critiqued the notion and any relevance for concepts such as "author", the "sacred text" or divine origins of Sruti; the Mimamsa school claimed that the relevant question is the meaning of the Sruti, values appropriate for human beings in it, and the commitment to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nāstika philosophical schools such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C4%81rv%C4%81ka" title="Cārvāka">Cārvākas</a> of the first millennium BCE did not accept the authority of the śrutis and considered them to be human works suffering from incoherent rhapsodies, inconsistencies and tautologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richardhayes_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-richardhayes-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ma_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ma-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smṛtis are considered to be human thoughts in response to the śrutis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydof_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-wendydof-2">[2]</a></sup> Traditionally, all smṛtis are regarded to ultimately be rooted in or inspired by śrutis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydof_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-wendydof-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Texts">Texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%9Aruti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The śruti literature include the four Vedas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">Rigveda</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yajurveda" title="Yajurveda">Yajurveda</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samaveda" title="Samaveda">Samaveda</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atharvaveda" title="Atharvaveda">Atharvaveda</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Each of these Vedas include the following texts, and these belong to the śruti canon:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samhita" title="Samhita">Samhitas</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brahmana" title="Brahmana">Brahmanas</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aranyaka" title="Aranyaka">Aranyakas</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The literature of the <a href="/wiki/Shakha" title="Shakha">shakhas</a>, or schools, further amplified the material associated with each of the four core traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the above śrutis, the Upanishads are most widely known, and the central ideas of them are the spiritual foundation of Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendydoniger_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-wendydoniger-14">[13]</a></sup> Patrick Olivelle writes,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Even though theoretically the whole of Vedic corpus is accepted as revealed truth [śruti], in reality it is the Upanishads that have continued to influence the life and thought of the various religious traditions that we have come to call Hindu. Upanishads are the scriptures par excellence of Hinduism.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Patrick Olivelle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patrickolivelle_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-patrickolivelle-15">[14]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Hindu_Law">Role in Hindu Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%9Aruti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role in Hindu Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shrutis have been considered the authority in Hinduism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elisa_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-elisa-8">[note 1]</a></sup> Smṛtis, including the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manusm%E1%B9%9Bti" title="Manusmṛti">Manusmṛti</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/N%C4%81radasm%E1%B9%9Bti" title="Nāradasmṛti">Nāradasmṛti</a></i> and the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Par%C4%81%C5%9Barasm%E1%B9%9Bti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Parāśarasmṛti (page does not exist)">Parāśarasmṛti</a></i>, are considered less authoritative than śrutis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>वेदोऽखिलो धर्ममूलं स्मृतिशीले च तद्विदाम् ।<br/>
आचारश्चैव साधूनामात्मनस्तुष्टिरेव च ॥<br/>
Translation 1: The whole Veda is the (first) source of the sacred law, next the tradition and the virtuous conduct of those who know the (Veda further), also the customs of holy men, and (finally) self-satisfaction (<i>Atmanastushti</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manusmriti2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-manusmriti2-32">[31]</a></sup><br/>
Translation 2: The root of the religion is the entire Veda, and (then) the tradition and customs of those who know (the Veda), and the conduct of virtuous people, and what is satisfactory to oneself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manusmriti3_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-manusmriti3-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>वेदः स्मृतिः सदाचारः स्वस्य च प्रियमात्मनः ।<br/>
एतच्चतुर्विधं प्राहुः साक्षाद् धर्मस्य लक्षणम् ॥<br/>
Translation 1: The Veda, the sacred tradition, the customs of virtuous men, and one's own pleasure, they declare to be the fourfold means of defining the sacred law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manusmriti2_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-manusmriti2-32">[31]</a></sup><br/>
Translation 2: The Veda, tradition, the conduct of good people, and what is pleasing to oneself – they say that is four fold mark of religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manusmriti3_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-manusmriti3-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>Only three of the four types of texts in the Vedas have behavioral precepts:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>For the Hindu all belief takes its source and its justification in the Vedas [Śruti]. Consequently every rule of <i>dharma</i> must find its foundation in the Veda. Strictly speaking, the Samhitas do not even include a single precept which could be used directly as a rule of conduct. One can find there only references to usage which falls within the scope of <i>dharma</i>. By contrast, the Brahmanas, the Aranyakas and the Upanishads contain numerous precepts which propound rules governing behavior.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Robert Lingat<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robertlingat_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-robertlingat-34">[33]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Bilimoria states the role of śruti in Hinduism has been inspired by "the belief in a higher natural cosmic order (<i><a href="/wiki/%E1%B9%9Ata" title="Ṛta">Rta</a></i> succeeded later by the concept <i>Dharma</i>) that regulates the universe and provides the basis for its growth, flourishing and sustenance – be that of the gods, human beings, animals and eco-formations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Levinson states that the role of śruti and smṛti in Hindu law is as a source of guidance, and its tradition cultivates the principle that "the facts and circumstances of any particular case determine what is good or bad".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidlevinson_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-davidlevinson-36">[35]</a></sup> The later Hindu texts include fourfold sources of dharma, states Levinson, which include <i>atmanastushti</i> (satisfaction of one's conscience), <i>sadacara</i> (local norms of virtuous individuals), smṛti and śruti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidlevinson_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-davidlevinson-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission">Transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%9Aruti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The śrutis, the oldest of which trace back to the second millennium BCE, had not been committed to writing in ancient times. These were developed and transmitted verbally, from one generation to the next, for nearly two millenniums. Almost all printed editions available in the modern era are copied manuscripts that are hardly older than 500 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Witzel" title="Michael Witzel">Michael Witzel</a> explains this oral tradition as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Vedic texts were orally composed and transmitted, without the use of script, in an unbroken line of transmission from teacher to student that was formalized early on. This ensured an impeccable textual transmission superior to the classical texts of other cultures; it is, in fact, something like a <i>tape-recording</i>.... Not just the actual words, but even the long-lost musical (tonal) accent (as in old Greek or in Japanese) has been preserved up to the present.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Michael Witzel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Ancient Indians developed techniques for listening, memorization and recitation of śrutis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scharfe28_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-scharfe28-39">[38]</a></sup> Many forms of recitation or <i>pathas</i> were designed to aid accuracy in recitation and the transmission of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veda" title="Veda">Vedas</a></i> and other knowledge texts from one generation to the next. All hymns in each Veda were recited in this way; for example, all 1,028 hymns with 10,600 verses of the Rigveda was preserved in this way; as were all other Vedas including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_Upanishads" title="Principal Upanishads">Principal Upanishads</a>, as well as the Vedangas. Each text was recited in a number of ways, to ensure that the different methods of recitation acted as a cross check on the other. Pierre-Sylvain Filliozat summarizes this as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filliozat-p139_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-filliozat-p139-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Samhita-patha</i>: continuous recitation of Sanskrit words bound by the phonetic rules of euphonic combination;</li>
<li><i>Pada-patha</i>: a recitation marked by a conscious pause after every word, and after any special grammatical codes embedded inside the text; this method suppresses euphonic combination and restores each word in its original intended form;</li>
<li><i>Krama-patha</i>: a step-by-step recitation where euphonically-combined words are paired successively and sequentially and then recited; for example, a hymn "word1 word2 word3 word4...", would be recited as "word1word2 word2word3 word3word4 ...."; this method to verify accuracy is credited to Vedic sages Gargya and Sakalya in the Hindu tradition and mentioned by the ancient Sanskrit grammarian Panini (dated to pre-Buddhism period);</li>
<li><i>Krama-patha</i> modified: the same step-by-step recitation as above, but without euphonic-combinations (or free form of each word); this method to verify accuracy is credited to Vedic sages Babhravya and Galava in the Hindu tradition, and is also mentioned by the ancient Sanskrit grammarian Panini;</li>
<li><i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Jata-pāṭha</span></i>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">dhvaja-pāṭha</span></i> and <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">ghana-pāṭha</span></i> are methods of recitation of a text and its oral transmission that developed after 5th century BCE, that is after the start of Buddhism and Jainism; these methods use more complicated rules of combination and were less used.</li>
</ul>
<p>These extraordinary retention techniques guaranteed an accurate Śruti, fixed across the generations, not just in terms of unaltered word order but also in terms of sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scharfe28_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-scharfe28-39">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> That these methods have been effective, is testified to by the preservation of the most ancient Indian religious text, the <i><a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda"><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Ṛgveda</span></a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">ca.</a> 1500 BCE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filliozat-p139_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-filliozat-p139-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>This part of a Vedic student's education was called <a href="/wiki/Sv%C4%81dhy%C4%81ya" title="Svādhyāya">svādhyāya</a>. The systematic method of learning, memorization and practice, enabled these texts to be transmitted from generation to generation with inordinate fidelity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotation">Quotation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%9Aruti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Quotation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max Müller">Max Müller</a> in an 1865 lecture stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The idea of revelation, and I mean more particularly book revelation, is not a modern idea, nor is it an idea peculiar to Christianity. In no country, I believe, has the theory of revelation been so minutely elaborated as in India. The name for revelation in Sanskrit is shruti, which means hearing; and this title distinguished the Vedic hymns and, at a later time, the Brahmanas also, from all other works, which however sacred and authoritative to the Hindu mind, are admitted to have been composed by human authors.<br/>
But let me state at once that there is nothing in the hymns themselves to warrant such extravagant theories. In many a hymn, the author says plainly that he or his friends made it to please the gods; that he made it, as a carpenter makes a chariot (Rv 1.130.6; 5.2.11), or like a beautiful vesture (Rv 5.29.15); that he fashioned it in his heart and kept it in his mind (Rv 1.171.2).</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Max Muller<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
