<p><b>Shmuel Yosef Agnon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">שמואל יוסף עגנון</span>‎‎) (July 17, 1888 – February 17, 1970)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_literature" title="Nobel Prize in literature">Nobel Prize</a> <a href="/wiki/Laureate" title="Laureate">laureate</a> writer and was one of the central figures of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">modern Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a>. In Hebrew, he is known by the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> <b>Shai Agnon</b> (ש"י עגנון). In English, his works are published under the name <b>S. Y. Agnon</b>.</p>
<p>Agnon was born in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia_and_Lodomeria" title="Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria">Polish Galicia</a>, then part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>, and later immigrated to <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a>, and died in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.</p>
<p>His works deal with the conflict between the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> life and language and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_world" title="Modern world">modern world</a>. They also attempt to recapture the fading traditions of the European <i><a href="/wiki/Shtetl" title="Shtetl">shtetl</a></i> (village). In a wider context, he also contributed to broadening the characteristic conception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrator" title="Narrator">narrator</a>'s role in literature. Agnon shared the Nobel Prize with the poet <a href="/wiki/Nelly_Sachs" title="Nelly Sachs">Nelly Sachs</a> in 1966.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OldBuchachPanorama.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="558" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/OldBuchachPanorama.jpg/250px-OldBuchachPanorama.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/OldBuchachPanorama.jpg/375px-OldBuchachPanorama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/OldBuchachPanorama.jpg/500px-OldBuchachPanorama.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OldBuchachPanorama.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Buczacz, Agnon's hometown</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Shmuel Yosef Halevi Czaczkes (later Agnon) was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buczacz" title="Buczacz">Buczacz</a> (Polish spelling, pronounced <i>Buchach</i>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butschatsch" title="Butschatsch">Butschatsch</a> (German spelling), Polish Galicia (then within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>), now <a href="/wiki/Buchach" title="Buchach">Buchach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. Officially, his date of birth on the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a> was 18 Av 5648 (July 26), but he always said his birthday was on the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Fast_day" title="Fast day">fast day</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tisha_B%27Av" title="Tisha B'Av">Tisha B'Av</a>, the Ninth of Av.</p>
<p>His father. Shalom Mordechai Halevy, was ordained as a <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a>, but worked in the <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a>, and had many connections among the <a href="/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism" title="Hasidic Judaism">Hasidim</a>, His mother's side had ties to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitnagdim" title="Mitnagdim">Mitnagdim</a>.</p>
<p>He did not attend school and was schooled by his parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition to studying Jewish texts, Agnon studied writings of the <a href="/wiki/Haskalah" title="Haskalah">Haskalah</a>, and was also tutored in German. At the age of eight, he began to write in Hebrew and <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a>, At the age of 15, he published his first poem – a Yiddish poem about the <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalist</a> Joseph della Reina. He continued to write poems and stories in Hebrew and Yiddish, which were published in Galicia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">Literary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG/220px-%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG/330px-%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG/440px-%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%D7%97%D7%93%D7%A8_%D7%94%D7%A2%D7%91%D7%95%D7%93%D7%94.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Agnon's study</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1908, he moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaffa,_Israel" title="Jaffa, Israel">Jaffa</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Palestine#Ottoman_era" title="History of Palestine">Ottoman Palestine</a>. The first story he published there was "<a href="/wiki/Agunah" title="Agunah">Agunot</a>" ("Forsaken Wives"), which appeared that same year in the journal <i>Ha`omer.</i> He used the <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a> "Agnon," derived from the title of the story, which he adopted as his official <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> in 1924. In 1910, "Forsaken Wives" was translated into German. In 1912, at the urging of <a href="/wiki/Yosef_Haim_Brenner" title="Yosef Haim Brenner">Yosef Haim Brenner</a>, he published a novella, "Vehaya Ha'akov Lemishor" ("The Crooked Shall Be Made Straight").</p>
<p>In 1913, Agnon moved to Germany, where he met Esther Marx (1889-1973). They married in 1920 and had two children. In Germany he lived in Berlin and <a href="/wiki/Bad_Homburg_vor_der_H%C3%B6he" title="Bad Homburg vor der Höhe">Bad Homburg vor der Höhe</a> (1921–24). <a href="/wiki/Salman_Schocken" title="Salman Schocken">Salman Schocken</a>, a businessman and later also publisher, became his literary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patron" title="Patron">patron</a> and freed him from financial worries. From 1931 on, his work was published by <a href="/wiki/Schocken_Books" title="Schocken Books">Schocken Books</a>, and his short stories appeared regularly in the newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Haaretz" title="Haaretz">Haaretz</a></i>, also owned by the Schocken family. In Germany, he continued to write short stories and collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Buber" title="Martin Buber">Martin Buber</a> on an anthology of <a href="/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism" title="Hasidic Judaism">Hasidic</a> stories. Many of his early books appeared in Buber's <i>Jüdischer Verlag</i> (Berlin). The mostly assimilated, secular German Jews, Buber and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Rosenzweig" title="Franz Rosenzweig">Franz Rosenzweig</a> among them, considered Agnon to be a legitimate relic, being a religious man, familiar with Jewish scripture. <a href="/wiki/Gershom_Scholem" title="Gershom Scholem">Gershom Scholem</a> called him "the Jews' Jew".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1924, a fire broke out in his home, destroying his manuscripts and rare book collection. This traumatic event crops up occasionally in his stories. Later that year, Agnon returned to Palestine and settled with his family in the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Talpiot" title="Talpiot">Talpiot</a>. In 1929, his library was destroyed again during <a href="/wiki/1929_Palestine_riots" title="1929 Palestine riots">anti-Jewish riots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beit_Agnon_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beit_Agnon-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>When his novel <i>Hachnasat Kalla</i> ("The Bridal Canopy") appeared in 1931 to great critical acclaim, Agnon's place in Hebrew literature was assured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1935, he published "Sippur Pashut" ("A Simple Story"), a <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a> set in Buczacz at the end of the 19th century. Another novel, "Tmol Shilshom" ("Only Yesterday"), set in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eretz_Yisrael" title="Eretz Yisrael">Eretz Yisrael</a> (Israel) of the early 20th century, appeared in 1945.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_themes_and_influences">Literary themes and influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literary themes and influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agnon's writing has been the subject of extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic_research" title="Academic research">academic research</a>. Many leading scholars of Hebrew literature have published books and papers on his work, among them <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Kurzweil" title="Baruch Kurzweil">Baruch Kurzweil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dov_Sadan" title="Dov Sadan">Dov Sadan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitza_Ben-Dov" title="Nitza Ben-Dov">Nitza Ben-Dov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Miron" title="Dan Miron">Dan Miron</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_Laor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dan Laor (page does not exist)">Dan Laor</a>. Agnon writes about Jewish life, but with his own unique perspective and special touch. In his Nobel acceptance speech, Agnon claimed "Some see in my books the influences of authors whose names, in my ignorance, I have not even heard, while others see the influences of poets whose names I have heard but whose writings I have not read." He went on to detail that his primary influences were the stories of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Agnon acknowledged that he was also influenced by German literature and culture, and European literature in general, which he read in German translation. A collection of essays on this subject, edited in part by <a href="/wiki/Hillel_Weiss" title="Hillel Weiss">Hillel Weiss</a>, with contributions from Israeli and German scholars, was published in 2010: <a class="external text" href="http://www.biupress.co.il/website_en/index.asp?id=729" rel="nofollow"><i>Agnon and Germany: The Presence of the German World in the Writings of S.Y. Agnon</i>.</a> The budding <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> literature also influenced his works, notably that of his friend, <a href="/wiki/Yosef_Haim_Brenner" title="Yosef Haim Brenner">Yosef Haim Brenner</a>. In Germany, Agnon also spent time with the Hebraists <a href="/wiki/Hayim_Nahman_Bialik" title="Hayim Nahman Bialik">Hayim Nahman Bialik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ahad_Ha%27am" title="Ahad Ha'am">Ahad Ha'am</a>.</p>
<p>The communities he passed through in his life are reflected in his works:</p>
<ul>
<li>Galicia: in the books "<a href="/wiki/The_Bridal_Canopy" title="The Bridal Canopy">The Bridal Canopy</a>", "A City and the Fullness Thereof", "A Simple Story" and "A Guest for the Night".</li>
<li>Germany: in the stories "Fernheim", "Thus Far" and "Between Two Cities".</li>
<li>Jaffa: in the stories "Oath of Allegiance", "Tmol Shilshom" and "The Dune".</li>
<li>Jerusalem: "Tehilla", "Tmol Shilshom", "Ido ve-Inam" and "Shira".</li>
</ul>
<p>Nitza Ben-Dov writes about Agnon's use of allusiveness, free-association and imaginative dream-sequences, and discusses how seemingly inconsequential events and thoughts determine the lives of his characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of Agnon's works, such as <i>The Bridal Canopy</i>, <i>And the Crooked Shall Be Made Straight</i>, and <i>The Doctor's Divorce</i>, have been adapted for <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a>. A play based on Agnon's letters to his wife, "Esterlein Yakirati", was performed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khan_Theater" title="Khan Theater">Khan Theater</a> in Jerusalem.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agnon's writing often used words and phrases that differed from what would become established modern Hebrew. His distinct language is based on traditional Jewish sources, such as the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nevi%27im" title="Nevi'im">Prophets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midrash" title="Midrash">Midrashic</a> literature, the <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">Mishnah</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_literature" title="Rabbinic literature">Rabbinic literature</a>. Some examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>bet kahava</i> for modern <i>bet kafe</i> (coffee house / café)</li>
<li><i>batei yadayim</i> (lit. "hand-houses") for modern <i>kfafot</i> (gloves)</li>
<li><i>yatzta</i> (יצתה) rather than the modern conjugation <i>yatz'a</i> (יצאה) ("she went out")</li>
<li><i>rotev</i> (רוטב) meaning soup in place of modern <i>marak</i> (מרק)</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bar-Ilan_University" title="Bar-Ilan University">Bar-Ilan University</a> has made a computerized <a href="/wiki/Concordance_(publishing)" title="Concordance (publishing)">concordance</a> of his works in order to study his language.</p>

<p>Agnon was twice awarded the <a href="/wiki/Bialik_Prize" title="Bialik Prize">Bialik Prize</a> for literature (1934<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bi01_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-bi01-8">[8]</a></sup> and 1950<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bi01_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-bi01-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bialik_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bialik-9">[9]</a></sup>). He was also twice awarded the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Prize" title="Israel Prize">Israel Prize</a>, for literature (1954<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and 1958<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>In 1966, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a> "for his profoundly characteristic narrative art with motifs from the life of the Jewish people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Literature1966_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Literature1966-12">[12]</a></sup> The prize was shared with German Jewish author <a href="/wiki/Nelly_Sachs" title="Nelly Sachs">Nelly Sachs</a>. In his speech at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Award_ceremony" title="Award ceremony">award ceremony</a>, Agnon introduced himself in Hebrew: "As a result of the historic catastrophe in which <a href="/wiki/Titus" title="Titus">Titus</a> of Rome destroyed Jerusalem and Israel was exiled from its land, I was born in one of the cities of the Exile. But always I regarded myself as one who was born in Jerusalem".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In later years, Agnon's fame was such that when he complained to the municipality that traffic noise near his home was disturbing his work, the city closed the street to cars and posted a sign that read: "No entry to all vehicles, writer at work!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Agnon died in Jerusalem on February 17, 1970. His daughter, Emuna Yaron, has continued to publish his work <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posthumous_work" title="Posthumous work">posthumously</a>. Agnon's archive was transferred by the family to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_National_and_University_Library" title="Jewish National and University Library">National Library in Jerusalem</a>. His home in <a href="/wiki/Talpiot" title="Talpiot">Talpiot</a>, built in 1931 in the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> style, was turned into a museum, <i>Beit Agnon.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The study where he wrote many of his works was preserved intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Agnon's image, with a list of his works and his Nobel Prize acceptance speech, appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_new_shekel#Banknotes" title="Israeli new shekel">fifty-shekel bill</a>, second series, in circulation from 1985 to 2014. The main street in Jerusalem's <a href="/wiki/Givat_Oranim" title="Givat Oranim">Givat Oranim</a> neighborhood is called Sderot Shai Agnon, and a synagogue in Talpiot, a few blocks from his home, is named after him. Agnon is also memorialized in Buchach, now in Ukraine, where he was born. There is an extensive (relative to the size of the museum) exhibition in the Historical Museum in Buchach and, just a few yards away, a bust of Agnon is mounted on a pedestal in a plaza across the street from the house where he lived. The house itself is preserved and marked as the home where Agnon lived from birth till the age of (approximately) 19; the street that runs in front of the house is named "Agnon Street" (in Ukrainian).</p>
<p>Agnotherapy is a method developed in Israel to help elderly people express their feelings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beit_Agnon">Beit Agnon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Beit Agnon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Agnon's death, the former mayor of Jerusalem <a href="/wiki/Mordechai_Ish-Shalom" title="Mordechai Ish-Shalom">Mordechai Ish-Shalom</a> initiated the opening of his home to the public. In the early 1980s, the kitchen and family dining room were turned into a lecture and conference hall, and literary and cultural evenings were held there. In 2005, the Agnon House Association in Jerusalem renovated the building, which reopened in January 2009. The house was designed by the German-Jewish architect Fritz Korenberg, who was also his neighbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beit_Agnon_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beit_Agnon-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Published_works">Published works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Published works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels_and_novellas">Novels and novellas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Novels and novellas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridal_Canopy" title="The Bridal Canopy">The Bridal Canopy</a></i> (1931), translated from <i>Hakhnāsat kallāh</i>. An epic describing Galician Judaism at the start of the 19th century. The story of a poor but devout Galician Jew, Reb Yudel, who wanders the countryside with his companion, Nuta, during the early 19th century, in search of bridegrooms for his three daughters.</li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=In_the_Heart_of_the_Seas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="In the Heart of the Seas (page does not exist)">In the Heart of the Seas</a>, a story of a journey to the land of Israel</i> (1933), translated from <i>Bi-levav yamim</i>. A short novel about a group of ten men who travel from Eastern Europe to Jerusalem.</li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Simple_Story_(Agnon_novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Simple Story (Agnon novel) (page does not exist)">A Simple Story</a></i> (1935), translated from <i>Sipur pashut</i>. A short novel about a young man, his search for a bride, and the lessons of marriage.</li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Guest_for_the_Night&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Guest for the Night (page does not exist)">A Guest for the Night</a></i> (1938), translated from <i>Ore'ah Noteh Lalun</i>. A novel about the decline of eastern European Jewry. The narrator visits his old hometown and discovers that great changes have occurred since World War I.</li>
<li><i>Betrothed</i> (1943), translated from <i>Shevuat Emunim</i>. A short novel.</li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Only_Yesterday_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Only Yesterday (novel) (page does not exist)">Only Yesterday</a></i> (1945), translated from <i>Temol shilshom</i>. An epic novel set in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Aliyah" title="Second Aliyah">Second Aliyah</a> period. It follows the story of the narrator from Galicia to Jaffa to Jerusalem. Sometimes translated as <i>Those Were The Days</i>.</li>
<li><i>Edo and Enam</i> (1950). A short novel.</li>
<li><i>To This Day</i> (1952), translated from <i>ʿAd henah</i>. A tale of a young writer stranded in Berlin during World War I.</li>
<li><i>Shira</i> (1971). A novel set in Jerusalem in the 1930s and 1940s. Manfred Herbst, a middle-aged professor suffering from boredom, spends his days prowling the streets searching for Shira, the beguiling nurse he met when his wife was giving birth to their third child. Against the background of 1930s Jerusalem, Herbst wages war against the encroachment of age.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Short_stories">Short stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Short stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Of Such and Of Such</i>, a collection of stories, including "And the Crooked Shall Be Made Straight", "Forsaken Wives", and "Belevav Yamim" ("In the Heart of the Seas") from 1933.</li>
<li><i>At the Handles of the Lock</i> (1923), a collection of love stories, including "Bidmay Yameha" ("In the Prime of Her Life"), "A Simple Story", and "The Dune".</li>
<li><i>Near and Apparent</i>, a collection of stories, including "The Two Sages Who Were In Our City", "Between Two Cities", "The Lady and the Peddler", the collection "The Book of Deeds", the satire "Chapters of the National Manual", and "Introduction to the Kaddish: After the Funerals of Those Murdered in the Land of Israel".</li>
<li><i>Thus Far</i>, a collection of stories, including "Thus Far", "Prayer", "Oath of Allegiance", "The Garment", "Fernheim", and "Ido ve-Inam" (Edo and Enam).</li>
<li><i>The Fire and the Wood</i>, a collection of stories including Hasidic tales, a semi-fictional account of Agnon's family history and other stories.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthologies">Anthologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anthologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Days of Awe</i> (1938), a book of customs, interpretations, and legends for the Jewish days of mercy and forgiveness: <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Hashanah" title="Rosh Hashanah">Rosh Hashanah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur" title="Yom Kippur">Yom Kippur</a>, and the days between.</li>
<li><i>Present at Sinai: The Giving of the Law</i> (1959), an anthology for the festival of <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_publications">Posthumous publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shmuel_Yosef_Agnon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Posthumous publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Ir Umeloah</i> ("A City and the Fullness Thereof") (1973), a collection of stories and legends about Buczacz, Agnon's hometown.</li>
<li><i>In Mr. Lublin's Shop</i> (1974), set in Germany of the First World War.</li>
<li><i>Within the Wall</i> (1975), a collection of four stories.</li>
<li><i>From Myself to Myself</i> (1976), a collection of essays and speeches.</li>
<li><i>Introductions</i> (1977), stories.</li>
<li><i>Book, Writer and Story</i> (1978), stories about writers and books from the Jewish sources.</li>
<li><i>The Beams of Our House</i> (1979), two stories, the first about a Jewish family in Galicia, the second about the history of Agnon's family.</li>
<li><i>Esterlein Yakirati</i> ("Dear Esther: Letters 1924–1931" (1983), letters from Agnon to his wife.</li>
<li><i>A Shroud of Stories</i> (1985).</li>
<li><i>The Correspondence between S.Y. Agnon and S. Schocken</i> (1991), letters between Agnon and his publisher.</li>
<li><i>Agnon's Alef Bet Poems</i> (1998), a children's guide to the Hebrew Alphabet.</li>
<li><i><a class="external text" href="http://www.amazon.com/Book-That-Was-Lost-Classics/dp/1592642543" rel="nofollow">A Book That Was Lost: Thirty Five Stories</a></i> (2008)</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1977 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_University" title="Hebrew University">Hebrew University</a> published <i>Yiddish Works</i>, a collection of stories and poems that Agnon wrote in Yiddish during 1903–1906.</p>
