<p><b>Schwerin</b> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ʃvɛˈʁiːn]</a></span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ʃvəˈʁiːn]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburgisch_dialect" title="Mecklenburgisch dialect">Mecklenburgian</a> <i><b>Swerin</b></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><b>Suerina</b></i>) is the capital and second-largest city of the northern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a>. The population is 91,583 (as of December 31, 2013) and 98.000 as of July 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schwerin was first mentioned in 1018 and was granted <a href="/wiki/Town_privileges" title="Town privileges">city rights</a> in 1160, thus it is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="List of cities in Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">oldest city of Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a>. It is known for its <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Schwerin_Palace" title="Schwerin Palace">Schwerin Palace</a>, crowning an island in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Schwerin" title="Lake Schwerin">Lake Schwerin</a>. The city also has a largely intact <a href="/wiki/Altstadt" title="Altstadt">old town</a>, thanks to only minor damage in World War II.</p>
<p>Schwerin is located within the metropolitan region of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Metropolitan_Region" title="Hamburg Metropolitan Region">Hamburg</a> and close to that of <a href="/wiki/Berlin/Brandenburg_Metropolitan_Region" title="Berlin/Brandenburg Metropolitan Region">Berlin</a>, and to nearby <a href="/wiki/Regiopolis" title="Regiopolis">regiopolises</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rostock" title="Rostock">Rostock</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwerin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schwerin is surrounded by lakes. The largest of these lakes, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schweriner_See" title="Schweriner See">Schweriner See</a></i>, has an area of 60 km<sup>2</sup>. In the midst of these lakes there was a settlement of the Slavic <a href="/wiki/Obotrites" title="Obotrites">Obotrite</a> (dated back to the 11th century). The area was called <i>Zuarin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Polabian_language" title="Polabian language">Polabian</a> <i>Zwierzyn</i>), and the name Schwerin is derived from that designation. In 1160, <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Lion" title="Henry the Lion">Henry the Lion</a> defeated the Obotrites and captured Schwerin. The town was subsequently expanded into a powerful regional centre. A castle was built on this site, and expanded to become a ducal palace. It is supposedly haunted by the small, impious ghost, called <a href="/wiki/Peterm%C3%A4nnchen" title="Petermännchen">Petermännchen</a> (<i>"Peterman"</i>).</p>
<p>In 1358, Schwerin became a part of the Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a>, making it the seat of the duchy from then on. About 1500, the construction of the Schwerin Palace began, as a residence for the dukes. After the division of Mecklenburg (1621), Schwerin became the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Duchy of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Duchy of Mecklenburg-Schwerin</a>. Between 1765 and 1837, the town of <a href="/wiki/Ludwigslust" title="Ludwigslust">Ludwigslust</a> served as the capital, until Schwerin was reinstated.</p>
<p>In the mid-1800s, many residents from Schwerin moved to the United States, many to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee,_Wisconsin" title="Milwaukee, Wisconsin">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a>. Today Milwaukee and Schwerin are sister cities.</p>
<p>After 1918, and during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Revolution" title="German Revolution">German Revolution</a>, resulting in the fall of all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_monarchies" title="German monarchies">German monarchies</a>, the Grand Duke abdicated. Schwerin became capital of the Free State of Mecklenburg-Vorpommern thereafter.</p>
<p>At the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, on 2 May 1945, Schwerin was taken by U.S. troops. It was turned over to the British on 1 June 1945, and one month later, on 1 July 1945,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it was handed over to the Soviet forces, as the British and American forces pulled back from the <a href="/wiki/Line_of_contact" title="Line of contact">line of contact</a> to the predesignated occupation zones. Schwerin was then in the Soviet Occupation Zone which was to become the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a> (GDR). Initially, it was the capital of the State of Mecklenburg which at that time included the western part of <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">Pomerania</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vorpommern" title="Vorpommern">Vorpommern</a>). After the states were dissolved in the GDR, in 1952, Schwerin served as the capital of the Schwerin district (Bezirk Schwerin).</p>
<p>After reunification in 1990, the former state of Mecklenburg-Vorpommern was recreated as one of the <i><a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Bundesländer</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Rostock" title="Rostock">Rostock</a> was a serious contender for state capital but the decision went in favour of Schwerin.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwerin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>City buses and trams are run by NVS (Nahverkehr Schwerin).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Schwerin_Hauptbahnhof" title="Schwerin Hauptbahnhof">Schwerin Hauptbahnhof</a> (central station) is connected by <a href="/wiki/Ludwigslust%E2%80%93Wismar_railway" title="Ludwigslust–Wismar railway">rail to Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hagenow_Land%E2%80%93Schwerin_railway" title="Hagenow Land–Schwerin railway">Hamburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bad_Kleinen%E2%80%93Rostock_railway" title="Bad Kleinen–Rostock railway">Rostock</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwerin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The landmark of the city is the <a href="/wiki/Schwerin_Palace" title="Schwerin Palace">Schwerin Palace</a>, located on an island in the lake of the same name (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schweriner_See" title="Schweriner See">Schweriner See</a></i>). It was, for centuries, the residence of the Dukes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Mecklenburg" title="Duchy of Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a> and today is the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Landtag" title="Landtag">Landtag</a> (state parliament).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schwerin_Cathedral" title="Schwerin Cathedral">Schwerin Cathedral</a>, built in 1260–1416 in <a href="/wiki/Brick_Gothic" title="Brick Gothic">Brick Gothic</a> style.</li>
<li>The <i>Alter Garten</i> (Old Garden) square, surrounded by buildings such as the 18th-century <i>Altes Palais</i> (Old Palace), the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> <i>Staatliches Museum Schwerin</i> (State Art Museum), built in 1877–1882, and the <i>Staatstheater</i> (City Theater), erected in 1886.</li>
<li>The town hall (18th century)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schelfkirche" title="Schelfkirche">Schelfkirche</a> (Saint Nicolai, originally built 1238, but rebuilt in 1713 after destruction by a storm)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernsehturm_Schwerin-Zippendorf" title="Fernsehturm Schwerin-Zippendorf">Television mast Schwerin-Zippendorf</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwerin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Staatliches_Museum_Schwerin" title="Staatliches Museum Schwerin">Staatliches Museum Schwerin-Kunstsammlungen</a></i> (State Art Museum) houses a remarkable collection of 17th-century Dutch paintings and German art from medieval and renaissance masters up to the present day. There are also a collection of Greek vases, the notable collection of Paintings of Jean-Baptiste Oudry, a collection of sculptures of Houdon, German 18th-century court paintings, and works by such modern artists as <a href="/wiki/Max_Liebermann" title="Max Liebermann">Max Liebermann</a>, Franz Stuck, Marcel Duchamp etc. The Graphic cabinet houses rich collections of Dutch and German drawings and prints (Jan van Goyen, Dürer, Cranach, Rembrandt, Merian etc.) and a notable collection of coloured graphics from the time of the GDR.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/State_Museum_of_Technology" title="State Museum of Technology">State Museum of Technology</a> (<i>Technische Museum</i>), housed in the former <i>Marstall</i> (Royal Stables). In 2012 the Technische Museum moved to the city of Wismar located 40 km north of Schwerin.</li>
</ul>
