<p><b>Choiseul Island</b>, native name <b>Lauru</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the largest island (2,971 km<sup>2</sup> (1,147 sq mi)) of the <a href="/wiki/Choiseul_Province" title="Choiseul Province">Choiseul Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Choiseul_Island&amp;params=7.08_S_157_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">7°05′S</span> <span class="longitude">157°00′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">7.08°S 157°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-7.08; 157</span></span></span></a></span>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choiseul_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This island is named after <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Fran%C3%A7ois,_duc_de_Choiseul" title="Étienne François, duc de Choiseul">Étienne François, duc de Choiseul</a>.</p>
<p>The first recorded sighting by Europeans was by the Spanish expedition of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira" title="Álvaro de Mendaña de Neira">Álvaro de Mendaña</a> in April 1568. More precisely the sighting was due to a local voyage done by a small boat, in the accounts the <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a> <i>Santiago</i>, commanded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maese_de_Campo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maese de Campo (page does not exist)">Maestre de Campo</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Ortega_Valencia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedro Ortega Valencia (page does not exist)">Pedro Ortega Valencia</a> and having <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hern%C3%A1n_Gallego&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hernán Gallego (page does not exist)">Hernán Gallego</a> as pilot. They charted it as <i>San Marcos</i>, and also who named the narrow channel separating San Jorge from <a href="/wiki/Santa_Isabel_Island" title="Santa Isabel Island">Santa Isabel Island</a> as the Ortega channel after the commander of the expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Choiseul islanders suffered attacks from <a href="/wiki/Blackbirding" title="Blackbirding">blackbirding</a> in the nineteenth century (the often brutal recruitment or kidnapping of labourers for the sugar plantations in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>).</p>
<p>In April 1885 a German Protectorate was declared over the <a href="/wiki/North_Solomon_Islands" title="North Solomon Islands">North Solomon Islands</a>, including Choiseul. In 1900, under the terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Berlin_(1899)" title="Treaty of Berlin (1899)">Treaty of Berlin (14 November 1899)</a>, Germany transferred the North Solomon Islands (except for Bougainville and its surrounding islands) to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Solomon_Islands_Protectorate" title="British Solomon Islands Protectorate">British Solomon Islands Protectorate</a> in exchange for the British giving up all claims to <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>. Missionaries settled on Choiseul under both protectorates, converting most of the population to Christianity. In the early 20th century several British and Australian firms began large-scale coconut planting.</p>
<p>Choiseul was visited by <a href="/wiki/Austrians" title="Austrians">Austrian</a> anthropologist and photographer <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Bernatzik" title="Hugo Bernatzik">Hugo Bernatzik</a> in 1932. Bernatzik documented some of the few remaining ancestral customs of the island people which are included in an <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnography</a> that he published a few years later. He also took some photographs of the islanders and brought back a stone urn with carvings, reflecting a culture that he deemed was dying in contact with the modern world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the Choiseul was occupied by Japanese forces in 1942.</p>
<p>With the independence of the Solomon Islands in July 1978, Choiseul has been administered as part of <a href="/wiki/Choiseul_Province" title="Choiseul Province">Choiseul Province</a>.</p>
<p>The administrative headquarters of Choiseul Province is situated in the town of Taro.</p>
