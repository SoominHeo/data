<p><b>Stefan Zweig</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[tsvaɪk]</a></span>; November 28, 1881 – February 22, 1942) was an Austrian novelist, playwright, journalist and biographer. At the height of his literary career, in the 1920s and 1930s, he was one of the most popular writers in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secretsuperstar_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-secretsuperstar-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stefan_Zweig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1005" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg/220px-Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg/330px-Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg/440px-Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stefan_Zweig_1900.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stefan Zweig in Vienna with his brother Alfred, circa 1900</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Zweig was born in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, the son of Moritz Zweig (1845–1926), a wealthy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> textile manufacturer, and Ida Brettauer (1854–1938), a daughter of a Jewish banking family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lohrmann2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lohrmann2003-3">[3]</a></sup> He was related to the Czech writer <a href="/wiki/Egon_Hostovsk%C3%BD" title="Egon Hostovský">Egon Hostovský</a>, who described him as "a very distant relative";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> some sources describe them as cousins.</p>
<p>Zweig studied philosophy at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">University of Vienna</a> and in 1904 earned a doctoral degree with a thesis on "The Philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Taine" title="Hippolyte Taine">Hippolyte Taine</a>". Religion did not play a central role in his education. "My mother and father were Jewish only through accident of birth," Zweig said later in an interview. Yet he did not renounce his Jewish faith and wrote repeatedly on Jews and Jewish themes, as in his story <i><a href="/wiki/Buchmendel" title="Buchmendel">Buchmendel</a></i>. Zweig had a warm relationship with <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Herzl" title="Theodor Herzl">Theodor Herzl</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a>, whom he met when Herzl was still literary editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Neue_Freie_Presse" title="Neue Freie Presse">Neue Freie Presse</a></i>, then Vienna's main newspaper; Herzl accepted for publication some of Zweig's early essays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Zweig believed in <a href="/wiki/Internationalism_(politics)" title="Internationalism (politics)">internationalism</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Europeanism" title="Europeanism">Europeanism</a>, as <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_of_Yesterday" title="The World of Yesterday">The World of Yesterday</a></i>, his autobiography, makes clear. According to <a href="/wiki/Amos_Elon" title="Amos Elon">Amos Elon</a>, Zweig called Herzl's book <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Judenstaat" title="Der Judenstaat">Der Judenstaat</a></i> an "obtuse text, [a] piece of nonsense".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elon_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-elon-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the beginning of World War I, patriotic sentiment was widespread, and extended to many German and Austrian Jews: Zweig, as well as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Buber" title="Martin Buber">Martin Buber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Cohen" title="Hermann Cohen">Hermann Cohen</a>, showed support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Zweig served in the Archives of the Ministry of War and adopted a pacifist stand like his friend <a href="/wiki/Romain_Rolland" title="Romain Rolland">Romain Rolland</a>, recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a> 1915. Zweig married Friderike Maria von Winternitz (born Burger) in 1920; they divorced in 1938. As Friderike Zweig she published a book on her former husband after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She later also published a picture book on Zweig.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the late summer of 1939, Zweig married his secretary Elisabet Charlotte "Lotte" Altmann at <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yomZweigAltmann_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-yomZweigAltmann-10">[10]</a></sup> Zweig's secretary in Salzburg from November 1919 to March 1938 was Anna Meingast (13 May 1881, Vienna – 17 November 1953, Salzburg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

Street named after Zweig in <a href="/wiki/Laranjeiras" title="Laranjeiras">Laranjeiras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a></div>

<p>In 1934, following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitler" title="Hitler">Hitler</a>'s rise to power in Germany, Zweig left Austria for England, living first in London, then from 1939 in <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>. Because of the swift advance of Hitler's troops westwards, Zweig and his second wife crossed the Atlantic to the United States, settling in 1940 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>; they lived for two months as guests of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>, then they rented a house in <a href="/wiki/Ossining_(village),_New_York" title="Ossining (village), New York">Ossining</a>.</p>
<p>On August 22, 1940, they moved again to <a href="/wiki/Petr%C3%B3polis" title="Petrópolis">Petrópolis</a>, a German-colonized mountain town 68 kilometers north of <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> known for historical reasons as Brazil's <i>Imperial city</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DW_30.04.2009_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-DW_30.04.2009-12">[12]</a></sup> Feeling more and more depressed by the growth of intolerance, <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarianism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>, and feeling hopeless for the future for humanity, Zweig wrote a note about his feelings of desperation. Then, on February 23, 1942, the Zweigs were found dead of a <a href="/wiki/Barbiturate" title="Barbiturate">barbiturate</a> <a href="/wiki/Drug_overdose" title="Drug overdose">overdose</a> in their house in the city of Petrópolis, holding hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time1942_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time1942-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He had been despairing at the future of Europe and its culture. "I think it better to conclude in good time and in erect bearing a life in which intellectual labour meant the purest joy and personal freedom the highest good on Earth," he wrote.</p>
<p>The Zweigs' house in Brazil was later turned into a cultural centre and is now known as <a href="/wiki/Casa_Stefan_Zweig" title="Casa Stefan Zweig">Casa Stefan Zweig</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stefan_Zweig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zweig was a prominent writer in the 1920s and 1930s, befriending <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schnitzler" title="Arthur Schnitzler">Arthur Schnitzler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He was extremely popular in the United States, South America and Europe, and remains so in continental Europe;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secretsuperstar_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-secretsuperstar-2">[2]</a></sup> however, he was largely ignored by the British public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walton2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walton2010-16">[16]</a></sup> His fame in America had diminished until the 1990s, when there began an effort on the part of several publishers (notably <a href="/wiki/Pushkin_Press" title="Pushkin Press">Pushkin Press</a>, Hesperus Press, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Review_of_Books" title="The New York Review of Books">The New York Review of Books</a></i>) to get Zweig back into print in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lezard2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lezard2009-17">[17]</a></sup> Plunkett Lake Press Ebooks began to publish electronic versions of his non-fiction works. Since that time there has been a marked resurgence and a number of Zweig's books are back in print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critical opinion of his oeuvre is strongly divided between those who despise his literary style as poor, lightweight and superficial,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walton2010_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walton2010-16">[16]</a></sup> and those who praise his humanism, simplicity and effective style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lezard2009_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lezard2009-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Hofmann" title="Michael Hofmann">Michael Hofmann</a> is scathingly dismissive of Zweig's work, which he dubbed a "vermicular dither", adding that "Zweig just tastes fake. He's the <a href="/wiki/Pepsi" title="Pepsi">Pepsi</a> of Austrian writing." Even the author's suicide note left Hofmann gripped by "the irritable rise of boredom halfway through it, and the sense that <i>he doesn't mean it</i>, his heart isn't in it (not even in his suicide)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hofmann_dither_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hofmann_dither-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Zweig is best known for his novellas (notably <i><a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Game" title="The Royal Game">The Royal Game</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Amok_(novella)" title="Amok (novella)">Amok</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Letter_from_an_Unknown_Woman" title="Letter from an Unknown Woman">Letter from an Unknown Woman</a></i> – which was <a href="/wiki/Letter_from_an_Unknown_Woman_(1948_film)" title="Letter from an Unknown Woman (1948 film)">filmed</a> in 1948 by <a href="/wiki/Max_Oph%C3%BCls" title="Max Ophüls">Max Ophüls</a>), novels (<i><a href="/wiki/Beware_of_Pity_(novel)" title="Beware of Pity (novel)">Beware of Pity</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confusion_of_Feelings" title="Confusion of Feelings">Confusion of Feelings</a></i>, and the posthumously published <i>The Post Office Girl</i>) and biographies (notably of <a href="/wiki/Erasmus" title="Erasmus">Erasmus of Rotterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a>, and also the posthumously published one on <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Balzac</a>). At one time his works were published without his consent in English under the pseudonym "Stephen Branch" (a translation of his real name) when anti-German sentiment was running high. His <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette:_The_Portrait_of_an_Average_Woman" title="Marie Antoinette: The Portrait of an Average Woman">1932 biography</a> of Queen <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">Marie Antoinette</a> was adapted by <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette_(1938_film)" title="Marie Antoinette (1938 film)">1938 film</a> starring <a href="/wiki/Norma_Shearer" title="Norma Shearer">Norma Shearer</a>.</p>
<p>Zweig's <a href="/wiki/Memoir" title="Memoir">memoir</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_of_Yesterday" title="The World of Yesterday">The World of Yesterday</a></i>, was completed in 1942 on the day before he committed suicide. It has been widely discussed as a record of "what it meant to be alive between 1881 and 1942" in central Europe; the book has attracted both critical praise<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lezard2009_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lezard2009-17">[17]</a></sup> and hostile dismissal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hofmann_dither_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hofmann_dither-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>Zweig enjoyed a close association with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a>, and provided the <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Die_schweigsame_Frau" title="Die schweigsame Frau">Die schweigsame Frau</a></i> (<i>The Silent Woman</i>). Strauss famously defied the Nazi regime by refusing to sanction the removal of Zweig's name from the programme <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StraussZweigLetters_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-StraussZweigLetters-24">[24]</a></sup> for the work's première on June 24, 1935 in <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>. As a result, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Goebbels</a> refused to attend as planned, and the opera was banned after three performances. Zweig later collaborated with Joseph Gregor, to provide Strauss with the libretto for one other opera, <i><a href="/wiki/Daphne_(opera)" title="Daphne (opera)">Daphne</a></i>, in 1937. At least<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> one other work by Zweig received a musical setting: the pianist and composer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jolles" title="Henry Jolles">Henry Jolles</a>, who like Zweig had fled to Brazil to escape the Nazis, composed a song, "Último poema de Stefan Zweig",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> based on "Letztes Gedicht", which Zweig wrote on the occasion of his 60th birthday in November 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BiogSketchCasaZweig_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BiogSketchCasaZweig-27">[27]</a></sup> During his stay in Brazil, Zweig wrote <i>Brasilien, Ein Land der Zukunft</i> (Brazil, Land of the Future) which was an accurate analysis of his newly adopted country; in this book he managed to demonstrate a fair understanding of the Brazilian culture that surrounded him.</p>
<p>Zweig was a passionate collector of manuscripts. There are important Zweig collections at the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/State_University_of_New_York_at_Fredonia" title="State University of New York at Fredonia">State University of New York at Fredonia</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Israel" title="National Library of Israel">National Library of Israel</a>. The British Library's <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Zweig_Collection" title="Stefan Zweig Collection">Stefan Zweig Collection</a> was donated to the library by his heirs in May 1986. It specialises in autograph music manuscripts, including works by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Haydn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahler</a>. It has been described as "one of the world's greatest collections of autograph manuscripts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SZBLcollection_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-SZBLcollection-28">[28]</a></sup> One particularly precious item is <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a>'s "Verzeichnüß aller meiner Werke"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMozartVerzeichn.C3.BC.C3.9F_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMozartVerzeichn.C3.BC.C3.9F-29">[29]</a></sup> – that is, the composer's own handwritten thematic catalogue of his works.</p>
<p>The 1993–1994 academic year at the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Europe" title="College of Europe">College of Europe</a> was named in his honour.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plays">Plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stefan_Zweig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stefan_Zweig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jeff_Gabel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jeff Gabel (page does not exist)">Jeff Gabel</a> created an English-language adaptation of <i>Vierundzwanzig Stunden aus dem Leben einer Frau</i> in a large-scale comic book format in 2004, titled <i>24 Hours in the Life of a Woman</i>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Beware_of_Pity_(novel)" title="Beware of Pity (novel)">Beware of Pity</a></i> was adapted into a 1946 film with <a href="/wiki/Beware_of_Pity" title="Beware of Pity">the same title</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Elvey" title="Maurice Elvey">Maurice Elvey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>An adaptation by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Wyatt" title="Stephen Wyatt">Stephen Wyatt</a> of <i>Beware of Pity</i> was broadcast by BBC Radio 4 in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2013 French film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Promise_(2013_film)" title="A Promise (2013 film)">A Promise</a></i> (<i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">Une promesse</span></i>) is based on Zweig's novella <i>Journey into the Past</i> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reise in die Vergangenheit</span></i>).</p>
<p>The 2013 Swiss film <i>Mary Queen of Scots</i> directed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Imbach" title="Thomas Imbach">Thomas Imbach</a> is based on Zweig's <i>Maria Stuart</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The end-credits for <a href="/wiki/Wes_Anderson" title="Wes Anderson">Wes Anderson</a>'s 2014 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grand_Budapest_Hotel" title="The Grand Budapest Hotel">The Grand Budapest Hotel</a></i> say that the film was inspired in part by Zweig's novels. Anderson said that he had "stolen" from Zweig's novels <i><a href="/wiki/Beware_of_Pity_(novel)" title="Beware of Pity (novel)">Beware of Pity</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Post_Office_Girl" title="The Post Office Girl">The Post-Office Girl</a></i> in writing the film, and it features actors <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wilkinson" title="Tom Wilkinson">Tom Wilkinson</a> as The Author, a character based loosely on Zweig, and <a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude Law</a> as his younger, idealised self seen in flashbacks. Anderson also said that the film's protagonist, the concierge Gustave H., played by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Fiennes" title="Ralph Fiennes">Ralph Fiennes</a>, was based on Zweig. In the film's opening sequence, a teenaged girl visits a shrine for The Author, which includes a bust of him wearing Zweig-like spectacles and celebrated as his country's "National Treasure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2016 Austrian-German Film <i>Vor der Morgenröte</i> (<i>Stefan Zweig: Farewell to Europe</i>) chronicles Stefan Zweig's travels in the North and South Americas, trying to come to terms with his exile from home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
