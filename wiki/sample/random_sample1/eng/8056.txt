<p><b>Supergrass</b> were an English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band from <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>. The band consisted of brothers <a href="/wiki/Gaz_Coombes" title="Gaz Coombes">Gaz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_vocals" title="Lead vocals">lead vocals</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Rob_Coombes" title="Rob Coombes">Rob Coombes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">keyboards</a> and backing vocals), <a href="/wiki/Mick_Quinn" title="Mick Quinn">Mick Quinn</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass</a> and backing vocals) and <a href="/wiki/Danny_Goffey" title="Danny Goffey">Danny Goffey</a> (<a href="/wiki/Drum_kit" title="Drum kit">drums</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backing_vocals" title="Backing vocals">backing vocals</a>).</p>
<p>Gaz Coombes, Mick Quinn and Danny Goffey formed Supergrass in 1993 in Oxford with Gaz's brother Rob Coombes officially joining the band in 2002. The band signed to <a href="/wiki/Parlophone" title="Parlophone">Parlophone</a> records in 1994 and produced <i><a href="/wiki/I_Should_Coco" title="I Should Coco">I Should Coco</a></i> (1995), the biggest selling debut album for the label since <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles'</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Please_Please_Me" title="Please Please Me">Please Please Me</a></i>. Their first album's fourth single "<a href="/wiki/Alright_(Supergrass_song)" title="Alright (Supergrass song)">Alright</a>" was a huge international hit that established the band's reputation. Since then the band have released five albums: <i><a href="/wiki/In_It_for_the_Money" title="In It for the Money">In It for the Money</a></i> (1997), <i><a href="/wiki/Supergrass_(album)" title="Supergrass (album)">Supergrass</a></i> (1999), <i><a href="/wiki/Life_on_Other_Planets" title="Life on Other Planets">Life on Other Planets</a></i> (2002), <i><a href="/wiki/Road_to_Rouen" title="Road to Rouen">Road to Rouen</a></i> (2005) and <i><a href="/wiki/Diamond_Hoo_Ha" title="Diamond Hoo Ha">Diamond Hoo Ha</a></i> (2008), as well as a decade-ending compilation called <i><a href="/wiki/Supergrass_is_10" title="Supergrass is 10">Supergrass is 10</a></i> (2004).</p>
<p>In August 2009 the band signed to <a href="/wiki/Cooking_Vinyl" title="Cooking Vinyl">Cooking Vinyl</a> and began work on their seventh studio album <i>Release the Drones</i>. The album remained unreleased and unfinished as, on 12 April 2010, the band announced that they were splitting up due to musical and creative differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The group disbanded after four farewell gigs, the final one at <a href="/wiki/La_Cigale" title="La Cigale">La Cigale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> on 11 June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_newsbeat_1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_newsbeat_1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supergrass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Jennifers_and_formation_.281990.E2.80.931993.29">The Jennifers and formation (1990–1993)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supergrass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Jennifers and formation (1990–1993)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the age of 16 and 18 respectively, <a href="/wiki/Gaz_Coombes" title="Gaz Coombes">Gaz Coombes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danny_Goffey" title="Danny Goffey">Danny Goffey</a> were members of <a href="/wiki/Shoegazing" title="Shoegazing">shoegaze</a> band <a href="/wiki/The_Jennifers" title="The Jennifers">The Jennifers</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Dom_and_Nic" title="Dom and Nic">Nick Goffey</a> and Andy Davis. The band played <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gig_(musical_performance)" title="Gig (musical performance)">gigs</a> at various venues around Oxfordshire, often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">public houses</a> and clubs. One pub the band played at was the <a href="/wiki/Jericho_Tavern" title="Jericho Tavern">Jericho Tavern</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>. The band enjoyed enough success to release one single in 1992, "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Just_Got_Back_Today&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Just Got Back Today (page does not exist)">Just Got Back Today</a>", on <a href="/wiki/Nude_Records" title="Nude Records">Nude Records</a> before they disbanded.</p>
<p>When Coombes began working at the local <a href="/wiki/Harvester_(restaurant)" title="Harvester (restaurant)">Harvester</a> he befriended co-worker <a href="/wiki/Mick_Quinn" title="Mick Quinn">Mick Quinn</a>. The two realised they had common music interests and Coombes invited Quinn to come and <a href="/wiki/Jam_session" title="Jam session">jam</a> with himself and Goffey. In February 1993 they formed <b>Theodore Supergrass</b>, "for about two months" Quinn explains, "then we realized that Theodore was a bit rubbish so we took that off."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goffey claims that the name was his idea and says; "Although the others will dispute it, it was me. We were Theodore Supergrass and the idea was the band would be a little black character, and we wouldn't ever have to do interviews. We'd get the questions in advance, script the answers and then animate Theodore Supergrass answering them. But it cost too much money." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gaz's brother, <a href="/wiki/Rob_Coombes" title="Rob Coombes">Rob Coombes</a>, played <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a> for the band's début gig at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Zodiac_(club)" title="The Zodiac (club)">Co-Op Hall</a>, Oxford in 1993. In January 1995 he first performed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keyboard_player" title="Keyboard player">keyboardist</a> with the band for a live <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">Radio 1</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a> session.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His role in the band progressed over the years, post-<a href="/wiki/I_Should_Coco" title="I Should Coco">I Should Coco</a> material is credited to "Supergrass and Rob Coombes", however, he wasn't introduced as a band member until almost a decade later.</p>

<p>In mid-1994, Supergrass issued their debut single "<a href="/wiki/Caught_by_the_Fuzz" title="Caught by the Fuzz">Caught by the Fuzz</a>" on the small independent local label Backbeat Records. The song recounts lead singer and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Gaz_Coombes" title="Gaz Coombes">Gaz Coombes</a>'s experience of being arrested by the police for possession of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thebiographychannel.co.uk_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-thebiographychannel.co.uk-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The limited release of vinyl copies sold out quickly, thanks in part to support from <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a> on his <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">Radio One</a> show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Parlophone" title="Parlophone">Parlophone</a> label signed the band and re-released the single in the autumn of the same year. It achieved the rare feat of both <a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a> "Single Of The Week" status in the same week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thebiographychannel.co.uk_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-thebiographychannel.co.uk-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Mansize_Rooster" title="Mansize Rooster">Mansize Rooster</a>", released in February 1995, peaked at number 20 in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Lenny_(Supergrass_song)" title="Lenny (Supergrass song)">Lenny</a>" was the band's first top 10 single. "Lenny" was followed soon afterwards by the band's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Should_Coco" title="I Should Coco">I Should Coco</a></i> (May 1995), which entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> at number one. It achieved half a million sales in the UK and over a million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>NME</i> reviewer Steve Sutherland gave the album a nine out of ten rating, writing, "These freaks shall inherit the earth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The album's fourth single, the double A-side release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alright/Time" title="Alright/Time">"Alright"/"Time"</a>, stayed in the UK Top Three for a month, peaking at number two.</p>
<p>Supergrass followed <i>I Should Coco</i> with 18 months of heavy touring, appearing at festivals such as Scotland's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T_In_The_Park" title="T In The Park">T In The Park</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Festival" title="Glastonbury Festival">Glastonbury Festival</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After Performing at <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio</a>'s <i>Hollywood Rock Festival</i> in April 1996, Supergrass met the infamous train robber <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Biggs" title="Ronnie Biggs">Ronnie Biggs</a>, and apparently said to him, "I was frightened for my life when I heard there was a <a href="/wiki/Supergrass_(informer)" title="Supergrass (informer)">supergrass</a> in the area."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A photograph of Ronnie Biggs and Gaz together was subsequently included in the music video for their 1996 single "<a href="/wiki/Going_Out" title="Going Out">Going Out</a>". Recorded at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Linford_Manor" title="Great Linford Manor">Great Linford Manor</a> the single peaked at number five in the UK charts, but was the last song produced by Sam Williams. Supergrass returned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sawmills_Studio" title="Sawmills Studio">Sawmills Studio</a> to co-produce follow up album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_It_For_The_Money" title="In It For The Money">In It For The Money</a></i> (released April 1997), with <a href="/wiki/John_Cornfield" title="John Cornfield">John Cornfield</a>. The album was a huge success and went platinum in the UK, but confused some fans expecting something similar to <i>I Should Coco</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The single, "<a href="/wiki/Richard_III_(song)" title="Richard III (song)">Richard III</a>", reached number two. Subsequent releases, "<a href="/wiki/Sun_Hits_the_Sky" title="Sun Hits the Sky">Sun Hits the Sky</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_In_The_Day" title="Late In The Day">Late In The Day</a>", reached numbers 10 and 18 respectively.</p>
<p>Around this time Supergrass also appeared on the front cover of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Issue" title="The Big Issue">The Big Issue</a></i>, interviewed for the magazine at <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> by infamous ex-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_smuggler" title="Drug smuggler">drug smuggler</a> <a href="/wiki/Howard_Marks" title="Howard Marks">Howard Marks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The band again took a short break before returning in 1999 with the single "<a href="/wiki/Pumping_on_Your_Stereo" title="Pumping on Your Stereo">Pumping on Your Stereo</a>". The promo video, produced in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Jim_Henson%27s_Creature_Shop" title="Jim Henson's Creature Shop">Jim Henson's Creature Shop</a>, featured the band with comical "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muppet" title="Muppet">muppet</a>" bodies. The single generated welcome publicity following their time out of the limelight, as did a small sold-out tour scheduled around the single release, the final night of which was at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepherds_Bush_Empire" title="Shepherds Bush Empire">Shepherds Bush Empire</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>'s "Five Night Stand" festival. The single and the tour were followed by their third LP <i><a href="/wiki/Supergrass_(album)" title="Supergrass (album)">Supergrass</a></i> (1999). The following spring the record was released in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> Once more, the album was recorded at Sawmills Studio with longtime associate Cornfield producing. <i>Supergrass</i> was well received critically and commercially and it has since gone platinum in the UK, but did not reap the same level of success as its predecessors. Critics claimed the album was "hit and miss", which showed up particularly as the "also-rans are surrounded by songs that are as great as anything Supergrass has ever recorded".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Their next single, "<a href="/wiki/Moving_(Supergrass_song)" title="Moving (Supergrass song)">Moving</a>", proved popular and reached the Top Ten in the UK. And their third single, "<a href="/wiki/Mary_(Supergrass_song)" title="Mary (Supergrass song)">Mary</a>" entered the <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a>. There followed a long hiatus.</p>
<p>After three years out of the limelight, the band returned with <i><a href="/wiki/Life_on_Other_Planets" title="Life on Other Planets">Life on Other Planets</a></i> (September 2002), recorded at <i>Heliocentric</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rockfield_Studios" title="Rockfield Studios">Rockfield</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mayfair_Studios" title="Mayfair Studios">Mayfair</a></i> Studios and produced by <a href="/wiki/Beck" title="Beck">Beck</a> collaborator <a href="/wiki/Tony_Hoffer" title="Tony Hoffer">Tony Hoffer</a>. The album was released in the UK on Parlophone, but in the US on the <a href="/wiki/The_Island_Def_Jam_Music_Group" title="The Island Def Jam Music Group">Island Def Jam</a> imprint. The record was not as commercially successful as Supergrass's first three albums, failing to make the Top Three in the UK albums chart. However, the critical response to the album was generally very positive, with <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> from <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> claiming "The world is a better place for having Supergrass in it.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It has since gone gold in the UK. <i>Life on Other Planets</i> was also notable as it was the first Supergrass album to recognise Rob Coombes as an official member. For the band's first three albums, Supergrass officially consisted of Gaz Coombes, Goffey and Quinn although Rob Coombes contributed to many of the band's songs and videos, and toured with them. Tracks recorded before this were often credited to "Supergrass and Rob Coombes". The band followed <i>Life on Other Planets</i> with another extended three-year hiatus, devoted to touring and personal engagements.</p>
<p>In June 2004 the band's record company suggested the band release a singles compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Supergrass_is_10" title="Supergrass is 10">Supergrass is 10</a></i>, spawning two new self-produced tracks: "<a href="/wiki/Kiss_of_Life_(Supergrass_song)" title="Kiss of Life (Supergrass song)">Kiss of Life</a>" and "Bullit". The companion DVD contained 'Home Movie', a humorous documentary charting the band's first 10 years achievements, made in collaboration with "<a href="/wiki/Seen_the_Light" title="Seen the Light">Seen the Light</a>" video director <a href="/wiki/Simon_Hilton" title="Simon Hilton">Simon Hilton</a>. The record entered the UK albums chart at number four and has since gone gold in the UK.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_in_recent_years_.282005.E2.80.932008.29">Development in recent years (2005–2008)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supergrass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development in recent years (2005–2008)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recording of their fifth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Road_to_Rouen" title="Road to Rouen">Road to Rouen</a></i>, began in France in a studio built by the band in Normandy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-19">[19]</a></sup> Working with French engineer Pierre-Olivier Margerand it represented a significant change in direction and was perceived as a more mature body of work.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg_(song)" title="St. Petersburg (song)">St. Petersburg</a>", the string-laden first single, was released on 8 August 2005. The album followed a week later (released 27 September in North America) and reached No. 9 on the UK chart, going on to achieve silver status in the UK. Opinion at the time was divided, but the album garnered the band many new fans and a measure of creative respect, some even embracing it as "the sound of a band at last hitting their stride".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NME-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Second single, "Low C", featured a video by acclaimed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pumping_On_Your_Stereo" title="Pumping On Your Stereo">Pumping On Your Stereo</a>" video director <a href="/wiki/Hammer_%26_Tongs" title="Hammer &amp; Tongs">Garth Jennings</a>, shot in <a href="/wiki/Weeki_Wachee_Springs" title="Weeki Wachee Springs">Weeki Wachee Springs</a> Florida. Third single "<a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">Fin</a>", interpreted as a missive to the Coombes brothers' recently-deceased mother, received much critical praise, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-21">[21]</a></sup> referring to it as "so gorgeously light and airy that listening to it is like sleepwalking in space".</p>
<p>The band toured the songs in both acoustic and electric formats with percussionist Satin Singh joining the live band throughout. From August 2005 to September 2006 they performed in Japan, South America, the United States, and Europe, finishing with a memorable gig at the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Pop_Festival" title="Beijing Pop Festival">Beijing Pop Festival</a>.</p>

<p>The follow-up album, <i><a href="/wiki/Diamond_Hoo_Ha" title="Diamond Hoo Ha">Diamond Hoo Ha</a></i> was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Hansa_Tonstudio" title="Hansa Tonstudio">Hansa Tonstudio</a>, Berlin, with producer Nick Launey, and mixed at Seedy Underbelly Studios in Los Angeles. The band toured in the summer of 2007, headlining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guilfest" title="Guilfest">Guilfest</a>, among others, and debuting new material, with the youngest sibling of the Coombes brothers, ex-<a href="/wiki/22-20s" title="22-20s">22-20s</a> keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Charly_Coombes" title="Charly Coombes">Charly</a>, on second guitar, percussion and backing vocals.</p>
<p>On 27 September 2007 bassist <a href="/wiki/Mick_Quinn" title="Mick Quinn">Mick Quinn</a> sustained a broken <a href="/wiki/Calcaneus" title="Calcaneus">heel bone</a> and two spinal fractures in a sleepwalking accident whilst on holiday in France. During his recuperation, Gaz and Danny promoted first single "<a href="/wiki/Diamond_Hoo_Ha_Man" title="Diamond Hoo Ha Man">Diamond Hoo Ha Man</a>" as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamond_Hoo_Ha_Men" title="Diamond Hoo Ha Men">Diamond Hoo Ha Men</a>, with a run of small club shows in December and January. To celebrate the single release, Mick Quinn appeared as Diamond Hoo Ha Man "Biff Hymenn" at the Apple Store, Regent Street, London, marking his return to touring duties on 15 January. <a href="/wiki/Charly_Coombes" title="Charly Coombes">Charly</a> directed <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glange_Fever&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glange Fever (page does not exist)">Glange Fever</a></i> (under pseudonym "Chas Harrison") a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rockumentary" title="Rockumentary">rockumentary</a> which followed their exploits. The single was released as a limited edition, chocolate vinyl 7". The inner photo shows a sign on a striped wall reading "ON A&amp;R" and was taken by fine art photographer <a class="external text" href="http://www.gregallum.co.uk" rel="nofollow">Greg Allum</a> (also responsible for the photography book "<a class="external text" href="http://www.gregallum.co.uk/wordpress/home/books/the-night-shines-like-fireflies/" rel="nofollow">The Night Shines Like Fireflies - A Portrait of Supergrass in Berlin</a>", which documented the recording sessions in Hansa Studios).</p>
<p>In February 2008, the video of their second single "<a href="/wiki/Bad_Blood_(Supergrass_song)" title="Bad Blood (Supergrass song)">Bad Blood</a>" was released on the band's official web-site, winning Best Rock Video at the <a href="/wiki/UK_Music_Video_Awards" title="UK Music Video Awards">UK Music Video Awards</a>, and the single followed on 17 March.</p>
<p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Parlophone" title="Parlophone">Parlophone</a> was taken over by venture capitalist group, <a href="/wiki/Terra_Firma_Capital_Partners" title="Terra Firma Capital Partners">Terra Firma</a>, and Supergrass ended their contract with the label. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rebel_In_You" title="Rebel In You">Rebel In You</a>", final single from the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Hoo_Ha" title="Diamond Hoo Ha">Diamond Hoo Ha</a> album, was released, under licence from Parlophone, on the band's own imprint, 'Supergrass Records'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_career_and_split_.282009.E2.80.932010.29">Independent career and split (2009–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supergrass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Independent career and split (2009–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band headlined <a href="/wiki/Wychwood_Festival" title="Wychwood Festival">Wychwood Festival</a> on 30 May and also <a href="/wiki/Sellindge_Music_Festival" title="Sellindge Music Festival">Sellindge Music Festival</a> (6 June), Provinssirock Festival (13 June) and a short European trek in July at BBK Live (10 July) at Bilbao, Bikini Festival (11 July) in Toulouse, Festival <a href="/wiki/Les_Ardentes" title="Les Ardentes">Les Ardentes</a> (12 July<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>) in Liège (Belgium) and <a href="/wiki/Paredes_de_Coura_Festival" title="Paredes de Coura Festival">Paredes de Coura Festival</a> (30 July) in Portugal. There was also a co-headlining date at 2009's <a href="/wiki/Truck_Festival" title="Truck Festival">Truck Festival</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Ash_(band)" title="Ash (band)">Ash</a>, on 25-26 July at Hill Farm in <a href="/wiki/Steventon,_Oxfordshire" title="Steventon, Oxfordshire">Steventon, Oxfordshire</a>.</p>
<p>On 18 November 2009, drummer Danny Goffey posted on Twitter: "No Mick. You're the problem".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 April 2010, the band announced they were to split up after a series of four farewell shows, with their final gig in Paris on 11 June 2010.</p>
<p>At the time of the split, Supergrass were working on their seventh studio album, tentatively titled <i>Release the Drones</i>. In early 2010, the band revealed that the album had been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Krautrock" title="Krautrock">krautrock</a> bands such as <a href="/wiki/Can_(band)" title="Can (band)">Can</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Drone_music" title="Drone music">drone music</a>, and that the members had swapped instruments on several tracks during its recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNCUT_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNCUT-25">[25]</a></sup> Coombes said of the approach to the album: "This record's actually been very collaborative. It's been cool to try something different and chaotic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clash_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clash-26">[26]</a></sup> Coombes stated that the album was "nearly finished", and it was scheduled for release in May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BT-27">[27]</a></sup> The album remains unfinished and unreleased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facebook_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-facebook-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_projects">Solo projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supergrass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solo projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During 1998, Coombes and Quinn were invited to play on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr_John" title="Dr John">Dr John</a>'s <i>Anutha Zone</i> album (they appear on the track "Voices In My Head"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> whilst Goffey contributed to the debut album by <a href="/wiki/Lodger_(British_band)" title="Lodger (British band)">Lodger</a> (which also featured his partner <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Lowe" title="Pearl Lowe">Pearl Lowe</a> and members of the band <a href="/wiki/Delicatessen_(band)" title="Delicatessen (band)">Delicatessen</a>).</p>
<p>Danny Goffey has also embarked on a solo project between Supergrass engagements called "Van Goffey" which saw tracks being released via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MySpace" title="MySpace">MySpace</a> in August 2006, the first three being "Crack House Blues", "I Feel so Gaye" and "Natalie Loves the F". He plays drums on the charity football song "Born In England" by a collective of musicians called Twisted X, which charted at number 8 in the UK Charts in 2004. Danny Goffey was also a drummer on the 2004 charity single "Do They Know It's Christmas?", along with members of <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Darkness_(band)" title="The Darkness (band)">The Darkness</a> under the name <a href="/wiki/Band_Aid_20" title="Band Aid 20">Band Aid 20</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2008, Coombes joined <a href="/wiki/Foo_Fighters" title="Foo Fighters">Foo Fighters</a> on stage during a show that saluted <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>'s musical career, <a href="/wiki/VH1_Rock_Honors" title="VH1 Rock Honors">VH1 Rock Honors</a>. Coombes performed vocals on The Who's classic song "Bargain".</p>
<p>In 2007 and 2008, while Mick Quinn was still recuperating from his injury, Danny Goffey and Gaz Coombes were performing as the duo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamond_Hoo_Ha_Men" title="Diamond Hoo Ha Men">Diamond Hoo Ha Men</a>, the name taken from the band's sixth album and its lead single (see <a href="/wiki/Supergrass#Recent_years:_2005.E2.80.93present" title="Supergrass">above</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> They appeared in character as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_Diamond" title="Duke Diamond">Duke Diamond</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randy_Hoo_Ha" title="Randy Hoo Ha">Randy Hoo Ha</a> to play gigs at small venues. Gigs included an appearance at the Apple Store on London's Regent Street, which featured Mick Quinn's first appearance in the band since his injury. He appeared on stage introduced by Gaz as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biff_Hymen" title="Biff Hymen">Biff Hymen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goffey and Coombes were members of side-project <a href="/wiki/The_Hotrats" title="The Hotrats">The Hotrats</a> (originally The Hot Rats). They released an album of covers produced by <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Godrich" title="Nigel Godrich">Nigel Godrich</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Turn_Ons" title="Turn Ons">Turn Ons</a></i> on 25 January 2010. A cover of "<a href="/wiki/Drive_My_Car" title="Drive My Car">Drive My Car</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> appears in an advert for Orange, a fragrance by <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Boss" title="Hugo Boss">Hugo Boss</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Since Supergrass announced they were to split, The Hotrats have joined with <a href="/wiki/Air_(French_band)" title="Air (French band)">Air</a> to perform <a href="/wiki/The_Virgin_Suicides_(score)" title="The Virgin Suicides (score)"><i>The Virgin Suicides</i></a> live for the first time, over several concert dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2010 Mick Quinn formed the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DB_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DB Band (page does not exist)">DB Band</a> with former <a href="/wiki/Shake_Appeal" title="Shake Appeal">Shake Appeal</a> bassist Fab Wilson. The band released their first EP "Stranger In The Alps" on 17 September 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> They have toured the Netherlands, France<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and appeared at Oxford's <a href="/wiki/Truck_Festival" title="Truck Festival">Truck Festival</a> in Steventon. The band plan to record a full-length LP for release in 2012.</p>
<p>Gaz Coombes completed his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Here_Come_the_Bombs" title="Here Come the Bombs">Here Come the Bombs</a></i>, at his home studio in Oxford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The album was recorded with Sam Williams, who produced 1995's <i><a href="/wiki/I_Should_Coco" title="I Should Coco">I Should Coco</a></i> for Supergrass, and released on 21 May 2012. Coombes' second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Matador_(Gaz_Coombes_album)" title="Matador (Gaz Coombes album)">Matador</a></i>, was released 26 January 2015, charting at #18 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and nominated for the 2015 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_Music_Prize" title="Mercury Music Prize">Mercury Music Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
