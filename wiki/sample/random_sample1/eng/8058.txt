<p><i><b>Superman: The Animated Series</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/List_of_animated_television_series" title="List of animated television series">animated television series</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> flagship character, <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was produced by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Animation" title="Warner Bros. Animation">Warner Bros. Animation</a> and originally aired on <a href="/wiki/Kids%27_WB" title="Kids' WB">Kids' WB</a> from September 6, 1996 to February 12, 2000. The series was the first of several <a href="/wiki/DC_animated_universe" title="DC animated universe">followups</a> of the acclaimed <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i>, and was equally praised for its thematic complexity, quality animation, maturity and modernization of its title character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Premiering ten years after the 1986 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reboot_(continuity)" title="Reboot (continuity)">reboot</a> of the Superman comic-book character, the animated series paid tribute to both the classic Superman of old and the newer "modern" Superman. For instance, the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Krypton_(comics)" title="Krypton (comics)">Krypton</a> reflects the older idealized version in the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Age_of_Comic_Books" title="Silver Age of Comic Books">Silver Age of Comic Books</a> while the scope of Superman's powers reflects the more restrained contemporary concept as developed by <a href="/wiki/John_Byrne_(comics)" title="John Byrne (comics)">John Byrne</a> in that the superhero has to struggle to perform spectacular feats, while Clark Kent is shown to be openly, if quietly, self-confident (similar to the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a>'s alter-ego, Bruce Wayne, in <i>Batman: The Animated Series</i>).</p>
<p>Midway through the series' run, it was combined with <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Batman_Adventures" title="The New Batman Adventures">The New Batman Adventures</a></i> to become <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Batman/Superman_Adventures" title="The New Batman/Superman Adventures">The New Batman/Superman Adventures</a></i>. The characters of Superman and Batman were then spun off into a new animated series, <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_(TV_series)" title="Justice League (TV series)">Justice League</a></i>, which also featured other popular DC Comics characters, including <a href="/wiki/Wonder_Woman" title="Wonder Woman">Wonder Woman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wally_West" title="Wally West">The Flash</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_(comics)" title="John Stewart (comics)">Green Lantern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martian_Manhunter" title="Martian Manhunter">Martian Manhunter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hawkgirl" title="Hawkgirl">Hawkgirl</a> which spawned a sequel series <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_Unlimited" title="Justice League Unlimited">Justice League Unlimited</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_production">Development and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally producer <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Timm" title="Bruce Timm">Bruce Timm</a> wanted the show to have a more 1940s <a href="/wiki/Fleischer_Studios" title="Fleischer Studios">Fleischer Studios</a> <a href="/wiki/Superman_(1940s_cartoons)" title="Superman (1940s cartoons)"><i>Superman</i>-cartoon</a> feel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Another original character design sheet showed the characters in a stylised 1950s style (not unlike that of the live-action <i><a href="/wiki/Adventures_of_Superman_(TV_series)" title="Adventures of Superman (TV series)">Adventures of Superman</a></i> TV series), suggesting that the producers also considered setting the series during that period, or possibly ending up like <i>Batman: The Animated Series</i> (set during modern times, but with an <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> feel) or as the producers said Gotham was Art Deco with Gothic elements, Metropolis was "Ocean Liner Deco".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As with the first season of <i>Batman</i>, the opening theme sequence of <i>Superman</i> lacked an on-screen title. Also like <i>Batman</i>, the opening theme for <i>Superman</i> lacked any lyrics, instead being an instrumental piece played over various scenes from the series.</p>
<p>One noticeable aspect of the series carried over from Byrne's work was Superman's powers were significantly downplayed compared to his comic book counterpart. Where as in the comic he could lift millions or billions of tons effortlessly, this version struggled lifting trucks, construction equipment, roadways, etc. The writers admit that he was made as strong as story permitted. His durability was also considerably less that while bullets bounced off him, heavier ordnance like high caliber bullets, cannons and missiles caused him pain or discomfort.</p>
<p>In the series, the evil computer <a href="/wiki/Brainiac_(comics)" title="Brainiac (comics)">Brainiac</a> is not only from Krypton, but is portrayed as responsible for preventing the knowledge of Krypton's imminent destruction from reaching its people so as to save himself, rather than be committed in the presumably futile task of saving the population of the planet. In addition, the ship that carries the infant Kal-El to Earth is designed to have a pilot, and the autopilot used instead was programmed to land smoothly upon reaching its destination. This was done so that the ship is in perfect working condition during Superman's adulthood and could be used as his mode of long range transportation in space. Access to Kryptonian technology and artifacts is initially severely restricted, such as the ship containing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phantom_zone" title="Phantom zone">phantom zone</a> projector and Braniac's technology, although Superman later finds a devastated colony in Krypton's solar system with partially salvageable technology, in addition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kara_Zor-El" title="Kara Zor-El">Kara In-Ze</a> in her functioning <a href="/wiki/Cryostasis_(clathrate_hydrates)" title="Cryostasis (clathrate hydrates)">cryostasis</a> capsule.</p>
<p>Season two was originally scheduled to run 26 episodes, but it was extended to 28 episodes in order to accommodate a two-part story featuring <a href="/wiki/Supergirl" title="Supergirl">Supergirl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WizCaged_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WizCaged-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the series features adaptations of much of Superman's <a href="/wiki/Rogues_gallery" title="Rogues gallery">rogues gallery</a>, the writers supplemented the supply of enemies by paying tribute to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby%27s_Fourth_World" title="Jack Kirby's Fourth World">Jack Kirby's Fourth World</a> creations that introduced the villain <a href="/wiki/Darkseid" title="Darkseid">Darkseid</a> to the series as Superman's archenemy. Darkseid had been portrayed as a villain in <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Friends:_The_Legendary_Super_Powers_Show" title="Super Friends: The Legendary Super Powers Show">Super Friends: The Legendary Super Powers Show</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Super_Powers_Team:_Galactic_Guardians" title="The Super Powers Team: Galactic Guardians">The Super Powers Team: Galactic Guardians</a></i> in the 1980s, but in this series, he was closer to the enormously powerful, evil cosmic emperor originally envisioned by Kirby. <a href="/wiki/Corey_Burton" title="Corey Burton">Corey Burton</a>'s voice performance as Brainiac was done in the same cold, <a href="/wiki/Affect_display" title="Affect display">low-affect</a> style as <a href="/wiki/HAL_9000" title="HAL 9000">HAL 9000</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Odyssey" title="Space Odyssey">Space Odyssey</a></i> films, and was also modeled after the 'Control Voice' heard during the opening narration of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Outer_Limits_(1963_TV_series)" title="The Outer Limits (1963 TV series)">The Outer Limits</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_adaptation">Comic adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Comic adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Superman_Adventures" title="Superman Adventures">Superman Adventures</a></div>
<p>As with the majority of shows in <a href="/wiki/DC_animated_universe" title="DC animated universe">DC animated universe</a>, <i>Superman: The Animated Series</i> received a <a href="/wiki/DC_animated_universe_(comics)" title="DC animated universe (comics)">comic adaptation taking place in the same universe</a>, that ran from 1996 to 2001, with 68 issues, an annual and a special issue featuring Lobo. Paul Dini wrote the first issue of the series, followed by Scott McDaniel, Mark Millar and Evan Dorkin. Among the artists that contrinuited with the series are Ty Templeton, Rick Burchett, Mike Manley, Aluir Amancio, Min S.Ku and Neil Vokes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Superman: The Animated Series</i> is widely regarded as one of the finest and most faithful adaptations of the character ever. It is considered the animated counterpart/equivalent to <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_The_Animated_Series" title="Batman: The Animated Series">Batman: The Animated Series</a></i>, sharing its unique animation style and adult-oriented approach. It currently holds an 8.2 rating on IMDb ranking 3rd on DCAU's best reviewed shows, behind only <i>Batman</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_League_(TV_series)" title="Justice League (TV series)">Justice League</a></i>, which are listed as 1st and 2nd respectively. In January 2009, <i>Superman</i> ranked #36 on IGN's 'Top 100 Animated Series' list, again listed behind fellow DCAU shows <i>Batman</i> and <i>Justice League</i>, which were ranked #2 and #20 respectively.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DVD_releases">DVD releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: DVD releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much like <i>Batman: The Animated Series</i> and other Warner Bros. cartoons adapted from popular DC Comic books, <i>Superman: The Animated Series</i> was released on DVD January 25, 2005, though it did not receive the same disc transfer as <i>Batman</i> did (the second disc of each volume was given the Side A/B treatment). The DVDs present the series' episodes in their airing order along with special features. Volume Two was released on December 6, 2005 and Volume Three was released on June 20, 2006. On November 24, 2009, <a href="/wiki/Warner_Home_Video" title="Warner Home Video">Warner Home Video</a> released <i>Superman the Complete Animated Series</i>, a 7-disc boxed set that includes all 54 episodes of the series as well as extensive bonus features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvshowsondvd1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-tvshowsondvd1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Direct-To-DVD feature, <i><a href="/wiki/Superman:_Brainiac_Attacks" title="Superman: Brainiac Attacks">Superman: Brainiac Attacks</a></i> was released in 2006, although it is not considered to be part of DCAU continuity, despite featuring the same character designs as <i>Superman: The Animated Series</i>, as well as both <a href="/wiki/Tim_Daly" title="Tim Daly">Tim Daly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dana_Delany" title="Dana Delany">Dana Delany</a> reprising their voice roles as Superman and Lois Lane, respectively.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deleted_scene_in_DVD_release">Deleted scene in DVD release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Deleted scene in DVD release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apokolips...Now!(Superman:_The_Animated_Series)" title="Apokolips...Now!(Superman: The Animated Series)">Apokolips...Now! Part II</a>" was later altered from its original airing on February 7, 1998. Originally the <a href="/wiki/Dan_Turpin" title="Dan Turpin">Dan Turpin</a> funeral at the episode's end was a true homage to late New Gods creator <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a> and featured several of his comic creations as attendees to the funeral including <a href="/wiki/Nick_Fury" title="Nick Fury">Nick Fury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Barda" title="Big Barda">Big Barda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mister_Miracle" title="Mister Miracle">Scott Free</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orion_(comics)" title="Orion (comics)">Orion</a> and others, alongside Kirby's friends and fans <a href="/wiki/Mark_Evanier" title="Mark Evanier">Mark Evanier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Timm" title="Bruce Timm">Bruce Timm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dini" title="Paul Dini">Paul Dini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Ross" title="Alex Ross">Alex Ross</a>, his father Norman Ross and <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>. These characters and persons were later removed and the scene pacing was re-edited for subsequent airings and its DVD release on <i>Superman: The Animated Series</i> Volume 3 Disc 3. The original sketches for this scene can be found at Michael Eury's book <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Fcm4JrX-F54C&amp;pg=PA226" rel="nofollow"><i>The Krypton Companion</i></a> published by TwoMorrow's Publishing (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/1893905616">ISBN 1-893905-61-6</a>). Neither DC nor Warner ever commented on the decision to alter this particular scene, but it's been speculated that copyright issues regarding the use of the likenesses of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> characters and the long-time rivalry might have motivated the deletion.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with <i>Batman: The Animated Series</i>, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Walker" title="Shirley Walker">Shirley Walker</a> was in charge of the series' music. Walker wrote the series theme and composed themes for various characters, as well as scoring six episodes herself – "Father's Day," "The Hand of Fate," "Obsession," "Absolute Power" and the two-parter "Legacy." Unlike the previous series, only four other composers worked on the series – <a href="/wiki/Kristopher_Carter" title="Kristopher Carter">Kristopher Carter</a>, Harvey R. Cohen, Michael McCuistion and <a href="/wiki/Lolita_Ritmanis" title="Lolita Ritmanis">Lolita Ritmanis</a>. Almost every episode had a completely original score; very few reused music from previous shows, and only one episode ("Superman's Pal," credited to Carter, McCuistion, Ritmanis and Walker) was entirely "scored" with tracked material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 28, 2014, <a href="/wiki/La-La_Land_Records" title="La-La Land Records">La-La Land Records</a> released a 4-disc compilation of music from the series, collecting 20 complete episode scores, including those of the "World's Finest" three-parter, the "Apokolips... Now!" two-parter, the "Little Girl Lost" two-parter and "In Brightest Day...". It is a limited edition release of 3000 units and can be purchased through the label's website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superman:_The_Animated_Series&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Superman_(1999_video_game)" title="Superman (1999 video game)">Superman 64</a></i>, released for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a> console in 1999, was the first video game to be produced based upon the series, however it is considered to be one of the worst Superman video games and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games_notable_for_negative_reception" title="Video games notable for negative reception">worst games ever</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameRankings_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-GameRankings-10">[10]</a></sup> A second video game, <i><a href="/wiki/Superman:_Shadow_of_Apokolips" title="Superman: Shadow of Apokolips">Superman: Shadow of Apokolips</a></i> was released in 2002 for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/GameCube" title="GameCube">GameCube</a> consoles. It was produced by a different company, and was described as "a respectable but average superhero game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
