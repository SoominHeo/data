<p><b>Supercell Oy</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_game" title="Mobile game">mobile game</a> development company founded in June 2010 in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. The company's debut game was the browser game <i>Gunshine.net</i>, and after its release in 2011, Supercell started developing games for mobile devices. Since then, the company has fully released four mobile games: <i><a href="/wiki/Hay_Day" title="Hay Day">Hay Day</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_of_Clans" title="Clash of Clans">Clash of Clans</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boom_Beach" title="Boom Beach">Boom Beach</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_Royale" title="Clash Royale">Clash Royale</a></i>, which are <a href="/wiki/Freemium" title="Freemium">freemium</a> games and have been very successful for the company, the first two generating revenue of $2.4 million a day in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Accel_Partners" title="Accel Partners">Accel Partners</a> invested $12 million in Supercell in 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and in October 2013 it was announced that the Japanese company <a href="/wiki/GungHo_Online_Entertainment" title="GungHo Online Entertainment">GungHo Online Entertainment</a> and its parent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SoftBank_Corp." title="SoftBank Corp.">SoftBank</a> had acquired 51% of the company for a reported $2.1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On June 1, 2015, SoftBank acquired an additional 22.7% stake in Supercell, which brought their total stake to 73.2% of the company and made them the sole external shareholder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2016, Supercell reported annual revenues of around €2.11 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kl_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-kl-2">[2]</a></sup> In three years, the company's revenues have grown a total of 800 percent, from 78.4 million (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kl_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-kl-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kl2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kl2-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 21, 2016 <a href="/wiki/Tencent" title="Tencent">Tencent</a> announced a deal to acquire 84.3% of Supercell with USD 8.6 billion. Japan's SoftBank values Supercell at $10.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osawa_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osawa-9">[9]</a></sup> On 8 December 2016 Supercell launched a Web Animated Series <a href="/wiki/Clash-A-Rama" title="Clash-A-Rama">Clash-A-Rama</a> for promoting the Clash Universe.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Company">Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supercell was part of the giant Japanese telecommunications company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SoftBank" title="SoftBank">SoftBank</a>, which owned 73.2% of the company. Ilkka Paananen owns 6% and Mikko Kodisoja 5.4% of the shares. The rest belong to the employees and other founders. Originally, SoftBank bought 51% of the company together with the game company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GungHo" title="GungHo">GungHo</a> for 1.1 billion euros in 2013. In the sale, all Supercell's employees sold the same amount of their shares. One condition for the sale was that the main office would stay in Finland. SoftBank and GungHo founded the Kahon 3 Inc. in Finland. The company's articles of incorporation state that the company will remain Finnish. Kahon bought Supercell, and later GungHo sold its shares to SoftBank. In May 2015, SoftBank finally bought the rest of the investors out of the company and increased its shares to 73.2%. At that time the technology magazine <a href="/wiki/VentureBeat" title="VentureBeat">VentureBeat</a> estimated the value of Supercell to be 4.9 billion euros. In March 2016, SoftBank owned over 75% of Supercell's shares. Supercell's main office is located in the former facilities of the <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> research center in Helsinki's <a href="/wiki/Ruoholahti" title="Ruoholahti">Ruoholahti</a> district. The company has offices also in Tokyo, Beijing, San Francisco, and Seoul.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_model">Business model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Business model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supercell focuses on the development of free-to-play games that yield profits through the in-game micropayments. The company's objective is to focus on the successful games that stay popular for years. The focus has not been on revenue, but on the principle "just design something great, something that users love."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> Game development focuses around "cells" of five to seven people which start with idea generation and an initial review by CEO Paananen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> The team subsequently develops the idea into a game, which the rest of the company's employees get to play-test, followed by play-testing in Canada's <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> App store; if the Canada reception is good, the next step is global rollout (via iTunes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> Supercell has discontinued a total of 14 game projects it did not deem promising enough,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> successful failures which are celebrated by employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> One of the games that was cancelled well into development was <i>Battle Buddies</i> which had also been rated well in the test market, but the amount of players was still too small. The final decision for cancelling a project is done by the development team themselves.</p>
<p>Supercell has only 180 employees, which is a relatively small amount compared to its revenue, as even the company's biggest rival <a href="/wiki/King_(company)" title="King (company)">King</a> has 1,400 employees. Supercell also does not have middle management, so the organisation is flat. Even though the teams have a lot of independence and freedom, due to the lack of directors they have a lot of responsibility for their result. New employees are on a four-month probation, and the turnover rate has been great.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_business">Development of business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development of business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2012, Supercell's revenue on Apple's <a href="/wiki/App_Store_(iOS)" title="App Store (iOS)">App Store</a> was greater than any other developer's. From 2013 to 2015, the revenue increased by 562% and by 198% in the next annual period, which is why the company's revenue had increased into 1.5 billion euros. In 2014, the company made into the top-50 of Finland's largest companies. In 2015, the revenue increased by 36% and the profits by 60%, and the company also bought shares and options from its employees for 114 million euros. Supercell does not use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_shelters" title="Tax shelters">tax shelters</a>, and pays all of its taxes to Finland. In 2013, the company and its founders paid a combined 260 million as corporate and income taxes, which was 0.6 percent of Finland's tax revenue that year. In the following year, the company paid 150 million in taxes, and 176 million euros of corporate taxes in 2015.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity">Charity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Charity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The employees of Supercell donated 3.4 million euros to the Finnish New Children's Hospital project. It is presumably the largest charity donation done by company employees in the history of Finland. Supercell has also given donations to the American charity organisation <a href="/wiki/Watsi" title="Watsi">Watsi</a>.</p>
<p>The company founders Mikko Kodisoja and Ilkka Paananen have founded the ME foundation that supports different kinds of charity projects. In November 2015, the ME foundation donated 2.5 million euros to the Helsinki youth department for the support of young immigrants. Supercell also organised a fundraising that collected old computers and tablets from game companies to donate to poor families with children through the Finnish Tukikummit Foundation, a registered non-profit organisation. Supercell was also the biggest individual investor in the <a href="/wiki/Crowdfunding" title="Crowdfunding">crowdfunding</a> campaign for the Finnish Game Museum in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_founding">Background and founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Background and founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before founding Supercell, two of the founders Mikko Kodisoja and Ilkka Paananen worked at the mobile game company Sumea. Kodisoja was one of the founders of Sumea in 1999, and Paananen was hired as the company's CEO in 2000. In 2003, Sumea gained 1.2 million euros of profit. In the following year, the American <a href="/wiki/Digital_Chocolate" title="Digital Chocolate">Digital Chocolate</a> bought Sumea and made the company its Finnish headquarters and Paananen the European manager. With the new owner, the business activity increased, but at the same time the business culture took a more problematic direction according to Kodisoja, who was the creative director at the time. He left the company in 2010, and soon after Paananen did so as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>At first, Paananen worked at the financier company Lifeline Ventures, but soon wanted to become an entrepreneur himself. He planned to found a game company where executives would not disturb the work of the game developers. Together, Paananen, Kodisoja, Petri Styrman, Lassi Leppinen, Visa Forstén, and Niko Derome who had known each other through work connections, founded Supercell in 2010. The company started its business in a cramped office in the <a href="/wiki/Niittykumpu" title="Niittykumpu">Niittykumpu</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Espoo" title="Espoo">Espoo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lappalainen20152_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-lappalainen20152-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kodisoja and Paananen invested 250,000 euros from their savings from their time at Sumea in the company. In addition, the Finnish funding agency for technology innovation <a href="/wiki/Tekes_(agency)" title="Tekes (agency)">Tekes</a> gave them a loan of 400,000 euros. Lifeline Ventures also became an investor. In the following October, Supercell raised 750,000 euros through seed funding from for example London Venture Partners and Initial Capital. The first game Supercell started to develop was the massive multiplayer online game <i>Gunshine</i> that could be played on <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> with a browser or on mobile platforms, and the game's prototype was ready in eight months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lappalainen20152_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-lappalainen20152-13">[13]</a></sup> After <i>Gunshine's</i> completion, <a href="/wiki/Accel_Partners" title="Accel Partners">Accel Partners</a> also invested 8 million euros in the company in May 2011, and shareholder Kevin Comolli became a member of Supercell's board of directors. Accel has also invested in <a href="/wiki/Rovio_Entertainment" title="Rovio Entertainment">Rovio</a>, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_of_strategies">Change of strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Change of strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2011, Supercell abandoned <i>Gunshine</i> for three reasons: it did not interest players for long enough, it was too difficult to play, and the mobile version did not work as well as the browser version. At best, the game had approximately half a million players. Supercell noticed the company could not catch up to the current leader of Facebook games, the <a href="/wiki/Zynga" title="Zynga">Zynga</a> company, and decided to focus on <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a> games. Another Facebook game on development was left unfinished as well. The change of direction did not drive away the investors, but the pressure to succeed became worse, and Paananen had to make more detailed reports of progress for the investors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lappalainen20152_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-lappalainen20152-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company simultaneously developed five games, and the first one to be released for test market was <i>Pets vs Orcs</i>. It was not very successful, and another concept, <i>Tower</i>, was abandoned soon as well. However, in May 2012 <i><a href="/wiki/Hay_Day" title="Hay Day">Hay Day</a></i> was published, and was quite promising in the test market. The game eventually became Supercell's first internationally released game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lappalainen20152_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-lappalainen20152-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Hay Day</i> was Supercell's version of Zynga's successful Facebook game <i><a href="/wiki/FarmVille" title="FarmVille">FarmVille</a></i>, an easy-to-play farm simulator. Supercell added to their farming simulator the ability to refine products, a production chain, and touch screen properties. The social aspect of the game was emphasised as well. The optimisation of the production chains had an important role in the game, and it could be sped up with in-game purchases. The game was published in Midsummer 2012, and it became a surprising success to the point the server capacity was not enough to support all players. There were also problems with balancing the game, as in the beginning all players had an oversupply of eggs, which made the in-game market depressed. The company solved the problem by adding more eggs as necessary ingredients in recipes. To avoid similar problems in the future, Supercell hired a data analyst who monitors the balance in the games. In four months, the game became one of the most profitable games in Apple's App Store in the US, and was one of the most profitable in the world for two and a half years. The game receives regular updates and is maintained by a team of 14 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_Clash_of_Clans">Development of <i>Clash of Clans</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Development of Clash of Clans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The employees of Supercell had a lot of experience from the video game industry. Lasse Louhento had started at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloodhouse" title="Bloodhouse">Bloodhouse</a>, and Lassi Leppinen was the chief programmer at Sumea and Digital Chocolate. Their team had spent months on a fantasy themed Facebook game when Supercell changed strategies. Leppinen and Louhento wanted to make a strategy game that would utilise a touch screen so playing would be as simple and pleasant as possible. The development of <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_of_Clans" title="Clash of Clans">Clash of Clans</a></i> took six months, and the game was released in August 2012. In three months, it became the most profitable app in the US. According to <a href="/wiki/App_Annie" title="App Annie">App Annie</a>, in the years 2013 and 2014 <i>Clash of Clans</i> was the most profitable mobile game in the world. The eponymous battle between the clans was added to the game as late as in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In summer 2013, Supercell started marketing collaboration with the Japanese GungHo: the companies cross-marketed each others' games in their own games in their own markets. As a result, <i>Clash of Clans</i> became one of the most downloaded apps in Japan. GungHo's chairman of the board <a href="/wiki/Taizo_Son" title="Taizo Son">Taizo Son</a> flew to Finland to thank Paananen and later introduced him to his brother <a href="/wiki/Masayoshi_Son" title="Masayoshi Son">Masayoshi Son</a>, the CEO of the SoftBank Corporation. Soon, they proposed a corporate acquisition which indeed happened in October 2013. SoftBank and GungHo bought 51% of Supercell's shares for 1.1 billion euros which is the largest price for a Finnish private company in history. In six months, Supercell's value had tripled, since in spring 2013 the company had sold 16.7% of its shares for 100 million euros.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both <i>Clash of Clans</i> and <i>Hay Day</i> were released in the summer 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> and Supercell did not release a new game in almost two years. The designing of the third game <i><a href="/wiki/Boom_Beach" title="Boom Beach">Boom Beach</a></i> started in the fall of 2012, and it was released in 2014. The new strategy game was released to the test market at the end of 2013, after which it went through large changes. The game was very successful in the US right after its release in March, but it did not stay at the top of the download charts for very long. However, it rose to the top 30 of the most downloaded <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> apps after Supercell started an expensive marketing campaign in December 2014. In 2015, the game surpassed <i>Hay Day</i> in the charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2016, Supercell released its fourth supported game, <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_Royale" title="Clash Royale">Clash Royale</a></i>, which uses similar characters from Clash of Clans. Between the releases of <i>Boom Beach</i> and <i>Clash Royale</i>, Supercell had discontinued nine game projects, two in their test release phase. One of them was <i>Smash Land</i> which had been developed by 4 to 5 people for 10 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLIX" title="Super Bowl XLIX">Super Bowl XLIX</a> in February 2015, Supercell spent $9 million for a 60-second runtime in front of 118.5 million viewers. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, the <i>Clash of Clans</i> advertisement was one of the most popular advertisements of the 61 spots aired on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The commercial, dubbed "Revenge", featured <a href="/wiki/Liam_Neeson" title="Liam Neeson">Liam Neeson</a> parodying his character from the <i><a href="/wiki/Taken_(franchise)" title="Taken (franchise)">Taken</a></i> film series by seeking revenge in a coffee shop for a random player destroying his village. The commercial has reached a total of 137 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> views on the game's official <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> channel so far,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and it was the most watched commercial on YouTube in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Despite the success of the commercial, Supercell has seen only a marginal increase in downloads following the advertisement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acknowledgements">Acknowledgements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell_(video_game_company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Acknowledgements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, Supercell was awarded as the best Nordic start-up company<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and chosen as the Finnish game developer of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The following year, Supercell won the Finnish Teknologiakasvattaja 2013 (Technology Educator 2013) contest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and the company was chosen as the software entrepreneur of the year. In 2014, the research and consultancy agency T-Media chose Supercell as Finland's most reputable company in their Luottamus&amp;Maine (Trust&amp;Reputation) report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
