<p>A <b>supercar</b> is a high-performance <a href="/wiki/Sports_car" title="Sports car">sports car</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grand_tourer" title="Grand tourer">grand tourer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_English_Dictionary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_English_Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term is used in marketing by <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automakers</a> for unusual, high-end vehicles, and has been used to refer to at least four different sorts of cars:</p>
<ul>
<li>Limited-production specials from an "elite" automaker<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheetham_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheetham-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Standard-looking cars modified for power and performance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheetham_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cheetham-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Models that appeal to enthusiasts, from smaller manufacturers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheetham_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cheetham-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>One-of-a-kind "showcase" project vehicles built by <a href="/wiki/Custom_car" title="Custom car">custom car</a> retrofitters (usually extensively modified collectible <a href="/wiki/Muscle_car" title="Muscle car">muscle cars</a> or grand tourers updated to the latest "<a href="/wiki/Street-legal_vehicle" title="Street-legal vehicle">streetable</a>" racing technology).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An advertisement for the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ensign_6&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ensign 6 (page does not exist)">Ensign 6</a>, a 6.7 L (410 cu in) high-performance car similar to the <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Speed_Six" title="Bentley Speed Six">Bentley Speed Six</a>, appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> for 11 November 1920 with the phrase "If you are interested in a supercar, you cannot afford to ignore the claims of the Ensign 6."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> also cites the use of the word in an advertisement for an unnamed car in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Motor_(magazine)" title="The Motor (magazine)">The Motor</a></i> dated 3 November 1920, "The Supreme development of the British super-car."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and defines the phrase as suggesting "a car superior to all others". A book published by the Research Institute of America in 1944, that previewed the economic and industrial changes to occur after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> used the term <i>"supercar"</i> (author's emphasis) to describe future automobiles incorporating advances in design and technology such as flat <a href="/wiki/Floorpan" title="Floorpan">floorpans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Automatic_transmission" title="Automatic transmission">automatic transmissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, the term "supercar" predates the classification of <a href="/wiki/Muscle_car" title="Muscle car">muscle car</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> to describe the "<a href="/wiki/Dragstrip" title="Dragstrip">dragstrip</a> bred" affordable <a href="/wiki/Mid-size_car" title="Mid-size car">mid-size cars</a> of the 1960s and early 1970s that were equipped with large, powerful <a href="/wiki/V8_engine" title="V8 engine">V8 engines</a> and rear wheel drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The combination of a potent engine in a lightweight car began with the 1957 <a href="/wiki/Rambler_Rebel" title="Rambler Rebel">Rambler Rebel</a> that was described as a "veritable supercar".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> "In 1966 the sixties supercar became an official industry trend"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> as the four domestic automakers "needed to cash in on the supercar market" with eye-catching, heart-stopping cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Among the numerous examples of the use of the supercar description include the May 1965 issue of the American magazine <i>Car Life</i>, in a road test of the <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_GTO" title="Pontiac GTO">Pontiac GTO</a>, as well as how "<a href="/wiki/Hurst_Performance" title="Hurst Performance">Hurst</a> puts <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors</a> into the Supercar club with the 390 Rogue"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (the <a href="/wiki/Rambler_American#SC.2FRambler" title="Rambler American">SC/Rambler</a>) to fight in "the Supercar street racer gang" market segment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The "SC" in the model name stood for "SuperCar".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The supercar market segment included regular production models<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in different muscle market segments (such as the "economy supercar"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>), as well as limited edition, documented dealer-converted vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word supercar later came to mean a "GT" or grand touring type of car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By the 1970s and 1980s the phrase was in regular use, if not precisely defined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the late 20th century, the term supercar was used to describe "a very expensive, fast or powerful car with a centrally located engine",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_English_Dictionary_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_English_Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup> and stated in more general terms: "it must be very fast, with sporting handling to match", "it should be sleek and eye-catching" and its price should be "one in a rarefied atmosphere of its own".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The supercar term has also been applied to technologically advanced vehicles using new fuel sources, powerplants, aerodynamics, and lightweight materials to develop an 80 mpg<sub>‑US</sub> (2.9 L/100 km; 96 mpg<sub>‑imp</sub>) family-sized sedan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> "Supercar" was the unofficial description for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">United States Department of Commerce</a> R&amp;D program, Partnership for a New Generation of Vehicles (PNGV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80mpg_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-80mpg-27">[27]</a></sup> The program was established to support the domestic U.S. automakers (GM, Ford, and Chrysler) develop prototypes of a safe, clean, affordable car the size of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taurus" title="Ford Taurus">Ford Taurus</a>, but delivering three times the fuel efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80mpg_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-80mpg-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
