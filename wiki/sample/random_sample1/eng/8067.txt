<p><i><b>Der Spiegel</b></i> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[deːɐ ˈʃpiːɡəl]</a></span>, lit. <i>"The Mirror"</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> weekly <a href="/wiki/News_magazine" title="News magazine">news magazine</a> published in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. It is one of Europe's largest publications of its kind, with a weekly circulation of 840,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was founded in 1947<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Seymour_Chaloner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Seymour Chaloner (page does not exist)">John Seymour Chaloner</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Seymour_Chaloner" title="de:John Seymour Chaloner">de</a>)</span>, a British army officer, and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Augstein" title="Rudolf Augstein">Rudolf Augstein</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> radio operator who was recognized in 2000 by the <a href="/wiki/International_Press_Institute" title="International Press Institute">International Press Institute</a> as one of the fifty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Press_Freedom_Heroes" title="World Press Freedom Heroes">World Press Freedom Heroes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPI_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPI-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Spiegel_Online" title="Spiegel Online">Spiegel Online</a></i>, the online sibling of <i>Der Spiegel</i>, was launched in 1994 with an independent editorial staff. Typically, the magazine has a content to advertising ratio of 2:1.</p>
<p><i>Der Spiegel</i> is known in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German-speaking</a> countries mostly for its <a href="/wiki/Investigative_journalism" title="Investigative journalism">investigative journalism</a>. It has played a key role in uncovering many political scandals such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiegel_scandal" title="Spiegel scandal">Spiegel scandal</a> in 1962 and the <a href="/wiki/Flick_affair" title="Flick affair">Flick affair</a> in the 1980s. According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, <i>Der Spiegel</i> is one of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a>'s most influential magazines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Spiegel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The first edition of <i>Der Spiegel</i> was published in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a> on Saturday, 4 January 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sixds_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sixds-7">[7]</a></sup> Its release was initiated and sponsored by the British occupational administration and preceded by a magazine titled, <i>Diese Woche</i> (meaning <i>This Week</i> in English),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sixds_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-sixds-7">[7]</a></sup> which had first been published in November 1946. After disagreements with the British, the magazine was handed over to <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Augstein" title="Rudolf Augstein">Rudolf Augstein</a> as chief editor, and was renamed <i>Der Spiegel</i>. From the first edition in January 1947, Augstein held the position of editor-in-chief, which he retained until his death on 7 November 2002.</p>
<p>After 1950, the magazine was owned by Rudolf Augstein and John Jahr; Jahr's share merged with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Gruner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Richard Gruner (page does not exist)">Richard Gruner</a> in 1965 to form the publishing company <a href="/wiki/Gruner_%2B_Jahr" title="Gruner + Jahr">Gruner + Jahr</a>. In 1969, Augstein bought out Gruner + Jahr for <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">DM</a> 42 million and became the sole owner of <i>Der Spiegel</i>. In 1971, Gruner + Jahr bought back a 25% share in the magazine. In 1974, Augstein restructured the company to make the employees shareholders. All employees with more than three years seniority were offered the opportunity to become an associate and participate in the management of the company, as well as in the profits.</p>
<p>Since 1952, <i>Der Spiegel</i> has been headquartered in its own building in the old town part of Hamburg.</p>
<p><i>Der Spiegel'</i>s circulation rose quickly. From 15,000 copies in 1947, it grew to 65,000 in 1948 and 437,000 in 1961. It was nearly 500,000 copies in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ser_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ser-8">[8]</a></sup> By the 1970s, it had reached a plateau at about 900,000 copies. When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_re-unification" title="German re-unification">German re-unification</a> in 1990 made it available to a new readership in former <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, the circulation exceeded one million.</p>
<p>The magazine's influence is based on two pillars; firstly the <a href="/wiki/Moral_authority" title="Moral authority">moral authority</a> established by investigative journalism since the early years and proven alive by several impressive scoops during the 1980s; secondly the economic power of the prolific <i>Spiegel</i> publishing house. Since 1988, it has produced the TV programme <i>Spiegel TV</i>, and further diversified during the 1990s.</p>
<p>During the second quarter of 1992 the circulation of <i>Der Spiegel</i> was 1.1 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1994, <i><a href="/wiki/Spiegel_Online" title="Spiegel Online">Spiegel Online</a></i> was launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chi5_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-chi5-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It has separate and independent editorial staff from <i>Der Spiegel</i>. In 1999 the circulation of <i>Der Spiegel</i> was 1,061,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Der Spiegel</i> had an average circulation of 1,076,000 copies in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2007 the magazine started a new regional <a href="/wiki/Supplement_(publishing)" title="Supplement (publishing)">supplement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dertag_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-dertag-14">[14]</a></sup> It was the first regional supplement of the magazine which covers 50-page review of Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dertag_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-dertag-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010 <i>Der Spiegel</i> was employing the equivalent of 80 full-time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fact_checker" title="Fact checker">fact checkers</a>, which the <i><a href="/wiki/Columbia_Journalism_Review" title="Columbia Journalism Review">Columbia Journalism Review</a></i> called "most likely the world's largest fact checking operation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The same year it was the third best-selling general interest magazine in Europe with a circulation of 1,016,373 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Spiegel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Aust" title="Stefan Aust">Stefan Aust</a> took over in 1994, the magazine's readers realised that his personality was different from his predecessor. In 2005, a documentary by Stephan Lamby quoted him as follows: "We stand at a very big cannon!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Politicians of all stripes who had to deal with the magazine's attention often voiced their disaffection for it. The outspoken conservative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Strau%C3%9F" title="Franz Josef Strauß">Franz Josef Strauß</a> contended that <i>Der Spiegel</i> was "the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> of our time". He referred to journalists in general as "rats".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Social Democrat <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a> called it "Scheißblatt" (i.e., a "shit paper") during his term in office as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(Federal_Republic)" title="Chancellor of Germany (Federal Republic)">Chancellor</a>.</p>
<p><i>Der Spiegel</i> often produces feature-length articles on problems affecting Germany (like demographic trends, the federal system's gridlock or the issues of its education system) and describes optional strategies and their risks in depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The magazine plays the role of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opinion_leader" title="Opinion leader">opinion leader</a> in the German press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigative_journalism">Investigative journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Spiegel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Investigative journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Der Spiegel</i> has a distinctive reputation for revealing political misconduct and scandals. Online Encyclopædia Britannica emphasizes this quality of the magazine as follows: "The magazine is renowned for its aggressive, vigorous, and well-written exposes of government malpractice and scandals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chi5_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-chi5-10">[10]</a></sup> It merited recognition for this as early as 1950, when the federal parliament launched an inquiry into <i>Spiegel</i><span style="padding-left:0.2em;">'</span>s accusations that bribed members of parliament had promoted <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a> over <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> as the seat of West Germany's government.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiegel_scandal" title="Spiegel scandal">Spiegel scandal</a> in 1962, which followed the release of a report about the possibly low state of readiness of the <a href="/wiki/Bundeswehr" title="Bundeswehr">German armed forces</a>, minister of defence and conservative figurehead <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Strau%C3%9F" title="Franz Josef Strauß">Franz Josef Strauß</a> had <i>Der Spiegel</i> investigated. In the course of this investigation, the editorial offices were raided by police while Rudolf Augstein and other <i>Der Spiegel</i> editors were arrested on charges of treason. Despite a lack of sufficient authority, Strauß even went after the article's author, Conrad Ahlers, who was consequently arrested in Spain where he was on holiday. When the legal case collapsed, the scandal led to a major shake-up in chancellor <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>'s cabinet and Strauß had to stand down. The affair was generally received as an attack on the freedom of the press. Since then, <i>Der Spiegel</i> has repeatedly played a significant role in revealing political grievances and misdeeds, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flick_Affair" title="Flick Affair">Flick Affair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ser_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ser-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Spiegel scandal is now remembered for altering the political culture of post-war Germany and—with the first mass demonstrations and public protests—being a turning point from the old Obrigkeitsstaat (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authoritarian" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a> state) to a modern <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>.</p>
<p>In 2010, the magazine supported <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> in publishing <a href="/wiki/United_States_diplomatic_cables_leak" title="United States diplomatic cables leak">leaked materials from the United States State Department</a>, along with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/El_Pa%C3%ADs" title="El País">El País</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Monde" title="Le Monde">Le Monde</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and in October 2013 with the help of former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSA" title="NSA">NSA</a> contractor <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">Edward Snowden</a> unveiled the systematic wiretapping of <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor of Germany</a> <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>'s private cell phone over a period of over 10 years at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Special_Collection_Service" title="Special Collection Service">Special Collection Service</a> (SCS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The leading role of the magazine in investigative journalism and its monopoly came to end in 2013 since other German media outlets, including <i><a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCddeutsche_Zeitung" title="Süddeutsche Zeitung">Süddeutsche Zeitung</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bild" title="Bild">Bild</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/ARD_(broadcaster)" title="ARD (broadcaster)">ARD</a> and <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">ZDF</a>, began to effectively deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_scandals" title="Political scandals">political scandals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt29_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt29-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Spiegel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>One of the main points of criticism that has been brought against <i>Der Spiegel</i> concerns the use of language in the magazine. In 1957, writer <a href="/wiki/Hans_Magnus_Enzensberger" title="Hans Magnus Enzensberger">Hans Magnus Enzensberger</a> published his essay <i>Die Sprache des Spiegels</i> (“The Language of Der Spiegel”), in which he criticised what he called a "pretended objectivity". Wolf Schneider, an eminent journalist and stylist has called <i>Der Spiegel</i> "the biggest mangler of the German language" and used quotations from the magazine as examples of inept German in his style guides. Their criticism was not so much one of linguistic <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> as an argument that <i>Der Spiegel</i> "hides and distorts its actual topics and issues by manipulative semantics and rhetoric rather than by reporting and analysing them". In 1957, however, Enzensberger admitted in a written statement that no other contemporary German magazine attained the <i>Spiegel</i><span style="padding-left:0.2em;">'</span>s level of objectivity.</p>
<p>Opinions about the level of language employed by <i>Der Spiegel</i> changed in the late 1990s. After hiring many of Germany's best feature writers, <i>Der Spiegel</i> has become known for its "Edelfedern" ("noble quills"—wordsmiths). The magazine frequently wins the <a href="/wiki/Egon_Erwin_Kisch_Prize" title="Egon Erwin Kisch Prize">Egon Erwin Kisch Prize</a> for the best German feature. <i>Der Spiegel</i> ended up joining the ranks of the guardians of proper grammar and jargon with the <i>Zwiebelfisch</i> ("(printer's) pie") column on the magazine's website, which has even produced several best-selling books.</p>
<p>Some critics, in particular the media historian <a href="/wiki/Lutz_Hachmeister" title="Lutz Hachmeister">Lutz Hachmeister</a> and the Augstein biographer and former <i>Der Spiegel</i> author Otto Köhler, have brought charges against the magazine's dealings with former Nazis, even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">SS</a> officers. Allegedly, <i>Der Spiegel</i>, which at other times showed no restraint when exposing the Nazi past of public figures, distorted history and covered up for criminals after enlisting insiders hired to write about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> topics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its early reports and serials about the <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_fire" title="Reichstag fire">Reichstag fire</a>, written by former SS officers <a href="/wiki/Paul_Carell" title="Paul Carell">Paul Carell</a> (who had also served as chief press spokesman for Nazi Germany's Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Joachim_von_Ribbentrop" title="Joachim von Ribbentrop">Joachim von Ribbentrop</a>) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fritz_Tobias&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fritz Tobias (page does not exist)">Fritz Tobias</a>, have since been considered influential in historiography because since the 1960s the <i>Spiegel</i> reports written by these two authors have been corroborated by authoritative historian <a href="/wiki/Hans_Mommsen" title="Hans Mommsen">Hans Mommsen</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bans">Bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Spiegel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A special 25 March edition of <i>Der Spiegel</i> on <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> was banned in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in April 2008 for publishing material deemed by authorities to be insulting Islam and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophet_Muhammed" title="Prophet Muhammed">Prophet Muhammed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_office">Head office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Der_Spiegel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Head office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Der Spiegel</i> began moving into its current head office, HafenCity, in September 2011. The facility was designed by Henning Larsen Architects of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. The magazine was previously located in a high-rise building with 8,226 square metres (88,540 sq ft) of office space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
