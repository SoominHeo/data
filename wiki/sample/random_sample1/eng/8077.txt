<p><b>Snowboarding</b> is a recreational activity and Olympic and Paralympic sport that involves descending a snow-covered slope while standing on a <a href="/wiki/Snowboard" title="Snowboard">snowboard</a> attached to a rider's feet.</p>
<p>The development of snowboarding was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Skateboarding" title="Skateboarding">skateboarding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sledding" title="Sledding">sledding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>. It was developed in the United States in the 1960s, became a <a href="/wiki/Winter_Olympic_Games" title="Winter Olympic Games">Winter Olympic Sport</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nagano,_Nagano" title="Nagano, Nagano">Nagano</a> in <a href="/wiki/1998_Winter_Olympics" title="1998 Winter Olympics">1998</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and first featured in the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Paralympic_Games" title="Winter Paralympic Games">Winter Paralympics</a> at <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">Sochi</a> in <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Paralympics" title="2014 Winter Paralympics">2014</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paralympics_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paralympics-2">[2]</a></sup> Its popularity (as measured by equipment sales) in the United States peaked in 2007 and has been in a decline since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Modern snowboarding began in 1965 when Sherman Poppen, an engineer in <a href="/wiki/Muskegon,_Michigan" title="Muskegon, Michigan">Muskegon, Michigan</a>, invented a toy for his daughters by fastening two skis together and attaching a rope to one end so he would have some control as they stood on the board and glided downhill. Dubbed the "<a href="/wiki/Snurfer" title="Snurfer">snurfer</a>" (combining snow and surfer) by his wife Nancy, the toy proved so popular among his daughters' friends that Poppen licensed the idea to a manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_Corporation" title="Brunswick Corporation">Brunswick Corporation</a>, that sold about a million snurfers over the next decade. And, in 1966 alone over half a million snurfers were sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1970s, Poppen organized snurfing competitions at a Michigan ski resort that attracted enthusiasts from all over the country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One of those early pioneers was <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sims" title="Tom Sims">Tom Sims</a>, a devotee of <a href="/wiki/Skateboarding" title="Skateboarding">skateboarding</a> (a sport born in the 1950s when kids attached roller skate wheels to small boards that they steered by shifting their weight). As an eighth grader in Haddonfield, New Jersey, in the 1960s, Sims crafted a snowboard in his school shop class by gluing carpet to the top of a piece of wood and attaching aluminum sheeting to the bottom.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He produced commercial snowboards in the mid-70s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Articles about his invention in such mainstream magazines as <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> helped publicize the young sport.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The pioneers were not all from the United States; in 1976, Welsh skateboard enthusiasts Jon Roberts and Pete Matthews developed their own snowboards to use at their local dry ski slope.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Also during this same period, in 1977, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Burton_Carpenter" title="Jake Burton Carpenter">Jake Burton Carpenter</a>, a Vermont native who had enjoyed snurfing since the age of 14, impressed the crowd at a Michigan snurfing competition with bindings he had designed to secure his feet to the board. That same year, he founded <a href="/wiki/Burton_Snowboards" title="Burton Snowboards">Burton Snowboards</a> in <a href="/wiki/Londonderry,_Vermont" title="Londonderry, Vermont">Londonderry, Vermont</a>. The "snowboards" were made of wooden planks that were flexible and had water ski foot traps. Very few people picked up snowboarding because the price of the board was considered too high at $38, but eventually Burton would become the biggest snowboarding company in the business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first competitions to offer prize money were the National Snurfing Championship, held at Muskegon State Park in Muskegon Michigan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1979, Jake Burton Carpenter, came from Vermont to compete with a snowboard of his own design. There were protests about Jake entering with a non-snurfer board. Paul Graves, and others, advocated that Jake be allowed to race. A "modified" "Open" division was created and won by Jake as the sole entrant. That race was considered the first competition for snowboards and is the start of what has now become competitive snowboarding. It was also the first competition to offer prize money. Ken Kampenga, John Asmussen and Jim Trim placed 1st, 2nd and 3rd respectively in the Standard competition with best 2 combined times of 24.71, 25.02 and 25.41 and Jake Carpenter won prize money as the sole entrant in the "open" division with a time of 26.35.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1980 the event moved to Pando Winter Sports Park near <a href="/wiki/Grand_Rapids,_Michigan" title="Grand Rapids, Michigan">Grand Rapids, Michigan</a> because of a lack of snow that year at the original venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pando_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pando-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>As snowboarding became more popular in the 1970s and 1980s, pioneers such as Dimitrije Milovich, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Burton_Carpenter" title="Jake Burton Carpenter">Jake Burton Carpenter</a> (founder of Burton Snowboards from Londonderry, Vermont), <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sims" title="Tom Sims">Tom Sims</a> (founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sims_Snowboards" title="Sims Snowboards">Sims Snowboards</a>), and Mike Olson (founder of <a href="/wiki/Mervin_Manufacturing" title="Mervin Manufacturing">Gnu Snowboards</a>) came up with new designs for boards and mechanisms that slowly developed into the snowboards and other related equipment that we know today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firststoke_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-firststoke-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1982, the first USA National Snowboard race was held near Woodstock, Vermont, at <a href="/wiki/Suicide_Six" title="Suicide Six">Suicide Six</a>. The race, organized by Graves, was won by Burton's first team rider Doug Bouton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SBH_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SBH-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1983, the first World Championship <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halfpipe" title="Halfpipe">halfpipe</a> competition was held at Soda Springs, California. <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sims" title="Tom Sims">Tom Sims</a>, founder of Sims Snowboards, organized the event with the help of Mike Chantry, a snowboard instructor at Soda Springs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transworld_article_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Transworld_article-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985, the first World Cup was held in Zürs, Austria, further cementing snowboarding's recognition as an official international competitive sport.</p>
<p>In 1990, the <a href="/wiki/International_Snowboard_Federation" title="International Snowboard Federation">International Snowboard Federation</a> (ISF) was founded to provide universal contest regulations. In addition, the United States of America Snowboard Association (USASA) provides instructing guidelines and runs snowboard competitions in the U.S. today, high-profile snowboarding events like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_X_Games" title="Winter X Games">Winter X Games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_%26_Style" title="Air &amp; Style">Air &amp; Style</a>, US Open, Olympic Games and other events are broadcast worldwide. Many alpine resorts have <a href="/wiki/Terrain_park" title="Terrain park">terrain parks</a>.</p>
<p>At the 1998 Winter Olympic Games in Nagano, Japan, Snowboarding became an official Olympic event. France's <a href="/wiki/Karine_Ruby" title="Karine Ruby">Karine Ruby</a> was the first ever to win an Olympic gold medal for Woman's Snowboarding at the 1998 Olympics, while Canadian Ross Rebagliati<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> was the first ever to win an Olympic gold medal for Men's Snowboarding.</p>
<p>Initially, ski areas adopted the sport at a much slower pace than the winter sports public. Indeed, for many years, there was animosity between skiers and snowboarders, which led to an ongoing skier vs snowboarder feud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Early snowboards were banned from the slopes by park officials. For several years snowboarders would have to take a small skills assessment prior to being allowed to ride the chairlifts. It was thought that an unskilled snowboarder would wipe the snow off the mountain. In 1985, only seven percent of U.S. ski areas allowed snowboarding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-15">[15]</a></sup> with a similar proportion in Europe. As equipment and skills improved, gradually snowboarding became more accepted. In 1990, most major ski areas had separate slopes for snowboarders. Now, approximately 97% of all ski areas in North America and Europe allow snowboarding, and more than half have jumps, rails and half pipes.</p>
<p>An excellent year for snowboarding was 2004, with 6.6 million participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USN080929_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-USN080929-16">[16]</a></sup> An industry spokesman said that "twelve year-olds are out-riding adults." The same article said that most snowboarders are 18–24 years old and that females constitute 25% of participants.</p>
<p>There were 8.2 million snowboarders in the USA and Canada for the 2009-2010 season. There was a 10% increase over the previous season, accounting for more than 30% of all snow sports participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 2 May 2012, the <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a> announced that adaptive snowboarding (dubbed "para-snowboarding") would debut as a men's and women's medal event in the 2014 Paralympic Winter Games taking place in Sochi, Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles">Styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since snowboarding's inception as an established winter sport, it has developed various styles, each with its own specialized equipment and technique. The most common styles today are: freeride, freestyle, and freecarve/race. These styles are used for both recreational and professional snowboarding. While each style is unique, there is overlap between them. <i>See also <a href="/wiki/List_of_snowboard_tricks" title="List of snowboard tricks">List of snowboard tricks</a>.</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jibbing">Jibbing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jibbing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Jibbing" is technical riding on non-standard surfaces, which usually includes performing tricks. The word "jib" is both a noun and a verb, depending on the usage of the word. As a noun: a jib includes metal rails, boxes, benches, concrete ledges, walls, vehicles, rocks and logs. As a verb: to jib is referring to the action of jumping, sliding or riding on top of objects other than snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It is directly influenced by grinding a skateboard. Jibbing is a freestyle snowboarding technique of riding. Typically jibbing occurs in a snowboard resort park but can also be done in urban environments.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freestyle">Freestyle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Freestyle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freestyle snowboarding is any riding that includes performing tricks. In freestyle, the rider utilizes natural and man-made features such as rails, jumps, boxes, and innumerable others to perform tricks. It is a popular all-inclusive concept that distinguishes the creative aspects of snowboarding, in contrast to a style like alpine snowboarding.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alpine_Snowboarding">Alpine Snowboarding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alpine Snowboarding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><b>Alpine snowboarding</b> is a discipline within the sport of snowboarding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is practised on <a href="/wiki/Snow_grooming" title="Snow grooming">groomed</a> <a href="/wiki/Piste" title="Piste">pistes</a>. It has been an Olympic event since <a href="/wiki/Snowboarding_at_the_1998_Winter_Olympics" title="Snowboarding at the 1998 Winter Olympics">1998</a>.</p>
<p>Sometimes called freecarving, this takes place on hard packed snow or groomed runs and focuses on carving linked turns, much like surfing or longboarding. Little or no jumping takes place in this discipline. Alpine Snowboarding consists of a small portion of the general snowboard population, that has a well connected social community and its own specific board manufacturers. Alpine Snowboard equipment is a ski-like hardshell boot and plate binding system with a true directional snowboard that is stiffer and narrower to manage linking turns with greater forces and speed. Shaped skis can thank these "freecarve" snowboards for the cutting-edge technology leading to their creation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bomberonline_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bomberonline-21">[21]</a></sup> A skilled alpine snowboarder can link numerous turns into a run placing their body very close to the ground each turn, similar to a motogp turn or waterski carve. Depending on factors including stiffness, turning radius and personality this can be done slowly or fast. Carvers make perfect half-circles out of each turn, changing edges when the snowboard is perpendicular to the fall line and starting every turn on the downhill edge. Carving on a snowboard is like riding a roller coaster, because the board will lock into a turn radius and provide what feels like multiple Gs of acceleration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alpine snowboarding shares more visual similarities with skiing equipment than it does with snowboarding equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-help_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-help-23">[23]</a></sup> Compared to freestyle snowboarding gear:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>boards are narrower, longer, and stiffer to improve <a href="/wiki/Carve_turn#Snowboarding" title="Carve turn">carving</a> performance</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ski_boot" title="Ski boot">boots</a> are made from a hard plastic shell</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ski_binding" title="Ski binding">bindings</a> have a bail or step-in design and are sometimes placed on suspension plates to provide a layer of isolation between an alpine snowboarder and the board</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slopestyle">Slopestyle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Slopestyle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Competitors perform tricks while descending a course, moving around, over, across, up, or down terrain features. The course is full of obstacles including boxes, rails, jumps, jibs (includes anything the board or rider can slide across). Slope-style contests consists of choosing your own line in a terrain park using a variety of boxes, jibs and jumps. To win a slope-style contest one must pick the best and most difficult line in the terrain park and have a smooth flowing line of tricks performed on the obstacles. Overall impression is also a huge factor in winning a slope-style contest. The rider who lands the hardest tricks will not always win over the rider who lands easier tricks.</p>

<p>Big air competitions are contests where riders perform tricks after launching off a man made jump built specifically for the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Competitors perform tricks in the air, aiming to attain sizable height and distance, all while securing a clean landing. Many competitions also require the rider to do a complex trick. But not all competitions call for a trick to win the gold; some intermittent competitions are based solely on height and distance of the launch of the snowboarder. Some competitions also require the rider to do a specific trick to win the major prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> One of the first snowboard competitions where Travis Rice attempted and landed a "double back flip backside 180" took place at the 2006 Red Bull Gap Session.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Half-pipe">Half-pipe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Half-pipe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The half-pipe is a semi-circular ditch dug into the mountain or purpose built ramp made up of snow, with walls between 8 and 23 feet (7.0 m). Competitors perform tricks while going from one side to the other and while in the air above the sides of the pipe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boardercross">Boardercross</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Boardercross">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowboard_cross" title="Snowboard cross">Snowboard cross</a></div>
<p>Boardercross, also known as "Boarder X" and "Snowboard X", is a very popular but relatively recent winter sport, starting in the 1980s and earning its place as an official Winter Olympic sport in the 2006 Turin games. In Boardercross, several riders (usually 4 to 6) race down a course similar to a motorcycle motocross track (with jumps, berms and other obstacles constructed out of snow on a downhill course). Unlike traditional head-to-head races, competitors use the same terrain, sometimes resulting in accidental collisions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snowboard_Racing">Snowboard Racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Snowboard Racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Snowboard_racing" title="Snowboard racing">Snowboard racing</a></div>
<p>In Snowboarding Racing, riders must complete a downhill course constructed of a series of turning indicators (gates) placed in the snow at prescribed distances apart. A gate consists of a tall pole, and a short pole, connected by a triangular panel. The racer must pass around the short side of the gate. There are 3 main formats used in snowboard racing including; single person, parallel courses or multiple people on the course at the same time (SBX).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitions">Competitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Competitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/FIS_Snowboard_World_Cup" title="FIS Snowboard World Cup">FIS Snowboard World Cup</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIS_Snowboarding_World_Championships" title="FIS Snowboarding World Championships">FIS Snowboarding World Championships</a></div>
<p>Some of the larger snowboarding contests include: the <a href="/wiki/Air_%26_Style" title="Air &amp; Style">Air &amp; Style</a>, the <a href="/wiki/X-Trail_Jam" title="X-Trail Jam">X-Trail Jam</a>, Burton Global Open Series, <a href="/wiki/Shakedown_(Snowboard_Games)" title="Shakedown (Snowboard Games)">Shakedown</a>, <a href="/wiki/FIS_Snowboard_World_Championships" title="FIS Snowboard World Championships">FIS world championships</a>, FIS World Cup, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_X_Games" title="Winter X Games">Winter X Games</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Dew_Tour" title="Winter Dew Tour">Winter Dew Tour</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Snowboarder_Magazine" title="Snowboarder Magazine">Snowboarder Magazine</a>'s Superpark<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> event was created in 1996. Over 150 of the World's top pros are invited to advance freestyle snowboarding on the most progressive terrain parks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Part of the snowboarding approach is to ensure maximum fun, friendship and event quality. Reflecting this perspective of snowboarding, you can find "Anti Contests" including<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> are an important part of its identity including The Holy Oly Revival<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/The_Summit_at_Snoqualmie" title="The Summit at Snoqualmie">The Summit at Snoqualmie</a>, The Nate Chute Hawaiian Classic at Whitefish, the original anti-contst, the World Quarterpipe Championships and the Grenade Games.</p>
<p>The United States of America Snowboarding Association (USASA) features three different divisions which include alpine, freestyle, and boardercross. Alpine consists of giant slalom and slalom which is a competition in which the agility and ability to make sharp turns of the snowboarders are tested. Freestyle consists of slopestyle and halfpipe. In boardercross, the idea is to be the first snowboarder down the mountain where everyone is racing each other through an obstacle course of harsh turns and wipeout potential is very likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The USASA has 36 regional snowboard series in which anyone can compete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subculture">Subculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Subculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The snowboarding way of life came about as a natural response to the culture from which it emerged. Early on, there was a rebellion against skiing culture and the view that snowboarders were inferior. Skiers did not easily accept this new culture on their slopes. The two cultures contrasted each other in several ways including how they spoke, acted, and their entire style of clothing. Snowboarders first embraced the punk and later the hip-hop look into their style. Words such as "dude", "gnarly", and "Shred the Gnar" are some examples of words used in the snowboarding culture. Snowboarding subculture became a crossover between the urban and suburban styles on snow, which made an easy transition from surfing and skateboarding culture over to snowboarding culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The early stereotypes of snowboarding included "lazy", "grungy", "punk", "stoners", "troublemakers", and numerous others, many of which are associated with skateboarding and surfing as well. However, these stereotypes may be considered "out of style". Snowboarding has become a sport that encompasses a very diverse international based crowd and fanbase of many millions, so much so that it is no longer possible to stereotype such a large community. Reasons for these dying stereotypes include how mainstream and popular the sport has become, with the shock factor of snowboarding's quick take off on the slopes wearing off. Skiers and snowboarders are becoming used to each other, showing more respect to each other on the mountain. "The typical stereotype of the sport is changing as the demographics change".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_precautions">Safety and precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safety and precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like some other winter sports, snowboarding comes with a certain level of risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The injury rate for snowboarding is about four to six per thousand persons per day, which is around double the injury rate for alpine skiing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Injuries are more likely amongst beginners, especially those who do not take lessons with professional instructors. A quarter of all injuries occur to first-time riders and half of all injuries occur to those with less than a year of experience. Experienced riders are less likely to suffer injury, but the injuries that do occur tend to be more severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull555_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-bull555-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two thirds of injuries occur to the upper body and one third to the lower body. This contrasts with alpine skiing where two thirds of injuries are to the lower body. The most common types of injuries are sprains, which account for around 40% of injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The most common point of injury is the wrists – 40% of all snowboard injuries are to the wrists and 24% of all snowboard injuries are wrist fractures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull555_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-bull555-38">[38]</a></sup> There are around 100,000 wrist fractures worldwide among snowboarders each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> For this reason the use of wrist guards, either separate or built into gloves, is very strongly recommended. They are often compulsory in beginner's classes and their use reduces the likelihood of wrist injury by half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In addition it is important for snow boarders to learn how to fall without stopping the fall with their hand by trying to "push" the slope away, as landing a wrist which is bent at a 90 degree angle increase the chance of it breaking. Rather, landing with the arms stretched out (like a wing) and slapping the slope with the entire arm is an effective way to break a fall. This is the method used by practitioners of judo and other martial arts to break a fall when they are thrown against the floor by a training partner.</p>
<p>The risk of head injury is two to six times greater for snowboarders than for skiers and injuries follow the pattern of being rarer, but more severe, with experienced riders. Head injuries can occur both as a consequence of a collision and when failing to carry out a heel-side turn. The latter can result in the rider landing on his or her back and slamming the back of his or her head onto the ground, resulting in an occipital head injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull557_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-bull557-42">[42]</a></sup> For this reason, helmets are widely recommended. Protective eyewear is also recommended as eye injury can be caused by impact and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_blindness" title="Snow blindness">snow blindness</a> can be a result of exposure to strong ultra-violet light in snow-covered areas. The wearing of ultra-violet-absorbing goggles is recommended even on hazy or cloudy days as ultra-violet light can penetrate clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike ski bindings, snowboard bindings are not designed to release automatically in a fall. The mechanical support provided by the feet being locked to the board has the effect of reducing the likelihood of knee injury – 15% of snowboard injuries are to the knee, compared with 45% of all skiing injuries. Such injuries are typically to the knee ligaments, bone fractures are rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull555_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-bull555-38">[38]</a></sup> Fractures to the lower leg are also rare but 20% of injuries are to the foot and ankle. Fractures of the <a href="/wiki/Talus_bone" title="Talus bone">talus bone</a> are rare in other sports but account for 2% of snowboard injuries – a lateral process talus fracture is sometimes called "<i>snowboarder's ankle</i>" by medical staff. This particular injury results in persistent lateral pain in the affected ankle yet is difficult to spot in a plain X-ray image. It may be misdiagnosed as just a sprain, with possibly serious consequences as not treating the fracture can result in serious long-term damage to the ankle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull555_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-bull555-38">[38]</a></sup> The use of portable ultrasound for mountainside diagnostics has been reviewed and appears to be a plausible tool for diagnosing some of the common injuries associated with the sport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak2009_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak2009-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="four"></span>Four to eight percent of snowboarding injuries take place while the person is waiting in ski-lift lines or entering and exiting ski lifts. Snowboarders push themselves forward with a free foot while in the ski-lift line, leaving the other foot (usually that of the lead leg) locked on the board at a 9–27 degree angle, placing a large torque force on this leg and predisposing the person to knee injury if a fall occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Snowboard_binding_rotating_device" title="Snowboard binding rotating device">Snowboard binding rotating devices</a> are designed to minimize the torque force, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quick_Stance" title="Quick Stance">Quick Stance</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> being the first developed in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> They allow snowboarders to turn the locked foot straight into the direction of the tip of the snowboard without removing the boot from the boot binding.</p>
<p>Avalanches are a clear danger when on snowy mountain slopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> It is best to learn the different kinds of avalanches, how to prevent causing one and how to react when one is going to happen. Also when going out onto the snow, all who practice an activity with increased chances of injury should have a basic First Aid knowledge and know how to deal with injuries that may occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Snowboarding boots should be well-fitted, with toes snug in the end of the boot when standing upright and slightly away from the end when in the snowboarding position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Padding or "armor" is recommended on other body parts such as hips, knees, spine, and shoulders. To further help avoid injury to body parts, especially knees, it is recommended to use the right technique. To acquire the right technique, one should be taught by a qualified instructor. Also, when snowboarding alone, precaution should be taken to avoid <a href="/wiki/Tree_well" title="Tree well">tree wells</a>, a particularly dangerous area of loose snow that may form at the base of trees.</p>
<p>Some care is also required when waxing a board as fluorocarbon waxes emit toxic fumes when overheated. Waxing is best performed in a ventilated area with care being taken to use the wax at the correct temperature – the wax should be melted but not smoking or smoldering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull557_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-bull557-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a study conducted to examine the types of snowboarding injuries and changes in injury patterns over time, data was collected on injured snowboarders and skiers in a base-lodge clinic of a ski resort in Vermont over 18 seasons (1988–2006) and included extensive information about injury patterns, demographics, and experience. In conclusion of the study, the highest rate of injury was among young, inexperienced, female snowboarders. Injury rates in snowboarders have fluctuated over time but still remain higher than skiers. No evidence was found that those who spend more time in terrain parks are over represented in the injury population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skiing_and_snowboarding_on_film_and_video" title="Skiing and snowboarding on film and video">Skiing and snowboarding on film and video</a></div>
<p>Snowboarding films have become a main part of progression in the sport. Each season, many films are released, usually in Autumn. These are made by many snowboard specific video production companies as well as manufacturing companies that use these films as a form of advertisement. Snowboarding videos usually contain video footage of professional riders sponsored by companies. An example of commercial use of snowboarding films would be <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_White_Album_(film)" title="The White Album (film)">The White Album</a></i>, a film by snowboarding legend and filmmaker Dave Seoane about <a href="/wiki/Shaun_White" title="Shaun White">Shaun White</a>, that includes cameos by <a href="/wiki/Tony_Hawk" title="Tony Hawk">Tony Hawk</a> and was sponsored by <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">PlayStation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Dew" title="Mountain Dew">Mountain Dew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burton_Snowboards" title="Burton Snowboards">Burton Snowboards</a>. Snowboarding films are also used as documentation of snowboarding and showcasing of current trends and styles of the sport.</p>
<p>However, sometimes the snowboarding industry is not supportive of all snowboarding-themed films. In 2013, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crash_Reel" title="The Crash Reel">The Crash Reel</a></i>, a feature-length documentary by filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Walker_(director)" title="Lucy Walker (director)">Lucy Walker</a> about former <a href="/wiki/Shaun_White" title="Shaun White">Shaun White</a> rival <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Pearce_(snowboarder)" title="Kevin Pearce (snowboarder)">Kevin Pearce</a>, premiered on the film festival circuit to critical acclaim and was subsequently broadcast on <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a>. Using Pearce's career-ending <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injury</a> and subsequent recovery as a backdrop, the film examines the physical dangers inherent to pro snowboarders and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_sports" title="Extreme sports">extreme sports</a> professional athletes under pressure by sponsors and the media to perform increasingly spectacular feats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Although there are significant references to various brands in the film, Walker is "adamant" that the snowboarding industry did not sponsor the film in any way and in fact has been unsupportive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> despite the film's mainstream media success.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magazines">Magazines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Magazines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Snowboard magazines are integral in promoting the sport, although less so with the advent of the internet age. Photo incentives are written into many professional riders' sponsorship contracts giving professionals not only a publicity but a financial incentive to have a photo published in a magazine. Snowboard magazine staff travel with professional riders throughout the winter season and cover travel, contests, lifestyle, rider and company profiles, and product reviews. Snowboard magazines have recently made a push to expand their brands to the online market, and there has also been a growth in online-only publications. Popular magazines include<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Kronicle (USA), <a href="/wiki/Transworld_Snowboarding" title="Transworld Snowboarding">Transworld Snowboarding</a> (USA), Snowboarder Magazine (USA), <a href="/wiki/Snowboard_Magazine" title="Snowboard Magazine">Snowboard Magazine</a> (USA), snowboarderMBM (Germany),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Yobeat, (USA) <a href="/wiki/Whitelines" title="Whitelines">Whitelines</a> (UK), Pleasure (Germany), Method (Europe), Onboard (Europe), Whiteroom Magazine (BG), Snowboard Canada (Canada),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> NZ Snowboarder, (New Zealand)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Pyramid Magazine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and Snowboard Colorado, (USA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowboarding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Snowboarding_video_game" title="Snowboarding video game">Snowboarding video games</a> provide interactive entertainment on and off season. Most games for this genre have been made for consoles, such as the Xbox and PlayStation. A plethora of online casual snowboarding games also exist along with games for mobile phone. More recently, snowboard simulators have been implemented as a way to practice during the off season or a way to learn altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
