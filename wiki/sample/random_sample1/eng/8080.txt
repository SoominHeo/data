<p><b>Srebrenica</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_script" title="Serbian Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a>: <span class="Unicode">Сребреница</span>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[srêbrenitsa]</a></span>) is a town in the easternmost <a href="/wiki/Republika_Srpska" title="Republika Srpska">Republika Srpska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>. Srebrenica is a small mountain town, its main industry being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_mine" title="Salt mine">salt mining</a> and a nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_bath" title="Thermal bath">spa</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_War" title="Bosnian War">Bosnian War</a>, the town was the site of a <a href="/wiki/Srebrenica_massacre" title="Srebrenica massacre">July 1995 massacre</a> of the town's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosniak" title="Bosniak">Bosniak</a> population, determined to have been a crime of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 March 2007, Srebrenica's municipal assembly adopted a resolution demanding independence from the <a href="/wiki/Republika_Srpska" title="Republika Srpska">Republika Srpska</a> entity (although not from Bosnia's sovereignty); the Serb members of the assembly did not vote on the resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before 1992, there was a metal factory in the town, and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and gold mines nearby. The town's name (Srebrenica) means "silver mine", the same meaning of its old <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name <i>Argentaria</i>.</p>
<p>Before the war, Srebrenica also had a big spa and the town prospered from tourism. Nowadays, Srebrenica has some tourism but a lot less developed than before the war. Currently, a pension, motel and a hostel are operating in the town.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_era">Roman era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times, there was a settlement of Domavia, known to have been near a mine. Silver ore from there was moved to the mints in <a href="/wiki/Salona" title="Salona">Salona</a> in the southwest and <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> in the northeast using the <a href="/wiki/Via_Argentaria" title="Via Argentaria">Via Argentaria</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 13th and 14th century the region was part of the <a href="/wiki/Banate_of_Bosnia" title="Banate of Bosnia">Banate of Bosnia</a>, and, subsequently, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_Kingdom" title="Bosnian Kingdom">Bosnian Kingdom</a>. The earliest reference to the name Srebrenica was in 1376, by which time it was already an important centre for trade in the western Balkans, based especially on the silver mines of the region. By that time, a large number of merchants of the Republic of Ragusa were established there, and they controlled the domestic silver trade and the export by sea, almost entirely via the port of Ragusa (<a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> During the 14th century, many German miners moved into the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There were often armed conflicts about Srebrenica because of its mines. According to Czech historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konstantin_Josef_Jire%C4%8Dek" title="Konstantin Josef Jireček">Konstantin Josef Jireček</a>, from 1410 to 1460, Srebrenica switched hands several times, being Serbian five times, Bosnian four times, and Ottoman three times. The mines of Bosnian <a href="/wiki/Podrinje" title="Podrinje">Podrinje</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usora_(region)" title="Usora (region)">Usora</a> were part of the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Despotate" title="Serbian Despotate">Serbian Despotate</a> prior to the Ottoman conquest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kulenovi.C4.871995_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kulenovi.C4.871995-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_period">Ottoman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ottoman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the town coming under Ottoman rule, becoming less influenced by the Republic of Ragusa, the economic importance of Srebrenica went into decline, as did the proportion of Christians in the population. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> monastery was converted into a mosque, but the large number of Catholics, Ragusan and Saxon, caused the transformation of the town to Islam to be slower than in most of the other towns in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area of <a href="/wiki/Osat" title="Osat">Osat</a> was liberated for a short time during the <a href="/wiki/First_Serbian_Uprising" title="First Serbian Uprising">First Serbian Uprising</a> (1804–13), under the leadership of Kara-Marko Vasić from <a href="/wiki/Crvica,_Srebrenica" title="Crvica, Srebrenica">Crvica</a>. Upon the breakout of the uprising, Metropolitan Hadži Melentije Stevanović contacted Vasić, who met with the rebel leadership. After participated in battles on the Drina (1804), Vasić asked <a href="/wiki/Kara%C4%91or%C4%91e" title="Karađorđe">Karađorđe</a> for an army to liberate Osat; Lazar Mutap was dispatched and the region came under rebel rule. In 1808, the Ottomans cleared out Osat, and by 1813, the rebels left the region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Usta%C5%A1e" title="Ustaše">Ustaše</a> massacred hundreds of Serbs in villages surrounding Srebrenica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paris104_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paris104-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bosnian_War">Bosnian War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bosnian War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Srebrenica_massacre" title="Srebrenica massacre">Srebrenica massacre</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_Genocide" title="Bosnian Genocide">Bosnian Genocide</a></div>
<p>The town of Srebrenica came to international prominence as a result of events during the <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_War" title="Bosnian War">Bosnian War</a> (1992–1995). The strategic objectives proclaimed by the secessionist Bosnian Serb presidency included the creation of a border separating the Serb people from Bosnia's other ethnic communities and the abolition of the border along the River <a href="/wiki/Drina" title="Drina">Drina</a> separating Serbia and the Bosnian Serbs' Republika Srpska.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Bosnian Muslim/Bosniak majority population of the Drina Valley posed a major obstacle to the achievement of these objectives. In the early days of the campaign of forcible transfer (ethnic cleansing) that followed the outbreak of war in April 1992 the town of Srebrenica was occupied by Serb/Serbian forces. It was subsequently retaken by Bosniak resistance groups. Refugees expelled from towns and villages across the central Drina valley sought shelter in Srebrenica, swelling the town's population.</p>
<p>The town and its surrounding area was surrounded and <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Srebrenica" title="Siege of Srebrenica">besieged by Serb forces</a>. On 16 April 1993, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> declared the Bosnian Muslim/Bosniak <a href="/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave" title="Enclave and exclave">enclave</a> a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Safe_Areas" title="United Nations Safe Areas">UN safe area</a>, to be "free from any armed attack or any other hostile act", and guarded by a small unit operating under the mandate of <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Protection_Force" title="United Nations Protection Force">United Nations Protection Force</a> (UNPROFOR).</p>
<p>Srebrenica and the other UN safe areas of <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDepa" title="Žepa">Žepa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gora%C5%BEde" title="Goražde">Goražde</a> were isolated pockets of Bosnian government-held territory in eastern Bosnia. In July 1995, despite the town's UN-protected status, it was attacked and captured by the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_Republika_Srpska" title="Army of Republika Srpska">Army of Republika Srpska</a>. Following the town's capture, all men of fighting age who fell into Bosnian Serb hands were massacred in a systematically organised series of summary executions. The women of the town and men below 12 years of age and above 65 were transferred by bus to <a href="/wiki/Tuzla" title="Tuzla">Tuzla</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Srebrenica_massacre" title="Srebrenica massacre">Srebrenica massacre</a> is considered the worst genocide in post-Second World War European history to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the Srebrenica massacre was determined by judgment of the <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Tribunal_for_the_former_Yugoslavia" title="International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia">International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia</a> (ICTY) to have been a crime of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> (confirmed on appeal in 2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This finding was upheld in 2007 by the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a>. The decision of the ICTY was followed by an admission to and an apology for the massacre by the Republika Srpska government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Agreement" title="Dayton Agreement">Dayton Agreement</a> which ended the Bosnian War, Srebrenica was included in the territory assigned to Bosnian Serb control as the Republika Srpska entity of Bosnia and Herzegovina. Although guaranteed under the provisions of the Dayton Agreement, the return of survivors was repeatedly obstructed. In 2007, verbal and physical attacks on returning refugees continued to be reported in the region around Srebrenica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate_of_Bosnian_Muslim_villages">Fate of Bosnian Muslim villages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebrenica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fate of Bosnian Muslim villages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1992, Bosniak villages around Srebrenica were under constant attacks by Serb forces. The Bosnian Institute in the United Kingdom has published a list of 296 villages destroyed by Serb forces around Srebrenica three years before the genocide and in the first three months of war (April–June 1992):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>More than three years before the 1995 Srebrenica genocide, Bosnian Serb nationalists - with the logistical, moral and financial support of Serbia and the Yugoslav People's Army (JNA) - destroyed 296 predominantly Bosniak (Bosnian Muslim) villages in the region around Srebrenica, forcibly uprooting some 70,000 Bosniaks from their homes and systematically massacring at least 3,166 Bosniaks (documented deaths) including many women, children and the elderly.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Naser_Ori%C4%87#The_verdict" title="Naser Orić">Naser Orić trial judgement</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Between April 1992 and March 1993, Srebrenica town and the villages in the area held by Bosnian Muslims were constantly subjected to Serb military assaults, including artillery attacks, sniper fire, as well as occasional bombing from aircraft. Each onslaught followed a similar pattern. Serb soldiers and paramilitaries surrounded a Bosnian Muslim village or hamlet, called upon the population to surrender their weapons, and then began with indiscriminate shelling and shooting. In most cases, they then entered the village or hamlet, expelled or killed the population, who offered no significant resistance and destroyed their homes. During this period, Srebrenica was subjected to indiscriminate shelling from all directions on a daily basis. Potočari in particular was a daily target for Serb artillery and infantry because it was a sensitive point in the defence line around Srebrenica. Other Bosnian Muslim settlements were routinely attacked as well. All this resulted in a great number of refugees and casualties."</p>
</blockquote>
