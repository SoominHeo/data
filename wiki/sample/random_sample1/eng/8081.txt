<p>A <b>thrall</b> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>: <i>þræll</i>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <i>trell</i>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <i>træl</i>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i>träl</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vikings_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-vikings-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave" title="Slave">slave</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serf</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Scandinavia" title="History of Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> lands during the <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking Age</a>. The corresponding term in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> was <i><b>þēow</b></i>. The status of slave (<i>þræll</i>, <i>þēow</i> ) contrasts with that of the <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Freeman_(disambiguation)" title="Freeman (disambiguation)">freeman</a> (<i>karl</i>, <i>ceorl</i>) and the nobleman (<i><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">jarl</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">eorl</a></i>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Latin" title="Middle Latin">Middle Latin</a> rendition of the term in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Germanic_law" title="Early Germanic law">early Germanic law</a> is <i>servus</i>. The social system of <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a> is continued in <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="border:1px solid #aaa;background-color:#f9f9f9">

<td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg"><img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="391" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-v2.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></a></td>

<p>Thrall is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%BEr%C3%A6ll#Old_Norse" title="wikt:þræll">þræll</a></i>, meaning a person who is in bondage or serfdom. The Old Norse term was lent into late Old English, as <i>þræl</i>. The term is from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Germanic" title="Common Germanic">Common Germanic</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/%C3%BEragilaz" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/þragilaz">þragilaz</a></i> ("runner", from a root *<i>þreh-</i> "to run"). <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> had a cognate, <i>dregil</i>, meaning "servant, runner". The English derivation <i>thraldom</i> is of High Medieval date. The verb "to enthrall" is of Early Modern origin (metaphorical use from the 1570s, literal use from 1610).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The corresponding native term in <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> society was <i>þeow</i> (from Germanic *<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/%C3%BEewaz" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/þewaz">þewa-</a></i>, perhaps from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">PIE</a> root *<i>tek<sup>w</sup>-</i>, "to run") A related Old English term is <i>esne</i> "labourer, hireling" (from Germanic *<i>asniz</i>, cognate with Gothic <i>asneis</i> "hireling", a derivation from <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/asunz" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/asunz">*asunz</a></i> "reward", from the same root as English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/earn" title="wikt:earn">earn</a></i>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Germanic_law">Early Germanic law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Germanic law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>thrall</i> represents the lowest of the three-tiered social order of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a>, noblemen, freemen and slaves, in Old Norse <i><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">jarl</a></i>, <i>karl</i> and <i>þræll</i> (c.f. <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADgs%C3%BEula" title="Rígsþula">Rígsþula</a></i>), in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> corresponding to <i><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">eorl</a></i>, <i>ceorl</i> and <i><b>þēow</b></i> , in Old Frisian <i>etheling</i>, <i>friling</i>, <i>lēt</i>, etc. The division is of importance in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_law" title="Germanic law">Germanic law codes</a>, which make special provisions for slaves: Slaves are property, and may be bought and sold, but they also enjoy some amount of protection under the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While the death of a freeman was compensated by means of a <a href="/wiki/Weregild" title="Weregild">weregild</a>, usually calculated at 200 <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">solidi</a> (<a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shillings</a>) for a freeman, the death of a slave was treated as loss of property to his owner and compensated depending on the value of the worker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thralls were the lowest class of workers in Scandinavian society. They were Northern Europeans brought into slavery due to debt, the losers of wars, and the children of previous thralls. Thralls in Scandinavia had no rights and their living conditions were variable depending on the master. The thrall trade as the prize of plunder was a key part of the Viking economy. While there are some estimates of as many as thirty slaves per household, most families only owned one or two slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1043, <a href="/wiki/Hallvard_Vebj%C3%B8rnsson" title="Hallvard Vebjørnsson">Hallvard Vebjørnsson</a>, the son of a local nobleman in the district of greater <a href="/wiki/Lier,_Norway" title="Lier, Norway">Lier</a>, was killed while trying to defend a thrall woman from men who accused her of theft. The Church strongly approved of his action, recognizing him as a <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canonization" title="Canonization">canonizing</a> him as Saint Hallvard, the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the existence of a caste system, thralls could experience a level of social fluidity. Thralls could be freed by their masters at any time, be freed in a will, or even buy their own freedom. Once a thrall was freed, he became a "freedman"—a member of an intermediary group between slaves and freemen. He still owed allegiance to his former master and would have to vote according to his former master's wishes. It took at least two generations for freedmen to lose the allegiance to their former masters and become full freemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> If a freedman had no descendants, his former master inherited his land and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>While thralls and freedmen did not have much economic or political power in Scandinavia, they were still given a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wergeld" title="Wergeld">wergeld</a></i>, or a man's price, which is to say, there was a monetary penalty for unlawfully killing a slave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Viking slave trade slowly ended in the 11th century, as a number of Vikings settled in the European territories they had once raided. They converted serfs to Christianity and merged with the local populace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vikings_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-vikings-1">[1]</a></sup> The thrall system was finally abolished in the mid-14th century in Scandinavia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
