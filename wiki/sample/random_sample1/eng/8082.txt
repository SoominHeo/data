<p><b>Sremska Mitrovica</b> (<a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>: <span lang="sr">Сремска Митровица / <i>Sremska Mitrovica</i></span>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[srêːmskaː mîtroʋitsa]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Serbia" title="List of cities in Serbia">city</a> and the administrative center of the <a href="/wiki/Srem_District" title="Srem District">Srem District</a> in the autonomous province of <a href="/wiki/Vojvodina" title="Vojvodina">Vojvodina</a>, Serbia. It is situated on the left bank of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sava_river" title="Sava river">Sava river</a>. As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the town had a total population of 37,751, while Sremska Mitrovica administrative area had a population of 79,940.</p>
<p>Once a capital of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">Tetrarchy</a>, the city was referred to as the <i>glorious mother of cities</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Likewise, ten <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperors" title="Roman Emperors">Roman Emperors</a> were born in or near this city, Emperors <a href="/wiki/Herennius_Etruscus" title="Herennius Etruscus">Herennius Etruscus</a> (251), <a href="/wiki/Hostilian" title="Hostilian">Hostilian</a> (251), <a href="/wiki/Decius" title="Decius">Decius Traian</a> (249-251), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_II" title="Claudius II">Claudius II</a> (268-270), <a href="/wiki/Quintillus" title="Quintillus">Quintillus</a> (270), <a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a> (270-275), <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius_Probus" title="Marcus Aurelius Probus">Probus</a> (276-282), <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">Maximianus Herculius</a> (285-310), <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a> (337-361) and <a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> (367-383).</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, the town is known as Сремска Митровица or <i>Sremska Mitrovica</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Rusyn_language" title="Pannonian Rusyn language">Rusyn</a> as Сримска Митровица, in <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a> as <i>Srijemska Mitrovica</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> as <i>Szávaszentdemeter</i> or <i>Mitrovica</i>, in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> as <i>Syrmisch Mitrowitz</i>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a></i>, and in <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> as <i>Dimitrofça</i>.</p>
<p>"Sremska Mitrovica" means "Mitrovica of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Srem_(region)" title="Srem (region)">Srem</a>" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosovska_Mitrovica" title="Kosovska Mitrovica">Mitrovica of Kosovo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ma%C4%8Dvanska_Mitrovica" title="Mačvanska Mitrovica">Mitrovica of Mačva</a> also exist), while "Mitrovica" itself stems from the name "Saint Demetrius" or "Sveti Dimitrije" in the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian language</a> (in Cyrillic form "Свети Димитрије").</p>
<p>The name of the city during the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> was <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a>. Beginning in 1180 AD the name changed from "Civitas Sancti Demetrii" to "Dmitrovica", "Mitrovica", and finally to the present form - "Sremska Mitrovica".</p>

<p>Sremska Mitrovica is one of the oldest cities in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Archaeologists have found a trace of organized <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> life dating from the 5000 BC onwards. <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionian</a> jewellery dating to 500BC was excavated in the city. When the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> conquered the city in the 1st century BC, Sirmium already was a settlement with a long tradition.</p>
<p>In the 1st century, Sirmium gained a status of a colony of the citizens of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, and became a very important military and strategic location in <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a> province. The war expeditions of Roman emperors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traian" title="Traian">Traian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_II" title="Claudius II">Claudius II</a>, were prepared in Sirmium.</p>
<p>In 103, Pannonia was split into two provinces: <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Superior" title="Pannonia Superior">Pannonia Superior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Inferior" title="Pannonia Inferior">Pannonia Inferior</a>, and Sirmium became the capital city of the latter. In 296, <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> operated a new territorial division of Pannonia. Instead of previous two provinces, there were four new provinces established in former territory of original Pannonia: <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Prima" title="Pannonia Prima">Pannonia Prima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Valeria" title="Pannonia Valeria">Pannonia Valeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Savia" title="Pannonia Savia">Pannonia Savia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Secunda" title="Pannonia Secunda">Pannonia Secunda</a>. Capital city of Pannonia Secunda was Sirmium.</p>
<p>In 293, with the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">tetrarchy</a>, the Roman Empire was split into four parts; Sirmium became one of the four capital cities of Roman Empire, the other three being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augusta_Treverorum" title="Augusta Treverorum">Augusta Treverorum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediolanum" title="Mediolanum">Mediolanum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izmit" title="Izmit">Izmit</a>). During the tetrarchy, Sirmium was the capital of emperor <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a>. With the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture" title="Praetorian prefecture">praetorian prefectures</a> in 318, the capital of the prefecture of <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture_of_Illyricum" title="Praetorian prefecture of Illyricum">Illyricum</a> was Sirmium.</p>
<p>Since the 4th century, the city was an important <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> centre, and was a seat of the Episcopate of Sirmium. Four Christian <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Sirmium" title="Council of Sirmium">councils</a> were held in Sirmium.</p>
<p>At the end of the 4th century, Sirmium was brought under the sway of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, and later, was again annexed to the Eastern Roman Empire. In 441, Sirmium was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>, and after this conquest, it remained for more than a century in the hands of various <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">Barbarian</a> tribes, such were <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Eastern Goths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gepids" title="Gepids">Gepids</a>. For a short time, Sirmium was the center of the <a href="/wiki/Gepids" title="Gepids">Gepide State</a> and the king <a href="/wiki/Cunimund" title="Cunimund">Cunimund</a> minted golden coins in it. After 567, Sirmium was again included into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>. The city was finally conquered and destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Avars" title="Pannonian Avars">Avars</a> in 582. This event marked the end of the period of late Antiquity in the history of Sirmium.</p>
<p>11 luxurious golden belts of Avar handicraft dating to the 6th century was excavated in the vicinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Avar_conquest">After the Avar conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the Avar conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For more than two centuries the fate of Sirmium was unknown. At the end of the 8th century, Sirmium belonged to the Frankish State. The historical role of Sirmium increased again in the 9th century, when it was part of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Empire" title="Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarian Empire</a>. Pope Adrian II gave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Methodius_of_Thessaloniki" title="Saint Methodius of Thessaloniki">St. Methodius</a> the title of Archbishop of Sirmium. After having adopted Christianity, the Bulgarians restored in Sirmium the Christian Episcopate, having in mind old Christian traditions and the reputation this city had in the ancient world.</p>
<p>In the 11th century, Sirmium was a residence of <a href="/wiki/Sermon_(ruler)" title="Sermon (ruler)">Sermon</a>, a duke of <a href="/wiki/Syrmia" title="Syrmia">Syrmia</a>, who was a vassal of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Empire" title="Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuil_of_Bulgaria" title="Samuil of Bulgaria">Samuil</a>. After 1018, the city was again included into the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, and since the end of the 11th century, Sirmium was a subject of a dispute between the Byzantine Empire and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, until 1180 when the Byzantine Empire gave up Sirmium, surrendering it to the Kingdom of Hungary. In the 11th century, a Byzantine province named <a href="/wiki/Theme_of_Sirmium" title="Theme of Sirmium">Theme of Sirmium</a> had its capital in this city.</p>
<p>For a while, about 1451, the city was in possession of the Serbian despot <a href="/wiki/%C4%90ura%C4%91_Brankovi%C4%87" title="Đurađ Branković">Đurađ Branković</a>. In 1521 the city came into <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> hands and it remained under the Ottoman rule for almost two centuries. According to Ottoman traveler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evliya_Celebi" title="Evliya Celebi">Evliya Celebi</a>, Mitrovica had been conquered by the Bosnian sanjak bey <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Husrev-bey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Husrev-bey (page does not exist)">Husrev-bey</a>. She was renamed as "Dimitrofça".</p>
<p>The name of the mayor of the city was Dimitar and since the middle of the 16th century, the city was mostly populated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a>. According to the 1566/69 data, the population of the city was composed of 592 Muslim and 30 <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> houses, while according to the 1572 data, it was composed of 598 Muslim and 18 Christian houses. According to the 1573 data, the city had 17 mosques and no Christian church. During the Ottoman rule, Sremska Mitrovica was the largest settlement in <a href="/wiki/Syrmia" title="Syrmia">Syrmia</a>, and was the administrative center of the Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Sanjak_of_Syrmia" title="Sanjak of Syrmia">Sanjak of Syrmia</a>. It was temporarily occupied by Austrian troops between 1688 and 1690. They finally took it in 1717 and took possession of it after signing <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Passarowitz" title="Treaty of Passarowitz">Treaty of Passarowitz</a> in 1718.</p>
<p>With the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg</a> administration in 1718, the Muslim population fled from the city and was replaced with <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croatian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> settlers. According to the 1765 data, the population of the city numbered 809 people, of whom 514 were Serbs and 290 Catholics.</p>
<p>Sremska Mitrovica was part of the Habsburg <a href="/wiki/Military_Frontier" title="Military Frontier">Military Frontier</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavonian_Krajina" title="Slavonian Krajina">Slavonian Krajina</a>). In 1848/1849, it was part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Voivodship" title="Serbian Voivodship">Serbian Voivodship</a> , a Serb autonomous region within Austrian Empire, but in 1849, it was returned under administration of the Military Frontier.</p>
<p>With the abolition of the Slavonian Military Frontier in 1881, Sremska Mitrovica was included into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Szer%C3%A9m_(former_county)" title="Szerém (former county)">Syrmia County</a>, which was part of the Kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croatia-Slavonia" title="Croatia-Slavonia">Croatia-Slavonia</a> within <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>. According to the 1910 census, the population of the city numbered 12,909 people, of which 8,793 spoke the Serbo-Croatian language (4,878 of those spoke Serbian and 3,915 spoke Croatian) and 2,341 German. The municipal area of the city (which did not included the city itself) had 32,012 inhabitants, of which 28,093 spoke Serbo-Croatian (27,022 of those spoke Serbian and 1,071 spoke Croatian) and 2,324 German.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_First_World_War">After the First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After the First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1918, the Austro-Hungarian Monarchy collapsed and the Syrmia region first became a part of the newly formed <a href="/wiki/State_of_Slovenes,_Croats_and_Serbs" title="State of Slovenes, Croats and Serbs">State of Slovenes, Croats and Serbs</a>, and then, on November 24, 1918, the assembly of Syrmia in <a href="/wiki/Ruma" title="Ruma">Ruma</a> decided that most of Syrmia (including Mitrovica) join to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbia" title="Kingdom of Serbia">Kingdom of Serbia</a>. Subsequently, on December 1, 1918, Kingdom of Serbia united with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Montenegro" title="Kingdom of Montenegro">Kingdom of Montenegro</a> and the State of Slovenes, Croats and Serbs to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbs,_Croats_and_Slovenes" title="Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes">Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes</a> (renamed to <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> in 1929). Between 1918 and 1922, Sremska Mitrovica was part of the Syrmia County, between 1922 and 1929 part of the Syrmia Oblast, between 1929 and 1931 part of the <a href="/wiki/Drina_Banovina" title="Drina Banovina">Drina Banovina</a>, and between 1931 and 1941 part of the <a href="/wiki/Danube_Banovina" title="Danube Banovina">Danube Banovina</a>.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the city was occupied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" title="Axis Powers">Axis</a> troops and was attached to the <a href="/wiki/Independent_State_of_Croatia" title="Independent State of Croatia">Independent State of Croatia</a>. During that time its name was changed to <i>Hrvatska Mitrovica</i> (meaning <i>Croatian Mitrovica</i>). One of the largest Nazi concentration/death camps in the Independent State of Croatia existed in Sremska Mitrovica and as many as 10,000 victims (Serbs, Jews, and antifascists) were killed here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Serbia" title="History of the Jews in Serbia">Serbian Jewish</a> population was to be interned in a <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">concentration camp</a> built first in Jarak and then at Zasavica.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, both locations proved to be too flooded for construction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Germans had to abandon these locations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and use <a href="/wiki/Sajmi%C5%A1te_concentration_camp" title="Sajmište concentration camp">Sajmište</a>, which resulted in <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust_in_Serbia" title="The Holocaust in Serbia">the destruction of 83% of Serbian Jewry</a>.</p>
<p>Beginning in 1944, the town was part of the Autonomous Province of <a href="/wiki/Vojvodina" title="Vojvodina">Vojvodina</a> within the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SFRY" title="SFRY">Socialist Yugoslavia</a> and, from 1945, within the Socialist Republic of Serbia. From 1992 to 2003 it was part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Federal Republic of Yugoslavia</a>, which was then transformed into the state union of <a href="/wiki/Serbia_and_Montenegro" title="Serbia and Montenegro">Serbia and Montenegro</a>. Since the 2006 independence of <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, Sremska Mitrovica is part of an independent <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements_by_ethnic_majority">Settlements by ethnic majority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Settlements by ethnic majority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the settlements in the municipality have an ethnic Serb majority. Ethnically mixed settlement with relative Serb majority is Stara Bingula. The main concentration of ethnic minorities is in the town.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, the population of municipality of Sremska Mitrovica included 76,290 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christians" title="Orthodox Christians">Orthodox Christians</a>, 3,935 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a>, 252 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestants" title="Protestants">Protestants</a> and 106 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a>. Orthodox Christians in Sremska Mitrovica are belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Eparchy_of_Srem" title="Eparchy of Srem">Eparchy of Syrmia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Church</a>. Catholic Christians are belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Syrmia" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Syrmia">Roman Catholic Diocese of Syrmia</a>, which has its seat in Sremska Mitrovica.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archeology">Archeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Archeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 1970s <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> archeologists sponsored by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_government" title="US government">US government</a> made an offer to the citizens of Sremska Mitrovica to completely rebuild the town on another location so that the town could be excavated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The town government refused the request immediately, under pressure from the then hard-socialist <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslav</a> government.</p>
<p>During work on the new Sremska Mitrovica trade center in 1972, a worker accidentally broke into an old <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> pot, about 2m deep, over the site of an old <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> settlement. 33 gold Roman coins enclosed in a leather pouch were found inside a Roman house wall, probably the hidden savings of a wealthy Roman family stashed centuries ago. Of this extraordinary rare find of Sirmium minted coins were four <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a> era coins, considered the most valuable examples from the late Roman empire of the 4th century AD. The young worker whose shovel brought this significant discovery to light was never rewarded.</p>
<p>The only known unexcavated <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> horse racing arena in the world is in <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a>. A colossal building about 150m wide and 450m long lays directly under the Sremska Mitrovica town center and just beside the old <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> Emperor's Palace (one of just a few Sirmium publicly accessible archeological sights). The presence of the arena has clearly affected the layout of the present town (Sremska Mitrovica is today about 2-4m above ground line of the former Sirmium settlement). Recently announced<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> cultural and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeological" title="Archeological">archeological</a> projects for preserving and popularising <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> sights haven't included any activity dealing with the arena, probably due to its size — the entire present town center might have to be excavated.</p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Decius" title="Decius">Decius Traian</a> (249–51)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herennius_Etruscus" title="Herennius Etruscus">Herennius Etruscus</a> (251-51)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hostilian" title="Hostilian">Hostilian</a> (251-51)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Quintillus" title="Quintillus">Quintillus</a> (270)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a> (270–75)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Gratian" title="Gratian">Gratian</a> (367–83)</li>

<p>The last emperor of the united Roman Empire, <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> (378–95), became emperor in Sirmium. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_usurper" title="Roman usurper">usurpers</a> <a href="/wiki/Ingenuus" title="Ingenuus">Ingenuus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regalianus" title="Regalianus">Regalianus</a> also declared themselves emperors in this city (in 260) and many other Roman emperors spent some time in Sirmium including <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> who might have written parts of his famous work <a href="/wiki/Meditations" title="Meditations">Meditations</a> in the city.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_antiquity">Classical antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sremska_Mitrovica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Classical antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<td class="navbox-abovebelow" colspan="3">
<div><sup><small>1</small></sup> <span class="wrap"><a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> is the subject of a territorial dispute between the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Republic of Kosovo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Republic of Serbia</a>. The Republic of Kosovo <a href="/wiki/2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence" title="2008 Kosovo declaration of independence">unilaterally declared independence</a> on 17 February 2008, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbia%27s_reaction_to_the_2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence" title="Serbia's reaction to the 2008 Kosovo declaration of independence">Serbia continues to claim</a> it as part of its <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Province_of_Kosovo_and_Metohija" title="Autonomous Province of Kosovo and Metohija">own sovereign territory</a>. The two governments <a href="/wiki/Belgrade%E2%80%93Pristina_negotiations" title="Belgrade–Pristina negotiations">began to normalise relations</a> in 2013, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Agreement_(2013)" title="Brussels Agreement (2013)">Brussels Agreement</a>. Kosovo has received recognition as an independent state from <a href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_Kosovo" title="International recognition of Kosovo">110</a> out of 193 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">member states</a>.</span></div>
