<p><b>Smara</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">السمارة</span>‎‎ <i>as-Samāra</i>; also Romanized <i>Semara</i>) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Moroccan</a>-controlled part of <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>, with a population estimated at 42,056 (2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Population-1">[1]</a></sup> It is served by <a href="/wiki/Smara_Airport" title="Smara Airport">Smara Airport</a> and Smara bus station.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The largest city in its province, Smara was founded in the <a href="/wiki/Saguia_el-Hamra" title="Saguia el-Hamra">Saguia el-Hamra</a> as an oasis for travellers in 1869. It is the only major city in Western Sahara that was not founded by the Spanish. In the center of the city the remains of a stone fortress can be found, the Zawiy Maalainin, which enclosed a mosque. The Maalainin lived there from 1830 until 1912. It was made a capital and religious center in 1902 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaykh" title="Shaykh">shaykh</a> <a href="/wiki/Ma_al-%27Aynayn" title="Ma al-'Aynayn">Ma al-'Aynayn</a>, in what was then <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sahara" title="Spanish Sahara">Spanish Sahara</a>. The location of the city was intended to ensure its becoming a <a href="/wiki/Caravan_(travellers)" title="Caravan (travellers)">caravan</a> trade hub in the sparsely populated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_desert" title="Sahara desert">Sahara desert</a>. The enlargement of Smara was carried out by local <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_people" title="Sahrawi people">Sahrawis</a> as well as craftsmen sent by the <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hassan_I" title="Hassan I">Hassan I</a>. In 1902, shaykh Ma al-'Aynayn moved to Smara and declared it his holy capital. Among other things, he created an important <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> library, and the town became a center of religious learning.</p>
<p>In 1904 the shaykh declared himself an <a href="/wiki/Imam" title="Imam">imam</a> and called for holy war (<a href="/wiki/Jihad" title="Jihad">jihad</a>) against <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> colonialism, which was increasingly pressing into the Sahara at this time. During the campaign against Ma al-'Aynayn, Smara was sacked almost completely in 1913 by the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a>, and its library destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The town was then handed over to the Spanish. In 1934 the town was once again destroyed after Sahrawi rebellions against the Spanish occupation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Vieuchange_brothers">The Vieuchange brothers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Vieuchange brothers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reaching Ma el Ainain's mysterious Smara was the goal of the brothers Vieuchange, early 20th-century French writers and romantics. <a href="/wiki/Michel_Vieuchange" title="Michel Vieuchange">Michel Vieuchange</a>'s painful journey through the rebel-held Sahrawi lands in 1930 disguised as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a> tribeswoman, eventually reaching Smara on 1 November 1930, and the illness that lead to his death on the return, is documented in his journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVieuchange19882.E2.80.933_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVieuchange19882.E2.80.933-3">[3]</a></sup> Comprising seven notebooks and more than 200 photographs, the account was published posthumously in 1932 as <i>Smara: The Forbidden City</i> (1932) by his brother <a href="/wiki/Jean_Vieuchange" title="Jean Vieuchange">Jean</a> and became a bestseller.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1975">After 1975</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After 1975">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1975, Morocco took control of Saguia Elhamra as <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> withdrew, according to the Madrid Accords. The Moroccan army took the city from the <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">Polisario Front</a> in 1976. Near <a href="/wiki/Tindouf" title="Tindouf">Tindouf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, there still exists a Sahrawi <a href="/wiki/Refugee_camp" title="Refugee camp">refugee camp</a> named after Smara. It is one of the four camps of the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a> administration in <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town is controlled by the Moroccan authorities, as part of what the government terms its <a href="/wiki/Southern_Provinces" title="Southern Provinces">Southern Provinces</a>, and enclosed by a section of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_Wall" title="Moroccan Wall">Moroccan Wall</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CTM, Supratours and Satas companies have daily travels from Smara to Agadir. Supratours have daily travel from Smara to Laayoune.</p>
