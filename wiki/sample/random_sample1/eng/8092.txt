<p><b>Home automation</b> or <b>smart home</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smart_Home_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smart_Home-1">[1]</a></sup> (also known as <b>domotics</b> or <b>domotica</b>) is the residential extension of <a href="/wiki/Building_automation" title="Building automation">building automation</a> and involves the control and automation of lighting, heating (such as <a href="/wiki/Smart_thermostat" title="Smart thermostat">smart thermostats</a>), ventilation, air conditioning (<a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a>), and security, as well as <a href="/wiki/Home_appliance" title="Home appliance">home appliances</a> such as washer/dryers, ovens or refrigerators/freezers that use WiFi for remote monitoring. Modern systems generally consist of switches and sensors connected to a central hub sometimes called a "gateway" from which the system is controlled with a <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a> that is interacted either with a wall-mounted terminal, mobile phone software, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computer</a> or a web interface, often but not always via internet cloud services.</p>
<p>While there are many competing vendors, there are very few world-wide accepted industry standards and the smart home space is heavily fragmented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Popular <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" title="Communications protocol">communications protocol</a> for products include <a href="/wiki/X10_(industry_standard)" title="X10 (industry standard)">X10</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>, <a href="/wiki/RS-485" title="RS-485">RS-485</a>, <a href="/wiki/6LoWPAN" title="6LoWPAN">6LoWPAN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth_low_energy" title="Bluetooth low energy">Bluetooth LE (BLE)</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZigBee" title="ZigBee">ZigBee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Z-Wave" title="Z-Wave">Z-Wave</a>, or other proprietary protocols all of which are incompatible with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crhomephone_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-crhomephone-3">[3]</a></sup> Manufacturers often prevent independent implementations by withholding documentation and by suing people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The home automation market was worth US$5.77 billion in 2015, predicted to have a market value over US$10 billion by the year 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Home_automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "<i>domotics</i>" (and "<i>domotica</i>" when used as a verb) is a contraction of the Latin word for a home "<i><a href="/wiki/Domus" title="Domus">domus</a></i>" and the words/fields <i><a href="/wiki/Informatics" title="Informatics">informatics</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Telematics" title="Telematics">telematics</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Early home automation began with labor-saving machines. Self-contained electric or gas powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_appliances" title="Home appliances">home appliances</a> became viable in the 1900s with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">electric power distribution</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gerhart_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-gerhart-6">[6]</a></sup> and led to the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Washing_machine" title="Washing machine">washing machines</a> (1904), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_heater" title="Water heater">water heaters</a> (1889), <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewing_machine" title="Sewing machine">sewing machines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dishwasher" title="Dishwasher">dishwashers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clothes_dryer" title="Clothes dryer">clothes dryers</a>.</p>
<p>In 1975, the first general purpose home automation network technology, <a href="/wiki/X10_(industry_standard)" title="X10 (industry standard)">X10</a>, was developed. It is a communication protocol for electronic devices. It primarily uses <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electric power transmission</a> wiring for signalling and control, where the signals involve brief <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> bursts of <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">digital data</a>, and remains the most widely available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1978, X10 products included a 16 channel command console, a lamp module, and an appliance module. Soon after came the wall switch module and the first X10 timer.</p>
<p>By 2012, in the United States, according to ABI Research, 1.5 million home automation systems were installed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Li et. al. (2016) there are three generations of home automation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>First generation: wireless technology with proxy server, e.g. Zigbee automation;</li>
<li>Second generation: artificial intelligence controls electrical devices, e.g. amazon echo;</li>
<li>Third generation: robot buddy "who" interacts with humans, e.g. Robot Rovio, Roomba.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_and_technologies">Applications and technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Home_automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications and technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li><a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">Heating, ventilation and air conditioning</a> (HVAC): it is possible to have remote control of all <a href="/wiki/Home_energy_monitor" title="Home energy monitor">home energy monitors</a> over the internet incorporating a simple and friendly user interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lighting_control_system" title="Lighting control system">Lighting control system</a></li>
<li>Appliance control and integration with the <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">smart meter</a>, taking advantage, for instance, of high solar panel output in the middle of the day to run washing machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BERGU14_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BERGU14-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Security: a household <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Security_system" title="Security system">security system</a> integrated with a home automation system can provide additional services such as remote surveillance of security cameras over the Internet, or central locking of all perimeter doors and windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>

<p>In a review of home automation devices, <i><a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a></i> found two main concerns for consumers:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>A WiFi network connected to the internet can be vulnerable to hacking.</li>
<li>Technology is still in its infancy, and consumers could invest in a system that becomes <a href="/wiki/Abandonware" title="Abandonware">abandonware</a>. In 2014, Google bought the company selling the Revolv Hub home automation system, integrated it with <a href="/wiki/Nest_Labs" title="Nest Labs">Nest</a> and in 2016 shut down the servers Revolv Hub depended on, rendering the hardware useless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a> found in 2011, that home automation could involve high cost of ownership, inflexibility of interconnected devices, and poor manageability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically systems have been sold as complete systems where the consumer relies on one vendor for the entire system including the hardware, the communications protocol, the central hub, and the user interface. However, there are now <a href="/wiki/Open-source_model" title="Open-source model">open source</a> software systems which can be used with proprietary hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocols">Protocols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Home_automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protocols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a wide variety of technology platforms, or protocols, on which a smart home can be built. Each one is, essentially, its own language. Each language speaks to the various connected devices and instructs them to perform a function.</p>
<p>The automation protocol transport has involved direct wire connectivity, powerline (UPB) and wireless hybrid and wireless.</p>
<p>Most of the protocols below are not <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">open</a>. All have an <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">API</a>.</p>

<p>Acronym explanation:</p>
<ul>
<li>PLC: <a href="/wiki/Power-line_communication" title="Power-line communication">Power line communication</a>, communication using existing mains wiring</li>
<li>RF: <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">RF</a>, transmission using a high frequency carrier</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Twisted_pair" title="Twisted pair">Twisted pair</a>: High speed signals using cables with twisted pairs to protect the signals</li>
<li>Infrared: Optical communication using <a href="/wiki/Consumer_IR" title="Consumer IR">infrared</a> light</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>: Well established standard for high capacity communications</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversies">Criticism and controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Home_automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism and controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Home automation suffers from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platform_fragmentation" title="Platform fragmentation">platform fragmentation</a> and lack of <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">technical standards</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> a situation where the variety of home automation devices, in terms of both hardware variations and differences in the software running on them, makes the task of developing applications that work consistently between different inconsistent technology <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> hard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Customers may be hesitant to bet their IoT future on <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary software</a> or hardware devices that use <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_protocol" title="Proprietary protocol">proprietary protocols</a> that may fade or become difficult to customize and interconnect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Home automation devices <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_computing" title="Amorphous computing">amorphous computing</a> nature is also a problem for security, since patches to bugs found in the core operating system often do not reach users of older and lower-price devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodbye.2C_Android_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodbye.2C_Android-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> One set of researchers say that the failure of vendors to support older devices with patches and updates leaves more than 87% of active devices vulnerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
