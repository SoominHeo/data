<p><i><b>The Smurfs</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i><b>Les Schtroumpfs</b></i>; <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i><b>De Smurfen</b></i>) is a Belgian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic" title="Comic">comic</a> franchise centered on a fictional colony of small blue <a href="/wiki/Humanoid" title="Humanoid">humanoids</a> who live in <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushroom</a>-shaped houses in the forest. <i>The Smurfs</i> was first created and introduced as a series of comic characters by the Belgian comics artist <a href="/wiki/Peyo" title="Peyo">Peyo</a> (pen name of Pierre Culliford) in 1958, where they were known as <i>Les Schtroumpfs</i>. There are more than one hundred Smurf characters, and their names are based on adjectives that emphasize their characteristics, such as "Jokey Smurf", who likes to play practical jokes on his fellow smurfs. "Smurfette" was the first female Smurf to be introduced in the series. The Smurfs wear <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_cap" title="Phrygian cap">Phrygian caps</a>, which came to represent freedom during the modern era.</p>
<p>The word “Smurf” is the original Dutch translation of the French "Schtroumpf", which, according to Peyo, is a word invented during a meal with fellow cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Franquin" title="André Franquin">André Franquin</a>, when he could not remember the word salt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franquin_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franquin-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Smurfs</i> franchise began as a comic and expanded into advertising, films, TV series, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_capade" title="Ice capade">ice capades</a>, video games, theme parks, and dolls.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time he came up with the idea for the Smurfs, Peyo was the creator, artist, and writer of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Belgian_comics" title="Franco-Belgian comics">Franco-Belgian comics</a> series titled <i>Johan et Pirlouit</i> (translated to English as <i><a href="/wiki/Johan_and_Peewit" title="Johan and Peewit">Johan and Peewit</a></i>), set in Europe during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and including elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sword-and-sorcery" title="Sword-and-sorcery">sword-and-sorcery</a>. Johan serves as a brave young page to the <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">king</a>, and Pirlouit (pronounced Peer-loo-ee) functions as his faithful, if boastful and cheating, <a href="/wiki/Midget" title="Midget">midget</a> <a href="/wiki/Sidekick" title="Sidekick">sidekick</a>. In 1958, <i><a href="/wiki/Spirou_(magazine)" title="Spirou (magazine)">Spirou</a></i> magazine started to publish the <i>Johan et Pirlouit</i> story <i>La Flûte à six trous</i> ("The Flute with Six Holes").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BDO-s_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BDO-s-2">[2]</a></sup> The adventure involved them recovering a magic flute, which required some sorcery by the wizard Homnibus. In this manner, they met a tiny, blue-skinned humanoid in white clothing called a "Schtroumpf", followed by his numerous peers who looked just like him, with an elderly leader who wore red clothing and had a white beard. Their first full appearance was published in <i>Spirou</i> on October 23, 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The characters proved to be a huge success, and the first independent Smurf stories appeared in <i>Spirou</i> in 1959, together with the first merchandising. The Smurfs shared more adventures with Johan and Pirlouit, got their own series and all subsequent publications of the original story were retitled <i>La Flûte à six Schtroumpfs</i> (also the title of the <a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_and_the_Magic_Flute" title="The Smurfs and the Magic Flute">movie version of the story</a>).</p>
<p><i>Schtroumpf</i> is pronounced like the German word "Strumpf" meaning "sock". However, according to Peyo, original author of the original Smurfs comic strip, the original term and the accompanying language of the Smurfs came during a meal he was having with his colleague and friend <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Franquin" title="André Franquin">André Franquin</a> at the Belgian Coast. Having momentarily forgotten the word "salt", Peyo asked him (in French) to pass the <i>schtroumpf</i>. Franquin jokingly replied: "Here's the <i>Schtroumpf</i>—when you are done <i>schtroumpfing</i>, <i>schtroumpf</i> it back..." and the two spent the rest of that weekend speaking in "<i>schtroumpf</i> language".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franquin_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Franquin-1">[1]</a></sup> The name was later translated into Dutch as Smurf, which was adopted in English.</p>
<p>With the commercial success of the Smurfs came the merchandising empire of Smurf miniatures, models, games, and toys. Entire collecting clubs have devoted themselves to collecting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PVC" title="PVC">PVC</a> Smurfs and Smurf merchandise.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurfs_universe">Smurfs universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Smurfs universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurfs">Smurfs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Smurfs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The storylines tend to be simple tales of bold adventure. The cast has a simple structure as well: almost all the characters look essentially alike—mostly male (a few female Smurfs have appeared: <a href="/wiki/Smurfette" title="Smurfette">Smurfette</a>, Sassette, and Nanny Smurf), short (three apples high),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> with blue skin, white trousers with a hole for their short tails, white hat in the style of a <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_cap" title="Phrygian cap">Phrygian cap</a>, and sometimes some additional accessory that identifies a personality (for example, "Handy Smurf" wears <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overall_(bib-and-brace)" title="Overall (bib-and-brace)">overalls</a> instead of the standard trousers, a brimmed hat, and a pencil above his ear). Smurfs can walk and run, but often move by skipping on both feet. They love to eat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smilax_regelii" title="Smilax regelii">sarsaparilla</a> (a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Smilax" title="Smilax">Smilax</a></i>) leaves, whose berries the Smurfs naturally call "smurfberries" (the smurfberries appear only in the cartoon; in the original comics, the Smurfs only eat the leaves from the sarsaparilla).</p>
<p>The Smurfs fulfill simple archetypes of everyday people: "Lazy Smurf", "Grouchy Smurf", "Brainy Smurf", and so on. All Smurfs, with the exception of Papa, Baby, Smurfette, Nanny and Grandpa, are said to be 100 years old. There were originally 99 Smurfs, but this number increased as new Smurf characters appeared, such as Sassette and Nanny. All of the original Smurfs were male; later female additions are Smurfette and Sassette—Smurfette being Gargamel's creation, while Sassette was created by the Smurflings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language <span id="smurf_as_a_word"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A characteristic of the Smurf language is the frequent use of the undefinable word "smurf" and its derivatives in a variety of meanings. The Smurfs frequently replace both <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a> in everyday speech with the word "smurf": "We're going <i>smurfing</i> on the River Smurf today." When used as a verb, the word "Smurf" typically means "to make", "to be", "to like", or "to do".</p>
<p>Humans have found that replacing ordinary words with the term "smurf" at random is not enough: in one adventure, Peewit explains to some other humans that the statement "I'm smurfing to the smurf" means "I'm going to the wood", but a Smurf corrects him by saying that the proper statement would be "I'm smurfing to the smurf"; whereas what Peewit said was "I'm warbling to the dawn". So "I'm smurfing to the smurf" is not the same as "I'm smurfing to the smurf".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the animated series, only some words (or a portion of the word) are replaced with the word "smurf". Context offers a reliable understanding of this speech pattern, but common vocabulary includes remarking that something is "just smurfy" or in some cases, "smurftastic".</p>
<p>In <i>Schtroumpf vert et vert Schtroumpf</i> (see <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smurf_Versus_Smurf" title="Smurf Versus Smurf">Smurf Versus Smurf</a></i>), published in Belgium in 1972, it was revealed that the smurf village was divided between North and South, and that the Smurfs on either side had different ideas as to how the term "smurf" should be used: for instance, the Northern Smurfs called a certain object a "bottle smurfer", while the Southern Smurfs called it a "smurf opener". This story is considered a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> on the still ongoing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taalstrijd" title="Taalstrijd">taalstrijd</a></i> (language war) between French- and Dutch-speaking communities in Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurf_village">Smurf village</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Smurf village">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When they first appeared in 1958, the Smurfs lived in a part of the world called "<i>Le Pays Maudit</i>" (French for "the Cursed Land"). To reach it required magic or travelling through dense <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, deep <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, a scorching <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> and a high <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Smurfs themselves use <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">storks</a> in order to travel long distances, such as to the kingdom where Johan and Pirlouit live, and keep up-to-date with events in the outside world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i>Johan et Pirlouit</i> stories, the Smurf village is made up of mushroom-like houses of different shapes and sizes in a desolate and rocky land with just a few trees. However, in the Smurf series itself, the mushroom-like houses are more similar to one another and are located in a clearing in the middle of a deep forest with grass, a river, and vegetation. Humans such as <a href="/wiki/Gargamel" title="Gargamel">Gargamel</a> are shown to live nearby, though it is almost impossible for an outsider to find the Smurf village except when led by a Smurf.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurf_economy">Smurf economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Smurf economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Smurfs' <a href="/wiki/Commune" title="Commune">community</a> generally takes the form of a <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>, sharing, and kind environment based on the principle that each Smurf has something he or she is good at, and thus contributes it to Smurf society as he or she can. In return, each Smurf appears to be given their necessities of life, from housing and clothes to food without using any <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> in exchange.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Smurfs_characters" title="List of The Smurfs characters">List of The Smurfs characters</a></div>
<p>Papa Smurf is the leader of the community. Other Smurfs are generally named after their personality disposition, much like <a href="/wiki/Snow_White_and_the_Seven_Dwarfs_(1937_film)" title="Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs (1937 film)">Disney's Seven Dwarfs</a> ; for example, Brainy, Greedy, Vanity, Lazy, Clumsy, Hefty, Jokey, Dreamy, Grouchy, or their profession, for example, Poet, Actor, Handy, Harmony, Farmer, Clockwork, Painter, Tailor, Miner, Architect, Reporter, Timber, Barber and Doctor Smurf. The first female Smurf, Smurfette, was created by Gargamel to lure the other Smurfs. Papa Smurf then changed her into what we see today. The non-Smurf characters who would appear later would include their enemies the wizard <a href="/wiki/Gargamel" title="Gargamel">Gargamel</a>, his cat Azrael, an ugly witch, Hogatha and Gargamel's godfather Balthazar; and their friends the page Johan and his young friend Peewit and the wizard Homnibus. There are 105 Smurfs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurf_comics">Smurf comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Smurf comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_(comics)" title="The Smurfs (comics)">The Smurfs (comics)</a></div>
<p>Since the first appearance of the Smurfs in <i>Johan et Pirlouit</i> in 1958, 31 Smurf comics have been created, 16 of them by Peyo, the others by his studio. Originally, the Smurf stories appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Spirou_(magazine)" title="Spirou (magazine)">Spirou</a></i> magazine with reprints in many different magazines, but after Peyo left the publisher <a href="/wiki/Dupuis" title="Dupuis">Dupuis</a>, many comics were first published in dedicated Smurf magazines, which existed in French, Dutch, German and Turkish. A number of short stories and one page gags have been collected into comic books next to the regular series of 30 including a newly released Smurfs comics enitiled <i>Les Schtroumpfs de L'ordre</i> (<i>The Order <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">Law</a> of the Smurfs</i>). English translations have been published in the U.S. by the graphic novel publisher Papercutz as well as a mini series published by <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> in the mid-1980s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motion_pictures">Motion pictures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Motion pictures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1965, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">black-and-white</a> 87-minute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animated" title="Animated">animated</a> <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Aventures_des_Schtroumpfs" title="Les Aventures des Schtroumpfs">Les Aventures des Schtroumpfs</a></i> was released in theatres in Belgium. It consisted of five short cartoons made in the previous years for broadcasting on <a href="/wiki/Walloons" title="Walloons">Walloon</a> TV. German copies and copies with Dutch subtitles are known to exist. The stories were based on existing Smurf stories like <i>The Black Smurfs</i> and <i>The Smurfs and the Egg</i>, and were created by writer <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Rosy" title="Maurice Rosy">Maurice Rosy</a> and artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_Ryssack&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eddy Ryssack (page does not exist)">Eddy Ryssack</a> from the small Dupuis animation studios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In total, ten animated shorts were created between 1961 and 1967, the first series in black and white and the later ones in colour.</p>
<p>In 1976, <i>La Flûte à six schtroumpfs</i> (an adaptation of the original "Johan et Pirlouit" story) was released. <a href="/wiki/Michel_Legrand" title="Michel Legrand">Michel Legrand</a> provided the musical score to the film. The film would be released in the United States in 1983 (after the animated series became popular there) in an English language dubbed version titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_and_the_Magic_Flute" title="The Smurfs and the Magic Flute">The Smurfs and the Magic Flute</a></i>. A few more full-length Smurf films were made, most notably <i>The Baby Smurf</i> and <i>Here are the Smurfs</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> created from episodes of the Hanna-Barbera television cartoon series.</p>

It was suggested that <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a> would play Brainy Smurf, but this "didn't work out" so <a href="/wiki/Fred_Armisen" title="Fred Armisen">Fred Armisen</a> voices Brainy instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A CGI/traditionally animated mini-film, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs:_A_Christmas_Carol" title="The Smurfs: A Christmas Carol">The Smurfs: A Christmas Carol</a></i>, was released on December 2, 2011, on <i>The Smurfs</i> DVD and Blu-ray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A sequel to <i>The Smurfs</i>, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_2" title="The Smurfs 2">The Smurfs 2</a></i>, was released on July 31, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComingSoon_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComingSoon-17">[17]</a></sup> A fully animated <i>Smurfs</i> reboot film, <i><a href="/wiki/Smurfs:_The_Lost_Village" title="Smurfs: The Lost Village">Smurfs: The Lost Village</a></i>, will be released in April 7, 2017 with <a href="/wiki/Demi_Lovato" title="Demi Lovato">Demi Lovato</a> starring as Smurfette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_series">Television series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Television series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_(TV_series)" title="The Smurfs (TV series)">The Smurfs (TV series)</a></div>
<p>The Smurfs secured their place in North American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture">pop culture</a> in 1981, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_morning_cartoon" title="Saturday morning cartoon">Saturday-morning cartoon</a> series <i>The Smurfs</i>, produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanna-Barbera_Productions" title="Hanna-Barbera Productions">Hanna-Barbera Productions</a> in association with SEPP International S.A.R.L, aired on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> from September 12, 1981 to December 2, 1989 (reruns until August 25, 1990). The show continued to air on the USA network until 1993, and on <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a> until 2003. The Smurfs is still broadcast on the <a href="/wiki/Boomerang_(TV_channel)" title="Boomerang (TV channel)">Boomerang</a> station throughout the United States. The show became a major success for NBC, spawning spin-off television specials on an almost yearly basis. <i>The Smurfs</i> was nominated multiple times for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daytime_Emmy" title="Daytime Emmy">Daytime Emmy</a> awards, and won Outstanding Children's Entertainment Series in 1982–1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time50_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time50-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>The Smurfs</i> television show enjoyed continued success until 1990, when, after nearly a decade of success, NBC cancelled it due to decreasing ratings and plans to extend their <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Today_(NBC_program)" title="Today (NBC program)">Today</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_television" title="Breakfast television">morning show</a> franchise to create a <a href="/wiki/Weekend_Today" title="Weekend Today">Saturday edition</a>, although they did not do so until <a href="/wiki/1992_in_American_television" title="1992 in American television">1992</a> (two years later). The decreased ratings were the result of the network changing the format, resulting in the final season featuring regular time travel with only a few Smurfs.</p>
<p>In the TV series, many classical masterpieces are used as background music during the episodes, among them <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Franz Schubert</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Schubert)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Schubert)">Unfinished Symphony</a> (Symphony No. 8 in B minor), <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Grieg" title="Edvard Grieg">Edvard Grieg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Peer_Gynt_(Grieg)" title="Peer Gynt (Grieg)">Peer Gynt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modest_Mussorgsky" title="Modest Mussorgsky">Modest Mussorgsky</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pictures_at_an_Exhibition" title="Pictures at an Exhibition">Pictures at an Exhibition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Reruns of the show are played on the Cartoon Network's sister channel Boomerang.</p>
<p><i>The Smurfs</i> was named the 97th best animated series by <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>. They called it "kiddie cocaine" for people growing up during the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign100_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign100-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchandising">Merchandising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Merchandising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_(merchandising)" title="The Smurfs (merchandising)">The Smurfs (merchandising)</a></div>
<p>From 1959 until the end of the 1960s, Dupuis produced Smurf figurines. But the best known and most widely available Smurf figurines are those made by <a href="/wiki/Schleich" title="Schleich">Schleich</a>, a German toy company. Most of the Smurf figurines given away as promotional material (e.g. by National Garages in the 1970s and <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> in the 1990s) are also made by Schleich.</p>
<p>New Smurf figures continue to appear; in fact, only in two years since 1969 (1991 and 1998) have no new Smurfs entered the market. Schleich currently produces 8 to 12 new figurines a year. Over 300 million of them have been sold so far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time50_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Time50-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other Smurf figurines have been created for advertising purposes, e.g. worldwide for McDonald's and <a href="/wiki/Kinder_Surprise" title="Kinder Surprise">Kinder Surprise</a>, or nationally for e.g. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Heijn" title="Albert Heijn">Albert Heijn</a> in the Netherlands, and <a href="/wiki/Delhaize_Group" title="Delhaize Group">Delhaize</a> in Belgium.</p>
<p>Special Smurf comics were often created for advertising campaigns. This started in the 1960s for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kwatta_(chocolate)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kwatta (chocolate) (page does not exist)">Kwatta</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kellogg_Company" title="Kellogg Company">Kellogg's</a>, and later for companies like <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a> in the UK and Australia, or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Benco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Benco (page does not exist)">Benco</a> (a Dutch chocolate drink).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> These comics were often only part of a larger campaign, e.g. the Benco comics were accompanied by a TV ad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Smurf balloon/float/<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Falloon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Falloon (page does not exist)">Falloon</a> (which is half float half balloon) continues to be presented in holiday parades such as <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" title="Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade">Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smurfs had two cereals in the 1980s made by <a href="/wiki/Post_Cereals" title="Post Cereals">Post Cereals</a>: Smurf-Berry Crunch cereal and Smurfs Magic Berries. Both had animated commercials on Saturday morning. Two Smurfs pastas, made by <a href="/wiki/Chef_Boyardee" title="Chef Boyardee">Chef Boyardee</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DelVerde&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DelVerde (page does not exist)">DelVerde</a> were made in the 1980s, as well. Also <a href="/wiki/Libby%27s" title="Libby's">Libby's</a> &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._J._Heinz_Company" title="H. J. Heinz Company">Heinz</a> came out with Smurf-A-Getti canned pasta for the American and Canadian market.</p>
<p>The Smurfs had a commercial in the 1980s for the Mexican snack cake company <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marinela&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marinela (page does not exist)">Marinela</a> Submarinos with a rare version of <i>Smurfette (Pitufina)</i> dressed in a red dress and her hair done in pigtails.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_recordings">Music recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Music recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_(music)" title="The Smurfs (music)">The Smurfs (music)</a></div>
<p>Over the decades, many singles and albums of Smurf music have been released in different countries and languages, sometimes very successfully, with millions of copies sold. The best known is the single <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Smurf_Song" title="The Smurf Song">The Smurf Song</a></i> and its accompanying album, created by Dutch musician <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Kartner" title="Pierre Kartner">Pierre Kartner</a> who sings under the alias Father Abraham, which reached the #1 position in 16 countries. Worldwide, more than 10 million CDs with Smurf music have been sold between 2005 and 2007 alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time50_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Time50-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, I.M.P.S and R-Tek Music, International created <a href="/wiki/Smurfin!:_Tenth_Anniversary_Commemorative_Album" title="Smurfin!: Tenth Anniversary Commemorative Album">Smurfin!: Tenth Anniversary Commemorative Album</a>, released by Quality Special Products in Canada and The US and Dino Music in Australia. It was also released in parts of Europe. The Album came out on LP, CD and cassette. The LP featured 20 tracks (The Canadian CD and cassette had 16, the American CD had 10). The songs were covers of popular songs like <a href="/wiki/Surfin%27_U.S.A._(song)" title="Surfin' U.S.A. (song)">Surfin' U.S.A.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kokomo_(song)" title="Kokomo (song)">Kokomo</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_Sleeps_Tonight" title="The Lion Sleeps Tonight">The Lion Sleeps Tonight</a> and <a href="/wiki/I_Think_We%27re_Alone_Now" title="I Think We're Alone Now">I Think We're Alone Now</a>, as well as two original songs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurfs_on_Ice">Smurfs on Ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Smurfs on Ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For several years, the Smurfs were the children's act in the <a href="/wiki/Ice_Capades" title="Ice Capades">Ice Capades</a> travelling ice show. After they were retired from that function, the Smurf suits from the show were issued to Ice Capades Chalets, the show's subsidiary chain of <a href="/wiki/Ice_rink" title="Ice rink">ice rinks</a>, lasting until the show was sold to a group of investors led by <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Hamill" title="Dorothy Hamill">Dorothy Hamill</a>. The Chalets were sold to Recreation World. The Smurfette suit in particular had a somewhat different hairstyle from what was portrayed in the Hanna-Barbera cartoons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smurfs_in_theme_parks">Smurfs in theme parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Smurfs in theme parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_(merchandising)" title="The Smurfs (merchandising)">The Smurfs (merchandising)</a></div>
<p>Around 1984, the Smurfs began appearing in North American theme parks owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KECO_Entertainment" title="KECO Entertainment">Kings Entertainment Corporation</a>. Each park featured a Smurfy attraction and Smurf walk-around figures. In 1989, in the France region of Lorraine, the Sorépark group opened a complete Smurfpark, named Big Bang Schtroumpf. In 1991, the park is bought by the successful Belgian Walibi Group and renamed Walibi Schtroumpf with new attractions. After the Walibi Group was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags" title="Six Flags">Six Flags</a>, the park was named Walibi Lorraine, and all the Smurfs in the park were removed (2003).</p>
<p>For a number of years <a href="/wiki/Canada%27s_Wonderland" title="Canada's Wonderland">Canada's Wonderland</a> had an entire Smurf village to walk through, ending with Gargamel's Castle.</p>
<p>Paramount's Carowinds has an artificial island that, for several years during the 1980s and 1990s, was named Smurf Island that had a complete Smurf village—including toadstool houses which could be entered.</p>
<p>In 2020 a Smurfs' theme park will be open in Lleida (Catalonia) (<a class="external free" href="http://www.naciodigital.cat/lleida/noticia/11325/parc/tematic/dels/barrufets/sera/realitat/basses/alpicat" rel="nofollow">http://www.naciodigital.cat/lleida/noticia/11325/parc/tematic/dels/barrufets/sera/realitat/basses/alpicat</a>)</p>

In 2010, the Smurfs expanded into the world of apps for Android, the iPad, iPhone, and iPod Touch with the game <i>Smurf Village</i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UNICEF">UNICEF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: UNICEF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, an advertisement featuring The Smurfs was aired in Belgium in which the Smurf village is annihilated by warplanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Designed as a <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> advertisement, and with the approval of the family of the Smurfs' late creator Peyo, the 25-second episode was shown on the national television after the 9pm timeslot to avoid children seeing it. It was the keystone in a fund-raising campaign by UNICEF's Belgian arm to raise money for the rehabilitation of former <a href="/wiki/Military_use_of_children" title="Military use of children">child soldiers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>—both former Belgian colonies.</p>
<p>In honor of their 50th anniversary in 2008, the Smurfs began a year-long "Happy Smurfday Euro Tour" in connection with UNICEF. The Smurfs visited 15 European countries on the day of their 50th "Smurfday" in the form of publicly distributed white figurines. The recipients could decorate and submit them to a competition. The results of this contest were auctioned off and raised a total amount of 124,700 euros for benefit of UNICEF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coins">Coins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Coins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 50th anniversary of the Smurfs and the 80th anniversary of the birth of its creator <a href="/wiki/Peyo" title="Peyo">Peyo</a>, were celebrated by issuing a high-value collectors' coin: the Belgian 5 euro <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Belgium)#2008_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Belgium)">50th anniversary of The Smurfs commemorative coin</a>, minted in 2008.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">Translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both the comics and cartoons have been translated in many languages. In most cases, the original name "Schtroumpf" is replaced by a new term. The most common are variations on the Dutch translation "Smurf", while other names are indicative of their <a href="/wiki/Gnome" title="Gnome">gnome-like</a> appearance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociological_discussion">Sociological discussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Smurfs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sociological discussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1998, writer Marc Schmidt wrote a parody article citing the Smurfs as an example of the impact of socialism in continental European culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> French sociologist Antoine Buéno described them in a 2011 book as a totalitarian and racist utopia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Studio Peyo head Thierry Culliford, the son of Peyo, dismissed Buéno's accusations as "grotesque and frivolous."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 2011 Marc Schmidt's essay was scrutinized in a response essay by Kate Krake who examined the nature of cultural theory built on textual observation and warned against creating false allegories out of texts like The Smurfs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
