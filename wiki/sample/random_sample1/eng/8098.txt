<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Smörgåsbord</h1>

<p><b>Smörgåsbord</b> (<small>Swedish: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Swedish" title="Help:IPA for Swedish">[ˈsmœ̞rɡɔs'buːɖ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Sv-Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord.oga" title="File:Sv-Smörgåsbord.oga"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/Sv-Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord.oga" title="Sv-Smörgåsbord.oga">listen</a>)</small>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> meal, originating in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, served <a href="/wiki/Buffet" title="Buffet">buffet</a>-style with multiple hot and cold dishes of various foods on a table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Northern Europe, the term varies between 'cold table' and 'buffet': In <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> it is called <b>koldtbord</b> or <b>kaldtbord</b> and in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> <b>det kolde bord</b> (lit. <i>the cold table</i>); in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> <b>kaltes Buffet</b> (lit. <i>cold buffet</i>); in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> it is called <b>hlaðborð</b> (<i>farmyard/courtyard buffet</i>), in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> it is called <b>külmlaud</b> (<i>cold table</i>) or <b>rootsi laud</b> (<i>Swedish table</i>), in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> <b>voileipäpöytä</b> (<i>butter-bread/sandwich table</i>) or <b>ruotsiksi pöytä</b> (<i>Swedish table</i>).</p>
<p>In Eastern Europe, each language has a term that literally means <i>Swedish table</i>: <b>zviedru galds</b> in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> (however <b>aukstais galds</b> - <i>the cold table</i> is also a popular name), <b>švediškas stalas</b> in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, <b>шведский стол</b> (shvedskiy stol) in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <b>szwedzki stół</b> in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <b>шведський стіл</b> (shvedskyi stil) in Ukraine, <b>švédský stůl</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <b>švédsky stôl</b> in <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <b>svédasztal</b> in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <b>шведски сто</b> / <b>švedski sto</b> in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, <b>švedski stol</b> in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <b>švedski stol</b> / <b>švedski sto</b> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnia-Herzegovina" title="Bosnia-Herzegovina">Bosnia-Herzegovina</a> and <b>шведска маса</b> (shvedska masa) in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>. Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> <b>バイキング</b> / <b>ヴァイキング</b> (<i>baikingu</i> / <i>vaikingu</i>, i.e. "Viking") is a popular name used.</p>
<p>Smörgåsbord became internationally known, spelled <i>smorgasbord</i>, at the <a href="/wiki/1939_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1939 New York World's Fair">1939 New York World's Fair</a> when it was offered at the Swedish Pavilion's "Three Crowns Restaurant."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is typically a celebratory meal and guests can help themselves from a range of dishes laid out for their choice. In a <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a>, the term refers to a <a href="/wiki/Buffet" title="Buffet">buffet-style table</a> laid out with many small dishes from which, for a fixed amount of money, one is allowed to choose as many as one wishes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ny_nordisk_mat_(12).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="3504" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Ny_nordisk_mat_%2812%29.jpg/220px-Ny_nordisk_mat_%2812%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Ny_nordisk_mat_%2812%29.jpg/330px-Ny_nordisk_mat_%2812%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Ny_nordisk_mat_%2812%29.jpg/440px-Ny_nordisk_mat_%2812%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ny_nordisk_mat_(12).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Nordic smorgasbord</div>
</div>

<p>The Swedish word <i>smörgåsbord</i> consists of the words <i>smörgås</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_faced_sandwich" title="Open faced sandwich">open-faced sandwich</a>) and <i>bord</i> (table). <i>Smörgås</i> in turn consists of the words <i>smör</i> (butter, cognate with English <i>smear</i>) and <i>gås</i>. <i>Gås</i> literally means goose, but later referred to the small pieces of <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a> that formed and floated to the surface of cream while it was churned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These pieces reminded the old Swedish peasants of fat geese swimming to the surface.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The small butter pieces were just the right size to be placed and flattened out on bread, so <i>smörgås</i> came to mean buttered bread. In Sweden, the term <i>breda smörgåsar</i> (to butter open-faced sandwiches) has been used since at least the 16th century.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and also in Scandinavian languages, the word <i>smörgåsbord</i> (or in English, more usually without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diacritical_mark" title="Diacritical mark">diacritics</a> as <i>smorgasbord</i>) refers loosely to any <a href="/wiki/Buffet" title="Buffet">buffet</a> with a variety of dishes — not necessarily with any connection to the Swedish Christmas traditions discussed in this article. In an extended sense, the word is used to refer to any situation which invites patrons to select whatever they wish among lots of pleasant things, such as the smorgasbord of university courses, books in a bookstore, etc.</p>
<p>The term <i>smörgåsbord</i> should not be confused with the somewhat similarly-named Danish <i><a href="/wiki/Sm%C3%B8rrebr%C3%B8d" title="Smørrebrød">smørrebrød</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sm.C3.B6rg.C3.A5sbord_and_julbord">Smörgåsbord and julbord</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Smörgåsbord and julbord">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A traditional <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <i>smörgåsbord</i> consists of both hot and cold dishes. Bread, butter, and cheese are always part of the <i>smörgåsbord</i>. It is customary to begin with the cold fish dishes which are generally various forms of <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eel</a>. After eating the first portion, people usually continue with the second course (other cold dishes), and round off with hot dishes. <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">Dessert</a> may or may not be included in a smörgåsbord.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Julbord">Julbord</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Julbord">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A special Swedish type of <i>smörgåsbord</i> is the <i>julbord</i> (literally "Christmas table"). The classic Swedish julbord is central to traditional <a href="/wiki/Swedish_cuisine" title="Swedish cuisine">Swedish cuisine</a>, often including bread dipped in ham <a href="/wiki/Broth" title="Broth">broth</a> and continuing with a variety of fish (salmon, herring, whitefish and eel), baked ham, meatballs, pork ribs, <a href="/wiki/Head_cheese" title="Head cheese">head cheese</a>, sausages, potato, <a href="/wiki/Janssons_frestelse" title="Janssons frestelse">Janssons frestelse</a>, boiled potatoes, cheeses, beetroot salad, various forms of boiled cabbage, <a href="/wiki/Kale" title="Kale">kale</a> and rice pudding.</p>
<p>It is customary to eat particular foods together; herring is typically eaten with boiled potatoes and hard-boiled eggs and is frequently accompanied by strong spirits like <a href="/wiki/Snaps" title="Snaps">snaps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%A4nnvin" title="Brännvin">brännvin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Akvavit" title="Akvavit">akvavit</a> with or without spices. Other traditional are smoked eel, <a href="/wiki/Rollmops" title="Rollmops">rollmops</a>, herring salad, baked herring and smoked salmon.</p>

<p>Other dishes are pork sausages (<i><a href="/wiki/Fl%C3%A4skkorv" title="Fläskkorv">fläskkorv</a></i>), smoked pork and potato sausages (<i><a href="/wiki/Isterband" title="Isterband">isterband</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabbage_rolls" title="Cabbage rolls">cabbage rolls</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%A5ldolmar" title="Kåldolmar">kåldolmar</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Baked_beans" title="Baked beans">baked beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omelette" title="Omelette">omelette</a> with shrimps or mushrooms covered with béchamel sauce. Side dishes include <a href="/wiki/Beetroot" title="Beetroot">beetroot</a> salad in mayonnaise and warm stewed red, green or brown <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutfisk" title="Lutfisk">Lutfisk</a>, lyed fish made of stockfish (dried <a href="/wiki/Common_ling" title="Common ling">ling</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> served with boiled potato and thick <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_sauce" title="White sauce">white sauce</a>) and green peas that can be served with the warm dishes or as a separate fourth course. Lutfisk is often served as dinner the second day after the traditional Christmas Yule-table dinner. Julbord desserts include <a href="/wiki/Rice_pudding" title="Rice pudding">rice pudding</a> (<i>risgrynsgröt</i>), sprinkled with <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a> powder.<sup><a class="external text" href="http://media.hd.se/2006/12/14/185624/large/grot1.jpg" rel="nofollow">photo</a></sup> Traditionally, an <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a> is hidden in the bowl of rice porridge and whoever finds it receives a small prize or is recognized for having good luck. Julbord is served from early December until just before Christmas at restaurants and until <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Epiphany</a> in some homes. It is tradition for most Swedish and Norwegian workplaces to hold an annual Julbord between November and January.</p>
<p>In Denmark a typical tradition resembling the Swedish "julbord" is "Julefrokost" ("Christmas lunch"), which involves a wellstocked Danish <a href="/wiki/Danish_cuisine#Det_kolde_bord" title="Danish cuisine">smörgåsbord</a> with cold as well as hot dishes, and plenty of beer and <a href="/wiki/Snaps" title="Snaps">snaps</a>. It is distinct from the Danish Christmas dinner which is served on December 24, and is served as a lunchtime meal, usually for family and friends on December 25 or 26. It is also a tradition for most Danish workplaces to hold an annual Julefrokost some time during the months of November to January as well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The members of the Swedish merchant and upper class in sixteenth-century Sweden and Finland served <a href="/wiki/Schnapps" title="Schnapps">schnapps</a> table (<i>brännvinsbord</i>), a small buffet presented on a side table offering a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hors_d%27oeuvres" title="Hors d'oeuvres">hors d'oeuvres</a> served prior to a meal before sitting at the dinner table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The most simple <i>brännvinsbord</i> was bread, butter, cheese, herring and several types of liqueurs, but smoked salmon, sausages and cold cuts were also served. The <i>brännvinsbord</i> was served as an appetizer for a gathering of people and eaten while standing before a dinner or supper, often two to five hours before dinner, sometimes with the men and women in separate rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordiska_Museet_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nordiska_Museet-5">[5]</a></sup> The <i>smörgåsbord</i> became popular in the mid-seventeenth century, when the food moved from the side table to the main table<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordiska_Museet_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordiska_Museet-5">[5]</a></sup> and service began containing both warm and cold dishes. <i>Smörgåsbord</i> was also served as an appetizer in hotels and later at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway_station" title="Railway station">railway stations</a>, before the <a href="/wiki/Dining_car" title="Dining car">dining cars</a> time for the guests. Restaurants in Stockholm at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1912_Olympic_Games" title="1912 Olympic Games">1912 Olympic Games</a> stopped serving <i>smörgåsbord</i> as an appetizer and started serving them instead as a main course.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_use_of_the_term_in_English">Other use of the term in English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sm%C3%B6rg%C3%A5sbord&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other use of the term in English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is also used as a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> to indicate any diverse group, synonymous with a vast array of possible choices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the final of the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2013" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2013">Eurovision Song Contest 2013</a>, host <a href="/wiki/Petra_Mede" title="Petra Mede">Petra Mede</a> performed a song about all the Swedish clichés, referring to it as a "Swedish Smörgåsbord".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
