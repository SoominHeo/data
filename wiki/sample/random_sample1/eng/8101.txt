<p><b>Smyrna</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Σμύρνη or Σμύρνα</span>) was an Ancient Greek city located at a central and strategic point on the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>. This place is known today as <b><a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">İzmir</a></b>, Turkey. Due to its advantageous port conditions, its ease of defence and its good inland connections, Smyrna rose to prominence. Two sites of the ancient city are today within the boundaries of İzmir. The first site, probably founded by indigenous peoples, rose to prominence during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaic_period_in_Greece" title="Archaic period in Greece">Archaic Period</a> as one of the principal ancient Greek settlements in western Anatolia. The second, whose foundation is associated with <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, reached metropolitan proportions during the period of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. Most of the present-day remains of the ancient city date from the Roman era, the majority from after a 2nd-century AD earthquake.</p>
<p>In practical terms, a distinction is often made between these. <b>Old Smyrna</b> was the initial settlement founded around the 11th century BC, first as an <a href="/wiki/Aeolians" title="Aeolians">Aeolian</a> settlement, and later taken over and developed during the Archaic Period by the <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionians</a>. <b>Smyrna</b> proper was the new city which residents moved to as of the 4th century BC and whose foundation was inspired by Alexander the Great.</p>
<p>Old Smyrna was located on a small peninsula connected to the mainland by a narrow isthmus at the northeastern corner of the inner <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_%C4%B0zmir" title="Gulf of İzmir">Gulf of İzmir</a>, at the edge of a fertile plain and at the foot of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Yamanlar" title="Mount Yamanlar">Mount Yamanlar</a>. This Anatolian settlement commanded the gulf. Today, the archeological site, named <i>Bayraklı Höyüğü,</i> is approximately 700 metres (770 yd) inland, in the Tepekule neighbourhood of <a href="/wiki/Bayrakl%C4%B1" title="Bayraklı">Bayraklı</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Smyrna&amp;params=38_27_51_N_27_10_13_E_type:landmark_region:TR"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">38°27′51″N</span> <span class="longitude">27°10′13″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">38.46417°N 27.17028°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">38.46417; 27.17028</span></span></span></a></span>.</p>
<p><i>New</i> Smyrna developed simultaneously on the slopes of the Mount <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagos" title="Pagos">Pagos</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kadifekale" title="Kadifekale">Kadifekale</a> today) and alongside the coastal strait, immediately below where a small bay existed until the 18th century.</p>
<p>The core of the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> Smyrna is preserved in the large area of <b>İzmir Agora Open Air Museum</b> at this site. Research is being pursued at the sites of both the old and the new cities. This has been conducted since 1997 for Old Smyrna and since 2002 for the Classical Period city, in collaboration between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir_Archaeology_Museum" title="İzmir Archaeology Museum">İzmir Archaeology Museum</a> and the Metropolitan Municipality of İzmir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dd><i>For further information on etymology of the city's name, see <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir#Names_and_etymology" title="İzmir">İzmir#Names and etymology</a>.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Several explanations have been offered for its name. A Greek myth derived the name from an <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponymous</a> <a href="/wiki/Amazons" title="Amazons">Amazon</a> named "Σμύρνα" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smyrna_(Amazon)" title="Smyrna (Amazon)">Smyrna</a>), which was also the name of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quarter_(country_subdivision)" title="Quarter (country subdivision)">quarter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>. This is the basis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myrina_(Mysia)" title="Myrina (Mysia)">Myrina</a>, a city of <a href="/wiki/Aeolis" title="Aeolis">Aeolis</a>.</p>
<p>In inscriptions and coins, the name often was written as "Ζμύρνα" (<i>Zmyrna</i>), "Ζμυρναῖος" (<i>Zmyrneos</i>), "of Smyrna".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name Smyrna may also have been taken from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> word for <a href="/wiki/Myrrh" title="Myrrh">myrrh</a>, "smyrna",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which was the chief export of the city in ancient times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-google-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_millennium_to_687.C2.A0BC">Third millennium to 687 BC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Third millennium to 687 BC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The region was settled at least as of the beginning of the third millennium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ">BC</a>, or perhaps earlier, as the recent finds in <a href="/wiki/Ye%C5%9Filova_H%C3%B6y%C3%BCk" title="Yeşilova Höyük">Yeşilova Höyük</a> suggests. It could have been a city of the autochthonous <a href="/wiki/Leleges" title="Leleges">Leleges</a> before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_people" title="Greek people">Greek</a> colonists started to settle along the coast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> as of the beginning of the first millennium BC. Throughout antiquity Smyrna was a leading city-state of <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionia</a>, with influence over the Aegean shores and islands. Smyrna was also among the cities that claimed <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> as a resident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The early <a href="/wiki/Aeolians" title="Aeolians">Aeolian</a> Greek settlers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lesbos_Island" title="Lesbos Island">Lesbos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyme_(Aeolis)" title="Cyme (Aeolis)">Cyme</a>, expanding eastwards, occupied the valley of Smyrna. It was one of the confederacy of Aeolian city-states, marking the Aeolian frontier with the Ionian colonies.</p>
<p>Strangers or refugees from the Ionian city of <a href="/wiki/Colophon_(city)" title="Colophon (city)">Colophon</a> settled in the city. During an uprising in 688 BC, they took control of the city, making it the thirteenth of the Ionian <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a>. Revised mythologies said it was a colony of Ephesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 688 BC, the Ionian boxer Onomastus of Smyrna won the prize at Olympia, but the <i>coup</i> was probably then a recent event. The Colophonian conquest is mentioned by Mimnermus (before 600 BC), who counts himself equally of Colophon and of Smyrna. The Aeolic form of the name was retained even in the <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">Attic dialect</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> "Aeolian Smyrna" remained current long after the conquest.</p>
<p>Smyrna was located at the mouth of the small river <a href="/wiki/Hermus" title="Hermus">Hermus</a> and at the head of a deep arm of the sea (<i>Smyrnaeus Sinus</i>) that reached far inland. This enabled Greek trading ships to sail into the heart of <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>, making the city part of an essential trade route between Anatolia and the Aegean. During the 7th century BC, Smyrna rose to power and splendor. One of the great trade routes which cross Anatolia descends the Hermus valley past <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a>, and then, diverging from the valley, passes south of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sipylus" title="Mount Sipylus">Mount Sipylus</a> and crosses a low pass into the little valley where Smyrna lies between the mountains and the sea. <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a> and later Ephesus were situated at the sea end of the other great trade route across Anatolia; they competed for a time successfully with Smyrna; but after both cities' harbors silted up, Smyrna was without a rival.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Meles" title="River Meles">Meles River</a>, which flowed by Smyrna, is famous in literature and was worshiped in the valley. A common and consistent tradition connects <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> with the valley of Smyrna and the banks of the Meles; his figure was one of the stock types on coins of Smyrna, one class of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numismatist" title="Numismatist">numismatists</a> call "Homerian." The epithet <i>Melesigenes</i> was applied to him; the cave where he was wont to compose his poems was shown near the source of the river; his temple, the <i>Homereum</i>, stood on its banks. The steady equable flow of the Meles, alike in summer and winter, and its short course, beginning and ending near the city, are celebrated by <a href="/wiki/Aristides" title="Aristides">Aristides</a> and Himerius. The stream rises from abundant springs east of the city and flows into the southeast extremity of the gulf.</p>
<p>The archaic city ("Old Smyrna") contained a temple of <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> from the 7th century BC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lydian_period">Lydian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lydian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>When the Mermnad kings raised the Lydian power and aggressiveness, Smyrna was one of the first points of attack. <a href="/wiki/Gyges_of_Lydia" title="Gyges of Lydia">Gyges</a> (ca. 687—652 BC) was, however, defeated on the banks of the Hermus, the situation of the battlefield showing that the power of Smyrna extended far to the east. A strong fortress was built probably by the Smyrnaean Ionians to command the valley of Nymphi, the <a href="/wiki/Ruins" title="Ruins">ruins</a> of which are still imposing, on a hill in the pass between Smyrna and Nymphi.</p>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theognis" title="Theognis">Theognis</a> (c. 500 BC), it was pride that destroyed Smyrna. <a href="/wiki/Mimnermus" title="Mimnermus">Mimnermus</a> laments the degeneracy of the citizens of his day, who could no longer stem the Lydian advance. Finally, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alyattes_II" title="Alyattes II">Alyattes II</a> (609—560 BC) conquered the city and sacked it, and though Smyrna did not cease to exist, the Greek life and political unity were destroyed, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">polis</a></i> was reorganized on the village system. Smyrna is mentioned in a fragment of Pindar and in an inscription of 388 BC, but its greatness was past.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_period">Hellenistic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hellenistic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> conceived the idea of restoring the Greek city in a scheme that was, according to <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, actually carried out under Antigonus (316—301 BC) and Lysimachus (301 BC—281 BC), who enlarged and fortified the city. The ruined acropolis of the ancient city, the "crown of Smyrna", had been on a steep peak about 380 metres (1,250 ft) high, which overhangs the northeast extremity of the gulf. Modern İzmir was constructed atop the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> city, partly on the slopes of a rounded hill the Greeks called <i>Pagos</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> near the southeast end of the gulf, and partly on the low ground between the hill and the sea. The beauty of the Hellenistic city, clustering on the low ground and rising tier over tier on the hillside, was frequently praised by the ancients and is celebrated on its coins.</p>
<p>Smyrna is shut in on the west by a hill now called Deirmen Tepe, with the ruins of a temple on the summit. The walls of Lysimachus crossed the summit of this hill, and the acropolis occupied the top of Pagus. Between the two the road from Ephesus entered the city by the Ephesian gate, near which was a gymnasium. Closer to the acropolis the outline of the stadium is still visible, and the theatre was situated on the north slopes of Pagus. Smyrna possessed two harbours. The outer harbour was simply the open roadstead of the gulf, and the inner was a small basin with a narrow entrance partially filled up by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamerlane" title="Tamerlane">Tamerlane</a> in 1402 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">AD</a>.</p>
<p>The streets were broad, well paved and laid out at right angles; many were named after temples: the main street, called the Golden, ran across the city from west to east, beginning probably from the temple of Zeus Akraios on the west slope of Pagus, and running round the lower slopes of Pagus (like a necklace on the statue, to use the favorite terms of Aristides the orator) towards Tepecik outside the city on the east, where probably stood the temple of <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a>, worshipped under the name of Meter Sipylene, the patroness of the city. The name is from the nearby Mount Sipylus, which bounds the valley of the city's backlands. The plain towards the sea was too low to be properly drained, and in rainy weather, the streets of the lower town were deep with mud and water.</p>
<p>At the end of the Hellenistic period, in 197 BC, the city suddenly cut its ties with King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eumenes_of_Pergamum" title="Eumenes of Pergamum">Eumenes of Pergamum</a> and instead appealed to Rome for help. Because Rome and Smyrna had no ties until then, Smyrna created a cult of Rome to establish a bond, and the cult eventually became widespread through the whole Roman Empire. As of 195 BC, the city of Rome started to be deified, in the cult to the goddess <a href="/wiki/Roma_(mythology)" title="Roma (mythology)">Roma</a>. In this sense, the Smyrneans can be considered as the creators of the goddess Roma.</p>
<p>In 133 BC, when the last Attalid king Attalus III died without an heir, his will conferred his entire kingdom, including Smyrna, to the Romans. They organized it into the Roman <a href="/wiki/Asia_(Roman_province)" title="Asia (Roman province)">province of Asia</a>, making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pergamum" title="Pergamum">Pergamum</a> the capital. Smyrna, however, as a major seaport, became a leading city in the newly constituted province.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_and_Byzantine_period">Roman and Byzantine period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman and Byzantine period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seven_churches_of_asia.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="944" data-file-width="623" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Seven_churches_of_asia.svg/200px-Seven_churches_of_asia.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Seven_churches_of_asia.svg/300px-Seven_churches_of_asia.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Seven_churches_of_asia.svg/400px-Seven_churches_of_asia.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>As one of the principal cities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Asia" title="Roman Asia">Roman Asia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Smyrna_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Smyrna-10">[10]</a></sup> Smyrna vied with Ephesus and Pergamum for the title "First City of Asia."</p>
<p>A Christian church and a <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_of_Smyrna" title="Metropolis of Smyrna">bishopric</a> existed here from a very early time, probably originating in the considerable Jewish colony. It was one of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_churches_of_Asia" title="Seven churches of Asia">seven churches</a> addressed in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Saint <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_of_Antioch" title="Ignatius of Antioch">Ignatius of Antioch</a> visited Smyrna and later wrote letters to its bishop, <a href="/wiki/Polycarp" title="Polycarp">Polycarp</a>. A mob of Jews and pagans abetted the martyrdom of Polycarp in AD 153.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Smyrna_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Smyrna-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Saint Irenaeus</a>, who heard Polycarp as a boy, was probably a native of Smyrna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford:Smyrna_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford:Smyrna-10">[10]</a></sup> Another famous resident of the same period was <a href="/wiki/Aelius_Aristides" title="Aelius Aristides">Aelius Aristides</a>.</p>
<p>Polycrates reports a succession of bishops including Polycarp of Smryna, as well as others in nearby cities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melito" title="Melito">Melito</a> of Sardis. Related to that time the German historian W. Bauer wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Asian Jewish Christianity received in turn the knowledge that henceforth the "church" would be open without hesitation to the Jewish influence mediated by Christians, coming not only from the apocalyptic traditions, but also from the synagogue with its practices concerning worship, which led to the appropriation of the Jewish passover observance. Even the observance of the sabbath by Christians appears to have found some favor in Asia...we find that in post-apostolic times, in the period of the formation of ecclesiastical structure, the Jewish Christians in these regions come into prominence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the late 2nd century, <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a> also noted:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Polycarp also was not only instructed by apostles, and conversed with many who had seen Christ, but was also, by apostles in Asia, appointed bishop of the Church in Smyrna…always taught the things which he had learned from the apostles, and which the Church has handed down, and which alone are true. To these things all the Asiatic Churches testify, as do also those men who have succeeded Polycarp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> wrote c. 208 AD.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Anyhow the heresies are at best novelties, and have no continuity with the teaching of Christ. Perhaps some heretics may claim Apostolic antiquity: we reply: Let them publish the origins of their churches and unroll the catalogue of their bishops till now from the Apostles or from some bishop appointed by the Apostles, as the Smyrnaeans count from Polycarp and John, and the Romans from Clement and Peter; let heretics invent something to match this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Hence, apparently the church in Smyrna was one of only two that Tertullian felt could have had some type of apostolic succession. During the mid-3rd century, however, changes occurred in Asia Minor, and most there became affiliated with the Greco-Roman churches.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> became the seat of government, the trade between Anatolia and the West diminished in importance, and Smyrna declined. The Seljuk commander <a href="/wiki/Tzachas" title="Tzachas">Tzachas</a> seized Smyrna in 1084 and used it as a base for naval raids, but the city was recovered by the general <a href="/wiki/John_Doukas_(megas_doux)" title="John Doukas (megas doux)">John Doukas</a>. The city was several times ravaged by the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turks</a>, and had become quite ruinous when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicaean_emperor" title="Nicaean emperor">Nicaean emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_III_Doukas_Vatatzes" title="John III Doukas Vatatzes">John III Doukas Vatatzes</a> rebuilt it about 1222.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Batuta" title="Ibn Batuta">Ibn Batuta</a> found it still in great part a ruin when the homonymous chieftain of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatolian_Turkish_Beyliks" title="Anatolian Turkish Beyliks">Beylik</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beylik_of_Ayd%C4%B1n" title="Beylik of Aydın">Aydın</a> had conquered it about 1330 and made his son Umur governor. It became the port of the emirate. Soon afterwards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_Saint_John" title="Knights of Saint John">Knights of Saint John</a> established themselves in the town but failed to conquer the citadel. In 1402, <a href="/wiki/Timur" title="Timur">Tamerlane</a> stormed the town and massacred almost all the inhabitants. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> conquest was only temporary, but Smyrna was recovered by the Turks under the Aydın dynasty after which it became <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a>, when the Ottomans took over the lands of Aydın.</p>
<p>Greek influence was so strong in the area that the Turks called it "Smyrna of the infidels" (Gavur İzmir).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> While Turkish sources track the emergence of the term to the 14th century when two separate parts of the city were controlled by two different powers, the upper İzmir being Muslim and the lower part of the city Christian.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the late 19th and early 20th century, the city was an important financial and cultural center of the Greek world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Out of the 391 factories 322 belonged to local Greeks, while 3 out of the 9 banks were backed by Greek capital. Education was also dominated by the local Greek communities with 67 male and 4 female schools in total. The Ottomans continued to control the area, with the exception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_administration_of_Smyrna_(1919%E2%80%931922)" title="Greek administration of Smyrna (1919–1922)">1919–1922 period</a>, when the city was assigned to Greece by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_S%C3%A8vres" title="Treaty of Sèvres">Treaty of Sèvres</a>.</p>
<p>The most important Greek educational institution of the region was the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_School_of_Smyrna" title="Evangelical School of Smyrna">Evangelical School</a> that operated from 1733 to 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_World_War_I">Post World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the end of the First World War Greece occupied Smyrna from 15 May 1919 and put in place a military administration. The Greek premier <a href="/wiki/Eleftherios_Venizelos" title="Eleftherios Venizelos">Venizelos</a> had plans to annex Smyrna and he seemed to be realizing his objective in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_S%C3%A8vres" title="Treaty of Sèvres">Treaty of Sèvres</a>, signed 10 August 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mango217_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mango217-17">[17]</a></sup> (However, this treaty was not ratified by the parties; the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lausanne" title="Treaty of Lausanne">Treaty of Peace of Lausanne</a> replaced it.)</p>
<p>The occupation of Smyrna <a href="/wiki/Liberation_of_%C4%B0zmir" title="Liberation of İzmir">came to an end</a> when the Turkish army of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Kemal Atatürk">Kemal Atatürk</a> entered the city on September 9, 1922, at the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Turkish_War_(1919%E2%80%931922)" title="Greco-Turkish War (1919–1922)">Greco-Turkish War (1919–1922)</a>. The occupation was marked by widespread murder, rape, and looting by the Turkish National Army of the Christian citizens, culminating in a purposefully set fire on September 13, 1922 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_Smyrna" title="Great Fire of Smyrna">Great Fire of Smyrna</a> which completed the massacre of the Greek and Armenian populations. The death toll is estimated to range from 10,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google92_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-google92-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naimark.2C_.27.27Fires_of_Hatred.27.27.2C_p._52_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naimark.2C_.27.27Fires_of_Hatred.27.27.2C_p._52-19">[19]</a></sup> to 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transaction233_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-transaction233-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naimark47_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-naimark47-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agora">Agora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Agora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The remains of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Agora" title="Agora">agora</a> of Smyrna constitute today the space of <i>İzmir Agora Museum</i> in İzmir's Namazgah quarter, although its area is commonly referred to as <b>"Agora"</b> by the city's inhabitants.</p>
<p>Situated on the northern slopes of the Pagos hills, it was the commercial, judicial and political nucleus of the ancient city, its center for artistic activities and for teaching.</p>
<p><i>İzmir Agora Open Air Museum</i> consists of five parts, including the agora area, the base of the northern <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> gate, the stoa and the ancient shopping centre.</p>
<p>The agora of Smyrna was built during the Hellenistic era. After a destructive earthquake in 178 AD, Smyrna was rebuilt in the Roman period (2nd century AD) under the emperor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, according to an urban plan drawn by <a href="/wiki/Hippodamus_of_Miletus" title="Hippodamus of Miletus">Hippodamus of Miletus</a>. The bust of the emperor's wife <a href="/wiki/Faustina_the_Younger" title="Faustina the Younger">Faustina</a> on the second arch of the western stoa confirms this fact. It was constructed on a sloping terrain in three floors, close to the city center. The terrain is 165 m wide and 200 m long. It is bordered on all sides by <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">porticos</a>. Because a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> and later an <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> cemetery were located over the ruins of the agora, it was preserved from modern constructions. This agora is now the largest and the best preserved among <a href="/wiki/Ionic_order" title="Ionic order">Ionian</a> agoras. The agora is now surrounded by modern buildings that still cover its eastern and southern parts. The agora was used until the Byzantine period.</p>
<p>On entering the courtyard, to the left is the western <a href="/wiki/Stoa" title="Stoa">stoa</a>, in the back the <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> and on the right side the Ottoman cemetery. The courtyard was surrounded by porticoes on three sides. The basilica and the western portico were built over an infrastructure of basements with round arches to protect them against future earthquakes. The eastern end and the southern porticoes consisted of a two-floor compounded structure. Beneath the basilica was a covered market place. The design of the basement has a strong resemblance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crypto-porticus" title="Crypto-porticus">crypto-porticus</a> constructions of the western provinces. The monumental entrance at the eastern side was one of the most magnificent and arched structures of the Hellenistic era.</p>
<p>A two-storied stoa, 17.5 m wide, was constructed at the eastern and western side of the agora. Each stoa was divided in three galleries by two rows of columns. Each stoa had an upper story. The stoas were protected from sun and rain by a roof. These impressive structures measured 75 m by 18 m. The southern part of the western stoa has many water channels and large water reservoirs, pointing to the presence of water in the agora.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Excavations">Excavations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Excavations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1088" data-file-width="2006" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg/250px-1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg/375px-1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg/500px-1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1843_Chenavard_Agora.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Engraving with a view of the site of Smyrna Agora a few years after the first explorations (1843).</div>
</div>

<p>Although Smyrna was explored by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Texier" title="Charles Texier">Charles Texier</a> in the 19th century and the German consul in İzmir had purchased the land around the ancient theater in 1917 to start excavations, the first scientific digs can be said to have started in 1927. Most of the discoveries were made by archaeological exploration carried as an extension during the period between 1931 and 1942 by the German archaeologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Naumann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rudolf Naumann (page does not exist)">Rudolf Naumann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sel%C3%A2hattin_Kantar" title="Selâhattin Kantar">Selâhattin Kantar</a>, the director of İzmir and Ephesus museums. They uncovered a three-floor, rectangular compound with stairs in the front, built on columns and arches around a large courtyard in the middle of the building.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>New excavations in the agora began in 1996. They have continued since 2002 under the sponsorship of the Metropolitan Municipality of İzmir. A primary school adjacent to the agora that had burned in 1980 was not reconstructed. Instead, its space was incorporated into the historical site. The area of the agora was increased to 16,590 square metres (178,600 sq ft). This permitted the evacuation of a previously unexplored zone. The archaeologists and the local authorities, means permitting, are also keenly eyeing a neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Multi-storey_car_park" title="Multi-storey car park">multi-storey car park</a>, which is known to cover an important part of the ancient settlement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During the present renovations the old restorations in concrete are gradually being replaced by marble.</p>
<p>The new excavation has uncovered the agora's northern gate. It has been concluded that embossed figures of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Hestia" title="Hestia">Hestia</a> found in these digs were a continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> uncovered during the first digs. Statues of the gods <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dionysos" title="Dionysos">Dionysos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eros_(mythology)" title="Eros (mythology)">Eros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> have also been found, as well as many statues, heads, embossments, figurines and monuments of people and animals, made of marble, stone, bone, glass, metal and terracotta. Inscriptions found here list the people who provided aid to Smyrna after the earthquake of 178 AD.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 20th-century, there were mills <a href="/wiki/Spinning_(textiles)" title="Spinning (textiles)">spinning</a> <a href="/wiki/Thread_(yarn)" title="Thread (yarn)">thread</a>. As of 1920, there were two factories in Smyrna dyeing <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">yarn</a>, which were owned by British companies. These companies employed over 60,000 people. During this time, there was also a <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> owned <a href="/wiki/Cotton_mill" title="Cotton mill">cotton spinning mill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero111_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero111-22">[22]</a></sup> The city also produced soap made of refuse <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Ironwork" title="Ironwork">ironworks</a>, also owned by the British, produced tools and equipment. Those tools were used to extract <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannin</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valonia_oak" title="Valonia oak">valonia oak</a>. As of 1920, the ironwork was exporting 5,000 tons of product a year. The city also produced wooden boxes, which were used for <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">fig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">raisin</a> storage. The wood for the boxes was imported from Austria and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero112_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero112-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponyms">Toponyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smyrna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Toponyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several American cities have been named after Smyrna, including <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Georgia" title="Smyrna, Georgia">Smyrna, Georgia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Tennessee" title="Smyrna, Tennessee">Smyrna, Tennessee</a>; <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Delaware" title="Smyrna, Delaware">Smyrna, Delaware</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Michigan" title="Smyrna, Michigan">Smyrna, Michigan</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-google2-24">[24]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/New_Smyrna_Beach,_Florida" title="New Smyrna Beach, Florida">New Smyrna Beach, Florida</a>.</p>
