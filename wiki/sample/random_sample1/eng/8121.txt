<p><b>Sveriges Riksbank</b>, or simply <b>Riksbanken</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. It is the world's oldest central bank<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_banks" title="List of oldest banks">3rd oldest bank still in operation</a>. It is sometimes called the <b>Swedish National Bank</b> or the <b>Bank of Sweden</b> (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Swedbank" title="Swedbank">Swedbank</a>, a retail bank).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Riksbank began operations in 1668. Previously, Sweden was served by the <a href="/wiki/Stockholms_Banco" title="Stockholms Banco">Stockholms Banco</a> (also known as the Bank of Palmstruch), which was founded by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Palmstruch" title="Johan Palmstruch">Johan Palmstruch</a> in 1656. Although the bank was private, it was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_monarch" title="Swedish monarch">king</a> who chose its management: in a letter to Palmstruch, he gave permission to its operations according to stated regulations. But Stockholms Banco collapsed as a result of the issuing of too many notes without the necessary collateral. Palmstruch, who was considered responsible for the bank's losses, was condemned to death, but later received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clemency" title="Clemency">clemency</a>. On 17 September 1668, the privilege of Palmstruch to operate a bank was transferred to the <i>Riksens Ständers Bank</i> ("Bank of the Estates of the Realm") and was run under the auspices of the parliament of the day. Due to the failure of Stockholm Banco, the new bank was managed under the direct control of the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag_of_the_Estates" title="Riksdag of the Estates">Riksdag of the Estates</a> to prevent the interference from the king. When a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Sweden" title="Parliament of Sweden">Riksdag</a> was instituted in 1866, the name of the bank was changed to Sveriges Riksbank.</p>
<p>Having learnt the lesson of the Stockholms Banco experience, the Riksbank was not permitted to issue bank-notes. Nevertheless, in 1701, permission was granted to issue so called credit-notes". Some time in the middle of the 18th century, counterfeit notes began appearing, which caused serious problems. To prevent <a href="/wiki/Forgery" title="Forgery">forgeries</a>, it was decided that the Riksbank should produce its own paper for bank-notes and a paper-mill, <a href="/wiki/Tumba_Bruk" title="Tumba Bruk">Tumba Bruk</a>, was founded in <a href="/wiki/Tumba,_Sweden" title="Tumba, Sweden">Tumba</a>, on the outskirts of Stockholm.</p>
<p>A few years later, the first commercial banks were founded and these were also allowed to issue bank-notes. The bank-notes represented a claim to the bank without <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> paid, and thus became a considerable source of <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> for banks. Nonetheless, security in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Deposit_account" title="Deposit account">deposit</a> at the Riksbank was required to cover the <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> of all notes issued.</p>

<p>During the 19th century, the Riksbank maintained a dominant position as a <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> institution and issuer of bank-notes. The bank also managed national trade transactions as well as continuing to provide credit to the general public. The first branch-office was opened in 1824, later followed with subsidiary branches opening in each county (<i>län</i>). The present operational activities as a central bank differ from those during the 19th century. For example, no interest-rate-related activities were conducted.</p>
<p>The position of the Riksbank as a <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> dates back to 1897, when the first Riksbank Act was accepted concurrently with a law giving the Riksbank the exclusive right to issue bank-notes. This <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> concluded its role and importance regarding <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> in a modern sense, as the exclusive right to issue notes is a condition when conducting monetary policy and defending the value of a currency. Behind the decision were repeated demands that the private banks should cease to issue notes as it was considered that the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profits</a> should befall the general public.</p>
<p>The Swedish currency was <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">backed by gold</a> and the paper-certificates could be exchanged for gold coins until 1931, when a specialized temporary law freed the bank from this obligation. This law was renewed every year until the new constitution was ratified in 1975 which split the bank from the government into a stand-alone organization not obligated to exchange notes for gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1992, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_exchange_rate" title="Fixed exchange rate">fixed exchange rate</a> regime of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Krona" title="Swedish Krona">Swedish Krona</a> collapsed. A few months later, in January 1993, the Governing Board of the Riksbank developed a new monetary policy regime based on a <a href="/wiki/Floating_exchange_rate" title="Floating exchange rate">floating exchange rate</a> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflation_target" title="Inflation target">inflation target</a>. These policies were extensively influenced by assistance from the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Canada" title="Bank of Canada">Bank of Canada</a>, which had extensive previous experience controlling <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>, while being a similar <a href="/wiki/Small_open_economy" title="Small open economy">small open economy</a>, heavily subject to foreign exchange rate swings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1991 to 1993, Sweden experienced its most severe <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a> since the 1930s termed the "<a href="/wiki/Swedish_banking_rescue" title="Swedish banking rescue">Swedish banking rescue</a>". It forced inflation down to around 2%, and inflation continued to be low during the subsequent years of strong growth in the late 1990s.</p>
<p>During the 2000s, the operations and administrative departments were downsized on behalf of the policy departments Financial Stability Department and Monetary Policy Department. A direct consequence of the changing times was that the Riksbank closed down all its branches in Sweden and outsourced the handling of coins and bills to a private company. Today the policy departments are the core of the central bank and they employ about half of the bank's 350 full-time posts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motto">Motto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Motto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The motto of the Bank is <i>Hinc robur et securitas</i>, which is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">latin</a> for "Herefore strength and safety" (<i>"Härav styrka och säkerhet"</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sveriges_Riksbank_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences_in_Memory_of_Alfred_Nobel">Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following its third centennial in 1968, the Bank instituted the annual <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences" title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences">Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel</a>, which is awarded with the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prizes</a> at the Prize Award Ceremony in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, on 10 December, the anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Nobel" title="Alfred Nobel">Alfred Nobel</a>'s death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_interest_rates">Negative interest rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Negative interest rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 2 July 2009 Sweden's Riksbank was the first central bank in the world to implement a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_interest_rate" title="Negative interest rate">negative interest rate</a>, when it lowered its <a href="/wiki/Bank_rate" title="Bank rate">repo rate</a> (the rate at which a central bank lends short-term money to commercial banks against securities) to 0.25%. This caused its linked overnight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposit_rate" title="Deposit rate">deposit rate</a> (the interest commercial banks get for depositing money with the central bank overnight) to be pushed down to −0.25%, while the overnight <a href="/wiki/Bank_rate" title="Bank rate">lending rate</a> (the interest a central bank charges commercial banks for overnight lending) was lowered to 0.75%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riksbanken_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riksbanken-6">[6]</a></sup> This was done to counter economic slowdown due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">financial crises of 2008</a>. The bank's Deputy Governor <a href="/wiki/Lars_E._O._Svensson" title="Lars E. O. Svensson">Lars E. O. Svensson</a> stated that he had preferred a cut of the repo rate to 0.00%, as this would "entail a better balanced monetary policy, with lower unemployment and higher resource utilisation without inflation deviating too far from the target."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riksbanken_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riksbanken-6">[6]</a></sup> The Swedish move to a negative discount rate was followed with great interest by central banks around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 October 2014, the Riksbank cut its repo rate to 0.00%, as advocated by Deputy Governor Svensson already in July 2009. which pushed the linked deposit rate to −0.75%, while the lending rate remained at 0.75%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 February 2015 the repo rate was lowered again, to –0.10%. The Riksbank announced at the same time that it would buy government bonds for SEK 30 billion, and that more measures was probably to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The deposit rate was lowered to –0.85%, and the lending rate to 0.65%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repotable_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Repotable-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 March 2015, the Riksbank cut the repo rate even further, to –0.25%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riksbanken032015_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riksbanken032015-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The bank announced at the same time that it was buying government bonds worth SEK 30 billion (USD 3.4 billion, €3.2 billion) to prevent an appreciating krona from hindering an uptick in inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-15">[15]</a></sup> Inflation has been close to zero in Sweden since late 2012 and in February it was at 0.1%, far below the target of 2.0%, and the purpose of these moves was to stimulate inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-15">[15]</a></sup> The bank announced that it intends to keep the rate at –0.25%. "at least until the second half of 2016."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riksbanken032015_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riksbanken032015-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-15">[15]</a></sup> The deposit rate was as a consequence lowered to –1.00% and the lending rate to 0.50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repotable_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Repotable-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Riksbank has consequently lowered the rate two additional times, first on 8 July 2015 down 0.10 percentage points to -0.35 and most recently, on 17 February 2016 it was down another 0.15 points to -0.50. The accompanying deposit and lending rates now lies at -1.25 and 0.25, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repotable_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Repotable-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repotable16_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Repotable16-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
