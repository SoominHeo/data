<p><b>The Swiss National Bank</b> (<b>SNB</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, and is therefore responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> of the nation of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and also for the issuing of <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">Swiss franc</a> <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknotes</a>.</p>
<p>The bank is otherwise known as: <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Schweizerische Nationalbank</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Banque nationale suisse</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Banca nazionale svizzera</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a>: <span lang="rm" xml:lang="rm"><i>Banca naziunala svizra</i></span>, which are the four official languages of the country.</p>
<p>The SNB is an <a href="/wiki/Aktiengesellschaft" title="Aktiengesellschaft">aktiengesellschaft</a> under special regulations, and has two head offices, one is in Bern and the other one in Zurich.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bank formed as a result of the need for a reduction in the number of banks of issue, which numbered 53 sometime after 1826. In the 1874 revision of the Federal Constitution it was given the task to oversee laws concerning the issuing of banknotes. Then in 1891 the Federal Constitution was revised again to entrust the Confederation with sole rights to issue banknotes. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Bank_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Bank Law (page does not exist)">National Bank Law</a> was enforced on 16 January 1906, and the Nationalbank began business activities on 20 June 1907, and is thought then founded sometime during either 1906 or 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manfred_Pohl.2C_Sabine_Freitag_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manfred_Pohl.2C_Sabine_Freitag-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ivan_Tibor_Berend_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ivan_Tibor_Berend-2">[2]</a></sup> SNB itself states that it was founded in 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sometime during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> (1914-1917 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>), the bank was instructed to release notes of a small denomination, for the first time, by the Federal Council of Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manfred_Pohl.2C_Sabine_Freitag_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Manfred_Pohl.2C_Sabine_Freitag-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Council_(Switzerland)" title="Federal Council (Switzerland)">Bundesrat</a> devalued the Swiss Franc during 1936, and as a result, there was made available to the Nationalbank, an amount of monies, which the bank subsequently stored in a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%A4hrungsausgleischsfonds&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Währungsausgleischsfonds (page does not exist)">Währungsausgleischsfonds</a> reserve for the future, for usage in situations of emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ulrich_Bindseil.2C_Rudolf_Richter.2C_Justus_Haucap.2C_Christian_Wey_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ulrich_Bindseil.2C_Rudolf_Richter.2C_Justus_Haucap.2C_Christian_Wey-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1981 the bank participated in research involving <a href="/wiki/Orell_F%C3%BCssli" title="Orell Füssli">Orell Füssli</a> and an optical research group named <a href="/wiki/Landis%2BGyr" title="Landis+Gyr">Landis and Gyr</a>, of matters of banknote design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 1994 the Bank was described as a <a href="/wiki/Joint-stock_company" title="Joint-stock company">joint-stock company</a> acting under the administration and supervision of the Confederation. It had eight branches and twenty sub-branches within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantons" title="Cantons">cantons</a>. The governing board had overall executive management of the Nationalbank, with supervision entrusted to its shareholders, the banks' council, the banks' committee, its local committees and auditing committee. There were three members of the governing board, who together decided the monetary policy of the Nationalbank. Towards the end of 1993, there were 566 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manfred_Pohl.2C_Sabine_Freitag_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Manfred_Pohl.2C_Sabine_Freitag-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the inception of Article 99 of the Federal Constitution, in May 2004, the Nationalbank achieved formal independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Currency_Review_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-International_Currency_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2015 the Nationalbank was privately owned,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stephen_Mitford_Goodson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stephen_Mitford_Goodson-8">[8]</a></sup> with the majority of shares belonging to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantons" title="Cantons">cantons</a> (45%) and banks of cantons (15%), and the smaller remainder in the possession of private individuals (40%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Shares of the SNB existed within <a href="/wiki/SIX_Swiss_Exchange" title="SIX Swiss Exchange">SIX Swiss Exchange</a> from 1907 onward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exchange_rates">Exchange rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exchange rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nationalbank made an announcement on 6 September 2011, of its intention to address changes in the value of the Swiss Franc to the Euro. More specifically, that it wanted the value of the Franc to fall below 1.2 to the Euro. A cap was placed on exchange-rates <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> in order to take measures to stem the development of a possible recession. The bank stated the 1.2 exchange value was defendable as the bank could potentially proceed to mint enough banknotes to control the rate sufficiently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMMA_THOMASSON_.26_CATHERINE_BOSLEY_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-EMMA_THOMASSON_.26_CATHERINE_BOSLEY-14">[14]</a></sup> The bank announced on January 15, 2015 the Euro currency arrangement would end as the Euro crisis had passed and the Europeans would be making financial policy changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The basic governing principles of the Nationalbank are contained within Article 99 of the Federal Constitution, which deals with matters of monetary policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swiss_National_Bank_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swiss_National_Bank-16">[16]</a></sup> There are three numbered factors concerning principles explicitly mentioning the Nationalbank, of four altogether shown within the Article. The SNB is therefore obliged by constitutional statute law to act in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a> interests of Switzerland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> accordingly, the prime function of the Nationalbank is:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>to pursue a reliable monetary policy for the benefit of the Swiss economy and the Swiss people.</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Nationalbank publishes within its own site a list of research done as work in progress by staff members, which begin at 2004 (2 papers), to 2005 (2), 2006 (11), 2007 (17), 2008 (19), 2009 (16), 2010 (19), 2011 (14), 2012 (16), 2013 (11), 2014 (13), and to 1 August 2015 there is shown nine papers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> a list of eight economic studies which relate to the tasks of the bank, listed from 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> in addition to a bi-annually published update of research, listed from 2012 to the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cash_supply_and_distribution">Cash supply and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cash supply and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Bank is entrusted with the note-issuing privilege. It supplies the economy with banknotes that meet high standards with respect to quality and security. It is also charged by the Confederation with the task of coin distribution.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cashless_payment_transactions">Cashless payment transactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cashless payment transactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the field of cashless payment transactions, the National Bank provides services for payments between banks. These are settled in the Swiss Interbank Clearing (SIC) system via sight deposit accounts held with the National Bank.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment_of_currency_reserves">Investment of currency reserves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Investment of currency reserves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Bank manages currency reserves. These engender confidence in the Swiss franc, help to prevent and overcome crises and may be utilized for interventions in the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange market</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_system_stability">Financial system stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Financial system stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Bank contributes to the stability of the financial system by acting as an arbiter over monetary policy. Within the context of this task, it analyses sources of risk to the financial system, oversees systemically important payment and securities settlement systems and helps to promote an operational environment for the financial sector.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_monetary_cooperation">International monetary cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International monetary cooperation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Together with the federal authorities, the National Bank participates in international monetary cooperation and provides technical assistance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Banker_to_the_Confederation">Banker to the Confederation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Banker to the Confederation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Bank acts as banker to the Swiss Confederation. It processes payments on behalf of the Confederation, issues money market debt register claims and bonds, handles the safekeeping of securities and carries out money market and foreign exchange transactions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Bank compiles statistical data on banks and financial markets, the balance of payments, the international investment position and the Swiss financial accounts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monetary_policy">Monetary policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Monetary policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SNB pursues a monetary policy serving the interests of the country as a whole. It must ensure price stability, while taking due account of economic developments. Monetary policy affects production and prices with a considerable time lag. Consequently, it is based on inflation forecasts rather than current inflation. The SNB’s monetary policy strategy consists of three elements: a definition of price stability (the SNB equates price stability with a rise in the national consumer price index of less than 2% per year), a medium-term conditional inflation forecast, and, at operational level, a target range for a reference interest rate, which is the Libor for three-month investments in Swiss francs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Meeting_of_Shareholders">General Meeting of Shareholders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: General Meeting of Shareholders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The General Meeting of Shareholders is held once a year, as a rule in April. Owing to the SNB’s public mandate, the powers of the Shareholders’ Meeting are not as extensive as in joint-stock companies under private law.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bank_Council">Bank Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bank Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bank Council oversees and controls the conduct of business by the Swiss National Bank and consists of 11 members. Six members, including the President and Vice President, are appointed by the Federal Council, and five by the Shareholders’ Meeting. The Bank Council sets up four committees from its own ranks: an Audit Committee, a Risk Committee, a Remuneration Committee and an Appointment Committee.</p>
<p>A list of the Bank Council members is published on the SNB website (see: <a class="external free" href="http://www.snb.ch/en/iabout/snb/bodies/id/snb_bodies_council" rel="nofollow">http://www.snb.ch/en/iabout/snb/bodies/id/snb_bodies_council</a> )</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governing_Board">Governing Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Governing Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Swiss National Bank’s management and executive body is the Governing Board. The Governing Board is responsible in particular for monetary policy, asset management strategy, contributing to the stability of the financial system and international monetary cooperation. The Governing Board consists of 3 members:</p>
<ul>
<li>Chairman: <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jordan_(economist)" title="Thomas Jordan (economist)">Thomas Jordan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snb.ch_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-snb.ch-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>Vice Chairman: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fritz_Zurbr%C3%BCgg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fritz Zurbrügg (page does not exist)">Fritz Zurbrügg</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snb.ch_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-snb.ch-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>Member: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9a_M._Maechler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andréa M. Maechler (page does not exist)">Andréa M. Maechler</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gold_reserves">Gold reserves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Gold reserves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Swiss_gold_reserves.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="220" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a3/Swiss_gold_reserves.jpg" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Swiss_gold_reserves.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Switzerland's gold reserves</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The SNB manages the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_gold_reserves" title="Official gold reserves">official gold reserves</a> of Switzerland, which as of 2008 amount to 1145 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> and are valued at 30.5 billion <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">CHF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bund2008_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bund2008-23">[23]</a></sup> The gold is believed to be stored in huge vaults beneath the Federal Square (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bundesplatz_(Bern)" title="Bundesplatz (Bern)">Bundesplatz</a></i>) to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Palace_of_Switzerland" title="Federal Palace of Switzerland">federal Parliament building</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bern" title="Bern">Bern</a>, but the SNB treats the location of the gold reserves as a secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bund2008_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bund2008-23">[23]</a></sup> Independent confirmation of the gold's location was obtained by the Bernese newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Bund" title="Der Bund">Der Bund</a></i> in 2008. It published a photograph of the bullion that a keystone photographer was allowed to take at the SNB premises in Bern in 2001. <i>Der Bund</i> also quoted a retired official of the city's <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">surveying</a> office as saying that the gold vaults take up an area of roughly half the Federal Square and have a depth of dozens of meters, down to the level of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aar" title="Aar">Aar</a> river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bund2008_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bund2008-23">[23]</a></sup> The SNB says that the gold reserves are stored in different safe places in Switzerland (70%) and abroad (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Canada" title="Bank of Canada">Bank of Canada</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the latter years of the 1990s until sometime during 2005, the Nationalbank transferred from its possession an amount of gold deemed superfluous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ulrich_Bindseil.2C_Rudolf_Richter.2C_Justus_Haucap.2C_Christian_Wey_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ulrich_Bindseil.2C_Rudolf_Richter.2C_Justus_Haucap.2C_Christian_Wey-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a></div>
<p>The Swiss National Bank provided 1.2 billion <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">CHF</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Reichsbank" title="Reichsbank">Reichsbank</a>, of this, a value of approximately 780 million CHF of the gold given to the Nationalbank was gold which had been looted by the forces of Germany. In addition the Nationalbank also exchanged between 1.2–1.6 billion CHF for gold from the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-P._Marguerat_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-P._Marguerat-25">[25]</a></sup> During 20 April 1944, gold from the gold reserves of Italy arrived from <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> at the railway station within <a href="/wiki/Chiasso" title="Chiasso">Chiasso</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gianni_Toniolo.2C_Piet_Clement_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gianni_Toniolo.2C_Piet_Clement-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is controversy over the role of the Swiss National Bank in the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_gold" title="Nazi gold">Nazi gold</a> during World War II. The SNB was the largest gold distribution centre in <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a> before the war. A study by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">U.S. Department of State</a> in 1997 notes that the Bank, "must have known that some portion of the gold it was receiving from the Reichsbank was looted from occupied countries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eizenstat_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eizenstat-27">[27]</a></sup> This was confirmed by the Swiss <a href="/wiki/Bergier_commission" title="Bergier commission">Bergier commission</a> in 1998 which concluded that the SNB received US$440 million in gold from Nazi sources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> of which US$316 million is estimated to have been looted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The gold from Nazi governship sources was in the form of ingots containing gold looted from Central Banks of Europe and gold from Jewish persons executed within the <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">concentration camps</a> established by the machination of the Nazi regime, which the SNB took without knowing these facts at the time, nor inquiring to any great degree in the process of its transfer into the possession of the SNB, according to a former Archivist of the SNB <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Vogler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Vogler (page does not exist)">Robert Vogler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reports">Reports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Swiss_National_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Reports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nationalbank had profits of $39.3 billion at the close of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 31 July 2015, the bank reported a first-half loss of £33 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
