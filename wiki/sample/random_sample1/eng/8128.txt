<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railway</a> <b>zig zag</b>, also called a <b>switchback</b>, is a method of climbing steep gradients with minimal need for <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a> and heavy earthworks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For a short distance (corresponding to the middle leg of the letter "Z"), the direction of travel is reversed, before the original direction is resumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A location on railways constructed by using a zig-zag alignment at which trains have to reverse direction in order to continue is a <b>reversing station</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zig_zag_(railway)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zig zags tend to be cheaper to construct because the grades required are discontinuous. Civil engineers can generally find a series of shorter segments going back and forth up the side of a hill more easily and with less grading than they can a continuous grade which has to contend with the larger scale geography of the hills to be surmounted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zig_zag_(railway)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zig zags suffer from a number of limitations:</p>
<ul>
<li>The length of a <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a> is limited to what will fit on the shortest stub track in the zig zag. The <a href="/wiki/Lithgow_Zig_Zag" title="Lithgow Zig Zag">Lithgow Zig Zag</a> stub was extended at great cost in 1908, only to be completely deviated in 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Reversing a locomotive-hauled train without running an engine around to the rear of the train is hazardous. <a href="/wiki/Top_and_tail" title="Top and tail">Top and tail</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Push-pull_train" title="Push-pull train">push pull</a> operation with engines at the rear of the train helps.</li>
<li>The process is slow due to the need to stop the train after each segment and reverse the switch.</li>
<li>It is by nature a single track configuration.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zig_zag_(railway)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If wagons in a freight train are marshalled poorly, with a light vehicle located between heavy ones (particularly with <a href="/wiki/Buffers_and_chain_coupler" title="Buffers and chain coupler">buffer couplings</a>), the move on the middle road of a zig zag can cause <a href="/wiki/Derailment" title="Derailment">derailment</a> of the light wagon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
