<p><i><b>Where the Truth Lies</b></i> is a 2005 British-Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erotic_film" title="Erotic film">erotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller film</a> written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Atom_Egoyan" title="Atom Egoyan">Atom Egoyan</a>. Based on <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Holmes" title="Rupert Holmes">Rupert Holmes</a>' 2003 novel of the same name, the film stars <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Bacon" title="Kevin Bacon">Kevin Bacon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Firth" title="Colin Firth">Colin Firth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alison_Lohman" title="Alison Lohman">Alison Lohman</a>.</p>
<p>The film alternates between 1957, when comedy duo Lanny Morris (Bacon) and Vince Collins (Firth) are at the height of their success, and 1972, when journalist Karen O'Connor (Lohman) is determined to unravel the mystery of a young woman found dead in their hotel suite 15 years before.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1957, immediately after co-hosting a 39-hour-long <a href="/wiki/Poliomyelitis" title="Poliomyelitis">polio</a> telethon in a <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> television studio, entertainers Lanny Morris and Vince Collins fly north to open the new showroom of a <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> hotel run by mobster Sally Sanmarco, who has intimidated them into appearing in order to improve his own image. In their New Jersey hotel suite, shortly after their arrival, the nude body of Miami college student Maureen O'Flaherty is found in a bathtub.</p>
<p>Maureen, an aspiring journalist working for the summer as a server at the comedy team's Miami hotel (which is also owned by Sanmarco), had been researching an article for her school newspaper on the comedy team, and had interviewed them in Miami just before she disappeared. Police investigation in no way connects either Morris or Collins to Maureen's death, which is officially attributed to a drug overdose. Soon after her body is discovered, the two men's comedy partnership is dissolved, despite their enormous success and the closeness of their dependence on one another.</p>
<p>Many unanswered questions remain for the investigators of Maureen's death; the most confusing aspect is how Maureen's body made it from Miami to New Jersey at the same time the comedians were traveling.</p>
<p>Fifteen years later, journalist Karen O'Connor, who as a young polio survivor first met the duo at the same telethon portrayed in the movie's opening sequence, accepts a job to <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriter" title="Ghostwriter">ghostwrite</a> Vince Collins' autobiography—a deal from which Collins will earn $1 million, which he badly needs. Karen makes a promise to Mrs. O'Flaherty that she will find the truth of how her daughter Maureen died. The project is complicated by the fact that she keeps receiving anonymously sent chapters from a book that Lanny Morris himself has written.</p>
<p>Karen, who has idolized the comedians ever since first meeting them, encounters Morris, accompanied by his faithful valet Reuben and manager Irv, by chance in the first-class section of a flight, where she shares a dinner table with them. Wishing to keep her identity secret, during the meal she introduces herself as "Bonnie Trout," the name of the best friend with whom she has traded apartments. Morris and Karen hit it off and have sex in his hotel. He disappears the next morning, apparently without leaving her a note.</p>
<p>Under her own name, Karen begins to work on the Collins autobiography. Complications arise when Collins invites her to an all-day working session at his <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> home and she learns that Morris will be joining them as well. Near panic ensues; she abruptly invents an excuse to leave, but meets Morris in the driveway, and her masquerade is revealed—Morris discovers she has lied about who she is, and Collins discovers that the woman helping him write his memoirs is having or has had an affair with his ex-partner.</p>
<p>Collins agrees to continue with the book, but creates a situation to blackmail Karen into staying away from the story of Maureen O'Flaherty, which is Karen's consuming interest. After plying Karen with wine and drugs, Collins manipulates her into having sex with a young aspiring singer named Alice. He photographs the two women in compromising positions. Karen is told that unless she tells the publisher that there is nothing odd or improper surrounding Maureen's death, he will make the pictures public.</p>
<p>Karen discovers that Maureen had secretly recorded her interactions with Morris and Collins. Gradually, it becomes clear what really happened that night 15 years before: the three had engaged in a <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9nage_%C3%A0_trois" title="Ménage à trois">ménage à trois</a>, fueled by drugs and booze, and at some point Collins tried to have sex with Morris, who resisted violently. Collins retreated to his room, whereupon Maureen tried to blackmail Morris into paying to keep this information a secret. (In 1957, it would have finished Collins professionally if it had come out that he was bisexual.) Morris tried to bribe Maureen to stay quiet but she wanted more money than he was either willing or able to give. Collins passed out in his room, Morris in his, and Maureen fell asleep on the couch. In the morning, she was dead.</p>
<p>Fifteen years later, Karen has begun to uncover the story. She discovers more about Morris' "fix-it man," Reuben. While both Morris and Collins were convinced the other murdered Maureen, they smuggled her body in a crate full of lobsters (a gift from Sanmarco) with Reuben's assistance, shipping it ahead of them to the New Jersey hotel. The tape recorder was on during the entire night, but the tape has been missing all these years.</p>
<p>Reuben confesses to Karen that he murdered Maureen to protect his employers' reputations, and produces the tape to confirm his guilt. He asks Karen if her publishing company will pay him, say, $1 million for the tape. Karen puts two and two together and realizes that Reuben was blackmailing Collins, demanding $1 million to keep quiet his bisexuality, proven on the tape, and perhaps his having murdered Maureen. (Collins was so drunk and drugged during that episode that he plainly does not remember what happened.) Reuben was demanding a million dollars for a murder he himself committed.</p>
<p>In the end, Collins is indeed destroyed, committing suicide. Morris is furious at Karen for all that she has set in motion, and Karen has the answer to her mystery. She goes to Mrs. O'Flaherty, saying she will publish the truth but only after an innocent bystander has died—referring to Maureen's mother herself, who would be crushed to learn of her daughter's behavior that contributed to her own death.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rupert_Holmes" title="Rupert Holmes">Rupert Holmes</a> admittedly patterned Vince and Lanny on his childhood idols, <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Lewis" title="Jerry Lewis">Jerry Lewis</a>, although the plot was pure fiction. Holmes called it a study of "the trust that must exist between any show business team who puts their lives in each other's hands" and "what happens when they no longer trust each other." Shortly after the novel was published, Holmes was asked who he envisioned playing the lead roles in a film adaptation. He suggested <a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Damon" title="Matt Damon">Matt Damon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Hudson" title="Kate Hudson">Kate Hudson</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cruise" title="Tom Cruise">Tom Cruise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Stiller" title="Ben Stiller">Ben Stiller</a> "and any actress in America who's shorter than they are." Tongue-in-cheek, he continued, "Or what about <a href="/wiki/Kukla,_Fran_and_Ollie" title="Kukla, Fran and Ollie">Kukla, Fran and Ollie</a>? This is probably why I'm not a studio head."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scenes in Vince's home were filmed at the <a href="/wiki/Stahl_House" title="Stahl House">Stahl House</a> in Los Angeles, while the <a href="/wiki/Brantford_Airport" title="Brantford Airport">Brantford Airport</a> stood in for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newark_International_Airport" title="Newark International Airport">Newark International Airport</a>. Other exteriors were filmed in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, with interiors shot at <a href="/wiki/Shepperton_Studios" title="Shepperton Studios">Shepperton Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, England.</p>
<p>The film's soundtrack includes "Josephine, Please No Lean on the Bell" performed by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Prima" title="Louis Prima">Louis Prima</a>, "Spinning Wheel" by <a href="/wiki/Blood,_Sweat_%26_Tears" title="Blood, Sweat &amp; Tears">Blood, Sweat &amp; Tears</a>, "Oye Como Va" by <a href="/wiki/Santana_(band)" title="Santana (band)">Santana</a>, and "Maggot Brain" by <a href="/wiki/Funkadelic" title="Funkadelic">Funkadelic</a>. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Rabbit_(song)" title="White Rabbit (song)">White Rabbit</a>", written by <a href="/wiki/Grace_Slick" title="Grace Slick">Grace Slick</a> and originally recorded by <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Airplane" title="Jefferson Airplane">Jefferson Airplane</a>, is featured prominently in one scene, while "You Know, You Know" and "Sanctuary", performed by <a href="/wiki/Mahavishnu_Orchestra" title="Mahavishnu Orchestra">Mahavishnu Orchestra</a>, appear in the film's most erotic sequence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rating">Rating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPAA_film_rating_system#NC-17" title="MPAA film rating system">NC-17</a> rating in the United States due to scenes depicting a <a href="/wiki/Threesome" title="Threesome">threesome</a> and graphic lesbian sex. Egoyan condemned the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">MPAA</a> decision as "a violent act of censorship", while Bacon stated, "I don't get it, when I see films (that) are extremely violent, extremely objectionable sometimes in terms of the roles that women play, slide by with an R, no problem, because the people happen to have more clothes on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Both suggested that homophobia may have played a role in the decision, as the film deals in part with repressed homosexuality. THINKFilm executives opted to release the film unrated in the United States. The rating was later a minor subject of analysis in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/This_Film_Is_Not_Yet_Rated" title="This Film Is Not Yet Rated">This Film Is Not Yet Rated</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Where the Truth Lies</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_premiere" title="Film premiere">premiered</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2005_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2005 Cannes Film Festival">2005 Cannes Film Festival</a> and was shown at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karlovy_Vary_Film_Festival" title="Karlovy Vary Film Festival">Karlovy Vary Film Festival</a>, the <a href="/wiki/2005_Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="2005 Toronto International Film Festival">2005 Toronto International Film Festival</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Woodstock_Film_Festival" title="Woodstock Film Festival">Woodstock Film Festival</a>, and Festival do Rio in Brazil, before going into theatrical release in Canada on 7 October 2005 and the United States the following week.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film grossed $872,142 in North America and $2,605,536 in other markets, for a total worldwide box office of $3,477,678.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-2">[2]</a></sup> The unrated designation hurt the film's financial return, since many theaters would not show it due to its NC-17 rating.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Where the Truth Lies</i> garnered mixed reviews, currently holding a 41% rating on review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>; the consensus states: "The belabored noir plotting feels unbelievable, thus removing any sense of suspense. Also, Lohman is badly miscast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Manohla Dargis of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> observed, "Mr. Egoyan . . . tends to stray from the storytelling straight and narrow, taking a generally <a href="/wiki/Metafiction" title="Metafiction">metafictional</a> approach to narrative. Here, he seems to want to deconstruct celebrity through the familiar mechanics of a murder mystery. Yet because he also doesn't want to be imprisoned by genre, he tries to shake loose its rules, much as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Altman" title="Robert Altman">Robert Altman</a> did in 1973 with his laid-back take on <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler" title="Raymond Chandler">Raymond Chandler</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Goodbye_(film)" title="The Long Goodbye (film)">Long Goodbye</a></i>. It almost works, at least in part . . . In the end, it is Mr. Egoyan's fealty to the novel, its feints and dodges, that proves the film's undoing." She called Kevin Bacon "excellent" but questioned "the calamitous miscasting" of Alison Lohman, "whose ingénue looks and uncontrolled voice are wildly out of sync with the film's other performances and self-consciously lurid atmosphere . . . [S]he has neither the chops nor the core mystery that might have made Mr. Egoyan's pseudo-<a href="/wiki/David_Lynch" title="David Lynch">David Lynch</a> ambitions for his film fly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> called it "<a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a> right down to the plot we can barely track; we're reminded of <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a> asking Raymond Chandler who did it in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Sleep" title="The Big Sleep">The Big Sleep</a></i> and Chandler saying he wasn't sure . . . Atom Egoyan, no stranger to labyrinthine plots, makes this one into a whodunit puzzle crossed with some faraway echoes of <i><a href="/wiki/Sunset_Boulevard_(film)" title="Sunset Boulevard (film)">Sunset Boulevard</a></i> . . . I have seen <i>Where the Truth Lies</i> twice and enjoyed it more when I understood its secrets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> rated the film one star, calling it a "monumental misfire" and adding, "This movie isn't over-the-top - it doesn't know where the top is. Trash addicts will eat up every graphic minute, even if they prefer to wait for the <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ruthe Stein of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> called the film "compulsively watchable even as laughably over-the-top moments start piling up. To be truthful, most of it is high-gloss trash. I'm prepared to recommend <i>Truth</i> despite this - or maybe because of it . . . Bacon has the showier role, and he wrings everything he can out of it. But Firth is equally impressive . . . <i>Truth'</i>s descent into <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">camp</a> happens mostly during the scenes set in the '70s. Lohman is a big part of the problem . . . she's so shrill and annoying as Karen that you end up wishing she were the one floating in that tub."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Todd McCarthy of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> called the film "unconvincing" and "jumbled" and added, "Fractured narrative devices are further encumbered by multiple narration sources, incidental characters who function as mere devices, and uncertain time frames. More bothersome still is the stiff, on-topic nature of most of the film; with Karen in full interrogation mode nearly all the time, scenes and characters are rarely allowed to breathe and develop of their own accord . . . a problem unrelieved by Lohman's performance, which reveals nothing beneath the surface or between the lines. Bacon and Firth both prove more than adept at conveying their characters' seamy sides, which at least lends weight to the distasteful revelations in which the story is rooted, and are reasonably effective overall in cutting the desired profiles of glib entertainers taking full advantage of fame's perks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> rated the film two out of five stars, saying it had "rich potential for suspense, for drama, for comedy, for tragedy, for historical colour, for just about everything. Yet in the most perplexing way, Egoyan's movie doesn't properly deliver on any of these. It is muddled, over-wrought, and somehow too cerebral and fastidious to tell the story straight . . . There are diverting moments but it adds up to nothing in particular. The question is not so much where the truth lies, but why we should care in the first place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philip_French" title="Philip French">Philip French</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> called the film "a rich brew that draws on <i><a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">Citizen Kane</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rashomon_(film)" title="Rashomon (film)">Rashomon</a></i>" and ultimately "holds the attention and makes us want to know the outcome."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">Awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Egoyan was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2005_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2005 Cannes Film Festival">2005 Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-festival-cannes.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-festival-cannes.com-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_Canada" title="Directors Guild of Canada">Directors Guild of Canada</a> honored Phillip Barker for Outstanding Production Design in a Feature Film and nominated Egoyan for Outstanding Direction of a Feature Film, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Shipton" title="Susan Shipton">Susan Shipton</a> for Outstanding Picture Editing of a Feature Film, and the movie itself for Outstanding Feature Film.</p>
<p>Egoyan won the <a href="/wiki/26th_Genie_Awards" title="26th Genie Awards">Genie Award for Best Adapted Screenplay</a>, and the film was nominated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genie_Award_for_Best_Achievement_in_Art_Direction/Production_Design" title="Genie Award for Best Achievement in Art Direction/Production Design">Art Direction/Production Design</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genie_Award_for_Best_Achievement_in_Editing" title="Genie Award for Best Achievement in Editing">Editing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genie_Award_for_Best_Achievement_in_Overall_Sound" title="Genie Award for Best Achievement in Overall Sound">Sound</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genie_Award_for_Best_Achievement_in_Music_-_Original_Score" title="Genie Award for Best Achievement in Music - Original Score">Original Score</a> categories.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Where_the_Truth_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 8 February 2006, <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures" title="Sony Pictures">Sony Pictures</a> released two versions of the film, one rated R and the other unrated, on DVD. Both are in <a href="/wiki/Anamorphic_widescreen" title="Anamorphic widescreen">anamorphic widescreen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Closed_captioning" title="Closed captioning">closed captioned</a>. The unrated version includes an audio track in French. Bonus features on both include <i>The Making of Where The Truth Lies</i> (which has neither commentary nor dialogue) and <a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">deleted scenes</a>.</p>
