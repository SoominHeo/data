<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sweet spot (sports)</h1>

<p>The <b>sweet spot</b> is a place where a combination of factors results in a maximum response for a given amount of effort. In <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)" title="Squash (sport)">squash</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racquet_ball" title="Racquet ball">racquetball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> or <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> a given swing will result in a more powerful hit if the ball strikes the racket, bat or club on the latter's sweet spot.</p>
<p>The sweet spot is the location at which the object being struck, usually a ball, absorbs the maximum amount of the available forward momentum and rebounds away from the racket, bat, club, etc. with a greater velocity than if struck at any other point on the racket, bat or club.</p>
<p>In endurance sports such as <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sweet_spot_training&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sweet spot training (page does not exist)">sweet spot training</a> aims to maximise training benefit (generally for performance at or near FTP, or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Functional_Threshold_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Functional Threshold Power (page does not exist)">Functional Threshold Power</a>) by optimally balancing training effect, physiological strain and maximum duration.</p>
