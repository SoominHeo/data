<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Seijun Suzuki</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Seijun Suzuki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">鈴木 清順</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Suzuki Seijun</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, born <b>Seitaro Suzuki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">鈴木 清太郎</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Suzuki Seitarō</i>)</span> on 24 May 1923, is a Japanese filmmaker, actor, and screenwriter. His films are known for their jarring visual style, irreverent humour, nihilistic cool and entertainment-over-logic sensibility.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He made 40 predominately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-movie" title="B-movie">B-movies</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Nikkatsu" title="Nikkatsu">Nikkatsu</a> Company between 1956 and 1967, working most prolifically in the <a href="/wiki/Yakuza_film" title="Yakuza film">yakuza genre</a>. His increasingly surreal style began to draw the ire of the studio in 1963 and culminated in his ultimate dismissal for what is now regarded as his magnum opus, <i><a href="/wiki/Branded_to_Kill" title="Branded to Kill">Branded to Kill</a></i> (1967), starring notable collaborator <a href="/wiki/Joe_Shishido" title="Joe Shishido">Joe Shishido</a>. Suzuki successfully sued the studio for <a href="/wiki/Wrongful_dismissal" title="Wrongful dismissal">wrongful dismissal</a>, but he was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blacklist" title="Blacklist">blacklisted</a> for 10 years after that. As an independent filmmaker, he won critical acclaim and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Academy_Award" title="Japanese Academy Award">Japanese Academy Award</a> for his Taishō Trilogy, <i><a href="/wiki/Zigeunerweisen_(film)" title="Zigeunerweisen (film)">Zigeunerweisen</a></i> (1980), <i><a href="/wiki/Kagero-za" title="Kagero-za">Kagero-za</a></i> (1981) and <i><a href="/wiki/Yumeji" title="Yumeji">Yumeji</a></i> (1991).</p>
<p>His films remained widely unknown outside Japan until a series of theatrical retrospectives beginning in the mid-1980s, <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">home video</a> releases of key films such as <i>Branded to Kill</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Drifter" title="Tokyo Drifter">Tokyo Drifter</a></i> in the late 1990s and tributes by such acclaimed filmmakers as <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jarmusch" title="Jim Jarmusch">Jim Jarmusch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takeshi_Kitano" title="Takeshi Kitano">Takeshi Kitano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wong_Kar-wai" title="Wong Kar-wai">Wong Kar-wai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a> signaled his international discovery. Suzuki has continued making films, albeit sporadically. In Japan, he is more commonly recognized as an actor for his numerous roles in Japanese films and television.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seijun_Suzuki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suzuki was born during the <a href="/wiki/Taish%C5%8D_period" title="Taishō period">Taishō period</a>, and three months before the <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kantō earthquake">Great Kantō earthquake</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Nihonbashi" title="Nihonbashi">Nihonbashi</a> Ward (now the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%AB%C5%8D,_Tokyo" title="Chūō, Tokyo">Chūō</a> Special Ward) in Tokyo. His younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Suzuki" title="Kenji Suzuki">Kenji Suzuki</a> (now a retired <a href="/wiki/NHK" title="NHK">NHK</a> television announcer), was born six years his junior. His family was in the textile trade. After earning a degree at a Tokyo Trade School in 1941, Suzuki applied to the college of the Ministry of Agriculture, but failed the entrance exam due to poor marks in chemistry and physics. A year later he successfully enrolled in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hirosaki,_Aomori" title="Hirosaki, Aomori">Hirosaki</a> college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1943, he was recruited by the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a> during the national student mobilization to serve in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Sent to East <a href="/wiki/Abiko,_Chiba" title="Abiko, Chiba">Abiko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiba_Prefecture" title="Chiba Prefecture">Chiba</a>, he was assigned the rank of <a href="/wiki/Private_(rank)" title="Private (rank)">Private Second Class</a>. He was shipwrecked twice throughout his military service; first the cargo ship that was to take him to the front was destroyed by an American submarine and he fled to the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. Later, the freighter that took him to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> sank after an attack by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">American air force</a>, and he spent 7 or 8 hours in the ocean before being rescued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biografie_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biografie-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1946, having attained the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Lieutenant" title="Second Lieutenant">Second Lieutenant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Meteorological</a> <a href="/wiki/Corps" title="Corps">Corps</a>, he returned to Hirosaki and completed his studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> About his time in the military Suzuki wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>While I stayed in the army out of fear of being executed as a deserter as soon as I threw down my rifle and ran, it wasn't long before I was promoted to trainee officer with a salary of twelve-and-a-half yen, comparable at the time to that of a departmental manager in business life. I went to the Philippines, where the war took a wrong turn for us. Then I was transferred to Taiwan, where I was stationed at an isolated airport at the foot of a mountain, with twelve others. Our wages were divided into thirteen equal parts; as in a perfect communist system. To avoid the outbreak of a revolt because of sexual deprivation, we didn't just get food, clothing and shelter, but the army staff had also considered it strategically necessary to supply us with three army prostitutes. This isn't a very edifying story, but I can't help it: I spent most of my money on booze and women, and when I arrived at Tanabe harbor the year after liberation, I was completely destitute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-My_Work_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-My_Work-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He has also said that he often found the horrors of war comical,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill-4">[4]</a></sup> such as men being hoisted on board his ship with ropes and being battered black and blue against the hull, or the bugler blasting his trumpet every time a coffin was thrown into the sea. <a href="/wiki/Ian_Buruma" title="Ian Buruma">Ian Buruma</a> writes, "The humour of these situations might escape one who was not there. But Suzuki assures us that it was funny."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eccentric_Imagination_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eccentric_Imagination-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>But war is very funny, you know! When you're in the middle of it, you can't help laughing. Of course it's different when you're facing the enemy. I was thrown into the sea during a bombing raid. As I was drifting, I got the giggles. When we were bombed, there were some people on the deck of the ship. That was a funny sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Tiger_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Tiger-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Next he applied to the prestigious <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>, but again failed the entrance exam. At the invitation of a friend, who had also failed the exam, Suzuki enrolled into the film department of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamakura,_Kanagawa" title="Kamakura, Kanagawa">Kamakura</a> Academy. In October 1948, he passed the <a href="/wiki/Shochiku" title="Shochiku">Shochiku Company</a>'s entrance exam and was hired as an <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">assistant director</a> in the company's Ōfuna Studio. There he worked under directors Minora Shibuya, Yasushi Sasaki, Noboru Nakamura and Hideo Oniwa before joining the regular crew of Tsuruo Iwama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I was a melancholy drunk, and before long I became known as a relatively worthless assistant director. At a large company such as Mitsui or Mitsubishi, these things would have led to my dismissal, especially in the old days, but as the studio as well as the assistant directors themselves were laboring under the strange misconception that they were brilliant artists, almost anything was tolerated, except arson, theft and murder. So I picked flowers for my wife during working hours, and when we were on location I stayed in the bus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-My_Work_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-My_Work-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_and_fall_at_Nikkatsu">Rise and fall at Nikkatsu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seijun_Suzuki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rise and fall at Nikkatsu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1954, the <a href="/wiki/Nikkatsu" title="Nikkatsu">Nikkatsu</a> Company reopened its doors after having ceased all film production at the onset of the war. It lured many assistant directors from the other major film studios with the promise of circumventing the usual long queue for promotion. Among these wayfarers was Suzuki, who took an assistant directing position there at approximately 3 times his previous salary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nikkatsu_bio_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nikkatsu_bio-7">[7]</a></sup> He worked under directors Hidesuke Takizawa, Kiyoshi Saeki, <a href="/wiki/So_Yamamura" title="So Yamamura">So Yamamura</a> and Hiroshi Noguchi. His first screenplay to be filmed was <i>Duel at Sunset</i> (落日の決闘 <i>Rakujitsu no ketto</i>, 1955). It was directed by Hiroshi Noguchi. In 1956, he became a full-fledged director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>His directorial debut, credited to his real name, Seitarō Suzuki, was <i><a href="/wiki/Victory_Is_Mine" title="Victory Is Mine">Victory Is Mine</a></i>, a <i>kayo eiga</i>, or pop song film, part of a subgenre that functioned as a vehicle for hit pop records and singers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_Victory_is_Mine_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG_Victory_is_Mine-8">[8]</a></sup> Impressed by the film's quality Nikkatsu signed him to a longterm contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filmography_With_Commentary_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filmography_With_Commentary-9">[9]</a></sup> Nearly all of the films that he made for Nikkatsu were program pictures, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-movie" title="B-movie">B-movies</a>, production-line genre films made on a tight schedule and shoestring budget that were meant to fill out the second half of a <a href="/wiki/Double_feature" title="Double feature">double feature</a>. B-directors were expected to work fast, taking any and every script that was assigned to them, and they refused scripts only at the risk being dismissed. Suzuki maintained an impressive pace, averaging 3½ films per year, and claims to have turned down only 2 or 3 scripts during his years at the studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kyoka_Factor_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kyoka_Factor-10">[10]</a></sup> He later said of his work schedule (and wrongful dismissal):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Actually making movies was painful work, as I often said to my wife. I had already wanted to quit four or five years before. I told her I hated this foolish, painful process. She told me I shouldn't say such a thing ... that if I talked that way, it would come true. And it eventually did. [This alludes to his unfair dismissal from Nikkatsu in 1968.] For me, it was a relief. I felt this way from the very start.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sato_on_Suzuki_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sato_on_Suzuki-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>His third film and first <a href="/wiki/Yakuza_film" title="Yakuza film">yakuza</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a> movie, <i>Satan's Town</i>, linked him inexorably to the genre. <i><a href="/wiki/Underworld_Beauty" title="Underworld Beauty">Underworld Beauty</a></i> (1958) marked his first <a href="/wiki/CinemaScope" title="CinemaScope">CinemaScope</a> film and was also the first to be credited to his pseudonym Seijun Suzuki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlaw_Masters_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Outlaw_Masters-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having enjoyed moderate success, his work began to draw more attention, especially among student audiences,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kyoka_Factor_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kyoka_Factor-10">[10]</a></sup> with 1963's <i><a href="/wiki/Youth_of_the_Beast" title="Youth of the Beast">Youth of the Beast</a></i> which is considered his "breakthrough" by film scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill-4">[4]</a></sup> Suzuki himself calls it his "first truly original film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlaw_Masters_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Outlaw_Masters-12">[12]</a></sup> His style increasingly shirked genre conventions, favouring visual excess and visceral excitement over a coherent plot and injecting madcap humour into a normally solemn genre, developing into a distinctive "voice". <a href="/wiki/Tony_Rayns" title="Tony Rayns">Tony Rayns</a> explained, "In his own eyes, the visual and structural qualities of his '60s genre films sprang from a mixture of boredom ('All company scripts were so similar; if I found a single line that was original, I could see room to do something with it') and self-preservation ('Since all of us contract directors were working from identical scripts, it was important to find a way of standing out from the crowd')."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kyoka_Factor_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kyoka_Factor-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If you hear the word B-movie, you will probably laugh heartily, but a B-movie director has his own worries. In newspaper ads the main feature usually has the most prominent place, and I'm way down at the bottom. The B-movie director's biggest worry is the question 'What effect will the main feature have that is shown before your film?' Films from Nikkatsu usually have the same plot: the main character falls in love with a woman, he kills the bad guy and gets the woman. This pattern is repeated in every film, so you concentrate on finding out all you can about who the actors are, who the director is, and the approach this director has. This is what the B-movie director does. For instance, the main feature's director has a habit of filming a love scene a certain way; this means that I have to handle it in another way. The director of the main feature has it easy. He doesn't have to find out how I work at all. He can just do whatever he wants. So actually a B-movie director has a harder task than his colleague who does the main feature. Because of this the studio should give me more money than him, actually, but it's just the other way around.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanto_Mushuku_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kanto_Mushuku-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This development was furthered with the assistance of like-minded collaborators. Suzuki considered the production designer to be among the most important:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Bastard_(1963_film)" title="The Bastard (1963 film)">The Bastard</a></i> was the real turning-point in my career, more so than <i>Youth of the Beast</i>, which I made just before. It was my first time with [Takeo Kimura] as designer, and that collaboration was decisive for me. It was with Kimura that I began to work on ways of making the fundamental illusion of cinema more powerful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_on_Suzuki_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_on_Suzuki-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>His fan base grew rapidly, but did not extend to studio president Kyusaku Hori. Beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Tattooed_Life" title="Tattooed Life">Tattooed Life</a></i>, the studio issued Suzuki his first warning for "going too far".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tattooed_Life_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tattooed_Life-15">[15]</a></sup> He responded with <i><a href="/wiki/Carmen_from_Kawachi" title="Carmen from Kawachi">Carmen from Kawachi</a></i> after which he was ordered to "play it straight"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokyo_Drifter_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tokyo_Drifter-16">[16]</a></sup> and had his budget slashed for his next film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outlaw_Masters_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Outlaw_Masters-12">[12]</a></sup> The result was <i><a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Drifter" title="Tokyo Drifter">Tokyo Drifter</a></i>, an "ostensibly routine potboiler" made into a "jaw-dropping, eye-popping fantasia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokyo_Drifter_2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tokyo_Drifter_2-17">[17]</a></sup> Further reduced to filming in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">black-and-white</a> Suzuki made his 40th film in his 12 years with the company, <i><a href="/wiki/Branded_to_Kill" title="Branded to Kill">Branded to Kill</a></i> (1967), considered an <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">masterpiece</a> by critics, for which Hori promptly fired him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suzuki_v._Nikkatsu">Suzuki v. Nikkatsu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seijun_Suzuki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Suzuki v. Nikkatsu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 25 April 1968, Suzuki received a telephone call from a Nikkatsu secretary informing him that he would not be receiving his salary for that month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> Two friends of Suzuki met with Hori the next day and were informed that "Suzuki's films were incomprehensible, that they did not make any money and that Suzuki might as well give up his career as a director as he would not be making films for any other companies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> At that time the student-run <a href="/wiki/Film_society" title="Film society">film society</a> Cine Club, headed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kazuko_Kawakita&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kazuko Kawakita (page does not exist)">Kazuko Kawakita</a>, was sponsoring a major <a href="/wiki/Retrospective" title="Retrospective">retrospective</a> of Suzuki's films; meant to be the first in Japan to honour a Japanese director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Films_of_Suzuki_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Films_of_Suzuki-19">[19]</a></sup> It was scheduled to begin on 10 May, but Hori withdrew all of his films from distribution and refused to release them to the Cine Club. The students were told that "Nikkatsu could not afford to cultivate a reputation for making films understood only by an exclusive audience and that showing incomprehensible and thus bad films would disgrace the company," adding that, "Suzuki's films would not be shown for some time in theaters or by the Cine Club."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suzuki reported the illegal termination of his contract and the removal of his films from distribution to the Japanese Film Directors Association. Association chairman <a href="/wiki/Heinosuke_Gosho" title="Heinosuke Gosho">Heinosuke Gosho</a> met with Hori on 2 May, but was unable to resolve the matter. Gosho then issued a public declaration condemning Nikkatsu for <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breach of contract</a> and violation of Suzuki's right to <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> On the day of the intended retrospective, the Cine Club met to discuss the situation. Two hundred people attended, much exceeding their expectations. A three-hour debate ensued as to whether they should negotiate the release of the films, or confront Nikkatsu directly. The former was agreed upon and it was decided that efforts had to be made to keep the public informed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 June, after repeated attempts to reason with Nikkatsu, Suzuki took the studio to court, suing for breach of contract and <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">personal damages</a> amounting to <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">¥</a>7 380 000. He also demanded that Hori send letters of apology to the three major newspapers on account that Hori's statements gave the impression that all of his films were bad. He then called a <a href="/wiki/News_conference" title="News conference">press conference</a> with representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_Japan" title="Directors Guild of Japan">Directors Guild of Japan</a>, the Actors Guild, the Scriptwriters Guild, ATG and the Cine Club. Among the participates were directors <a href="/wiki/Nagisa_Oshima" title="Nagisa Oshima">Nagisa Oshima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masahiro_Shinoda" title="Masahiro Shinoda">Masahiro Shinoda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kei_Kumai" title="Kei Kumai">Kei Kumai</a>. The only group not represented was the Nikkatsu Directors Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cine Club held a public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demonstration_(people)" title="Demonstration (people)">demonstration</a> on 12 June, which resulted in the formation of a joint committee supporting Suzuki against Nikkatsu. The committee was composed mostly of directors, actors, large <a href="/wiki/Student_society" title="Student society">student film groups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">independent filmmakers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> This also marked the first time the public became involved in a type of dispute normally confined to the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> The Cine Club, and other similar groups, mobilized the public, holding panel discussions and leading mass demonstrations against the studio. The public support, garnered at the height of student movement, was based on a wide appreciation of Suzuki's films and the idea that audiences should be able to see the types of films they wanted to see. This shook the film industry by the fact that the public was making demands rather than passively accepting their product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-8"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the lawsuit, 19 witnesses were heard over a two and a half-year process including directors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reporter" title="Reporter">newspaper reporters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film_criticism" title="Film criticism">film critics</a> and two members of the <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">film-going public</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-9"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> Kohshi Ueno writes of Suzuki's own <a href="/wiki/Testimony" title="Testimony">testimony</a> on the making of <i>Branded to Kill</i>, "A film scheduled for production was suddenly deemed inappropriate and Suzuki was called in at very short notice to fill the gap. The release date had already been set when Suzuki was asked to write the script. He suggested dropping the script when the head of the studio told him he had to read it twice before he understood it, but the company directed him to make the film. According to Suzuki, Nikkatsu was in no position to criticize him for a film that he made to help them out in an emergency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-10"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> Suzuki had never before disclosed this information or discussed any internal company affairs and his testimony exposed the fact that the major studios assigned films to directors at random, improperly publicized them and expected directors to carry any blame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-11"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>It also came to light that, with the industry in decline since the early 1960s, by 1968 Nikkatsu was in the midst of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crisis_(economic)" title="Crisis (economic)">financial crisis</a>. The studio had accumulated a ¥1 845 000 000 debt due to irresponsible management and was to undergo a massive restructuring. Film crew sizes were to be reduced, time cards introduced and advanced approval was required for all overtime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-12"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> Hori, known as a totalitarian figure, unaccustomed to retracting statements or granting requests, had made an example of Suzuki apparently on the basis of his dislike of the film. In a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_New_Year" title="Japanese New Year">New Year's</a> speech to the company he repeatedly emphasized that he wanted to make films that were "easily understandable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-13"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 February 1971 testimony was completed and a verdict expected. However, in March the court advised a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Settlement_(law)" title="Settlement (law)">settlement</a>, explaining appeals were extremely time consuming. Negotiations began on 22 March and concluded on 24 December, three and a half years after the case had begun. Nikkatsu paid Suzuki ¥1 000 000, a fraction of his original claim, and Hori was forced to apologize for comments he made while serving as president. In a separate agreement Nikkatsu donated <i>Fighting Elegy</i> and <i>Branded to Kill</i> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_National_Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Tokyo National Museum of Modern Art">Tokyo National Museum of Modern Art</a>'s Film Centre. At the time of settlement Suzuki expressed fears that if he had continued to fight he might not even be able to get an apology from the failing company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-14"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> During the course of the litigation Nikkatsu was being slowly dismantled. Hori's plans to restructure the company were unsuccessful and Nikkatsu was forced to liquidate studios and headquarter buildings. It released two final films in August 1971 and by November began producing <i><a href="/wiki/Pink_film" title="Pink film">roman poruno</a></i>, softcore romantic pornography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu_18-15"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_Battles_Nikkatsu-18">[18]</a></sup> Despite Suzuki's victory with wide support from the public and film world he was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blacklist" title="Blacklist">blacklisted</a> by all major production companies and unable to make another film for 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_recognition">Late recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seijun_Suzuki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Late recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To sustain himself during the trial and the blacklist years that followed Suzuki published books of essays, and directed several television movies, series and commercials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> The trial and protests had made him into a countercultural icon and his Nikkatsu films became quite popular at <a href="/wiki/Midnight_movie" title="Midnight movie">midnight screenings</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sato_on_Suzuki_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sato_on_Suzuki-11">[11]</a></sup> playing to "packed audiences who wildly applauded."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Films_of_Suzuki_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Films_of_Suzuki-19">[19]</a></sup> He also began acting for other directors in small parts and cameos. His first credited screen role was a special appearance in Kazuki Omori's <i>Don't Wait Until Dark!</i> (1975).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMDb_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMDb-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shochiku, the company that started him as an assistant director, produced his return to film direction in 1977, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Tale_of_Sorrow_and_Sadness" title="A Tale of Sorrow and Sadness">A Tale of Sorrow and Sadness</a></i>, a golf expose cum psychological thriller penned by sports-oriented <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> illustrator <a href="/wiki/Ikki_Kajiwara" title="Ikki Kajiwara">Ikki Kajiwara</a>. Joe Shishido appears in a brief cameo. The film was met poorly critically and popularly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filmography_With_Commentary_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Filmography_With_Commentary-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He collaborated with producer <a href="/wiki/Genjiro_Arato" title="Genjiro Arato">Genjiro Arato</a> in 1980 and made the first part of what would become his Taishō trilogy, <i><a href="/wiki/Zigeunerweisen_(film)" title="Zigeunerweisen (film)">Zigeunerweisen</a></i>, a psychological, period, ghost story, named after a <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">gramophone record</a> of gypsy violin music by <a href="/wiki/Pablo_de_Sarasate" title="Pablo de Sarasate">Pablo de Sarasate</a> featured prominently in the film. When exhibitors declined to show the film, Arato screened it himself in an inflatable mobile dome to great success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zigeunerweisen_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zigeunerweisen-21">[21]</a></sup> It won Honourable Mention at the <a href="/wiki/31st_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="31st Berlin International Film Festival">31st Berlin International Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIFF_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIFF-22">[22]</a></sup> was nominated for 9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Academy_Awards" title="Japanese Academy Awards">Japanese Academy Awards</a> and won four, including best director and best film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAA_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-JAA-23">[23]</a></sup> and was voted the no. 1 Japanese film of the 1980s by Japanese critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zigeunerweisen_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zigeunerweisen-21">[21]</a></sup> He followed the film with <i><a href="/wiki/Kagero-za" title="Kagero-za">Kagero-za</a></i>, made the following year, and completed the trilogy ten years later with <i><a href="/wiki/Yumeji" title="Yumeji">Yumeji</a></i>. Suzuki commented on working outside of the studio system:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Speaking very practically, I don't change as a filmmaker. But the studio system offered a very convenient way of working, and independent filmmaking is different. At Nikkatsu, if I had an idea in the morning, it could be implemented by the afternoon in the studio. It's much more complicated now. I guess I'm still trying to use locations as I once used the studio, but the problem of lighting makes it hard. In the studio, you have lighting gantries to hang lights from. Setting up lights at a location takes so long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suzuki_on_Suzuki_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki_on_Suzuki-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Italy hosted the first partial retrospective of his films outside Japan at the 1984 Pesaro International Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branded_to_Thrill_bio_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Branded_to_Thrill_bio-1">[1]</a></sup> The 1994 touring retrospective <i>Branded to Thrill: The Delirious Cinema of Suzuki Seijun</i> showcased 14 of his films. In 2001 Nikkatsu hosted the <i>Style to Kill</i> retrospective featuring more than 20 of his films. In celebration of 50th anniversary of his directorial debut Nikkatsu again hosted the 2006 <i>Suzuki Seijun 48 Film Challenge</i> showcasing all of his films to date at the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_International_Film_Festival" title="Tokyo International Film Festival">Tokyo International Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>He made a loose sequel to <i>Branded to Kill</i> with <i><a href="/wiki/Pistol_Opera" title="Pistol Opera">Pistol Opera</a></i> (2001). <a href="/wiki/Makiko_Esumi" title="Makiko Esumi">Makiko Esumi</a> replaced Joe Shishido as the number 3 killer. This was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Princess_Raccoon" title="Princess Raccoon">Princess Raccoon</a></i> (2005), starring <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Ziyi" title="Zhang Ziyi">Zhang Ziyi</a>, a musical love story. In a 2006 interview, he said that he has no plans to direct any further films, citing health concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-24">[24]</a></sup> He had been diagnosed with pulmonary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> and is permanently hooked up to a portable respirator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryuganji_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ryuganji-25">[25]</a></sup> However, he attended the 2008 Tokyo Project Gathering, a venue serving <a href="/wiki/Film_finance" title="Film finance">film financing</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_co-production" title="International co-production">international co-productions</a>, and pitched a film titled <i>A Goldfish of the Flame</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmmaking_technique">Filmmaking technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seijun_Suzuki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filmmaking technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a contract B director at Nikkatsu, Suzuki's films were made following a rigid structure. He was assigned a film and script, and could only refuse it at the risk of losing his job. He claims to have turned down only 2 or 3 scripts in his time with Nikkatsu but always modified the scripts both in preproduction and during shooting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kyoka_Factor_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kyoka_Factor-10">[10]</a></sup> Nikkatsu also assigned an actor for the lead, or leads, either a (usually 2nd-tier) star or one being groomed for stardom. The rest of the cast was not assigned but typically drawn from the studio's pool of contract actors. Most studio A films had a set budget of ¥45 million where Suzuki's black-and-white Bs ran 20 million and his colour films were provided an additional 3 million. His films were scheduled 10 days for <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">pre-production</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Location_scouting" title="Location scouting">location scouting</a>, set design and costumes, 25 days for <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">shooting</a> and 3 days for <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">post-production</a>, such as editing and dubbing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokyo_Drifter_DVD_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tokyo_Drifter_DVD-27">[27]</a></sup> Within this framework he had a greater degree of control than the A directors as the cheaper B productions drew a less watchful eye from the head office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanto_Mushuku_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kanto_Mushuku-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
