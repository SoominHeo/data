<p><b>Scotch Tape</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brand_name" title="Brand name">brand name</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Pressure-sensitive_tape" title="Pressure-sensitive tape">pressure-sensitive tapes</a> manufactured by <a href="/wiki/3M" title="3M">3M</a> as part of the company's Scotch brand. Their <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic recording tape</a> products were also sold under the Scotch brand.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotch_Tape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The precursor to the current tapes was developed in the 1930s in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul, Minnesota</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Drew_(inventor)" title="Richard Drew (inventor)">Richard Drew</a> to seal a then-new transparent material known as <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_names">Trade names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotch_Tape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trade names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although it is a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> name, <i>Scotch tape</i> is sometimes used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">generic term</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Scotch brand includes many different constructions (backings, adhesives, etc.) and colors of tape.</p>
<p>The use of the term <i><a href="/wiki/Scotch_(adjective)" title="Scotch (adjective)">Scotch</a></i> in the name was a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> meaning "stingy" in the 1920s and 1930s. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brandname" title="Brandname">brandname</a> Scotch came about around 1925 while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Drew_(inventor)" title="Richard Drew (inventor)">Richard Drew</a> was testing his first masking tape to determine how much adhesive he needed to add. The bodyshop painter became frustrated with the sample masking tape and exclaimed, "Take this tape back to those Scotch bosses of yours and tell them to put more adhesive on it!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotch_bosses_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotch_bosses-3">[3]</a></sup> The name was soon applied to the entire line of 3M tapes.</p>
<p><i>Scotty McTape</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Kilt" title="Kilt">kilt</a>-wearing <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoon</a> boy, was the brand's <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascot</a> for two decades, first appearing in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tailplaid_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tailplaid-4">[4]</a></sup> The familiar <a href="/wiki/Tartan" title="Tartan">tartan</a> design, a take on the well-known <a href="/wiki/Clan_Wallace" title="Clan Wallace">Wallace</a> tartan, was introduced in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tailplaid_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-tailplaid-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Scotch brand and Scotch Tape are registered trademarks of 3M. Besides using <i>Scotch</i> as a prefix in its brand names (<a href="/wiki/Scotchgard" title="Scotchgard">Scotchgard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotchlite" title="Scotchlite">Scotchlite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Brite" title="Scotch-Brite">Scotch-Brite</a>), the company also used the Scotch name for its (mainly professional) audiovisual magnetic tape products, until the early 1990s when the tapes were branded solely with the 3M logo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recording_Tape_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recording_Tape-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1996, 3M exited the magnetic tape business, selling its assets to <a href="/wiki/Quantegy" title="Quantegy">Quantegy</a> (which is a spin-off of <a href="/wiki/Ampex" title="Ampex">Ampex</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="X-rays">X-rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scotch_Tape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: X-rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1953, Soviet scientists showed that <a href="/wiki/Triboluminescence" title="Triboluminescence">triboluminescence</a> caused by peeling a roll of an unidentified Scotch brand tape in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> can produce <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2008, American scientists performed an experiment that showed the rays can be strong enough to leave an X-ray image of a finger on <a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper" title="Photographic paper">photographic paper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
