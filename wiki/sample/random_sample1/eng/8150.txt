<p><b>Script Creation Utility for Maniac Mansion Virtual Machine</b> (<b>ScummVM</b>) is a set of <a href="/wiki/Game_engine_recreation" title="Game engine recreation">game engine recreations</a>. Originally designed to play <a href="/wiki/LucasArts_adventure_games" title="LucasArts adventure games">LucasArts adventure games</a> that use the <a href="/wiki/SCUMM" title="SCUMM">SCUMM</a> system, it also supports a variety of non-SCUMM games by companies like <a href="/wiki/Revolution_Software" title="Revolution Software">Revolution Software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adventure_Soft" title="Adventure Soft">Adventure Soft</a>. It was originally written by <a href="/wiki/Ludvig_Strigeus" title="Ludvig Strigeus">Ludvig Strigeus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Released under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">GNU General Public License</a>, ScummVM is <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a>.</p>
<p>ScummVM is a reimplementation of the part of the <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> used to <a href="/wiki/Interpreter_(computing)" title="Interpreter (computing)">interpret</a> the <a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">scripting languages</a> such games used to describe the game world rather than emulating the hardware the games ran on; as such, ScummVM allows the games it supports to be played on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_platform" title="System platform">platforms</a> other than those for which they were originally released.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ScummVM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>ScummVM</i> is a game emulator, allowing the user to play supported adventure games on their platform of choice. <i>ScummVM</i> provides none of the original assets for the games it supports, and expects the user to properly own the original game's media so as to use the software legally. The official project website offers games that are <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> that work directly with <i>ScummVM</i>. Atop emulating the games, <i>ScummVM</i> enables players to save and load the state of the emulator at any time, enabling a save system atop whatever the emulated game may provide. It has also begun to work at providing alternate controls for newer devices, such as mobile devices with touch screens, which work atop the original games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ports">Ports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ScummVM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">Portability</a> is a design goal of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ports of ScummVM are available for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a> and a variety of <a href="/wiki/Unix-like" title="Unix-like">Unix-like</a> systems including <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> (based on RPM, Debian, or source), members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD" title="BSD">BSD</a> family (<a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a>, <a href="/wiki/NetBSD" title="NetBSD">NetBSD</a>, <a href="/wiki/OpenBSD" title="OpenBSD">OpenBSD</a>, <a href="/wiki/DragonFly_BSD" title="DragonFly BSD">DragonFly BSD</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system)" title="Solaris (operating system)">Solaris</a>. It has also been ported to console systems. Less mainstream personal computer ports include those to <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a>, Atari-Free<a href="/wiki/MiNT" title="MiNT">MiNT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiku_(operating_system)" title="Haiku (operating system)">Haiku</a>-<a href="/wiki/BeOS" title="BeOS">BeOS</a>-<a href="/wiki/Magnussoft_ZETA" title="Magnussoft ZETA">ZETA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/OS/2" title="OS/2">OS/2</a>.</p>
<p>A variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_console" title="Game console">game consoles</a> have official ports. consoles</a> including the <a href="/wiki/GCW_Zero" title="GCW Zero">GCW Zero</a>, <a href="/wiki/GP2X" title="GP2X">GP2X</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">Nintendo DS</a>, <a <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ScummVM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>ScummVM</i> was created in September 2001 by computer science student Ludvig Strigeus. Looking to write his own adventure game, he looked to seeing how the mechanics of an existing game engine, specifically working to create an emulator to play <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monkey_Island_2" title="Monkey Island 2">Monkey Island 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> At about the same time, Vincent Hamm was also looking to develop a SCUMM emulator, and though he had done deeper research into understanding how the SCUMM engine worked, found that Strigeus was much farther along, and the two joined together to craft the emulator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> While Strigeus finished the required emulation for <i>Monkey Island 2</i>, Hamm worked separately to prepare the engine for <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Fate_of_Atlantis" title="Indiana Jones and the Fate of Atlantis">Indiana Jones and the Fate of Atlantis</a></i>, and once completed, the two found some dis-coordination on their efforts but eventually got the emulator working for both games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>News of <i>ScummVM</i> was picked up by the tech news website <i><a href="/wiki/Slashdot" title="Slashdot">Slashdot</a></i> in November 2001, drawing a large interest to the project, and several other developers became part of the project to help support other games. These developers often turned to the creators of the original games to obtain information in informal ways, to help create the emulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> Further developers helped to support games that did not use SCUMM, such as <a href="/wiki/Adventure_Soft" title="Adventure Soft">Adventure Soft</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simon_the_Sorceror" title="Simon the Sorceror">Simon the Sorceror</a></i>; there was some debate about changing the name of the program at this point, but they ultimately kept the <i>ScummVM</i> title, believing that SCUMM was the most well-recognized adventure game engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> Strigeus had built support for iMUSE, the sound software used by many LucasArts games, but feared including it due to potential backlash from LucasArts. Other developers on the project advised him that there should be no legal issues and it was eventually included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> Though Strigeus and Hamm would leave the project in 2002, by then it had a large enough development team to allow it to grow, led by James "Ender" Brown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> Following this shift, the engine's source code was changed from <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> to <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">graphical user interface</a> (GUI) was added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>With increased awareness of the project, LucasArts sent a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cease_%26_desist" title="Cease &amp; desist">cease &amp; desist</a> letter to the project, believing they were using some of LucasArts' proprietary code. Brown worked over the next four years with LucasArts' legal representatives to explain the nature of the emulator and the source of their information to demonstrate that what they had created was legal. Brown considered that LucasArts was trying to be accommodating as <i>ScummVM</i> helped to raise interest in these titles. They ultimately came to a legal agreement to allow <i>ScummVM</i> to continue to be developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The project would also incorporate other parallel efforts to make game emulators for other adventure games. Games from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sierra_Online" title="Sierra Online">Sierra Online</a> were of high demand for the project, requiring them to emulate the <a href="/wiki/Adventure_Game_Interpreter" title="Adventure Game Interpreter">Adventure Game Interpreter</a> (AGI) and the more advanced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sierra%27s_Creative_Interpreter" title="Sierra's Creative Interpreter">Sierra's Creative Interpreter</a> (SCI) engines. AGI support was added in 2006 by incorporating efforts from the Sarien project, but efforts for SCI support were hampered by the parallel project, FreeSCI. Though both <i>ScummVM</i> and FreeSCI aimed to reverse engineer the workings of SCI, FreeSCI had stated they took a more clean-room approach to avoid any legal question about their reverse engineering, and believed the <i>ScummVM</i> project had run afoul of some of Sierra's approaches and thus were hesitant to work together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> However, FreeSCI began to languish in interest compared to <i>ScummVM</i>; after a developer took it upon themselves to make the FreeSCI engine work in <i>ScummVM</i>, the FreeSCI saw more participation in their project, and they agreed to merge their efforts into <i>ScummVM</i>. Initial SCI support was subsequently released in a 2010 version of <i>ScummVM</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>An attempt to bring in <i><a href="/wiki/Another_World_(video_game)" title="Another World (video game)">Another World</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ric_Chahi" title="Éric Chahi">Éric Chahi</a> brought some internal stress within the project in 2004. <i>Another World</i> was not a point-and-click adventure game, and used polygon-based graphics instead of pixel-based ones most adventure games employ, and thus was considered a serious departure from the focus of <i>ScummVM</i>. Though the project was scrapped in a few days after Chahi requested its removal as he was preparing a 15th anniversary remastered for sale, the current leads of the project had to refocus the group and define the ideals that <i>ScummVM</i> should meet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>ScummVM</i> has been a participant in the <a href="/wiki/Google_Summer_of_Code" title="Google Summer of Code">Google Summer of Code</a> every year since 2007 except for 2015. A sister project, <i><a href="/wiki/ResidualVM" title="ResidualVM">ResidualVM</a></i>, was started to implement engines for three-dimensional adventure games, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Grim_Fandango" title="Grim Fandango">Grim Fandango</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Myst_III:_Exile" title="Myst III: Exile">Myst III: Exile</a></i>, named as such as these games reflect the residual of those not already covered by <i>ScummVM</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developer_support">Developer support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ScummVM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Developer support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With increased attention, <i>ScummVM</i> has entered into favorable agreements with adventure game developers to help bring their titles into the engine, or in some cases, being given source code and other assets to work from. Notably, <a href="/wiki/Revolution_Software" title="Revolution Software">Revolution Software</a> helped the developers with source code and technical advice for their games, and once <i>ScummVM</i> supported their engine, they released <i><a href="/wiki/Lure_of_the_Temptress" title="Lure of the Temptress">Lure of the Temptress</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Beneath_a_Steel_Sky" title="Beneath a Steel Sky">Beneath a Steel Sky</a></i> as <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> and provided assets from its first two <i><a href="/wiki/Broken_Sword" title="Broken Sword">Broken Sword</a></i> games in an open media format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> The renewed interest in these games from younger players enabled Revolution to work on two more <i>Broken Sword</i> games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup> Other developers that have worked closely with <i>ScummVM</i> include:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mistic.27s_GPL_violations">Mistic's GPL violations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ScummVM&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mistic's GPL violations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>ScummVM</i> is distributed as <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a> under the <i><a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">GNU General Public License</a></i>, enabling anyone to use the emulator as an engine for a game. For example, Revolution Software repackageed their <i>Broken Sword</i> games for a DVD release, using the <i>ScummVM</i> engine to support modern computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2008, the ScummVM teams learned that three games for the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>, console, <i><a href="/wiki/Freddi_Fish_and_the_Case_of_the_Missing_Kelp_Seeds" title="Freddi Fish and the Case of the Missing Kelp Seeds">Freddi Fish and the Case of the Missing Kelp Seeds</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pajama_Sam#Pajama_Sam:_No_Need_To_Hide_When_It.27s_Dark_Outside" title="Pajama Sam">Pajama Sam: No Need to Hide When It's Dark Outside</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_Fox_in_%22Dry_Cereal%22" title='Spy Fox in "Dry Cereal"'>Spy Fox: Dry Cereal</a></i> had used <i>ScummVM</i> without proper attribution. The games were published on request of <a href="/wiki/Atari" title="Atari">Atari</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majesco_Entertainment" title="Majesco Entertainment">Majesco Entertainment</a>, who turned to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mistic_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mistic Software (page does not exist)">Mistic Software</a> to port the games. Mistic had used <i>ScummVM</i> for these, but failed to credit the developers. While the <i>ScummVM</i> team contacted <a href="/wiki/Gpl-violations.org" title="Gpl-violations.org">gpl-violations.org</a> for legal advice, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> began to investigate the claims as their license agreements prevent the use of open-source software on the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>, which led Nintendo to question if the reverse engineering used by <i>ScummVM</i> was legal and threatened legal action. A settlement was made in 2009, in which <i>ScummVM</i> would drop the investigation of the GPL violation, while Mistic was required to sell or destroy all GPL-violating copies of the games, make a donation to the <a href="/wiki/Free_Software_Foundation" title="Free Software Foundation">Free Software Foundation</a>, and pay the legal fees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech_scummvm_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_scummvm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
