<p><b>Skellig Michael</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Sceilig Mhichíl</i></span>), also called <b>Great Skellig</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Sceilig Mhór</i></span>), is the larger of the two <a href="/wiki/Skellig_Islands" title="Skellig Islands">Skellig Islands</a>, 11.6 kilometres (7.2 mi) west of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iveragh_peninsula" title="Iveragh peninsula">Iveragh peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>, Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">Christian monastery</a> was founded on the island at some point between the 6th and 8th century and remained continuously occupied until it was abandoned in the late 12th century. The remains of the monastery, and most of the island, became a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skellig_Michael&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-monastic">Pre-monastic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skellig_Michael&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-monastic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Skellig Michael was uninhabited before its monastery was founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup> Folklore holds that Ir, son of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADl_Esp%C3%A1ine" title="Míl Espáine">Míl Espáine</a>, was buried on the island, and a text from the 8th or 9th century states that Duagh, King of <a href="/wiki/Iarmuman" title="Iarmuman">West Munster</a>, fled to "Scellecc" after a feud with the Kings of Cashel, although it is not known whether these events actually took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monastic">Monastic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skellig_Michael&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Monastic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The monastery's exact date of foundation is not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup> The first definite reference to monastic activity on the island is a record of the death of "Suibhini of Skelig" dating from the 8th century; however, <a href="/wiki/Finnian_of_Clonard" title="Finnian of Clonard">Saint Fionán</a> is claimed to have founded the monastery in the 6th century.</p>
<p>The monastic site on the island is on a terraced shelf 600 feet (180 metres) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" title="Above sea level">above sea level</a>, and developed between the sixth and eighth centuries. It contains six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beehive_cell" title="Beehive cell">beehive cells</a>, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oratories" title="Oratories">oratories</a> as well as a number of stone crosses and slabs. It also contains a later medieval church. The cells and oratories are all of dry-built <a href="/wiki/Corbel_arch" title="Corbel arch">corbel</a> construction. A carefully designed system for collecting and purifying water in cisterns was developed. It has been estimated that no more than twelve monks and an abbot lived here at any one time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerry_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerry-4">[4]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">hermitage</a> is on the south peak.</p>
<p>The diet of monks living on the North Atlantic islands was somewhat different from that of those who lived on the mainland. With less arable land available to grow grain, vegetable gardens became an important part of monastic life. Of necessity, fish and the meat and eggs of birds nesting on the islands became staples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "<a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Inisfallen" title="Annals of Inisfallen">Annals of Inisfallen</a>" record a Viking attack in 823. The site had been dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Michael_(archangel)" title="Michael (archangel)">Saint Michael</a> by at least 1044 (when the death of "Aedh of Scelic-Mhichí" is recorded).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Excavation_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Excavation-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup> However, this dedication may have occurred as early as 950, around which time a new church was added to the monastery (typically done to celebrate a <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecration</a>) which was called Saint Michael's Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The monastery remained continuously occupied until the 12th or 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup> During this time, the climate around Skellig Michael became colder and more prone to storms, and this, along with changes to the structure of the Irish Church, prompted the community to abandon the island and move to the abbey in <a href="/wiki/Ballinskelligs" title="Ballinskelligs">Ballinskelligs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-monastic">Post-monastic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skellig_Michael&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-monastic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Skellig Michael remained in the possession of the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Canons_Regular" title="Canons Regular">Canons Regular</a> until the dissolution of the Ballinskelligs abbey during the Protestant Reformation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> in 1578.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evaluation_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evaluation-6">[6]</a></sup> Ownership was then passed to the <a href="/wiki/Butler_dynasty" title="Butler dynasty">Butler family</a> with whom it stayed until the early 1820s, when the Corporation for Preserving and Improving the Port of Dublin (the predecessor to the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Irish_Lights" title="Commissioners of Irish Lights">Commissioners of Irish Lights</a>) purchased the island from John Butler of Waterville in a <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_purchase_order" title="Compulsory purchase order">compulsory purchase order</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evaluation_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Evaluation-6">[6]</a></sup> The Corporation constructed two lighthouses on the Atlantic side of the island, and associated living quarters, all of which was completed by 1826.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Excavation_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Excavation-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Public_Works" title="Office of Public Works">Office of Public Works</a> took the remains of the monastery into guardianship in 1880, and repaired certain collapsed structures, before buying the island (with the exception of the lighthouses and associated structures) from the Commissioners of Irish Lights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Excavation_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Excavation-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Skellig Michael was made a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 1996, at the 20th Session of the <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Committee" title="World Heritage Committee">World Heritage Committee</a> in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Mérida, Yucatán">Mérida</a>, Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decision_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Decision-7">[7]</a></sup> After being nominated for inclusion on 28 October 1995, an evaluation of the site by the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_on_Monuments_and_Sites" title="International Council on Monuments and Sites">International Council on Monuments and Sites</a> (an advisory body of the World Heritage Committee), recommended that the island be inscribed on the basis of criteria (iii) and (iv) of the <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site#Selection_criteria" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage List's selection criteria</a>, which relate the cultural significance of a site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evaluation_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Evaluation-6">[6]</a></sup> The Committee approved this recommendation, describing Skellig Michael as of "exceptional universal value", and a "unique example of an early religious settlement", while also noting the site's preservation as a result of its "remarkable environment", and its ability to illustrate "as no other site can, the extremes of a <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">Christian monasticism</a> characterizing much of North Africa, the Near East and Europe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decision_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Decision-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_and_activities">Access and activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skellig_Michael&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Access and activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each year at least four boat licences are granted to tour operators<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> who run trips to Skellig Michael during the summer season (May to October, inclusive), weather permitting. For safety reasons, because the steps up to the monastery are rocky, steep, and old, climbs are not permitted during very wet or windy weather. There are <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">dive sites</a> immediately around the rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skellig_Michael&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The acclaimed 1969 <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> documentary <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civilisation:_A_Personal_View_by_Kenneth_Clark" title="Civilisation: A Personal View by Kenneth Clark">Civilisation: A Personal View by Kenneth Clark</a></i> featured a short segment on the use of Skellig Michael in the first episode.</li>
<li>Skellig Michael is featured in the second episode of the BBC documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_Christianity_(TV_series)" title="A History of Christianity (TV series)">A History of Christianity</a></i>.</li>
<li>The island served as a location for the final scene in <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Glass_(film)" title="Heart of Glass (film)">Heart of Glass</a></i> by Werner Herzog.</li>
<li>The island served as a filming location for the final scene of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Force_Awakens" title="Star Wars: The Force Awakens">Star Wars: The Force Awakens</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was also used for the next film in the series, <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Last_Jedi" title="Star Wars: The Last Jedi">Star Wars: The Last Jedi</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This caused concerns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> because of the significance of the site as a special protection area<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> for its colonies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puffins" title="Puffins">puffins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manx_shearwaters" title="Manx shearwaters">Manx shearwaters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_petrels" title="Storm petrels">storm petrels</a>.</li>
<li>Skellig Michael is a major influence on the fictional Skellige in the award-winning video game series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Witcher_3:_Wild_Hunt" title="The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt">The Witcher</a></i>, which features similar landmarks and islands, and the inhabitants of which share a culture and accent with that of the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
