<p><b>Škoda Auto</b> (<small>Czech pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Czech" title="Help:IPA for Czech">[ˈʃkoda]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Cs-Skoda.ogg" title="File:Cs-Skoda.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/Cs-Skoda.ogg" title="Cs-Skoda.ogg">listen</a>)</small>), more commonly known as <b>Škoda</b>, is a Czech <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> manufacturer founded in 1895 as <a href="/wiki/Laurin_%26_Klement" title="Laurin &amp; Klement">Laurin &amp; Klement</a>. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav" title="Mladá Boleslav">Mladá Boleslav</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>.</p>
<p>In 1925 Laurin &amp; Klement was acquired by <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which itself became state owned during the communist regime. After 1991 it was gradually privatized and in 2000 Škoda became a wholly owned subsidiary of the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, the company was meant to serve the role of the VW Group’s entry brand. Over time, however, the Škoda brand has shifted progressively more upmarket, with most models overlapping with their Volkswagen counterparts on price and features, while eclipsing them on space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its total global sales reached 1.13 million cars in 2016 and had risen annually by 6.8 percent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.C5.A0KODA_car_sales_2016_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-.C5.A0KODA_car_sales_2016-1">[1]</a></sup> profit had risen by 6,5% in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.C5.A0KODA_car_sales_2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-.C5.A0KODA_car_sales_2015-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2015, a corporate strategy was launched to produce an <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">all-electric</a> car by 2020 or 2021 with a range of over 500 kilometres (310 mi), 15-minute charging time, and a cost below comparative combustion-engine vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Autocar_March_16.2C_2016_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Autocar_March_16.2C_2016-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a> were established as an arms manufacturing plant in 1859. Škoda Auto (and its predecessors) is one of the five oldest companies producing cars and has an unbroken history alongside <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Motoren-Gesellschaft" title="Daimler-Motoren-Gesellschaft">Daimler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tatra_(company)" title="Tatra (company)">Tatra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laurin_.26_Klement">Laurin &amp; Klement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Laurin &amp; Klement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laurin_%26_Klement" title="Laurin &amp; Klement">Laurin &amp; Klement</a></div>

<p>The origins of what became Škoda Auto go back to the early 1890s when, like many long-established <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">car manufacturers</a>, a company started manufacturing bicycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Škoda (then Laurin &amp; Klement) factories were founded in 1896 as a <a href="/wiki/Velocipede" title="Velocipede">velocipede</a> manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nkp1905_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nkp1905-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1894, 26-year-old <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Klement" title="Václav Klement">Václav Klement</a>, who was a bookseller in <a href="/wiki/Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav" title="Mladá Boleslav">Mladá Boleslav</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a> (today's Czech Republic, then part of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>), was unable to obtain spare parts to repair his German bicycle. Klement returned his bicycle to the manufacturers, Seidel and Naumann, with a letter, in Czech, asking them to carry out repairs, only to receive a reply, in German, stating: "If you would like an answer to your inquiry, you should try writing in a language we can understand". Not satisfied with the reply and realising the business potential, Klement, despite having no technical experience, decided to start a bicycle repair shop, which he and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Laurin" title="Václav Laurin">Václav Laurin</a> opened in 1896 in <a href="/wiki/Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav" title="Mladá Boleslav">Mladá Boleslav</a>. Before going into partnership with Klement, Laurin was an established bicycle manufacturer in the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Turnov" title="Turnov">Turnov</a>.</p>
<p>In 1898, after moving to their newly built factory, the pair bought a <a href="/wiki/Werner_Motors" title="Werner Motors">Werner "Motocyclette"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[nb 1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Laurin_%26_Klement" title="Laurin &amp; Klement">Laurin &amp; Klement</a>'s first motorcyclette, powered by an engine mounted on the handlebars driving the front wheels, proved dangerous and unreliable—an early accident on it cost Laurin a front tooth. To design a safer machine with its structure around the engine, the pair wrote to German ignition specialist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bosch" title="Robert Bosch">Robert Bosch</a> for advice on a different electromagnetic system.</p>
<p>Their new <i>Slavia</i> motorcycle made its debut in 1899 and the company became the first motorcycle factory in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nkp1905_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nkp1905-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1900, with a company workforce of 32, Slavia exports began and 150 machines were shipped to London for the Hewtson firm. Shortly afterwards, the press credited them as makers of the first motorcycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1905 the firm was manufacturing automobiles, making it the second-oldest car manufacturer in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_lands" title="Czech lands">Czech lands</a> after <a href="/wiki/Tatra_(company)" title="Tatra (company)">Tatra</a>. The company, with an area of 7800 <a href="/wiki/Square_metre" title="Square metre">m<sup>2</sup></a>, had a workforce of 320 and used 170 special machine-tools, power-driven by 100 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a> of steam power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nkp1905_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-nkp1905-11">[11]</a></sup> The first model, Voiturette A, was a success and the company was established both within Austria-Hungary and internationally.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".C5.A0koda">Škoda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Škoda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="624" data-file-width="936" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg/220px-Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg/330px-Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg/440px-Old_%C5%A0koda_Prague2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>After World War I the Laurin &amp; Klement company began producing <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">trucks</a>, but in 1924, after running into problems and being affected by a fire on their premises, the company sought a new partner.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, "<i>Akciová společnost, dříve Škodovy závody</i>" (Limited Company, formerly the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a>), an arms manufacturer and multi-sector concern which had become one of the largest industrial enterprises in Europe and the largest in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, sought to enlarge its non-arms manufacturing base and acquired Laurin &amp; Klement in 1925. It also started manufacturing cars in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Hispano-Suiza" title="Hispano-Suiza">Hispano-Suiza</a>. Most of the later production took place under Škoda's name.</p>

<p>An assembly line was used for production from 1930 onwards. In the same year a formal spin-off of the car manufacture into a new company, <i>Akciová společnost pro automobilový průmysl</i> or abbreviated <i>ASAP</i>, took place. ASAP remained a wholly owned subsidiary of the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a> and continued to sell cars under the Škoda marque. Apart from the factory in Mladá Boleslav it included also the firm's representation, sales offices and services, as well as a central workshop in Prague. At the time, the car factory in Mladá Boleslav covered an area of 215,000 m2 and employed 3,750 blue-collar and 500 white-collar workers.</p>
<p>After a decline caused by the economic depression, Škoda introduced a new line of cars in the 1930s which significantly differed from its previous products. A new design of <a href="/wiki/Chassis" title="Chassis">chassis</a> with <a href="/wiki/Backbone_chassis" title="Backbone chassis">backbone tube</a> and all-around independent suspension was developed under the leadership of chief engineer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vladim%C3%ADr_Matou%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vladimír Matouš (page does not exist)">Vladimír Matouš</a> and modelled on the one first introduced by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Ledwinka" title="Hans Ledwinka">Hans Ledwinka</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tatra_(company)" title="Tatra (company)">Tatra</a>. First used on model Škoda 420 Standard in 1933, it aimed at solving insufficient torsional stiffness of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_(vehicle)#Ladder_frame" title="Frame (vehicle)">ladder frame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kralik_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-kralik-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new design of chassis became the basis for models <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Popular" title="Škoda Popular">Popular</a> (845-1,089 cc), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Rapid_(1935-1947)" title="Škoda Rapid (1935-1947)">Rapid</a> (1165–1766 cc), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Favorit_(1936-1941)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Škoda Favorit (1936-1941) (page does not exist)">Favorit</a> (1802–2091 cc) and the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Superb_(1934%E2%80%931949)" title="Škoda Superb (1934–1949)">Superb</a> (2.5–4 l).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kralik_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-kralik-15">[14]</a></sup> While in 1933 Škoda had a 14% share of the Czechoslovak car market and occupied third place behind <a href="/wiki/Praga_(company)" title="Praga (company)">Praga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tatra_(company)" title="Tatra (company)">Tatra</a>, the new line made it a market leader by 1936, with a 39% share in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kralik_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-kralik-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Occupation of Czechoslovakia">occupation of Czechoslovakia</a> in World War II the Škoda Works were turned into part of the <i><a href="/wiki/Reichswerke_Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Reichswerke Hermann Göring">Reichswerke Hermann Göring</a></i> serving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_German" title="Nazi German">German</a> war effort by producing components for military terrain vehicles, military planes, other weapon components and cartridge cases. Vehicle output decreased from 7,052 in 1939 to 683 in 1944, of which only 35 were passenger cars. A total of 316 trucks were produced between January and May 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The UK and US air forces bombed the Škoda works repeatedly between 1940 and 1945. The final massive air raid took place on 25 April 1945 and resulted in almost the complete destruction of the Škoda armament works and approximately 1,000 dead and injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_World_War_II">Post World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>When, by July 1945, the <a href="/wiki/Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav" title="Mladá Boleslav">Mladá Boleslav</a> factory had been reconstructed, production of Škoda's first post-World War II car, the 1101 series began. It was essentially an updated version of the pre-World War II Škoda Popular. In the autumn of 1948, Škoda (along with all other large manufacturers) became part of the communist <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">planned economy</a>, which meant it was separated from the parent company, <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a>. In spite of unfavourable political conditions and losing contact with technical development in non-communist countries, Škoda retained a good reputation until the 1960s, producing models such as the Škoda 440 Spartak, 445 Octavia, Felicia and Škoda 1000 MB.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg/220px-13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg/330px-13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg/440px-13-04-05-Skoda_Museum_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav_by_RalfR-122.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In late 1959, the Škoda Felicia, a compact four-cylinder convertible coupe, was imported into the United States for model year 1960. Its retail price was around US$2,700, for which one could purchase a nicely-equipped V8 domestic car that was larger, more comfortable, and had more luxury and convenience features (gasoline retailed for less than 30 cents per gallon, so fuel economy was not of primary importance in America at that time). Those Felicias that made it to American ownership soon experienced a number of reliability problems, further damaging the car's reputation. The Felicia was therefore a poor seller in the States and leftover cars ended up being hied off at a fraction of the original retail list. Since that time, Škoda automobiles have not been imported into the U.S. for retail sale.</p>

<p>In the late 1980s, Škoda (then named <i>Automobilové závody, národní podnik</i> or abbreviated <i>AZNP</i>) was still manufacturing cars that conceptually dated back to the 1960s. <a href="/wiki/Rear-engine_design" title="Rear-engine design">Rear-engined</a> models such as the Škoda 105/120, Estelle and Rapid sold steadily and performed well against more modern makes in races such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAC_Rally" title="RAC Rally">RAC Rally</a> in the 1970s and 1980s. They won their class in the RAC rally for 17 years running. They were powered by a 130 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower#Brake_horsepower" title="Horsepower">brake horsepower</a> (97 kW), 1,289 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_centimetre" title="Cubic centimetre">cubic centimetres</a> (78.7 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_inch" title="Cubic inch">cu in</a>) engine. In spite of its dated image and becoming the subject of negative jokes, Škodas remained a common sight on the roads of UK and Western Europe throughout the 1970s and 1980s.</p>
<p>Sport versions of the Estelle and earlier models were produced, using the name "Rapid". Soft-top versions were also available. The Rapid was once described as the "poor man's Porsche",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> and had significant sales success in the UK during the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>"Of course, that the Škoda became such a figure of fun was in part due to its ubiquity on Britain's roads. The company must have been doing something right."</i> (from a BBC report on Škoda sales in 1980s)</dd>
</dl>
<p>In 1987 the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Favorit" title="Škoda Favorit">Favorit</a> was introduced, and was one of a triumvirate of compact Western-influenced front-wheel drive hatchbacks from the three main Eastern Bloc manufacturers around that time, the others being <a href="/wiki/AvtoVAZ" title="AvtoVAZ">VAZ</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lada_Samara" title="Lada Samara">Lada Samara</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zastava_Automobiles" title="Zastava Automobiles">Zastava</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugo_Sana" title="Yugo Sana">Yugo Sana</a>. The Favorit's appearance was the work of the Italian design company <a href="/wiki/Gruppo_Bertone" title="Gruppo Bertone">Bertone</a>. With some motor technology licensed from western Europe, but still using the Škoda-designed 1289 cc engine, Škoda engineers designed a car comparable to western production. The technological gap was still there, but began closing rapidly. The Favorit was very popular in Czechoslovakia and other <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a> countries. It also sold well in Western Europe, especially in the UK and Denmark due to its low price and was regarded as solid and reliable. However, it was perceived as having poor value compared with contemporary Western European designs. The Favorit's trim levels were improved and it continued to be sold until the introduction of the Felicia in 1994.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volkswagen_Group_subsidiary">Volkswagen Group subsidiary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Volkswagen Group subsidiary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SkodaSuperbII.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2480" data-file-width="3508" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/SkodaSuperbII.jpg/220px-SkodaSuperbII.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/SkodaSuperbII.jpg/330px-SkodaSuperbII.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/SkodaSuperbII.jpg/440px-SkodaSuperbII.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SkodaSuperbII.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Škoda Auto is one of the largest car manufacturers in Central Europe. In 2014, 1.037.200 cars were sold worldwide, a record for the company</div>
</div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_communism" title="Fall of communism">fall of communism</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a> brought great changes to Czechoslovakia and most industries were subject to <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a>. In the case of Škoda Automobile, the state authorities brought in a strong foreign partner. Volkswagen was chosen by the Czech government on 9 December 1990, and, as a result, on 28 March 1991 a joint-venture partnership agreement with Volkswagen took place, marked by the transfer of a 30% share to the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a> on 16 April 1991. By this stage, Škoda was still making its outdated range of rear engine saloons, although it had started production of the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Favorit" title="Škoda Favorit">Favorit</a> front-wheel drive hatchback in 1987 as an eventual replacement.</p>
<p>In the following years, Škoda became the fourth brand of the German group, as the Volkswagen Group raised its equity share first on 19 December 1994, to 60.3%, followed on 11 December 1995, to 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the competition for Škoda, Volkswagen was pitted against French car-maker <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a>, which lost out because its strategic plan did not include producing high-value models in the Czech factories; Renault proposed to manufacture the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Twingo" title="Renault Twingo">Renault Twingo</a> <a href="/wiki/City_car" title="City car">city car</a> in the Škoda factories.</p>
<p>At the time the decision was made, privatization to a major German company was somewhat controversial, since there was still anti-German sentiment lingering in the Czech Republic from WW2 and its aftermath. However, it could be argued that the subsequent fortunes of other Eastern-Bloc automobile manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Lada" title="Lada">Lada</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AutoVAZ" title="AutoVAZ">AutoVAZ</a>, and of <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a> itself – once <i>Škoda Auto's</i> parent company – suggested that Volkswagen's involvement was not necessarily a result of poor judgement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Backed by Volkswagen Group expertise and investments, the design — both style and engineering — has improved greatly. The 1994 model <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Felicia" title="Škoda Felicia">Felicia</a> was effectively a reskin of the Favorit, but quality and equipment improvements helped, and in the Czech Republic the car was perceived as good value for money and became popular. Sales improved across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, where the Felicia was one of the best-ranking cars in customer satisfaction surveys.</p>
<p>Volkswagen AG chairman <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Pi%C3%ABch" title="Ferdinand Piëch">Ferdinand Piëch</a> personally chose <a href="/wiki/Dirk_van_Braeckel" title="Dirk van Braeckel">Dirk van Braeckel</a> as head of design, and the subsequent <i>Octavia</i> and <i>Fabia</i> models made their way to the demanding <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> markets. They are built on common <a href="/wiki/List_of_Volkswagen_Group_platforms" title="List of Volkswagen Group platforms">Volkswagen Group floorpans</a>. The Fabia, launched at the end of 1999, formed the basis for later versions of the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Polo" title="Volkswagen Polo">Volkswagen Polo</a> and <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Ibiza" title="SEAT Ibiza">SEAT Ibiza</a>, while the Octavia, launched in 1996, has shared its floorpan with a host of cars, the most popular of which is the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf" title="Volkswagen Golf">Volkswagen Golf</a>.</p>

<p>The perception of Škoda in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> has completely changed since the takeover by VW,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> in stark comparison with the reputation of the cars throughout the 1980s—often described as "the laughing stock" of the automotive world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> As technical development progressed and attractive new models were marketed, Škoda's image was initially slow to improve. In the UK, a major turnabout was achieved with the ironic <i>"It is a Škoda, honest"</i> campaign, which began in 2000 when the Fabia launched. In a 2003 advertisement on British television, a new employee on the production line is fitting Škoda badges on the car bonnets. When some attractive looking cars come along he stands back, not fitting the badge, since they look so good they <i>cannot be Škodas</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> This market campaign worked by confronting Škoda's image problem head-on — a tactic which marketing professionals regarded as high risk. By 2005 Škoda was selling over 30,000 cars a year in the UK, a market share of over 1%. For the first time in its UK history, a waiting list developed for deliveries from Škoda. UK owners have consistently ranked the brand at or near the top of customer satisfaction surveys since the late 1990s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_strategy">Growth strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Growth strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2983" data-file-width="8107" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg/400px-%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg/600px-%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg/800px-%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%C5%A0koda_Auto_Mlad%C3%A1_Boleslav.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Škoda Auto plant in Mladá Boleslav</div>

<p>2010 was a year of important changes for Škoda Auto, in terms of both products and management. On 1 September 2010, Prof. Dr. h.c. Winfried Vahland assumed responsibility for the management of the company, becoming the CEO of Škoda Auto. Under Vahland's leadership, Škoda set forth plans to double the company's annual sales<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> to at least 1.5 million by 2018 (later known as the 'Growth Strategy', Czech: 'Růstová strategie').</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2010_Paris_Motor_Show" title="2010 Paris Motor Show">2010 Paris Motor Show</a> in September 2010, the company unveiled the Octavia Green E Line. This e-car concept was the forerunner to the e-car test fleet that Škoda released in 2012. The final 1st-generation <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">Octavia</a> (Tour) was produced at the Mladá Boleslav plant in November 2010. The worldwide production of this model exceeded 1.4 million units since its release in 1996. In 2010 for the first time in history, China overtook German sales to become Škoda's largest individual market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Škoda Auto celebrated its 20-year partnership with the Volkswagen Group. More than 75,000 visitors attended an open-house event held in Mladá Boleslav in the April. Earlier that year, the company provided details on its 2018 Growth Strategy: for at least one new or completely revised model to be released every six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> With this in mind, the company redesigned its logo and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_identity" title="Corporate identity">CI</a>, which was presented at the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Motor_Show" title="Geneva Motor Show">Geneva Motor Show</a>. Škoda's main attraction at the event was the Vision<i>D</i> design concept; a forerunner to the future 3rd generation <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">Octavia</a>. Škoda presented the Mission<i>L</i> design study at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Motor_Show" title="Frankfurt Motor Show">IAA in Frankfurt am Main</a> in September, which was to become the basis of the company's forthcoming compact model the European <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Rapid_(2012)" title="Škoda Rapid (2012)">Rapid</a>.</p>
<p>In the same year, the company started production of the new <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Rapid_(2011)" title="Škoda Rapid (2011)">Rapid</a> model in Pune, India (October 2011), and launched the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Up" title="Volkswagen Up">Citigo</a> at Volkswagen's Bratislava plant (November 2011).</p>
<p>In 2012 Škoda introduced two new mass production models. The <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Rapid_(2012)" title="Škoda Rapid (2012)">European version of the Rapid</a> premiered at the <a href="/wiki/2012_Paris_Motor_Show" title="2012 Paris Motor Show">Paris Motor Show</a>. This car was a successor to the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">1st-generation Octavia</a> in terms of its price bracket. The second model was the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">3rd-generation Octavia</a>, which premiered In December 2012. In the same month, local production of the Yeti was launched at the Nizhny Novgorod GAZ factory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexander_Rogan_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander_Rogan-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 Škoda introduced an emission-free fleet of Octavia Green E Line e-cars on Czech roads to be used by external partners. Since internal tests on the fleet in late 2011, the e-fleet had driven more than 250,000 km. During the same year, Škoda celebrated several milestones, including fourteen million Škoda cars being produced since 1905 (January),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> three million Fabias (May),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> 500,000 Superbs at the Kvasiny plant (June )<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> and 5 years of Škoda operations in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexander_Rogan_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander_Rogan-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Massive rejuvenation of the model range was a major tune for 2013 at Škoda: The Czech car maker launched the third-generation Octavia Combi and Octavia RS (both liftback and estate) as well as facelifted Superb and Superb Combi. They were accompanied by brand new members of the Rapid family as the Rapid Spaceback, the first Škoda hatchback car in the compact segment, and the Chinese version of the Rapid. The Yeti also faced significant changes. With the facelift, two design variants of Škoda's compact SUV are now available: city-like Yeti and rugged Yeti Outdoor. Chinese customers were also given a Yeti with an extended wheelbase.</p>
<p>In 2015, Volkswagen admitted that it had installed pollution-cheating software in many of its cars to fool regulators that its cars met emissions standards when in fact they polluted at much higher-levels than government standards. 1.2 million Skoda cars worldwide were fitted with this emissions-cheating device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Skoda stated that Volkswagen would recall and cover refitting costs for all of the cars affected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volkswagen_emissions_testing_scandal" title="Volkswagen emissions testing scandal">Volkswagen emissions testing scandal</a>.</p>
<p>In 2015 Škoda was voted the most dependable car brand in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Škoda Auto is planning to manufacture a large, seven-seat <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">SUV</a> <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Kodiaq" title="Škoda Kodiaq">Škoda Kodiaq</a>, which should be a true <a href="/wiki/Off-road_vehicle" title="Off-road vehicle">off-roader</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> and will be introduced at the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Motor_Show" title="Paris Motor Show">Paris Motor Show</a> in October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of August 2016, Skoda was being sold in 102 countries with planned expansions to <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> within a year. The decision whether to expand into the North American market is planned to be made in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrification_strategy">Electrification strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electrification strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2015, new Škoda chairman Bernhard Maier stated that the Volkswagen Group "is working on a modular, new electric platform and we are in the team", and that "there is no alternative to electrification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Autocar_March_16.2C_2016_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Autocar_March_16.2C_2016-8">[8]</a></sup> The target of Škoda is to produce an <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric car</a> with a range of over 500 kilometres (310 mi), 15-minute charging time and a cost below a comparative combustion-engine vehicle. New Škoda corporate "Strategy 2025", which replaces the previous "Strategy 2018", aims to start production of a fully electric vehicle in 2020 or 2021, after a <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> Superb in 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Autocar_March_16.2C_2016_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Autocar_March_16.2C_2016-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_results">Financial results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Financial results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>ŠKODA has maintained sound financial stability over recent years. In 2013 the brand achieved sales revenues totalling €10.3 billion (2012: €10.4 billion). Due to the weak economic situation in many European countries and the expansion of the ŠKODA model range, operating profit reached a modest 522 million euros (2012: €712 million). ŠKODA achieved a successful start to 2014: As well as recording the highest number of deliveries to customers in a first quarter ever (247,200; up 12.1%), ŠKODA recorded a significant increase in sales revenue (23.7%) to almost 3 billion euros. Operating profit increased 65.2% to 185 million Euros over the previous year.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Rally_Championship">World Rally Championship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: World Rally Championship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a></div>
<p>Following a long history of class victories in lower levels of motorsport, Škoda became a participant in the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile">FIA</a> World Rally Championship in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1999_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="1999 World Rally Championship season">1999 season</a>, with <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Car" title="World Rally Car">World Rally Car</a> models of the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">Škoda Octavia</a>. Škoda's best result with the Octavia WRC was <a href="/wiki/Armin_Schwarz" title="Armin Schwarz">Armin Schwarz</a>'s third place at the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Safari_Rally" title="Safari Rally">Safari Rally</a>. From mid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2003_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="2003 World Rally Championship season">2003</a>, the Octavia was replaced by the smaller <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Fabia" title="Škoda Fabia">Škoda Fabia</a>. Škoda used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="2004 World Rally Championship season">2004 season</a> to develop the car further, but did not achieve much success the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="2005 World Rally Championship season">following season</a>. However, at the season-ending <a href="/wiki/Rally_Australia" title="Rally Australia">Rally Australia</a>, 1995 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_World_Rally_Championship_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of World Rally Championship Drivers' Champions">world champion</a> <a href="/wiki/Colin_McRae" title="Colin McRae">Colin McRae</a> was running second before retiring. Škoda then withdrew from the series, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="2006 World Rally Championship season">2006 season</a> saw Škoda represented by the semi-privateer <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull" title="Red Bull">Red Bull</a> Škoda Team. <a href="/wiki/Jan_Kopeck%C3%BD" title="Jan Kopecký">Jan Kopecký</a> drove the Fabia WRC to fifth place at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rally_Catalunya" title="Rally Catalunya">Rally Catalunya</a>, and as late as the <a href="/wiki/2007_Rallye_Deutschland" title="2007 Rallye Deutschland">2007 Rallye Deutschland</a> the Fabia still achieved a fifth-place result, again in the hands of Kopecký. Former works <a href="/wiki/Ford_World_Rally_Team" title="Ford World Rally Team">Ford</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citroen_World_Rally_Team" title="Citroen World Rally Team">Citroen</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Duval" title="François Duval">François Duval</a> also drove a Fabia WRC in 2006 for the privateer First Motorsport team, achieving a sixth-place finish in Catalunya.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Rally_Championship-2">World Rally Championship-2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: World Rally Championship-2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship-2" title="World Rally Championship-2">World Rally Championship-2</a></div>
<p>In 2009, Škoda entered the <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_Rally_Challenge" title="Intercontinental Rally Challenge">Intercontinental Rally Challenge</a> (IRC) for the first time, using the Fabia S2000, winning three rallies and finishing second in both the drivers and manufacturers championship. In 2010, Škoda won a total of seven IRC events winning both the manufacturers and driver championship for <a href="/wiki/Juho_H%C3%A4nninen" title="Juho Hänninen">Juho Hänninen</a>. These achievements were repeated in the following two seasons, with <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Mikkelsen" title="Andreas Mikkelsen">Andreas Mikkelsen</a> as the drivers' champion. In 2013, the Intercontinental Rally Challenge was merged with the <a href="/wiki/European_Rally_Championship" title="European Rally Championship">European Rally Championship</a> (ERC) and the team gained the drivers' championship title once again for <a href="/wiki/Jan_Kopeck%C3%BD" title="Jan Kopecký">Jan Kopecký</a>. The car was also raced by privateers in several championships, including Red Bull, Barwa, Rene Georges and Rufa in the 2010 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_2000_World_Rally_Championship" title="Super 2000 World Rally Championship">Super 2000 World Rally Championship</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonneville_Speedway">Bonneville Speedway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0koda_Auto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bonneville Speedway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2011, a special <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">Škoda Octavia vRS</a> set the world record at the <a href="/wiki/Bonneville_Speedway" title="Bonneville Speedway">Bonneville Speedway</a> and became the fastest car in the world with an engine up to two litres, when it hit 227 mph (365 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup> The current fastest production Škoda car is the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Superb#Third_generation_.282015-present.29" title="Škoda Superb">Škoda Superb III</a>, with a top speed of 250 km/h (160 mph) and an acceleration from 0 to 100 km/h (0 to 62 mph) in 5.8 seconds.</p>

<p>In 1923, two different trademarks were registered at the Office for Innovation and Model Registration in <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88" title="Plzeň">Plzeň</a>. The first depicted a winged arrow pointing to the right with five feathers in a circle and the second was a winged arrow with three feathers. The famous winged arrow with three feathers still forms the Škoda logo today. The <i>ŠKODA</i> text was added to the logo in 1936. The arrow represents speed, the wings progress and freedom, the eye precision and the circle unity, completeness, world and harmony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[48]</a></sup></p>
