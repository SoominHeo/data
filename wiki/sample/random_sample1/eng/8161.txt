<p>For <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">local government</a> purposes, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> is divided into 32 areas designated as "council areas", which are all governed by <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> designated as "councils". They have the option under the Local Government (Gaelic Names) (Scotland) Act 1997<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> of being known (but not re-designated) as a "comhairle" when opting for a Gaelic name; only Comhairle Nan <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Eilean Siar</a> (Council of the Western Isles) has chosen this option, whereas the Highland Council (Comhairle na Gaidhealtachd) has adopted its Gaelic form alongside its English equivalent informally.</p>
<p>The council areas have been in existence since 1 April 1996, under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_etc._(Scotland)_Act_1994" title="Local Government etc. (Scotland) Act 1994">Local Government etc. (Scotland) Act 1994</a>.</p>
<p>Historically, Scotland has been divided into 34 counties or shires. Although these no longer have any administrative function, they are still used to some extent in Scotland for cultural and geographical purposes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_subdivisions_of_Scotland">History of the subdivisions of Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the subdivisions of Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_High_Middle_Ages#Burghs" title="Scotland in the High Middle Ages">History of burghs</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_local_government_in_Scotland" title="History of local government in Scotland">History of local government in Scotland</a></div>
<p>Traditionally burghs have been the key unit of the <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">local government of Scotland</a>, being highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_entity" title="Autonomous entity">autonomous</a> entities, with rights to representation in the old <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a>. Even after the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a>, burghs continued to be the principal subdivision. Until 1889 administration was on a <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> basis.</p>
<p>The years following 1889 saw the introduction of a hierarchy of local government administration comprising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counties_of_Scotland" title="Counties of Scotland">counties</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counties_of_cities" title="Counties of cities">counties of cities</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_burghs" title="Large burghs">large burghs</a> and small burghs.</p>
<p>With effect from 16 May 1975 and until 31 March 1996 the local government divisions of Scotland consisted of an upper tier of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regions_and_districts_of_Scotland" title="Regions and districts of Scotland">regions</a></i> each containing a lower tier of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regions_and_districts_of_Scotland" title="Regions and districts of Scotland">districts</a></i> except for the single-tier <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_council_areas_of_Scotland" title="Island council areas of Scotland">island council areas</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_subdivisions">Other subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scotland has several other administrative divisions, some of which are handled by joint boards of the councils.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Police_and_fire_services">Police and fire services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Police and fire services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Police and Fire Reform (Scotland) Act 2012 resulted in the merger of local <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire services</a> on 1 April 2013 to form the <a href="/wiki/Police_Scotland" title="Police Scotland">Police Service of Scotland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd"><i>Seirbheis Phoilis na h-Alba</i></span>) and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Scottish Fire and Rescue Service">Scottish Fire and Rescue Service</a> (SFRS, Scottish Gaelic: <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd"><i>Seirbheis Smàlaidh agus Teasairginn na h-Alba</i></span>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historically">Historically</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historically">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id=".28Pre_1975.29">(Pre 1975)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: (Pre 1975)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Policing was the responsibility of the Cities and Burghs of Scotland. (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_burghs_in_Scotland" title="List of burghs in Scotland">List of burghs in Scotland</a>)</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id=".281975_-_2013.29">(1975 - 2013)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: (1975 - 2013)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_registration">Land registration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Land registration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The current land registration system in Scotland divides Scotland into 33 counties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> each coming into effect on various dates between 1981 and 2003. These areas in most cases resemble those of the pre-1975 administrative counties with Glasgow being the only current city to form a registration county.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sheriffdoms">Sheriffdoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sheriffdoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sheriffdom" title="Sheriffdom">Sheriffdoms</a> are judicial areas. Since 1 January 1975, these have been six in number:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_parishes">Civil parishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Civil parishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scotland is divided into <a href="/wiki/List_of_civil_parishes_in_Scotland" title="List of civil parishes in Scotland">871 civil parishes</a> which often resemble same-named but legally different ecclesiastical parishes. Although they have had no administrative function since 1930, they still exist and are still used for statistical purposes such as the census. Many former civil parish areas also continue to form current <a href="/wiki/Registration_district" title="Registration district">registration districts</a>. It should be noted that many boundary changes have occurred over the years and that an area currently derived from an old parish might no longer contain a place previously within that parish. Similarly, county boundaries (as still used for land registration) have also changed over the years such that a parish mentioned historically (generally before the 1860s) as being in one county (or sometimes two due to straddling a border) might now be in a neighbouring county and consequentially in a different succeeding council area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communities">Communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The base level of sub-division in Scotland is that of <a href="/wiki/Community_council" title="Community council">communities</a>, which may elect <i>community councils (CCs)</i>. The main role of the CCs is to channel local opinion to larger local-government bodies. Otherwise, they have very limited powers. There are around 1,200 communities in Scotland. Not all communities have councils; some have joint councils.</p>
<p>Scottish communities are the nearest equivalent to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parishes</a> in England.</p>
