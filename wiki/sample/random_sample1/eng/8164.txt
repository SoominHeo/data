<p><b>Scofield</b> is a town in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_County,_Utah" title="Carbon County, Utah">Carbon County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The population was 23 at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-3">[3]</a></sup> Scofield's name is frequently applied to the <a href="/wiki/Scofield_Mine_disaster" title="Scofield Mine disaster">1900 mine disaster</a> in the Pleasant Valley Coal Company's Winter Quarters mine. The community was named for General Charles W. Scofield, a timber contractor and local mine official. It is the smallest incorporated area in Utah by population.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scofield_Cemetery.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="146" data-file-width="275" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Scofield_Cemetery.jpg/220px-Scofield_Cemetery.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a7/Scofield_Cemetery.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scofield_Cemetery.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Scofield Cemetery and some of its wooden gravestones</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The town of Scofield is situated on high ground two miles south of the reservoir of the same name, the oldest and largest of the major impoundments on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasatch_Plateau" title="Wasatch Plateau">Wasatch Plateau</a>. Once the most populous community in Carbon County, Scofield has shrunk to only a few permanent residents. What has slowed the continual decline has become outsiders constructing summer vacation homes. The old brick school stands empty at the upper end of town, and there are abandoned buildings scattered through what was once the business district. Only the cemetery on a hill to the east suggests that this was once a community of some size. Visitors to the cemetery quickly become aware of the diverse ethnicity that populated the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-edge-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement">Settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the luxuriant growth of the native grasses indigenous to this region, this valley was first utilized as pastures for the immense herds of cattle that came from <a href="/wiki/Utah_Valley" title="Utah Valley">Utah Valley</a>. Among the first settlers of this region were S. J. Harkness, T. H. Thomas, William Burrows, O. G. Kimball, D. D. Green, A. H. Earll, R. McKecheney, who were attracted by the immense ranges for their cattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disast_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-disast-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_coal_and_coming_of_the_railroad">Discovery of coal and coming of the railroad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery of coal and coming of the railroad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">Coal</a> was discovered in the valley in 1875, and two years later a small mine was opened on the western slopes of the canyon, with the coal transported out along narrow roads. The winter of 1877 came early and was very severe, stranding the miners in the coal pit and keeping them snowbound until the following February. The ordeal led the miners to name their forced camp "<a href="/wiki/Winter_Quarters,_Utah" title="Winter Quarters, Utah">Winter Quarters</a>". It became one of the first commercial coal mines in the state. Most of the first miners at the Winter Quarters were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mormon" title="Mormon">Mormon</a> converts from the coal districts of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. One of them, David Williams, leased the Winter Quarters Mine from the Pleasant Valley Coal Company from 1880 to 1885, operating in the dual capacity of mine manager and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Bishop (Latter Day Saints)">bishop</a> of the local <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">LDS</a> <a href="/wiki/Ward_(LDS_Church)" title="Ward (LDS Church)">ward</a>. When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_Western_Railway" title="Rio Grande Western Railway">Rio Grande Western Railway</a> acquired the coal company, they undertook the development of a new mine on Mud Creek, a mile from Winter Quarters, much to the chagrin of Williams, who protested that the new mine infringed on his lease. Because Bishop Williams controlled the Mormon miners, the Pleasant Valley Coal Company brought in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Chinese_Americans" title="History of Chinese Americans">Chinese laborers</a> to work the Mud Creek mine. Soon a large contingent of <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a> were recruited, along with <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-edge-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As coal veins commenced to be worked, it was not long before the discoverers found out that the supply seemed inexhaustible. The railway companies finding that the coal fields were of such magnitude and covered so much territory, began to survey for practicable routes to reach the coal. The route through <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Fork_River" title="Spanish Fork River">Spanish Fork Canyon</a> was finally decided upon, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrow-gauge_railroad" title="Narrow-gauge railroad">narrow-gauge railroad</a> was built into Scofield. Before the railroad was completed, winter set in, and the last few miles were laid upon the ice and snow as it was impossible to find the ground due to the snow drifts. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Pacific" title="Union Pacific">Union Pacific</a> Coal Company claimed all of the land on the town site, and those building a home were compelled to lease the spot of ground desired from this company. Not knowing how long they would be permitted to remain as laborers in the mines, the men did not build elaborate homes, as they knew that should the work cease, they would be compelled to sacrifice their homes. Log cabins were the order of the day, as lath and plaster was out of reach on account of high prices. The Union Pacific still continued to collect their ground rent, and the knowledge that the miners had of the company that was ruling the camp at that time deterred them from beautifying their homes as they desired.</p>

<p>However, this state of affairs was not to continue for long, as the rights of the company to hold the land came to be questioned. Part of their land was <a href="/wiki/Land_claim" title="Land claim">jumped</a>, and their title was found to be imperfect. In fact, they had no title whatever to the land now incorporated in the townsite. The business district was more than a mile long and boasted dozens of substantial stone buildings, including an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Order_of_Odd_Fellows" title="Independent Order of Odd Fellows">Odd Fellows Hall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disast_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-disast-5">[5]</a></sup> As the mine and the community grew, new and more efficient methods were sought to move the coal from the mines, and so the Utah &amp; Pleasant Valley Railroad was constructed, running from Springville to Winter Quarters and Scofield. It connected with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver_%26_Rio_Grande" title="Denver &amp; Rio Grande">Denver &amp; Rio Grande</a> line in <a href="/wiki/Colton,_Utah" title="Colton, Utah">Colton</a> 18 miles (29 km) to the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-edge-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1882, the Utah Fuel Company took over the mine and town, and it soon became a subsidiary of the Denver &amp; Rio Grande Railroad. The region continued to thrive until 1900, when there were several hundred men in the mines and residents that numbered as high as 1,800. The mine was considered to be the safest in the region and according to reports, was free from the gases that plagued so many other coal operations.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disaster">Disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scofield_Mine_disaster" title="Scofield Mine disaster">Scofield Mine disaster</a></div>
<p>On May 1, 1900, an errant spark touched off the fine haze of coal dust deep underground, and the Winter Quarters #4 mine exploded with fury. Exactly 100 of the men were killed in an instant, and another 99 died from the poisonous <a href="/wiki/Afterdamp" title="Afterdamp">afterdamp</a>, making this one of the worst coal mine disasters in history. Death came so swiftly that some of the mineworkers were found still clutching their tools. That one moment of time left 105 widows and 270 fatherless children behind. Almost 150 of the slain miners were buried in the cemetery in nearby Scofield, and two special funeral trains carried the rest of the victims to burial grounds in Utah and in other states.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 28, just four weeks after the disaster in Number Four, coal mining resumed at Winter Quarters. While some of those who had escaped the explosion left the area for other work, many others returned to the mines. The dead were soon replaced, though production evidently suffered somewhat from the loss of more experienced miners. Despite declining profitability, the Winter Quarters mines continued to operate until the 1930s. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_tipple" title="Coal tipple">coal tipple</a> and other large structures were dismantled for their salvage value during World War II; the chapel and the boarding house were leveled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-edge-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scofield, sustained by several mines in Pleasant Valley, was still the largest town in Carbon County in 1915, when its citizens made an attempt to have the county seat moved to their community from <a href="/wiki/Price,_Utah" title="Price, Utah">Price</a>. By the 1920s, however, the coal industry in Pleasant Valley was clearly in decline and most mines ceased operation causing the town to lose nearly all its residents.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town of Scofield lies in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bituminous" title="Bituminous">bituminous</a> coal field of Carbon County, Utah, about 19 miles (31 km) from the main line of the Rio Grande Western Railroad, and is nestled among the hills that surround the upper part of Pleasant Valley. The town is situated along Mud Creek, just south (upstream) of the Scofield Reservoir. The outlet of the reservoir is the <a href="/wiki/Price_River" title="Price River">Price River</a>, which flows southeast to the <a href="/wiki/Green_River_(Colorado_River)" title="Green River (Colorado River)">Green River</a> and is thus part of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a> basin.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, Scofield has a total area of 0.69 square miles (1.8 km<sup>2</sup>), all of it land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Utah_State_Route_96" title="Utah State Route 96">Utah State Route 96</a> passes through Scofield, leading north then east 17 miles (27 km) to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_6" title="U.S. Route 6">U.S. Route 6</a> near <a href="/wiki/Colton,_Utah" title="Colton, Utah">Colton</a>, and south 6 miles (9.7 km) to its end at <a href="/wiki/Clear_Creek,_Utah" title="Clear Creek, Utah">Clear Creek</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scofield has an <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">alpine subarctic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a> <i>Dfc</i>) with enormous yearly snowfall in its long, cold winters due to its location in the Wasatch Range.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-2">[2]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 28 people, 12 households, and 9 families residing in the town. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 55.9 people per square mile (21.6/km²). There were 78 housing units at an average density of 155.6 per square mile (60.2/km²). The racial makeup of the town was 100.00% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>.</p>
<p>There were 12 households out of which 25.0% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 66.7% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 8.3% had a female householder with no husband present, and 16.7% were non-families. 16.7% of all households were made up of individuals and 8.3% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.33 and the average family size was 2.50.</p>
<p>In the town the population was spread out with 14.3% under the age of 18, 10.7% from 18 to 24, 25.0% from 25 to 44, 28.6% from 45 to 64, and 21.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 45 years. For every 100 females there were 75.0 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 100.0 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the town was $26,250, and the median income for a family was $28,750. Males had a median income of $26,250 versus $14,375 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the town was $10,764. There were no families and 9.1% of the population living below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including no under eighteens and none of those over 64.</p>
<p>Although the number of people living in Scofield is low, many own cabins, summer and winter homes, and fishing shacks around Scofield Reservoir. There is also a state park located by the lake.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scofield_State_Park">Scofield State Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Scofield State Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scofield.jpg"><img alt="Scofield.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="236" data-file-width="350" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Scofield.jpg/220px-Scofield.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Scofield.jpg/330px-Scofield.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Scofield.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Scofield State Park surrounds <a href="/wiki/Scofield_Reservoir" title="Scofield Reservoir">Scofield Reservoir</a> and has three different camping areas: Mountain View, Madsen Bay, and Lake Side. Mountain View is a 34-unit campground that includes restrooms, heated showers, fish cleaning and disposal stations, a group use pavilion, a boat launching ramp, and rental boats. Madsen Bay is a 40-unit campground with restrooms, fish cleaning and disposal areas, a boat ramp, and parking for day use. Lake Side has no camping areas and is strictly day use, offering restrooms, a group use pavilion and a fishing platform for the disabled.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clear_Creek_Camp">Clear Creek Camp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scofield,_Utah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Clear Creek Camp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Clear Creek Camp is an outdoor education center located 2 miles (3 km) south of the town of Scofield, for post-fifth grade students in the Alpine School District. Each summer, Clear Creek Camp hosts approximately 1,100 students. The summer camp experience is designed to educate students about the outdoors and help foster a deep appreciation for the abundant natural resources and beauty in their surroundings. The camp has its roots in an outdoor education program held at Central Elementary School in the 1940s. Between 1940 and the 1960s, the program eventually grew to be so successful that a summer camp was requested. It was held at various locations for many years, including the town of <a href="/wiki/Clear_Creek,_Utah" title="Clear Creek, Utah">Clear Creek</a> for which it is named. After being there for a few years, property became available from Questar (then Mountain Fuel) with six cabins and a multi-purpose facility in 1973. The facilities were ready to use for groups of students in 1974, and Alpine School District has used the same grounds since then.</p>
