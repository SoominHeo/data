<p><b>Scholastic Corporation</b> is an American publishing, education and media company known for publishing, selling, and distributing books and educational materials for schools, teachers, parents, and children. Products are distributed to schools and districts, to consumers through the schools via <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reading_clubs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reading clubs (page does not exist)">reading clubs</a> and fairs, and through retail stores and online sales. The business has three segments: Children Book Publishing &amp; Distribution (Trade, Book Clubs and Book Fairs), Education, and International. Scholastic holds the perpetual U.S. publishing rights to <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games" title="The Hunger Games">The Hunger Games</a></i> book series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Scholastic is the world's largest publisher and distributor of children's books and a leader in print and digital educational materials for pre-K to grade 12.</p>
<p>In addition to <i>Harry Potter</i> and <i>The Hunger Games</i>, the company is known for its school book clubs and book fairs, classroom magazine <i>Scholastic News</i>, and popular book series: <i><a href="/wiki/Clifford_the_Big_Red_Dog" title="Clifford the Big Red Dog">Clifford the Big Red Dog</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Goosebumps" title="Goosebumps">Goosebumps</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_School_Bus" title="The Magic School Bus">The Magic School Bus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Underpants" title="Captain Underpants">Captain Underpants</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Animorphs" title="Animorphs">Animorphs</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/I_Spy_(Scholastic)" title="I Spy (Scholastic)">I Spy</a></i>. Scholastic also publishes instructional reading and writing programs, and offers professional learning and consultancy services for school improvement. Clifford the Big Red Dog serves as the mascot for Scholastic.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholastic_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1920, Maurice R. "Robbie" Robinson founded the business he named Scholastic Publishing Company in his hometown of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilkinsburg" title="Wilkinsburg">Wilkinsburg</a>, right outside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. As a publisher of youth magazines, the first publication was <i>The Western Pennsylvania Scholastic.</i> It covered high school sports and social activities and debuted on October 22, 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1926, Scholastic published its first book, <i>Saplings</i>, a collection of selected student writings by winners of the Scholastic Writing Awards.</p>
<p>For many years the company continued its focus on serving the youth market, publishing low-cost magazines and later paperback books. The company continued under the name Scholastic Magazines throughout the 1970s.</p>
<p>After World War II, cheap paperback books became available. In 1948, Scholastic entered the school book club business with its division T.A.B., or Teen Age Book Club, offering classic titles priced at 25 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">cents</a>.</p>
<p>In 1957, Scholastic established its first international subsidiary in Toronto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholastic_Canada" title="Scholastic Canada">Scholastic Canada</a>, later moving to <a href="/wiki/Markham,_Ontario" title="Markham, Ontario">Markham, Ontario</a>.</p>
<p>By the 1960s, international publishing locations were added in England (1964), New Zealand (1964) and Sydney (1968).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, Richard "Dick" Robinson, the son of founder M. R. Robinson, became President of Scholastic Inc. Named Chief Executive Officer in 1975 and Chairman in 1982, he remains in these positions.</p>
<p>During the 1970s, Scholastic was well known for Scholastic Book Clubs, now called Scholastic Reading Club, a book purchasing service delivered through schools, and magazine publications aimed at youths: <i>Wow</i> (preschoolers and elementary schoolers), <i><a href="/wiki/Dynamite_(magazine)" title="Dynamite (magazine)">Dynamite</a></i> (pre-teens), and <i>Bananas</i> (teens). Scholastic now publishes 33 classroom magazines including <i>Scholastic News</i>, <i>Action</i>, <i>Scope</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Storyworks" title="Storyworks">Storyworks</a></i>, <i>SuperScience</i>, <i>Science World</i>, <i>Math</i> and more, that reach 14 million readers.</p>
<p>The Scholastic Education business sells instructional reading and writing programs such as Guided and leveled reading and print and digital classroom magazines, along with professional learning programs and consulting/training on Family &amp; Community Engagement and Learning Supports. Classroom Magazines have 15 million subscribers.</p>
<p>During the mid-1990s, Scholastic entered the educational technology market, working with Dr. Ted Hasselbring of Vanderbilt University to create READ 180, a blended-learning, reading intervention program for students in grades 4 through 12 who are two or more grades below grade level. Since then, READ 180 has been listed in the What Works Clearinghouse and has a record of positive results in a wide range of efficacy studies with various student populations, including special education students and English language learners. Scholastic Education has since created SYSTEM 44, a technology-based phonics program for students in grades 3 through 12, iREAD, a supplemental educational technology program for grades K-2, MATH 180, mathematics intervention for middle school, and FasttMath, a technology based program to teach basic math facts. The EdTech and Services business was sold to Houghton Mifflin Harcourt in 2015 for $575 million.</p>
<p>To appeal to American children, in 1997, Scholastic (through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_A._Levine_Books" title="Arthur A. Levine Books">Arthur A. Levine Books</a>) purchased the U.S. publication rights to the first <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> book, <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone">Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone</a></i>; it was renamed <i>Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone</i>. It continues to publish Harry Potter books, each title a best seller.</p>
<p>Scholastic's growth has continued by acquiring other media companies. In February 2012, it bought <a href="/wiki/Weekly_Reader_Publishing" title="Weekly Reader Publishing">Weekly Reader Publishing</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reader%27s_Digest_Association" title="Reader's Digest Association">Reader's Digest Association</a>, and announced in July that year that it planned to discontinue separate issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Reader" title="Weekly Reader">Weekly Reader</a></i> magazines after more than a century of publication, and co-branded the magazines as "Scholastic News/Weekly Reader".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Other acquisitions include <a href="/wiki/Klutz_Press" title="Klutz Press">Klutz</a> in 2002, and the reference publisher <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a>, which publishes the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grolier_Multimedia_Encyclopedia" title="Grolier Multimedia Encyclopedia">Grolier Multimedia Encyclopedia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Book_of_Knowledge" title="The New Book of Knowledge">The New Book of Knowledge</a></i> in 2000 and <a href="/wiki/Weston_Woods_Studios" title="Weston Woods Studios">Weston Woods Studios</a> in 1996. In 2015, Scholastic acquired Troubadour, Ltd. in the U.K.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2000" title="United States presidential election, 2000">2000 presidential election</a>, Scholastic organized the <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_News_Kids_Press_Corps" title="Scholastic News Kids Press Corps">Scholastic News Kids Press Corps</a>, which today includes more than 30 national and International kid reporters ages 10–14.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Scholastic_Art_.26_Writing_Awards">The Scholastic Art &amp; Writing Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholastic_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Scholastic Art &amp; Writing Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Founded in 1923 by Maurice R. Robinson, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Scholastic_Art_%26_Writing_Awards" title="The Scholastic Art &amp; Writing Awards">The Scholastic Art &amp; Writing Awards</a>, administered by the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_for_Young_Artists_%26_Writers" title="Alliance for Young Artists &amp; Writers">Alliance for Young Artists &amp; Writers</a>, have motivated more than 13 million students, recognized more than 9 million young artists and writers, and provided more than $25 million in awards and scholarships. These Awards have been the largest source of scholarship funding for teenage artists and writers, and the nation's longest-running, most prestigious art and writing awards.</p>
<p>In the U.S.A, the process begins as young artists and writers submit creative works to the Alliance's regional affiliates. The most outstanding works of art and writing (Gold Key and Silver Key winners) from each region are forwarded to the Alliance for Young Artists &amp; Writers in New York City to be reviewed on a national level. Panels of professional jurors select the national award recipients. Regional awards are administered by a network of nearly 100 affiliates that include school systems and school boards, nonprofit organizations, government agencies, foundations, arts agencies, businesses, libraries, museums, teacher councils and institutions of higher education, which share a commitment to identifying emerging local artists and writers.</p>
<p>The Awards recognize written and artistic works in 30 categories, including Architecture, Comic Art, Ceramics &amp; Glass, Digital Art, Design, Drawing, Fashion, Film &amp; Animation, Jewelry, Mixed Media, Painting, Photography, Printmaking, Sculpture, Video Games, Art Portfolio, Photography Portfolio, Dramatic Script, Humor, Journalism, Personal Essay/Memoir, Persuasive Writing, Poetry, Novel Writing, Science Fiction/Fantasy, Short Story, Short, Short Story, General Writing Portfolio, Nonfiction Portfolio, and Creativity &amp; Citizenship.</p>

<p>Scholastic <a href="/wiki/Book_sales_club" title="Book sales club">reading clubs</a> are offered at schools in many countries. Typically, teachers administer the program to the students in their own classes, but in some cases, the program is administered by a central contact for the entire school. Within Scholastic, Reading Clubs is a separate unit (compared to, e.g., Education). Reading clubs are arranged by age/grade.</p>
<p>Scholastic also offers a host of specialty book club fliers including <b>Club Leo</b> (Spanish language for grades K–8), and <b>Click</b> (Computer games and media for all ages).</p>
<p>Scholastic typically offers participating schools and classrooms 1 "point" for every dollar (or local unit of currency) of products ordered. Additional points may be earned during special promotion times, such as the beginning of the school year. Points may then be redeemed for books and school supplies at a rate of approximately 20 points to the dollar. At minimum, schools earn 5% of book orders in free products. With special promotions, return rate can be higher (15–100%).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholastic_Parents_Media">Scholastic Parents Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholastic_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scholastic Parents Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholastic Parents Media publishes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholastic_Parent_%26_Child_Magazine" title="Scholastic Parent &amp; Child Magazine">Scholastic Parent &amp; Child Magazine</a>. The group also specializes in online advertising sales and custom programs designed for parents and children ages 0–6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scholastic_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholastic has been criticized for inappropriately marketing to children. Also, Scholastic now requires parents to submit children's names with birth dates to place online orders, creating controversy. A significant number of titles carried have strong media tie-ins and are considered relatively short in literary and artistic merit by some critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Consumer groups have also attacked Scholastic for selling too many toys and video games to children, rather than focusing on just books. In July, 2005, Scholastic determined that certain leases previously accounted for as operating leases should have been accounted for as capital leases. The cumulative effect, if recorded in the current year, would be material. As a result, it decided to restate its financial statements.</p>
