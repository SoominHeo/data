<p><b>Scott Christopher Proctor</b> (born January 2, 1977) is an American former professional baseball <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a>. He played in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) between 2004 and 2011 for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a>. In 2012, he played for the <a href="/wiki/Doosan_Bears" title="Doosan Bears">Doosan Bears</a> of the <a href="/wiki/KBO_League" title="KBO League">KBO League</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball_career">Baseball career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baseball career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_career">Amateur career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amateur career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proctor was drafted in the 17th round of the <a href="/wiki/1995_Major_League_Baseball_draft" title="1995 Major League Baseball draft">1995 Major League Baseball draft</a> by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a> out of <a href="/wiki/Martin_County_High_School" title="Martin County High School">Martin County High School</a>, but decided to attend <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_University" title="Florida State University">Florida State University</a>, where he played for the <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_Seminoles_baseball" title="Florida State Seminoles baseball">Florida State Seminoles baseball</a> team under head coach <a href="/wiki/Mike_Martin_(baseball_coach)" title="Mike Martin (baseball coach)">Mike Martin</a>. He was 10–2 in 60 career games for Florida State.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_leagues">Minor leagues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minor leagues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, Proctor was drafted in the fifth round by the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> and assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Yakima_Bears" title="Yakima Bears">Yakima Bears</a> to start his professional career. He played for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vero_Beach_Dodgers" title="Vero Beach Dodgers">Vero Beach Dodgers</a> (2000–2001), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Suns" title="Jacksonville Suns">Jacksonville Suns</a> (2001–2003) and <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_51s" title="Las Vegas 51s">Las Vegas 51s</a> (2003).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_Yankees">New York Yankees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: New York Yankees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On July 31, 2003, Proctor was traded to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> with <a href="/wiki/Bubba_Crosby" title="Bubba Crosby">Bubba Crosby</a> for <a href="/wiki/Robin_Ventura" title="Robin Ventura">Robin Ventura</a>. The Yankees assigned him to the Triple-A <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Clippers" title="Columbus Clippers">Columbus Clippers</a>. Proctor made his <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> debut for the Yankees on April 20, 2004 against the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a>, working <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> innings in relief and allowing 2 earned runs.</p>
<p>Throughout the course of his first season, he appeared in 26 games, pitched 25 innings, finished 12 games, and posted a 2–1 record and a 5.40 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">ERA</a> with 21 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strikeouts" title="Strikeouts">strikeouts</a>. He finished the 2005 season with a 6.04 ERA and a 1–0 record.</p>
<p>In 2006, Proctor emerged as a durable, reliable late-inning option for manager <a href="/wiki/Joe_Torre" title="Joe Torre">Joe Torre</a>. Finally harnessing his breaking pitches, Proctor led the American League with 83 appearances, often pitching more than one inning or in consecutive games. Proctor notched his first career save in 2006. Along with <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Farnsworth" title="Kyle Farnsworth">Kyle Farnsworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Bruney" title="Brian Bruney">Brian Bruney</a>, Proctor was a part of the Yankees' bridge between the starters and closer <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Rivera" title="Mariano Rivera">Mariano Rivera</a>, and reprised that role for the first part of the 2007 season.</p>
<p>Proctor's heavy use as a Yankee reliever led to concerns that Torre was overusing him. General manager <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cashman" title="Brian Cashman">Brian Cashman</a> revealed in 2011 that he confronted Torre about Proctor's workload and asked the pitcher to be more honest about whether he could pitch on a given day: "I met with Proctor and said, 'You better stop telling the manager [that you can pitch] because the way he manages' — I'm not criticizing Joe, that's just the way he is — 'He wants an honest answer. Just tell him no.<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proctor pitched in 5 postseason games for the Yankees: two against <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles</a> and three against <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit</a>. In six innings, he struck out two and gave up just one run.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Los_Angeles_Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Los Angeles Dodgers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>On July 31, 2007, Proctor was traded to the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Infielder" title="Infielder">infielder</a> <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Betemit" title="Wilson Betemit">Wilson Betemit</a>. He was mostly used as a late inning setup man. He finished the 2007 season with an impressive 3–0 record and a 3.38 ERA in 31 relief appearances for the Dodgers.</p>
<p>He had a rough 2008 season and spent most of it on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disabled_List" title="Disabled List">Disabled List</a>. However, he pitched much better in the month of September, helping the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a> win the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_League_West_Division" title="National League West Division">National League West Division</a> title. He finished the season with a 2–0 record, but a high ERA of 6.05 in just 41 games. He became a free agent at the end of the season.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida_Marlins">Florida Marlins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Florida Marlins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2009, he signed a one-year contract with the Florida Marlins worth $750,000, with an additional $250,000 in incentives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> During Spring training, he was placed on the disabled list with elbow pain. When the pain remained, and ligament fraying was found, he underwent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tommy_John_surgery" title="Tommy John surgery">Tommy John surgery</a> on May 12, 2009, and missed the entire 2009 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On October 9, 2009, Proctor was released by the Marlins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlanta_Braves">Atlanta Braves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Atlanta Braves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 4, 2009 Proctor's agent, Mark Rodgers, released that Proctor had signed a split contract with the Braves and received an invitation to Spring training. He spent 2010 pitching for the Double-A <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Braves" title="Mississippi Braves">Mississippi Braves</a> and Triple-A <a href="/wiki/Gwinnett_Braves" title="Gwinnett Braves">Gwinnett Braves</a>. As of August 14, he had compiled a 7.82 ERA in 31 appearances between the 2 teams.</p>
<p>On March 27, 2011 after pitching with a 5.06 ERA in ten appearances in the <a href="/wiki/2011_Atlanta_Braves_season" title="2011 Atlanta Braves season">2011 Atlanta Braves</a> Spring training camp, Proctor was released from the Braves' organization. Had he remained on the Braves' roster the following day, his $750,000 salary would have been paid in full. Instead, the Braves merely had to pay him a fraction of his salary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just over a week after being released from the Braves organization, Proctor re-signed to the Braves on April 3, 2011, with a minor league contract. He has been added to the roster of their Triple-A affiliate, the <a href="/wiki/Gwinnett_Braves" title="Gwinnett Braves">Gwinnett Braves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He had his contract purchased on May 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proctor was released from the Braves on August 10, 2011 after posting a 6.44 ERA in 31 appearances. His roster spot was filled by <a href="/wiki/Arodys_Vizca%C3%ADno" title="Arodys Vizcaíno">Arodys Vizcaíno</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In two seasons with Atlanta, Proctor, with a record of 2–3, had a 6.43 ERA in 37 appearances, allowing 35 hits, 5 Home runs, 25 earned runs and 23 walks with 24 strikeouts in 35 innings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_the_Yankees">Return to the Yankees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Return to the Yankees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 13, he signed a minor league contract with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> and was assigned to the Triple-A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scranton/Wilkes-Barre_Yankees" title="Scranton/Wilkes-Barre Yankees">Scranton/Wilkes-Barre Yankees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was called up on September 1 and made his debut for the Yankees on September 5, entering in relief against the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>. He declared for free agency on October 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doosan_Bears">Doosan Bears</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Doosan Bears">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proctor signed with the <a href="/wiki/Doosan_Bears" title="Doosan Bears">Doosan Bears</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Baseball_Organization" title="Korea Baseball Organization">Korea Baseball Organization</a> for the 2012 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="San_Francisco_Giants">San Francisco Giants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: San Francisco Giants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proctor signed a minor league contract with the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> on January 2, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baltimore_Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Baltimore Orioles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proctor was traded to the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Orioles</a> on March 29, 2013 for cash considerations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitching">Pitching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pitching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proctor is a <a href="/wiki/Power_pitcher" title="Power pitcher">power pitcher</a> with a diverse pitch repertoire. Proctor's fastball ranges from 94 mph to almost 100, although he was known to throw beyond 100 as the closer for the <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Clippers" title="Columbus Clippers">Columbus Clippers</a>, the Yankees' former Triple-A affiliate. As a former minor league starter, Proctor features several pitches. He will throw both four and <a href="/wiki/Two-seam_fastball" title="Two-seam fastball">two-seam fastballs</a>. Proctor's secondary pitches include a mid-eighties <a href="/wiki/Slider" title="Slider">slider</a>, a high seventies <a href="/wiki/Curveball" title="Curveball">curveball</a>, and an occasional <a href="/wiki/Changeup" title="Changeup">changeup</a> in the low eighties. He will throw all but his change-up in any count, but his primary weapons are the <a href="/wiki/Four-seam_fastball" title="Four-seam fastball">four-seam fastball</a>, the curve, and the slider.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proctor has run into some instances where he has appeared to assume the responsibility of retaliation for the Yankees. In two instances, Proctor allegedly threw at batters with the intention for retaliation.</p>
<p>One instance occurred in a game against the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> in which Scott Proctor threw behind <a href="/wiki/Yuniesky_Betancourt" title="Yuniesky Betancourt">Yuniesky Betancourt</a>. Betancourt and Proctor exchanged heated words. Proctor may have felt that the Yankees were in position to retaliate after his teammate, Yankees first baseman <a href="/wiki/Josh_Phelps" title="Josh Phelps">Josh Phelps</a>, was hit by a pitch. But Phelps was hit in retaliation for his part in, what appeared to be, an intentional hit on Seattle catcher <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Johjima" title="Kenji Johjima">Kenji Johjima</a>. Therefore, the incident between Phelps and Johjima has already been settled with the "beaning" of Phelps but Proctor took offense to the fact the retaliation was exercised on Phelps. Proctor was suspended after that incident.</p>
<p>Another instance occurred on June 1, 2007 when Proctor hit <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> first baseman <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Youkilis" title="Kevin Youkilis">Kevin Youkilis</a> in the shoulder after two Yankee batters were hit. Youkilis was the fifth hit batsman in the game. A visibly enraged Youkilis had to be held back by Yankee catcher Jorge Posada. Proctor was soon ejected after both benches calmed, though Proctor remained upset over his ejection. After the game, he protested to the media that the pitch had gotten away from him and that he had no reason to hit Youkilis as he had a 2–2 count. Before the following game, during the YES Pre-Game Show, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Torre" title="Joe Torre">Joe Torre</a> told reporters that after Proctor's ejection, Proctor stormed into Torre's office and insisted that he did not throw at Youkilis intentionally, an explanation Torre accepted. Torre, however, did not protest Proctor's resulting one-game suspension.</p>
<p>On June 30, after a poor performance in a loss to the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> (and after taking the loss in each of the Yankees' last two games), Proctor lit fire to his equipment on the field, just feet from the Yankees dugout.</p>
<p>In June 2009, Proctor revealed that he was a recovering alcoholic and had been attending <a href="/wiki/Alcoholics_Anonymous" title="Alcoholics Anonymous">Alcoholics Anonymous</a> meetings. He credits <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Rivera" title="Mariano Rivera">Mariano Rivera</a> with urging him to straighten his life out.</p>
<p>Proctor is now the assistant coach of Martin County Highschool varsity baseball team.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_use">Heavy use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Proctor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Heavy use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The heavy usage of Scott Proctor early in his career has drawn criticism of the way then Yankee manager Joe Torre used relievers. Proctor appeared in 83 games in both 2006 and 2007, 3rd and 5th most amongst all MLB pitchers respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Yankees <a href="/wiki/General_manager" title="General manager">general manager</a> <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cashman" title="Brian Cashman">Brian Cashman</a> says he told Proctor to give an "honest answer" to manager <a href="/wiki/Joe_Torre" title="Joe Torre">Joe Torre</a> when he asked if Proctor was ready to pitch again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
