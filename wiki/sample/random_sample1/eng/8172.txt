<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Scuba set</h1>

<p>A <b>scuba set</b> is any breathing apparatus that is carried entirely by an underwater diver and provides the diver with <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> at the ambient pressure. (Scuba is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anacronym" title="Anacronym">anacronym</a> for <b>self-contained underwater breathing apparatus</b>.) Although strictly speaking the scuba set is only the diving equipment which is required for providing breathing gas to the diver, general usage includes the harness by which it is carried, and those accessories which are integral parts of the harness and breathing apparatus assembly, such as a jacket or wing style buoyancy compensator and instruments mounted in a combined housing with the pressure gauge, and in the looser sense it has been used to refer to any <a href="/wiki/Diving_equipment" title="Diving equipment">diving equipment</a> used by the scuba diver, though this would more commonly and accurately be termed scuba equipment. Scuba is overwhelmingly the most common underwater breathing system used by recreational divers and is also used in <a href="/wiki/Professional_diving" title="Professional diving">professional diving</a> when it provides advantages, usually of mobility and range, over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_supplied_diving" title="Surface supplied diving">surface supplied diving</a> systems, and is allowed by the relevant code of practice.</p>
<p>Two basic configurations of scuba are in general use:</p>
<ul>
<li>Open-circuit-demand scuba expels exhaled air to the environment, and requires each breath be delivered to the diver on demand by a diving regulator, which reduces the pressure from the storage cylinder and supplies it through the demand valve when the diver reduces the pressure in the demand valve during inhalation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">Rebreather</a> scuba recycles the exhaled gas, removes carbon dioxide, and compensates for the used <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> before the diver is supplied with gas from the breathing circuit. The amount of gas lost from the circuit during each breathing cycle depends on the design of the rebreather and depth change during the breathing cycle. Gas in the breathing circuit is at ambient pressure, and stored gas is provided through <a href="/wiki/Rebreather#Closed_circuit_rebreather" title="Rebreather">regulators</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rebreather#Semi-closed_circuit_rebreathers" title="Rebreather">injectors</a>, depending on design.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>SCUBA</i> was coined in 1952 by <a href="/wiki/Major_(United_States)" title="Major (United States)">Major</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_J._Lambertsen" title="Christian J. Lambertsen">Christian Lambertsen</a> who served in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Medical_Corps" title="U.S. Army Medical Corps">U.S. Army Medical Corps</a> from 1944 to 1946 as a physician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vann2004_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-vann2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Lambertsen first called the closed circuit <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> apparatus he had invented "Laru", an (<a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lambertsen_Amphibious_Respiratory_Unit" title="Lambertsen Amphibious Respiratory Unit">Lambertsen Amphibious Respiratory Unit</a>) but, in 1952, rejected the term "Laru" for "SCUBA" ("Self-Contained Underwater Breathing Apparatus").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambertson_obituary_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambertson_obituary-2">[2]</a></sup> Lambertsen's invention, for which he held several patents registered from 1940 to 1989, was a <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> and is different from the open circuit <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">diving regulator</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a> assemblies also commonly referred to as scuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Open circuit demand scuba is a 1943 invention by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">Frenchmen</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Gagnan" title="Émile Gagnan">Émile Gagnan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques-Yves_Cousteau" title="Jacques-Yves Cousteau">Jacques-Yves Cousteau</a>, but in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> Lambertsen's acronym has become common usage and the name <i><a href="/wiki/Aqua-lung" title="Aqua-lung">Aqua-Lung</a></i>, (often spelled "aqualung"), coined by Cousteau for use in <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking countries</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aqua-lung_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aqua-lung-4">[4]</a></sup> has fallen into secondary use. As with <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, the acronym <i>scuba</i> has become so familiar that it is generally not capitalized and is treated as an ordinary noun. For example, it has been translated into the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a> as <i>sgwba</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">Scuba diving</a></div>
<p>A diver uses a self-contained underwater breathing apparatus (scuba) to breathe <a href="/wiki/Underwater" title="Underwater">underwater</a>. Scuba provides the diver with the advantages of mobility and horizontal range far beyond the reach of an umbilical hose attached to surface-supplied diving equipment (SSDE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_Navy_Diving_Manual_2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_Navy_Diving_Manual_2006-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike other modes of diving, which rely either on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-diving" title="Free-diving">breath-hold</a> or on <a href="/wiki/Surface-supplied_diving" title="Surface-supplied diving">breathing supplied under pressure from the surface</a>, scuba divers carry their own source of <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a>, usually <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brubakk_2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brubakk_2003-6">[6]</a></sup> allowing them greater freedom of movement than with an <a href="/wiki/Air_line" title="Air line">air line</a> or <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable#Diver" title="Umbilical cable">diver's umbilical</a> and longer underwater endurance than breath-hold. Scuba diving may be done <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diving" title="Recreational diving">recreationally</a> or <a href="/wiki/Professional_diving" title="Professional diving">professionally</a> in a number of applications, including scientific, military and public safety roles, but most commercial diving uses surface supplied diving equipment for main gas supply when this is practicable. Surface supplied divers may be required to carry scuba as an emergency breathing gas supply to get them to safety in the event of a failure of surface gas supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_Navy_Diving_Manual_2006_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-US_Navy_Diving_Manual_2006-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_4th_Ed_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_4th_Ed-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are divers who work, full or part-time, in the recreational diving community as instructors, assistant instructors, divemasters and dive guides. In some jurisdictions the professional nature, with particular reference to responsibility for health and safety of the clients, of recreational diver instruction, dive leadership for reward and dive guiding is recognised and regulated by national legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other specialist areas of scuba diving include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_diving" title="Military diving">military diving</a>, with a long history of military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frogmen" title="Frogmen">frogmen</a> in various roles. Their roles include direct combat, infiltration behind enemy lines, placing mines or using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_torpedo" title="Manned torpedo">manned torpedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bomb_disposal" title="Bomb disposal">bomb disposal</a> or engineering operations. In civilian operations, many police forces operate <a href="/wiki/Police_diving" title="Police diving">police diving</a> teams to perform "search and recovery" or "search and rescue" operations and to assist with the detection of crime which may involve bodies of water. In some cases <a href="/wiki/Diver_rescue" title="Diver rescue">diver rescue</a> teams may also be part of a <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire department</a>, paramedical service or <a href="/wiki/Lifeguard" title="Lifeguard">lifeguard</a> unit, and may be classed as public service diving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also professional divers involved with underwater environment, such as <a href="/wiki/Underwater_photography" title="Underwater photography">underwater photographers</a> or underwater videographers, who document the underwater world, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_diving" title="Scientific diving">scientific diving</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biology</a>, geology, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Underwater_archaeology" title="Underwater archaeology">underwater archaeology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_4th_Ed_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_4th_Ed-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The choice between scuba and surface supplied diving equipment is based on both legal and logistical constraints. Where the diver requires mobility and a large range of movement, scuba is usually the choice if safety and legal constraints allow. Higher risk work, particularly in commercial diving, may be restricted to surface supplied equipment by legislation and codes of practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Diving_at_Work_Regulations_1997-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_Diving_Regulations_2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA_Diving_Regulations_2009-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_scuba_for_diving">Alternatives to scuba for diving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alternatives to scuba for diving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are alternative methods that a person can use to survive and function while underwater, currently including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-diving" title="Free-diving">free-diving</a> - swimming underwater on a single breath of air.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snorkeling" title="Snorkeling">snorkeling</a> - a form of free-diving where the diver's mouth and nose can remain underwater when breathing, because the diver is able to breathe at the surface through a short tube known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snorkel_(swimming)" title="Snorkel (swimming)">snorkel</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Surface-supplied_diving" title="Surface-supplied diving">surface-supplied diving</a> - originally used in <a href="/wiki/Professional_diving" title="Professional diving">professional diving</a> for long or deep dives where an umbilical line connects the diver with the surface providing <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a>, and sometimes warm water to heat the <a href="/wiki/Diving_suit" title="Diving suit">diving suit</a>, and usually nowadays voice communications. Some tourist resorts now offer a surface-supplied diving arrangement, <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarked</a> as <a href="/wiki/Snuba" title="Snuba">Snuba</a>, as an introduction to diving for the inexperienced. Using the same type of equipment as scuba diving, the diver breathes from compressed air cylinders, which float on a free floating raft at the surface, allowing the diver only 20–30 feet (6–9 m) of depth to travel.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_diving_suit" title="Atmospheric diving suit">Atmospheric diving suit</a> - an armored suit which protects the diver from the surrounding water pressure.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lethbridge_diving_machine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="270" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Lethbridge_diving_machine.jpg/220px-Lethbridge_diving_machine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/Lethbridge_diving_machine.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lethbridge_diving_machine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/John_Lethbridge" title="John Lethbridge">John Lethbridge</a>'s diving dress, the first enclosed diving suit, built in the 1710s.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A scuba set is characterized by full independence from the surface during use, by providing <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> carried by the diver. Early attempts to reach this autonomy from the surface were made in the 18th century by the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">Englishman</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Lethbridge" title="John Lethbridge">John Lethbridge</a>, who invented and successfully built his own underwater diving machine in 1715.</p>
<p>An early diving dress using a compressed air reservoir was designed and built in 1771 by <i>Sieur</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Fréminet from <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. He conceived an autonomous breathing machine equipped with a reservoir, dragged behind the diver or mounted on his back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scaphandre_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-scaphandre-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrier_2008_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perrier_2008-12">[12]</a></sup> Fréminet called his invention <i>machine hydrostatergatique</i> and used it successfully for more than ten years in the harbors of <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brest,_France" title="Brest, France">Brest</a>, as stated in the explanatory text of a 1784 painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silent_World_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silent_World-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freminet_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freminet-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Frenchman Paul Lemaire d'Augerville built and used autonomous <a href="/wiki/Diving_equipment" title="Diving equipment">diving equipment</a> in 1824,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_2008-15">[15]</a></sup> as did the British William H. James in 1825. James' helmet was made of "thin copper or sole of leather" with a plate window, and the air was supplied from an iron reservoir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis1955-563_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis1955-563-16">[16]</a></sup> A similar system was used in 1831 by the American Charles Condert, who died in 1832 while testing his invention in the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a> at only 20 feet (6 m) deep.</p>
<p>The oldest known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_rebreather" title="Oxygen rebreather">oxygen rebreather</a> was patented on June 17, 1808 by <i>Sieur</i> Touboulic from <a href="/wiki/Brest,_France" title="Brest, France">Brest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanic" title="Mechanic">mechanic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s Imperial Navy, but there is no evidence of any prototype having been manufactured. This early rebreather design worked with an oxygen reservoir, the oxygen being delivered progressively by the diver himself and circulating in a closed circuit through a <a href="/wiki/Sponge_(material)" title="Sponge (material)">sponge</a> soaked in <a href="/wiki/Limewater" title="Limewater">limewater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plongeesout.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-plongeesout.com-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plongeesout_2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-plongeesout_2-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>After having travelled to England and discovered William James' invention, the French <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> Manuel Théodore Guillaumet, from <a href="/wiki/Argentan" title="Argentan">Argentan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>), patented in 1838 the oldest known regulator mechanism. Guillaumet's invention was <a href="/wiki/Surface-supplied_diving" title="Surface-supplied diving">air-supplied from the surface</a> and was never <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass-produced</a> due to problems with safety. The oldest practical rebreather relates to the 1849 patent from the Frenchman Pierre Aimable De Saint Simon Sicard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siccard_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siccard-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_successful_scuba_equipment">First successful scuba equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First successful scuba equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>None of those inventions solved the problem of high pressure when compressed air must be supplied to the diver (as in modern regulators); they were mostly based on a <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Constant_flow" title="Diving regulator">constant-flow</a> supply of the air. The compression and storage technology was not advanced enough to allow compressed air to be stored in containers at sufficiently high pressures to allow useful dive times.</p>
<p>By the turn of the twentieth century, two basic templates for a scuba had emerged; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-circuit_scuba" title="Open-circuit scuba">open-circuit scuba</a> where the diver's exhaled gas is vented directly into the water, and <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">closed-circuit scuba</a> where the diver's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is filtered from unused oxygen, which is then recirculated.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-circuit_scuba">Open-circuit scuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Open-circuit scuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dykeri,_fig_6,_Nordisk_familjebok.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1523" data-file-width="1075" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dykeri%2C_fig_6%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/200px-Dykeri%2C_fig_6%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dykeri%2C_fig_6%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/300px-Dykeri%2C_fig_6%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dykeri%2C_fig_6%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/400px-Dykeri%2C_fig_6%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dykeri,_fig_6,_Nordisk_familjebok.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Rouquayrol-Denayrouze apparatus was the first regulator to be <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass-produced</a> (from 1865 to 1965). In this picture the air reservoir presents its surface-supplied configuration.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first important step for the development of scuba technology was the invention of the demand regulator. In 1864, the French engineers <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Denayrouze" title="Auguste Denayrouze">Auguste Denayrouze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Rouquayrol" title="Benoît Rouquayrol">Benoît Rouquayrol</a> designed and patented their "Rouquayrol-Denayrouze diving suit" after adapting a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_regulator" title="Pressure regulator">pressure regulator</a> and developing it for underwater use. This would be the first diving suit that could supply air to the diver on demand by adjusting the flow of air from the tank to meet the diver’s breathing and pressure requirements. The system still had to use surface supply, as the cylinders of the 1860s would not have been able to withstand the necessary high pressures.</p>
<p>The first open-circuit scuba system was devised in 1925 by <a href="/wiki/Yves_Le_Prieur" title="Yves Le Prieur">Yves Le Prieur</a> in France. Inspired by the simple apparatus of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Fernez" title="Maurice Fernez">Maurice Fernez</a> and the freedom it allowed the diver, he conceived an idea to make it free of the tube to the surface pump by using Michelin cylinders as the air supply, containing three litres of air compressed to 150 kilograms per square centimetre (2,100 psi; 150 bar). The "Fernez-Le Prieur" diving apparatus was demonstrated at the swimming pool of Tourelles in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1926. The unit consisted of a cylinder of compressed air carried on the back of the diver, connected to a pressure regulator designed by Le Prieur adjusted manually by the diver, with two gauges, one for tank pressure and one for output (supply) pressure. Air was supplied continually to the mouthpiece and ejected through a short exhaust pipe fitted with a valve as in the Fernez design,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Le_Prieur_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Le_Prieur-20">[20]</a></sup> however, the lack of a demand regulator and the consequent low endurance of the apparatus limited the practical use of LePrieur’s device.</p>
<p>Le Prieur's design was the first autonomous breathing device used by the first scuba diving clubs in history - <i>Racleurs de fond</i> founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glenn_Orr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glenn Orr (page does not exist)">Glenn Orr</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 1933, and <i>Club des sous-l'eau</i> founded by Le Prieur himself in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zurcher_2002_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zurcher_2002-21">[21]</a></sup> Fernez had previously invented the <a href="/wiki/Noseclip" title="Noseclip">noseclip</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mouthpiece_(scuba)" title="Mouthpiece (scuba)">mouthpiece</a> (equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Check_valve" title="Check valve">one-way valve</a> for exhalation) and diving <a href="/wiki/Goggles" title="Goggles">goggles</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yves_le_Prieur" title="Yves le Prieur">Yves le Prieur</a> just joined to those three Fernez elements a hand-controlled regulator and a compressed-air cylinder. Fernez's goggles didn't allow a dive deeper than ten metres due to "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mask_squeeze" title="Mask squeeze">mask squeeze</a>", so, in 1933, Le Prieur replaced all the Fernez equipment (goggles, noseclip and valve) by a <a href="/wiki/Full_face_diving_mask" title="Full face diving mask">full face mask</a>, directly supplied with constant flow air from the cylinder.</p>
<p>In 1942, during the German occupation of France, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques-Yves_Cousteau" title="Jacques-Yves Cousteau">Jacques-Yves Cousteau</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Gagnan" title="Émile Gagnan">Émile Gagnan</a> designed the first successful and safe open-circuit scuba, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqua-Lung" title="Aqua-Lung">Aqua-Lung</a>. Their system combined an improved demand regulator with high-pressure air tanks. <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Gagnan" title="Émile Gagnan">Émile Gagnan</a>, an engineer employed by the <a href="/wiki/Air_Liquide" title="Air Liquide">Air Liquide</a> company, miniaturized and adapted the regulator to use with <a href="/wiki/Gas_generator" title="Gas generator">gas generators</a>, in response to constant fuel shortage that was a consequence of German requisitioning. Gagnan's boss, Henri Melchior, knew that his son-in-law Jacques-Yves Cousteau was looking for an automatic demand regulator to increase the useful period of the underwater breathing apparatus invented by Commander le Prieur,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cousteau_1953_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cousteau_1953-22">[22]</a></sup> so he introduced Cousteau to Gagnan in December 1942. On Cousteau's initiative, the Gagnan's regulator was adapted to diving, and the new Cousteau-Gagnan patent was registered some weeks later in 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scaphandre_2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scaphandre_2-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1957, <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Admetlla_i_L%C3%A1zaro" title="Eduard Admetlla i Lázaro">Eduard Admetlla i Lázaro</a> used a modified version of the Aqua-Lung made by <a href="/wiki/Nemrod" title="Nemrod">Nemrod</a> and broke the world record by descending to a depth of 100 metres (330 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanguardia_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanguardia-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Closed-circuit_scuba">Closed-circuit scuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Closed-circuit scuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">Rebreather</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Henry_Albert_Fleuss.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="203" data-file-width="180" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/Henry_Albert_Fleuss.jpg" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Henry_Albert_Fleuss.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Henry_Fleuss" title="Henry Fleuss">Henry Fleuss</a> (1851-1932) improved the <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> technology.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The alternative concept, developed in roughly the same time frame was the closed-circuit scuba. The body consumes and metabolises only a small fraction of inhaled <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>—the situation is even more wasteful of oxygen when the <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> is <a href="/wiki/Boyle%27s_law" title="Boyle's law">compressed</a> as it is in scuba systems underwater. The <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> therefore recycles the exhaled oxygen, while constantly replenishing it from the supply so that the oxygen level does not get depleted. The apparatus also has to chemically remove the exhaled carbon dioxide, as a buildup of CO<sub>2</sub> levels would result in respiratory distress and <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>.</p>
<p>The first commercially practical closed-circuit scuba was designed and built by the diving engineer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fleuss" title="Henry Fleuss">Henry Fleuss</a> in 1878, while working for <a href="/wiki/Siebe_Gorman" title="Siebe Gorman">Siebe Gorman</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scubahalloffame_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-scubahalloffame-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis1955_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-davis1955-26">[26]</a></sup> His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_contained_breathing_apparatus" title="Self contained breathing apparatus">self contained breathing apparatus</a> consisted of a rubber mask connected to a breathing bag, with (estimated) 50-60% O<sub>2</sub> supplied from a copper tank and CO<sub>2</sub> scrubbed by rope yarn soaked in a solution of caustic potash; the system giving a duration of about three hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis1955_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-davis1955-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quick_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-quick-27">[27]</a></sup> Fleuss tested his device in 1879 by spending an hour submerged in a water tank, then one week later by diving to a depth of 5.5 metres (18 ft) in open water, upon which occasion he was slightly injured when his assistants abruptly pulled him to the surface.</p>
<p>His apparatus was first used under operational conditions in 1880 by the lead diver on the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Tunnel" title="Severn Tunnel">Severn Tunnel</a> construction project, who was able to travel 1,000 feet (300 m) in the darkness to close several submerged <a href="/wiki/Sluice" title="Sluice">sluice</a> doors in the tunnel; this had defeated the best efforts of <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">hard hat divers</a> due to the danger of their air supply hoses becoming fouled on submerged debris, and the strong water currents in the workings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis1955_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-davis1955-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fleuss continually improved his apparatus, adding a demand regulator and tanks capable of holding greater amounts of oxygen at higher pressure. Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Davis_(inventor)" title="Robert Davis (inventor)">Robert Davis</a>, head of <a href="/wiki/Siebe_Gorman" title="Siebe Gorman">Siebe Gorman</a>, perfected the oxygen rebreather in 1910<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis1955_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-davis1955-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rebreather_hx_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-rebreather_hx-28">[28]</a></sup> with his invention of the <a href="/wiki/Davis_Submerged_Escape_Apparatus" title="Davis Submerged Escape Apparatus">Davis Submerged Escape Apparatus</a>, the first rebreather to be made in quantity. While intended primarily as an emergency escape apparatus for <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> crews, it was soon also used for <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">diving</a>, being a handy shallow water diving apparatus with a thirty-minute endurance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rebreather_hx_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-rebreather_hx-28">[28]</a></sup> and as an <a href="/wiki/Self-contained_breathing_apparatus" title="Self-contained breathing apparatus">industrial breathing set</a>.</p>

</div>

<p>The rig comprised a rubber breathing/buoyancy bag containing a canister of <a href="/wiki/Barium_hydroxide" title="Barium hydroxide">barium hydroxide</a> to scrub exhaled <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> and, in a pocket at the lower end of the bag, a steel pressure cylinder holding approximately 56 litres (2.0 cu ft) of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> at a pressure of 120 bars (1,700 psi). The cylinder was equipped with a control valve and was connected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathing_bag" title="Breathing bag">breathing bag</a>. Opening the cylinder's valve admitted oxygen to the bag at ambient pressure. The rig also included an emergency buoyancy bag on the front of to help keep the wearer afloat. The DSEA was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> after further development by Davis in 1927.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemp_1990_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp_1990-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1912 the German firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr%C3%A4ger" title="Dräger">Dräger</a> developed their own version of standard diving dress with a self-contained gas supply from a rebreather with the circulation driven by an injector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draegerwerk1899_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Draegerwerk1899-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1930s and all through <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germans</a> developed and extensively used oxygen rebreathers to equip the first <a href="/wiki/Frogman" title="Frogman">frogmen</a>. The British used the <a href="/wiki/Davis_Submerged_Escape_Apparatus" title="Davis Submerged Escape Apparatus">Davis apparatus</a> for submarine escape, but they soon adapted it for their frogmen during World War II. Germans used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr%C3%A4ger" title="Dräger">Dräger</a> rebreathers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diving_Heritage_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diving_Heritage-31">[31]</a></sup> which were also originally designed as submarine escape sets and only adapted for use by frogmen during World War II.</p>
<p>The Italians developed similar rebreathers for the combat swimmers of the <a href="/wiki/Decima_Flottiglia_MAS" title="Decima Flottiglia MAS">Decima Flottiglia MAS</a>, especially the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pirelli_ARO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pirelli ARO (page does not exist)">Pirelli ARO</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pirelli_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pirelli-32">[32]</a></sup> In the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Major_(United_States)" title="Major (United States)">Major</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_J._Lambertsen" title="Christian J. Lambertsen">Christian J. Lambertsen</a> invented an underwater free-swimming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_rebreather" title="Oxygen rebreather">oxygen rebreather</a> in 1939, which was accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Strategic_Services" title="Office of Strategic Services">Office of Strategic Services</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shapiro_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1952 he patented a modification of his apparatus, this time named SCUBA. Although he coined the most common English word used for autonomous open circuit diving equipment, Lambertsen did not invent that equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patent_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-patent-34">[34]</a></sup> After World War II, military frogmen continued to use rebreathers since they do not make bubbles which would give away the presence of the divers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_WWII">Post WWII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post WWII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aqualung_(PSF).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2768" data-file-width="1615" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Aqualung_%28PSF%29.png/200px-Aqualung_%28PSF%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Aqualung_%28PSF%29.png/300px-Aqualung_%28PSF%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Aqualung_%28PSF%29.png/400px-Aqualung_%28PSF%29.png 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Air Liquide started selling the Cousteau-Gagnan regulator commercially as of 1946 under the name of <i>scaphandre Cousteau-Gagnan</i> or CG45 ("C" for Cousteau, "G" for Gagnan and 45 for the 1945 patent). The same year Air Liquide created a division called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Spirotechnique" title="La Spirotechnique">La Spirotechnique</a></i>, to develop and sell regulators and other diving equipment. To sell his regulator in English-speaking countries Cousteau registered the <a href="/wiki/Aqua-lung" title="Aqua-lung">Aqua-Lung</a> trademark, which was first licensed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Divers" title="U.S. Divers">U.S. Divers</a> company (the American division of Air Liquide) and later sold with La Spirotechnique and U.S. Divers to finally become the name of the company, Aqua-Lung/La Spirotechnique, currently located in <a href="/wiki/Carros" title="Carros">Carros</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">Nice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grima_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grima-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1948 the Cousteau-Gagnan patent was also licensed to <a href="/wiki/Siebe_Gorman" title="Siebe Gorman">Siebe Gorman</a> of England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadpole_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tadpole-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (August 2016)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> when Siebe Gorman was directed by Robert Henry Davis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugsley_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pugsley-37">[37]</a></sup> Siebe Gorman was allowed to sell in Commonwealth countries, but had difficulty in meeting the demand and the U.S. patent prevented others from making the product. This demand was eventually met by Ted Eldred of <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, who had been developing a rebreather called the Porpoise. When a demonstration resulted in a diver passing out, he began to develop the single-hose open-circuit scuba system, which separates the first and second stages by a low-pressure hose, and releases exhaled gas at the second stage. This avoided the Cousteau-Gagnan <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a>, which protected the twin-hose scuba regulator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byron_2014_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Byron_2014-38">[38]</a></sup> In the process, Eldred also improved performance of the regulator.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Eldred sold the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porpoise_(make_of_scuba_gear)" title="Porpoise (make of scuba gear)">Porpoise</a> Model CA single hose scuba early in 1952.</p>
<p>Early scuba sets were usually provided with a plain harness of shoulder straps and waist belt. The waist belt buckles were usually quick-release, and shoulder straps sometimes had adjustable or quick release buckles. Many harnesses did not have a backplate, and the cylinders rested directly against the diver's back. The harnesses of many diving rebreathers made by <a href="/wiki/Siebe_Gorman" title="Siebe Gorman">Siebe Gorman</a> included a large back-sheet of reinforced rubber.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Early scuba divers dived without any buoyancy aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silent_World_b_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silent_World_b-39">[39]</a></sup> In emergency they had to jettison their weights. In the 1960s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjustable_buoyancy_life_jacket" title="Adjustable buoyancy life jacket">adjustable buoyancy life jackets</a> (ABLJ) became available. One early make, since 1961, was <a href="/wiki/Fenzy" title="Fenzy">Fenzy</a>. The ABLJ is used for two purposes: to adjust the buoyancy of the diver to compensate for loss of buoyancy at depth, mainly due to compression of the <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a> <a href="/wiki/Wetsuit" title="Wetsuit">wetsuit</a>) and more importantly as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lifejacket" title="Lifejacket">lifejacket</a> that will hold an unconscious diver face-upwards at the surface, and that can be quickly inflated. It was put on before putting on the cylinder harness. The first versions were inflated with a small <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> cylinder, later with a small direct coupled air cylinder. An extra low-pressure feed from the regulator first-stage lets the lifejacket be controlled as a buoyancy aid. This invention in 1971 of the "direct system,"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ScubaPro" title="ScubaPro">ScubaPro</a>, resulted in what was called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stabilizer_jacket" title="Stabilizer jacket">stabilizer jacket</a> or stab jacket, and is now increasingly known as a buoyancy compensator (device), or simply "BCD".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scuba sets are of two types:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <b>open-circuit</b> scuba the diver inhales from the equipment and all the exhaled gas is exhausted to the surrounding water. This type of equipment is relatively simple, economical and reliable.</li>
<li>In <b>closed-circuit</b> or <b>semi-closed circuit</b>, also referred to as a <b><a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a></b>, the diver inhales from the set, and exhales back into the set, where the exhaled gas is processed to make it fit to breathe again.</li>
</ul>
<p>Both types of scuba set include a means of supplying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a>, nearly always from a high <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a>, and a harness to attach it to the diver. Most open-circuit scuba sets have a <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">demand regulator</a> to control the supply of breathing gas, and most rebreathers have a <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Constant_flow" title="Diving regulator">constant-flow injector</a>, or an electronically controlled injector to supply fresh gas, but also usually have an automatic diluent valve (ADV), which functions in the same way as a demand valve to maintain the loop volume during descent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_circuit">Open circuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Open circuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2952" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG/200px-Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG/300px-Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG/400px-Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diving_cilinder_schematic.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A diving cylinder with its various components</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Open circuit demand scuba exhausts exhaled air to the environment, and requires each breath to be delivered to the diver on demand by a diving regulator, which reduces the pressure from the storage cylinder and supplies it through the demand valve when the diver reduces the pressure in the demand valve slightly during inhalation.</p>
<p>The essential subsystems of an open circuit scuba set are;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinders</a>, with cylinder valves, which may be interconnected by a manifold,</li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">regulator</a> mechanism to control gas pressure,</li>
<li>a demand valve with mouthpiece, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-face_mask" title="Full-face mask">full-face mask</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">helmet</a>, with supply hose, to control flow and deliver gas to the diver.</li>
<li>an exhaust valve system to dispose of used gas,</li>
<li>A harness or other method to attach the set to the diver.</li>
</ul>
<p>Additional components which when present are considered part of the scuba set are;</p>
<ul>
<li>external reserve valves and their control rods or levers, (currently uncommon)</li>
<li>submersible pressure gauges, (almost ubiquitous) and</li>
<li>secondary (backup) demand valves (common).</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy_compensator_(diving)" title="Buoyancy compensator (diving)">buoyancy compensator</a> is generally assembled as an integrated part of the set, but is not technically part of the breathing apparatus.</p>
<p>The cylinder is usually worn on the back. "Twin sets" with two low capacity back-mounted cylinders connected by a high pressure manifold were more common in the 1960s than now for recreational diving, although larger capacity twin cylinders ("doubles") are commonly used by technical divers for increased dive duration and redundancy. At one time a firm called <a href="/wiki/Submarine_Products" title="Submarine Products">Submarine Products</a> sold a sport air scuba set with three manifolded back-mounted cylinders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Cave and wreck penetration divers sometimes carry cylinders <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side_mount_diving" title="Side mount diving">attached at their sides</a> instead, allowing them to swim through more confined spaces.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newspapers" title="Newspapers">Newspapers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> <a href="/wiki/News" title="News">news</a> often describe open circuit scuba wrongly as "oxygen" equipment.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Constant_flow_scuba">Constant flow scuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Constant flow scuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Constant_flow" title="Diving regulator">Diving regulator § Constant flow</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Constant_flow" title="Diving regulator">Constant flow scuba sets</a> do not have a demand regulator; the breathing gas flows at a constant rate, unless the diver switches it on and off by hand. They use more air than demand regulated scuba. There were attempts at designing and using these for diving and for industrial use before the Cousteau-type aqualung became commonly available circa 1950. Examples were <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Condert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles Condert (page does not exist)">Charles Condert</a> dress in the USA (as of 1831), "Ohgushi's Peerless Respirator" in Japan (a bite-controlled regulator, as of 1918), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yves_le_Prieur" title="Yves le Prieur">Commandant le Prieur</a>'s hand-controlled regulator in France (as of 1926); see <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_diving_technology" title="Timeline of diving technology">Timeline of diving technology</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_circuit_demand_scuba">Open circuit demand scuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Open circuit demand scuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">Diving regulator</a></div>
<p>This system consists of one or more <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinders</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> at high pressure, typically 200–300 bars (2,900–4,400 psi), connected to a <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">diving regulator</a>. The demand regulator supplies the diver with as much gas as needed at the ambient pressure.</p>
<p>This type of breathing set is sometimes called an <i>aqualung</i>. The word <i>Aqua-Lung</i>, which first appeared in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques-Yves_Cousteau" title="Jacques-Yves Cousteau">Cousteau</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gagnan" title="Gagnan">Gagnan</a> <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a>, currently owned by <a href="/wiki/Aqua_Lung/La_Spirotechnique" title="Aqua Lung/La Spirotechnique">Aqua Lung/La Spirotechnique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-justia_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-justia-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Twin-hose_demand_regulator">Twin-hose demand regulator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Twin-hose demand regulator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Twin-hose_open_circuit_demand_scuba_regulators" title="Diving regulator">Diving regulator § Twin-hose open circuit demand scuba regulators</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aqualung_old_type.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Aqualung_old_type.jpg/220px-Aqualung_old_type.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Aqualung_old_type.jpg/330px-Aqualung_old_type.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Aqualung_old_type.jpg/440px-Aqualung_old_type.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aqualung_old_type.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Classic twin-hose Cousteau-type aqualung</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is the first type of diving demand valve to come into general use, and the one that can be seen in classic 1960s television scuba adventures, such as <a href="/wiki/Sea_Hunt" title="Sea Hunt">Sea Hunt</a>. They were often use with manifolded twin cylinders.</p>
<p>All the stages of this type of regulator are in a large valve assembly mounted directly to the cylinder valve or manifold, behind the diver's neck. Two large bore corrugated rubber <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathing_tube_(in_breathing_apparatus)" title="Breathing tube (in breathing apparatus)">breathing hoses</a> connect the regulator with the mouthpiece, one for supply and one for exhaust. The exhaust hose is used to return the exhaled air to the regulator, to avoid pressure differences due to depth variation between the exhaust valve and final stage <a href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(mechanical_device)" title="Diaphragm (mechanical device)">diaphragm</a>, which would cause a free-flow of gas, or extra resistance to breathing, depending on the diver's orientation in the water. In modern single-hose sets this problem is avoided by moving the second-stage regulator to the diver's <a href="/wiki/Mouthpiece_(scuba)" title="Mouthpiece (scuba)">mouthpiece</a>. The twin-hose regulators came with a mouthpiece as standard, but a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-face_diving_mask" title="Full-face diving mask">full-face diving mask</a> was an option.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Single-hose_regulator">Single-hose regulator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Single-hose regulator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Single_hose_two-stage_open_circuit_demand_regulators" title="Diving regulator">Diving regulator § Single hose two-stage open circuit demand regulators</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aqua_lung.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="500" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Aqua_lung.jpg/220px-Aqua_lung.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Aqua_lung.jpg/330px-Aqua_lung.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Aqua_lung.jpg/440px-Aqua_lung.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aqua_lung.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A single-hose regulator with 2nd stage, gauges, BC attachment, and dry suit hose mounted on a cylinder</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most modern open-circuit scuba sets have a <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">diving regulator</a> consisting of a first-stage pressure-reducing valve connected to the <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a>'s output valve or manifold. This regulator reduces the pressure from the cylinder, which may be up to 300 bars (4,400 psi), to a lower pressure, generally between about 9 and 11 bar above the ambient pressure. A low-pressure hose links this with the second-stage regulator, or "demand valve", which is mounted on the mouthpiece. Exhalation occurs through a rubber one-way mushroom valve in the chamber of the demand valve, directly into the water quite close to the diver's mouth. Some early single hose scuba sets used full-face masks instead of a mouthpiece, such as those made by Desco <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desco_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Desco-41">[41]</a></sup> and Scott Aviation <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_Aviation_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_Aviation-42">[42]</a></sup> (who continue to make breathing units of this configuration for use by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firefighters" title="Firefighters">firefighters</a>).</p>
<p>Modern regulators typically feature high-pressure ports for pressure sensors of dive-computers and submersible pressure gauges, and additional low-pressure ports for hoses for inflation of dry suits and BC devices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_demand_valve_on_a_regulator">Secondary demand valve on a regulator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Secondary demand valve on a regulator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Secondary_demand_valve_.28Octopus.29" title="Diving regulator">Diving regulator § Secondary demand valve (Octopus)</a></div>

<p>Most recreational scuba sets have a secondary second-stage demand valve on a separate hose, a configuration called a "secondary", or "octopus" demand valve, "alternate air source", "safe secondary" or "safe-second". It is frequently yellow in color for high visibility, signaling that it is an emergency or backup device. It is often worn secured into a clip on the <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy_compensator_(diving)" title="Buoyancy compensator (diving)">buoyancy compensator</a> (BC) or a special friction plug attached in the diver's chest area, easily available to be grabbed by, or offered to, a second diver short of air. Other divers secure it while diving by sliding a loop of the hose into the shoulder strap cover of a jacket style BC, or suspend it under the chin on a break-away bungee loop known as a necklace. These methods also allow easy access and keep the secondary from dangling in the mud or snagging on the bottom, which is common when it is left to hang at the end of the hose. Some divers will store it in a BC pocket, but this reduces availability in an emergency. By providing a secondary demand valve the need to alternately breathe off the same mouthpiece when sharing air is eliminated. This reduces the stress on divers who are already in a stressful situation, and this in turn reduces air consumption during the rescue.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some diving instructors continue to teach single demand valve buddy-breathing as an obsolete but still useful technique; then they show the method that has superseded it, since availability of two second stages per diver is now assumed as standard in recreational scuba.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The original octopus idea was conceived by cave-diving pioneer <a href="/wiki/Sheck_Exley" title="Sheck Exley">Sheck Exley</a> as a way for cave divers to share air while swimming single-file in a narrow tunnel,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but has now become the standard in recreational diving.</p>
<p>Occasionally, the secondary second-stage is combined with the inflation and exhaust valve assembly of the buoyancy compensator device. This combination eliminates the need for a separate low pressure hose for the BC, though the low pressure hose connector for the combined use must have a larger bore than for standard BC inflation hoses, because it will need to deliver a higher flow rate if it is used for breathing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>No matter which configuration of secondary demand valve is used, some diver training agencies now recommend<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that a diver routinely offer another diver in trouble their "primary" demand valve, <i>i.e.</i>, the one in their mouth, then switch to their own secondary demand valve. The idea behind this technique is that the primary demand valve is known to be working, and the diver donating the air has more time to sort out his/her own equipment after temporarily losing ability to breathe. In many instances, panicked out-of-air divers have grabbed the primary regulators out of the mouths of other divers,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> so changing breathing regulators suddenly in an out-of-air emergency becomes necessary for the rescue diver, in any case. With integrated DV/BC inflator designs, the secondary demand valve is at the end of the even shorter BC inflation hose than is the case with the conventional octopus demand valve hose, and the donor must retain access to it for buoyancy control, so deliberate use of the primary regulator and hose to help another diver becomes even more appropriate, and almost essential, with this configuration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryogenic">Cryogenic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cryogenic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There have been designs for a cryogenic open-circuit scuba which has liquid-air tanks instead of cylinders. Underwater cinematographer Jordan Klein, Sr. of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> co-designed such a scuba in 1967, called "Mako", and made at least a <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">prototype</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Russian <i>Kriolang</i> (from Greek <i>cryo-</i> (= "frost" taken to mean "cold") + English "lung") was copied from Jordan Klein's "Mako" cryogenic open-circuit scuba. <a class="external text" href="http://www.therebreathersite.nl/03_Historical/cryo_pjotrr.htm" rel="nofollow">Janwillem Bech's rebreather site</a> shows pictures of a Kriolang that was made in 1974. Its diving duration is likely several hours. It would have to be filled immediately before use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebreathers">Rebreathers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Rebreathers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inspiration_front.JPg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="500" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Inspiration_front.JPg/220px-Inspiration_front.JPg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Inspiration_front.JPg/330px-Inspiration_front.JPg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Inspiration_front.JPg/440px-Inspiration_front.JPg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inspiration_front.JPg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">Rebreather</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> recirculates the breathing gas already used by the diver after replacing oxygen used by the diver and removing the carbon dioxide metabolic product. Rebreather diving is used by recreational, military and scientific divers where it can have advantages over open circuit scuba. Since 80% or more of the oxygen remains in normal exhaled gas, and is thus wasted, rebreathers use gas very economically, making longer dives possible and special mixes cheaper to use at the cost of more complicated technology and more possible failure points. More stringent and specific training and greater experience is required to compensate for the higher risk involved. The rebreather's economic use of gas, typically 1.6 litres (0.06 cu ft) of oxygen per minute, allows dives of much longer duration for an equivalent gas supply than is possible with open circuit equipment where gas consumption may be ten times higher.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are two main variants of rebreather — semi-closed circuit rebreathers, and fully closed circuit rebreathers, which include the subvariant of oxygen rebreathers. Oxygen rebreathers have a <a href="/wiki/Maximum_operating_depth" title="Maximum operating depth">maximum safe operating depth</a> of around 6 metres (20 ft), but several types of fully closed circuit rebreathers, when using a <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>-based diluent, can be used deeper than 100 metres (330 ft). The main limiting factors on rebreathers are the duration of the carbon dioxide scrubber, which is generally at least 3 hours, increased work of breathing at depth, reliability of gas mixture control, and the requirement to be able to safely bail out at any point of the dive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rebreathers are generally used for scuba applications, but are also occasionally used for bailout systems for surface supplied diving.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The possible endurance of a rebreather dive is longer than an open-circuit dive, for similar weight and bulk of the set, if the set is bigger than the practical lower limit for rebreather size,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shreeves2006_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-shreeves2006-43">[43]</a></sup> and a rebreather can be more economical when used with expensive gas mixes such as <a href="/wiki/Heliox" title="Heliox">heliox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)" title="Trimix (breathing gas)">trimix</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shreeves2006_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-shreeves2006-43">[43]</a></sup> but this may require a lot of diving before the break-even point is reached, due to the high initial and running costs of most rebreathers, and this point will be reached sooner for deep dives where the gas saving is more pronounced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breathing_gases_for_scuba">Breathing gases for scuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Breathing gases for scuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">Breathing gas</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">Air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">Nitrox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)" title="Trimix (breathing gas)">Trimix (breathing gas)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heliox" title="Heliox">Heliox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Decompression_(diving)" title="Decompression (diving)">Decompression (diving)</a></div>
<p>Until <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">nitrox</a> was widely accepted in the late 1990s, almost all recreational scuba used simple compressed and filtered air. Other gas mixtures currently used in scuba are intended to reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> and the severity of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis" title="Nitrogen narcosis">nitrogen narcosis</a>.</p>
<p>Some divers use <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">nitrox</a>, which usually has a higher percentage of oxygen than air, often 32% or 36% in EAN32 and EAN36, respectively. This lets them stay underwater longer for the same decompression requirement as for air, because less nitrogen is absorbed into the body's tissues. The drawback to the higher oxygen content is that at higher than normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_pressures" title="Partial pressures">partial pressures</a>, oxygen becomes <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity" title="Oxygen toxicity">toxic</a>, so scuba divers generally limit their exposure to oxygen partial pressures of less than 1.6 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lang2001_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-lang2001-44">[44]</a></sup> by limiting the maximum operating depth for the mixture.</p>
<p>Open-circuit scuba sets may supply various <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gases</a>, but rarely pure oxygen, except during shallow <a href="/wiki/Decompression_(diving)" title="Decompression (diving)">decompression</a> stops in <a href="/wiki/Technical_diving" title="Technical diving">technical diving</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diving_cylinders">Diving cylinders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Diving cylinders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">Diving cylinder</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">Gas cylinders</a> used for scuba diving come in various sizes and materials and are typically designated by material — usually <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, and size. In the U.S. the size is designated by their <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder#Nominal_volume_of_gas_stored" title="Diving cylinder">nominal capacity</a>, the volume of the gas they contain when expanded to normal atmospheric pressure. Common sizes include 80, 100, 120 cubic feet, etc., with the most common being the "Aluminum 80". In most of the rest of the world the size is given as the actual internal volume of the cylinder, sometimes referred to as water capacity, as that is how it is measured and marked (WC) on the cylinder (10 liter, 12 liter, etc.).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Cylinder working pressure will vary according to the standard of manufacture, generally ranging from 200 bar (2,900 psi) up to 300 bar (4,400 psi).</p>
<p>An aluminium cylinder is thicker and bulkier than a steel cylinder of the same capacity and working pressure, as suitable aluminium alloys have lower tensile strength than steel, and is more buoyant although actually heavier out of the water, which means the diver would need to carry more ballast weight. Steel is also more often used for high pressure cylinders, which carry more air for the same internal volume.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The common <a href="/wiki/Gas_blending#Blending_nitrox" title="Gas blending">method of blending nitrox by partial pressure</a> requires that the cylinder is in "oxygen service", which means that the cylinder and cylinder valve have had any non-oxygen-compatible components replaced and any contamination by combustible materials removed by cleaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RRR6310_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-RRR6310-45">[45]</a></sup> Diving cylinders are sometimes colloquially called "tanks", "bottles" or "flasks" although the proper technical term for them is "cylinder".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Another popular configuration is the <a href="/wiki/Backplate_and_wing" title="Backplate and wing">backplate and wing</a> arrangement, which uses a back inflation buoyancy compensator bladder sandwiched between a rigid backplate and the main gas cylinder or cylinders. Additional cylinders for <a href="/wiki/Decompression_(diving)" title="Decompression (diving)">decompression</a> can be sling mounted at the diver's sides.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p><a href="/wiki/Sidemount_diving" title="Sidemount diving">Side-mount</a> harnesses support the cylinders by clipping them to D-rings at chest and hip on either or both sides, and the cylinders hang roughly parallel to the diver's torso when underwater. The harness usually includes a buoyancy compensator bladder. It is possible for a skilled diver to carry up to 3 cylinders on each side with this system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An unusual configuration which does not appear to have become popular is the integrated harness and storage container. These units comprise a bag which contains the buoyancy bladder and the cylinder, with a harness and regulator components which are stored in the bag and unfolded to the working position when the bag is unzipped. Some military rebreathers such as the <a href="/wiki/Interspiro_DCSC" title="Interspiro DCSC">Interspiro DCSC</a> also store the breathing hoses inside the housing when not in use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is also possible to use a plain backpack harness to support the set, either with a horse-collar buoyancy compensator, or without any buoyancy compensator. This was the standard arrangement before the introduction of the buoyancy compensator, and is still used by some recreational and professional divers when it suits the diving operation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessories">Accessories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Accessories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most scuba sets, a <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy_compensator_(diving)" title="Buoyancy compensator (diving)">buoyancy compensator</a> (BC) or buoyancy control device (BCD), such as a back-mounted wing or stabilizer jacket (also known as a "stab jacket"), is built into the harness. Although strictly speaking this is not a part of the breathing apparatus, it is usually connected to the diver's air supply, to provide easy inflation of the device. This can usually also be done manually via a mouthpiece, in order to save air while on the surface, or in case of a malfunction of the pressurized inflation system. The BCD inflates with air from the low pressure inflator hose to increase the volume of the scuba equipment and cause the diver gain buoyancy. Another button opens a valve to deflate the BCD and decrease the volume of the equipment and causes the diver to lose buoyancy. Some BCDs allow for integrated weight, meaning that the BCD has special pockets for the weights that can be dumped easily in case of an emergency. The function of the BCD, while underwater, is to keep the diver neutrally buoyant, <i>i.e.</i>, neither floating up or sinking. The BCD is used to compensate for the compression of a wet suit, and to compensate for the decrease of the diver's mass as the air from the cylinder is breathed away.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diving_weighting_system" title="Diving weighting system">Diving weighting systems</a> increase the average density of the scuba diver and equipment to compensate for the buoyancy of diving equipment, particularly the diving suit, allowing the diver to fully submerge with ease by obtaining neutral or slightly negative buoyancy. Weighting systems originally consisted of solid lead blocks attached to a belt around the diver's waist, but some diving weighting systems are incorporated into the BCD or harness. These systems may use small nylon bags of lead shot or small weights which are distributed around the BCD, allowing a diver to gain a better overall weight distribution leading to a more horizontal <a href="/wiki/Diver_trim" title="Diver trim">trim</a> in the water. Tank weights can be attached to the cylinder or threaded on the cambands holding the cylinder into the BCD.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many closed circuit rebreathers use advanced <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> to monitor and regulate the composition of the breathing gas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rebreather divers and some open circuit scuba divers carry extra <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinders</a> for bailout in case the main breathing gas supply is used up or malfunctions. If the bailout cylinder is small, they may be called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pony_cylinders" title="Pony cylinders">pony cylinders</a>". They have their own <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator" title="Diving regulator">demand regulators</a> and mouthpieces, and are technically distinct extra scuba sets. In <a href="/wiki/Technical_diving" title="Technical diving">technical diving</a>, the diver may carry different equipment for different phases of the dive. Some <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> mixes, such as trimix, may only be used at depth, and others, such as pure <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, may only be used during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decompression_stop" title="Decompression stop">decompression stops</a> in shallow water. The heaviest cylinders are generally carried on the back supported by a <a href="/wiki/Backplate_and_wing" title="Backplate and wing">backplate</a> while others are side slung from strong points on the harness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When the diver carries many diving cylinders, especially those made of <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, lack of <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> can be a problem. High-capacity BCs may be needed to allow the diver to effectively control buoyancy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An excess of tubes and connections passing through the water tend to decrease swimming performance by causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic" title="Hydrodynamic">hydrodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_endurance_of_a_scuba_set">Gas endurance of a scuba set</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Gas endurance of a scuba set">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scuba_gas_planning" title="Scuba gas planning">Scuba gas planning</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder#Gas_calculations" title="Diving cylinder">Diving cylinder § Gas calculations</a></div>
<p>Gas endurance of a scuba set is the time that the gas supply will last during a dive. This is influenced by the type of scuba set and the circumstances in which it is used.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_circuit_2">Open circuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Open circuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The gas endurance of open circuit demand scuba depends on factors such as the capacity (volume of gas) in the <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a>, the depth of the dive and the breathing rate of the diver, which is dependent on exertion, fitness, physical size of the diver, and experience among other factors. New divers frequently consume all the air in a standard "aluminum 80" cylinder in 30 minutes or less on a typical dive, while experienced divers frequently dive for 60 to 70 minutes at the same average depth, using the same capacity cylinder, as they have learned more efficient diving techniques.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An open circuit diver whose breathing rate at the surface (atmospheric pressure) is 15 litres per minute will consume 3 x 15 = 45 litres of gas per minute at 20 metres. [(20 m/10 m per bar) + 1 bar atmospheric pressure] × 15 L/min = 45 L/min). If an 11-litre cylinder filled to 200 bar is used until there is a reserve of 17% there is (83% × 200 × 11) = 1826 litres. At 45 L/min the dive at depth will be a maximum of 40.5 minutes (1826/45). These depths and times are typical of experienced sport divers leisurely exploring a <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a> using 200 bar aluminum cylinders rented from a commercial sport diving operation in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> island or coastal resorts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-closed_rebreather">Semi-closed rebreather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Semi-closed rebreather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A semi-closed circuit rebreather may have an endurance of about 3 to 10 times that of the equivalent open circuit dive, and is less affected by depth; gas is recycled but fresh gas must be constantly injected to replace at least the oxygen used, and any excess gas from this must be vented. Although it uses gas more economically, the weight of the rebreathing equipment means the diver carries smaller cylinders. Still, most semi-closed systems allow at least twice the duration of open circuit systems (around 2 hours) and are often limited by scrubber endurance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closed_circuit_rebreathers">Closed circuit rebreathers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scuba_set&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Closed circuit rebreathers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An oxygen rebreather diver or a fully closed circuit rebreather diver consumes about 1 litre of oxygen per minute. Except during ascent or descent, the fully closed circuit rebreather that is operating correctly uses no or very little diluent. So, a diver with a 3-litre oxygen cylinder filled to 200 bar who leaves 25% in reserve will be able to do a 450-minute = 7.5 hour dive (3 L × 200 bar × 0.75 / 1). The life of the <a href="/wiki/Soda_lime" title="Soda lime">soda lime</a> scrubber is likely to be less than this and so will be the limiting factor of the dive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In practice, dive times for rebreathers are more often influenced by other factors, such as <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">water temperature</a> and the need for safe ascent (see <a href="/wiki/Decompression_(diving)" title="Decompression (diving)">Decompression (diving)</a>), and this is generally also true for large capacity open circuit sets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
