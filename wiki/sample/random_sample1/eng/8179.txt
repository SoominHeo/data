<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Scram</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <b>scram</b> or <b>SCRAM</b> is an emergency shutdown of a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a>, though the term has been extended to cover shutdowns of other complex operations, such as <a href="/wiki/Server_farm" title="Server farm">server farms</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="No Source For Usage In Server Farms Found (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In commercial reactor operations, this type of shutdown is often referred to as a "SCRAM" at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_water_reactors" title="Boiling water reactors">boiling water reactors</a> (BWR), and as a "reactor <i>trip</i>" at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactors" title="Pressurized water reactors">pressurized water reactors</a> (PWR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In many cases, a SCRAM is part of the routine shutdown procedure as well.</p>
<p>The term is usually cited as being an acronym for <b>safety control rod axe man</b>, which was supposedly coined by <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> when the world's first nuclear reactor was built under the spectator seating at the University of Chicago's Stagg Field, but <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">NRC</a> Historian Tom Wellock calls the axe-man story "a bunch of baloney". It could also stand for "<a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">Safety</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_Rods" title="Control Rods">Control Rods</a> <a href="/wiki/Activation" title="Activation">Activation</a> <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering)" title="Mechanism (engineering)">Mechanism</a>" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_Rods" title="Control Rods">Control Rods</a> <a href="/wiki/Actuator" title="Actuator">Actuator</a> <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering)" title="Mechanism (engineering)">Mechanism</a>", both of which are probably <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronyms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> "Scram" is American English slang for leaving quickly and urgently, which has been cited as the original basis for the use of scram in the technical context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc-gateway.gov_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc-gateway.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In any reactor, a SCRAM is achieved by inserting large amounts of negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactivity_(nuclear)" title="Reactivity (nuclear)">reactivity</a> mass(es) into the midst of the fissile material.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_water_reactors" title="Light water reactors">light water reactors</a>, this is achieved by inserting neutron-absorbing <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> into the core, although the mechanism by which rods are inserted depends on the type of reactor. In PWRs, the control rods are held above a reactor's core by electric motors against both their own weight and a powerful spring. Any cessation of the electric current releases the rods. Another design uses electromagnets to hold the rods suspended, with any cut to the electric current resulting in an immediate and automatic control rod insertion. A SCRAM is designed to release the control rods from those motors and allows their weight and the spring to drive them into the reactor core, rapidly halting the nuclear reaction by absorbing liberated neutrons.</p>
<p>In BWRs, the control rods are inserted up from underneath the reactor vessel. In this case a hydraulic control unit with a pressurized storage tank provides the force to rapidly insert the control rods upon any interruption of the electric current. In both the PWR and the BWR there are secondary systems (and often even tertiary systems) that will insert control rods in the event that primary rapid insertion does not promptly and fully actuate.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2533" data-file-width="3799" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg/220px-NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg/330px-NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg/440px-NS_Savannah_SCRAM_MD1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Liquid neutron absorbers are also used in rapid shutdown systems for light water reactors. Following a SCRAM, if the reactor (or section(s) thereof) are not below the shutdown margin (that is, they could return to a critical state due to insertion of positive reactivity from cooling, poison decay, or other uncontrolled conditions), the operators can inject solutions containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_poison" title="Nuclear poison">neutron poisons</a> directly into the reactor coolant. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_poison" title="Nuclear poison">Neutron poisons</a> are <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">water-based solutions</a> that contain chemicals that absorb <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Borax" title="Borax">common household borax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">sodium polyborate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gadolinium_nitrate" title="Gadolinium nitrate">gadolinium nitrate</a>, causing a decrease in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effective_neutron_multiplication_factor" title="Effective neutron multiplication factor">neutron multiplication</a>, and thus shutting down the reactor without use of the control rods. In the PWR, these neutron absorbing solutions are stored in pressurized tanks (called accumulators) that are attached to the primary coolant system via valves; a varying level of neutron absorbent is kept within the primary coolant at all times, and is increased using the accumulators in the event of a failure of all of the control rods to insert, which will promptly bring the reactor below the shutdown margin. In the BWR, soluble neutron absorbers are found within the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_water_reactor_safety_systems#Standby_liquid_control_system_.28SLCS.29" title="Boiling water reactor safety systems">Standby Liquid Control System (SLCS)</a>, which uses redundant battery-operated injection pumps, or, in the latest models, high pressure nitrogen gas to inject the neutron absorber solution into the reactor vessel against any pressure within. Because they may delay the restart of a reactor, these systems are only used to shut down the reactor if control rod insertion fails. This concern is especially significant in a BWR, where injection of liquid boron would cause <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of solid boron compounds on fuel cladding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which would prevent the reactor from restarting until the boron deposits were removed.</p>
<p>In most reactor designs, the routine shutdown procedure also uses a SCRAM to insert the control rods, as it is the most reliable method of completely inserting the control rods, and prevents the possibility of accidentally withdrawing them during or after the shutdown.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactor_response">Reactor response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactor response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most neutrons in a reactor are <i>prompt neutrons</i>; that is, neutrons produced directly by a fission reaction. These neutrons move at a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_temperature" title="Neutron temperature">high velocity</a>, so they are likely to escape into the <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">moderator</a> before being <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">captured</a>. On average, it takes about 13 Î¼s for the neutrons to be slowed by the moderator enough to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_cross-section" title="Neutron cross-section">facilitate</a> a sustained reaction, which allows the insertion of neutron absorbers to affect the reactor quickly. As a result, once the reactor has been SCRAMed, the reactor power will drop significantly almost instantaneously. However, a small fraction (about 0.65%) of neutrons in a typical power reactor comes from the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of a fission product. These <i>delayed neutrons,</i> which are emitted at lower velocities, will limit the rate at which a nuclear reactor will shut down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decay_heat">Decay heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decay heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Decay_heat" title="Decay heat">Decay heat</a></div>
<p>For a reactor that is SCRAMed after holding a constant power level for an extended period (greater than 100 hrs), about 7% of the steady-state power will remain after initial shutdown due to fission product decay. For a reactor that has not had a constant power history, the exact percentage is determined by the concentrations and half-lives of the individual fission products in the core at the time of the SCRAM. The power produced by <a href="/wiki/Decay_heat" title="Decay heat">decay heat</a> decreases as the fission products decay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1591" data-file-width="1280" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg/170px-Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg/255px-Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg/340px-Szilard_and_Hilberry.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

Norman Hilberry (left) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3_Szil%C3%A1rd" title="LeÃ³ SzilÃ¡rd">LeÃ³ SzilÃ¡rd</a> at Stagg Field, site of the first self-sustaining nuclear chain-reaction.</div>

<p>Scram is usually cited as being an acronym for <b>safety control rod axe man</b>; however, the term is probably a <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronym</a>. The actual axe man at the first chain-reaction was <a href="/wiki/Norman_Hilberry" title="Norman Hilberry">Norman Hilberry</a>. In a letter to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Raymond_Murray&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dr. Raymond Murray (page does not exist)">Dr. Raymond Murray</a> (January 21, 1981), Hilberry wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When I showed up on the balcony on that <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Manhattan_Project#1942" title="Timeline of the Manhattan Project">December 2, 1942</a> afternoon, I was ushered to the balcony rail, handed a well sharpened fireman's axe and told, "if the safety rods fail to operate, cut that <a href="/wiki/Manila_rope" title="Manila rope">manila rope</a>." The safety rods, needless to say, worked, the rope was not cut... I don't believe I have ever felt quite as foolish as I did then. ...I did not get the SCRAM [Safety Control Rod Axe Man] story until many years after the fact. Then one day one of my fellows who had been on Zinn's construction crew called me Mr. Scram. I asked him, "How come?" And then the story.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a May 17, 2011, entry on the Nuclear Regulatory Commission's official blog, NRC historian Tom Wellock argues that this account is effectively an <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a> and arose many years after the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc-gateway.gov_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrc-gateway.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Articles from <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> (ORNL) indicate that the term stands for "safety cut rope axe man", referring in that case to the early neutronic safety mechanism of using a person equipped with an axe to cut the rope suspending the control rods over the Chicago Pile nuclear reactor, at which point the rods would fall by gravity into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactor_core" title="Reactor core">reactor core</a>, shutting the reactor down. Specifically, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wallace_Koehler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wallace Koehler (page does not exist)">Wallace Koehler</a>, a technician working for the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago_Pile_1" title="Chicago Pile 1">Chicago Pile 1</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Stagg_Field" title="Stagg Field">Stagg Field</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, and later a research physicist at ORNL, reportedly said that <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> coined the term as this acronym. Although Koehler did not serve as a rope-cutting control rod axe-man, he was responsible for dumping a bucket of aqueous <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> solution into the reactor if reactor period entered into the sub-optimal range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ornl.gov_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ornl.gov-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leona_Marshall_Libby" title="Leona Marshall Libby">Leona Marshall Libby</a>, who was present that day at the Chicago Pile, recalled<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-libby_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-libby-8">[8]</a></sup> that the term was coined by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Volney_Wilson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Volney Wilson (page does not exist)">Volney Wilson</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[T]he safety rods were coated with cadmium foil, and this metal absorbed so many neutrons that the chain reaction was stopped. Volney Wilson called these "scram" rods. He said that the pile had "scrammed," the rods had "scrammed" into the pile.</p>
</blockquote>
