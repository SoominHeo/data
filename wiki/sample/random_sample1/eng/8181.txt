<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Skírnismál</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Skírnismál</b></i> (<i>Sayings of <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%ADrnir" title="Skírnir">Skírnir</a></i>) is one of the poems of the <i><a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a></i>. It is preserved in the 13th-century manuscripts <a href="/wiki/Codex_Regius" title="Codex Regius">Codex Regius</a> and <a href="/wiki/AM_748_I_4to" title="AM 748 I 4to">AM 748 I 4to</a> but may have been originally composed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" title="Norse paganism">heathen</a> times. Many scholars believe that the poem was acted out, perhaps in a sort of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hi%C3%A9ros_gamos" title="Hiéros gamos">hiéros gamos</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="803" data-file-width="599" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg/220px-Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg/330px-Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg/440px-Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l-748-2v.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
AM 748 I 4<sup>to</sup>, one of the two manuscripts to preserve <i>Skírnismál</i>, has notes on the margin indicating the speaker of each verse. Some scholars consider this a clue that the poem might have been performed as ritual drama.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The prose prologue to the poem says that the god <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Nj%C3%B6r%C3%B0r" title="Njörðr">Njörðr</a>, sits in <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>'s throne, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hli%C3%B0skj%C3%A1lf" title="Hliðskjálf">Hliðskjálf</a> and looked over all the worlds. On looking to <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunheimr" title="Jötunheimr">Jötunheimr</a>, the land of the giants, Freyr sees a beautiful girl and is immediately seized by love. Fearing that the object of his heart's desire is unattainable, gloom settles upon him.</p>
<p>The poem itself starts with the wife of Njörðr, Skaði, bidding Skírnir to ask of Freyr why he is so sad. Skírnir, fearing his master's wrath, nevertheless does as he is bidden. Freyr's response is sullen, yet he pours his heart out. Skírnir agrees to undertake a journey to woo Gerðr, and Freyr furnishes him with his magical steed and sword.</p>
<p>Skírnir duly fetches up in Jötunheimr, at the hall of the giant <a href="/wiki/Gymir" title="Gymir">Gymir</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ger%C3%B0r" title="Gerðr">Gerðr</a>, the daughter of Gymir bids him enter the hall; without further ado, Skírnir tries to woo Gerðr on Freyr's behalf, offering first gifts then threats (in other versions Skírnir does not use threats but manages to successfully woo Gerðr). Eventually, Gerðr succumbs. Skírnir reports to Freyr, who asks him:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Tell me, Skírnir, before unsaddling</dd>
<dd>Or stepping forth another pace</dd>
<dd>Is the news you bring from Jotunheim</dd>
<dd>For better or for worse?</dd>
</dl>
<p>Skírnir replies:</p>
<dl>
<dd>In the woods of Barri, which know we both so well,</dd>
<dd>A quiet still and tranquil place</dd>
<dd>In nine nights time to Njörd's son</dd>
<dd>Will Gerd give herself.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Freyr responds:</p>
<dl>
<dd>One night is long enough, yet longer still are two;</dd>
<dd>How then shall I contend with three?</dd>
<dd>For months have passed more quickly</dd>
<dd>Than half a bridal eve.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Later, <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%ADrnir" title="Skírnir">Skírnir</a> speaks to <a href="/wiki/Ger%C3%B0r" title="Gerðr">Gerd</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Seest thou, maiden, this keen, bright sword</dd>
<dd>That I hold here in my hand?</dd>
<dd>Before its blade the old giant bends,—</dd>
<dd>Thy father is doomed to die.</dd>
<dd>I strike thee, maid, with my <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambantein" title="Gambantein">gambantein</a>,</dd>
<dd>To tame thee to work my will;</dd>
<dd>There shalt thou go where never again</dd>
<dd>The sons of men shall see thee.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sk%C3%ADrnism%C3%A1l&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Skírnir's curse has partial parallels in a number of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> texts, including the curse known as <i>Buslubæn</i> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%B3sa_saga" title="Bósa saga">Bósa saga</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Bergen_rune-charm" title="Bergen rune-charm">Bergen rune-charm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Misty_Mountains" title="Misty Mountains">Misty Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> are likely to have been inspired by the <i>úrig fiöll</i> in the <i>Skírnismál</i>. Tolkien was familiar with the <i>Poetic Edda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
