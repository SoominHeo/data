<p>The <b>Stade de France</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[stad də fʁɑ̃s]</a></span>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_stadium" title="National stadium">national stadium</a> of France, just north of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in the commune of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Denis,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Saint-Denis, Seine-Saint-Denis">Saint-Denis</a>. Its seating capacity of 81,338 makes it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_stadiums_by_capacity" title="List of European stadiums by capacity">sixth-largest stadium in Europe</a>. The stadium is used by the <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France national football team</a> and <a href="/wiki/France_national_rugby_union_team" title="France national rugby union team">French rugby union team</a> for international competition. Originally built for the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1998 FIFA World Cup">1998 FIFA World Cup</a>, the stadium's name was recommended by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Platini" title="Michel Platini">Michel Platini</a>, head of the organising committee. On 12 July 1998, France defeated <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil</a> 3–0 in the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup_Final" title="1998 FIFA World Cup Final">1998 FIFA World Cup Final</a> contested at the stadium.</p>
<p>Stade de France, listed as a <a href="/wiki/UEFA_stadium_categories" title="UEFA stadium categories">Category 4</a> stadium by <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a>, hosted matches at the 1998 FIFA World Cup, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_European_Cup_and_UEFA_Champions_League_winners" title="List of European Cup and UEFA Champions League winners">UEFA Champions League finals</a> in <a href="/wiki/2000_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2000 UEFA Champions League Final">2000</a> and <a href="/wiki/2006_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2006 UEFA Champions League Final">2006</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/1999_Rugby_World_Cup" title="1999 Rugby World Cup">1999</a> and <a href="/wiki/2007_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2007 Rugby World Cup">2007 Rugby World Cup</a>, making it the only stadium in the world to have hosted both a <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">Football World Cup</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup_Final" title="FIFA World Cup Final">final</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Rugby_World_Cup" title="Rugby World Cup">Rugby World Cup</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rugby_World_Cup_Final" title="Rugby World Cup Final">final</a>. The facility also hosted the <a href="/wiki/Race_of_Champions" title="Race of Champions">Race of Champions</a> auto race in <a href="/wiki/2004_Race_of_Champions" title="2004 Race of Champions">2004</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_Race_of_Champions" title="2005 Race of Champions">2005</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2006_Race_of_Champions" title="2006 Race of Champions">2006</a>. The stadium hosted the <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="2003 World Championships in Athletics">2003 World Championships in Athletics</a> and since 1999 it has hosted the annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meeting_Areva" title="Meeting Areva">Meeting Areva</a> athletics meet. It also hosted some matches at <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016" title="UEFA Euro 2016">UEFA Euro 2016</a>, including the final, where <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France</a> lost to <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team" title="Portugal national football team">Portugal</a> by 1-0 after extra-time.</p>
<p>Domestically, the Stade de France serves as a secondary home facility of Parisian rugby clubs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stade_Fran%C3%A7ais_Paris" title="Stade Français Paris">Stade Français</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racing_M%C3%A9tro_92" title="Racing Métro 92">Racing Métro 92</a>, hosting a few of their regular-season fixtures. The stadium also hosts the main French domestic <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament" title="Single-elimination tournament">cup</a> finals, which include the <a href="/wiki/Coupe_de_France" title="Coupe de France">Coupe de France</a> (both football and rugby), <a href="/wiki/Coupe_de_la_Ligue" title="Coupe de la Ligue">Coupe de la Ligue</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Challenge_de_France" title="Challenge de France">Challenge de France</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Coupe_Gambardella" title="Coupe Gambardella">Coupe Gambardella</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Top_14" title="Top 14">Top 14</a> <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> championship match.</p>
<p>The stadium is also used for music concerts with <a href="/wiki/Roger_Waters" title="Roger Waters">Roger Waters</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A9line_Dion" title="Céline Dion">Céline Dion</a>, <a href="/wiki/AC/DC" title="AC/DC">AC/DC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beyonc%C3%A9" title="Beyoncé">Beyoncé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay_Z" title="Jay Z">Jay Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a>, <a href="/wiki/One_Direction" title="One Direction">One Direction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">The Police</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myl%C3%A8ne_Farmer" title="Mylène Farmer">Mylène Farmer</a> having performed here. The facility is owned and operated by the Consortium Stade de France.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1878" data-file-width="3005" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG/220px-Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG/330px-Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG/440px-Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sacrecoeur_stadedefrance.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stade de France visible from central Paris behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilique_du_Sacr%C3%A9-C%C5%93ur,_Paris" title="Basilique du Sacré-Cœur, Paris">Sacré-Cœur</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The discussion of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_stadium" title="National stadium">national stadium</a> in France came about as a result of the country's selection to host the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1998 FIFA World Cup">1998 FIFA World Cup</a> on 2 July 1992. As a result of the selection, the country and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/France_Football_Federation" title="France Football Federation">France Football Federation</a> made a commitment to construct an 80,000+ capacity all-seater stadium with every seat in the facility being covered. It was the first time in over 70 years since the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Stade_Olympique_Yves-du-Manoir" title="Stade Olympique Yves-du-Manoir">Stade Olympique Yves-du-Manoir</a> that a stadium in France was being constructed for a specific event. Due to the magnitude and importance of the facility, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_State_(France)" title="Council of State (France)">Council of State</a> was allowed first hand approach to how the stadium would be constructed and paid for. The Council sought for the stadium to be built as close as possible to the capital of France, Paris, and that the constructor and operator of the facility would receive significant financial contribution for a period of 30 months following the completion of the stadium. The stadium's design was handled by the team of architects composed of Michel Macary, Aymeric Zublena, Regembal Michel, and Claude Costantini who were associated with CR SCAU Architecture.</p>
<p>The stadium was officially ready for construction following the government's selection of manufacturers, <a href="/wiki/Bouygues" title="Bouygues">Bouygues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vinci_SA" title="Vinci SA">Dumez</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vinci_SA" title="Vinci SA">SGE</a>, and the signing of building permits on 30 April 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With only 31 months to complete the stadium, construction commenced on 2 May 1995. The laying of the first cornerstone took place five months later on 6 September. After over a year of construction, over 800,000m ² of <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">earthworks</a> had been created and as much as 180,000 m³ of concrete had been poured. The installation of the roof, which cost €45 million, and the mobile platform also took more than a year to complete.</p>
<p>During the developmental phase, the stadium was referred to in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> as the <i>Grand Stade</i> ("large stadium" or "great stadium"). On 4 December 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Youth_Affairs_and_Sports_(France)" title="Minister of Youth Affairs and Sports (France)">Ministry of Sport</a> launched a design competition to decide on a name for the stadium. The stadium was officially named the Stade de France after the Ministry heard a proposal from French <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> legend <a href="/wiki/Michel_Platini" title="Michel Platini">Michel Platini</a>, who recommended the name.</p>
<p>The stadium was inaugurated on 28 January 1998 as it hosted a football match between France and <a href="/wiki/Spain_national_football_team" title="Spain national football team">Spain</a>. The total cost of the stadium was approximately €290 million. The match was played in front of 78,368 spectators, which included <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">Jacques Chirac</a>, with France winning the match 1–0 with <a href="/wiki/Zinedine_Zidane" title="Zinedine Zidane">Zinedine Zidane</a> scoring the lone goal, and the first-ever in the Stade de France, in the 20th minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Six months later, France returned to the stadium and defeated <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup_Final" title="1998 FIFA World Cup Final">1998 FIFA World Cup Final</a> to earn their first World Cup title.Stade de France has hosted group, quarter-final, semi-final and the final match of 1998 FIFA World Cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The national rugby team's first match in the facility was contested five days after its opening, on 2 February, with France earning a 24–17 win over <a href="/wiki/England_national_rugby_union_team" title="England national rugby union team">England</a> in front of 77,567 spectators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Bernat-Salles" title="Philippe Bernat-Salles">Philippe Bernat-Salles</a> converted the first ever try at the stadium scoring it in the 11th minute of play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 May 2000, the Stade de France hosted the <a href="/wiki/2000_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2000 UEFA Champions League Final">2000 UEFA Champions League Final</a>. In the match, which saw 78,759 spectators attend, Spanish club <a href="/wiki/Real_Madrid_C.F." title="Real Madrid C.F.">Real Madrid</a> defeated fellow Spaniards <a href="/wiki/Valencia_CF" title="Valencia CF">Valencia</a> 3–0. In 2003, the Stade de France was the primary site of the <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="2003 World Championships in Athletics">2003 World Championships in Athletics</a>. Three years later in 2006, the facility hosted another UEFA Champions League final with another Spanish club <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F.C._Barcelona" title="F.C. Barcelona">Barcelona</a> defeating England's <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal</a> 2–1. On 9 May 2009, the Stade de France set the national attendance record for a sporting match played in France with 80,832 showing up to watch <a href="/wiki/En_Avant_de_Guingamp" title="En Avant de Guingamp">Guingamp</a> upset <a href="/wiki/Derby_Breton" title="Derby Breton">Brittany rivals</a> <a href="/wiki/Stade_Rennais_F.C." title="Stade Rennais F.C.">Rennes</a> 2–1 in the <a href="/wiki/2009_Coupe_de_France_Final" title="2009 Coupe de France Final">2009 Coupe de France Final</a>. On 22 May 2010, the Stade de France hosted the <a href="/wiki/2010_Heineken_Cup_Final" title="2010 Heineken Cup Final">2010 Heineken Cup Final</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On 11 February 2012, a Six Nations international rugby game between France and Ireland had to be cancelled just before kick-off due to the pitch freezing as the stadium lacks under-soil heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 November 2015, in one of <a href="/wiki/November_2015_Paris_attacks" title="November 2015 Paris attacks">a series of coordinated shootings and bombings</a> across Paris, the Stade de France was targeted with explosions occurring outside the stadium during an international friendly between France and Germany, with French President <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Hollande" title="François Hollande">François Hollande</a> in attendance. The terrorist, however, was unable to enter the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg/220px-World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg/330px-World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg/440px-World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_championships_in_athletics_2003_Paris_Saint-Denis_stadium.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stade de France with uncovered athletics track during the <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="2003 World Championships in Athletics">2003 World Championships</a></div>

</div>
<p>The Stade de France has a <a href="/wiki/Movable_seating" title="Movable seating">movable stand</a> which can be retracted to uncover part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_track" title="Athletics track">athletics track</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The stadium was notably designed with the assistance of a software simulation of crowd in order to get an accurate observation of how it would look fully developed. The facility was also intended to draw interest in and develop the area of the Plaine Saint-Denis, which straddle the communes of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Denis,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Saint-Denis, Seine-Saint-Denis">Saint-Denis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aubervilliers" title="Aubervilliers">Aubervilliers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Ouen,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Saint-Ouen, Seine-Saint-Denis">Saint-Ouen</a>. The primary goal was to renovate the area by building new residential and tertiary sites.</p>
<p>The stadium was built without any undersoil heating as noted when Ireland were due to play France in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_Nations_Tournament" title="Six Nations Tournament">Six Nations Tournament</a> rugby union match of 2012, in which the game had to be canceled.</p>
<p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_for_Bridge_and_Structural_Engineering" title="International Association for Bridge and Structural Engineering">International Association for Bridge and Structural Engineering</a> (IABC) awarded a prize recognizing the unique structure of the Stade de France commenting that the Stade de France exhibited "a construction of an attractive open architecture of the city, with an elegance and natural lightness".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roof">Roof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5776" data-file-width="8713" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg/220px-Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg/330px-Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg/440px-Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pose_du_dernier_troncon_de_la_toiture_du_stade_de_france.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The last part of the metallic construction of the roof, 1997</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Construction of the Stade de France's roof cost over €45 million. Its elliptical shape symbolizes the universality of <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_France" title="Sport in France">sport in France</a>. Its area of six hectares and weight, 13,000 tons, is considered a technical marvel by many. It was designed to easily protect the 80,000 spectators without covering the playing field. All lighting and sound, which include 550 lights and 36 blocks of 5 speakers, are housed inside to avoid obstructing visibility. The tinted glass in the center reduces the contrast and distributes natural light. It filters out red and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, however, it allows blue and green lights, due to their necessity involving the health of the turf.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stands">Stands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Stade de France is the biggest modular stadium in the world with three galleries.</p>
<p>The forum is a low mobile platform of 25,000 seats. It is reached by the level 1. It may fall 15 feet to reveal all of the running track and jumping pits. It then retains 22,000 seats. The movement lasts 80 hours, 40 people 20h/24h mobilized, and carried by ten distinct elements of 700 tons each.</p>
<p>Access to the gallery is through with 22 bridges and can be found at level 3 with a concentration of restaurants, entertainment areas, shops and central station security.</p>
<p>18 staircases lead viewers to the upper gallery located at Level 6.</p>
<p>The evacuation of 80,000 spectators on the porch out can occur in less than 15 minutes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Field">Field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located at 11 meters below the court, the playing area measures 9,000 square meters (120 meters long and 75 meters wide) to a grassed area of 11,000 square meters. Nearly one billion seeds were sown to produce the first pitch in 1997. Today, the grass comes in rolls of 1.20 mx 8 m. Changing the pitch calls for three days of preparation and five days of installation. The change takes place several times a year, depending on the programming stage. Unlike many other stadiums, the Stade de France was built without under pitch heating, as the stadium was constructed on the site of an old gasworks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and there were concerns it could cause an explosion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Giant_screens">Giant screens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Giant screens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As part of its policy of renewing its infrastructure, the Stade de France added two new big screens in September 2006. Covering an area of 196 sqm each, these screens are the largest located in a stadium in Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The new displays have a surface 58% greater than the previous screens installed in 1998. The newer giant screens are each composed of 4 423 680 LED (Light Emitting Diode) for a self-made images more fluid, faster, and more particularly bright.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tenants">Tenants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tenants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Stade de France has no regular tenant other than the French national football and rugby teams. Repeated attempts to persuade a professional football or rugby team to move there have failed so far. <a href="/wiki/Paris_Saint-Germain_F.C." title="Paris Saint-Germain F.C.">Paris Saint-Germain</a> has remained at <a href="/wiki/Parc_des_Princes" title="Parc des Princes">Parc des Princes</a> under pressure from its parent company (pay-TV network <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canal_Plus" title="Canal Plus">Canal Plus</a>) and the Paris city government.</p>
<p>However, the Paris rugby club <a href="/wiki/Stade_Fran%C3%A7ais" title="Stade Français">Stade Français</a> have now established themselves as a semi-regular tenant. They began by scheduling their Top 14 home fixture on 15 October 2005 against <a href="/wiki/Toulouse_FC" title="Toulouse FC">Toulouse</a> at Stade de France. Stade Français's president, <a href="/wiki/Max_Guazzini" title="Max Guazzini">Max Guazzini</a>, publicly said that the club would have to sell 25,000 to 30,000 tickets to break even. Three weeks before the match, 61,000 tickets had been sold, setting a French record for tickets sold to a league match for any sport, including football. The final attendance was 79,454, smashing the national attendance record for a league match in any sport by more than 20,000. Five minutes before the end of the Toulouse match, Guazzini announced to the crowd that Stade Français's scheduled home fixture against <a href="/wiki/Biarritz_Olympique" title="Biarritz Olympique">Biarritz</a> in March 2006 would also be held at Stade de France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Stade-Biarritz match broke the attendance record from earlier in the season, with 79,604 present.</p>
<p>Guazzini then booked the Stade de France for the same two league fixtures in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006-07_Top_14_season" title="2006-07 Top 14 season">2006–07</a>. The Biarritz match on 16 October 2006 drew 79,619, making this the third consecutive Stade Français fixture at the Stade de France to set an all-time French attendance record. The record was broken yet again at a match against Toulouse match on 27 January 2007, with 79,741 filling the stands. Stade Français went on to schedule three home matches at Stade de France in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007-08_Top_14_season" title="2007-08 Top 14 season">2007–08 season</a>. For the 2008–09 season, they booked Stade de France for three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008-09_Top_14_season" title="2008-09 Top 14 season">home league matches</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008-09_Heineken_Cup_pool_stage#Pool_4" title="2008-09 Heineken Cup pool stage">a Heineken Cup pool match</a>. The number of Stade Français home matches at Stade de France increased again for 2009–10, with five <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Top_14_season" title="2009–10 Top 14 season">Top 14 fixtures</a> already announced for the stadium.</p>
<p>Even with the lack of a regular league tenant, the stadium's revenue increased greatly in 2007, as it was used extensively during the 2007 Rugby World Cup in France, where it hosted numerous pool matches, a quarterfinal match, both of the semi finals and the final.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lille_OSC" title="Lille OSC">Lille OSC</a> football team played all its "home" games in European competition during the 2005–06 season, both in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UEFA_Cup" title="UEFA Cup">UEFA Cup</a>, at the Stade de France because its own stadium was then under renovation, and the only nearer alternative on French soil, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stade_F%C3%A9lix-Bollaert" title="Stade Félix-Bollaert">Stade Félix-Bollaert</a>, was not available as that ground's occupant, Lille's local rival <a href="/wiki/RC_Lens" title="RC Lens">Lens</a>, was also participating in the UEFA Cup. The Stade de France has hosted the Champions League final on two occasions: <a href="/wiki/2000_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2000 UEFA Champions League Final">2000</a> (<a href="/wiki/Real_Madrid_C.F." title="Real Madrid C.F.">Real Madrid</a> 3 <a href="/wiki/Valencia_CF" title="Valencia CF">Valencia</a> 0), and <a href="/wiki/2006_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2006 UEFA Champions League Final">2006</a> (<a href="/wiki/FC_Barcelona" title="FC Barcelona">Barcelona</a> 2 <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal</a> 1).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_developments">Future developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Future developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>France's governing body for rugby union, the <a href="/wiki/French_Rugby_Federation" title="French Rugby Federation">French Rugby Federation</a> (FFR), announced in November 2010 that it would not renew its deal to use Stade de France for international rugby matches when it expires in 2013. FFR also stated that it planned to build a new stadium of its own in the Paris region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_FFR_stadium3_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_FFR_stadium3-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reportedly, the FFR had become increasingly frustrated with several aspects of the deal. According to rugby journalist Ian Moriarty, "The deal with the Stade de France has been a disaster for the FFR financially over the years, forcing France's powerbrokers to look across the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English channel</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Football_Union" title="Rugby Football Union">RFU's</a> <a href="/wiki/Twickenham_Stadium" title="Twickenham Stadium">Twickenham</a> cash cow with ever increasing envy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moriarty3_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moriarty3-22">[22]</a></sup> Reports vary widely as to how much the FFR must spend to rent out the stadium, but estimates range from €3 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moriarty3_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moriarty3-22">[22]</a></sup> to €5 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPN_Scrum_FFR_plans3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN_Scrum_FFR_plans3-23">[23]</a></sup> per match. Although the Stade de France and Twickenham are roughly the same size, the rental expense means that the FFR reportedly makes about one-third as much from a Stade de France sellout as does the RFU from a sellout at Twickenham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPN_Scrum_FFR_plans3_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN_Scrum_FFR_plans3-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition, the national rugby team does not enjoy primacy at the Stade de France; the national football team and major concerts take priority. FFR had to move two of its 2010–11 home Tests to Montpellier and Nantes due to fixture clashes with the national football team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moriarty3_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Moriarty3-22">[22]</a></sup> Also, former FFR president <a href="/wiki/Serge_Blanco" title="Serge Blanco">Serge Blanco</a> claimed that the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_Top_14_season#Final" title="2008–09 Top 14 season">2009 Top 14 final</a> had to be moved from May to June because of a conflict with a <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Hallyday" title="Johnny Hallyday">Johnny Hallyday</a> rock concert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_FFR_stadium3_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_FFR_stadium3-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2012, FFR announced that it had selected the site for its new ground, tentatively known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Stade_FFR&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grand Stade FFR (page does not exist)">Grand Stade FFR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The 82,000-seat stadium, featuring a retractable roof and slide-out pitch, will be built on a former horse racing track in <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vry,_Essonne" title="Évry, Essonne">Évry</a>, about 25 km south of Paris. The new stadium, estimated to cost €600 million, was originally scheduled to open in 2017,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moriarty3_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Moriarty3-22">[22]</a></sup> but completion has since been pushed back to the 2021/2022 time frame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access">Access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stade_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although located at the crossroads of auto-routes A1 and A86, it is not advisable to go there by car unless you have reserved parking. The Stadium was built with a very limited number of parking spaces, which is why <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> is considered the primary means of getting to the stadium. River shuttles are provided by the <a href="/wiki/Canal_Saint-Denis" title="Canal Saint-Denis">Canal Saint-Denis</a>.</p>
