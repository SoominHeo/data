<p><b>Stadion Feijenoord</b> (<small>pronounced</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[ˌstaːdijɔn ˈfɛiənoːrt]</a></span>), more commonly known by its nickname <b>De Kuip</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<small>pronounced</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[də ˈkœyp]</a></span>; the Tub), is a <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> that was completed in 1937. The name is derived from the area "<a href="/wiki/Feijenoord_district" title="Feijenoord district">Feijenoord</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, and from the club with the same name (although the club's name was internationalised to Feyenoord in 1973). The stadium's original capacity was 64,000. In 1949 it was expanded to 69,000, and in 1994 it was renovated again for a capacity of 51,117. In 1999, a significant amount of restoration and interior work took place at the stadium prior to its use as a venue in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_2000" title="Euro 2000">Euro 2000</a> tournament, although capacity was largely unaffected.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Kuip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Leen van Zandvliet, Feyenoord's president in the 1930s, came up with the idea of building an entirely new stadium, unlike any other on the continent, with two free hanging tiers and no obstacles blocking the view. Contemporary examples were <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_Stadium" title="Arsenal Stadium">Highbury</a>, where the West and East stands had been recently built as a double deck, and <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium_(1923)" title="Yankee Stadium (1923)">Yankee Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brinkman" title="Johannes Brinkman">Johannes Brinkman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leendert_van_der_Vlugt" title="Leendert van der Vlugt">Leendert van der Vlugt</a>, the famous designers of the van Nelle factories in Rotterdam were asked to design a stadium out of glass, concrete and steel, cheap materials at that time. In fact, "de Kuip" acted as an example for many of the greatest stadia we know today, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Camp_Nou" title="Camp Nou">Camp Nou</a>. The stadium was co-financed by the billionaire <a href="/wiki/Dani%C3%ABl_George_van_Beuningen" title="Daniël George van Beuningen">Daniël George van Beuningen</a>, who made his fortune in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, exporting <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> from Germany to Britain through neutral <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the stadium was nearly torn down for scrap by German occupiers. After the war the stadium's capacity was expanded (1949) and stadium lights were added (1958). On 29 October 1991 De Kuip was named as being one of Rotterdam's monuments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuiphist_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-kuiphist-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1994 the stadium was extensively renovated to its present form:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuiphist_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-kuiphist-2">[2]</a></sup> It became an <a href="/wiki/All-seater_stadium" title="All-seater stadium">all seater</a> and the roof was extended to cover all the seats. An extra building was constructed for commercial use by Feyenoord, it also houses a restaurant and a museum, <i>The Home of History</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of January 2007, the stadium can be found in 3D format on <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities_and_related_buildings">Facilities and related buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Kuip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilities and related buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adjacent to De Kuip inside the Maasgebouw (Maas building) is a restaurant, Brasserie “De Kuip Eten en Drinken”, which a sporty decoration combined with a cosy atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuyperij_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cuyperij-5">[5]</a></sup> The brasserie can host 180 customers during dinner times and 300 customers for business meetings. The brasserie is closed when Feyenoord plays at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuyperij_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cuyperij-5">[5]</a></sup> A visit to the brasserie is often combined with a stadium tour in De Kuip, or a visit during a Feyenoord training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Next to De Kuip and Feyenoord's training ground there is another, but smaller sports arena, the <a href="/wiki/Topsportcentrum_Rotterdam" title="Topsportcentrum Rotterdam">Topsportcentrum Rotterdam</a>. This arena hosts events in many sports and in various levels of competition. Some examples of sports that can be seen in the topsportcentrum are <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_handball" title="Team handball">handball</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_uses">Commercial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Kuip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football_history">Football history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Kuip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Football history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>De Kuip is currently the home stadium of football club <a href="/wiki/Feyenoord" title="Feyenoord">Feyenoord</a>, one of the traditional top teams in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. It has also long been one of the home grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_national_football_team" title="Netherlands national football team">Dutch national team</a>, having hosted over 150 international matches, with the first one being a match against <a href="/wiki/Belgium_national_football_team" title="Belgium national football team">Belgium</a> on 2 May 1937. In 1963 De Kuip staged the final of the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Cup_Winners%27_Cup" title="UEFA Cup Winners' Cup">UEFA Cup Winners' Cup</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> becoming the first British club to win a European trophy -- beating <a href="/wiki/Atl%C3%A9tico_Madrid" title="Atlético Madrid">Atlético Madrid</a> 5-1. A record ten European finals have taken place in the Feijenoord stadium, the last one being the <a href="/wiki/2002_UEFA_Cup_Final" title="2002 UEFA Cup Final">2002 UEFA Cup Final</a> in which Feyenoord, coincidentally playing a home match, defeated <a href="/wiki/Borussia_Dortmund" title="Borussia Dortmund">Borussia Dortmund</a> 3–2. In 2000, the Feijenoord stadium hosted the final of <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2000" title="UEFA Euro 2000">Euro 2000</a>, played in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, where <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France</a> defeated <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team" title="Italy national football team">Italy</a> 2–1 in extra time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuiphist_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-kuiphist-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerts">Concerts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Kuip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concerts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stadium has hosted concerts since 1978. Among the first performers at De Kuip were Andy Ha, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuiphist_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-kuiphist-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> held his dress rehearsals and subsequently opened his 1987 <a href="/wiki/Glass_Spider_Tour" title="Glass Spider Tour">Glass Spider Tour</a> at the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlassIdol_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlassIdol-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> performed at the stadium 5 times, 3 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bad_World_Tour" title="Bad World Tour">Bad World Tour</a> (1988) and 2 during the <a href="/wiki/Dangerous_World_Tour" title="Dangerous World Tour">Dangerous World Tour</a> (1992), performing to a combined crowd of 270,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Fewer concerts have been held at this venue since the opening of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amsterdam_ArenA" title="Amsterdam ArenA">Amsterdam ArenA</a> in 1996. Pink Floyd held a few concerts there as well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="De_Nieuwe_Kuip">De Nieuwe Kuip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Kuip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: De Nieuwe Kuip">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 2006 Feyenoord Rotterdam has been working on plans for a new stadium, with plans for 2017 completion and an estimated capacity for 85,000 people. In 2014 Feyenoord decided to renovate the stadium making it a 70,000 seater with a retractable roof. Building is planned to start in summer 2015, and to finish in 2018 with total costs of an estimated €200 million. Part of the plan is a new training facility, costing an extra €16 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
