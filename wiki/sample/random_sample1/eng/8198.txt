<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Starrcade</h1>

<p><b>Starrcade</b> was an annual <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling" title="Professional wrestling">professional wrestling</a> event, originally broadcast via closed-circuit television and eventually broadcast via pay-per-view television, held from 1983 to 2000 by the <a href="/wiki/National_Wrestling_Alliance" title="National Wrestling Alliance">National Wrestling Alliance</a> (NWA) and later <a href="/wiki/World_Championship_Wrestling" title="World Championship Wrestling">World Championship Wrestling</a> (WCW). Starrcade was regarded by the NWA and WCW as their flagship event of the year, much in the same vein that its rival, the <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">World Wrestling Entertainment</a>, regards <a href="/wiki/WrestleMania" title="WrestleMania">WrestleMania</a>. As a result, the buildup to each Starrcade featured the largest <a href="/wiki/Feud_(professional_wrestling)" title="Feud (professional wrestling)">feuds</a> of the promotion.</p>
<p>From 1983 to 1987, Starrcade was produced by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crockett_Promotions" title="Jim Crockett Promotions">Jim Crockett Promotions</a> (JCP), the dominant promotion of the NWA, and aired on <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">Thanksgiving Day</a>. In 1987, the WWF scheduled a pay-per-view of their own, <a href="/wiki/Survivor_Series_(1987)" title="Survivor Series (1987)">Survivor Series</a>, on Thanksgiving night and demanded exclusivity from cable providers on carriage of the event. In order to prevent such a problem, Starrcade was moved to December the following year and the show was held around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>, mostly in the days following, beginning in 1988.</p>
<p>Also in 1988, JCP was sold to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turner_Broadcasting" title="Turner Broadcasting">Turner Broadcasting</a> due to financial problems and became WCW, though Starrcade was held under the NWA banner until 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 1960s to the 1980s, it was tradition for JCP to hold major professional wrestling events on Thanksgiving and <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, mostly at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greensboro_Coliseum" title="Greensboro Coliseum">Greensboro Coliseum</a>. In 1983, JCP created Starrcade as their <a href="/wiki/Card_(sports)" title="Card (sports)">supercard</a> to continue the Thanksgiving tradition, and spread it across its territory on <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television" title="Closed-circuit television">closed-circuit television</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It popularized broadcasting on closed-circuit television and was financially successful. From 1987, Starrcade was broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Pay-per-view" title="Pay-per-view">pay-per-view</a>, the first NWA event to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rights to the event now belong to WWE (formerly the World Wrestling Federation). In November 2008, WWE 24/7 Classics aired a special as a celebration of the 25th anniversary of the event called <i>The Essential Starrcade</i>. This five-part series counts down the top 25 matches in Starrcade history. In January 2009, <a href="/wiki/WWE_Home_Video" title="WWE Home Video">WWE Home Video</a> released <i>Starrcade: The Essential Collection</i> as a three disc <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> With the launch of the <a href="/wiki/WWE_Network" title="WWE Network">WWE Network</a> in 2014 all Starrcade shows became available on demand to Network subscribers.</p>
