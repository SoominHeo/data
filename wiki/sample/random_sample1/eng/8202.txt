<p><b>Stanley "Stan" Getz</b> (February 2, 1927 – June 6, 1991) was an American <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> saxophonist. Playing primarily the <a href="/wiki/Tenor_saxophone" title="Tenor saxophone">tenor saxophone</a>, Getz was known as "The Sound" because of his warm, lyrical tone, his prime influence being the wispy, mellow timbre of his idol, <a href="/wiki/Lester_Young" title="Lester Young">Lester Young</a>. Coming to prominence in the late 1940s with <a href="/wiki/Woody_Herman" title="Woody Herman">Woody Herman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big band</a>, Getz is described by critic <a href="/wiki/Scott_Yanow" title="Scott Yanow">Scott Yanow</a> as "one of the all-time great tenor saxophonists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALLMUSIC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALLMUSIC-1">[1]</a></sup> Getz went on to perform in <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cool_jazz" title="Cool jazz">cool jazz</a> groups, but is perhaps best known for popularizing <a href="/wiki/Bossa_nova" title="Bossa nova">bossa nova</a> with the worldwide hit single "<a href="/wiki/The_Girl_from_Ipanema" title="The Girl from Ipanema">The Girl from Ipanema</a>" (1964) performed with <a href="/wiki/Astrud_Gilberto" title="Astrud Gilberto">Astrud Gilberto</a>, and for his work done under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Gilberto" title="João Gilberto">João Gilberto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B4nio_Carlos_Jobim" title="Antônio Carlos Jobim">Antônio Carlos Jobim</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Getz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Getz was born on February 2, 1927, at St. Vincent's Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>. His grandparents Harris and Beckie Gayetski were from the <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> area of Russian Empire but migrated to Whitechapel, in the East End of London and owned the Harris Tailor Shop at 52 Oxford Street for more than 13 years. In 1913, Harris and Beckie emigrated to the United States with their three sons Al, Phil, and Ben after their son Louis Gayetski in 1912 (Getz's father Al was born in Mile End, London, England in 1904 and his mother Goldie Yampolsky in Philadelphia in 1907).</p>
<p>The Getz family first settled in Philadelphia, but during the depression the family moved to New York City, seeking better employment opportunities. Getz worked hard in school, receiving straight As, and finished sixth grade close to the top of his class. Getz's major interest was in musical instruments and he played a number of them before his father bought him his first saxophone at the age of 13. Even though his father also got him a clarinet, Getz instantly fell in love with the saxophone and began practicing eight hours a day.</p>
<p>He attended <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe_High_School_(New_York_City)" title="James Monroe High School (New York City)">James Monroe High School</a> in the Bronx. In 1941, he was accepted into the All City High School Orchestra of New York City. This gave him a chance to receive private, free tutoring from the New York Philharmonic's <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kovar" title="Simon Kovar">Simon Kovar</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a> player. He also continued playing the saxophone. He eventually dropped out of school in order to pursue his musical career, but was later sent back to the classroom by the school system's truancy officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALLMUSIC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ALLMUSIC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1943 at the age of 16,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pbs.org_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pbs.org-2">[2]</a></sup> he was accepted into <a href="/wiki/Jack_Teagarden" title="Jack Teagarden">Jack Teagarden</a>'s band, and because of his youth he became Teagarden's ward. Getz also played along with <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">Nat King Cole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Hampton" title="Lionel Hampton">Lionel Hampton</a>. After playing for <a href="/wiki/Stan_Kenton" title="Stan Kenton">Stan Kenton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dorsey" title="Jimmy Dorsey">Jimmy Dorsey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Benny_Goodman" title="Benny Goodman">Benny Goodman</a>, Getz was a soloist with <a href="/wiki/Woody_Herman" title="Woody Herman">Woody Herman</a> from 1947 to 1949 in "The Second Herd", and he first gained wide attention as one of the band's saxophonists, who were known collectively as "<a href="/wiki/Four_Brothers_(jazz_standard)" title="Four Brothers (jazz standard)">The Four Brothers</a>", the others being <a href="/wiki/Serge_Chaloff" title="Serge Chaloff">Serge Chaloff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Sims" title="Zoot Sims">Zoot Sims</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Steward" title="Herbie Steward">Herbie Steward</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pbs.org_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pbs.org-2">[2]</a></sup> With Herman, he had a hit with "<a href="/wiki/Early_Autumn_(song)" title="Early Autumn (song)">Early Autumn</a>" and after Getz left "The Second Herd" he was able to launch his solo career. He was the leader on almost all of his recording sessions after 1950.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Getz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Getz's reputation was greatly enhanced by his featured status on <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Smith" title="Johnny Smith">Johnny Smith</a>'s 1952 album <i><a href="/wiki/Moonlight_in_Vermont_(album)" title="Moonlight in Vermont (album)">Moonlight in Vermont</a></i>, that year's top jazz album. The single of the title tune became a hit that stayed on the charts for months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid to late 1950s working from Scandinavia, Getz became popular playing <a href="/wiki/Cool_jazz" title="Cool jazz">cool jazz</a> with <a href="/wiki/Horace_Silver" title="Horace Silver">Horace Silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Smith" title="Johnny Smith">Johnny Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Peterson" title="Oscar Peterson">Oscar Peterson</a>, and many others. His first two quintets were notable for their personnel, including <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>'s rhythm section of drummer <a href="/wiki/Roy_Haynes" title="Roy Haynes">Roy Haynes</a>, pianist <a href="/wiki/Al_Haig" title="Al Haig">Al Haig</a> and bassist <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Potter" title="Tommy Potter">Tommy Potter</a>. A 1953 line-up of the <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Gillespie" title="Dizzy Gillespie">Dizzy Gillespie</a>/Stan Getz Sextet featured Gillespie, Getz, Peterson, <a href="/wiki/Herb_Ellis" title="Herb Ellis">Herb Ellis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Brown_(musician)" title="Ray Brown (musician)">Ray Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Roach" title="Max Roach">Max Roach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALLMUSIC_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ALLMUSIC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Returning to the U.S. from Europe in 1961, Getz became a central figure in introducing <a href="/wiki/Bossa_nova" title="Bossa nova">bossa nova</a> music to the American audience. Teaming with guitarist <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Byrd" title="Charlie Byrd">Charlie Byrd</a>, who had just returned from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._State_Department" title="U.S. State Department">U.S. State Department</a> tour of Brazil, Getz recorded <i><a href="/wiki/Jazz_Samba" title="Jazz Samba">Jazz Samba</a></i> in 1962 and it quickly became a hit. Getz won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1963" title="Grammy Awards of 1963">Grammy for Best Jazz Performance of 1963</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Desafinado" title="Desafinado">Desafinado</a>", from the same album. It sold over one million copies, and was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">gold disc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs-4">[4]</a></sup> His second bossa nova album, also recorded in 1962, was <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Band_Bossa_Nova_(Stan_Getz_album)" title="Big Band Bossa Nova (Stan Getz album)">Big Band Bossa Nova</a></i> with composer and arranger <a href="/wiki/Gary_McFarland" title="Gary McFarland">Gary McFarland</a>. As a follow-up, Getz recorded the album, <i><a href="/wiki/Jazz_Samba_Encore!" title="Jazz Samba Encore!">Jazz Samba Encore!</a></i>, with one of the originators of bossa nova, Brazilian guitarist <a href="/wiki/Luiz_Bonf%C3%A1" title="Luiz Bonfá">Luiz Bonfá</a>. It also sold more than a million copies by 1964, giving Getz his second gold disc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>He then recorded the album <i><a href="/wiki/Getz/Gilberto" title="Getz/Gilberto">Getz/Gilberto</a></i>, in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonio_Carlos_Jobim" title="Antonio Carlos Jobim">Antonio Carlos Jobim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Gilberto" title="João Gilberto">João Gilberto</a> and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Astrud_Gilberto" title="Astrud Gilberto">Astrud Gilberto</a>. Their "<a href="/wiki/The_Girl_from_Ipanema" title="The Girl from Ipanema">The Girl from Ipanema</a>" won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>. The piece became one of the most well-known <a href="/wiki/Latin_jazz" title="Latin jazz">Latin jazz</a> tracks. <i>Getz/Gilberto</i> won two Grammys (Best Album and Best Single). A live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Getz/Gilberto_Vol._2" title="Getz/Gilberto Vol. 2">Getz/Gilberto Vol. 2</a></i>, followed, as did <i><a href="/wiki/Getz_Au_Go_Go" title="Getz Au Go Go">Getz Au Go Go</a></i> (1964), a live recording at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cafe_Au_Go_Go" title="Cafe Au Go Go">Cafe Au Go Go</a>. Getz's love affair with Astrud Gilberto brought an end to his musical partnership with her and her husband, and he began to move away from bossa nova and back to cool jazz. While still working with the Gilbertos, he recorded the jazz album <i>Nobody Else but Me</i> (1964), with a new quartet including <a href="/wiki/Vibraphone" title="Vibraphone">vibraphonist</a> <a href="/wiki/Gary_Burton" title="Gary Burton">Gary Burton</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Verve_Records" title="Verve Records">Verve Records</a>, wishing to continue building the Getz brand with bossa nova, refused to release it. It came out 30 years later, after Getz had died.</p>
<p>In 1972, Getz recorded in the fusion idiom with <a href="/wiki/Chick_Corea" title="Chick Corea">Chick Corea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Williams_(drummer)" title="Tony Williams (drummer)">Tony Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Clarke" title="Stanley Clarke">Stanley Clarke</a>, and in this period experimented with an <a href="/wiki/Echoplex" title="Echoplex">Echoplex</a> on his saxophone. He had a cameo in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Exterminator" title="The Exterminator">The Exterminator</a></i> (1980).</p>
<p>In the mid-1980s Getz worked regularly in the San Francisco Bay area and taught at Stanford University as an artist-in-residence at the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Jazz_Workshop" title="Stanford Jazz Workshop">Stanford Jazz Workshop</a> until 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1986, he was inducted into the <i>Down Beat</i> <a href="/wiki/Down_Beat" title="Down Beat">Jazz Hall of Fame</a>. During 1988, Getz worked with <a href="/wiki/Huey_Lewis_and_the_News" title="Huey Lewis and the News">Huey Lewis and the News</a> on their <i><a href="/wiki/Small_World_(Huey_Lewis_and_the_News_album)" title="Small World (Huey Lewis and the News album)">Small World</a></i> album. He played the extended solo on part 2 of the title track, which became a minor hit single.</p>
<p>His tenor saxophone of choice was the <a href="/wiki/Selmer_Mark_VI" title="Selmer Mark VI">Selmer Mark VI</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Getz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="864" data-file-width="617" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg/220px-Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg/330px-Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg/440px-Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dad_Katie_1987_Lincoln_Center2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
With his granddaughter Katie in 1987 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center" title="Lincoln Center">Lincoln Center</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Getz married Beverly Byrne, a vocalist with the <a href="/wiki/Gene_Krupa" title="Gene Krupa">Gene Krupa</a> band, on November 7, 1946 in Los Angeles; they had three children together.</p>
<p>Getz became involved with drugs and alcohol while a teenager. In 1954, he was arrested for attempting to rob a pharmacy to get a <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> fix. As he was being processed in the prison ward of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County-USC_Medical_Center" title="Los Angeles County-USC Medical Center">Los Angeles County-USC Medical Center</a>, Beverly gave birth to their third child one floor below. Immediately after his divorce from Beverly Byrne in 1956, on November 3, 1956 in Nevada, he married Monica Silfverskiöld,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> daughter of Swedish physician and former Olympic medalist <a href="/wiki/Nils_Silfverski%C3%B6ld" title="Nils Silfverskiöld">Nils Silfverskiöld</a>, and had two children with her: Pamela and Nicolaus. Partly to escape Getz's legal problems, the pair lived in Copenhagen, Denmark. Getz was abusive towards his children, and occasionally towards Monica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-divorce-contest_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-divorce-contest-8">[8]</a></sup> Getz filed for divorce in 1981<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-divorce-contest_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-divorce-contest-8">[8]</a></sup> but the petition was not granted until 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-obit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-obit-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1990 Monica Getz petitioned the United States Supreme Court to have their divorce verdict overturned. New York State law required that settlement agreements be heard in trial court instead of family court and Getz claimed that the law discriminated against women who, like her, could be bankrupted by paying trial lawyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-divorce-contest_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-divorce-contest-8">[8]</a></sup> The Supreme Court declined to hear the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-obit_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-obit-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Sims" title="Zoot Sims">Zoot Sims</a>, who had known Getz since their time with Herman, once described him as "a nice bunch of guys", as a consequence of the wide behavioural range of which Getz was capable.</p>
<p>Getz died of <a href="/wiki/Hepatocellular_carcinoma" title="Hepatocellular carcinoma">liver cancer</a> on June 6, 1991. Stan's ashes were poured out of his saxophone case six miles off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Marina_del_Rey,_California" title="Marina del Rey, California">Marina del Rey, California</a> by his grandson, Chris.</p>
<p>In 1998, the Stan Getz Media Center and Library at <a href="/wiki/Berklee_College_of_Music" title="Berklee College of Music">Berklee College of Music</a> was dedicated through a donation from the <a href="/wiki/Herb_Alpert" title="Herb Alpert">Herb Alpert</a> Foundation.</p>
