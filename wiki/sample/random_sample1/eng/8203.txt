<p><b>Stanley "Stan" Marsh</b> is a main character in the animated television series <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i>. He is voiced by and loosely based on series co-creator <a href="/wiki/Trey_Parker" title="Trey Parker">Trey Parker</a>. Stan is one of the show's four central characters, along with his friends <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Broflovski" title="Kyle Broflovski">Kyle Broflovski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_McCormick" title="Kenny McCormick">Kenny McCormick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Cartman" title="Eric Cartman">Eric Cartman</a>. He debuted on television when <i>South Park</i> first aired on August 13, 1997, after having first appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spirit_of_Christmas_(short_film)" title="The Spirit of Christmas (short film)">The Spirit of Christmas</a></i> shorts created by Parker and long-time collaborator <a href="/wiki/Matt_Stone" title="Matt Stone">Matt Stone</a> in 1992 (<i>Jesus vs. Frosty</i>) and 1995 (<i>Jesus vs. Santa</i>).</p>
<p>Stan is a third, then fourth-grade student who commonly has extraordinary experiences not typical of conventional small-town life in his fictional hometown of South Park, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. Stan is generally depicted as friendly, knowledgeable, helpful, and relaxed. He often shares with his best friend Kyle a leadership role as the <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> of the show. Stan is unreserved in expressing his distinct lack of esteem for adults and their influences, as adult South Park residents rarely make use of their critical faculties.</p>
<p>Like the other <i>South Park</i> characters, Stan is animated by computer in a way to emulate the show's original method of <a href="/wiki/Cutout_animation" title="Cutout animation">cutout animation</a>. He also appears in the full-length <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">feature film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park:_Bigger,_Longer_%26_Uncut" title="South Park: Bigger, Longer &amp; Uncut">South Park: Bigger, Longer &amp; Uncut</a></i> (1999), as well as <i>South Park</i>-related media and merchandise. While Parker and Stone portray Stan as having common childlike tendencies, his dialogue is often intended to reflect stances and views on more adult-oriented issues and has been frequently cited in numerous publications by experts in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Marsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stan lives in South Park at 2001 Bonanza Street with his parents <a href="/wiki/Randy_and_Sharon_Marsh" title="Randy and Sharon Marsh">Randy and Sharon Marsh</a>. Randy is a <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geologist</a>, and Sharon is a <a href="/wiki/Secretary" title="Secretary">secretary</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Rhinoplasty" title="Rhinoplasty">rhinoplasty</a> clinic. Stan's family includes his 13-year-old sister Shelly, who bullies and beats him, and his <a href="/wiki/Centenarian" title="Centenarian">centenarian</a> grandfather, Marvin, who calls Stan "Billy" and who previously begged Stan <a href="/wiki/Assisted_suicide" title="Assisted suicide">to help him commit suicide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesmorals_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesmorals-1">[1]</a></sup> Stan attends South Park Elementary as part of <a href="/wiki/Mr._Garrison" title="Mr. Garrison">Mr. Garrison</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fourth_grade" title="Fourth grade">fourth grade</a> class. During the show's first 58 episodes (1997 through the <a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_4)" title="South Park (season 4)">season 4</a> episode "<a href="/wiki/4th_Grade_(South_Park)" title="4th Grade (South Park)">4th Grade</a>" in 2000), Stan and the other main child characters were in the <a href="/wiki/Third_grade" title="Third grade">third grade</a>. His birthday is listed as October 19 (the same day as co-creator <a href="/wiki/Trey_Parker" title="Trey Parker">Trey Parker</a>), 2001 on his <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> page during the <a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_14)" title="South Park (season 14)">season 14</a> episode "<a href="/wiki/You_Have_0_Friends" title="You Have 0 Friends">You Have 0 Friends</a>". Stan is frequently embarrassed and/or annoyed by his father's antics and frequent acts of <a href="/wiki/Public_intoxication" title="Public intoxication">public drunkenness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc3-2">[2]</a></sup> Stan's relationship as nephew to his uncle Jimbo received moderate attention in the show's first two seasons.</p>
<p>Amongst the show's main characters, Kyle is defined as being the only <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> kid, Cartman is recognized by his <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, greed, and bigotry, and Kenny is notable for being poor and frequently dying; as opposed to having a prominent distinguishing trait, Stan is portrayed (in words of the show's official website) as "a normal, average, American, mixed-up kid".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stan is modeled after Parker, while Kyle is modeled after Stone. Stan and Kyle are best friends, and their relationship, which is intended to reflect the real-life friendship between Parker and Stone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-4">[4]</a></sup> is a common topic throughout the series. The two do have their disagreements, but always reconcile without any long-term damage to their friendship. As is the case with his other friends and classmates, Stan is frequently at odds with Cartman, resenting Cartman's behavior and openly mocking his weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Stan also shares a close friendship with Kenny, while Kenny professes that Stan is one of "the best friends a guy could have".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Stan can understand Kenny's muffled voice perfectly, and typically exclaims the catchphrase "Oh my God! They killed Kenny!", following one of Kenny's trademark deaths, allowing Kyle to follow up with "You bastards!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Stan is the only character in the group to have had a steady girlfriend, Wendy Testaburger, and their relationship was a recurring topic in the show's earlier seasons. Despite reconciling and declaring to be a couple again in the <a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_11)" title="South Park (season 11)">season 11</a> (2007) episode "<a href="/wiki/The_List_(South_Park)" title="The List (South Park)">The List</a>" after Wendy had dumped him in the <a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_7)" title="South Park (season 7)">season seven</a> (2003) episode "<a href="/wiki/Raisins_(South_Park)" title="Raisins (South Park)">Raisins</a>", their relationship has received relatively less focus in recent seasons. As a <a href="/wiki/Running_gag" title="Running gag">running gag</a>, a nervous Stan often vomits whenever Wendy approaches to kiss or speak to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortune_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fortune-8">[8]</a></sup> In many episodes, Stan contemplates ethics in beliefs, moral dilemmas, and contentious issues, and will often reflect on the lessons he has attained with a speech that often begins with "You know, I learned something today...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-philo-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Character">Character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Marsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_and_design">Creation and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Marsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Creation and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>An unnamed precursor to Stan first appeared in the first <i>The Spirit of Christmas</i> short, dubbed <i>Jesus vs. Frosty</i>, created by Parker and Stone in 1992 while they were students at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">University of Colorado</a>. The character was composed of <a href="/wiki/Construction_paper" title="Construction paper">construction paper</a> cutouts and animated through the use of <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">stop motion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-method_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-method-10">[10]</a></sup> When asked three years later by friend <a href="/wiki/Brian_Graden" title="Brian Graden">Brian Graden</a> to create another short as a <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> <a href="/wiki/Christmas_card" title="Christmas card">Christmas card</a> that he could send to friends, Parker and Stone created another similarly-animated <i>The Spirit of Christmas</i> short, dubbed <i>Jesus vs. Santa</i>, in which Stan also appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VH1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-11">[11]</a></sup> Stan next appeared on August 13, 1997, when <i>South Park</i> debuted on <a href="/wiki/Comedy_Central" title="Comedy Central">Comedy Central</a> with the episode "<a href="/wiki/Cartman_Gets_an_Anal_Probe" title="Cartman Gets an Anal Probe">Cartman Gets an Anal Probe</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the tradition of the show's <a href="/wiki/Cutout_animation" title="Cutout animation">animation style</a>, Stan is composed of simple geometrical shapes and <a href="/wiki/Primary_color" title="Primary color">primary colors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-method_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-method-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avr_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-avr-13">[13]</a></sup> He is not offered the same free range of motion associated with hand-drawn characters; his character is mostly shown from only one angle, and his movements are animated in an intentionally jerky fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-method_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-method-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avr_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-avr-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-growsup2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-growsup2-14">[14]</a></sup> Ever since the show's second episode, "<a href="/wiki/Weight_Gain_4000" title="Weight Gain 4000">Weight Gain 4000</a>" (<a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_1)" title="South Park (season 1)">season one</a>, 1997), Stan, like all other characters on the show, has been animated with computer software, though he is portrayed to give the impression that the show still utilizes its original technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-method_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-method-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stan is usually depicted in winter attire which consists of a brown jacket, blue denim jeans, red gloves/mittens, and a red-brimmed blue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuque" title="Tuque">knit cap</a> adorned with a decorative red <a href="/wiki/Pom-pom" title="Pom-pom">pom-pom</a>. In the rare instances Stan is seen without his cap, he is shown to have shaggy black hair. He was given his full name in the season one episode "<a href="/wiki/An_Elephant_Makes_Love_to_a_Pig" title="An Elephant Makes Love to a Pig">An Elephant Makes Love to a Pig</a>", sharing his surname of "Marsh" with Parker's paternal step-grandfather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc3_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-abc3-2">[2]</a></sup> While originally voicing Stan without any computer manipulation, Parker now speaks within his normal vocal range while adding a childlike inflection. The recorded audio is then edited with <a href="/wiki/Pro_Tools" title="Pro Tools">Pro Tools</a>, and the pitch is altered to make the voice sound more like that of a fourth grader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sps40_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sps40-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stan's birthday is October 19, which is Trey Parker's birthday, except Stan's year of birth is 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality_and_traits">Personality and traits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Marsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personality and traits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="732" data-file-width="642" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg/125px-TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg/188px-TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg/250px-TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg 2x" width="125"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TreyParkerHWOFApr2013.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stan is modeled after his voice actor, series co-creator <a href="/wiki/Trey_Parker" title="Trey Parker">Trey Parker</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Stan is foul-mouthed (a trait present in his friends as well) as a means for Parker and Stone to display how they claim young boys really talk when they are alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avr_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-avr-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc4_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc4-18">[18]</a></sup> In responding to certain situations, particularly during earlier seasons, Stan often exclaims "Dude, this is pretty fucked up right here". While Stan is cynical and profane, Parker still notes that there is an "underlying sweetness" to the character,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loudlewd_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-loudlewd-19">[19]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine described Stan and his friends as "sometimes cruel but with a core of innocence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-time-4">[4]</a></sup> He is amused by bodily functions and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toilet_humor" title="Toilet humor">toilet humor</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-time-4">[4]</a></sup> and his favorite television personalities are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrance_and_Phillip" title="Terrance and Phillip">Terrance and Phillip</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> duo whose comedy routines on their show-within-the-show revolve substantially around <a href="/wiki/Flatulence_humor" title="Flatulence humor">fart jokes</a>.</p>
<p>Stan is an avid animal lover. He is highly against his uncle Jimbo's hunting, and was also known to commit to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetarian" title="Vegetarian">vegetarianism</a> after feeling compassion for baby calves in a farm, even going as far to hide them in his room to protect them from being slaughtered. Later, he was forced to quit vegetarianism because of a severe illness he developed, however, he still fights for animal rights, becoming a member of <a href="/wiki/People_for_the_Ethical_Treatment_of_Animals" title="People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals">PETA</a> in "<a href="/wiki/Douche_and_Turd" title="Douche and Turd">Douche and Turd</a>" as well as saving whales and dolphins in "<a href="/wiki/Whale_Whores" title="Whale Whores">Whale Whores</a>".</p>
<p>The only adult on the show that Stan consistently trusted was <a href="/wiki/Chef_(South_Park)" title="Chef (South Park)">Chef</a>, the cafeteria worker at his school, as Stan generally holds the rest of the show's adult population in low regard due to their tendency to both behave irrationally when subjected to the scams, cults, and sensationalized media stories of which he is often <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skeptical</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philo_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-philo-9">[9]</a></sup> and engage in hypocritical behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvamerica_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-tvamerica-20">[20]</a></sup> He doubts the legitimacy of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">holistic medicine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cityjournal_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cityjournal-21">[21]</a></sup> declares cults to be dangerous,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and regards those claiming to be psychic mediums as frauds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orst_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-orst-23">[23]</a></sup> specifically by declaring <a href="/wiki/John_Edward" title="John Edward">John Edward</a> to be "the biggest douche in the universe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>After turning ten years old, Stan was diagnosed with "cynicism",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> which in the series is treated as a serious medical disorder where the sufferer sees nothing but <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> instead of people and animals and hear <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">Flatulence</a> noises during music and voices. Stan's friendships with the other main characters ended, his parents divorced and he moved out of his home. This episode formed a <a href="/wiki/Cliffhanger" title="Cliffhanger">cliffhanger</a> and set off widespread speculation that the series was coming to an end,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but the premiere of the second half of the season resolved the arc as Stan was erroneously diagnosed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asperger%27s_Syndrome" title="Asperger's Syndrome">Asperger's Syndrome</a> and discovers <a href="/wiki/Jameson_Irish_Whiskey" title="Jameson Irish Whiskey">Jameson Irish Whiskey</a> cures cynicism. After struggling to repair his life, he finally explains he doesn't want things to go back to normal, when his parents get back together and his life is repaired, although the end of the episode implies Stan may be <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">permanently bound to whiskey</a> to continue an everyday life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact">Cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stan_Marsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="354" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a1/SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg/175px-SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a1/SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg/263px-SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a1/SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg/350px-SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SouthParkStanInRobe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stan being presented as the reincarnation of <a href="/wiki/L._Ron_Hubbard" title="L. Ron Hubbard">L. Ron Hubbard</a> in the season nine episode "<a href="/wiki/Trapped_in_the_Closet_(South_Park)" title="Trapped in the Closet (South Park)">Trapped in the Closet</a>".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2014, Stan was ranked by <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> at third place on their list of "The Top 25 South Park Characters", commenting that he "often acts as the voice of reason in the midst of the show's insane events, and in many ways he's more mature than his father Randy." The website concluded that "his history as one of the more stable and thoughtful characters in the series made him the perfect choice for the voice of Trey and Matt's own creative/professional frustrations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_top_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_top-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stan frequently offers his perspective on <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usask_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-usask-29">[29]</a></sup> and he was at the center of one of the most controversial episodes of the series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnnemmy_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnnemmy-30">[30]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Trapped_in_the_Closet_(South_Park)" title="Trapped in the Closet (South Park)">Trapped in the Closet</a>" (<a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_9)" title="South Park (season 9)">season nine</a>, 2005), where he was recognized as the reincarnation of <a href="/wiki/Scientology" title="Scientology">Scientology</a> founder <a href="/wiki/L._Ron_Hubbard" title="L. Ron Hubbard">L. Ron Hubbard</a> before denouncing the church as nothing more than "a big fat global scam".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the show's nineteen seasons, Stan has addressed other topics such as <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-windy_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-windy-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sydney_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sydney-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hate_crime_laws_in_the_United_States" title="Hate crime laws in the United States">hate crime legislation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesconserv_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesconserv-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cityjournal_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-cityjournal-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">parenting</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seriously_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-seriously-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigration" title="Illegal immigration">illegal immigration</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statesman_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-statesman-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">voting</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philo2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-philo2-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seriously_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-seriously-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_relations" title="Race relations">race relations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boston_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-boston-38">[38]</a></sup> His commentary on these issues have been interpreted as statements Parker and Stone are attempting to make to the viewing public,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seriously_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-seriously-35">[35]</a></sup> and these opinions have been subject to much critical analysis in the media and literary world. The book <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park_and_Philosophy:_You_Know,_I_Learned_Something_Today" title="South Park and Philosophy: You Know, I Learned Something Today">South Park and Philosophy: You Know, I Learned Something Today</a></i> includes an essay in which <a href="/wiki/East_Carolina_University" title="East Carolina University">East Carolina University</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> professor Henry Jacoby compares Stan's actions and reasoning within the show to the philosophical teachings of <a href="/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford" title="William Kingdon Clifford">William Kingdon Clifford</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blackwell_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-blackwell-39">[39]</a></sup> and another essay by <a href="/wiki/Southern_Illinois_University" title="Southern Illinois University">Southern Illinois University</a> philosophy professor John S. Gray which references Stan's decision to not vote for either candidate for a school mascot in the <a href="/wiki/South_Park_(season_8)" title="South Park (season 8)">season eight</a> (2004) episode "<a href="/wiki/Douche_and_Turd" title="Douche and Turd">Douche and Turd</a>" when describing <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> and the claimed pitfalls of a <a href="/wiki/Two-party_system" title="Two-party system">two-party system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blackwell_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-blackwell-39">[39]</a></sup> Essays in the books <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park_and_Philosophy:_Bigger,_Longer,_and_More_Penetrating" title="South Park and Philosophy: Bigger, Longer, and More Penetrating">South Park and Philosophy: Bigger, Longer, and More Penetrating</a></i>, <i>Blame Canada! South Park and Contemporary Culture</i>, and <i>Taking South Park Seriously</i> have also analyzed Stan's perspectives within the framework of popular philosophical, theological, and political concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seriously_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-seriously-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmp_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmp-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blame_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-blame-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
