<p><b>Stephanie Jayne "Steph" Cook</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Member of the Order of the British Empire">MBE</a> (born 7 February 1972)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsreference_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sportsreference-1">[1]</a></sup> is a British retired <a href="/wiki/Modern_pentathlon" title="Modern pentathlon">modern pentathlete</a>. She was the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> champion at this event in 2000.</p>
<p>Cook was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irvine,_Scotland" title="Irvine, Scotland">Irvine</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Ayrshire" title="North Ayrshire">North Ayrshire</a>, Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsreference_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sportsreference-1">[1]</a></sup> She was educated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedford_High_School_(Bedfordshire)" title="Bedford High School (Bedfordshire)">Bedford High School</a>; The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perse_School_for_Girls" title="Perse School for Girls">Perse School for Girls</a>; <a href="/wiki/Peterhouse,_Cambridge" title="Peterhouse, Cambridge">Peterhouse, Cambridge</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_College,_Oxford" title="Lincoln College, Oxford">Lincoln College, Oxford</a>, where she read medicine.</p>
<p>Having rowed at Cambridge she took up modern pentathlon whilst completing her course in clinical medicine at Oxford. She was president of the Oxford University Modern Pentathlon Association in 1995–1996. She won the women's individual title in the <a href="/wiki/Varsity_match" title="Varsity match">Varsity match</a> against Cambridge in 1997.</p>
<p>Although widely reported as "having put her medical career on hold", she was supported through her training from 1998 to 2000 by Consultant Surgeon <a href="/wiki/Mark_Whiteley" title="Mark Whiteley">Mark Whiteley</a> who funded a research job for her in Guildford – during which she published 3 papers with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She paid him tribute during the TV show <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/This_is_Your_Life" title="This is Your Life">This is Your Life</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> She went on to she won the gold medal in the <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> <a href="/wiki/2000_Summer_Olympics" title="2000 Summer Olympics">2000 Olympics</a> in the women's <a href="/wiki/Modern_pentathlon" title="Modern pentathlon">modern pentathlon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsreference_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-sportsreference-1">[1]</a></sup> the first time that the event was included in the games. She went on to win individual World and European titles in 2001 before retiring from the sport. Her particular strength was running.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsreference_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-sportsreference-1">[1]</a></sup> j</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steph_Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2001 New Year Honours List Cook was appointed as a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Most_Excellent_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Member of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire">Order of The British Empire</a> for services to Modern Pentathlon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2008 she was awarded an Honorary Degree (Doctor of Medicine) from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bath" title="University of Bath">University of Bath</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
