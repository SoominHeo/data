<p><b>Strand</b> (or <b>the Strand</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup>) is a major thoroughfare in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_London" title="Central London">Central London</a>. It runs just over <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> mile (1,200 m) from <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a> eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Temple_Bar,_London" title="Temple Bar, London">Temple Bar</a>, where the road becomes <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a> inside the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>, and is part of the <a href="/wiki/A4_road_(England)" title="A4 road (England)">A4</a>, a main road running west from inner London.</p>
<p>The road's name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>strond</i>, meaning the edge of a river, as it historically ran alongside the north bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>. The street was popular with the British upper classes between the 12th and 17th centuries, with many historically important mansions being built between the Strand and the river. These included <a href="/wiki/Essex_House_(London)" title="Essex House (London)">Essex House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arundel_House" title="Arundel House">Arundel House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset_House" title="Somerset House">Somerset House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Palace" title="Savoy Palace">Savoy Palace</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durham_House_(London)" title="Durham House (London)">Durham House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cecil_House" title="Cecil House">Cecil House</a>. The aristocracy moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_End,_London" title="West End, London">West End</a> over the 17th century, following which the Strand became well known for coffee shops, restaurants and taverns. The street was a centre point for theatre and <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> during the 19th century, and several venues remain on the Strand. At the east end of the street are two historic churches: <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_le_Strand" title="St Mary le Strand">St Mary le Strand</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Clement_Danes" title="St Clement Danes">St Clement Danes</a>.</p>
<p>Several authors, poets and philosophers have lived on or near the Strand, including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>. The street has been commemorated in the song "Let's All Go Down the Strand", now recognised as a typical piece of <a href="/wiki/Cockney" title="Cockney">Cockney</a> music hall.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strand,_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The street is the main link between the two cities of <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">Westminster</a> and <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882-4">[3]</a></sup> It runs eastward from <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>, parallel to the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Temple_Bar,_London" title="Temple Bar, London">Temple Bar</a> which is the boundary between the two city districts; the road ahead being <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gmap_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gmap-1">[1]</a></sup> Traffic travelling eastbound follows a short crescent around <a href="/wiki/Aldwych" title="Aldwych">Aldwych</a>, connected at both ends to the Strand. The road marks the southern boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a> district<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008213.E2.80.93214_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008213.E2.80.93214-5">[4]</a></sup> and forms part of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Northbank,_London&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Northbank, London (page does not exist)">Northbank</a> business improvement district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name was first recorded in 1002 as <i>strondway</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882-4">[3]</a></sup> then in 1185 as <i>Stronde</i> and in 1220 as <i>la Stranda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills2010237_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills2010237-8">[7]</a></sup> It is formed from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word 'strond', meaning the edge of a river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882-4">[3]</a></sup> Initially it referred to the shallow bank of the once much wider Thames, before the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Embankment" title="Victoria Embankment">Victoria Embankment</a>. The name was later applied to the road itself. In the 13th century it was known as 'Densemanestret' or 'street of the Danes', referring to the community of <a href="/wiki/Danes" title="Danes">Danes</a> in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills2010237_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills2010237-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> stations were once named Strand: a <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly_line" title="Piccadilly line">Piccadilly line</a> station (which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Aldwych_tube_station" title="Aldwych tube station">Aldwych station</a>) that operated between 1907 and 1994<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003198_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003198-9">[8]</a></sup> and a former <a href="/wiki/Northern_line" title="Northern line">Northern line</a> station which today forms part of <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross_tube_station" title="Charing Cross tube station">Charing Cross station</a>. 'Strand Bridge' was the name given to <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Bridge" title="Waterloo Bridge">Waterloo Bridge</a> during its construction; it was renamed for its official opening on the second anniversary of the coalition victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008991_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008991-10">[9]</a></sup> London Bus routes <a href="/wiki/London_Buses_route_6" title="London Buses route 6">6</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Buses_route_23" title="London Buses route 23">23</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Buses_route_139" title="London Buses route 139">139</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Buses_route_176" title="London Buses route 176">176</a> all run along the Strand, as do numerous night bus services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strand,_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="446" data-file-width="712" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_%281%29.jpg/250px-1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_%281%29.jpg/375px-1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_%281%29.jpg/500px-1593_Norden%27s_map_of_Westminster_surveyed_and_publ_1593_%281%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>During <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>, what is now the Strand was part of the route to <a href="/wiki/Silchester" title="Silchester">Silchester</a>, known as "Iter VIII" on the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Itinerary" title="Antonine Itinerary">Antonine Itinerary</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> and which later became known by the name <a href="/wiki/Akeman_Street" title="Akeman Street">Akeman Street</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> It was briefly part of a trading town called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lundenwic" title="Lundenwic">Lundenwic</a> that developed around 600 AD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark-15">[14]</a></sup> and stretched from <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a> to <a href="/wiki/Aldwych" title="Aldwych">Aldwych</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lund_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lund-16">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a> gradually moved the settlement into the old Roman town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_London" title="Roman London">Londinium</a> from around 886 AD onwards, leaving no mark of the old town, and the area returned to fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clark-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the Strand became the principal route between the separate settlements of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> (the civil and commercial centre) and the royal <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a> (the national political centre). In the archaeological record, there is considerable evidence of occupation to the north of Aldwych, but much along the former foreshore has been covered by rubble from the demolition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tudor_era" title="Tudor era">Tudor</a> Somerset Place, a former royal residence, to create a large platform for the building of the first <a href="/wiki/Somerset_House" title="Somerset House">Somerset House</a>, in the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onl_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-onl-17">[16]</a></sup> The landmark Eleanor's Cross was built in the 13th century at the western end of the Strand at Charing Cross by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a> commemorating his wife <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Castile" title="Eleanor of Castile">Eleanor of Castile</a>. It was demolished in 1647 by the request of Parliament during the <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First English Civil War</a>, but reconstructed in 1865.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The west part of the Strand was in the parish of <a href="/wiki/St_Martin_in_the_Fields_(parish)" title="St Martin in the Fields (parish)">St Martin in the Fields</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> and in the east it extended into the parishes of <a href="/wiki/St_Clement_Danes_(parish)" title="St Clement Danes (parish)">St Clement Danes</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_le_Strand_(parish)" title="St Mary le Strand (parish)">St Mary le Strand</a>. Most of its length was in the <a href="/wiki/City_and_Liberty_of_Westminster" title="City and Liberty of Westminster">Liberty of Westminster</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> although part of the eastern section in St Clement Danes was in the <a href="/wiki/Ossulstone" title="Ossulstone">Ossulstone</a> hundred of Middlesex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Precinct_of_the_Savoy" title="Precinct of the Savoy">precinct of the Savoy</a>, located approximately where the approach to <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Bridge" title="Waterloo Bridge">Waterloo Bridge</a> is now, had the Strand as its northern boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> All of these parishes and places became part of the <a href="/wiki/Strand_District_(Metropolis)" title="Strand District (Metropolis)">Strand District</a> in 1855, except St Martin in the Fields which was governed separately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> The Strand District Board of Works was based at No. 22, <a href="/wiki/Tavistock_Street" title="Tavistock Street">Tavistock Street</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> Strand District was abolished in October 1900 and became part of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Westminster" title="Metropolitan Borough of Westminster">Metropolitan Borough of Westminster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaces">Palaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strand,_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Palaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>From the 12th century onwards, large mansions lined the Strand including several palaces and <a href="/wiki/Townhouse" title="Townhouse">townhouses</a> inhabited by bishops and royal courtiers, mainly located on the south side, with their own river gates and landings directly on the Thames. The road was poorly maintained, with many pits and sloughs, and a paving order was issued in 1532 to improve traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>What later became <a href="/wiki/Essex_House_(London)" title="Essex House (London)">Essex House</a> on the Strand was originally an Outer Temple of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> in the 11th century. In 1313, ownership passed to the Knights of St John. <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> gave the house to <a href="/wiki/William_Paget,_1st_Baron_Paget" title="William Paget, 1st Baron Paget">William, Baron Paget</a> in the early 16th century. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dudley,_1st_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Robert Dudley, 1st Earl of Leicester">Robert Dudley, Earl of Leicester</a>, rebuilt the house in 1563, originally calling it Leicester House. It was renamed Essex House after being inherited by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Devereux,_2nd_Earl_of_Essex" title="Robert Devereux, 2nd Earl of Essex">Robert Devereux, 2nd Earl of Essex</a>, in 1588. It was demolished around 1674 and Essex Street, leading up to the Strand, was built on the location by property speculator <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Barbon" title="Nicholas Barbon">Nicholas Barbon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008276_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008276-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arundel_House" title="Arundel House">Arundel House</a> was originally the town house of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Bath_and_Wells" title="Bishop of Bath and Wells">Bishops of Bath and Wells</a>. It was owned by <a href="/wiki/William_FitzWilliam,_1st_Earl_of_Southampton" title="William FitzWilliam, 1st Earl of Southampton">William FitzWilliam, 1st Earl of Southampton</a> between 1539 and his death in 1542, with ownership passing to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Seymour,_1st_Baron_Seymour_of_Sudeley" title="Thomas Seymour, 1st Baron Seymour of Sudeley">Thomas Seymour</a> in 1545. After Seymour was executed in 1549, the property was sold to <a href="/wiki/Henry_FitzAlan,_19th_Earl_of_Arundel" title="Henry FitzAlan, 19th Earl of Arundel">Henry FitzAlan, Earl of Arundel</a>, and was owned by the Earldom for much of the 16th and 17th century. In 1666, it became the meeting place of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> following the destruction of the previous venue due to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a>. The house was demolished in 1678 and Arundel Street, adjoining the Strand, was built on the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.200829.E2.80.9330_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.200829.E2.80.9330-28">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Somerset_House" title="Somerset House">Somerset House</a> was built by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset">Edward Seymour, Duke of Somerset</a>, regent of England from 1547 to 1549, demolishing three inns and the church of the Nativity of Our Lady and the Innocents in the process. After Somerset was executed in 1552, it became an occasional residence for <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Princess Elizabeth</a>. On her ascension to the throne in 1558, she returned part of the house to Seymour's family (with ownership passing to his son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Earl_of_Hertford" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Earl of Hertford">Edward Seymour, 1st Earl of Hertford</a>) with the remainder being an occasional meeting place for the Royal Society. After Elizabeth's death in 1603, it was owned by <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Denmark" title="Anne of Denmark">Anne of Denmark</a>, wife of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI and I</a>. The building was renamed Denmark House in commemoration of Anne's brother, <a href="/wiki/Christian_IV_of_Denmark" title="Christian IV of Denmark">Christian IV of Denmark</a>. Following James' death in 1625, his body lay there intestate for a month. It was taken over by Parliament in 1645 following the Civil War, renaming it back to Somerset House.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008849_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008849-29">[28]</a></sup> It had an irregular series of owners and residents for much of the 18th century until its demolition in 1775. The house was rebuilt as a series of government buildings. The Stamp Office, later to become the <a href="/wiki/Inland_Revenue" title="Inland Revenue">Inland Revenue</a> was established in Somerset House in 1789. In the late 20th century, a number of art galleries were set up on vacant parts, including the <a href="/wiki/Courtauld_Institute_of_Art" title="Courtauld Institute of Art">Courtauld Institute of Art</a> and the <a href="/wiki/The_Dickson_Poon_School_of_Law" title="The Dickson Poon School of Law">King's College London School of Law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008849_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008849-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Savoy_Palace" title="Savoy Palace">Savoy Palace</a> was the London residence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt,_1st_Duke_of_Lancaster" title="John of Gaunt, 1st Duke of Lancaster">John of Gaunt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">King Richard II</a>'s uncle and the nation's power broker. In the 14th century the Savoy was the most magnificent nobleman's mansion in England. During the <a href="/wiki/Peasants%27_Revolt" title="Peasants' Revolt">Peasants' Revolt</a> of 1381, rebels, led by <a href="/wiki/Wat_Tyler" title="Wat Tyler">Wat Tyler</a>, inflamed by opposition to the poll tax promoted by John of Gaunt, systematically demolished the Savoy and everything in it. In 1512 it was rebuilt as the Savoy Hospital for the poor. It gradually fell into dereliction and was divided into multiple tenancies. It was demolished in 1816–20 to build the approach road to <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Bridge" title="Waterloo Bridge">Waterloo Bridge</a>. eventually being demolished in the 19th century. The <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Hotel" title="Savoy Hotel">Savoy Hotel</a> now occupies this site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008825_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008825-30">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durham_House_(London)" title="Durham House (London)">Durham House</a>, the historic London residence of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a>, was built circa 1345 and demolished in the mid-17th century. Having once been the home of <a href="/wiki/Anne_Boleyn" title="Anne Boleyn">Anne Boleyn</a>, it had become derelict by the mid-17th century and was demolished in 1660. Durham Street and the Adelphi Buildings were built on its site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008255_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008255-31">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/York_House,_Strand" title="York House, Strand">York House</a> was built as the London residence for the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Norwich" title="Bishop of Norwich">Bishop of Norwich</a> not later than 1237. At the time of the Reformation it was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">King Henry VIII</a> and came to be known as York House when he granted it to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> in 1556. In the 1620s it was acquired by the royal favourite <a href="/wiki/George_Villiers,_1st_Duke_of_Buckingham" title="George Villiers, 1st Duke of Buckingham">George Villiers, 1st Duke of Buckingham</a>, and after an interlude during the Civil War it was returned to <a href="/wiki/George_Villiers,_2nd_Duke_of_Buckingham" title="George Villiers, 2nd Duke of Buckingham">George Villiers, 2nd Duke of Buckingham</a>, who sold it to developers in 1672. It was then demolished and new streets and buildings built on the site, including George Street, <a href="/wiki/Villiers_Street" title="Villiers Street">Villiers Street</a>, Duke Street, Of Alley, and Buckingham Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.20081036_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.20081036-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cecil_House" title="Cecil House">Cecil House</a>, also called Exeter House or Burghley House, built in the 16th century by <a href="/wiki/William_Cecil,_1st_Baron_Burghley" title="William Cecil, 1st Baron Burghley">Lord Burghley</a> as an expansion of an existing Tudor house. Exeter House was demolished in 1676 and <a href="/wiki/Exeter_Exchange" title="Exeter Exchange">Exeter Exchange</a> built on the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008283_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008283-33">[32]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Menagerie" title="Menagerie">menagerie</a> was built on the upper floors in 1773, which was later run by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Cross_(zoo_proprietor)" title="Edward Cross (zoo proprietor)">Edward Cross</a>, who housed lions, tigers, monkeys and hippopotami. In 1826, an elephant, <a href="/wiki/Chunee" title="Chunee">Chunee</a>, nearly broke free from its cage and had to be destroyed; the skeleton was later put on display. The exchange was demolished in 1829, with the menagerie moving to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrey_Zoological_Gardens" title="Surrey Zoological Gardens">Surrey Zoological Gardens</a>, and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Exeter_Hall" title="Exeter Hall">Exeter Hall</a>, noted for its <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical</a> meetings. This was demolished in 1907, and the site is now occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Strand_Palace_Hotel" title="Strand Palace Hotel">Strand Palace Hotel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008282.E2.80.93283_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008282.E2.80.93283-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other significant palaces along the Strand include Worcester House, formerly the Inn, or residence, of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Carlisle" title="Bishop of Carlisle">Bishop of Carlisle</a>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.20081032_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.20081032-35">[34]</a></sup> Salisbury House, used for royal lodgings in the 15th and 16th centuries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008820_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008820-36">[35]</a></sup> Hungerford House, which was demolished and replaced, in turn, by <a href="/wiki/Hungerford_Market" title="Hungerford Market">Hungerford Market</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross_railway_station" title="Charing Cross railway station">Charing Cross station</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland_House" title="Northumberland House">Northumberland House</a>, a large <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_architecture" title="Jacobean architecture">Jacobean</a> mansion, the historic London residence of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Northumberland" title="Duke of Northumberland">Dukes of Northumberland</a>; built in 1605 and demolished in 1874. <a href="/wiki/Northumberland_Avenue" title="Northumberland Avenue">Northumberland Avenue</a> now occupies the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008593_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008593-37">[36]</a></sup> The official residence of the Secretary of State, next door at No. 1 the Strand, became the first numbered address in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003197_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003197-38">[37]</a></sup> Apart from the rebuilt Somerset House, all of these buildings have been demolished and replaced from the 17th century onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup> A New Exchange was built on part of the gardens of Durham House, in 1608-9, facing the Strand. This high-class shopping centre enjoyed considerable popularity but was eventually destroyed in 1737.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008539_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008539-39">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the 17th century, many of the grand mansions on the Strand were demolished as the aristocracy moved to the <a href="/wiki/West_End_of_London" title="West End of London">West End</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup> The Duck and Drake tavern on Strand was famed as a venue for the conspirators involved in the <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder_Plot" title="Gunpowder Plot">Gunpowder Plot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003197_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003197-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> In the time of the Civil War, the Nag's Head tavern was the venue of a meeting between <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ireton" title="Henry Ireton">Henry Ireton</a> and some of the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a> which resulted in the production of <i>Remonstrance of the Army</i>, demanding the abolition of the monarchy and the trial of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 18th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_house" title="Coffee house">coffee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steakhouse" title="Steakhouse">chop houses</a> were established on the street; <a href="/wiki/Twinings" title="Twinings">Twinings</a> was established at No. 206 in 1706 by Thomas Twining, supplier of tea to Queen Anne. The company claims to be the oldest ratepayer in Westminster. The Grecian Coffee House ran from around 1702 to 1803, while Tom's ran from 1706 to around 1775. Though these premises were well-known, the alleyways around the Strand were regular haunts for pickpockets and prostitutes during this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup> The Rose Tavern, at the eastern end of the street, was frequented by lawyers during the 18th century. It was later demolished and became Thanet Place. The Crown and Anchor was the main meeting place for the Catholic Association and helped established the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Relief_Act_1829" title="Roman Catholic Relief Act 1829">Roman Catholic Relief Act 1829</a>. It was later used for Catholic politicians such as <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O'Connell">Daniel O'Connell</a> to address constituents. The original premises burned down in 1854, but was rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onl_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-onl-17">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Simpson%27s-in-the-Strand" title="Simpson's-in-the-Strand">Simpson's-in-the-Strand</a> originally started at No. 100 in 1828 as a smoking and dining club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> It later became a restaurant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008882-4">[3]</a></sup> The Strand was also notable in the 18th century as a centre for the British book trade, with numerous printers and publishers along the street. The prominent bookseller <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Millar" title="Andrew Millar">Andrew Millar</a> is an example of one of the most successful publishers who owned a shop there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a> was founded in 1828. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strand_Campus" title="Strand Campus">Strand Campus</a>, based next to the church of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Mary-le-Strand" title="St Mary-le-Strand">St Mary-le-Strand</a>, was designed by Robert Smirke and constructed in 1829–31. <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_Hospital" title="King's College Hospital">King's College Hospital</a> opened as a branch of the college in 1840, and became a constituent part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> in 1908. The current campus building was constructed in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008462_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008462-44">[43]</a></sup> In 2015, the college acquired Strand House, Bush House and other buildings in the Aldwych Quarter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Courts_of_Justice" title="Royal Courts of Justice">Royal Courts of Justice</a>, at the eastern end of the Strand, was designed in the mid-1860s by G.E. Street as a replacement for the older courts at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westminster_Hall" title="Westminster Hall">Westminster Hall</a>, though construction was so delayed that he died shortly before <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> opened the courts in 1882. The West Green extension to the courts opened in 1911, while the Queen's Building opened in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008716.E2.80.937_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008716.E2.80.937-47">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>The architect <a href="/wiki/John_Nash_(architect)" title="John Nash (architect)">John Nash</a> redeveloped the western end of the Strand in the 1830s, including the construction of <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross_Hospital" title="Charing Cross Hospital">Charing Cross Hospital</a>. In the 19th century, much of the Strand was rebuilt and the houses to the south no longer backed onto the Thames, separated from the river by the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Embankment" title="Victoria Embankment">Victoria Embankment</a> constructed in 1865–70 and reclaiming 37 acres (15 ha) of land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008974_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008974-48">[47]</a></sup> The street became well known for theatres, and at one point contained more than any other; including the Tivoli Music Hall at No. 65, the <a href="/wiki/Adelphi_Theatre" title="Adelphi Theatre">Adelphi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaiety_Theatre,_London" title="Gaiety Theatre, London">Gaiety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Theatre" title="Savoy Theatre">Savoy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry%27s_Theatre" title="Terry's Theatre">Terry's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville_Theatre" title="Vaudeville Theatre">Vaudeville</a>. In the 21st century, only the Adelphi, Vaudeville and Savoy remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008316.2C883.2C991_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008316.2C883.2C991-49">[48]</a></sup> The Piccadilly branch line from Holborn to Aldwych was built partly to serve theatre traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003198_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003198-9">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Coal_Hole,_Strand" title="Coal Hole, Strand">Coal Hole</a> tavern was founded at No. 91 in the early 19th century, and frequented by coal-heavers working on the Thames.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008198_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008198-50">[49]</a></sup> The impresario <a href="/wiki/Renton_Nicholson" title="Renton Nicholson">Renton Nicholson</a> held song-and-supper evenings at the inn, featuring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tableaux_vivants" title="Tableaux vivants">tableaux vivants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> The actor <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Kean" title="Edmund Kean">Edmund Kean</a> established the Wolf Club at the venue, which is now commemorated by the Wolf Room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008198_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008198-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross_railway_station" title="Charing Cross railway station">Charing Cross railway station</a> was built on the Strand in 1864, providing a boat train service to Europe, which stimulated the growth of hotels in the area to cater for travellers. These included the Charing Cross Hotel, attached to the station itself. Today, there are several luggage outlets and tourist agents on the Strand, as well as old postage stamp dealers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003200_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003200-52">[51]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Philately" title="Philately">philatelist</a> <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Gibbons" title="Stanley Gibbons">Stanley Gibbons</a> opened a shop at No. 435 in 1891. It moved to No. 391 in 1893, and is now currently based at No. 399.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Strand_Palace_Hotel" title="Strand Palace Hotel">Strand Palace Hotel</a> was designed by F.J.Wills and constructed in 1925–30. The entrance was rebuilt in 1968, with the original being moved to the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008884_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008884-54">[53]</a></sup> The Strand underwent extensive redevelopment in the mid-20th century. The length of road from St Mary's eastwards up to St Clement's was widened in 1900, subsuming the former Holywell Street which forked from the Strand and ran parallel with it to the north, leaving the two churches of St Mary Le Strand and St.Clement Danes as islands in the centre of the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> Gaiety Theatre was demolished, to be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Citibank_House" title="Citibank House">Citibank House</a>, while <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Villiers_House&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Villiers House (page does not exist)">Villiers House</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_South_Wales_House&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New South Wales House (page does not exist)">New South Wales House</a> were both built in 1957–59. New South Wales House was subsequently demolished in 1996 and replaced by an office block.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup> In 1998, a statue of <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a> was built at the junction of Adelaide Street and Duncannon Street, adjoining the western end of the Strand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>

<p>The church of <a href="/wiki/St_Clement_Danes" title="St Clement Danes">St Clement Danes</a> is believed to date from the 9th century. The name may have come from <a href="/wiki/Harold_Harefoot" title="Harold Harefoot">Harold Harefoot</a>, a Danish king who ruled England around 1035–40 and is buried in the church, or from a place of refuge for Danes after the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>. It was transferred to the Order of the Knights Templar by <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> in 1189. It survived the Great Fire in 1666, but was declared unsafe and rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a> in 1679. The building was damaged during <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a> in 1941, gutting much of the interior, and was rebuilt in 1958 by Sam Lloyd, since when it has served as the central church of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008752.E2.80.93753_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008752.E2.80.93753-58">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> The church is one of two possible origins for the "St Clement's" in the <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">nursery rhyme</a> "<a href="/wiki/Oranges_and_Lemons" title="Oranges and Lemons">Oranges and Lemons</a>", though more contemporary accounts suggest <a href="/wiki/St_Clement%27s,_Eastcheap" title="St Clement's, Eastcheap">St Clement's, Eastcheap</a> in the City of London to be more likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008753_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008753-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/St_Mary_le_Strand" title="St Mary le Strand">St Mary le Strand</a> was designed by James Gibbs and completed in 1717, to replace a previous church demolished to make way for Somerset House. It was the first building to be designed by Gibbs, who was influenced by Wren and <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>. The column on top of the church was originally designed to support a statue of <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a>, but was replaced with a spire following Anne's death in 1714. It survived the Blitz but was in poor shape until <a href="/wiki/John_Betjeman" title="John Betjeman">John Betjeman</a> led a campaign to restore it in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008792_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008792-62">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Essex_Street_Chapel" title="Essex Street Chapel">Essex Street Chapel</a>, the birthplace of British <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a>, abuts onto the Strand. The original chapel was built in 1774, but damaged in the Blitz. It was restored after the war, and now serves as the <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_Unitarian_and_Free_Christian_Churches" title="General Assembly of Unitarian and Free Christian Churches">denominational headquarters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008277_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008277-63">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strand,_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The print seller <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Ackermann" title="Rudolph Ackermann">Rudolph Ackermann</a> lived and worked at No. 101 The Strand between 1797 and 1827. His shop was one of the first to have <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a> fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, The Strand became a newly fashionable address and many avant-garde writers and thinkers gathered here, among them <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Thomas Carlyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> and the scientist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Huxley" title="Thomas Henry Huxley">Thomas Henry Huxley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup> No. 142 was the home of radical publisher and physician <a href="/wiki/John_Chapman_(publisher)" title="John Chapman (publisher)">John Chapman</a>, who published contemporary authors from this house during the 1850s and edited the journal <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westminster_Review" title="Westminster Review">Westminster Review</a></i> from 1851.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a> lived at No. 142 between 1851 and 1855.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883_26-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008883-26">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a> regularly travelled along the Strand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> and a King's College building named after her is in nearby <a href="/wiki/Kingsway,_London" title="Kingsway, London">Kingsway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strand,_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Strand is the subject of a famous <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> song "Let's All Go Down the Strand", composed by Harry Castling and <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Wainwright_Murphy" title="Clarence Wainwright Murphy">C. W. Murphy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup> The song opens with a group of tourists, staying the night at Trafalgar Square about to embark for <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup> The chorus of "Let's all go down the Strand – have a banana"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[b]</a></sup> is now recognised as a stereotypical part of <a href="/wiki/Cockney" title="Cockney">Cockney</a> music hall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003198_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003198-9">[8]</a></sup> and parodied by English comedian <a href="/wiki/Bill_Bailey" title="Bill Bailey">Bill Bailey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup> English composer <a href="/wiki/Percy_Grainger" title="Percy Grainger">Percy Grainger</a> used the name for his 1911 piano trio <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handel_in_the_Strand" title="Handel in the Strand">Handel in the Strand</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a> has written about the Strand in several of her essays, including "Street Haunting: A London Adventure,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup> and the novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mrs._Dalloway" title="Mrs. Dalloway">Mrs. Dalloway</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a> alludes to the Strand in his 1905 poem "At Graduation" and in his 1922 poem "The Waste Land" (part III, The Fire Sermon, v. 258: "and along the Strand, up Queen Victoria Street").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Masefield" title="John Masefield">John Masefield</a> also refers to a "jostling in the Strand" in his poem "On Growing Old".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup> The street name also figures in the 1958 poem "Buses on the Strand" by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Percival_Lister" title="Richard Percival Lister">Richard Percival Lister</a>, which in 2013 was featured as part of <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_London" title="Transport for London">TFL</a>’s "Poems on the Underground" scheme, appearing in tube carriages all over London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Betjeman" title="John Betjeman">John Betjeman</a> used the title of the song for a television documentary made for <a href="/wiki/Associated-Rediffusion" title="Associated-Rediffusion">Associated-Rediffusion</a> in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[77]</a></sup> The same year, Margaret Williams used it for a stage comedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[78]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Strand_Magazine" title="The Strand Magazine">The Strand Magazine</a></i>, which began publishing in 1891, was named after the street. A <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a> arts and culture radio series was called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Strand_(radio)" title="The Strand (radio)">The Strand</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bush_House" title="Bush House">Bush House</a>, situated on the Strand, was home to the World Service between 1941 and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008117_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinreb_et_al.2008117-82">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>The standard British <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a> board has the Strand in a group with two roads it connects to; <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2003185_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2003185-83">[81]</a></sup></p>
