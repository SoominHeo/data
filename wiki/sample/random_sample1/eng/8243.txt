<p><i><b>Striga</b></i>, commonly known as <b>witchweed</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>witchers weed</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_plant" title="Parasitic plant">parasitic plants</a> that occur naturally in parts of Africa, Asia, and Australia. It is in the family <a href="/wiki/Orobanchaceae" title="Orobanchaceae">Orobanchaceae</a>. Some species are serious <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> of cereal crops, with the greatest effects being in savanna agriculture in Africa. It also causes considerable crop losses in other regions, including other tropical and subtropical crops in its native range and in the Americas.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Witchweeds are characterized by bright-green stems and leaves and small, brightly colored and attractive flowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> They are <a href="/wiki/Obligate_parasite" title="Obligate parasite">obligate hemiparasites</a> of roots and require a living host for germination and initial development, though they can then survive on their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wainwright_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The genus is classified in the family <a href="/wiki/Orobanchaceae" title="Orobanchaceae">Orobanchaceae</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> although older classifications place it in the <a href="/wiki/Scrophulariaceae" title="Scrophulariaceae">Scrophulariaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of species is uncertain, but may exceed 40 by some counts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wainwright_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hosts_and_symptoms">Hosts and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hosts and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although most species of <i>Striga</i> are not pathogens that affect human agriculture, some species have devastating effects upon crops, particularly those planted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence_farm" title="Subsistence farm">subsistence farmers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Crops most commonly affected are corn, sorghum, rice and sugarcane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Three species cause the most damage: <i>Striga asiatica</i>, <i>S. gesnerioides</i>, and <i>S. hermonthica</i>.</p>
<p>Witchweed parasitizes maize, millet, sorghum, sugarcane, rice, legumes, and a range of weedy grasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> It is capable of significantly reducing yields, in some cases wiping out the entire crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Host plant symptoms, such as <a href="/wiki/Stunt_(botany)" title="Stunt (botany)">stunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilting" title="Wilting">wilting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chlorosis" title="Chlorosis">chlorosis</a>, are similar to those seen from severe drought damage, nutrient deficiency, and vascular disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-text_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-text-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle">Lifecycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lifecycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Striga_root_connections.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2963" data-file-width="2047" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Striga_root_connections.jpg/220px-Striga_root_connections.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Striga_root_connections.jpg/330px-Striga_root_connections.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Striga_root_connections.jpg/440px-Striga_root_connections.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Striga_root_connections.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Photo of plant roots with connected <i>Striga</i> plant</div>

</div>
<p>Each plant is capable of producing between 90,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bebawi_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bebawi-11">[11]</a></sup> and 500,000 seeds, which may remain viable in the soil for over 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pest_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pest-12">[12]</a></sup> Most seeds produced are not viable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bebawi_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-bebawi-11">[11]</a></sup> An annual plant, witchweed overwinters in the seed stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> Its seeds germinate in the presence of host root <a href="/wiki/Exudate" title="Exudate">exudate</a>, and develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haustoria" title="Haustoria">haustoria</a> which penetrate host root cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> Host root exudate contain <a href="/wiki/Strigolactone" title="Strigolactone">strigolactones</a>, signaling molecules that promote striga seed germination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strigalactone_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strigalactone-13">[13]</a></sup> A bell-like swelling forms where the parasitic roots attach to the roots of the host.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> The pathogen develops underground, where it may spend the next four to seven weeks before emergence, when it rapidly flowers and produces seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> Witchweed seeds spread readily via wind and water, and in soil via animal vectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> The chief means of dispersal, however, is through human activity, by means of machinery, tools, and clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pest_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pest-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haustorium_Development">Haustorium Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Haustorium Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once germination is stimulated, the <i>Striga</i> seed sends out an initial root to probe the soil for the host root. The initial root secretes an oxidizing enzyme that digests the host root surface, releasing <a href="/wiki/Quinone" title="Quinone">quinone</a>s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> If the quinone product is within the appropriate concentrations, a haustorium will develop from the initial root. The haustorium grows toward the host root until it makes contact with the root surface, establishing parasitic contact in relatively short order. Within 12 hours of initial haustorium growth, the haustorium recognizes the host root and begins rapid cell division and elongation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup> The haustorium forms a wedge shape and uses mechanical force and chemical digestion to penetrate the host root, pushing the host cells out of the way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> Within 48–72 hours, the haustorium has penetrated the host root <a href="/wiki/Cortex_(botany)" title="Cortex (botany)">cortex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup> Finger-like structures on the haustorium, called oscula (from Latin <i>osculum</i>, "little mouth") penetrate the host xylem through pits in the membrane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> The oscula then swell to secure their position within the xylem membrane. <i>Striga</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sieve_tubes" title="Sieve tubes">sieve tubes</a> develop along with the oscula. Shortly after the host xylem is penetrated, <i>Striga</i> sieve tubes develop and approach the host phloem within eight cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">[18]</a></sup> This eight cell layer allows for nonspecific nutrient transport from the host to the <i>Striga</i> seedling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">[18]</a></sup> Within 24 hours after tapping the host xylem and phloem, the <i>Striga</i> cotyledons emerge from the seed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Temperatures ranging from 30 to 35 °C (86 to 95 °F) in a moist environment are ideal for germination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> Witchweed will not develop in temperatures below 20 °C (68 °F). Agricultural soils with a light texture and low nitrogen levels tend to favor Striga's development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cali_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cali-19">[19]</a></sup> Still, witchweed has demonstrated a wide tolerance for soil types if soil temperatures are favorably high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> Seeds have been shown to survive in frozen soil of temperatures as low as −15 °C (5 °F), attesting to their aptitude as <a href="/wiki/Overwintering" title="Overwintering">overwintering</a> structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soil temperature, air temperature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoperiod" title="Photoperiod">photoperiod</a>, soil type, and soil nutrient and moisture levels do not greatly deter the development of witchweed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> These findings,while limited to the Carolinas in the United States, seem to suggest that the pathogen could successfully infect the massive corn crops of the American Midwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Management of witchweed is difficult because the majority of its life cycle takes place below ground. If it is not detected before emergence, it is too late to reduce crop losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> To prevent witchweed from spreading it is necessary to plant uncontaminated seeds and to clean soil and plant debris off of machinery, shoes, clothing, and tools before entering fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cali_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cali-19">[19]</a></sup> If populations are low, hand weeding before seeds are produced is an option.</p>
<p><i>Striga</i> in the United States has been controlled through the use of several management strategies, including quarantines imposed on affected areas, control of movement of farm equipment between infected and uninfected areas, herbicide application, and imposed "suicidal germination". For the latter, in fields not yet planted in crops, seeds present in the soil are induced to germinate by injecting <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> gas, which mimics the natural physiological response tied to host recognition. Because no host roots are available, the seedlings die. Unfortunately, each <i>Striga</i> plant can produce tens of thousands of tiny seeds, which may remain dormant in the soil for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Thus, such treatments do not remove all seeds from the soil. Moreover, this method is expensive and not generally available to farmers in developing nations of Africa and Asia.</p>
<p>Another method called <a href="/wiki/Trap_crop" title="Trap crop">trap cropping</a> involves planting a species in an infested field that will induce the <i>Striga</i> seeds to germinate but will not support attachment of the parasite. This method has been used in sorghum plantations by planting <i><a href="/wiki/Celosia_argentea" title="Celosia argentea">Celosia argentea</a></i> between the sorghum rows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Cotton, sunflower and linseed are also effective trap crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> Planting silverleaf (<i><a href="/wiki/Desmodium" title="Desmodium">Desmodium</a> uncinatum</i>) inhibits striga seed germination and has worked effectively intercropped with maize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Increasing nitrogen levels in the soil, growing striga-tolerant varieties, trap-cropping, and planting susceptible crops harvested before witchweed seed is produced, are proven anti-striga tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cali_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cali-19">[19]</a></sup> Coating maize seeds with fungi or a herbicide also appears to be a promising approach. An example is TAN222, the "striga-resistant" maize variety which is coated with the systemic herbicide <a href="/wiki/Imazapyr" title="Imazapyr">imazapyr</a>, to which it is resistant. Any striga seeds sprouting when this maize is in the seedling stage are poisoned when their haustoria embed in the seedling's roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Africa-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several sorghum varieties have high levels of resistance in local conditions, including 'N-13', 'Framida', and 'Serena'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bio_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bio-27">[27]</a></sup> 'Buruma', 'Shibe', 'Okoa' and 'Serere 17' millet cultivars are considered to be resistant in Tanzania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bio_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bio-27">[27]</a></sup> Some maize varieties show partial resistance to witchweed, including 'Katumani' in Kenya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bio_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bio-27">[27]</a></sup> In a number of rice cultivars, including some cultivars of NERICA (New Rice for Africa), effective pre- and post- attachment resistance mechanisms have been identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Africa-25">[25]</a></sup> 'StrigAway'™ herbicide-resistant, herbicide impregnated maize has been shown to reduce the seed bank of striga by 30% in two seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Africa-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance">Importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maize, sorghum, and sugarcane crops affected by witchweed in the United States have an estimated value well over $20 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sand_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Sand-2">[2]</a></sup> Furthermore, witchweed is capable of wiping out an entire crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wales_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-Wales-9">[9]</a></sup> In fact, it is so prolific that in 1957 Congress allocated money in an attempt to eradicate witchweed. Because of Striga, the Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APHIS" title="APHIS">APHIS</a>) of the U.S. Department of Agriculture established a research station and control methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pest_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pest-12">[12]</a></sup> Through infestation mapping, quarantine, and control activities such as contaminated seed destruction, the acreage parasitized by witchweed has been reduced 99% since its discovery in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pest_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pest-12">[12]</a></sup> APHIS has even offered cash rewards those who identify and report the weed, and encourages landowners to check their own acreage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pest_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Pest-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parasitizing important economic plants, witchweed is one of the most destructive pathogens in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-text_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-text-10">[10]</a></sup> In fact, witchweed affects 40% of Africa’s arable savanna region, resulting in up to $13 billion lost every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bio_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bio-27">[27]</a></sup> Striga affects 40 million hectares (98,842,153 acres) of crops in sub-Saharan Africa alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Africa-25">[25]</a></sup> The witchweed infestation is so bad in parts of Africa, some farmers must relocate every few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gene_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gene-30">[30]</a></sup> The majority of crops in Africa are grown by subsistence farmers who cannot afford expensive witchweed controls, who therefore suffer much as a result of this pathogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gene_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gene-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><i>Striga asiatica</i> has a very wide geographic distribution, from Africa through southern and eastern Asia to Australia. Since the 1950s, it is also known from the United States. This introduction, likely a result of human activity, resulted in an infestation of corn (<a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>) across many counties in North and South Carolina. The United States Department of Agriculture and state agencies imposed a quarantine on this area to control its spread - a process that was apparently successful.</li>
<li><i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Striga_gesnerioides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Striga gesnerioides (page does not exist)">Striga gesnerioides</a></i>, cowpea witchweed, as its name implies, is a parasite of <a href="/wiki/Cowpea" title="Cowpea">cowpea</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vigna_unguiculata" title="Vigna unguiculata">Vigna unguiculata</a></i>), which is not a grass, but a member of the legume family (<a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leguminosae" title="Leguminosae">Leguminosae</a>). This species was also accidentally introduced into Florida in the United States, where it was found parasitizing <i><a href="/wiki/Indigofera" title="Indigofera">Indigofera</a></i> <i>hirsuta</i> (hairy indigo, another legume).</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Striga_hermonthica" title="Striga hermonthica">Striga hermonthica</a></i> (purple witchweed) is also a parasite that affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grasses</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pearl_millet" title="Pearl millet">pearl millet</a> in sub-Saharan Africa (Senegal to Ethiopia, Democratic Republic of Congo and Tanzania, Angola, Namibia).</li>
