<p><i><b>Street Fighter II': Champion Edition</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ストリートファイターIIダッシュ -CHAMPION EDITION-</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> is a competitive <a href="/wiki/Fighting_game" title="Fighting game">fighting game</a> released for the arcades by <a href="/wiki/Capcom" title="Capcom">Capcom</a> in <a href="/wiki/1992_in_video_gaming" title="1992 in video gaming">1992</a>. It was the first of several updated versions of the original <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Fighter_II:_The_World_Warrior" title="Street Fighter II: The World Warrior">Street Fighter II: The World Warrior</a></i>. The main changes were the addition of the Grand Masters (the final four computer-controlled opponents in the <a href="/wiki/Single-player_video_game" title="Single-player video game">single-player mode</a>) as playable characters and mirror matches (same character vs. matches). The fighting techniques of the eight main characters from the original game were also further refined to allow for more balanced competitive play.</p>
<p><i>Champion Edition</i> was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2_Turbo:_Hyper_Fighting" title="Street Fighter II′ Turbo: Hyper Fighting">Street Fighter II′ Turbo: Hyper Fighting</a></i>, released several months later.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="384" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png/220px-Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png/330px-Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/be/Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Street_Fighter_II_Dash_screenshot.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sagat vs. M. Bison. The ability to play as the four Grand Masters was made possible in <i>Champion Edition</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The following changes were made from the original <i>World Warrior</i> edition of the game.</p>
<p><b>Additional playable characters:</b> The four Grand Masters (<a href="/wiki/Balrog_(Street_Fighter)" title="Balrog (Street Fighter)">Balrog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vega_(Street_Fighter)" title="Vega (Street Fighter)">Vega</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sagat_(Street_Fighter)" title="Sagat (Street Fighter)">Sagat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/M._Bison" title="M. Bison">M. Bison</a>), who were controlled exclusively by the CPU in <i>World Warrior</i>, are now playable characters. The Grand Masters were considerably toned down from the previous iterations, but are still relatively stronger compared to the standard eight fighters.</p>
<p><b>Mirror matches:</b> In <i>World Warrior</i>, players were not allowed to choose the same character. This restriction has been eliminated in <i>Champion Edition</i>. Each fighter now has a standard palette and an alternate palette that can be chosen by pressing the Start button. If a palette is already chosen by one player, the other player will be automatically assigned the remaining palette.</p>
<p><b>Refined character balance:</b> The returning eight main characters had many of their techniques and priorities modified in order to allow for more balanced competition between different characters. <a href="/wiki/Ryu_(Street_Fighter)" title="Ryu (Street Fighter)">Ryu</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Ken_Masters" title="Ken Masters">Ken</a>'s fighting techniques in particular were changed in order to differentiate their common fighting styles.</p>
<p><b>Single-player mode:</b> The matches in the single-player mode was increased from 11 opponents to 12 due to the addition of the mirror matches. This also changed the order in which the third bonus stage occurred (the drum-breaking minigame): in the original game it took place after the match with Vega; wherein <i>Champion Edition</i>, it takes place after defeating Balrog. The endings of some of the returning characters were redrawn (particularly Ryu's, Ken's, and Zangief's), while each of the four bosses received an ending as well. The ending for the boss characters consist of an image of all four Grand Masters (with the character used by the player on top), with scrolling text overlaid on it specific to the player character with a large army of demonic looking soldiers marching below and accompanied by the same ending music.</p>
<p><b>End credits:</b> Like in <i>World Warrior</i>, the game only shows the end credits sequence if the player completes the single-player tournament without losing a match. Whereas the music played in the <i>World Warrior</i> version of the credits was the same music used for the Continue countdown, <i>Champion Edition</i> plays an entirely new track instead. The special credits sequence where the player completes the game without losing a single round was also changed, which now depicts the twelve playable fighters performing their special moves on oil drums and crates.</p>
<p><b>Graphical changes:</b> Graphical changes were minor. Color changes were made for background stages and were improved overall. Ryu's face in his ending was redrawn with a more serious expression. Ken's fiancee (Eliza) in his ending was given a more realistic design. The Soviet President (a caricature of <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> despite him having stepped down after the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">Dissolution of the Soviet Union</a>) is drawn with a more serious expression in Zangief's ending. The clothes Chun-Li threw away were redrawn in her ending. The drinks Guile's wife is holding were redrawn.</p>
<p><b>Number of Rounds:</b> The maximum number of rounds was reduced from ten to four. If a "double k.o." or "draw game" occurs when the third round ends, the fourth round will be the final one instead. Like in <i>World Warrior</i>, if both fighters are human-controlled characters, then both players will be declared losers if neither win the final round. If it's the player against the CPU, the CPU will win by default.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PC_Engine">PC Engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: PC Engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The PC Engine version (published by NEC Home Electronics and developed by Capcom) was released exclusively in Japan on June 12, 1993. The accuracy of this port is high compared to the <a href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Super Nintendo Entertainment System">Super NES</a> port of the <i>The World Warrior</i>, as it featured the barrel breaking bonus stage that was cut out from that version, along with numerous voice clips by the announcer and the elephants in Dhalsim's stage (these were later integrated in <i>Street Fighter II Turbo</i> for the SNES). This version was released on a 20-Megabit HuCard. NEC Avenue released the Avenue Pad 6 specifically for the PC Engine version of <i>Champion Edition</i>, which added four additional action buttons (labelled III through VI) in addition to the standard I and II buttons. Other six button controllers were later released for the platform such as the Fighting Commander PC by HORI and the Arcade Pad 6 by NEC Home Electronics. When the game is played on a standard two-button controller, the Run button, along with buttons I and II, are used as switchable attack buttons, while the Select button is used to toggle between punches and kicks. This version was released on Virtual Console on November 16, 2009.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mega_Drive.2FGenesis">Mega Drive/Genesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mega Drive/Genesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Mega Drive/Genesis</a> version of <i>Champion Edition</i>, titled <i><b>Street Fighter II′ Plus: Champion Edition</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[b]</a></sup> in Japan and <i><b>Street Fighter II′: Special Champion Edition</b></i> in North America and Europe, was released on September 28, 1993 in Japan, September 27, 1993 in North America, and October 29, 1993 in Europe. It was first of two <i>Street Fighter II</i> ports for the console and came in a 24 Megabit cartridge.</p>
<p>The Genesis version was originally intended to be a standalone port of <i>Champion Edition</i>, similar to the PC Engine version. However, following the announcement of <i>Street Fighter II Turbo</i> for the SNES, Sega ordered their version to be delayed so that Capcom could add all of the extra content from the SNES <i>Turbo</i> version as well, resulting in the title change.</p>
<p>A six-button control pad for the Genesis, the MK-1653 (or SJ-6000 in Japan), was made primarily for the game, adding three action buttons labelled XYZ in addition to the standard ABC buttons. The game can also be played with the original three-button controller, in which the ABC buttons are used for attacks (light, medium, and heavy), while the Start button is used to toggle between punches and kicks (the pause function cannot be used with a three-button controller as a result).</p>
<p><i>Special Champion Edition</i> consists of a "Champ" mode with <i>Champion Edition</i> rules and a "Hyper" mode with <i>Hyper Fighting</i> rules,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[c]</a></sup> similar to the "Normal" and "Turbo" modes in the SNES <i>Turbo</i> version. This was the first console version of a <i>Street Fighter II</i> game to feature the original opening sequence which depicted two generic martial artists fighting in front of a cheering public (the Japanese version features a white fighter hitting a black opponent, while the overseas versions replaced the black opponent with another white fighter). The ten-stars speed settings in "Hyper" mode, which were only accessible in the SNES version through a cheat code 4, is available by default in the Genesis version, and a cheat code that allows players to adjust the speed in "Champion" mode as well was added in its place. <i>Special Champion Edition</i> was also the only home version at the time to allow players to cancel simultaneous button inputs.</p>
<p>This version was a bestseller in Japan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[3]</a></sup> the UK<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[4]</a></sup> and USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[5]</a></sup> In November 1993, <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> magazine's Reader Cross Review gave the <i>Dash Plus</i> version of the game a 10 out of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-fam-9">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Special Champion Edition</i> was released as a plug'n play system in 2005 as part of the "Play TV Legends" series by Radica. It also included the Genesis version of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghouls%27n_Ghosts" title="Ghouls'n Ghosts">Ghouls'n Ghosts</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X68000">X68000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: X68000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 26, 1993, Capcom released an <a href="/wiki/X68000" title="X68000">X68000</a> port of <i>Champion Edition</i> exclusively in Japan, which consisted of four floppy disks. The port is almost identical to the arcade version, with the same exact graphics and almost identical soundtrack. However, the X68000 version forces player to switch floppy disks when loading different stages and characters (it is possible to avoid this by installing the game to the system's hard drive if the computer has more than 6 Megabytes). The game also included a joystick adapter that allowed players to use the Super Famicom and Mega Drive versions of Capcom's <i>CPS Fighter</i> joystick controller. On an X68030 with multiple PCM drivers installed, the music and voice quality can match that of the arcade version's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADPCM" title="ADPCM">ADPCM</a> sound system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Master_System">Master System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Master System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Master_System" title="Sega Master System">Master System</a> port of <i>Champion Edition</i> was also released in 1997 for the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> market, published by <a href="/wiki/Tectoy" title="Tectoy">Tec Toy</a>, although the character portraits in the player select screen are based on <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Street_Fighter_II" title="Super Street Fighter II">Super Street Fighter II</a></i>. It features only eight characters; Dhalsim, E. Honda, Zangief and Vega are not in this version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sf2sms_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sf2sms-10">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_releases">Other releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Street Fighter II Turbo</i> for the SNES, while based on the succeeding game in the series (<i>Street Fighter II′ Turbo: Hyper Fighting</i>), allows players to choose between <i>Champion Edition</i> rules (Normal mode) and <i>Hyper Fighting</i> rules (Turbo mode).</p>
<p>The arcade version is also included in <i>Street Fighter Collection 2</i> (<i>Capcom Generation 5</i>) for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">PlayStation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sega_Saturn" title="Sega Saturn">Sega Saturn</a>, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Capcom_Classics_Collection#Volume_1" title="Capcom Classics Collection">Capcom Classics Collection Vol. 1</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>, and <i>Capcom Classics Collection: Reloaded</i> for <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PlayStation Portable</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Street Fighter II Dash</i> was awarded <a href="/wiki/List_of_Game_of_the_Year_awards" title="List of Game of the Year awards">Best Game of 1992</a> in the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%B2%E3%83%BC%E3%83%A1%E3%82%B9%E3%83%88%E5%A4%A7%E8%B3%9E" title="ja:ゲーメスト大賞">Sixth Annual Grand Prize</a>, as published in the February 1993 issue of <i><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamest" title="ja:Gamest">Gamest</a></i>, winning once again in the category of Best Action Game. <i>Dash</i> placed No. 3 in Best VGM, No. 6 in Best Graphics, No. 5 in Best Direction. The <i>Street Fighter II Image Album</i> was the No. 1 Best Album in the same issue, with the Drama CD version of <i>Street Fighter II</i> tied for No. 7 with the soundtrack for <i>Star Blade</i>. The List of Best Characters was not dominated by <i>Street Fighter II</i> characters this time, with the only character at the Top Ten being Chun-Li at No. 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamest84_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamest84-29">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Mega Drive version, <i>Special Champion Edition</i>, received positive reviews. In November 1993, <i><a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">Famitsu</a></i> magazine's Reader Cross Review gave <i>Special Champion Edition</i> a 10 out of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fam_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-fam-9">[6]</a></sup> It received 10 out of 10 for both graphics and addiction from <i><a href="/wiki/Mega_(magazine)" title="Mega (magazine)">Mega</a></i>, who described it as "a candidate for best game ever and without a doubt the best beat-'em-up of all time" and gave it an overall 92% score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mega_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-mega-27">[24]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/MegaTech" title="MegaTech">MegaTech</a></i> scored it 95%, and commented "the greatest coin-op hits the Megadrive in perfect form".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-megatech_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-megatech-28">[25]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(magazine)" title="Edge (magazine)">Edge</a></i> gave the PC Engine version of <i>Champion Edition</i> a score of 8 out of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edge_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-edge-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_reception">Commercial reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_Fighter_II%E2%80%B2:_Champion_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Street Fighter II′: Champion Edition</i> sold 140,000 arcade cabinets in Japan alone, where it cost <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">¥</a>160,000 (<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>1300) for each cabinet, amounting to ¥22.4 billion ($182 million) revenue generated from cabinet sales of <i>Champion Edition</i> in Japan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edge-Makingof_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edge-Makingof-30">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curran-38_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curran-38-31">[28]</a></sup> which is equivalent to $320 million in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In North America, it was number-one on <i>RePlay</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s May 1992 <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">coin-op</a> earnings chart for upright arcade cabinets, above <a href="/wiki/Midway_Games" title="Midway Games">Midway</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mortal_Kombat_(1992_video_game)" title="Mortal Kombat (1992 video game)">Mortal Kombat</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-replay_1992-07_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-replay_1992-07-33">[30]</a></sup> On <i>RePlay</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s April 1993 charts, <i>Champion Edition</i> was number-four on the upright cabinets chart,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-replay_1993-04_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-replay_1993-04-34">[31]</a></sup> and remained number-four on the uprights cabinet chart in May 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-replay_1993-05_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-replay_1993-05-35">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Street Fighter II: Champion Edition</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s arcade earnings exceeded $2.3 billion in gross revenue, making it one of the top 10 <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_video_games" title="List of best-selling video games">biggest grossing</a> arcade games of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Sega Mega Drive/Genesis version, <i>Street Fighter II′: Special Champion Edition</i>, sold 1.65 million cartridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-platinum_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-platinum-37">[34]</a></sup> Despite this, the port sold under expectations, with competition of the original <i><a href="/wiki/Mortal_Kombat_(1992_video_game)" title="Mortal Kombat (1992 video game)">Mortal Kombat</a></i> game being cited as a reason for not sales not meeting Capcom's expectations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[35]</a></sup></p>
