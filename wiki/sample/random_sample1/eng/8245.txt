<p><b>Steven Louis Dalkowski</b> (born June 3, 1939<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Register-1">[1]</a></sup>), nicknamed <b>Dalko</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nickname_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nickname-2">[2]</a></sup> is an American retired left-handed <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a>. He is sometimes called the fastest pitcher in <a href="/wiki/History_of_baseball_in_the_United_States" title="History of baseball in the United States">baseball history</a> and had a <a href="/wiki/Fastball" title="Fastball">fastball</a> that probably exceeded 100 mph (160 km/h). Some experts believed it went as fast as 125 mph (201 km/h), others that his pitches traveled at 120 mph (190 km/h) or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman-3">[3]</a></sup> As no <a href="/wiki/Radar_gun" title="Radar gun">radar gun</a> or other device was available at games to measure the speed of his pitches precisely, the actual top speed of his pitches remains unknown. Regardless of its actual speed, his fastball earned him the nickname "White Lightning".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WORSTINA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WORSTINA-4">[4]</a></sup> Such was his reputation that despite never reaching the major leagues, and finishing his minor league years in class-B ball, the 1966 <i><a href="/wiki/Sporting_News" title="Sporting News">Sporting News</a></i> item about the end of his career was headlined "LIVING LEGEND RELEASED."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsillustrated.cnn.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sportsillustrated.cnn.com-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dalkowski was also famous for his unpredictable performance and inability to control his pitches. His <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a> and violent behavior off the field caused him problems during his career and after his retirement. After he retired from baseball, he spent many years as an alcoholic, making a meager living as a manual laborer. He recovered in the 1990s, but his alcoholism has left him with <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a> and he has difficulty remembering his life after the mid-1960s.</p>
<p>Screenwriter and film director <a href="/wiki/Ron_Shelton" title="Ron Shelton">Ron Shelton</a> played in the Baltimore Orioles minor league organization soon after Dalkowski. His 1988 film <i><a href="/wiki/Bull_Durham" title="Bull Durham">Bull Durham</a></i> features a character named "Nuke" LaLoosh (played by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Robbins" title="Tim Robbins">Tim Robbins</a>) who is based loosely on the tales Shelton was told about Dalkowski.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Fraser" title="Brendan Fraser">Brendan Fraser</a>'s character in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scout_(film)" title="The Scout (film)">The Scout</a></i> is loosely based on him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (September 2007)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1970, <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Pat_Jordan_(author)" title="Pat Jordan (author)">Pat Jordan</a> wrote, "Inevitably, the stories outgrew the man, until it was no longer possible to distinguish fact from fiction. But, no matter how embellished, one fact always remained: Dalkowski struck out more batters and walked more batters per nine-inning game than any professional pitcher in baseball history."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsillustrated.cnn.com_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-sportsillustrated.cnn.com-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball_career">Baseball career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Dalkowski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baseball career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dalkowski began playing baseball in high school, and also played <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Quarterback" title="Quarterback">quarterback</a> for <a href="/wiki/New_Britain,_Connecticut" title="New Britain, Connecticut">New Britain</a> High School. During his time with the football team, they won the division championship twice, in 1955 and 1956. However, he excelled the most in baseball, and still holds a <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> state record for <a href="/wiki/Strikeout" title="Strikeout">striking out</a> 24 batters in a single game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating from high school in 1957, Dalkowski was immediately signed by the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> for a $4,000 bonus, and initially played for their class-D minor league affiliate in <a href="/wiki/Kingsport,_Tennessee" title="Kingsport, Tennessee">Kingsport, Tennessee</a>. He spent his entire career in the minor leagues, playing in nine different leagues during his nine-year career. His only appearance at the Orioles' <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Stadium_(Baltimore)" title="Memorial Stadium (Baltimore)">Memorial Stadium</a> was during an exhibition game in 1959, when he struck out the opposing side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WORSTINA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-WORSTINA-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dalkowski's claim to fame was the high velocity of his fastball. Accurate measurements at the time were difficult to make, but the consensus is that Dalkowski regularly threw well above 100 miles per hour. Dalkowski's raw speed was aided by his highly flexible left (pitching) arm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and by his unusual "buggy-whip" pitching motion, which ended in a cross-body arm swing. "I hit my left elbow on my right knee so often, they finally made me a pad to wear", recalled Dalkowski.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>But Dalkowski also often had extreme difficulty controlling his pitches. He often <a href="/wiki/Base_on_balls" title="Base on balls">walked</a> more batters than he struck out, and many times his pitches would go wild — sometimes so wild that they ended up in the stands. Batters found the combination of extreme velocity and lack of control intimidating. Oriole <a href="/wiki/Paul_Blair_(baseball)" title="Paul Blair (baseball)">Paul Blair</a> stated that "He threw the hardest I ever saw. He was the wildest I ever saw".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During a typical season in 1960, while pitching in the <a href="/wiki/California_League" title="California League">California League</a>, Dalkowski struck out 262 batters and walked 262 in 170 <a href="/wiki/Inning" title="Inning">innings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffman_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman-3">[3]</a></sup> Dalkowski for 1960 thus figures at both 13.81 K/9IP and 13.81 BB/9IP (see lifetime statistics below). In comparison, <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a> currently holds the major league record for strikeouts per nine innings in a season with 13.41.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In separate games, Dalkowski struck out 21 batters, and walked 21 batters.</p>
<p>Because a pitcher is generally considered wild if he averages four walks per nine innings, a pitcher of average repertoire who consistently walked as many as nine men per nine innings would not normally be considered a prospect. But such was the allure of Dalkowski's explosive arm that the Orioles gave him chance after chance to harness his "stuff", knowing that if he ever managed to control it, he would be a great weapon.</p>
<p>Pitching for the Kingsport (Tennessee) Orioles on August 31, 1957 in Bluefield, West Virginia, Dalkowski struck out 24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluefield_Orioles" title="Bluefield Orioles">Bluefield</a> hitters in a single minor league game, yet issued 18 walks, and threw six wild pitches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Dalkowski pitched a total of 62 innings in 1957, struck out 121 (averaging 18 strikeouts per game), but won only once because he walked 129 and threw 39 wild pitches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WORSTINA_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-WORSTINA-4">[4]</a></sup> Moving to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_League_(baseball,_1902%E2%80%9371)" title="Northern League (baseball, 1902–71)">Northern League</a> in 1958–59, he threw a one-hitter but lost 9–8 on the strength of 17 walks. In 1957–58, Dalkowski either struck out or walked almost three out of every four batters he faced.</p>
<p>During the 1960s under <a href="/wiki/Earl_Weaver" title="Earl Weaver">Earl Weaver</a>, then the manager for the Orioles' double-A affiliate in <a href="/wiki/Elmira,_New_York" title="Elmira, New York">Elmira, New York</a>, Dalkowski's game began to show improvement. Weaver had given all of the players an <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_quotient" title="Intelligence quotient">IQ test</a> and discovered that Dalkowski had a lower than normal IQ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Weaver believed that Dalkowski had had such difficulty keeping his game under control because he did not have the mental capacity. Weaver kept things simple for Dalkowski, telling him to only throw the fastball and a <a href="/wiki/Slider" title="Slider">slider</a>, and to just aim the fastball down the middle of the plate. This allowed Dalkowski to concentrate on just throwing the ball for strikes. Weaver knew that Dalkowski's fastball was practically unhittable no matter where it was in the strike zone, and if Dalkowski missed his target, he might end up throwing it on the corners for a strike anyway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Under Weaver's stewardship, Dalkowski had his best season in 1962, posting personal bests in complete games and ERA, and walking less than a batter an inning for the first time in his career. In an extra-inning game, Dalkowski recorded 27 strikeouts (while walking 16 and throwing 283 pitches).</p>
<p>Dalkowski was invited to major league spring training in 1963, and the Orioles expected to call him up to the majors. On March 23, Dalkowski was used as a relief pitcher during a game against the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>. Most sources say that while throwing a slider to <a href="/wiki/Phil_Linz" title="Phil Linz">Phil Linz</a>, he felt something pop in his left elbow, which turned out to be a severe muscle strain. Some uncertainty over the cause of his injury exists, however, with other sources contending that he damaged his elbow while throwing to first after fielding a bunt from Yankees pitcher <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bouton" title="Jim Bouton">Jim Bouton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Either way, his arm never fully recovered.</p>
<p>When he returned in 1964, Dalkowski's fastball had dropped to 90 mph (145 km/h), and midway through the season he was released by the Orioles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baseball-reference.com_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-baseball-reference.com-18">[18]</a></sup> He played for two more seasons with the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels" title="Los Angeles Angels">Los Angeles Angels</a> organizations before returning briefly to the Orioles farm system but was unable to regain his form before retiring in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baseball-reference.com_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-baseball-reference.com-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dalkowski had a lifetime <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Win-loss_record" title="Win-loss record">win-loss record</a> of 46–80 and an ERA of 5.57 in nine minor league seasons, striking out 1,396 and walking 1,354 in 995 innings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baseball-reference.com_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-baseball-reference.com-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitching_speed">Pitching speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Dalkowski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pitching speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dalkowski's wildness frightened even the bravest of hitters. <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a> faced Dalkowski once in a spring training game. "Fastest ever", said Williams. "I never want to face him again."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Longtime umpire <a href="/wiki/Doug_Harvey_(umpire)" title="Doug Harvey (umpire)">Doug Harvey</a> also cited Dalkowski as the fastest pitcher he had seen: "Nobody could bring it like he could."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Estimates of Dalkowski's top pitching speed abound. <a href="/wiki/Cal_Ripken_Sr." title="Cal Ripken Sr.">Cal Ripken Sr.</a> guessed that he threw up to 115 miles per hour (185 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Most observers agree that he routinely threw well over 110 miles an hour (177 km/h), and sometimes reached 115 mph (180 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radar_gun" title="Radar gun">Radar guns</a>, which were used for many years in professional baseball, did not exist when Dalkowski was playing, so the only evidence supporting this level of velocity is anecdotal. It is certain that with his high speed and penchant for throwing <a href="/wiki/Wild_pitch" title="Wild pitch">wild pitches</a>, he would have been an intimidating opponent for any batter who faced him. <a href="/wiki/Andy_Etchebarren" title="Andy Etchebarren">Andy Etchebarren</a>, a catcher for Dalkowski at Elmira, described his fastball as "light" and fairly easy to catch. According to Etchebarren his wilder pitches usually went high, sometimes low; "Dalkowski would throw a fastball that looked like it was coming in at knee level, only to see it sail past the batter's eyes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-google1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dalkowski's greatest legacy may be the number of anecdotes (some more believable than others) surrounding his pitching ability. He was said to have thrown a pitch that tore off part of a batter's ear. Some observers believed that this incident made Dalkowski even more nervous and contributed further to his wildness. Another story says that in 1960 at <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">Stockton, California</a>, he threw a pitch that broke <a href="/wiki/Umpire_(baseball)" title="Umpire (baseball)">umpire</a> <a href="/wiki/Doug_Harvey_(umpire)" title="Doug Harvey (umpire)">Doug Harvey</a>'s mask in three places, knocking him 18 feet (5 m) back and sending him to a hospital for three days with a <a href="/wiki/Head_injury" title="Head injury">concussion</a>. Dalkowski once won a $5 bet with teammate <a href="/wiki/Herm_Starrette" title="Herm Starrette">Herm Starrette</a> who said that he could not throw a baseball through a wall. Dalkowski warmed up and then moved 15 feet (5 m) away from the wooden outfield fence. His first pitch went right through the boards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WORSTINA_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-WORSTINA-4">[4]</a></sup> On another bet, Dalkowski threw a ball over a fence 440 feet (134 m) away.</p>
<p>The only recorded evidence of his pitching speed stems from 1958, when Dalkowski was sent by the Orioles to <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Proving_Ground" title="Aberdeen Proving Ground">Aberdeen Proving Ground</a>, a military installation. Here, using a radar machine, he was clocked at 93.5 miles per hour (150.5 km/h), a fast but not outstanding speed for a professional pitcher. However, several factors worked against Dalkowski: he had pitched a game the day before, he was throwing from a flat surface instead of from a pitcher's mound, and he had to throw pitches for 40 minutes at a small target before the machine could capture an accurate measurement. Further, the device measured speed from a few feet away from the plate, instead of 10 feet from release as in modern times. This cost Dalkowski approximately 9 MPH, not even considering the other factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a>, the current record holder for fastest pitch is <a href="/wiki/Nolan_Ryan" title="Nolan Ryan">Nolan Ryan</a>, with a pitch clocked at 100.9 mph (162.4 km/h) in 1974, though the record has not been updated in many years and several pitchers have recorded faster pitches since then. That seems to be because Ryan's speed was recorded 10' from the plate, unlike 10' from release as today, costing him up to 10 MPH. <a class="external free" href="http://www.efastball.com/baseball/stats/fastest-pitch-speed-in-major-leagues/" rel="nofollow">http://www.efastball.com/baseball/stats/fastest-pitch-speed-in-major-leagues/</a> Earl Weaver, who had years of exposure to both pitchers, said, "[Dalkowski] threw a lot faster than <a href="/wiki/Nolan_Ryan" title="Nolan Ryan">Ryan</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The hardest thrower in baseball currently is recognized as <a href="/wiki/Aroldis_Chapman" title="Aroldis Chapman">Aroldis Chapman</a>, who has been clocked with the fastest pitch on record at 105.1 mph (169 km/h).</p>
<p>Scientists contend that the theoretical maximum speed that a pitcher can throw is slightly above 100 mph (161 km/h). Beyond that the pitcher would cause himself a serious injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> There is no doubt that a pitcher who can throw at 100 mph+ is rare, with only a small handful of pitchers every generation being capable of this feat.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_baseball">Life after baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Dalkowski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life after baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1965 he married schoolteacher Linda Moore in <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a>, but they divorced two years later. Unable to find any gainful employment, he became a migrant worker. Dalkowski experienced problems with alcohol abuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fall_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fall-26">[26]</a></sup> He drank heavily as a player and his drinking escalated after the end of his career. He received help from the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Professional_Ball_Players_of_America" title="Association of Professional Ball Players of America">Association of Professional Ball Players of America</a> (APBPA) periodically from 1974 to 1992 and went through <a href="/wiki/Drug_rehabilitation" title="Drug rehabilitation">rehabilitation</a>. He was able to find a job and stay sober for several months but soon went back to drinking. The APBPA stopped providing financial assistance to him because he was using the funds to purchase alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Poor health in the 1980s prevented him from working altogether, and by the end of the decade he was living in a small apartment in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, penniless and suffering from alcohol-induced <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>. At some point during this time he married a motel clerk named Virginia, who moved him to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City,_Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma City, Oklahoma">Oklahoma City</a> in 1993. She died of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerebral_aneurysm" title="Cerebral aneurysm">brain aneurysm</a> in 1994. Dalkowski has lived at a long-term care facility in New Britain for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandelaro_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mandelaro-28">[28]</a></sup> In a 2003 interview, Dalkowski said that he was unable to remember life events that occurred from 1964 to 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>For his contributions to baseball lore, he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Reliquary#Shrine_of_the_Eternals" title="Baseball Reliquary">Shrine of the Eternals</a> on July 19, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a>'</i>s 1970 profile of Dalkowski concluded, "His failure was not one of deficiency, but rather of excess. He was too fast. His ball moved too much. His talent was too superhuman... It mattered only that once, just once, Steve Dalkowski threw a fastball so hard that <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a> never even saw it. No one else could claim that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
