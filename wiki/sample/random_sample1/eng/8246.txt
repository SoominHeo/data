<p><b>Stephen J.</b> "<b>Steve</b>" <b>Ditko</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb14_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bb14-1">[1]</a></sup> (born November 2, 1927) is an American <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic book</a> artist and writer best known as the artist and co-creator, with <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> superheroes <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a>.</p>
<p>Ditko studied under <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Robinson" title="Jerry Robinson">Jerry Robinson</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartoonist_and_Illustrators_School" title="Cartoonist and Illustrators School">Cartoonist and Illustrators School</a> in New York City. He began his professional career in 1953, working in the studio of <a href="/wiki/Joe_Simon" title="Joe Simon">Joe Simon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>, beginning as an inker and coming under the influence of artist <a href="/wiki/Mort_Meskin" title="Mort Meskin">Mort Meskin</a>. During this time, he then began his long association with <a href="/wiki/Charlton_Comics" title="Charlton Comics">Charlton Comics</a>, where he did work in the genres of science fiction, horror, and mystery. He also co-created the <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> <a href="/wiki/Captain_Atom" title="Captain Atom">Captain Atom</a> in 1960.</p>
<p>During the 1950s, Ditko also drew for <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Comics_(1950s)" title="Atlas Comics (1950s)">Atlas Comics</a>, a forerunner of Marvel Comics. He went on to contribute much significant work to Marvel. In 1966, after being the exclusive artist on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Spider-Man" title="The Amazing Spider-Man">The Amazing Spider-Man</a></i> and the "Doctor Strange" feature in <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Tales" title="Strange Tales">Strange Tales</a></i>, Ditko left Marvel for reasons never specified.</p>
<p>Ditko continued to work for Charlton and also <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>, making major contributions, including a revamp of the long-running character <a href="/wiki/Blue_Beetle" title="Blue Beetle">Blue Beetle</a>, and creating or co-creating the <a href="/wiki/Question_(comics)" title="Question (comics)">Question</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Creeper_(comics)" title="Creeper (comics)">Creeper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shade,_the_Changing_Man" title="Shade, the Changing Man">Shade, the Changing Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hawk_and_Dove" title="Hawk and Dove">Hawk and Dove</a>. Ditko also began contributing to small independent publishers, where he created <a href="/wiki/Mr._A" title="Mr. A">Mr. A</a>, a hero reflecting the influence of <a href="/wiki/Ayn_Rand" title="Ayn Rand">Ayn Rand</a>'s philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Objectivism_(Ayn_Rand)" title="Objectivism (Ayn Rand)">Objectivism</a>. Since the 1960s, Ditko has declined most interviews, stating that it is his work he offers readers, not his personality.</p>
<p>Ditko was inducted into the comics industry's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby_Hall_of_Fame" title="Jack Kirby Hall of Fame">Jack Kirby Hall of Fame</a> in 1990, and into the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Eisner_Award_winners#The_Will_Eisner_Award_Hall_of_Fame" title="List of Eisner Award winners">Will Eisner Award Hall of Fame</a> in 1994.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stephen J. Ditko was born on November 2, 1927 in <a href="/wiki/Johnstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Johnstown, Pennsylvania">Johnstown, Pennsylvania</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the son of first-generation Americans of Slovak<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbend1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbend1-3">[3]</a></sup> descent: Stephen Ditko, an artistically talented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenter" title="Carpenter">master carpenter</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mill</a>, and Anna, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homemaker" title="Homemaker">homemaker</a>. The second-eldest child in a working-class family, he was preceded by sister Anna Marie,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbend1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbend1-3">[3]</a></sup> and followed, according to the 1940 census, by sister Elizabeth and brother Patrick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb14_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bb14-1">[1]</a></sup> Inspired by his father's love of newspaper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_strips" title="Comic strips">comic strips</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Hal_Foster" title="Hal Foster">Hal Foster</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Prince_Valiant" title="Prince Valiant">Prince Valiant</a></i>, Ditko found his interest in comics accelerated by the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a> in 1940, and by <a href="/wiki/Will_Eisner" title="Will Eisner">Will Eisner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Spirit_(comics)" title="Spirit (comics)">The Spirit</a></i>, which appeared in a <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid</a>-sized comic-book insert in Sunday newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb15_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bb15-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Good with his hands, Ditko in junior high school was part of a group of students who crafted wooden models of German airplanes to aid civilian <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> aircraft-spotters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb15_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bb15-4">[4]</a></sup> Upon graduating from Johnstown High School in 1945,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb15_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-bb15-4">[4]</a></sup> he enlisted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> on October 26, 1945,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbend1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbend1-3">[3]</a></sup> and did <a href="/wiki/Military_service" title="Military service">military service</a> in postwar Germany, where he drew comics for an Army newspaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb15_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-bb15-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following his discharge, Ditko learned that his idol, Batman artist <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Robinson" title="Jerry Robinson">Jerry Robinson</a>, was teaching at the Cartoonists and Illustrators School (later the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Visual_Arts" title="School of Visual Arts">School of Visual Arts</a>) in New York City. Moving there in 1950, he enrolled in the art school under the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Bill" title="G.I. Bill">G.I. Bill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell16_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell16-5">[5]</a></sup> Robinson found the young student "a very hard worker who really focused on his drawing"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AE39_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AE39-6">[6]</a></sup> and someone who "could work well with other writers as well as write his own stories and create his own characters",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AE39_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-AE39-6">[6]</a></sup> and he helped Ditko acquire a scholarship for the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell19_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell19-7">[7]</a></sup> "He was in my class for two years, four or five days a week, five hours a night. It was very intense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-student_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-student-8">[8]</a></sup> Robinson, who invited artists and editors to speak with his class, once brought in <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, then editor of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>' 1950s precursor, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Comics_(1950s)" title="Atlas Comics (1950s)">Atlas Comics</a>, and, "I think that was when Stan first saw Steve's work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-student_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-student-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thing,The,Ditko1stcover.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="444" data-file-width="300" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/18/Thing%2CThe%2CDitko1stcover.jpg/220px-Thing%2CThe%2CDitko1stcover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/18/Thing%2CThe%2CDitko1stcover.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thing,The,Ditko1stcover.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Thing</i> #12 (Feb. 1954), Ditko's first published comic-book cover</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ditko began professionally illustrating comic books in early 1953, drawing writer Bruce Hamilton's science-fiction story "Stretching Things" for the <a href="/wiki/Key_Publications" title="Key Publications">Key Publications</a> <a href="/wiki/Imprint_(trade_name)" title="Imprint (trade name)">imprint</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanmor_Publications" title="Stanmor Publications">Stanmor Publications</a>, which sold the story to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ajax/Farrell" title="Ajax/Farrell">Ajax/Farrell</a>, where it finally found publication in <i>Fantastic Fears</i> #5 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover-date" title="Cover-date">cover-dated</a> Feb. 1954).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb20_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bb20-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Ditko's first published work was his second professional story, the six-page "Paper Romance" in <i>Daring Love</i> #1 (Oct. 1953),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb20_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bb20-9">[9]</a></sup> published by the Key imprint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillmor_Magazines" title="Gillmor Magazines">Gillmor Magazines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly afterward, Ditko found work at the studio of celebrated writer-artists <a href="/wiki/Joe_Simon" title="Joe Simon">Joe Simon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>, who had created <a href="/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a> and other characters and had instituted numerous industry innovations. Beginning as an inker on backgrounds, Ditko was soon working with and learning from <a href="/wiki/Mort_Meskin" title="Mort Meskin">Mort Meskin</a>, an artist whose work he had long admired. "Meskin was fabulous," Ditko once recalled. "I couldn't believe the ease with which he drew: strong compositions, loose pencils, yet complete; detail without clutter. I loved his stuff".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Ditko's known assistant work includes aiding inker Meskin on the Jack Kirby pencil work of <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Comics" title="Harvey Comics">Harvey Comics</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_3-D" title="Captain 3-D">Captain 3-D</a></i> #1 (Dec. 1953).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> For his own third published story, Ditko penciled and inked the six-page "A Hole in His Head" in <i>Black Magic</i> vol. 4, #3 (Dec. 1953), published by Simon &amp; Kirby's <a href="/wiki/Crestwood_Publications" title="Crestwood Publications">Crestwood Publications</a> imprint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prize_Comics" title="Prize Comics">Prize Comics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ditko then began a long association with the <a href="/wiki/Derby,_Connecticut" title="Derby, Connecticut">Derby, Connecticut</a> publisher <a href="/wiki/Charlton_Comics" title="Charlton Comics">Charlton Comics</a>, a low-budget division of a company best known for song-lyric magazines. Beginning with the cover of <i>The Thing</i> #12 (Feb. 1954) and the eight-page <a href="/wiki/Vampire" title="Vampire">vampire</a> story "Cinderella" in that issue, Ditko would continue to work intermittently for Charlton until the company's demise in 1986, producing science fiction, <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mystery_fiction" title="Mystery fiction">mystery</a> stories, as well as co-creating <a href="/wiki/Captain_Atom" title="Captain Atom">Captain Atom</a>, with writer <a href="/wiki/Joe_Gill" title="Joe Gill">Joe Gill</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Adventures_(comics)" title="Space Adventures (comics)">Space Adventures</a></i> #33 (March 1960).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He first went on hiatus from the company, and comics altogether, in mid-1954, when he contracted <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> and returned to his parents' home in Johnstown to recuperate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strangesuspense_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-strangesuspense-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Comics">Marvel Comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marvel Comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After he recovered and moved back to New York City in late 1955,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strangesuspense_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-strangesuspense-16">[16]</a></sup> Ditko began drawing for <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Comics_(1950s)" title="Atlas Comics (1950s)">Atlas Comics</a>, the 1950s precursor of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>, beginning with the four-page "There'll Be Some Changes Made" in <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_into_Mystery" title="Journey into Mystery">Journey into Mystery</a></i> #33 (April 1956); this debut tale would be reprinted in Marvel's <i>Curse of the Weird</i> #4 (March 1994). Ditko would go on to contribute a large number of stories, many considered classic, to Atlas/Marvel's <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Tales" title="Strange Tales">Strange Tales</a></i> and the newly launched <i><a href="/wiki/Amazing_Adventures" title="Amazing Adventures">Amazing Adventures</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Worlds" title="Strange Worlds">Strange Worlds</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Suspense" title="Tales of Suspense">Tales of Suspense</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_to_Astonish" title="Tales to Astonish">Tales to Astonish</a></i>, issues of which would typically open with a Kirby-drawn monster story, followed by one or two twist-ending thrillers or sci-fi tales drawn by <a href="/wiki/Don_Heck" title="Don Heck">Don Heck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Reinman" title="Paul Reinman">Paul Reinman</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Joe_Sinnott" title="Joe Sinnott">Joe Sinnott</a>, all capped by an often-surreal, sometimes self-reflexive short by Ditko and writer-editor <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>These Lee-Ditko short stories proved so popular that <i>Amazing Adventures</i> was reformatted to feature such stories exclusively beginning with issue #7 (Dec. 1961), when the comic was rechristened <i>Amazing Adult Fantasy</i> â a name intended to reflect its more "sophisticated" nature, as likewise the new tagline "The magazine that respects your intelligence". Lee in 2009 described these "short, five-page filler strips that Steve and I did together", originally "placed in any of our comics that had a few extra pages to fill", as "odd fantasy tales that I'd dream up with <a href="/wiki/O._Henry" title="O. Henry">O. Henry</a>-type endings." Giving an early example of what would later be known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marvel_Method" title="Marvel Method">Marvel Method</a>" of writer-artist collaboration, Lee said, "All I had to do was give Steve a one-line description of the plot and he'd be off and running. He'd take those skeleton outlines I had given him and turn them into classic little works of art that ended up being far cooler than I had any right to expect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_Spider-Man">Creation of Spider-Man</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Creation of Spider-Man">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AMZ-Ann1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="462" data-file-width="300" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dc/AMZ-Ann1.jpg/220px-AMZ-Ann1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/dc/AMZ-Ann1.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AMZ-Ann1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Amazing Spider-Man Annual</i> #1 (1964). Cover art by Ditko, featuring a rogue's gallery of supervillains he co-created.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After Marvel Comics editor-in-chief Stan Lee obtained permission from publisher <a href="/wiki/Martin_Goodman_(publisher)" title="Martin Goodman (publisher)">Martin Goodman</a> to create a new "ordinary teen" superhero named "Spider-Man",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Lee originally approached his leading artist, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>. Kirby told Lee about his own 1950s character conception, variously called the Silver Spider and Spiderman, in which an orphaned boy finds a magic ring that gives him superpowers. Comics historian <a href="/wiki/Greg_Theakston" title="Greg Theakston">Greg Theakston</a> says Lee and Kirby "immediately sat down for a story conference" and Lee afterward directed Kirby to flesh out the character and draw some pages. "A day or two later", Kirby showed Lee the first six pages, and, as Lee recalled, "I hated the way he was doing it. Not that he did it badlyÂ â it just wasn't the character I wanted; it was too heroic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lee turned to Ditko, who developed a visual motif Lee found satisfactory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> although Lee would later replace Ditko's original cover with one penciled by Kirby. Ditko said, "The Spider-Man pages Stan showed me were nothing like the (eventually) published character. In fact, the only drawings of Spider-Man were on the splash [<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I.e." title="I.e.">i.e.</a>, page 1] and at the end [where] Kirby had the guy leaping at you with a web gun... Anyway, the first five pages took place in the home, and the kid finds a ring and turns into Spider-Man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ditko also recalled that, "One of the first things I did was to work up a costume. A vital, visual part of the character. I had to know how he looked ... before I did any breakdowns. For example: A clinging power so he wouldn't have hard shoes or boots, a hidden wrist-shooter versus a web gun and holster, etc. ... I wasn't sure Stan would like the idea of covering the character's face but I did it because it hid an obviously boyish face. It would also add mystery to the character...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much earlier, in a rare contemporaneous account, Ditko described his and Lee's contributions in a mail interview with Gary Martin published in <i>Comic Fan</i> #2 (Summer 1965): "Stan Lee thought the name up. I did costume, web gimmick on wrist &amp; spider signal". He added he would continue drawing Spider-Man "[i]f nothing better comes along."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comicfan2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-comicfan2-24">[24]</a></sup> That same year, he expressed to the fanzine <i>Voice of Comicdom</i>, regarding a poll of "Best Liked" fan-created comics, "It seems a shame, since comics themselves have so little variety of stories and styles that you would deliberately restrict your own creative efforts to professional comics['] shallow range. What is 'Best Liked' by most readers is what they are most familiar in seeing and any policy based on readers likes has to end up with a lot of look-a-like (<a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a>) strips. You have a great opportunity to show everyone a whole new range of ideas, unlimited types of stories and stylesâwhy FLUB it!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1958 to either 1966,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ditkostanton_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ditkostanton-26">[26]</a></sup> or 1968<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theakston-reader-p14_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-theakston-reader-p14-27">[27]</a></sup> (accounts differ), Ditko shared a <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> studio at 43rd Street and Eighth Avenue with noted fetish artist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Stanton" title="Eric Stanton">Eric Stanton</a>, an art-school classmate. When either artist was under deadline pressure, it was not uncommon for them to pitch in and help the other with his assignment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ditkostanton_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-ditkostanton-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Ditko biographer Blake Bell, without citing sources, said, "At one time in history, Ditko denied ever touching Stanton's work, even though Stanton himself said they would each dabble in each other's art; mainly spot-inking",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ditkostanton_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-ditkostanton-26">[26]</a></sup> and the introduction to one book of Stanton's work says, "Eric Stanton drew his pictures in <a href="/wiki/India_ink" title="India ink">India ink</a>, and they were then hand-coloured by Ditko".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In a 1988 interview with Theakston, Stanton recalled that although his contribution to Spider-Man was "almost nil", he and Ditko had "worked on storyboards together and I added a few ideas. But the whole thing was created by Steve on his own... I think I added the business about the webs coming out of his hands".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theakston-reader-p14_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-theakston-reader-p14-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spider-Man debuted in <i><a href="/wiki/Amazing_Fantasy" title="Amazing Fantasy">Amazing Fantasy</a></i> #15 (Aug. 1962), the final issue of that science-fiction/fantasy anthology series. When the issue proved to be a top seller, Spider-Man was given his own series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Spider-Man" title="The Amazing Spider-Man">The Amazing Spider-Man</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Lee and Ditko's collaboration on the series saw the creation of many of the character's best known antagonists including <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Octopus" title="Doctor Octopus">Doctor Octopus</a> in issue #3 (July 1963);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Sandman_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Sandman (Marvel Comics)">Sandman</a> in #4 (Sept. 1963);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Lizard_(comics)" title="Lizard (comics)">Lizard</a> in #6 (Nov. 1963);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Electro_(comics)" title="Electro (comics)">Electro</a> in #9 (March 1964);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Goblin" title="Green Goblin">Green Goblin</a> in #14 (July 1964).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Ditko eventually demanded credit for the plotting he was contributing under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marvel_Method" title="Marvel Method">Marvel Method</a>. Lee concurred, and starting with #25 (June 1965), Ditko received plot credit for the stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most celebrated issues of the Lee-Ditko run is #33 (Feb. 1966), the third part of the story arc "<a href="/wiki/If_This_Be_My_Destiny...!" title="If This Be My Destiny...!">If This Be My Destiny...!</a>", and featuring the dramatic scene of Spider-Man, through force of will and thoughts of family, escaping from being pinned by heavy machinery. Comics historian <a href="/wiki/Les_Daniels" title="Les Daniels">Les Daniels</a> noted, "Steve Ditko squeezes every ounce of anguish out of Spider-Man's predicament, complete with visions of the uncle he failed and the aunt he has sworn to save."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DanielsMarvel_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-DanielsMarvel-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_David" title="Peter David">Peter David</a> observed, "After his origin, this two-page sequence from <i>Amazing Spider-Man</i> #33 is perhaps the best-loved sequence from the Stan Lee/Steve Ditko era."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Steve Saffel stated the "full page Ditko image from <i>The Amazing Spider-Man</i> #33 is one of the most powerful ever to appear in the series and influenced writers and artists for many years to come."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saffel_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-saffel-40">[40]</a></sup> Matthew K. Manning wrote that "Ditko's illustrations for the first few pages of this Lee story included what would become one of the most iconic scenes in Spider-Man's history."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The story was chosen as #15 in the <a href="/wiki/100_Greatest_Marvels_of_All_Time" title="100 Greatest Marvels of All Time">100 Greatest Marvels of All Time</a> poll of Marvel's readers in 2001. Editor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Greenberger" title="Robert Greenberger">Robert Greenberger</a> wrote in his introduction to the story, "These first five pages are a modern-day equivalent to Shakespeare as Parker's soliloquy sets the stage for his next action. And with dramatic pacing and storytelling, Ditko delivers one of the great sequences in all comics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Doctor_Strange_and_other_characters">Doctor Strange and other characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Doctor Strange and other characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DitkoEternity.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="250" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/68/DitkoEternity.jpg" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DitkoEternity.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Dormammu" title="Dormammu">Dormammu</a> attacks <a href="/wiki/Eternity_(comics)" title="Eternity (comics)">Eternity</a> in a Ditko "Dr. Strange" panel from <i>Strange Tales</i> #146 (July 1966).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After drawing the final issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incredible_Hulk_(comic_book)" title="The Incredible Hulk (comic book)">The Incredible Hulk</a></i> (#6, March 1963), Ditko created<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> hero <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Tales" title="Strange Tales">Strange Tales</a></i> #110 (July 1963).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Ditko and Lee shortly thereafter relaunched a <a href="/wiki/Hulk_(comics)" title="Hulk (comics)">Hulk</a> series as a short feature in the <a href="/wiki/Anthology" title="Anthology">anthology</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_to_Astonish" title="Tales to Astonish">Tales to Astonish</a></i>, beginning with issue #60 (Oct. 1964). Ditko, inked by <a href="/wiki/George_Roussos" title="George Roussos">George Roussos</a>, penciled the feature through #67 (May 1965). Ditko designed the Hulk's primary antagonist, the <a href="/wiki/Leader_(comics)" title="Leader (comics)">Leader</a>, in #62 (Dec. 1964).</p>
<p>Ditko also penciled the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">Iron Man</a> feature in <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Suspense" title="Tales of Suspense">Tales of Suspense</a></i> #47â49 (Nov. 1963Â â Jan. 1964), with various inkers. The first of these debuted the initial version of Iron Man's modern red-and-golden armor, though whether Ditko or cover-penciler and principal character designer <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a> designed the costume is uncertain.</p>
<p>Though often overshadowed by his <i>Amazing Spider-Man</i> work, Ditko's "Doctor Strange" artwork has been equally acclaimed, for its <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealistic</a> mystical landscapes and increasingly psychedelic visuals that helped make the feature a favorite of college students. "People who read 'Doctor Strange' thought people at Marvel must be heads [i.e. drug users]," recalled then-associate editor and former Doctor Strange writer <a href="/wiki/Roy_Thomas" title="Roy Thomas">Roy Thomas</a> in 1971, "because they had had similar experiences high on <a href="/wiki/Psilocybin_mushroom" title="Psilocybin mushroom">mushrooms</a>. But ... I don't use hallucinogens, nor do I think any artists do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eventually Lee &amp; Ditko would take Strange into ever-more-abstract realms. In an epic 17-issue story arc in <i>Strange Tales</i> #130â146 (March 1965 â July 1966), Lee &amp; Ditko introduced the cosmic character <a href="/wiki/Eternity_(comics)" title="Eternity (comics)">Eternity</a>, who personified the universe and was depicted as a silhouette whose outlines are filled with the cosmos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st134_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-st134-47">[47]</a></sup> As historian Bradford W. Wright describes,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Steve Ditko contributed some of his most surrealistic work to the comic book and gave it a disorienting, hallucinogenic quality. Dr. Strange's adventures take place in bizarre worlds and twisting dimensions that resembled <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador DalÃ­">Salvador DalÃ­</a> paintings. ... Inspired by the pulp-fiction magicians of Stan Lee's childhood as well as by contemporary <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">Beat</a> culture. Dr. Strange remarkably predicted the youth <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">counterculture</a>'s fascination with Eastern mysticism and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">psychedelia</a>. Never among Marvel's more popular or accessible characters, Dr. Strange still found a niche among an audience seeking a challenging alternative to more conventional superhero fare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">cartoonist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_artist" title="Fine artist">fine artist</a> <a href="/wiki/Seth_(cartoonist)" title="Seth (cartoonist)">Seth</a> in 2003 described Ditko's style as "oddball for mainstream comics. Whereas Kirby's stuff clearly appealed to a boy's sensibility because there was so much raw power, Ditko's work was really delicate and cartoony. There was a sense of design to it. You can always recognize anything that Ditko designed because it's always flowery. There is a lot of embroidered detail in the art, which is almost psychedelic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whichever feature he drew, Ditko's idiosyncratic, cleanly detailed, instantly recognizable art style, emphasizing mood and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anxiety_(mood)" title="Anxiety (mood)">anxiety</a>, found great favor with readers. The character of Spider-Man and his troubled personal life meshed well with Ditko's own interests, which Lee eventually acknowledged by giving the artist plotting credits on the latter part of their 38-issue run. But after four years on the title, Ditko left Marvel;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> he and Lee had not been on speaking terms for some time, with art and editorial changes handled through intermediaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomsinclair_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomsinclair-51">[51]</a></sup> The details of the rift remain uncertain, even to Lee, who confessed in 2003, "I never really knew Steve on a personal level."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomsinclair_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-tomsinclair-51">[51]</a></sup> Ditko later claimed it was Lee who broke off contact and disputed the long-held belief<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ross_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ross-52">[52]</a></sup> that the disagreement was over the true identity of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Goblin" title="Green Goblin">Green Goblin</a>: "Stan never knew what he was getting in my Spider-Man stories and covers until after [production manager] <a href="/wiki/Sol_Brodsky" title="Sol Brodsky">Sol Brodsky</a> took the material from me ... so there couldn't have been any disagreement or agreement, no exchanges ... no problems between us concerning the Green Goblin or anything else from before issue #25 to my final issues".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiz_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiz-53">[53]</a></sup> Spider-Man successor artist <a href="/wiki/John_Romita_Sr." title="John Romita Sr.">John Romita</a>, in a 2010 <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(law)" title="Deposition (law)">deposition</a>, recalled that Lee and Ditko "ended up not being able to work together because they disagreed on almost everything, cultural, social, historically, everything, they disagreed on characters...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> A friendly farewell was given to Ditko in the "<a href="/wiki/Bullpen_Bulletins" title="Bullpen Bulletins">Bullpen Bulletins</a>" of <i>Fantastic Four</i> #52 (July 1966): "Steve recently told us he was leaving for personal reasons. After all these years, we're sorry to see him go, and we wish the talented guy success with his future endeavors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regardless, said Lee in 2007, "Quite a few years ago I met him up at the Marvel offices when I was last in New York. And we spoke; he's a hell of a nice guy and it was very pleasant. ... I haven't heard from him since that meeting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charlton_and_DC_Comics">Charlton and DC Comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Charlton and DC Comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Back at CharltonÂ â where the page rate was low but creators were allowed greater freedomÂ â Ditko worked on such characters as the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Beetle" title="Blue Beetle">Blue Beetle</a> (1967â1968),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Question_(comics)" title="Question (comics)">Question</a> (1967â1968), and <a href="/wiki/Captain_Atom" title="Captain Atom">Captain Atom</a> (1965â1967), returning to the character he'd co-created in 1960. In addition, in 1966â1967, he drew 16 stories, most of them written by <a href="/wiki/Archie_Goodwin_(comics)" title="Archie Goodwin (comics)">Archie Goodwin</a> for <a href="/wiki/Warren_Publishing" title="Warren Publishing">Warren Publishing</a>'s horror comic magazines <i><a href="/wiki/Creepy" title="Creepy">Creepy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eerie" title="Eerie">Eerie</a></i>, most of which were done using <a href="/wiki/Wash_(visual_arts)" title="Wash (visual arts)">ink-wash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1967, Ditko gave his Objectivist ideas ultimate expression in the form of <a href="/wiki/Mr._A" title="Mr. A">Mr. A</a>, published in <a href="/wiki/Wally_Wood" title="Wally Wood">Wally Wood</a>'s independent title <i><a href="/wiki/Witzend" title="Witzend">witzend</a></i> #Â 3. Ditko's hard line against criminals was controversial<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ditko returned to Mr. A in 2000 and in 2009.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Ditko moved to <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> in 1968, where he co-created the <a href="/wiki/Creeper_(comics)" title="Creeper (comics)">Creeper</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Showcase_(comics)" title="Showcase (comics)">Showcase</a></i> #73 (April 1968) with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Segall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Don Segall (page does not exist)">Don Segall</a>, under editor <a href="/wiki/Murray_Boltinoff" title="Murray Boltinoff">Murray Boltinoff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> DC Comics writer and executive <a href="/wiki/Paul_Levitz" title="Paul Levitz">Paul Levitz</a> observed that Ditko's art on the <i>Creeper</i> stories made "them look unlike anything else being published by DC at the time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Ditko co-created the team <a href="/wiki/Hawk_and_Dove" title="Hawk and Dove">Hawk and Dove</a> in <i>Showcase</i> #75 (June 1968), with writer <a href="/wiki/Steve_Skeates" title="Steve Skeates">Steve Skeates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Around this time, he penciled the lead story, written and inked by Wally Wood, in Wood's early mature-audience, <a href="/wiki/Alternative_media" title="Alternative media">independent-comics</a> publication <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes,_Inc._Presents_Cannon" title="Heroes, Inc. Presents Cannon">Heroes, Inc. Presents Cannon</a></i> (1969).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ditko's stay at DC was shortÂ â he would work on all six issues of the Creeper's own title, <i>Beware the Creeper</i> (June 1968 â April 1969), though leaving midway through the final oneÂ â and the reasons for his departure uncertain. But while at DC, Ditko recommended Charlton staffer <a href="/wiki/Dick_Giordano" title="Dick Giordano">Dick Giordano</a> to the company,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> who would go on to become a top DC penciller, inker, editor, and ultimately, in 1981, the managing editor.</p>
<p>From this time up through the mid-1970s, Ditko worked exclusively for Charlton and various <a href="/wiki/Alternative_comics" title="Alternative comics">small press/independent</a> publishers. <a href="/wiki/Frank_McLaughlin_(artist)" title="Frank McLaughlin (artist)">Frank McLaughlin</a>, Charlton's art director during this period, describes Ditko as living "in a local hotel in <a href="/wiki/Derby,_Connecticut" title="Derby, Connecticut">Derby</a> for a while. He was a very happy-go-lucky guy with a great sense of humor at that time, and always supplied the [female] color separators with candy and other little gifts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBA9_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBA9-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>For Charlton in 1974 he did <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Belle_(comics)#Charlton_Comics" title="Liberty Belle (comics)">Liberty Belle</a> backup stories in <i><a href="/wiki/E-Man" title="E-Man">E-Man</a></i> and conceived <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Killjoy_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Killjoy (comics) (page does not exist)">Killjoy</a>. Ditko produced much work for Charlton's science-fiction and horror titles, as well as for former Marvel publisher <a href="/wiki/Martin_Goodman_(publisher)" title="Martin Goodman (publisher)">Martin Goodman</a>'s start-up line <a href="/wiki/Atlas/Seaboard_Comics" title="Atlas/Seaboard Comics">Atlas/Seaboard Comics</a>, where he co-created the superhero the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Destructor_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Destructor (comics) (page does not exist)">Destructor</a> with writer Archie Goodwin, and penciled all four issues of the namesake series (Feb.âAug. 1975), the first two of which were inked by Wally Wood. Ditko worked on the second and third issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Tiger-Man" title="Tiger-Man">Tiger-Man</a></i> and the third issue of <i>Morlock 2001</i>, with <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Wrightson" title="Bernie Wrightson">Bernie Wrightson</a> inking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latter-day_Ditko">Latter-day Ditko</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Latter-day Ditko">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ditko returned to DC Comics in 1975, creating a short-lived title, <i><a href="/wiki/Shade,_the_Changing_Man" title="Shade, the Changing Man">Shade, the Changing Man</a></i> (1977â1978).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Shade was later revived, without Ditko's involvement, in DC's mature-audience imprint <a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(comics)" title="Vertigo (comics)">Vertigo</a>. With writer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Levitz" title="Paul Levitz">Paul Levitz</a>, he co-created the four issue <a href="/wiki/Sword_and_sorcery" title="Sword and sorcery">sword and sorcery</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Stalker_(comics)" title="Stalker (comics)">Stalker</a></i> (1975â1976).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Ditko and writer <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Conway" title="Gerry Conway">Gerry Conway</a> produced the first issue of a short-lived <i><a href="/wiki/Man-Bat" title="Man-Bat">Man-Bat</a></i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> He also revived the Creeper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> and did such various other jobs as a short <a href="/wiki/Etrigan_the_Demon" title="Etrigan the Demon">Demon</a> backup series in 1979 and stories in DC's horror and science-fiction anthologies. Editor <a href="/wiki/Jack_C._Harris" title="Jack C. Harris">Jack C. Harris</a> hired Ditko as guest artist on several issues of <i>The <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Super-Heroes" title="Legion of Super-Heroes">Legion of Super-Heroes</a></i>, a decision which garnered a mixed reaction from the title's readership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PartTwo_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-PartTwo-70">[70]</a></sup> Ditko also drew the Prince Gavyn version of <a href="/wiki/Starman_(comics)" title="Starman (comics)">Starman</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Adventure_Comics" title="Adventure Comics">Adventure Comics</a></i> #467â478 (1980).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-3"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> He then decamped to do work for a variety of publishers, briefly contributing to DC again in the mid-1980s, with four pinups of his characters for <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Who_in_the_DC_Universe" title="Who's Who in the DC Universe">Who's Who in the DC Universe</a></i> and a pinup for <i><a href="/wiki/Superman_(comic_book)" title="Superman (comic book)">Superman</a></i> #400 (Oct. 1984)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> and its companion portfolio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ditko returned to Marvel in 1979, taking over Jack Kirby's <i><a href="/wiki/Machine_Man" title="Machine Man">Machine Man</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> drawing <i>The <a href="/wiki/Micronauts_(comics)" title="Micronauts (comics)">Micronauts</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Captain_Universe" title="Captain Universe">Captain Universe</a>, and continuing to freelance for the company into the late 1990s. Starting in 1984, he penciled the last two years of the series <a href="/wiki/Rom_(comics)" title="Rom (comics)">Rom</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Godzilla_(comics)" title="Godzilla (comics)">Godzilla</a> story by Ditko and <a href="/wiki/Marv_Wolfman" title="Marv Wolfman">Marv Wolfman</a> was changed into a <a href="/wiki/Dragon_Lord_(comics)" title="Dragon Lord (comics)">Dragon Lord</a> story published in <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Spotlight" title="Marvel Spotlight">Marvel Spotlight</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Ditko and writer <a href="/wiki/Tom_DeFalco" title="Tom DeFalco">Tom DeFalco</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Robbie_Baldwin" title="Robbie Baldwin">Speedball</a> character in <i>The Amazing Spider-Man Annual</i> #22 (1988)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> and Ditko drew a ten-issue series based on the character.</p>
<p>In 1982, he also began freelancing for the early independent comics label <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Comics" title="Pacific Comics">Pacific Comics</a>, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Victory_and_the_Galactic_Rangers" title="Captain Victory and the Galactic Rangers">Captain Victory and the Galactic Rangers</a></i> #6 (Sept. 1982), in which he introduced the superhero <a href="/wiki/Missing_Man" title="Missing Man">Missing Man</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Mark_Evanier" title="Mark Evanier">Mark Evanier</a> scripting to Ditko's plot and art. Subsequent Missing Man stories appeared in <i>Pacific Presents</i> #1â3 (Oct. 1982 â March 1984), with Ditko scripting the former and collaborating with longtime friend <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robin_Snyder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robin Snyder (page does not exist)">Robin Snyder</a> on the script for the latter two. Ditko also created <a href="/wiki/Mocker_(comics)" title="Mocker (comics)">The Mocker</a> for Pacific, in <i><a href="/wiki/Silver_Star_(comics)" title="Silver Star (comics)">Silver Star</a></i> #2 (April 1983).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-4"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_Comics" title="Eclipse Comics">Eclipse Comics</a>, he contributed a story featuring his character <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Static_(Steve_Ditko_character)" title="Static (Steve Ditko character)">Static</a> (no relation to the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milestone_Comics" title="Milestone Comics">Milestone Comics</a> character) in <i><a href="/wiki/Eclipse_Monthly" title="Eclipse Monthly">Eclipse Monthly</a></i> #1â3 (Aug.âOct. 1983), introducing <a href="/wiki/Supervillain" title="Supervillain">supervillain</a> the Exploder in #2. With writer Jack C. Harris, Ditko drew the backup feature "The Faceless Ones" in <a href="/wiki/First_Comics" title="First Comics">First Comics</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warp_(First_Comics)" title="Warp (First Comics)">Warp</a></i> #2â4 (AprilâJune 1983). Working with that same writer and others, Ditko drew a handful of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fly_(Red_Circle_Comics)" title="Fly (Red Circle Comics)">Fly</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fly-Girl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fly-Girl (page does not exist)">Fly-Girl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_(Archie_Comics)" title="Jaguar (Archie Comics)">Jaguar</a> stories for <i>The Fly</i> #2â8 (July 1983 â Aug. 1984), for <a href="/wiki/Archie_Comics" title="Archie Comics">Archie Comics</a>' short-lived 1980s superhero line; in a rare latter-day instance of Ditko <a href="/wiki/Inker" title="Inker">inking</a> another artist, he inked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penciler" title="Penciler">penciler</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Ayers" title="Dick Ayers">Dick Ayers</a> on the Jaguar story in <i>The Fly</i> #9 (Oct. 1984).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-5"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Western_Publishing" title="Western Publishing">Western Publishing</a> in 1982 announced a series by Ditko and Harris would appear in a new science-fiction comic, <i>Astral Frontiers</i>, but that title never materialized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992 Ditko worked with writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Will_Murray_(writer)" title="Will Murray (writer)">Will Murray</a> to produce one of his last original characters for Marvel Comics, the satirical superheroine <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_Girl" title="Squirrel Girl">Squirrel Girl</a>, who debuted in <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Super-Heroes_(comics)" title="Marvel Super-Heroes (comics)">Marvel Super-Heroes</a></i> vol. 2, #8, a.k.a. <i>Marvel Super-Heroes Winter Special</i> (Jan. 1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd-msh_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-msh-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, he did the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Horse_Comics" title="Dark Horse Comics">Dark Horse Comics</a> <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">one-shot</a> <i>The Safest Place in the World</i>. For the <a href="/wiki/Defiant_Comics" title="Defiant Comics">Defiant Comics</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Dominion" title="Dark Dominion">Dark Dominion</a>,</i> he drew issue #0, which was released as a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trading_cards" title="Trading cards">trading cards</a>. In 1995, he pencilled a four-issue series for Marvel based on the <i><a href="/wiki/Phantom_2040" title="Phantom 2040">Phantom 2040</a></i> animated TV series. This included a poster that was inked by <a href="/wiki/John_Romita_Sr." title="John Romita Sr.">John Romita Sr.</a> <i>Steve Ditko's Strange Avenging Tales</i> was announced as a quarterly series from <a href="/wiki/Fantagraphics_Books" title="Fantagraphics Books">Fantagraphics Books</a>, although it only ran one issue (Feb. 1997) due to publicly unspecified disagreements between Ditko and the publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> assessed in 2008 that, "By the '70s he was regarded as a slightly old-fashioned odd-ball; by the '80s he was a commercial has-been, picking up wretched work-for-hire gigs. ...following the example of [Ayn] Rand's John Galt, Ditko hacked out moneymaking work, saving his care for the crabbed Objectivist screeds he published with tiny presses. And boy, could Ditko hack: seeing samples of his <a href="/wiki/Transformers" title="Transformers">Transformers</a> coloring book and his Big Boy comic is like hearing <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> sell frozen peas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ditko retired from mainstream comics in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> His later work for Marvel and DC included such established superheroes as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-Mariner" title="Sub-Mariner">Sub-Mariner</a> (in <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics_Presents" title="Marvel Comics Presents">Marvel Comics Presents</a></i>) and newer, licensed characters such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Mighty_Morphin_Power_Rangers" title="Mighty Morphin Power Rangers">Mighty Morphin Power Rangers</a></i>. The last mainstream character he created was Marvel's Longarm in <i>Shadows &amp; Light</i> #1 (Feb. 1998), in a self-inked, 12-page <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">Iron Man</a> story "A Man's Reach....", scripted by <a href="/wiki/Len_Wein" title="Len Wein">Len Wein</a>. His final mainstream work was a five-page <a href="/wiki/New_Gods" title="New Gods">New Gods</a> story for DC Comics, "Infinitely Gentle Infinitely Suffering", inked by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mick_Gray&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mick Gray (page does not exist)">Mick Gray</a> and believed to be intended for the 2000â2002 <i>Orion</i> series<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-df_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-df-85">[85]</a></sup> but not published until the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Trade_paperback_(comics)" title="Trade paperback (comics)">trade paperback</a> <i>Tales of the New Gods</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-df_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-df-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since then, Ditko's solo work has been published intermittently by Robin Snyder, who was his editor at Charlton, Archie Comics, and <a href="/wiki/Renegade_Press" title="Renegade Press">Renegade Press</a> in the 1980s. The Snyder publications have included a number of original books as well as reprints such as <i>Static</i>, <i>The Missing Man</i>, <i>The Mocker</i> and, in 2002, <i>Avenging World</i>, a collection of stories and essays spanning 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-6"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, Ditko and Snyder released <i>The Avenging Mind</i>, a 32-page essay publication featuring several pages of new artwork; and <i>Ditko, Etc...</i>, a 32-page comic book composed of brief vignettes and editorial cartoons. Releases have continued in that format, with stories introducing such characters as the Hero, Miss Eerie, the Cape, the Madman, the Grey Negotiator, theÂ !? and the Outline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> He said in 2012 of his self-published efforts, âI do those because thatâs all theyâll let me do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorkpost2012_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-newyorkpost2012-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the new material, Ditko and Snyder have reprinted earlier Ditko material. In 2010 they published a new edition of the 1973 <i>Mr. A</i> comic and a selection of Ditko covers in <i>The Cover Series</i>. In 2011 they published a new edition of the 1975 comic <i>...Wha...!? Ditko's H. Series</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcd_62-7"><a href="#cite_note-gcd-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two "lost" stories drawn by Ditko in 1978 have been published by DC in hardcover collections of the artist's work. A Creeper story scheduled for the never published <i><a href="/wiki/Showcase_(comics)" title="Showcase (comics)">Showcase</a></i> #106 appears in <i>The Creeper by Steve Ditko</i> (2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> and an unpublished <i>Shade, the Changing Man</i> story appears in <i>The Steve Ditko Omnibus Vol. 1</i> (2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Hulk_(comics)" title="Hulk (comics)">Hulk</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Human_Torch" title="Human Torch">Human Torch</a> story written by Jack C. Harris and drawn by Ditko in the 1980s was published by Marvel as <i>Incredible Hulk and the Human Torch: From the Marvel Vault</i> #1 in August 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of mid-2012, Ditko continues to work at a studio in Manhattan's Midtown West neighborhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorkpost2012_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-newyorkpost2012-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> He has refused to give interviews or make public appearances since the 1960s, explaining in 1969 that, "When I do a job, it's not my personality that I'm offering the readers but my artwork. It's not what I'm like that counts; it's what I did and how well it was done.... I produce a product, a comic art story. Steve Ditko is the brand name".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> He has, however, contributed numerous essays to Robin Snyder's fanzine <i>The Comics</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ditko is an ardent supporter and advocate of the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Objectivism_(Ayn_Rand)" title="Objectivism (Ayn Rand)">Objectivism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has a nephew who is an artist, also named Steve Ditko.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomsinclair_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-tomsinclair-51">[51]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Will_Eisner" title="Will Eisner">Will Eisner</a>, Ditko has one son,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> but this may have conflated the nephew, as Ditko is not known to ever have married or had children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorkpost2012_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-newyorkpost2012-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ditko said in 2012 that he had made no income on the four <i>Spider-Man</i> films released to that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorkpost2012_87-3"><a href="#cite_note-newyorkpost2012-87">[87]</a></sup> However, a neighbor of Ditko's has stated that he has received royalty checks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BBC_documentary">BBC documentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Ditko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: BBC documentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2007, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ross" title="Jonathan Ross">Jonathan Ross</a> hosted a one-hour documentary for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">BBC Four</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Steve_Ditko" title="In Search of Steve Ditko">In Search of Steve Ditko</a></i>. The program covers Ditko's work at Marvel, DC, and Charlton Comics and at <a href="/wiki/Wally_Wood" title="Wally Wood">Wally Wood</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Witzend" title="Witzend">witzend</a></i>, as well as his following of <a href="/wiki/Objectivism_(Ayn_Rand)" title="Objectivism (Ayn Rand)">Objectivism</a>. It includes testimonials by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Millar" title="Mark Millar">Mark Millar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Robinson" title="Jerry Robinson">Jerry Robinson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, among others. Ross, accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">Neil Gaiman</a>, met Ditko briefly at his New York office but he declined to be filmed, interviewed or photographed for the show. He did however, give the two a selection of some comic books. At the end of the show, Ross said he has since spoken to Ditko on the telephone and continued to say, as a joke, that he was now on first name terms with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ross_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-ross-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
