<p><b>Stephen Francis O'Neill</b> (July 6, 1891 â January 26, 1962) was an American <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball" title="Professional baseball">professional</a> <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> player and <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steve_O.27Neill_statistics_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steve_O.27Neill_statistics-1">[1]</a></sup> He played in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a>, most notably with the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a>. As a manager, he led the <a href="/wiki/1945_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1945 Major League Baseball season">1945</a> <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> to the <a href="/wiki/1945_World_Series" title="1945 World Series">World Series championship</a>,</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball_career">Baseball career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baseball career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born to Irish immigrants in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minooka,_Pennsylvania" title="Minooka, Pennsylvania">Minooka, Pennsylvania</a> (now a part of <a href="/wiki/Scranton,_Pennsylvania" title="Scranton, Pennsylvania">Scranton</a>), O'Neill was one of four brothers who escaped a life in the coal mines by playing in the major leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus14_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus14-2">[2]</a></sup> Other notable members of the O'Neill family were <a href="/wiki/Jack_O%27Neill_(baseball)" title="Jack O'Neill (baseball)">Jack</a>, a catcher in the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (1902â06); <a href="/wiki/Mike_O%27Neill_(baseball)" title="Mike O'Neill (baseball)">Mike</a>, a right-handed pitcher in the NL (1901â04, 1907);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus14_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus14-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_O%27Neill_(baseball)" title="Jim O'Neill (baseball)">Jim</a>, an infielder with the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="Washington Senators (1901â60)">Washington Senators</a> (1920, 1923). Baseball historian William C. Kashatus noted that Michael and Jack "would become the first brother battery in major league history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus14_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus14-2">[2]</a></sup> The O'Neill brothers "were known to exchange their signals in <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Gaelic</a> in order to fool the opposing coaches".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus14_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus14-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, two of Steve O'Neill's daughters married professional baseball players, one of whom was <a href="/wiki/Skeeter_Webb" title="Skeeter Webb">Skeeter Webb</a>, who played under O'Neill in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball">minor leagues</a> in 1939 and again from 1945â47, when O'Neill piloted the Tigers.</p>
<p>Steve had by far the most successful playing career of the O'Neill brothers, serving as a catcher for 17 years in the American League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus101-103_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus101-103-3">[3]</a></sup> He played with the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> (1911â23), <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> (1924), <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> (1925), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis_Browns" title="St. Louis Browns">St. Louis Browns</a> (1927â28). His playing career curtailed by an injury sustained in a car accident, O'Neill compiled a <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a> of .263 in 1,586 games, and, in his only <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> appearance in <a href="/wiki/1920_World_Series" title="1920 World Series">1920</a>, hit .333 in seven games as the backstop for the world champion Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus101_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus101-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>When his playing career ended, O'Neill turned to managing in the minors and gained a reputation for cultivating talented young players, some of whom went on to become Hall of Famers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus103_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus103-5">[5]</a></sup> He managed the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Maple_Leafs_(International_League)" title="Toronto Maple Leafs (International League)">Toronto Maple Leafs</a> (1929â31), <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Mud_Hens" title="Toledo Mud Hens">Toledo Mud Hens</a> (1933â34), <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bisons" title="Buffalo Bisons">Buffalo Bisons</a> (1938â40) and <a href="/wiki/Beaumont_Exporters" title="Beaumont Exporters">Beaumont Exporters</a> (1942).</p>
<p>As a big league manager with four teamsâthe Indians (1935â37), Tigers (1943â48), Red Sox (1950â51) and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> (1952â54)âO'Neill never had a losing record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus103_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus103-5">[5]</a></sup> His Tigers won the <a href="/wiki/1945_World_Series" title="1945 World Series">1945 World Series</a> (when they defeated the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> in the Cubs' last Fall Classic appearance until <a href="/wiki/2016_World_Series" title="2016 World Series">2016</a>) and O'Neill was known for turning around under-performing teams, often in mid-season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus103_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus103-5">[5]</a></sup> His career winning percentage over 14 seasons was a stalwart .559 (1,040 victories against 821 lost). He also served as a coach for Cleveland, Detroit and Boston. Legendary players who benefited from O'Neill's guidance included <a href="/wiki/Lou_Boudreau" title="Lou Boudreau">Lou Boudreau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Feller" title="Bob Feller">Bob Feller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hal_Newhouser" title="Hal Newhouser">Hal Newhouser</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Roberts_(baseball)" title="Robin Roberts (baseball)">Robin Roberts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashatus103_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-kashatus103-5">[5]</a></sup> O'Neill was inducted into the International League Hall of Fame. He was also an inaugural member of the Cleveland Indians Hall of Fame.</p>
<p>O'Neill died at age 70 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, after suffering a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>, and is interred in St. Joseph's Cemetery, Minooka.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
