<p><b>Stephen Ray</b> "<b>Steve</b>" <b>Perry</b> (born January 22, 1949) is an American singer, songwriter, and record producer. He is best known as the lead singer of the rock band <a href="/wiki/Journey_(band)" title="Journey (band)">Journey</a> during their most commercially successful periods from 1977 to 1987 and again from 1995 to 1998. Perry had a successful solo career between the mid-1980s and mid-1990s.</p>
<p>Perry's singing has garnered acclaim from prominent musical peers and publications; he has been described as "The Voice", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moniker" title="Moniker">moniker</a> originally coined by friend and former chart peer <a href="/wiki/Jon_Bon_Jovi" title="Jon Bon Jovi">Jon Bon Jovi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He ranks No. 76 on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> <i>Magazine'</i>s 100 Greatest Singers Of All Time and will be inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> as a member of Journey on April 7, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rrhofjourney_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rrhofjourney-2">[2]</a></sup> Perry resides in <a href="/wiki/Del_Mar,_California" title="Del Mar, California">Del Mar, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Perry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stephen Ray Perry was born in <a href="/wiki/Hanford,_California" title="Hanford, California">Hanford, California</a> to Portuguese parents. Perry grew up interested in music, as his father, Raymond Perry (Pereira), was a vocalist and co-owner of radio station <a href="/wiki/KIGS" title="KIGS">KNGS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On his 12th birthday (January 22, 1961), his mother, Mary Quaresma, presented her son with a gold <a href="/wiki/Eighth_note" title="Eighth note">eighth note</a> necklace, which he still wears for good luck. When Perry was 12 years old, he heard <a href="/wiki/Sam_Cooke" title="Sam Cooke">Sam Cooke</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Cupid_(Sam_Cooke_song)" title="Cupid (Sam Cooke song)">Cupid</a>" on his mother's car radio. It inspired Perry to become a singer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>His family moved to <a href="/wiki/Lemoore,_California" title="Lemoore, California">Lemoore, California</a> during Perry's teen years. He attended high school there, drumming in the marching band as well as in extracurricular bands. He attended <a href="/wiki/College_of_the_Sequoias" title="College of the Sequoias">College of the Sequoias</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Visalia,_California" title="Visalia, California">Visalia, California</a>, for a short time after graduation, where he sang first <a href="/wiki/Tenor" title="Tenor">tenor</a> in the choir. Perry's mother encouraged his musical growth during this time.</p>
<p>In his early 20s, Perry moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento" title="Sacramento">Sacramento</a> to start a band with a 16-year-old future music producer <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mathews" title="Scott Mathews">Scott Mathews</a>, who co-wrote, played drums, guitar and sang. That band, Ice, wrote strong original material and were poised to "make it" as they recorded during the day at the <a href="/wiki/Record_Plant" title="Record Plant">Record Plant</a> studios in Los Angeles in 1972 while <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> recorded his <i><a href="/wiki/Talking_Book" title="Talking Book">Talking Book</a></i> album by night. Upon returning to Sacramento, Ice disbanded as the band had no management, Mathews was stuck in high school, and the recordings went virtually unheard. In 1975, Perry moved to <a href="/wiki/Thousand_Oaks,_California" title="Thousand Oaks, California">Thousand Oaks, California</a>, where he formed a <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> band called Pieces with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Bogert" title="Tim Bogert">Tim Bogert</a> (who had previously worked with <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck" title="Jeff Beck">Jeff Beck</a>), Denver Cross, and Eddie Tuduri. After a year and a half, the group was unable to secure a record deal and disbanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Perry then ended up in <a href="/wiki/Banta,_California" title="Banta, California">Banta, California</a> outside of <a href="/wiki/Tracy,_California" title="Tracy, California">Tracy, California</a>, where he fronted the band Alien Project in his mid-20s. He nearly gave up music when the bassist of that band, Richard Michaels, was killed in an automobile accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-May1985_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-May1985-8">[8]</a></sup> Perry returned to Lemoore and decided not to continue his singing career, but at the urging of his mother, Perry answered a call from <a href="/wiki/Walter_Herbert_(manager)" title="Walter Herbert (manager)">Walter "Herbie" Herbert</a>, manager of struggling San Francisco-based band Journey.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Journey_(band)" title="Journey (band)">Journey (band)</a></div>
<p>Original Journey organizer/manager Walter "Herbie" Herbert had been given a demo of an Alien Project song, "If You Need Me, Call Me", and was told by producer <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mathews" title="Scott Mathews">Scott Mathews</a> that the young singer would be a great replacement for then current frontman <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fleischman" title="Robert Fleischman">Robert Fleischman</a>. Fleischman had never signed with Herbert's company, preferring his previous manager and he had not fully coalesced with the band's then <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> style. Perry was brought on tour and to avoid alarming Fleischman, Perry was clandestinely included as roadie "John Villanueva's Portuguese cousin" and on the tour during a sound check in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a> surreptitiously performed a song with Journey while Fleischman was away from the stage, and after that Herbert informed the band members of the lineup change.</p>
<p>Perry brought a radically new, more pop-influenced style sense to the band's music, despite some grumblings from his new bandmates and fans of Journey's former progressive rock sound. He made his public debut on October 28, 1977 in San Francisco to a mixed reception. Perry eventually won over new fans on his first album with the group, <i><a href="/wiki/Infinity_(Journey_album)" title="Infinity (Journey album)">Infinity</a>,</i> which included a song he wrote called "<a href="/wiki/Lights_(Journey_song)" title="Lights (Journey song)">Lights</a>." The band's style had changed dramatically, but as Journey began to garner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airplay_(radio)" title="Airplay (radio)">radio airplay</a> and media buzz over <i>Infinity,</i> Perry's arrival was fully accepted.</p>
<p>Steve Perry provided lead vocals on nine of Journey's albums: <i>Infinity</i> (1978), <i><a href="/wiki/Evolution_(Journey_album)" title="Evolution (Journey album)">Evolution</a></i> (1979), <i><a href="/wiki/Departure_(Journey_album)" title="Departure (Journey album)">Departure</a></i> (1980), <i><a href="/wiki/Dream,_After_Dream" title="Dream, After Dream">Dream, After Dream</a></i> (1980, a Japanese movie soundtrack), <i><a href="/wiki/Captured_(Journey_album)" title="Captured (Journey album)">Captured</a></i> (1980, a live album), <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_(Journey_album)" title="Escape (Journey album)">Escape</a></i> (1981, which reached No. 1 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_chart" title="Billboard chart">Billboard chart</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Frontiers_(Journey_album)" title="Frontiers (Journey album)">Frontiers</a></i> (1983), <i><a href="/wiki/Raised_on_Radio" title="Raised on Radio">Raised on Radio</a></i> (1986), and <i><a href="/wiki/Trial_by_Fire_(Journey_album)" title="Trial by Fire (Journey album)">Trial By Fire</a></i> (1996). The single "Open Arms" from <i>Escape</i> was their biggest hit single, charting at No. 2 for six weeks on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>. Perry had become the unmistakable voice of Journey.</p>
<p>During his Journey tenure, Perry also sang backing vocals on several <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Hagar" title="Sammy Hagar">Sammy Hagar</a> songs, including the 1980 tracks "The Iceman" (a nickname Hagar had for <a href="/wiki/Scott_Mathews" title="Scott Mathews">Scott Mathews</a>) and "Run For Your Life", and duetting with <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Loggins" title="Kenny Loggins">Kenny Loggins</a> on the 1982 No. 17 hit single "Don't Fight It". Perry also worked with other musicians including <a href="/wiki/Sheena_Easton" title="Sheena Easton">Sheena Easton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clannad_(musical_group)" title="Clannad (musical group)">Clannad</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Bon_Jovi" title="Jon Bon Jovi">Jon Bon Jovi</a> during the height of his career. A 1983 Gallup poll of people between the ages of 13 and 25 voted Journey their favorite rock band.</p>
<p>In 1984, following the release of <i>Frontiers</i> and the tour supporting this effort, Perry released his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Talk" title="Street Talk">Street Talk</a></i> (the album's title was derived from the original name of Perry's earlier band Alien Project). The record sold more than 2 million units, scoring the hit singles No. 3 "<a href="/wiki/Oh_Sherrie" title="Oh Sherrie">Oh Sherrie</a>", written for his then-girlfriend Sherrie Swafford, and No. 18 "Foolish Heart". The music video for "Oh Sherrie" saw <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_rotation" title="Heavy rotation">heavy rotation</a> on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>. "She's Mine" and "Strung Out" were also released as singles from this project, which featured former Alien Project drummer Craig Krampf on a few tracks, guitarist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Landau" title="Michael Landau">Michael Landau</a>, and future <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a></i> judge <a href="/wiki/Randy_Jackson" title="Randy Jackson">Randy Jackson</a> on bass, among others.</p>
<p>In 1985, Perry was one of 21 singers in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA_for_Africa" title="USA for Africa">USA for Africa</a> all-star benefit song "<a href="/wiki/We_Are_the_World" title="We Are the World">We Are the World</a>". He also recorded a song, "If Only For the Moment, Girl" for the <i><a href="/wiki/We_Are_the_World_(album)" title="We Are the World (album)">We Are the World</a></i> album. This song was added to the reissue of his album <i>Street Talk</i>. Also during this period Perry worked with the Irish folk-rock group Clannad on their 1987 album <i><a href="/wiki/Sirius_(album)" title="Sirius (album)">Sirius</a></i>.</p>
<p>While Perry was reuniting with Journey, his mother became ill. The recording of <i>Raised on Radio</i>, which Perry was producing, was stop-and-go as he frequently returned to the San Joaquin Valley to visit his mother (who died during the production of the album). It took a major toll on Journey to have intermittent recording sessions and a vocalist who was not with the band much of the time. Eventually, as Perry later said, he was "toast" (exhausted from the ordeal). Journey then disbanded in 1987 after the <i>Raised on Radio</i> tour.</p>
<p>In 1988, Perry began to work on another solo album, <i>Against the Wall</i>, which he ultimately left unfinished (though several of the songs that were recorded for <i>Against the Wall</i> would appear on Perry's 1998 solo compilation, <i><a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_%2B_Five_Unreleased" title="Greatest Hits + Five Unreleased">Greatest Hits + Five Unreleased</a></i>). A year later, on April 30, 1989, Perry joined <a href="/wiki/Bon_Jovi" title="Bon Jovi">Bon Jovi</a> to perform Sam Cooke's "<a href="/wiki/Bring_It_On_Home_to_Me" title="Bring It On Home to Me">Bring It On Home to Me</a>" and the Four Tops' "<a href="/wiki/Reach_Out_I%27ll_Be_There" title="Reach Out I'll Be There">Reach Out</a>". He would also reunite with Journey at the <a href="/wiki/Bill_Graham_(promoter)" title="Bill Graham (promoter)">Bill Graham</a> tribute concert, "Laughter, Love and Music" on November 3, 1991, performing "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faithfully_(Journey_song)" title="Faithfully (Journey song)">Faithfully</a>" and "Lights". Other than those three events, however, Perry mostly disappeared from the public eye for seven years, taking a break from the music industry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1994_to_1998">1994 to 1998</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Perry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1994 to 1998">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1994 Perry released <i><a href="/wiki/For_the_Love_of_Strange_Medicine" title="For the Love of Strange Medicine">For the Love of Strange Medicine</a></i>, his second solo effort. The album enjoyed great success, partly due to the Strange Medicine world tour.</p>
<p>Journey's classic 1981–85 lineup reunited in 1996 to record <i><a href="/wiki/Trial_by_Fire_(Journey_album)" title="Trial by Fire (Journey album)">Trial by Fire</a></i>. The album was a huge success, entering the Billboard charts at No. 3 and going platinum before year's end, but its triumph was short-lived. Before the <i>Trial By Fire</i> tour could begin, Perry suffered a hip injury while hiking in Hawaii and was unable to perform. Perry was diagnosed with a degenerative bone condition and a hip replacement was required, and as he was reluctant to rush into the surgery, Perry wanted to postpone the tour.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, long-time Journey drummer <a href="/wiki/Steve_Smith_(musician)" title="Steve Smith (musician)">Steve Smith</a> resigned. (Smith had rejoined, along with original Journey bassist <a href="/wiki/Ross_Valory" title="Ross Valory">Ross Valory</a>.)</p>
<p>The remaining members waited until 1998, nearly 17 months after Perry's injury, before making a decision on Journey's future. Growing impatient and realizing the window of opportunity was closing to follow up the success of the platinum-selling <i>Trial By Fire</i> LP with a world tour, Journey members <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Cain" title="Jonathan Cain">Jonathan Cain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neal_Schon" title="Neal Schon">Neal Schon</a> met with Perry and presented an ultimatum that he either undergo hip replacement surgery so the tour could proceed upon his recovery, or a replacement singer would be hired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melody_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-melody-9">[9]</a></sup> Perry, still hesitant to undergo surgery and now apparently upset at his bandmates' intractability and their perceived meddling in personal health decisions, decided to leave Journey. Perry's lead vocal duties were later taken over by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Augeri" title="Steve Augeri">Steve Augeri</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tall_Stories_(band)" title="Tall Stories (band)">Tall Stories</a>, and nearly two years after the album's initial release, Journey began its long-postponed tour.</p>
<p>Perry underwent successful hip replacement surgery in 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melody_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-melody-9">[9]</a></sup> to correct the problem he had been diagnosed with two years earlier. He released the <i>Greatest Hits + Five Unreleased</i> <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> later in 1998; the unreleased tracks included an original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alien_Project_(band)" title="Alien Project (band)">Alien Project</a> demo as well as selections from the abandoned <i>Against the Wall</i> CD. Also in 1998, Perry recorded two songs for the <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Quest_for_Camelot" title="Quest for Camelot">Quest for Camelot</a>,</i> which can be found on the motion picture's soundtrack. Journey was the subject of an episode of <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Behind_the_Music" title="Behind the Music">Behind the Music</a></i> in 2001, where Perry made the statement that he "never really felt like [he] was part of the band," to which former manager <a href="/wiki/Walter_Herbert_(manager)" title="Walter Herbert (manager)">Herbie Herbert</a> reacted saying "that's like the Pope saying he never really felt Catholic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_21st_century">In the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Perry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Perry appeared with other Journey members at a ceremony on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a> on January 24, 2005, after previously stating it was unlikely that he would ever stand with the band again. He indicated that, though it was a good experience, his rejoining Journey is not likely. However, he has also stated "[n]ever say never, unless you mean never, nevertheless" when the issue of returning to Journey has been mentioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, Perry produced "The Secret of Moving On," a track on a solo album for former <a href="/wiki/Ambrosia_(band)" title="Ambrosia (band)">Ambrosia</a> lead vocalist <a href="/wiki/David_Pack" title="David Pack">David Pack</a>. Perry also provided background vocals for "A Brand New Start," among the many songs he and Pack co-wrote shortly after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>. That album released in September 2005 includes <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">covers</a> of two of Pack's biggest hits with Ambrosia, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biggest_Part_of_Me_(song)" title="Biggest Part of Me (song)">Biggest Part of Me</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_the_Only_Woman_(You_%26_I)" title="You're the Only Woman (You &amp; I)">You're the Only Woman</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/2005_in_baseball" title="2005 in baseball">2005 baseball season</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> adopted Journey's "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Stop_Believin%27" title="Don't Stop Believin'">Don't Stop Believin'</a>" as their unofficial team anthem. As a result, Perry (an avid <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> fan) was asked to attend the <a href="/wiki/2005_World_Series" title="2005 World Series">World Series</a> and even traveled with the White Sox to Houston where Perry joined the players on the field and in the locker room as they celebrated their championship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melody_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-melody-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2006, Perry's two solo projects, <i>Street Talk</i> and <i>For the Love of Strange Medicine</i> (both featuring previously unreleased material), and his <i>Greatest Hits</i> CD were remastered and re-released. Sony Legacy released <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playlist_(album_series)" title="Playlist (album series)">Playlist</a>: The Very Best of Steve Perry</i> on January 13, 2009, a compilation of some of his best songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> fan, Perry was spotlighted during their 2010 World Championship run. He was spotted in game 5 of the <a href="/wiki/National_League_Championship_Series" title="National League Championship Series">NLCS</a> leading the crowd in a singalong of "Don't Stop Believin'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the eighth inning of the second game of the <a href="/wiki/2010_World_Series" title="2010 World Series">2010 World Series</a> in San Francisco, fans at <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Park" title="AT&amp;T Park">AT&amp;T Park</a> began singing along to the song "Lights" by Journey. Perry was shown on the scoreboard singing, jumping and pumping up the crowd. The Giants went on to win 9–0 over the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2014, Perry was observed leading AT&amp;T Park in a chorus of "Don't Stop Believin'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(magazine)" title="Classic Rock (magazine)">Classic Rock Presents AOR</a>,</i> released in December 2010, Perry revealed that he had written many new songs and was contemplating his first solo project since 1994. "A little over a year and a half ago, I felt I could maybe come back to it, I have been writing. I have about 50 songs in a little over a year. I never thought I would do it again, but I decided to open up that room and see what I could find. It's been interesting. Some of it sounds familiar, some of it sounds contemporary, some of it you can't put a label on—and I've never been big on labels anyway." Perry is reluctant however to put a timescale on his return. "I'll be honest, I don't really know how to do that, but I can definitely smell a solo project on the horizon. I will be recording some music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2012 Giants versus Tigers World Series in San Francisco at AT&amp;T Park, Perry was shown on the scoreboard singing along to "Lights" once again.</p>
<p>In May 2013 Perry had a mole removed that turned out to be melanoma. He had two surgeries to remove the cancer cells and was told the surgeries were successful, requiring no further treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In a lengthy blog post in June, Perry wrote that he fell in love with psychologist and breast cancer survivor Kellie Nash, who died from cancer in December 2012, and that he himself suffered a recent cancer scare. Perry was by Nash's side as she battled cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 25, 2014, Perry joined the indie rock band <a href="/wiki/Eels_(band)" title="Eels (band)">Eels</a> onstage at the Fitzgerald Theater in St. Paul, Minnesota, for the final three songs of their encore, singing Eels' "It's a Motherfucker" followed by Journey's "<a href="/wiki/Open_Arms_(Journey_song)" title="Open Arms (Journey song)">Open Arms</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lovin%27,_Touchin%27,_Squeezin%27" title="Lovin', Touchin', Squeezin'">Lovin', Touchin', Squeezin'</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This marked the first time he had performed on stage since the end of his Strange Medicine world tour in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Perry performed with The Eels again May 31 at the Lincoln Theater in Washington, D.C., singing the same three songs previously sung in St. Paul, MN plus a cover of Sam Cooke's "Only Sixteen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Perry joined the Eels a third time June 11 at L.A.'s Orpheum Theater. In addition to the same three songs previously performed, he added the Journey hit 'Lights' explaining to the audience that he wrote the song originally for L.A., but after receiving a call to join Journey, the song was changed to say 'city by the Bay'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, Perry announced that he was completing a new album, which he stated was to be released in 2016, but it has yet to materialize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocal_style">Vocal style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Perry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vocal style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Perry's singing has garnered acclaim from prominent musical peers and publications. <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a> said: "Perry is a truly luminous singer, in my opinion—a voice in a million."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-may_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-may-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> record executive, former <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a></i> judge, record producer, and former Journey band member<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Randy_Jackson" title="Randy Jackson">Randy Jackson</a> described Perry's as "the golden voice," and opined that, "Other than <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a>, there's no singer in rock that even came close to Steve Perry." "The power, the range, the tone—he created his own style. He mixed a little <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a>, a little <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Everly_Brothers" title="Everly Brothers">Everly Brothers</a>, a little <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greg Prato of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> wrote: "If only one singer could be selected as the most identifiable with '80s <a href="/wiki/Arena_rock" title="Arena rock">arena rock</a>, it would have to be Journey's Steve Perry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Colleague John Franck praised Perry's as a "soaring, whale of a voice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He was voted among the ten greatest rock singers of all time in a 2009 <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(magazine)" title="Classic Rock (magazine)">Classic Rock</a></i> reader poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> ranked Perry No. 34 in "The 100 Greatest Singers of All Time," reflecting the magazine's editorial opinion. They lauded his "technical skills," as well as his "pure tone and passionate sincerity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sam_Cooke" title="Sam Cooke">Sam Cooke</a>, to whom Perry has been compared,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> was Perry's primary influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
