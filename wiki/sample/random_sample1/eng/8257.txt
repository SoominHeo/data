<p><b>Stephen Collins Foster</b> (July 4, 1826 – January 13, 1864), known as "<a href="/wiki/List_of_people_considered_a_founder_in_a_Humanities_field" title="List of people considered a founder in a Humanities field">the father of American music</a>", was an American songwriter primarily known for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parlor_music" title="Parlor music">parlor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minstrel_show" title="Minstrel show">minstrel</a> music. Foster wrote over 200 songs; among his best-known are "<a href="/wiki/Oh!_Susanna" title="Oh! Susanna">Oh! Susanna</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hard_Times_Come_Again_No_More" title="Hard Times Come Again No More">Hard Times Come Again No More</a>" , "<a href="/wiki/Camptown_Races" title="Camptown Races">Camptown Races</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Old_Folks_at_Home" title="Old Folks at Home">Old Folks at Home</a>" ("Swanee River"), "<a href="/wiki/My_Old_Kentucky_Home" title="My Old Kentucky Home">My Old Kentucky Home</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Jeanie_with_the_Light_Brown_Hair" title="Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair">Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Old_Black_Joe" title="Old Black Joe">Old Black Joe</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Beautiful_Dreamer" title="Beautiful Dreamer">Beautiful Dreamer</a>". Many of his compositions remain popular more than 150 years after he wrote them. His compositions are thought to be autobiographical. He has been identified as "the most famous songwriter of the nineteenth century" and may be the most recognizable American composer in other countries. His compositions are sometimes referred to as "childhood songs" because they have been included in the music curriculum of early education. Most of his handwritten music manuscripts are lost, but copies printed by publishers of his day can be found in various collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Root-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>There are many biographers who have published works on the life of Stephen Collins Foster, but details can differ widely. In addition, Foster wrote very little biographical information himself. His brother <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Foster" title="Morrison Foster">Morrison Foster</a> destroyed much of the information about Stephen that he judged to reflect negatively upon the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard1944_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howard1944-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stephen Foster was born on July 4, 1826.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAND_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAND-6">[6]</a></sup> His parents were <a href="/wiki/William_Barclay_Foster" title="William Barclay Foster">William Barclay Foster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Clayland_Tomlinson_Foster" title="Eliza Clayland Tomlinson Foster">Eliza Clayland Tomlinson</a>. He was the youngest of three sisters and six brothers. Foster attended private academies in <a href="/wiki/Allegheny,_Pennsylvania" title="Allegheny, Pennsylvania">Allegheny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athens,_Pennsylvania" title="Athens, Pennsylvania">Athens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Towanda,_Pennsylvania" title="Towanda, Pennsylvania">Towanda, Pennsylvania</a>. He received an education in English grammar, diction, the classics, penmanship, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, and mathematics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>Though they lived in a northern city, his family did not support the abolition of slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAND_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-SAND-6">[6]</a></sup> His older brother Morrison was a notable influence throughout Stephen's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard1944_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Howard1944-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foster was able to teach himself to play the clarinet, violin, guitar, flute and piano. He did not have formal instruction in composition but he was helped by Henry Kleber (1816–97), a German-born music dealer in Pittsburgh. Kleber was a songwriter, <a href="/wiki/Impresario" title="Impresario">impresario</a>, accompanist, and conductor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFAM_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CFAM-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1839, his elder brother William was serving his apprenticeship as an engineer at nearby Towanda and thought Stephen would benefit from being under his supervision. The site of the <a href="/wiki/Camptown_Races" title="Camptown Races">Camptown Races</a> is 30 miles (48 km) from Athens, PA, and 15 miles from Towanda. Stephen attended Athens Academy from 1839 to 1841. He wrote his first composition, "Tioga Waltz" while attending Athens Academy and performed it during the 1841 commencement exercises; he was 14. It was not published during the composer's lifetime, but it is included in the collection of published works by Morrison Foster.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Foster's education included a brief period at Jefferson College in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Pennsylvania" title="Washington, Pennsylvania">Washington, Pennsylvania</a>, (now <a href="/wiki/Washington_%26_Jefferson_College" title="Washington &amp; Jefferson College">Washington &amp; Jefferson College</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doodah_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-doodah-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[nb 1]</a></sup> His tuition was paid, but he had little spending money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doodah_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-doodah-8">[8]</a></sup> He left Canonsburg to visit Pittsburgh with another student and did not return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doodah_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-doodah-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his teenage years, Foster was influenced by two men. Henry Kleber (1816–1897), one of Foster's few formal music instructors, was a classically trained musician who emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt" title="Darmstadt">Darmstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, to Pittsburgh and opened a music store. <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rice" title="Dan Rice">Dan Rice</a> was an entertainer, a clown, and <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">blackface</a> singer, making his living in traveling circuses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1846, Foster moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>, and became a bookkeeper with his brother's steamship company. While he was in Cincinnati, Foster penned his first successful songs in 1848–1849, among them "Oh! Susanna", which became an anthem of the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a>. In 1849, he published <i>Foster's Ethiopian Melodies</i>, which included the successful song "Nelly Was a Lady" as made famous by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christy_Minstrels" title="Christy Minstrels">Christy Minstrels</a>. A plaque marks the site of Foster's residence in Cincinnati, where the <a href="/wiki/Guilford_School_building" title="Guilford School building">Guilford School building</a> is now located.</p>

<p>Then he returned to Pennsylvania and signed a contract with the Christy Minstrels. It was during this period that Foster would write most of his best-known songs: "<a href="/wiki/Camptown_Races" title="Camptown Races">Camptown Races</a>" (1850), "Nelly Bly" (1850), "Ring de Banjo" (1851), "<a href="/wiki/Old_Folks_at_Home" title="Old Folks at Home">Old Folks at Home</a>" (known also as "Swanee River", 1851), "<a href="/wiki/My_Old_Kentucky_Home" title="My Old Kentucky Home">My Old Kentucky Home</a>" (1853), "Old Dog Tray" (1853), and "<a href="/wiki/Jeanie_with_the_Light_Brown_Hair" title="Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair">Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair</a>" (1854), written for his wife Jane Denny McDowell.</p>

<p>Many of Foster's songs were of the blackface <a href="/wiki/Minstrel_show" title="Minstrel show">minstrel show</a> tradition popular at the time. Foster sought, in his own words, to "build up taste ... among refined people by making words suitable to their taste, instead of the trashy and really offensive words which belong to some songs of that order". Many of his songs had Southern themes, yet Foster never lived in the South and visited it only once in 1852, by riverboat voyage on his honeymoon on his brother Dunning's steamboat the <i>Millinger</i>, which took him down the Mississippi River to <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.</p>
<p>Foster's last four years were spent in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Biographical information during this period of his life has not been located or remains lost, though correspondence to, from, and between other family members has been preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Root-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Foster became ill with a fever in January 1864. Weakened, he fell in his hotel in the Bowery, cutting his neck. His writing partner <a href="/wiki/George_Cooper_(poet)" title="George Cooper (poet)">George Cooper</a> found him still alive, naked, lying in a pool of blood. He died in Bellevue Hospital three days later, at age 37.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other versions exist concerning Foster's death described by other biographers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ocon2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ocon2007-13">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>When Foster died, his leather wallet contained a scrap of paper that simply said, "Dear friends and gentle hearts", along with 38 cents in Civil War <a href="/wiki/Scrip" title="Scrip">scrip</a> and three U.S. pennies. The note is said to have inspired <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hilliard" title="Bob Hilliard">Bob Hilliard</a>'s lyric for "<a href="/wiki/Dear_Hearts_and_Gentle_People" title="Dear Hearts and Gentle People">Dear Hearts and Gentle People</a>" (1949). Foster was buried in the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Cemetery" title="Allegheny Cemetery">Allegheny Cemetery</a> in Pittsburgh. After his death, Morrison Foster became his "literary executor". As such, he answered requests for copies of manuscripts, autographs, and biographical information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Root-4">[4]</a></sup> One of the best-loved of his works, <span class="nowrap">"<a href="/wiki/Beautiful_Dreamer" title="Beautiful Dreamer">Beautiful Dreamer</a>"</span> was published shortly after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Growing up in a section of the city where many European immigrants had settled, Foster was accustomed to hearing the music and musical styles of the Italian, Scots-Irish, and German residents in the neighborhood. He composed his first song when he was 14 and entitled it the "Tioga Waltz". The first song he had published was "Open thy Lattice Love" (1844).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFAM_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-CFAM-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barcousky2016_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barcousky2016-15">[14]</a></sup> In addition to his well-known and familiar songs which are still widely performed, Foster wrote songs in support of both drinking (such as "My Wife Is a Most Knowing Woman" or "Mr. and Mrs. Brown" or "When the Bowl Goes Round") and <a href="/wiki/Temperance_songs" title="Temperance songs">temperance</a>, such as "Comrades Fill No Glass for Me" or "The Wife".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAND_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-SAND-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foster also authored many church <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hymns" title="Hymns">hymns</a>. The inclusion of his hymns in hymnals ended by 1910. Some titles of the hymns are: "Seek and ye shall find",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> "All around is bright and fair, While we work for Jesus",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> and "Blame not those who weep and sigh".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foster usually sent his hand-written scores directly to his publishers. The publishers kept the sheet music manuscripts and did not give them to libraries nor return them to his heirs. Some of his original, hand-written scores were bought and put into private collections and the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Root-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_songs">Popular songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Popular songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally speaking, Foster's songs lyrics and melodies have often been changed and altered both by publishers, and performers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/My_Old_Kentucky_Home" title="My Old Kentucky Home">My Old Kentucky Home</a>" is the official state song of <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, adopted by the General Assembly on March 19, 1928. "<a href="/wiki/Old_Folks_at_Home" title="Old Folks at Home">Old Folks at Home</a>" became the official state song of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, designated in 1935. Because of the song lyrics which are perceived in modern times to be derogatory, "Old Folks at Home" was modified with approval from the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster_Memorial" title="Stephen Foster Memorial">Stephen Foster Memorial</a>; after a lengthy debate, the modified song was kept as the official state song, while "<a href="/wiki/Florida_(Where_the_Sawgrass_Meets_the_Sky)" title="Florida (Where the Sawgrass Meets the Sky)">Florida (Where the Sawgrass Meets the Sky)</a>" was added as the state anthem.</p>
<p>American baritone <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Eddy" title="Nelson Eddy">Nelson Eddy</a> recorded 35 Foster songs over three recording sessions in July, August, and September 1947 on Columbia Records, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/78_rpm" title="78 rpm">78 format</a>, 2 songs per record. Columbia issued these recordings in 1948 as <i>Nelson Eddy in Songs of Stephen Foster</i> (Volume 1: A-745 and Volume 2: A-795). In 2005, Jasmine Records compiled all 35 Foster songs in one CD, <i>Nelson Eddy Sings the Stephen Foster Songbook</i>, JASCD 421. "In these performances, arranger/conductor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Armbruster" title="Robert Armbruster">Robert Armbruster</a> made every attempt to frame Nelson Eddy's voice with a simple, yet colorful, orchestral and choral background—the norm of Stephen Foster's time." (Liner notes by Robert Nickora July 2005).</p>
<p>American classical composer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Ives" title="Charles Ives">Charles Ives</a> freely quoted a wide variety of Foster's songs in many of his own works.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Douglas_Jimerson" title="Douglas Jimerson">Douglas Jimerson</a>, a tenor from Baltimore who has released <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Disc" title="Compact Disc">CDs</a> of music from the Civil War era, released <i>Stephen Foster's America</i> in 1998. Just before his death in 2004, singer-songwriter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randy_Vanwarmer" title="Randy Vanwarmer">Randy Vanwarmer</a> completed an entire album of Stephen Foster songs; it was released posthumously as <i>Sings Stephen Foster</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> released a version of "Old Folks at Home" entitled "Swanee River Rock (Talkin' 'Bout That River)" which became his first pop hit in November 1957 (#34, R&amp;B #14).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tribute album, <i><a href="/wiki/Beautiful_Dreamer_(album)" title="Beautiful Dreamer (album)">Beautiful Dreamer: The Songs of Stephen Foster</a></i> won the Grammy for Best Traditional Folk album in 2005. Among the artists who are featured on the album are <a href="/wiki/John_Prine" title="John Prine">John Prine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Sexsmith" title="Ron Sexsmith">Ron Sexsmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alison_Krauss" title="Alison Krauss">Alison Krauss</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yo_Yo_Ma" title="Yo Yo Ma">Yo Yo Ma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_McGuinn" title="Roger McGuinn">Roger McGuinn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mavis_Staples" title="Mavis Staples">Mavis Staples</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suzy_Bogguss" title="Suzy Bogguss">Suzy Bogguss</a>.</p>
<p>Singer/songwriter <a href="/wiki/Syd_Straw" title="Syd Straw">Syd Straw</a> covered "Hard Times Come Again No More" on her 1989 album, <i>Surprise.</i> The same song (as "Hard Times") appears on <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>'s 1992 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Good_as_I_Been_to_You" title="Good as I Been to You">Good as I Been to You</a>.</i> <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Warnes" title="Jennifer Warnes">Jennifer Warnes</a> did another version of this song on her 1979 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Shot_Through_the_Heart" title="Shot Through the Heart">Shot Through the Heart</a>.</i> <a href="/wiki/The_Byrds" title="The Byrds">The Byrds</a> did a rock 'n roll version of "Oh Susanna" on their 1965 album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn_Turn_Turn" title="Turn Turn Turn">Turn Turn Turn</a>.</i>, while James Taylor offered a traditional folk version of "Oh! Susanna" on his 1970 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sweet_Baby_James" title="Sweet Baby James">Sweet Baby James</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2012, performer and educator Jonathan Guyot Smith taught a college course devoted exclusively to the study of Foster's music and released a CD of Foster songs, <i>Stephen Foster Melodies and Serenades for the American Parlor</i>, which contains several seldom-heard Foster songs. The performances are in the style of a 19th-century parlor performance rather than in the manner of a formal concert.</p>
<p>Four rare Civil War-era hymns by Foster were released in 2012, performed by The Old Stoughton Musical Society Chorus: "The Pure, The Bright, The Beautiful"; "Over The River"; "Give Us This Day" and "What Shall The Harvest Be?"; on a CD-ROM titled <i>"Glory, Hallelujah: Songs and Hymns of the Civil War Era"</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics_and_controversies">Critics and controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critics and controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historians speculate that Foster may have been "a drunkard". Thirty years after his death, one reporter described him as paying "the penalty of an irregular life", being "weak-willed" and writing songs about people of "a pathetic character".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Superficial interpretation of Foster's compositions in modern times could be considered disparaging to African Americans in current cultural contexts, however, Foster unveiled the realities of slavery in his work, while also imparting dignity to African-Americans in his compositions, especially as he grew as an artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Foster was the first to refer to an African American black woman as "a lady" in his composition "Nelly Was a Lady."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foster composed many songs that were used in minstrel shows. This form of public entertainment <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">lampooned</a> African Americans as buffoonish, superstitious, without-a-care, musical, lazy, and dim-witted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coon_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-coon-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenrick_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenrick-25">[24]</a></sup> In the early 1830s, these minstrel shows gained popularity. The shows evolved, and by 1848 blackface minstrel shows were a separate musical art form accessible to the general public (contrasted with opera, which was more upper-class at the time.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenfield_Village_and_the_Henry_Ford_Museum">Greenfield Village and the Henry Ford Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Greenfield Village and the Henry Ford Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1935, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> ceremonially presented a new addition to his historical collection of early American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorabilia" title="Memorabilia">memorabilia</a> - the "Home of Stephen Foster". The structure was identified by notable <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a> of the time as being authentic and was then deconstructed and moved "piece by piece" from Lawrenceville, Pennsylvania (now Pittsburgh) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenfield_Village" title="Greenfield Village">Greenfield Village</a>, Michigan. Foster's niece insisted that it was not his birthplace, and in 1953 the claim was withdrawn. Greenfield Village still displays a structure that is identified as the birthplace of Stephen Foster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> The Foster family stated that the original Foster birthplace structure was torn down in 1865.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribmem_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-tribmem-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_influence">Musical influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Musical influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Early fimmakers selected Foster's songs for their work because his copyrights had expired and cost them nothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>Professor of Folklore and musician John Minton wrote a song titled "Stephen C. Foster's Blues".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Erika M. Anderson, of the band EMA, refers to Foster's "Camptown Races" in the song "California", from <i>past Life Martyred Saints</i> (2011): "I bet my money on the bobtail nag/somebody bet on the bay."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Firesign Theatre makes many references to Foster's compositions in their CD, <i>Boom Dot Bust</i> (1999, Rhino Records)</li>
<li>Larry Kirwan of <a href="/wiki/Black_47" title="Black 47">Black 47</a> mixes the music of Foster with his own in the musical <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_Times_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hard Times (musical) (page does not exist)">Hard Times</a></i>, which earned a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> accolade in its original run: "a knockout entertainment". Kirwan gives a contemporary interpretation of Foster's troubled later years and sets it in the tumultuous time of the New York draft riots and the Irish–Negro relations of the period. A revival ran at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cell_Theater&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cell Theater (page does not exist)">Cell Theater</a> in New York in early 2014.</li>
<li>Spike Jones recorded a comedy send-up "I Dream of Brownie in the Light Blue Jeans."</li>
<li>The swing revival band <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_Nut_Zippers" title="Squirrel Nut Zippers">Squirrel Nut Zippers</a> released a song entitled "The ghost of Stephen Foster."</li>
<li>Humorist Stan Freberg imagined a 1950s style version of Foster's music in "Rock Around Stephen Foster" and, with Harry Shearer, had a sketch about Foster having writer's block in a bit from his "United States of America" project.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Two television shows about the life of Foster and his childhood friend (and later wife) Jeanie MacDowell were produced in Japan, the first in 1979 with 13 episodes, and the second from 1992 to 1993 with 52 episodes; both were titled <i><a href="/wiki/Jeanie_with_the_Light_Brown_Hair_(anime)" title="Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair (anime)">Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair</a></i> after the song of the same name.</li>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Honeymooners" title="The Honeymooners">Honeymooners</a></i> episode, "The $99,000 Answer," Ed Norton warms up on the piano by playing the opening to "Swanee River." Later, when Ralph returns to the game show, the first question asked is "Who is the composer of 'Swanee River'?" Ralph nervously responds, "Ed Norton," and loses the game.</li>
<li>In a "Fractured Fairy Tales" segment of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rocky_and_Bullwinkle_Show" title="The Rocky and Bullwinkle Show">The Rocky and Bullwinkle Show</a></i>, Aladdin finds a lamp with a female <a href="/wiki/Jeanie_with_the_Light_Brown_Hair" title="Jeanie with the Light Brown Hair">genie with light brown hair</a>, who immediately asks, "Are you Stephen Foster?"</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Three Hollywood films have been made of Foster's life: <i><a href="/wiki/Harmony_Lane" title="Harmony Lane">Harmony Lane</a></i> (1935) with <a href="/wiki/Douglass_Montgomery" title="Douglass Montgomery">Douglass Montgomery</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Swanee_River_(film)" title="Swanee River (film)">Swanee River</a></i> (1939) with <a href="/wiki/Don_Ameche" title="Don Ameche">Don Ameche</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/I_Dream_of_Jeanie_(film)" title="I Dream of Jeanie (film)">I Dream of Jeanie</a></i> (1952), with <a href="/wiki/Bill_Shirley" title="Bill Shirley">Bill Shirley</a>. The 1939 production was one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twentieth_Century_Fox" title="Twentieth Century Fox">Twentieth Century Fox</a>'s more ambitious efforts, filmed in <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">Technicolor</a>; the other two were low-budget affairs made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-movie" title="B-movie">B-movie</a> studios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the film <i><a href="/wiki/Tombstone_(film)" title="Tombstone (film)">Tombstone</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Clanton" title="Billy Clanton">Billy Clanton</a> (played by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Haden_Church" title="Thomas Haden Church">Thomas Haden Church</a>) tries to bait <a href="/wiki/Doc_Holliday" title="Doc Holliday">Doc Holliday</a> (<a href="/wiki/Val_Kilmer" title="Val Kilmer">Val Kilmer</a>), who is playing a <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">Chopin</a> <a href="/wiki/Nocturne" title="Nocturne">nocturne</a> on the piano, by saying, "Is that 'Old Dog Tray'? That sounds like 'Old Dog Tray' to me." When the goad fails, Clanton asks whether Doc knows any other songs, like "'Camptown Races?', 'Oh Susanna', "You know, Stephen stinkin' Foster?!?"</li>
<li>In the film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Million_Ways_to_Die_in_the_West" title="A Million Ways to Die in the West">A Million Ways to Die in the West</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Seth_MacFarlane" title="Seth MacFarlane">Seth MacFarlane</a>'s character Albert can't get a song out of his head. <a href="/wiki/Charlize_Theron" title="Charlize Theron">Charlize Theron</a>'s character suggests singing a different song, to which he replies, "There are only like 3 songs," and she adds, "And they're all by Stephen Foster." The song in question, "If You've Only Got a Mustache," was actually composed by Foster.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_events">Other events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>"Stephen Foster Super Saturday" is a day of <a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred" title="Thoroughbred">thoroughbred</a> racing during the Spring/Summer meet at <a href="/wiki/Churchill_Downs" title="Churchill Downs">Churchill Downs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>. During the call to the post, selections of Stephen Foster songs are played by the track bugler, Steve Buttleman. The day is headlined by the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster_Handicap" title="Stephen Foster Handicap">Stephen Foster Handicap</a> a Grade I dirt race for older horses at 9 furlongs.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/36_U.S.C." title="36 U.S.C.">36 U.S.C.</a> §140 designates January 13 as <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster_Memorial_Day" title="Stephen Foster Memorial Day">Stephen Foster Memorial Day</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holidays_of_the_United_States#Federal_observances" title="Holidays of the United States">United States National Observance</a>. In 1936, Congress authorized the minting of a silver half dollar in honor of the Cincinnati Musical Center. Foster was featured on the obverse of the coin.</li>
<li>"Stephen Foster Music Camp" is a summer music camp held on EKU's campus of Richmond, Kentucky. The camp offers piano courses, choir, band, and orchestra ensembles.</li>
</ul>

The painting inspired <a href="/wiki/Florence_Foster_Jenkins" title="Florence Foster Jenkins">Florence Foster Jenkins</a> to author a tableau in which she personally plays the role of the angel depicted in Christy's painting. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Foster is honored on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pittsburgh" title="University of Pittsburgh">University of Pittsburgh</a> campus with the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster_Memorial" title="Stephen Foster Memorial">Stephen Foster Memorial</a>, a landmark building that houses the Stephen Foster Memorial Museum, the Center for American Music, as well as two theaters: the Charity Randall Theatre and Henry Heymann Theatre, both performance spaces for Pitt's Department of Theater Arts. It is the largest repository for original Stephen Foster compositions, recordings, and other memorabilia his songs have inspired worldwide.</p>
<p>Two state parks are named in Foster's honor: the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster_Folk_Culture_Center_State_Park" title="Stephen Foster Folk Culture Center State Park">Stephen Foster Folk Culture Center State Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/White_Springs,_Florida" title="White Springs, Florida">White Springs, Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_C._Foster_State_Park" title="Stephen C. Foster State Park">Stephen C. Foster State Park</a> in Georgia. Both parks are on the <a href="/wiki/Suwannee_River" title="Suwannee River">Suwannee River</a>. Stephen Foster Lake at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Pisgah_State_Park" title="Mount Pisgah State Park">Mount Pisgah State Park</a> in Pennsylvania is also named in his honor.</p>
<p>One state park is named in honor of Foster's songs, <a href="/wiki/My_Old_Kentucky_Home_State_Park" title="My Old Kentucky Home State Park">My Old Kentucky Home</a>, an historic mansion formerly named Federal Hill, located in <a href="/wiki/Bardstown,_Kentucky" title="Bardstown, Kentucky">Bardstown, Kentucky</a> where Stephen is said to have been an occasional visitor according to his brother, Morrison Foster. The park dedicated a bronze statue in honor of Stephen's work.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lawrenceville_(Pittsburgh)" title="Lawrenceville (Pittsburgh)">Lawrenceville (Pittsburgh)</a> Historical Society, together with the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Cemetery" title="Allegheny Cemetery">Allegheny Cemetery</a> Historical Association, hosts the annual Stephen Foster Music and Heritage Festival (Doo Dah Days!). Held the first weekend of July, Doo Dah Days! celebrates the life and music of one of the most influential songwriters in America's history. His home in the Lawrenceville Section of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>, still remains on Penn Avenue nearby the Stephen Foster Community Center.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Stephen_Foster_Collection_.28archives.29">The Stephen Foster Collection (archives)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The Stephen Foster Collection (archives)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Stephen_Foster_Collection_and_archive" title="The Stephen Foster Collection and archive">The Stephen Foster Collection and archive</a></div>
<p>Most primary sources related to his life, family and music have been retained by the University of Pittsburgh Library System as the Foster Hall Collection housed in the Stephen Foster Memorial. These materials where obtained from philanthropists, donated by collectors or his heirs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Root-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of Stephen Foster's best-known songs, "Camptown Races", is actually titled "Gwine to Run All Night", though "Camptown Races" is the name that gained popularity.</p>
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">His grandfather, James Foster, was an associate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_McMillan_(pastor)" title="John McMillan (pastor)">John McMillan</a> and a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Canonsburg_Academy_contract" title="s:Canonsburg Academy contract">founding trustee</a> of Canonsburg Academy, a predecessor institution to Jefferson College; his father, William Barclay Foster, attended Canonsburg Academy until the age of 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milligan_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-milligan-9">[9]</a></sup></span></li>
</ol>
