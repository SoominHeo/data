<p><b>Stephen Arthur Frears</b> (born 20 June 1941) is an English film director. Frears has directed British films since the 1980s including <i><a href="/wiki/My_Beautiful_Laundrette" title="My Beautiful Laundrette">My Beautiful Laundrette</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Liaisons" title="Dangerous Liaisons">Dangerous Liaisons</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/High_Fidelity_(film)" title="High Fidelity (film)">High Fidelity</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Queen_(film)" title="The Queen (film)">The Queen</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Philomena_(film)" title="Philomena (film)">Philomena</a></i>. He has been nominated for two <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Awards for Best Director</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grifters_(film)" title="The Grifters (film)">The Grifters</a></i> and <i>The Queen</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Frears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frears was born in <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>, England. His father, Russell E. Frears, was a general practitioner and accountant, and his mother, Ruth M. (<i>née</i> Danziger), was a social worker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Frears was brought up <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a>, and did not find out that his mother was <a href="/wiki/British_Jews" title="British Jews">Jewish</a> until he was in his late 20s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Frears was educated at <a href="/wiki/Gresham%27s_School" title="Gresham's School">Gresham's School</a> from 1954 to 1959, and later went on to study law at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>, from 1960 to 1963.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Frears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from Cambridge, Frears worked as an assistant director on the films <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgan!" title="Morgan!">Morgan!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/If...." title="If....">if....</a></i>. He spent most of his early directing career in television mainly for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, but also for the commercial sector. Frears contributed to several high-profile anthology series such as the BBC's <i><a href="/wiki/Play_for_Today" title="Play for Today">Play for Today</a></i>, and produced a series of <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bennett" title="Alan Bennett">Alan Bennett</a>'s plays for <a href="/wiki/London_Weekend_Television" title="London Weekend Television">LWT</a>, taking responsibility for working in the <a href="/wiki/Television_studio#Production_control_room" title="Television studio">gallery</a> on <i>The Old Crowd</i> while <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Anderson" title="Lindsay Anderson">Lindsay Anderson</a> worked with the actors.</p>

<p>In the late 1980s, Frears came to international attention as a director of feature films. His directorial film debut was the detective spoof <i><a href="/wiki/Gumshoe_(film)" title="Gumshoe (film)">Gumshoe</a></i> (1971), but it was his direction of <i><a href="/wiki/My_Beautiful_Laundrette" title="My Beautiful Laundrette">My Beautiful Laundrette</a></i> that unexpectedly led to wider notice. The interracial romance, based on a <a href="/wiki/Hanif_Kureishi" title="Hanif Kureishi">Hanif Kureishi</a> screenplay and shot on <a href="/wiki/16_mm_film" title="16 mm film">16 mm film</a>, was released theatrically in 1985 to great acclaim, and received an Academy Award nomination and two nominations for BAFTA Awards: it is known as the film that helped launch both Frears and actor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_Day_Lewis" title="Daniel Day Lewis">Daniel Day Lewis</a>. In 1987, Frears worked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrian_Edmondson" title="Adrian Edmondson">Adrian Edmondson</a> on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mr_Jolly_Lives_Next_Door" title="Mr Jolly Lives Next Door">Mr Jolly Lives Next Door</a></i>, for a 45-minute programme by cult ensemble <i><a href="/wiki/The_Comic_Strip" title="The Comic Strip">The Comic Strip Presents</a></i>. In 1985, Frears also directed a Comic Strip parody of <i><a href="/wiki/Rebecca_(novel)" title="Rebecca (novel)">Rebecca</a></i>.</p>
<p>Frears next directed <a href="/wiki/Joe_Orton" title="Joe Orton">Joe Orton</a> biopic <i><a href="/wiki/Prick_Up_Your_Ears" title="Prick Up Your Ears">Prick Up Your Ears</a></i>, another collaboration with playwright Alan Bennett, which was followed by his second film from a Kureishi screenplay, <i><a href="/wiki/Sammy_and_Rosie_Get_Laid" title="Sammy and Rosie Get Laid">Sammy and Rosie Get Laid</a></i> (1987). The following year, Frears made <i><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Liaisons" title="Dangerous Liaisons">Dangerous Liaisons</a></i> in France, with a cast that included <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Close" title="Glenn Close">Glenn Close</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Malkovich" title="John Malkovich">John Malkovich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Pfeiffer" title="Michelle Pfeiffer">Michelle Pfeiffer</a>. Based on the novel of romantic game playing, the film received numerous Academy Awards and BAFTA nominations, and Frears himself was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Direction" title="BAFTA Award for Best Direction">BAFTA Award for Best Direction</a>. Frears had further critical success with his next film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grifters_(film)" title="The Grifters (film)">The Grifters</a></i>, another tale of con-artists. The film earned Frears his first <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Award nomination for best direction</a>. In 2006, Frears directed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Queen_(film)" title="The Queen (film)">The Queen</a></i>, that depicts the <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Death of Diana, Princess of Wales">death of Princess Diana</a> on 31 August 1997. <i>The Queen</i> also achieved immense critical acclaim, box-office success, and awards. Frears himself received his second Academy Award nomination for best direction, and <a href="/wiki/Helen_Mirren" title="Helen Mirren">Helen Mirren</a> won numerous awards for playing the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">title role</a>.</p>
<p>Frears' other films include Western <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hi-Lo_Country" title="The Hi-Lo Country">The Hi-Lo Country</a></i> (1998), which won him the best director award at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Film_Festival" title="Berlin Film Festival">Berlin Film Festival</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/High_Fidelity_(film)" title="High Fidelity (film)">High Fidelity</a></i> (2000), which features a number of scenes where star <a href="/wiki/John_Cusack" title="John Cusack">John Cusack</a> addresses the audience directly, <i><a href="/wiki/Dirty_Pretty_Things_(film)" title="Dirty Pretty Things (film)">Dirty Pretty Things</a></i> (2002), and British theatre comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs_Henderson_Presents" title="Mrs Henderson Presents">Mrs Henderson Presents</a></i> (2005). Frears returned to directing for television with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deal_(2003_film)" title="The Deal (2003 film)">The Deal</a></i> (2003), which depicts an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blair-Brown_deal" title="Blair-Brown deal">alleged deal between Tony Blair and Gordon Brown</a> over which of them should become leader of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> in 1994.</p>
<p>Frears has also directed two films based on novels by <a href="/wiki/Roddy_Doyle" title="Roddy Doyle">Roddy Doyle</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Snapper_(film)" title="The Snapper (film)">The Snapper</a></i> (1993) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Van_(1996_film)" title="The Van (1996 film)">The Van</a></i> (1996).</p>
<p>He holds the "David Lean Chair in Fiction Direction" from the <a href="/wiki/National_Film_and_Television_School" title="National Film and Television School">National Film and Television School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beaconsfield" title="Beaconsfield">Beaconsfield</a>, where he teaches.</p>
<p>His 1992 film <i><a href="/wiki/Hero_(1992_film)" title="Hero (1992 film)">Hero</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Hoffman" title="Dustin Hoffman">Dustin Hoffman</a>, was a major <a href="/wiki/Box_office_bomb" title="Box office bomb">box office disappointment</a>. Frears was nominated for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1996_Golden_Raspberry_Awards" title="1996 Golden Raspberry Awards">Razzie Award</a> for his direction of <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Reilly_(film)" title="Mary Reilly (film)">Mary Reilly</a></i>.</p>
<p>His 2013 adoption drama <i><a href="/wiki/Philomena_(film)" title="Philomena (film)">Philomena</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Pope" title="Jeff Pope">Jeff Pope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Coogan" title="Steve Coogan">Steve Coogan</a>, won the best screenplay award at the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">Venice Film Festival</a> and the BAFTAS, and was nominated that year for Best Picture at the BAFTAs and the Academy Awards. It stars Coogan and <a href="/wiki/Judi_Dench" title="Judi Dench">Judi Dench</a>. The same year, HBO released his television drama <i><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali%27s_Greatest_Fight" title="Muhammad Ali's Greatest Fight">Muhammad Ali's Greatest Fight</a>,</i> which depicts the US supreme court deliberation over banning <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali" title="Muhammad Ali">Muhammad Ali</a> from boxing for refusing to serve in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam war</a>.</p>
<p>His biopic of disgraced cycling champion <a href="/wiki/Lance_Armstrong" title="Lance Armstrong">Lance Armstrong</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Program_(2015_film)" title="The Program (2015 film)">The Program</a> was premiered in the 2015 <a href="/wiki/BFI_London_Film_Festival" title="BFI London Film Festival">BFI London Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>Many of Frears' films are based on stories of real life characters, but Frears has never met any of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Frears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frears currently lives in London with his wife, the painter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Rothenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anne Rothenstein (page does not exist)">Anne Rothenstein</a>, and their two children. He also has two children from his previous marriage to <a href="/wiki/Mary-Kay_Wilmers" title="Mary-Kay Wilmers">Mary-Kay Wilmers</a>. Early in his career, Frears made a programme featuring the band <a href="/wiki/The_Scaffold" title="The Scaffold">the Scaffold</a> and is name-checked ("Mr Frears had sticky out ears...") in their hit song "<a href="/wiki/Lily_the_Pink_(song)" title="Lily the Pink (song)">Lily the Pink</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was one of several celebrities who endorsed the parliamentary candidacy of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Frears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1982: Won <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_Television_Awards" title="British Academy Television Awards">BAFTA TV Award</a> for best Single Play <i>BBC2 Playhouse: Going Gently</i> (#7.27)</li>
<li>1989: Won <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Film" title="César Award for Best Foreign Film">César Award for Best Foreign Film</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Liaisons" title="Dangerous Liaisons">Dangerous Liaisons</a></i></li>
<li>1990: Nominated for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Award for Best Director</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grifters_(film)" title="The Grifters (film)">The Grifters</a></i></li>
<li>1995: Won <a href="/wiki/Goya_Award_for_Best_European_Film" title="Goya Award for Best European Film">Goya Award for Best European Film</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Snapper_(film)" title="The Snapper (film)">The Snapper</a></i></li>
<li>1996: Nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Razzie_Award_for_Worst_Director" title="Razzie Award for Worst Director">Razzie Award for Worst Director</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Reilly_(film)" title="Mary Reilly (film)">Mary Reilly</a></i></li>
<li>1996: Nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bear" title="Golden Bear">Golden Bear</a> in <a href="/wiki/46th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="46th Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin</a> for <i>Mary Reilly</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale1996_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale1996-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>1999: Won <a href="/wiki/Silver_Bear_for_Best_Director" title="Silver Bear for Best Director">Silver Bear for Best Director</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hi-Lo_Country" title="The Hi-Lo Country">The Hi-Lo Country</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/49th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="49th Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale1999_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale1999-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>2003: Won <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_Television_Awards_2004" title="British Academy Television Awards 2004">BAFTA TV Awards</a> for Best Single Drama for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deal_(2003_film)" title="The Deal (2003 film)">The Deal</a></i></li>
<li>2006: Nominated for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Award for Best Director</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Queen_(film)" title="The Queen (film)">The Queen</a></i></li>
<li>2007: Won <a href="/wiki/Goya_Award_for_Best_European_Film" title="Goya Award for Best European Film">Goya Award for Best European Film</a> for <i>The Queen</i></li>
<li>2009: Commandeur de l'<a href="/wiki/Ordre_des_Arts_et_des_Lettres" title="Ordre des Arts et des Lettres">Ordre des Arts et des Lettres</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>2014: Nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Directing_for_a_Miniseries,_Movie_or_a_Dramatic_Special" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Directing for a Miniseries, Movie or a Dramatic Special">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Directing for a Miniseries, Movie or a Dramatic Special</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali%27s_Greatest_Fight" title="Muhammad Ali's Greatest Fight">Muhammad Ali's Greatest Fight</a></i></li>
<li>2014: Golden Duke for Lifetime Achievement of the 5th <a href="/wiki/Odessa_International_Film_Festival" title="Odessa International Film Festival">Odessa International Film Festival</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
