<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Palazzo Spada</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Palazzo Spada</b> is a palace in the historic centre of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, Italy. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/Rioni_of_Rome" title="Rioni of Rome">rione</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regola_(rione_of_Rome)" title="Regola (rione of Rome)">Regola</a>, at Piazza Capo di Ferro, 13, very close to the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Farnese" title="Palazzo Farnese">Palazzo Farnese</a>. It has a garden facing towards the River <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>.</p>
<p>The palace accommodates a large art collection, the <a href="/wiki/Galleria_Spada" title="Galleria Spada">Galleria Spada</a>. The collection was originally assembled by Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_Spada" title="Bernardino Spada">Bernardino Spada</a> in the 17th century, by his brother <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Virgilio_Spada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Virgilio Spada (page does not exist)">Virgilio Spada</a> and added to by his grandnephew Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Fabrizio_Spada" title="Fabrizio Spada">Fabrizio Spada</a>,</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palazzo_Spada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was originally built in 1540 for Cardinal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girolamo_Capodiferro" title="Girolamo Capodiferro">Girolamo Capodiferro</a>. Bartolomeo Baronino, of <a href="/wiki/Casale_Monferrato" title="Casale Monferrato">Casale Monferrato</a>, was the architect, while <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Mazzoni" title="Giulio Mazzoni">Giulio Mazzoni</a> and a team provided lavish stuccowork inside and out.</p>
<p>The palazzo was purchased by Cardinal Spada in 1632. He commissioned the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Borromini" title="Francesco Borromini">Francesco Borromini</a> to modify it for him, and it was Borromini who created the masterpiece of <a href="/wiki/Forced_perspective" title="Forced perspective">forced perspective</a> optical illusion in the arcaded courtyard, in which diminishing rows of columns and a rising floor create the visual illusion of a gallery 37 meters long (it is 8 meters) with a lifesize sculpture at the end of the vista, in daylight beyond: the sculpture is 60 cm high. Borromini was aided in his perspective trick by a mathematician.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerist</a> <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> sculptural decor of the palazzo's front and its courtyard façades feature sculptures crowded into niches and fruit and flower swags, grotesches and vignettes of symbolic devices (<i>impresi</i>) in bas-relief among the small framed windows of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mezzanine_(architecture)" title="Mezzanine (architecture)">mezzanine</a>, the richest <i>cinquecento</i> façades in Rome.</p>

<p>The colossal sculpture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pompey_the_Great" title="Pompey the Great">Pompey the Great</a>, erroneously believed to be the very one at whose feet <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> fell, was discovered under the party wall of two Roman houses in 1552: it was to be decapitated to satisfy the claims of both parties, which appalled Cardinal Capodiferro so, that he interceded on the sculpture's behalf with <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_III" title="Pope Julius III">Pope Julius III</a>, who purchased it and then gave it to the Cardinal.</p>
<p>The palazzo hosts the <a href="/wiki/Galleria_Spada" title="Galleria Spada">Galleria Spada</a>, the Cardinal Spada's collection, which includes four galleries of 16th and 17th-century paintings by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_del_Sarto" title="Andrea del Sarto">Andrea del Sarto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guido_Reni" title="Guido Reni">Guido Reni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder" title="Jan Brueghel the Elder">Jan Brueghel the Elder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Rubens</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%BCrer" title="Dürer">Dürer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Merisi" title="Michelangelo Merisi">Caravaggio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domenichino" title="Domenichino">Domenichino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Annibale_Carracci" title="Annibale Carracci">the Carracci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvator_Rosa" title="Salvator Rosa">Salvator Rosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parmigianino" title="Parmigianino">Parmigianino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Solimena" title="Francesco Solimena">Francesco Solimena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Cerquozzi" title="Michelangelo Cerquozzi">Michelangelo Cerquozzi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Testa" title="Pietro Testa">Pietro Testa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giambattista_Gaulli" title="Giambattista Gaulli">Giambattista Gaulli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orazio_Gentileschi" title="Orazio Gentileschi">Orazio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_Gentileschi" title="Artemisia Gentileschi">Artemisia Gentileschi</a>, has the additional interest of being hung in the 17th-century manner, frame-to-frame, with smaller pictures "skied" above larger ones.</p>
<p>Palazzo Spada was purchased by the Italian State in 1927 and today houses the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Council_of_State" title="Italian Council of State">Italian Council of State</a>, which meets in its richly frescoed and stuccoed rooms.</p>
