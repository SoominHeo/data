<p><i><b>Spy Kids 2: The Island of Lost Dreams</b></i> (credited as <i><b>Spy Kids 2: Island of Lost Dreams</b></i> onscreen) is a 2002 American <a href="/wiki/Spy_film" title="Spy film">spy</a> <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> written, shot, edited, and directed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rodriguez" title="Robert Rodriguez">Robert Rodriguez</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Troublemaker_Studios" title="Troublemaker Studios">Troublemaker Studios</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Avellan" title="Elizabeth Avellan">Elizabeth Avellan</a>, and Rodriguez, and starring <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Banderas" title="Antonio Banderas">Antonio Banderas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carla_Gugino" title="Carla Gugino">Carla Gugino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Vega" title="Alexa Vega">Alexa Vega</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Sabara" title="Daryl Sabara">Daryl Sabara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Judge" title="Mike Judge">Mike Judge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Montalb%C3%A1n" title="Ricardo Montalbán">Ricardo Montalbán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holland_Taylor" title="Holland Taylor">Holland Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_McDonald" title="Christopher McDonald">Christopher McDonald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Trejo" title="Danny Trejo">Danny Trejo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheech_Marin" title="Cheech Marin">Cheech Marin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Buscemi" title="Steve Buscemi">Steve Buscemi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Momsen" title="Taylor Momsen">Taylor Momsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_O%27Leary" title="Matt O'Leary">Matt O'Leary</a>, and introducing <a href="/wiki/Emily_Osment" title="Emily Osment">Emily Osment</a>.</p>
<p>The second installment in the <a href="/wiki/Spy_Kids_(franchise)" title="Spy Kids (franchise)"><i>Spy Kids</i> film series</a>, which began with 2001's <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_Kids" title="Spy Kids">Spy Kids</a></i>, the film was theatrically released on August 7, 2002 by <a href="/wiki/Dimension_Films" title="Dimension Films">Dimension Films</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFI_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFI-1">[1]</a></sup> Upon release, <i>Spy Kids 2</i> received mostly positive reviews from critics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-4">[4]</a></sup> and grossed over $119 million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_Kids_2:_The_Island_of_Lost_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The OSS now has a full child spy section, of which 13-year-old <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Cortez" title="Carmen Cortez">Carmen Cortez</a> and 10-year-old <a href="/wiki/Juni_Cortez" title="Juni Cortez">Juni Cortez</a> have become agents. They face particularly difficult competition with Gary and Gerti Giggles, the children of double-dealing agent Donnagon Giggles, whom Carmen and Juni helped to rescue in the previous film. It is shown that Carmen defends Gary, and has a crush on him, which strains her relationship with Juni.</p>
<p>After an incident at a local <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement park</a> owned by Dinky Winks where the President's daughter Alexandra deliberately sabotages a thrill ride which juggles its passenger, forcing the Giggles and the Cortez kids to compete in the rescue. Donnagon, who has somehow hacked into the <a href="/wiki/Teleprompter" title="Teleprompter">teleprompter</a> which the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> was reading from, is named the director of the OSS. Juni is fired after being framed by Gary (who was actually to blame) for losing the "Transmooker," a highly coveted device which can shut off all electronic devices. In his new position as director, Donnagon can carry on with his plan to steal the Transmooker, so he can rule the world.</p>
<p>Carmen manages to hack into the database, and reinstates Juni's level as an agent. She and Juni use some hints from Alexander Minion, and follow the trail to a mysterious island near <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, which is home to Romero, a lunatic scientist. Romero has been attempting to create <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically-miniaturized</a> animals, so he can make a profit by selling the animals to kids in "miniature zoos." He had an experiment go wrong after accidentally pouring growth concoction onto the mutated set of animals, as a result, he is unwilling to leave his lab, out of fear of being eaten. When Carmen is captured by a Spork, a breed of flying pigs, she meets Gerti, who reveals to her that Gary is actually evil. Carmen changes her feelings for Gary after he tries to kill Juni, and she sides with her brother. Meanwhile, Romero finds out that his creatures are much friendlier than he thought. After fighting pirate skeletons (that came alive after Juni stole a necklace from them), Carmen and Juni, along with the help of their family (who came to the island when they heard their kids were missing), Gerti, Romero and his creatures, destroy the Transmooker, and defeat Donnagon and Gary. Afterwards, Gregorio and Donnagon fight each other, as Gregorio put it, "the old-fashioned way."</p>
<p>Donnagon is fired by the President and his daughter, Gary is temporarily disavowed, and Gregorio is appointed director of the OSS by Alexandra on her father's behalf, while Juni resigns due to the impersonal treatment he had received by agents of the OSS, after being framed. As the Cortez family leaves the island, Romero gives Juni a miniature spider-monkey as a gift, and all the island's inhabitants bid farewell to the Cortez family.</p>
<p>During the credits, Machete has Carmen sing as an undercover pop star in a concert. Carmen says she can't sing so Machete gives her a mic which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto-tune" title="Auto-tune">auto-tunes</a> her voice, and a belt that helps her dance. He also gives Juni a guitar that plays itself. After the performance, Carmen asks Machete, "What, are you surprised your gadgets work?" Machete then tells Carmen, "I just realized, I never put any batteries in this." Carmen is shocked to find out she can, in fact, sing without auto-tune. As the credits come to a close, Dinky Winks paddles to Romero's island to strike up a business deal.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">Special effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_Kids_2:_The_Island_of_Lost_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Special effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the fact that this film uses over twice the amount of special effects shots than the first film, Rodriguez did not ask the producers for a larger budget; he said<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-5">[5]</a></sup> "...I told the studio I don't want more money. I just want to be more creative".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-5">[5]</a></sup> Rodriguez picked some visual effects companies who were eager and less established, as well as starting up his own <a href="/wiki/Troublemaker_Studios" title="Troublemaker Studios">Troublemaker Studios</a>, and reemploying Hybride, who had worked with him on the first film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Punchatz" title="Gregor Punchatz">Gregor Punchatz</a>, the film's lead animator, employed a certain technique to make the movements of the computer generated creatures resemble the stop-motion work of filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Ray_Harryhausen" title="Ray Harryhausen">Ray Harryhausen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-5">[5]</a></sup> who has a cameo in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The scene with the army of live skeletons was shot on a real rock formation, with the two young actors on safety wires,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComputerCafe_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ComputerCafe-8">[8]</a></sup> and the computer generated skeletons added later to over three dozen shots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ComputerCafe_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ComputerCafe-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_Kids_2:_The_Island_of_Lost_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_Kids_2:_The_Island_of_Lost_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Spy Kids 2: The Island of Lost Dreams</i> opened theatrically on August 7, 2002 in 3,307 venues and earned $16,711,716 in its first weekend, ranking third in the North American box office behind <i><a href="/wiki/XXX_(2002_film)" title="XXX (2002 film)">XXX</a></i> and the second weekend of <i><a href="/wiki/Signs_(film)" title="Signs (film)">Signs</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The film ended its run on January 12, 2003, having grossed $85,846,429 in the United States and Canada, and $33,876,929 overseas for a worldwide total of $119,723,358.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_Kids_2:_The_Island_of_Lost_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film received mostly positive reviews from critics. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, it currently has a 74% "Certified Fresh" approval score based on 133 reviews, with an average rating of 6.6/10. The site's critical consensus reads: "Though the concept is no longer fresh, <i>Spy Kids 2</i> is still an agreeable and energetic romp."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RT-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> reports a 66 out of 100 rating based on 29 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> gave the film 3 out of 4 stars and commented, "With "<i>Spy Kids 2: The Island of Lost Dreams</i>," the <i>Spy Kids</i> franchise establishes itself as a durable part of the movie landscape: a <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> series for kids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Turan" title="Kenneth Turan">Kenneth Turan</a> of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> gave it 4 out of 5 stars said, "The movie is a gaudy, noisy thrill ride -- hyperactive, slightly out of control and full of kinetic, mischievous charm."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Lisa Schwarzbaum of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> wrote, "The antics are a tad more frantic, and the gizmos work overtime, as if ... Robert Rodriguez felt the hot breath of el diablo on his neck. On the other hand, the inventiveness is still superior and the network of fiends [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] and family is extended."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Michael Wilmington of Metromix Chicago, noting how Rodriguez borrows many elements from television and earlier films, stated that, "Rodriguez recycles and refurbishes all these old movie bits with the opportunistic energy of a man looting his old attic toy chest -- but he also puts some personal feeling into the movie. This is a film about families staying together, children asserting themselves and even, to some degree, Latino power".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy_Kids_2:_The_Island_of_Lost_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> in the United States on February 18, 2003. The film is also available to download on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> re-release was scheduled for August 2, 2011 to coincide with the fourth film.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">film score</a> was co-written by director <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rodriguez" title="Robert Rodriguez">Robert Rodriguez</a> and composer <a href="/wiki/John_Debney" title="John Debney">John Debney</a>, who had also co-written the score for <i>Spy Kids</i>. The sound is a mix of <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_rock" title="Independent rock">indie rock</a>, and includes songs performed by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cumming" title="Alan Cumming">Alan Cumming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Vega" title="Alexa Vega">Alexa Vega</a>. Unusually, the orchestral score was recorded in the auditorium of a local high school in Austin, Georgetown High School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
