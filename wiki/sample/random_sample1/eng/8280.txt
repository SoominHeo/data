<p><b>Spyware</b> is software that aims to gather information about a person or organization without their knowledge, that may send such information to another entity without the consumer's consent, or that asserts control over a device without the consumer's knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTC-REPORT-2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTC-REPORT-2005-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Spyware" is mostly classified into four types: <a href="/wiki/Adware" title="Adware">adware</a>, system monitors, tracking <a href="/wiki/HTTP_cookie" title="HTTP cookie">cookies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">trojans</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spyware-definition_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spyware-definition-2">[2]</a></sup> examples of other notorious types include <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a> capabilities that "phone home", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keylogger" title="Keylogger">keyloggers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rootkit" title="Rootkit">rootkits</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_beacons" title="Web beacons">web beacons</a>.</p>
<p>Spyware is mostly used for the purposes of tracking and storing Internet users' movements on the Web and serving up pop-up ads to Internet users. Whenever spyware is used for malicious purposes, its presence is typically hidden from the user and can be difficult to detect. Some spyware, such as keyloggers, may be installed by the owner of a shared, corporate, or <a href="/wiki/Public_computer" title="Public computer">public computer</a> intentionally in order to monitor users.</p>
<p>While the term <i>spyware</i> suggests software that monitors a user's computing, the functions of spyware can extend beyond simple monitoring. Spyware can collect almost any type of data, including <a href="/wiki/Personally_identifiable_information" title="Personally identifiable information">personal information</a> like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_surfing" title="Internet surfing">internet surfing</a> habits, user logins, and bank or credit account information. Spyware can also interfere with a user's control of a computer by installing additional software or redirecting <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browsers</a>. Some spyware can change computer settings, which can result in slow Internet connection speeds, un-authorized changes in browser settings, or changes to software settings.</p>
<p>Sometimes, spyware is included along with genuine software, and may come from a malicious website or may have been added to the intentional functionality of genuine software (see the paragraph about <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, below). In response to the emergence of spyware, a small industry has sprung up dealing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-spyware" title="Anti-spyware">anti-spyware</a> software. Running anti-spyware software has become a widely recognized element of <a href="/wiki/Computer_security" title="Computer security">computer security</a> practices, especially for computers running <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>. A number of jurisdictions have passed anti-spyware laws, which usually target any software that is surreptitiously installed to control a user's computer.</p>
<p>In German-speaking countries, spyware used or made by the government is called <i>govware</i> by computer experts (in common parlance: <i>Regierungstrojaner</i>, literally "Government Trojan"). Govware is typically a trojan horse software used to intercept communications from the target computer. Some countries, like Switzerland and Germany, have a legal framework governing the use of such software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the US, the term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policeware" title="Policeware">policeware</a>" has been used for similar purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Use of the term "spyware" has eventually declined as the practice of tracking users has been pushed ever further into the mainstream by major websites and data mining companies; these generally break no known laws and compel users to be tracked, not by fraudulent practices <i>per se</i>, but by the default settings created for users and the language of terms-of-service agreements. In one documented example, on CBS/CNet News reported, on March 7, 2011, on a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> analysis revealing the practice of Facebook and other websites of tracking users' browsing activity, linked to their identity, far beyond users' visits and activity within the Facebook site itself. The report stated: "Here's how it works. You go to Facebook, you log in, you spend some time there, and then ... you move on without logging out. Let's say the next site you go to is <i>New York Times</i>. Those buttons, without you clicking on them, have just reported back to Facebook and Twitter that you went there and also your identity within those accounts. Let's say you moved on to something like a site about depression. This one also has a tweet button, a Google widget, and those, too, can report back who you are and that you went there." The <i>WSJ</i> analysis was researched by Brian Kennish, founder of Disconnect, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Routes_of_infection">Routes of infection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Routes of infection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spyware does not necessarily spread in the same way as a <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">virus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Computer_worm" title="Computer worm">worm</a> because infected systems generally do not attempt to transmit or copy the software to other computers. Instead, spyware installs itself on a system by deceiving the user or by <a href="/wiki/Exploit_(computer_security)" title="Exploit (computer security)">exploiting</a> software vulnerabilities.</p>
<p>Most spyware is installed without knowledge, or by using deceptive tactics. Spyware may try to deceive users by bundling itself with desirable software. Other common tactics are using a <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">Trojan horse</a>, spy gadgets that look like normal devices but turn out to be something else, such as a USB Keylogger. These devices actually are connected to the device as memory units but are capable of recording each stroke made on the keyboard. Some spyware authors infect a system through security holes in the Web browser or in other software. When the user navigates to a Web page controlled by the spyware author, the page contains code which attacks the browser and forces the download and installation of spyware.</p>
<p>The installation of spyware frequently involves <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a>. Its popularity and history of security issues have made it a frequent target. Its deep integration with the Windows environment make it susceptible to attack into the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a>. <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> also serves as a point of attachment for spyware in the form of <a href="/wiki/Browser_Helper_Object" title="Browser Helper Object">Browser Helper Objects</a>, which modify the browser's behavior to add toolbars or to redirect traffic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_and_behaviors">Effects and behaviors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects and behaviors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">

<p>A spyware program rarely operates alone on a computer; an affected machine usually has multiple infections. Users frequently notice unwanted behavior and degradation of system performance. A spyware infestation can create significant unwanted <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> activity, disk usage, and network traffic. Stability issues, such as applications freezing, failure to boot, and system-wide crashes are also common. Spyware, which interferes with networking software commonly causes difficulty connecting to the Internet.</p>
<p>In some infections, the spyware is not even evident. Users assume in those situations that the performance issues relate to faulty hardware, Windows installation problems, or another <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> infection. Some owners of badly infected systems resort to contacting <a href="/wiki/Technical_support" title="Technical support">technical support</a> experts, or even buying a new computer because the existing system "has become too slow". Badly infected systems may require a clean reinstallation of all their software in order to return to full functionality.</p>
<p>Moreover, some types of spyware disable software <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firewall_(networking)" title="Firewall (networking)">firewalls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antivirus_software" title="Antivirus software">antivirus software</a>, and/or reduce browser security settings, which opens the system to further <a href="/wiki/Opportunistic_infection" title="Opportunistic infection">opportunistic infections</a>. Some spyware disables or even removes competing spyware programs, on the grounds that more spyware-related annoyances increase the likelihood that users will take action to remove the programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-competitor_removal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-competitor_removal-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keylogger" title="Keylogger">Keyloggers</a> are sometimes part of malware packages downloaded onto computers without the owners' knowledge. Some keylogger software is freely available on the internet, while others are commercial or private applications. Most keyloggers allow not only keyboard keystrokes to be captured, they also are often capable of collecting screen captures from the computer.</p>
<p>A typical Windows user has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_privileges" title="Administrative privileges">administrative privileges</a>, mostly for convenience. Because of this, any program the user runs has unrestricted access to the system. As with other <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, Windows users are able to follow the <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_least_privilege" title="Principle of least privilege">principle of least privilege</a> and use non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_privileges" title="Administrative privileges">administrator</a> accounts. Alternatively, they can reduce the <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(computing)" title="Privilege (computing)">privileges</a> of specific vulnerable Internet-facing <a href="/wiki/Process_(computing)" title="Process (computing)">processes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a>.</p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Windows_Vista" title="Windows Vista">Windows Vista</a> is, by default, a computer administrator that runs everything under limited user privileges, when a program requires administrative privileges, a <a href="/wiki/User_Account_Control" title="User Account Control">User Account Control</a> pop-up will prompt the user to allow or deny the action. This improves on the design used by previous versions of Windows.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remedies_and_prevention">Remedies and prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Remedies and prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus#Virus_removal" title="Computer virus">Computer virus § Virus removal</a></div>
<p>As the spyware threat has worsened, a number of techniques have emerged to counteract it. These include programs designed to remove or block spyware, as well as various user practices which reduce the chance of getting spyware on a system.</p>
<p>Nonetheless, spyware remains a costly problem. When a large number of pieces of spyware have infected a Windows computer, the only remedy may involve <a href="/wiki/Backup" title="Backup">backing up</a> user data, and fully reinstalling the <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a>. For instance, some spyware cannot be completely removed by Symantec, Microsoft, PC Tools.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-spyware_programs">Anti-spyware programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anti-spyware programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Spyware_removal" title="Category:Spyware removal">Category:Spyware removal</a></div>
<p>Many programmers and some commercial firms have released products dedicated to remove or block spyware. Programs such as PC Tools' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spyware_Doctor" title="Spyware Doctor">Spyware Doctor</a>, Lavasoft's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ad-Aware_SE" title="Ad-Aware SE">Ad-Aware SE</a></i> and Patrick Kolla's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spybot_-_Search_%26_Destroy" title="Spybot - Search &amp; Destroy">Spybot - Search &amp; Destroy</a></i> rapidly gained popularity as tools to remove, and in some cases intercept, spyware programs. On December 16, 2004, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> acquired the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GIANT_AntiSpyware" title="GIANT AntiSpyware">GIANT AntiSpyware</a></i> software,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> rebranding it as <i>Windows AntiSpyware beta</i> and releasing it as a free download for Genuine Windows XP and Windows 2003 users. (In 2006 it was renamed <a href="/wiki/Windows_Defender" title="Windows Defender">Windows Defender</a>).</p>
<p>Major anti-virus firms such as <a href="/wiki/Symantec" title="Symantec">Symantec</a>, <a href="/wiki/PC_Tools_(company)" title="PC Tools (company)">PC Tools</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McAfee" title="McAfee">McAfee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sophos" title="Sophos">Sophos</a> have also added anti-spyware features to their existing anti-virus products. Early on, anti-virus firms expressed reluctance to add anti-spyware functions, citing lawsuits brought by spyware authors against the authors of web sites and programs which described their products as "spyware". However, recent versions of these major firms' home and business anti-virus products do include anti-spyware functions, albeit treated differently from viruses. Symantec Anti-Virus, for instance, categorizes spyware programs as "extended threats" and now offers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real-time_protection" title="Real-time protection">real-time protection</a> against these threats.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="How_anti-spyware_software_works">How anti-spyware software works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: How anti-spyware software works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anti-spyware programs can combat spyware in two ways:</p>
<ol>
<li>They can provide real-time protection in a manner similar to that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti_virus" title="Anti virus">anti-virus</a> protection: they scan all incoming <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">network</a> data for spyware and blocks any threats it detects.</li>
<li>Anti-spyware software programs can be used solely for detection and removal of spyware software that has already been installed into the computer. This kind of anti-spyware can often be set to scan on a regular schedule.</li>
</ol>
<p>Such programs inspect the contents of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_registry" title="Windows registry">Windows registry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> files, and <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">installed programs</a>, and remove files and entries which match a list of known spyware. Real-time protection from spyware works identically to real-time anti-virus protection: the software scans disk files at download time, and blocks the activity of components known to represent spyware. In some cases, it may also intercept attempts to install start-up items or to modify browser settings. Earlier versions of anti-spyware programs focused chiefly on detection and removal. Javacool Software's <a href="/wiki/SpywareBlaster" title="SpywareBlaster">SpywareBlaster</a>, one of the first to offer real-time protection, blocked the installation of <a href="/wiki/ActiveX" title="ActiveX">ActiveX</a>-based spyware.</p>
<p>Like most anti-virus software, many anti-spyware/adware tools require a frequently updated database of threats. As new spyware programs are released, anti-spyware developers discover and evaluate them, adding to the list of known spyware, which allows the software to detect and remove new spyware. As a result, anti-spyware software is of limited usefulness without regular updates. Updates may be installed automatically or manually.</p>
<p>A popular generic spyware removal tool used by those that requires a certain degree of expertise is <a href="/wiki/HijackThis" title="HijackThis">HijackThis</a>, which scans certain areas of the Windows OS where spyware often resides and presents a list with items to delete manually. As most of the items are legitimate windows files/registry entries it is advised for those who are less knowledgeable on this subject to post a HijackThis log on the numerous antispyware sites and let the experts decide what to delete.</p>
<p>If a spyware program is not blocked and manages to get itself installed, it may resist attempts to terminate or uninstall it. Some programs work in pairs: when an anti-spyware scanner (or the user) terminates one running process, the other one respawns the killed program. Likewise, some spyware will detect attempts to remove registry keys and immediately add them again. Usually, booting the infected computer in <a href="/wiki/Safe_mode" title="Safe mode">safe mode</a> allows an anti-spyware program a better chance of removing persistent spyware. Killing the process tree may also work.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_practices">Security practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Security practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To detect spyware, computer users have found several practices useful in addition to installing anti-spyware programs. Many users have installed a <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a> other than <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a> or <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a>. Though no browser is completely safe, Internet Explorer was once at a greater risk for spyware infection due to its large user base as well as vulnerabilities such as <a href="/wiki/ActiveX" title="ActiveX">ActiveX</a> but these three major browsers are now close to equivalent when it comes to security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">ISPs</a>—particularly colleges and universities—have taken a different approach to blocking spyware: they use their network <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firewall_(networking)" title="Firewall (networking)">firewalls</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_proxy" title="Web proxy">web proxies</a> to block access to Web sites known to install spyware. On March 31, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>'s Information Technology department released a report detailing the behavior of one particular piece of proxy-based spyware, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marketscore" title="Marketscore">Marketscore</a></i>, and the steps the university took to intercept it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Many other educational institutions have taken similar steps.</p>
<p>Individual users can also install <a href="/wiki/Personal_firewall" title="Personal firewall">firewalls</a> from a variety of companies. These monitor the flow of information going to and from a networked computer and provide protection against spyware and malware. Some users install a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hosts_file" title="Hosts file">hosts file</a> which prevents the user's computer from connecting to known spyware-related web addresses. Spyware may get installed via certain <a href="/wiki/Shareware" title="Shareware">shareware</a> programs offered for download. Downloading programs only from reputable sources can provide some protection from this source of attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAA_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAA-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Stealware.22_and_affiliate_fraud">"Stealware" and affiliate fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "Stealware" and affiliate fraud'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few spyware vendors, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/180_Solutions" title="180 Solutions">180 Solutions</a>, have written what the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> has dubbed "<a href="/wiki/Stealware" title="Stealware">stealware</a>", and what spyware researcher <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Edelman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ben Edelman (page does not exist)">Ben Edelman</a> terms <i>affiliate fraud</i>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Click_fraud" title="Click fraud">click fraud</a>. Stealware diverts the payment of <a href="/wiki/Affiliate_marketing" title="Affiliate marketing">affiliate marketing</a> revenues from the legitimate affiliate to the spyware vendor.</p>
<p>Spyware which attacks <a href="/wiki/Affiliate_network" title="Affiliate network">affiliate networks</a> places the spyware operator's affiliate tag on the user's activity – replacing any other tag, if there is one. The spyware operator is the only party that gains from this. The user has their choices thwarted, a legitimate affiliate loses revenue, networks' reputations are injured, and vendors are harmed by having to pay out affiliate revenues to an "affiliate" who is not party to a contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edelman-180_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-edelman-180-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Click_fraud" title="Click fraud">Affiliate fraud</a> is a violation of the <a href="/wiki/Terms_of_service" title="Terms of service">terms of service</a> of most affiliate marketing networks. As a result, spyware operators such as 180 Solutions have been terminated from affiliate networks including LinkShare and ShareSale.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Mobile devices can also be vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Chargeware" title="Chargeware">chargeware</a>, which manipulates users into illegitimate mobile charges.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_theft_and_fraud">Identity theft and fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Identity theft and fraud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In one case, spyware has been closely associated with <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In August 2005, researchers from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Security_software" title="Security software">security software</a> firm Sunbelt Software suspected the creators of the common <a href="/wiki/CoolWebSearch" title="CoolWebSearch">CoolWebSearch</a> spyware had used it to transmit "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_chat_room" title="Internet chat room">chat sessions</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/User_name" title="User name">user names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Password" title="Password">passwords</a>, bank information, etc.";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> however it turned out that "it actually (was) its own sophisticated criminal little trojan that's independent of CWS."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This case is currently under investigation by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> estimates that 27.3 million Americans have been victims of identity theft, and that financial losses from identity theft totaled nearly $48 billion for businesses and financial institutions and at least $5 billion in out-of-pocket expenses for individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_rights_management">Digital rights management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Digital rights management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some copy-protection technologies have borrowed from spyware. In 2005, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_BMG_Music_Entertainment" title="Sony BMG Music Entertainment">Sony BMG Music Entertainment</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_BMG_CD_copy_protection_scandal" title="Sony BMG CD copy protection scandal">found to be using</a> <a href="/wiki/Rootkit" title="Rootkit">rootkits</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Extended_Copy_Protection" title="Extended Copy Protection">XCP</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a> technology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sony_scandal_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sony_scandal-18">[18]</a></sup> Like spyware, not only was it difficult to detect and uninstall, it was so poorly written that most efforts to remove it could have rendered computers unable to function. <a href="/wiki/Texas_Attorney_General" title="Texas Attorney General">Texas Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Greg_Abbott" title="Greg Abbott">Greg Abbott</a> filed suit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_AG_lawsuit_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Texas_AG_lawsuit-19">[19]</a></sup> and three separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class-action" title="Class-action">class-action</a> suits were filed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-class-action_suits_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-class-action_suits-20">[20]</a></sup> Sony BMG later provided a workaround on its website to help users remove it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sony_workaround_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sony_workaround-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning on April 25, 2006, Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Windows_Genuine_Advantage" title="Windows Genuine Advantage">Windows Genuine Advantage</a> Notifications application<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> was installed on most Windows PCs as a "critical security update". While the main purpose of this deliberately uninstallable application is to ensure the copy of Windows on the machine was lawfully purchased and installed, it also installs software that has been accused of "<a href="/wiki/Phoning_home" title="Phoning home">phoning home</a>" on a daily basis, like spyware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It can be removed with the RemoveWGA tool.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_relationships">Personal relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spyware has been used to monitor electronic activities of partners in intimate relationships. At least one software package, Loverspy, was specifically marketed for this purpose. Depending on local laws regarding communal/marital property, observing a partner's online activity without their consent may be illegal; the author of Loverspy and several users of the product were indicted in California in 2005 on charges of wiretapping and various computer crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Browser_cookies">Browser cookies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Browser cookies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anti-spyware programs often report Web advertisers' <a href="/wiki/HTTP_cookie" title="HTTP cookie">HTTP cookies</a>, the small text files that track browsing activity, as spyware. While they are not always inherently malicious, many users object to third parties using space on their personal computers for their business purposes, and many anti-spyware programs offer to remove them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These common spyware programs illustrate the diversity of behaviors found in these attacks. Note that as with computer viruses, researchers give names to spyware programs which may not be used by their creators. Programs may be grouped into "families" based not on shared program code, but on common behaviors, or by "following the money" of apparent financial or business connections. For instance, a number of the spyware programs distributed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claria" title="Claria">Claria</a> are collectively known as "Gator". Likewise, programs that are frequently installed together may be described as parts of the same spyware package, even if they function separately.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/CoolWebSearch" title="CoolWebSearch">CoolWebSearch</a></b>, a group of programs, takes advantage of Internet Explorer vulnerabilities. The package directs traffic to advertisements on Web sites including <i>coolwebsearch.com</i>. It displays pop-up ads, rewrites <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">search engine</a> results, and alters the infected computer's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hosts_file" title="Hosts file">hosts file</a> to direct <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">DNS</a> lookups to these sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doxdb-coolwebsearch_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-doxdb-coolwebsearch-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/FinFisher" title="FinFisher">FinFisher</a></b>, sometimes called <b>FinSpy</b> is a high-end surveillance suite sold to law enforcement and intelligence agencies. Support services such as training and technology updates are part of the package.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT83012_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT83012-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/HuntBar" title="HuntBar">HuntBar</a></b>, aka <b>WinTools</b> or <b>Adware.Websearch</b>, was installed by an ActiveX <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drive-by_installation" title="Drive-by installation">drive-by download</a> at affiliate Web sites, or by advertisements displayed by other spyware programs—an example of how spyware can install more spyware. These programs add toolbars to IE, track aggregate browsing behavior, redirect affiliate references, and display advertisements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caspywareic_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-caspywareic-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pchell_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-pchell-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Internet_Optimizer" title="Internet Optimizer">Internet Optimizer</a></b>, also known as <b>DyFuCa</b>, redirects Internet Explorer error pages to advertising. When users follow a broken link or enter an erroneous URL, they see a page of advertisements. However, because password-protected Web sites (HTTP Basic authentication) use the same mechanism as HTTP errors, Internet Optimizer makes it impossible for the user to access password-protected sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doxdb-internetoptimizer_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-doxdb-internetoptimizer-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Spyware such as <b>Look2Me</b> hides inside system-critical processes and start up even in safe mode. With no process to terminate they are harder to detect and remove, which is a combination of both spyware and a <a href="/wiki/Rootkit" title="Rootkit">rootkit</a>. Rootkit technology is also seeing increasing use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rootkit_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-rootkit-32">[32]</a></sup> as newer spyware programs also have specific countermeasures against well known <a href="/wiki/Malware#Anti-malware_strategies" title="Malware">anti-malware</a> products and may prevent them from running or being installed, or even uninstall them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Movieland" title="Movieland">Movieland</a></b>, also known as <b>Moviepass.tv</b> and <b>Popcorn.net</b>, is a movie download service that has been the subject of thousands of complaints to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> (FTC), the <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Attorney_General" title="State Attorney General">State Attorney General's Office</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Better_Business_Bureau" title="Better Business Bureau">Better Business Bureau</a>, and other agencies. Consumers complained they were held hostage by a cycle of oversized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop-up_window" title="Pop-up window">pop-up windows</a> demanding payment of at least $29.95, claiming that they had signed up for a three-day free trial but had not cancelled before the trial period was over, and were thus obligated to pay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTCNEWSRELEASE_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTCNEWSRELEASE-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WANEWSRELEASE_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-WANEWSRELEASE-34">[34]</a></sup> The FTC filed a <a href="/wiki/Complaint" title="Complaint">complaint</a>, since <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settled</a>, against Movieland and <a href="/wiki/Movieland#Defendants" title="Movieland">eleven other defendants</a> charging them with having "engaged in a nationwide scheme to use <a href="/wiki/Deception" title="Deception">deception</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">coercion</a> to extract payments from consumers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTCCOMPLAINT_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTCCOMPLAINT-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>WeatherStudio</b> has a plugin that displays a window-panel near the <i>bottom</i> of a browser window. The official website notes that it is easy to remove (uninstall) WeatherStudio from a computer, using its own uninstall-program, such as under C:\Program Files\WeatherStudio. Once WeatherStudio is removed, a browser returns to the prior display appearance, without the need to modify the browser settings.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Zango_(company)" title="Zango (company)">Zango</a></b> (formerly <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/180_Solutions" title="180 Solutions">180 Solutions</a></b>) transmits detailed information to advertisers about the Web sites which users visit. It also alters HTTP requests for <a href="/wiki/Affiliate_marketing" title="Affiliate marketing">affiliate</a> advertisements linked from a Web site, so that the advertisements make unearned profit for the 180 Solutions company. It opens pop-up ads that cover over the Web sites of competing companies (as seen in their [Zango End User License Agreement]).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edelman-180_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-edelman-180-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Zlob_trojan" title="Zlob trojan">Zlob trojan</a></b>, or just <b>Zlob</b>, downloads itself to a computer via an <a href="/wiki/ActiveX" title="ActiveX">ActiveX</a> <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codec</a> and reports information back to <b>Control Server</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Some information can be the search-history, the Websites visited, and even keystrokes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> More recently, Zlob has been known to hijack routers set to defaults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Malware_Changes_Router_Settings_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Malware_Changes_Router_Settings-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first recorded use of the term <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/spyware" title="wikt:spyware">spyware</a> occurred on October 16, 1995 in a <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> post that poked fun at <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">business model</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coinage_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-coinage-37">[37]</a></sup> <i>Spyware</i> at first denoted <i>software</i> meant for espionage purposes. However, in early 2000 the founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zone_Labs" title="Zone Labs">Zone Labs</a>, Gregor Freund, used the term in a press release<sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since April 2014">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> for the <a href="/wiki/ZoneAlarm" title="ZoneAlarm">ZoneAlarm Personal Firewall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wienbar_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-wienbar-38">[38]</a></sup> Later in 2000, a parent using ZoneAlarm was alerted to the fact that "Reader Rabbit," educational software marketed to children by the <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> toy company, was surreptitiously sending data back to Mattel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hawkins_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hawkins-39">[39]</a></sup> Since then, "spyware" has taken on its present sense.</p>
<p>According to a 2005 study by <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> and the National Cyber-Security Alliance, 61 percent of surveyed users' computers were infected with form of spyware. 92 percent of surveyed users with spyware reported that they did not know of its presence, and 91 percent reported that they had not given permission for the installation of the spyware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aolstudy_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-aolstudy-40">[40]</a></sup> As of 2006<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, spyware has become one of the preeminent security threats to computer systems running Microsoft Windows <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>. Computers on which <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> (IE) is the primary <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">browser</a> are particularly vulnerable to such attacks, not only because IE is the most widely used,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcworld-ie_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcworld-ie-41">[41]</a></sup> but because its tight integration with Windows allows spyware access to crucial parts of the operating system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcworld-ie_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcworld-ie-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer_6" title="Internet Explorer 6">Internet Explorer 6</a> SP2 was released as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_XP_Service_Pack_2" title="Windows XP Service Pack 2">Windows XP Service Pack 2</a>, the browser would automatically display an installation window for any <a href="/wiki/ActiveX" title="ActiveX">ActiveX</a> component that a website wanted to install. The combination of user ignorance about these changes, and the assumption by <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> that all <a href="/wiki/ActiveX" title="ActiveX">ActiveX</a> components are benign, helped to spread spyware significantly. Many spyware components would also make use of <a href="/wiki/Exploit_(computer_security)" title="Exploit (computer security)">exploits</a> in <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a>, Internet Explorer and Windows to install without user knowledge or permission.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Windows_Registry" title="Windows Registry">Windows Registry</a> contains multiple sections where modification of key values allows software to be executed automatically when the operating system boots. Spyware can exploit this design to circumvent attempts at removal. The spyware typically will link itself from each location in the <a href="/wiki/Windows_Registry" title="Windows Registry">registry</a> that allows execution. Once running, the spyware will periodically check if any of these links are removed. If so, they will be automatically restored. This ensures that the spyware will execute when the operating system is booted, even if some (or most) of the registry links are removed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_law">Criminal law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Criminal law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unauthorized access to a computer is illegal under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_crime" title="Computer crime">computer crime</a> laws, such as the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Computer_Fraud_and_Abuse_Act" title="Computer Fraud and Abuse Act">Computer Fraud and Abuse Act</a>, the U.K.'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_Misuse_Act" title="Computer Misuse Act">Computer Misuse Act</a>, and similar laws in other countries. Since owners of computers infected with spyware generally claim that they never authorized the installation, a <i>prima facie</i> reading would suggest that the promulgation of spyware would count as a criminal act. Law enforcement has often pursued the authors of other malware, particularly viruses. However, few spyware developers have been prosecuted, and many operate openly as strictly legitimate businesses, though some have faced lawsuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spyware producers argue that, contrary to the users' claims, users do in fact give <a href="/wiki/Consent" title="Consent">consent</a> to installations. Spyware that comes bundled with <a href="/wiki/Shareware" title="Shareware">shareware</a> applications may be described in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legalese" title="Legalese">legalese</a> text of an <a href="/wiki/End-user_license_agreement" title="End-user license agreement">end-user license agreement</a> (EULA). Many users habitually ignore these purported contracts, but spyware companies such as Claria say these demonstrate that users have consented.</p>
<p>Despite the ubiquity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EULA" title="EULA">EULAs</a> agreements, under which a single click can be taken as consent to the entire text, relatively little <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caselaw" title="Caselaw">caselaw</a> has resulted from their use. It has been established in most <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions that this type of agreement can be a binding contract <i>in certain circumstances.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clickwrap_legality_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-clickwrap_legality-63">[63]</a></sup> This does not, however, mean that every such agreement is a contract, or that every term in one is enforceable.</p>
<p>Some jurisdictions, including the U.S. states of <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> have passed laws criminalizing some forms of spyware. Such laws make it illegal for anyone other than the owner or operator of a computer to install software that alters Web-browser settings, monitors keystrokes, or disables computer-security software.</p>
<p>In the United States, lawmakers introduced a bill in 2005 entitled the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Spyware_Prevention_Act" title="Internet Spyware Prevention Act">Internet Spyware Prevention Act</a>, which would imprison creators of spyware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_sanctions">Administrative sanctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Administrative sanctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="US_FTC_actions">US FTC actions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: US FTC actions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The US <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> has sued Internet marketing organizations under the "<a href="/wiki/Unfairness_doctrine" title="Unfairness doctrine">unfairness doctrine</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> to make them stop infecting consumers' PCs with spyware. In one case, that against Seismic Entertainment Productions, the FTC accused the defendants of developing a program that seized control of PCs nationwide, infected them with spyware and other malicious software, bombarded them with a barrage of pop-up advertising for Seismic's clients, exposed the PCs to security risks, and caused them to malfunction. Seismic then offered to sell the victims an "antispyware" program to fix the computers, and stop the popups and other problems that Seismic had caused. On November 21, 2006, a settlement was entered in federal court under which a $1.75 million judgment was imposed in one case and $1.86 million in another, but the defendants were insolvent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a second case, brought against CyberSpy Software LLC, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">FTC</a> charged that CyberSpy marketed and sold "RemoteSpy" keylogger spyware to clients who would then secretly monitor unsuspecting consumers' computers. According to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">FTC</a>, Cyberspy touted RemoteSpy as a "100% undetectable" way to "Spy on Anyone. From Anywhere." The FTC has obtained a temporary order prohibiting the defendants from selling the software and disconnecting from the Internet any of their servers that collect, store, or provide access to information that this software has gathered. The case is still in its preliminary stages. A complaint filed by the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Privacy_Information_Center" title="Electronic Privacy Information Center">Electronic Privacy Information Center</a> (EPIC) brought the RemoteSpy software to the FTC's attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands_OPTA">Netherlands OPTA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Netherlands OPTA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An administrative fine, the first of its kind in Europe, has been issued by the Independent Authority of Posts and Telecommunications (OPTA) from the Netherlands. It applied fines in total value of Euro 1,000,000 for infecting 22 million computers. The spyware concerned is called DollarRevenue. The law articles that have been violated are art. 4.1 of the Decision on universal service providers and on the interests of end users; the fines have been issued based on art. 15.4 taken together with art. 15.10 of the Dutch telecommunications law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_law">Civil law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Civil law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State_Attorney_General" title="New York State Attorney General">New York State Attorney General</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a> <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer" title="Eliot Spitzer">Eliot Spitzer</a> has pursued spyware companies for fraudulent installation of software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In a suit brought in 2005 by Spitzer, the California firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermix_Media,_Inc." title="Intermix Media, Inc.">Intermix Media, Inc.</a> ended up settling, by agreeing to pay US$7.5 million and to stop distributing spyware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hijacking of Web advertisements has also led to litigation. In June 2002, a number of large Web publishers sued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claria" title="Claria">Claria</a> for replacing advertisements, but settled out of court.</p>
<p>Courts have not yet had to decide whether advertisers can be held <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">liable</a> for spyware that displays their ads. In many cases, the companies whose advertisements appear in spyware pop-ups do not directly do business with the spyware firm. Rather, they have contracted with an <a href="/wiki/Advertising_agency" title="Advertising agency">advertising agency</a>, which in turn contracts with an online subcontractor who gets paid by the number of "impressions" or appearances of the advertisement. Some major firms such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dell_Computer" title="Dell Computer">Dell Computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> have sacked advertising agencies that have run their ads in spyware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libel_suits_by_spyware_developers">Libel suits by spyware developers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Libel suits by spyware developers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Litigation has gone both ways. Since "spyware" has become a common <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a>, some makers have filed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slander_and_libel" title="Slander and libel">libel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a> actions when their products have been so described. In 2003, Gator (now known as Claria) filed suit against the website <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=PC_Pitstop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="PC Pitstop (page does not exist)">PC Pitstop</a> for describing its program as "spyware".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> PC Pitstop settled, agreeing not to use the word "spyware", but continues to describe harm caused by the Gator/Claria software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> As a result, other anti-spyware and anti-virus companies have also used other terms such as "<b>potentially unwanted programs</b>" or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greyware" title="Greyware">greyware</a> to denote these products.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="WebcamGate">WebcamGate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spyware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: WebcamGate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Robbins_v._Lower_Merion_School_District" title="Robbins v. Lower Merion School District">Robbins v. Lower Merion School District</a></div>
<p>In the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Robbins_v._Lower_Merion_School_District" title="Robbins v. Lower Merion School District">WebcamGate</a> case, plaintiffs charged two suburban Philadelphia high schools secretly spied on students by surreptitiously and remotely activating webcams embedded in school-issued laptops the students were using at home, and therefore infringed on their privacy rights. The school loaded each student's computer with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LANrev" title="LANrev">LANrev</a>'s remote activation tracking software. This included the now-discontinued "TheftTrack". While TheftTrack was not enabled by default on the software, the program allowed the school district to elect to activate it, and to choose which of the TheftTrack surveillance options the school wanted to enable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>TheftTrack allowed school district employees to secretly remotely activate the webcam embedded in the student's laptop, above the laptop's screen. That allowed school officials to secretly take photos through the webcam, of whatever was in front of it and in its line of sight, and send the photos to the school's server. The LANrev software disabled the webcams for all other uses (<i>e.g.</i>, students were unable to use <a href="/wiki/Photo_Booth" title="Photo Booth">Photo Booth</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_chat" title="Video chat">video chat</a>), so most students mistakenly believed their webcams did not work at all. In addition to webcam surveillance, TheftTrack allowed school officials to take screenshots, and send them to the school's server. In addition, LANrev allowed school officials to take snapshots of instant messages, web browsing, music playlists, and written compositions. The schools admitted to secretly snapping over 66,000 webshots and <a href="/wiki/Screenshot" title="Screenshot">screenshots</a>, including webcam shots of students in their bedrooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USATODAY_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-USATODAY-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
