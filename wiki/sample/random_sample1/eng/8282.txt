<p><b>Sparks</b> are an American <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a> band formed in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> in 1971 by brothers <a href="/wiki/Ron_Mael" title="Ron Mael">Ron</a> (keyboards) and <a href="/wiki/Russell_Mael" title="Russell Mael">Russell Mael</a> (vocals), renamed from <b>Halfnelson</b>, formed in 1968. Known for their quirky approach to songwriting,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-1">[1]</a></sup> Sparks' music is often accompanied by intelligent, sophisticated, and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acerbic" title="wikt:acerbic">acerbic</a> lyrics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAWeekly_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeekly-2">[2]</a></sup> and an idiosyncratic, theatrical stage presence, typified in the contrast between Russell's animated, hyperactive frontman antics and Ron's deadpan scowling. They are also noted for Russell Mael's <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsetto</a> voice and Ron Mael's keyboard style.</p>
<p>While achieving chart success in various countries around the world including the United Kingdom, Germany, France, and the United States, they have enjoyed a cult following since their first releases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epitonic_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epitonic-3">[3]</a></sup> During the late 1970s, when in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Moroder" title="Giorgio Moroder">Giorgio Moroder</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Telex_(band)" title="Telex (band)">Telex</a> subsequently), Sparks reinvented themselves as a <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a>/<a href="/wiki/Synthpop" title="Synthpop">synthpop</a> duo, and abandoned the traditional rock band line up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> Their frequently changing styles and visual presentations have kept the band at the forefront of modern, artful pop music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkPress_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkPress-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2002 release of <i><a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Beethoven" title="Lil' Beethoven">Lil' Beethoven</a></i>, their "genre-defying opus",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MusicOMH_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MusicOMH-6">[6]</a></sup> as well as the more recent albums <i><a href="/wiki/Hello_Young_Lovers_(Sparks_album)" title="Hello Young Lovers (Sparks album)">Hello Young Lovers</a></i> (2006, their 20th studio album), <i><a href="/wiki/Exotic_Creatures_of_the_Deep" title="Exotic Creatures of the Deep">Exotic Creatures of the Deep</a></i> (2008), and their latest fantasy musical <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seduction_of_Ingmar_Bergman" title="The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman">The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman</a></i> (2009–2010) have brought Sparks renewed critical and commercial success, and seen them continue to "steer clear of pop conventions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-7">[7]</a></sup> The band also released an album with Scottish <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(band)" title="Franz Ferdinand (band)">Franz Ferdinand</a>, as the supergroup <a href="/wiki/FFS_(band)" title="FFS (band)">FFS</a>, titled <i><a href="/wiki/FFS_(album)" title="FFS (album)">FFS</a></i>, released on June 8, 2015.</p>
<p>Sparks are best known for the songs "<a href="/wiki/This_Town_Ain%27t_Big_Enough_for_Both_of_Us" title="This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us">This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us</a>", which reached number 2 in the British charts in 1974; the electronic hit "<a href="/wiki/The_Number_One_Song_in_Heaven" title="The Number One Song in Heaven">The Number One Song in Heaven</a>" in 1979; "When I'm With You" which topped the French Charts in 1980; the single "<a href="/wiki/I_Predict" title="I Predict">I Predict</a>", which provided Sparks' first appearance on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, reaching No. 60 in May 1982; the 1983 single "<a href="/wiki/Cool_Places" title="Cool Places">Cool Places</a>" with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Go-Gos" title="The Go-Gos">Go-Go</a> <a href="/wiki/Jane_Wiedlin" title="Jane Wiedlin">Jane Wiedlin</a>, and "When Do I Get To Sing 'My Way'" which topped the German and European charts in 1994/95 and was the top airplay record in Germany for 1994.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sparks_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inception">Inception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sparks_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of Sparks spans five decades, from inception in the late 1960s LA club scene, through adoption by British fans in the middle 1970s, electronic experimentation in the late 1970s, and finally breaking through in the US in the early 1980s, a cinematic sojourn at the end of that decade with a return to form<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in the mid-1990s which continues to this day as they continue to push the boundaries of pop music<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Brothers Ron and Russell Mael grew up in <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Palisades,_Los_Angeles" title="Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles">Pacific Palisades</a>, in western <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County, California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> during the "Golden Age" of the LA club scene, with <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">the Doors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Love_(band)" title="Love (band)">Love</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Standells" title="The Standells">the Standells</a> regularly playing the <a href="/wiki/Whisky_a_Go_Go" title="Whisky a Go Go">Whisky a Go Go</a> on <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Strip" title="Sunset Strip">Sunset Strip</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Beach_Boys" title="The Beach Boys">the Beach Boys</a> playing the afternoon event Teenage Fair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-9">[9]</a></sup> Both Ron and Russell Mael are seen in the audience during <a href="/wiki/The_Ronettes" title="The Ronettes">the Ronettes</a>' section of the rock movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_T.N.T._Show" title="The Big T.N.T. Show">The Big T.N.T. Show</a></i>, filmed in 1966. Both attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a>, Ron studying cinema and graphic art, Russell theatre arts and filmmaking. Detesting the folk music scene, which they considered "cerebral and sedate and we had no time for that",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-9">[9]</a></sup> they developed a particular taste in English bands of the time such as <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syd_Barrett" title="Syd Barrett">Syd Barrett</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">the Kinks</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Move" title="The Move">the Move</a>, which led to their description of themselves as <a href="/wiki/Anglophile" title="Anglophile">"Anglophiles"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Forming Halfnelson in 1968, they soon came to the attention of producer <a href="/wiki/Todd_Rundgren" title="Todd Rundgren">Todd Rundgren</a>, at whose urging <a href="/wiki/Albert_Grossman" title="Albert Grossman">Albert Grossman</a> signed the band to his <a href="/wiki/Bearsville_Records" title="Bearsville Records">Bearsville</a> record label. Their self-titled debut – with the line-up consisting of college friend <a href="/wiki/Earle_Mankey" title="Earle Mankey">Earle Mankey</a> on guitar, Mankey's brother <a href="/wiki/James_Mankey" title="James Mankey">James</a> on bass, Harley Feinstein now on drums and Rundgren producing – sold poorly, but after switching labels to <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a> and renaming themselves Sparks, a play on the <a href="/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="Marx Brothers">Marx Brothers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllSparks_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AllSparks-10">[10]</a></sup> the re-issued debut spawned the minor regional hit "Wonder Girl".</p>
<p>Their follow-up album, <a href="/wiki/A_Woofer_in_Tweeter%27s_Clothing" title="A Woofer in Tweeter's Clothing">A Woofer in Tweeter's Clothing</a>, led to a tour of the United Kingdom, including a residency at the <a href="/wiki/Marquee_Club" title="Marquee Club">Marquee Club</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> which incidentally saw on one night <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> open for Sparks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These London appearances helped them to secure a significant cult following.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> An appearance on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Television" title="BBC Television">BBC Television</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Grey_Whistle_Test" title="The Old Grey Whistle Test">The Old Grey Whistle Test</a></i> led to wider interest where host <a href="/wiki/Bob_Harris_(radio)" title="Bob Harris (radio)">Bob Harris</a> compared the band to a cross between <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Mothers_of_Invention" title="The Mothers of Invention">Mothers of Invention</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Monkees" title="The Monkees">the Monkees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllSparks_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-AllSparks-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>

<p>Relocating to England in 1973 with a new manager, John Hewlett, founder of <a href="/wiki/John%27s_Children" title="John's Children">John's Children</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoadOfOld_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoadOfOld-12">[12]</a></sup> and a deal from <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island Records</a>, thanks in part to the exposure garnered by their <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC 2</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Old_Grey_Whistle_Test" title="The Old Grey Whistle Test"><i>Whistle Test</i></a> performance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> they placed an ad in music weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a></i> ("Wanted bass player for Sparks. Must be beard free and exciting")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mojo_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mojo-13">[13]</a></sup> and through this hired <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gordon" title="Martin Gordon">Martin Gordon</a>. With <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Fisher_(musician)" title="Adrian Fisher (musician)">Adrian Fisher</a> on guitar and <a href="/wiki/Norman_%22Dinky%22_Diamond" title='Norman "Dinky" Diamond'>Norman "Dinky" Diamond</a> on drums, in the midst of power strikes and a threatened vinyl shortage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman2-14">[14]</a></sup> they recorded their breakthrough <i><a href="/wiki/Kimono_My_House" title="Kimono My House">Kimono My House</a></i> in 1974, scoring a number 2 hit with the single "<a href="/wiki/This_Town_Ain%27t_Big_Enough_for_Both_of_Us" title="This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us">This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us</a>", despite <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> betting producer <a href="/wiki/Muff_Winwood" title="Muff Winwood">Muff Winwood</a> that the song would not break into the charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman2-14">[14]</a></sup> Sparks became a UK teen sensation appearing on the cover of <i>Melody Maker</i>, <i>Record Mirror</i> and countless other pop magazines in the UK and Europe. Hits such as "This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us", "Amateur Hour" and "Never Turn Your Back on Mother Earth" led to many appearances on the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s flagship music show <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i>. Russell's hyperactive movements were in sharp contrast to the keyboard-bound, soberly dressed Ron's expressionless squint and Chaplin-esque mustache.</p>
<p>Gordon and Fisher were later replaced by Trevor White and Ian Hampton. In 1975, the revised band returned to the USA to tour supporting the Kimono &amp; Propaganda albums which had gained strong cult attention in New York, Cleveland, Chicago, San Francisco and Los Angeles primarily from FM radio play and a national TV appearance on <a href="/wiki/Don_Kirshner%27s_Rock_Concert" title="Don Kirshner's Rock Concert">Don Kirshner's Rock Concert</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flo_and_Eddie" title="Flo and Eddie">Flo and Eddie</a> were the supporting act. Influential 1970s progressive FM radio station powerhouse WMMS in Cleveland and its famed DJs such as Kid Leo initially championed the band in America. Sparks also performed on <a href="/wiki/American_Bandstand" title="American Bandstand">American Bandstand</a> in 1975 with host <a href="/wiki/Dick_Clark" title="Dick Clark">Dick Clark</a> mugging with Ron and on countless other TV shows in the US and abroad post 1977.</p>
<p>The follow-up albums, <i>Propaganda</i> and <i>Indiscreet</i>, the latter produced by <a href="/wiki/Tony_Visconti" title="Tony Visconti">Tony Visconti</a>, were similarly successful and produced the hit singles "Looks, Looks, Looks", "Never Turn Your Back on Mother Earth" and "Something for the Girl With Everything".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>1976 saw the Maels return home to LA. Concerned that their music may have become stale, they changed to a more "American" sound and recorded <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Beat_(album)" title="Big Beat (album)">Big Beat</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Holmes" title="Rupert Holmes">Rupert Holmes</a> and Jeffrey Lesser on production, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Introducing_Sparks" title="Introducing Sparks">Introducing Sparks</a></i>; both were mostly recorded with session musicians. This new "West Coast" sound yielded such songs as "Nothing To Do", "Everybody's Stupid", and "Throw Her Away (and Get A New One)". In 1976, Sparks made one of their first forays into the movie business, making a cameo appearance in the thriller film <i><a href="/wiki/Rollercoaster_(1977_film)" title="Rollercoaster (1977 film)">Rollercoaster</a></i>, after <a href="/wiki/Kiss_(band)" title="Kiss (band)">Kiss</a> turned down the roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mojo2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mojo2-15">[15]</a></sup> They performed the songs "Fill 'Er Up" and "Big Boy."</p>
<p>By 1977 the brothers found themselves at a crossroads. They had tired of the rock band format and determined to take their music in a more electronic direction. They had expressed admiration for <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Moroder" title="Giorgio Moroder">Giorgio Moroder</a> to a German journalist who turned out to be a friend of his. As a result, they teamed up with Moroder and created <i><a href="/wiki/No._1_in_Heaven" title="No. 1 in Heaven">No. 1 in Heaven</a></i>, an album which would not only redefine the Sparks sound and challenge the concept of what is meant by a band<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> but also become a major influence on emerging electro pop artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAWeekly_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeekly-2">[2]</a></sup> The follow-up in 1980, <i><a href="/wiki/Terminal_Jive" title="Terminal Jive">Terminal Jive</a></i>, scored a massive hit single in France, "When I'm With You", which led to the Maels' staying in the country for a year promoting the album, Russell being sufficiently fluent in French.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> The single also hit the Top 20 in Australia, reaching No. 14.</p>
<p>In 1978 and 1979 Sparks was finding the electronic equipment they had adopted for their new sound, too cumbersome to tour with and they returned to the more traditional band format for their next three releases. <i><a href="/wiki/Whomp_That_Sucker" title="Whomp That Sucker">Whomp That Sucker</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Angst_in_My_Pants" title="Angst in My Pants">Angst in My Pants</a></i> (two tracks from which appear later in the 1983 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Valley_Girl_(film)" title="Valley Girl (film)">Valley Girl</a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/In_Outer_Space" title="In Outer Space">Sparks in Outer Space</a></i>. They finally broke into the US singles chart with "<a href="/wiki/Cool_Places" title="Cool Places">Cool Places</a>" from 1983's <i>Sparks in Outer Space</i>. The track was a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/The_Go-Go%27s" title="The Go-Go's">Go-Go</a> <a href="/wiki/Jane_Wiedlin" title="Jane Wiedlin">Jane Wiedlin</a>, who, at one time, ran her own Sparks fanclub,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mojo2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mojo2-15">[15]</a></sup> and its success was in part thanks to Los Angeles' <a href="/wiki/KROQ-FM" title="KROQ-FM">KROQ-FM</a> radio station, which hailed them as local heroes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAWeekly_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeekly-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1984, the Maels wrote and performed several original songs on the soundtrack for the teen comedy feature film <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Manners_(film)" title="Bad Manners (film)">Bad Manners</a></i> (aka: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Growing_Pains_(1984_film)" title="Growing Pains (1984 film)">Growing Pains</a></i>), including the film's title song, "Bad Manners".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amazon.co.uk_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amazon.co.uk-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The late 1980s and early 1990s saw the brothers concentrate on filmmaking, particularly an attempt to make a <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mai,_The_Psychic_Girl" title="Mai, The Psychic Girl">Mai, The Psychic Girl</a></i>, into a movie musical. Despite interest from <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">Tim Burton</a> and six years' work on the project, the film has not yet gone into production. There currently is speculation that Tim Burton may once again take on the project and direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1993, Ron and Russell returned with the single "National Crime Awareness Week," their first release since the 1988 album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_Design" title="Interior Design">Interior Design</a></i>. The song was produced by the Scottish band <a href="/wiki/Finitribe" title="Finitribe">Finitribe</a>. In 1994, the Maels released <i><a href="/wiki/Gratuitous_Sax_%26_Senseless_Violins" title="Gratuitous Sax &amp; Senseless Violins">Gratuitous Sax &amp; Senseless Violins</a></i>, providing the hit singles "When Do I Get To Sing My Way" and "When I Kiss You (I Hear Charlie Parker Playing)". In Germany, "When Do I Get To Sing My Way" was the No. 1 airplay song for 1994 as well as being hailed critically for its poignant lyrics and touching melody. The group toured in support of the album with percussionist Christi Haydon playing drums. Haydon also appeared in the videos for "When Do I Get To Sing My Way" and "When I Kiss You (I Hear Charlie Parker Playing)," which were both directed by <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Muller" title="Sophie Muller">Sophie Muller</a>.</p>
<p>1997 saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Plagiarism_(album)" title="Plagiarism (album)">Plagiarism</a></i>, an album of cover versions of their own songs featuring collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Faith_No_More" title="Faith No More">Faith No More</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erasure" title="Erasure">Erasure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Somerville" title="Jimmy Somerville">Jimmy Somerville</a>. Half of the album was recorded by Tony Visconti in London with the other half recorded by the brothers in their own purpose-built studio in LA, surrounded by busts of <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAWeekly_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeekly-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1998 they recorded the soundtrack for the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Knock_Off_(film)" title="Knock Off (film)">Knock Off</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Jean-Claude_Van_Damme" title="Jean-Claude Van Damme">Jean-Claude Van Damme</a>, directed by the acclaimed Hong Kong-based producer/director <a href="/wiki/Tsui_Hark" title="Tsui Hark">Tsui Hark</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkPress_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkPress-5">[5]</a></sup> (who had appeared on his own tribute song by the band on the album <i>Gratuitous Sax and Senseless Violins</i>).</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Balls_(Sparks_album)" title="Balls (Sparks album)">Balls</a></i>, released in 2000, again put the band in a context of electronic instrumentation with some of Ron Mael's most striking and perceptive lyrics. With the release of "Balls" the band toured the UK, Germany, Japan and Australia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sparks_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <i>Balls</i>, the band resurfaced in 2002 with the release of the album which has been described as their "genre-defying opus". <i>Lil' Beethoven</i>, featuring quasi-classical arrangements strings and choirs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> led to renewed interest in the band, for instance <i><a href="/wiki/Record_Collector" title="Record Collector">Record Collector magazine</a></i> named the album as one of its "Best New Albums of 2002", describing it as "... possibly the most exciting and interesting release ever from such a long established act"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector2-19">[19]</a></sup> and later in 2003 saying "... it really does feel like one of the best albums ever made."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> A UK and European tour had the band playing the entire album each night in the first half of the show, with fan favourites making up the second. The line-up now included former <a href="/wiki/Faith_No_More" title="Faith No More">Faith No More</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Dean_Menta" title="Dean Menta">Dean Menta</a> in addition to Tammy Glover on drums. Long-time fan <a href="/wiki/Morrissey" title="Morrissey">Morrissey</a> invited Sparks to perform at the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Meltdown_(festival)" title="Meltdown (festival)">Meltdown Festival</a>, of which he was curator. They performed their breakthrough <i>Kimono My House</i> album, followed by <i>Lil Beethoven</i>, both in their entirety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MusicOMH_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-MusicOMH-6">[6]</a></sup> Also in this period, the duo appeared in the video for <a href="/wiki/Justin_Hawkins" title="Justin Hawkins">Justin Hawkins</a>'s cover of "This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us", in which Ron and Russell play the referee and MC at a darts match between Hawkins and darts champion <a href="/wiki/Phil_Taylor_(darts_player)" title="Phil Taylor (darts player)">Phil Taylor</a>. This version of "This Town" reached No. 6 in the UK charts.</p>

<p>February 2006 saw the release of <i>Hello Young Lovers</i>, their twentieth studio album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman-18">[18]</a></sup> The album is regarded as carrying on where <i>Lil' Beethoven</i> left off, being described as "... cynical, intelligent and very, very funny",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2-20">[20]</a></sup> it has met with considerable acclaim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stylus_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus-22">[22]</a></sup> Sparks lead off the album with the striking tune that the BBC deemed too provocative in its title to play, "Dick Around." The song is a multi-section, multi-mood, highly layered track that many felt should have been a UK smash hit had the BBC not misinterpreted the title of the song as being other than it was.</p>
<p>The brothers tend to be dismissive of the latest trends in popular music, seeing most current bands as lacking musical ambition and experimental drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MusicOMH_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-MusicOMH-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-23">[23]</a></sup> Indeed, the predictable trends in much of modern rock, as they see it, served as inspiration for their latest album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-7">[7]</a></sup> However, they have expressed admiration for <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morrissey" title="Morrissey">Morrissey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MusicOMH_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-MusicOMH-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pair appeared in the season 6 finale of the US TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Gilmore_Girls" title="Gilmore Girls">Gilmore Girls</a></i>, performing "Perfume" from the album <i>Hello Young Lovers</i>. They released a live DVD of a September 2006 show at the London Forum as well as a long-awaited CD release of their previously unavailable 1977 album <i><a href="/wiki/Introducing_Sparks" title="Introducing Sparks">Introducing Sparks</a></i>. The track "Perfume" was featured in a <a href="/wiki/Dolce_%26_Gabbana" title="Dolce &amp; Gabbana">Dolce &amp; Gabbana</a> TV commercial in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DaG_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-DaG-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 12, 2008, Sparks released the single "Good Morning", taken from the album <i><a href="/wiki/Exotic_Creatures_of_the_Deep" title="Exotic Creatures of the Deep">Exotic Creatures of the Deep</a></i>.</p>
<p>May and June 2008 saw the 21-night "<a href="/wiki/Exotic_Creatures_of_the_Deep#Sparks_Spectacular" title="Exotic Creatures of the Deep">Sparks Spectacular</a>" in London, where they played each of their albums in chronological order during the first twenty nights, and premiered their new album on the twenty-first concert on June 13, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Each night, they performed an album in its entirety followed by a rare track – many of the songs have never been performed live before. The band asked their fans to visit their website and vote for the track that they'd most like to hear the band perform during the second half of the 21st concert after the premiere of <i>Exotic Creatures of the Deep</i>, though Russell Mael admitted that he and Ron would probably influence the poll a little.</p>
<p>Fans who bought a "Golden Ticket" (which allowed entry into all 21 gigs) also received a poster signed by the band and a CD single entitled <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Islington_N1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Islington N1 (page does not exist)">Islington N1</a>, a reference to the postal address of the venue for the first 20 gigs.</p>
<p>In 2009 the band played two consecutive nights at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Forum" title="London Forum">London Forum</a> on 20 and 21 March. They played Exotic Creatures of the Deep in its entirety at both gigs, followed by Kimono My House in its entirety on the first night and No. 1 in Heaven in its entirety on the second night.</p>
<p>Valentine's Day evening 2009 saw Sparks perform the same show featuring their Exotic Creatures of the Deep and Kimono My House albums played in their entirety before a sold-out hometown crowd at <a href="/wiki/Royce_Hall" title="Royce Hall">Royce Hall</a> at the Mael Bros. alma mater in Los Angeles, UCLA.</p>
<p>Ron and Russell Mael appear as interview subjects in the 2009 documentary <i>The Magnificent Tati</i>, discussing their involvement during the early 1980s in <i>Confusion</i>, a proposed <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Tati" title="Jacques Tati">Jacques Tati</a> movie for which a screenplay was written but never shot (due to Tati's death).</p>

<p>On August 14, 2009, the band premièred the radio musical <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seduction_of_Ingmar_Bergman" title="The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman">The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> commissioned by the Swedish public radio (SR) and featuring the Mael brothers themselves and Swedish actors <a href="/wiki/Elin_Klinga" title="Elin Klinga">Elin Klinga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Malmsj%C3%B6" title="Jonas Malmsjö">Jonas Malmsjö</a>, both of whom worked with Bergman in his lifetime. The musical, partly in English, partly in Swedish, tells the story of Bergman's relocation to Hollywood after his breakthrough with <i><a href="/wiki/Smiles_of_a_Summer_Night" title="Smiles of a Summer Night">Smiles of a Summer Night</a></i> (1956), and the surreal and discomforting encounter with the movie capital. On October 28, 2009, the UK's BBC 6 Music held a similar event in London whereby the musical was played in its entirety before a live audience at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Broadcasting_House" title="BBC Broadcasting House">BBC Broadcasting House</a> in London and later to be broadcast with a Q&amp;A with the Maels. In interviews for Swedish newspapers, the band said they hoped to be able to theatrically stage the musical as well as turn the musical into a feature film. Amongst the filmmakers cited as undertaking "The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman" with the Mael Brothers is Canadian avant-garde director <a href="/wiki/Guy_Maddin" title="Guy Maddin">Guy Maddin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 25, 2011 as part of the Los Angeles Film Festival, Sparks presented the World Premiere live performance of <i>The Seduction of Ingmar Bergman</i>. Canadian film director <a href="/wiki/Guy_Maddin" title="Guy Maddin">Guy Maddin</a> provided directions based on the screenplay, with Ron and Russell reprising their recorded roles on stage. The role of <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">Ingmar Bergman</a> was performed by Finnish movie actor <a href="/wiki/Peter_Franz%C3%A9n" title="Peter Franzén">Peter Franzén</a> and American actress <a href="/wiki/Ann_Magnuson" title="Ann Magnuson">Ann Magnuson</a> portrayed the role of Greta Garbo. The show was held at the <a href="/wiki/John_Anson_Ford_Amphitheatre" title="John Anson Ford Amphitheatre">John Anson Ford Amphitheatre</a> in Hollywood. The group showcased at the film festival in their quest for funding for the feature film version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmThreat_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FilmThreat-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Star-Ledger_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Star-Ledger-29">[29]</a></sup> The performance garnered glowing reviews from journals such as The Huffington Post, the LA Times, the LA Weekly, and LA Record.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sparks_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Sparks remixed <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Ono" title="Yoko Ono">Yoko Ono</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Give_Me_Something" title="Give Me Something">Give Me Something</a>". In July 2010 Sparks contributed a remix of sorts to singer <a href="/wiki/Katie_Melua" title="Katie Melua">Katie Melua</a>'s new single, <a href="/wiki/A_Happy_Place" title="A Happy Place">A Happy Place</a>, calling it <i>Sparks VS. Melua.</i> September 2, 2010 marked the debut of the new theme songs that Ron and Russell have composed and recorded for <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> radio's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bookworm" title="Bookworm">Bookworm</a> show, broadcast in Los Angeles on station <a href="/wiki/KCRW" title="KCRW">KCRW</a>. The two songs mark the first time in 21 years that the Bookworm show has used a new theme song, these being commissioned by show host and Sparks fan <a href="/wiki/Michael_Silverblatt" title="Michael Silverblatt">Michael Silverblatt</a>. The compositions are entitled, <i>"Where Would We Be Without Books?"</i> and <i>"I Am A Bookworm."</i></p>
<p>For the encore of what may have been the final live date ever in America for <a href="/wiki/Faith_No_More" title="Faith No More">Faith No More</a> on December 1, 2010 at the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Palladium" title="Hollywood Palladium">Hollywood Palladium</a> in Los Angeles, Ron and Russell Mael were invited by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Patton" title="Mike Patton">Mike Patton</a> and Co. to perform the Sparks' classic hit "<a href="/wiki/This_Town_Ain%27t_Big_Enough_for_Both_of_Us" title="This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us">This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us</a>." To a surprised and ecstatic sold-out crowd, the brothers reprised the song that also appears as a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Faith_No_More" title="Faith No More">Faith No More</a> on Sparks' 1997 album <i><a href="/wiki/Plagiarism_(album)" title="Plagiarism (album)">Plagiarism</a></i>. The original Sparks' version is from their 1974 LP, <i><a href="/wiki/Kimono_My_House" title="Kimono My House">Kimono My House</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2012 Ron and Russell collaborated with singer <a href="/wiki/Gemma_Ray" title="Gemma Ray">Gemma Ray</a> who released a limited 12-inch single titled "Gemma Ray Sings Sparks (with Sparks)," which included Ray's covers of Sparks' <i>"How Do I Get To Carnegie Hall"</i> and <i>"Eaten by the Monster of Love."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Though the songs were covers sung by Ray, they were arranged and produced by the Maels.</p>
<p>In October 2012, Ron and Russell performed for the first time ever as a duo, with no band. The 18-city European tour titled "Two Hands One Mouth" began in Lithuania and followed in Latvia, Finland, Norway, Sweden, Germany, Belgium, the UK and Ireland. The final UK concert of the tour was at the sold-out <a href="/wiki/Barbican_Centre" title="Barbican Centre">Barbican Centre</a> in London. The tour then took the group to Japan with concerts in Tokyo and Osaka in January 2013. In April 2013, the show was presented for the first time in the US with two performances at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coachella_Festival" title="Coachella Festival">Coachella Festival</a>. A short US tour with shows in New York City, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Portland, Seattle, and Philadelphia followed. In May 2013 Ron and Russell played the show in Paris, France, which coincided with their visit to the Cannes Film Festival. In Paris they were joined on stage by <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Ringer" title="Catherine Ringer">Catherine Ringer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Les_Rita_Mitsouko" title="Les Rita Mitsouko">Les Rita Mitsouko</a> to sing on their 1989 collaboration <i>"Singing in The Shower."</i></p>
<p>Sparks first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_album" title="Live album">live album</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_Hands,_One_Mouth:_Live_In_Europe" title="Two Hands, One Mouth: Live In Europe">Two Hands, One Mouth: Live In Europe</a></i> was released March 18, 2013. It contains concert recordings gathered from various cities during the October 2012 European tour.</p>
<p>In Fall 2013 Ron and Russell continued touring in the duo format for a second round. The tour was titled "The Revenge of Two Hands One Mouth." The two-and-a-half-month tour started with shows in the US and Canada. Sparks played in Los Angeles (<a href="/wiki/The_Fonda_Theatre" title="The Fonda Theatre">The Fonda Theatre</a>), New York City (<a href="/wiki/Webster_Hall" title="Webster Hall">Webster Hall</a>), <a href="/wiki/Fun_Fun_Fun_Fest" title="Fun Fun Fun Fest">Fun Fun Fun Fest</a> in Austin, Mountain Oasis Electronic Summit in Asheville, Atlanta, Boston, Washington D.C. (<a href="/wiki/9:30_Club" title="9:30 Club">9:30 Club</a>), Toronto, Montreal, Chicago, and Pontiac. The tour then continued to Europe, where they played three nights at Union Chapel in London (where <a href="/wiki/Thurston_Moore" title="Thurston Moore">Thurston Moore</a> (ex-<a href="/wiki/Sonic_Youth" title="Sonic Youth">Sonic Youth</a> joined them to play guitar on <i>"This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us"</i> on one of the nights), as well as additional shows in the UK, Switzerland, France, Spain, and Sweden.</p>
<p>Both "Two Hands One Mouth" and "The Revenge of Two Hands One Mouth" tours were critically well received.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2014 it was announced the Sparks (Ron and Russell) are working on material with <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(band)" title="Franz Ferdinand (band)">Franz Ferdinand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 17, Ron Mael was featured in an interview on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Today_(BBC_Radio_4)" title="Today (BBC Radio 4)">Today</a></i> discussing the band's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>2014 marked the 40th Anniversary of Sparks' most popular and most influential album, <i><a href="/wiki/Kimono_My_House" title="Kimono My House">Kimono My House</a></i>. To celebrate the event, the Mael brothers performed the album in its entirety at the <a href="/wiki/Barbican_Centre" title="Barbican Centre">Barbican Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> on 19 and 20 December. The album was performed with the 35-piece <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Orchestra" title="Heritage Orchestra">Heritage Orchestra</a>. The show included a second half consisting of songs from Sparks' entire career, that were also orchestrated for the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The first night sold out within the first few hours. On the second night, they announced that they had completed the recording of their new album. Ron and Russell took the <i><a href="/wiki/Kimono_My_House" title="Kimono My House">Kimono My House</a></i> celebrations to Los Angeles as they performed the album on two consecutive nights with a 38-piece orchestra at the United Artists Theatre at <a href="/wiki/Ace_Hotel_Los_Angeles" title="Ace Hotel Los Angeles">Ace Hotel Los Angeles</a> on Valentine's Day 14 February and on 15 February 2015. The orchestra was conducted by Suzie Katayama. Both shows sold out and they received glowing reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes.com_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes.com-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alex_Kapranos" title="Alex Kapranos">Alex Kapranos</a> of <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(band)" title="Franz Ferdinand (band)">Franz Ferdinand</a> joined them on stage for duet with Russell on the song <i>"When Do I Get To Sing 'My Way'"</i> on both nights and it was then announced that Sparks and FF had joined forces to form a new, then yet-to-be-revealed band and to record an album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes.com_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-latimes.com-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the fall of 2015 the title track from <i><a href="/wiki/Angst_in_My_Pants" title="Angst in My Pants">Angst in My Pants</a></i> was used in a commercial for men's underwear by retailer Tommy John. The ad was titled "The Big Adjustment" and depicted men, from various walks of life, attempting to secretly adjust their ill-fitting underwear to humorous effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 19th, it was announced on the official Facebook page that the band is working on a new album set to release in 2017, followed by a world tour. In a video posted on December 19th, the album's title was confirmed to be <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hippopotamus_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hippopotamus (album) (page does not exist)">Hippopotamus</a></i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="FFS">FFS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sparks_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: FFS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FFS_(band)" title="FFS (band)">FFS (band)</a></div>
<p>On March 9, 2015, it was announced that the band had formed a supergroup with <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(band)" title="Franz Ferdinand (band)">Franz Ferdinand</a> under the name <a href="/wiki/FFS_(band)" title="FFS (band)">FFS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-43">[43]</a></sup> A short teaser of the FFS song, "The Power Couple", was released on the group's <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The alternative meaning behind the bands name "Franz Ferdinand / Sparks" was confirmed with a clip of the musicians playing "Chinese Whispers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> It was also confirmed that the band would be releasing a studio album in 2015 with producer <a href="/wiki/John_Congleton" title="John Congleton">John Congleton</a> and that the band would be touring in the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork-43">[43]</a></sup> On April 1, it was announced that the group's new album would be titled <i><a href="/wiki/FFS_(album)" title="FFS (album)">FFS</a></i> and that it would be released on June 8 in the UK and on June 9 in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> They are featured on the Beck album <i>Song Reader</i> doing an ethereal rendition of his song "Why Did You Make Me Care." Recently, they performed at the <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Festival" title="Glastonbury Festival">Glastonbury Festival</a> John Peel Stage on 28 June 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sparks_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sparks' musical style varies dramatically over their forty-year career. In the beginning they attempted to emulate the sound of their English idols, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syd_Barrett" title="Syd Barrett">Syd Barrett</a>-era <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">the Kinks</a>, sometimes even pretending to be an English band while on the LA club circuit. They relocated to England during the <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rock</a> era where, despite cutting an odd figure on this scene, they found success with their polished brand of intricate pop tunes and convoluted lyrics. Early albums such as <i><a href="/wiki/Kimono_My_House" title="Kimono My House">Kimono My House</a></i> combined glam rock with elements of <a href="/wiki/Bubblegum_pop" title="Bubblegum pop">bubblegum pop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">baroque music</a>. By the second half of the decade, they were concerned that the sound they had developed while based in England was in danger of becoming stale; they returned to LA, determined to adopt a more "<a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a>" sound. This they achieved with producer Rupert Holmes on <i>Big Beat</i> and (sans Holmes) on <i>Introducing Sparks</i>.</p>
<p>The band were not satisfied with the results, which they felt lacked personality, perhaps because of the reliance on <a href="/wiki/Session_musician" title="Session musician">session musicians</a>. This led to the most dramatic change of style the band would attempt, when they teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Moroder" title="Giorgio Moroder">Giorgio Moroder</a>, dropped the rock-group format altogether and produced the <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> record <i>No. 1 in Heaven</i> which relied on synthesizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> This album is regarded as a landmark in the development of electronic music and greatly influenced bands which would emerge in the following years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAWeekly_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeekly-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> They soon returned to a more traditional line-up, which remained until 1988's <i>Interior Design</i>. There then followed a long hiatus until 1994's <i>Gratuitous Sax &amp; Senseless Violins</i>, which was a foray into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Techno_(music)" title="Techno (music)">techno</a> dance world, which they had helped to spawn back in the late 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MusicCom_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-MusicCom-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, the band switched back to a classically influenced rock/pop style with the release of their album <i><a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Beethoven" title="Lil' Beethoven">Lil' Beethoven</a></i>, replacing the beat-driven <a href="/wiki/Synthpop" title="Synthpop">synthpop</a> of their previous albums with complex orchestral arrangements. The band vocally acknowledged this change in style on the album's opening track "The Rhythm Thief".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lyrically, the band's style has been described as coming from "the school of <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a>, favouring caustic wit over trivial personal problems, ... achingly clever lyrics seesaw between superficial gloss, profound sentiment and the incomprehensibly bizarre."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAWeekly_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeekly-2">[2]</a></sup> Repeated lyrical motifs have become a distinct feature on recent albums. On "My Baby's Taking Me Home" off <i>Lil' Beethoven</i> (2002), the song title is repeated 104 times, with no other words being used, other than a spoken interlude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RecordCollector_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-RecordCollector-4">[4]</a></sup> Similarly, on the same album, "Your Call Is Very Important To Us", uses a corporation style call-hold message: "Your call is very important to us. Please hold" which is then sung with some additional words: "At first she said your call is very important to us, then she said please, please hold." The only other lyrics in the song are "Red light", "Green light", "I'm Getting Mixed Signals" and "Sorry, I'm Going To Have To Put You Back On Hold". These elements are layered with a simple piano line to create a highly textured effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stylus_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stylus-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The vocal sound on the single "<a href="/wiki/This_Town_Ain%27t_Big_Enough_for_Both_of_Us" title="This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us">This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us</a>" was criticised as being "stylised". This may be because the song was written without any regard for the vocal style of <a href="/wiki/Russell_Mael" title="Russell Mael">Russell Mael</a>. Songwriter <a href="/wiki/Ron_Mael" title="Ron Mael">Ron Mael</a> has explained:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"This Town Ain't Big Enough For Both of Us" was written in A, and by God it'll be sung in A. I just feel that if you're coming up with most of the music, then you have an idea where it's going to go. And no singer is gonna get in my way.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Russell Mael has claimed in reply:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When he wrote "This Town Ain't Big Enough For Both of Us", Ron could only play it in that key. It was so much work to transpose the song and one of us had to budge, so I made the adjustment to fit in. My voice ain't a "rock" voice. It's not soulful, in the traditional rock way; It's not about "guts". It's untrained, unschooled, I never questioned why I was singing high. It just happened, dictated by the songs. Ron has always written Sparks' lyrics and never transposed them into a rock key for me to sing. He always packed each line with words and I had to sing them as they were.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
