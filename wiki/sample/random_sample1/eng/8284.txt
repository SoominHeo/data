<p><b>Spartanburg</b> is the largest city in and the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_County,_South_Carolina" title="Spartanburg County, South Carolina">Spartanburg County</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR62_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR62-3">[3]</a></sup> and the twelfth largest city by population in the state. Spartanburg has a municipal population of 37,013 and an urban population of 180,786 as of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_20102_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census_20102-4">[4]</a></sup> The Spartanburg Metropolitan Statistical Area, corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_County,_South_Carolina" title="Spartanburg County, South Carolina">Spartanburg County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Union_County,_South_Carolina" title="Union County, South Carolina">Union County</a>, had a population of 317,057 as of the 2010-2014 American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spartanburg is the second-largest city in the greater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenville-Spartanburg-Anderson_Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Greenville-Spartanburg-Anderson Combined Statistical Area">Greenville–Spartanburg–Anderson</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Combined Statistical Area">Combined Statistical Area</a> which has a population of 1,385,045 as of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is part of a 10-county region of northwestern South Carolina known as "<a href="/wiki/Upstate_South_Carolina" title="Upstate South Carolina">The Upstate</a>," and is located 98 miles (158 km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_South_Carolina" title="Columbia, South Carolina">Columbia</a>, 80 miles (130 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, and about 190 miles (310 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spartanburg was formed in 1785 and was named after a local militia called the Spartan Regiment in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. The Spartan Regiment, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Pickens_(congressman)" title="Andrew Pickens (congressman)">Andrew Pickens</a>, participated in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowpens" title="Battle of Cowpens">Battle of Cowpens</a>. In 1831, Spartanburg was incorporated, later becoming known as the "Hub City" due to the railroad lines forming wheel hub shapes in the area. Around 40 textile mills were established in the late 1800s and early 1900s. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> Camp Wadsworth was used to train 100,000 soldiers for the war. Camp Croft also trained soldiers during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and is now called <a href="/wiki/Croft_State_Park" title="Croft State Park">Croft State Park</a>. By the 1950s, mills began to decline as wages and the automobile industry increased. <a href="/wiki/BMW_US_Manufacturing_Company" title="BMW US Manufacturing Company">BMW US Manufacturing Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW's</a> only North American manufacturing plant, was established in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 19.2 square miles (50 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 19.1 square miles (49 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 0.1 square miles (0.26 km<sup>2</sup>), or 0.47%, is water.</p>
<p>The city of Spartanburg has a humid subtropical climate with long, hot and humid summers, and cool to semi mild winters. The average annual temperature is 61.6 °F (16.4 °C). In the summer season from June through September, average highs are in the 80's to low 90's F, while in the winter months average highs are in the mid 50's F. Annual rainfall is spread fairly evenly throughout the whole year. Spartanburg sees very little snowfall, with the annual average being only 1.4 inches. Average precipitation is 51.3 inches (130 cm) and the average growing season is 231 days.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Points_of_interest">Points of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Points of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Lawson’s Fork Creek</b>, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Pacolet_River" title="Pacolet River">Pacolet River</a>, was once known for its plentiful wildlife and crystal clear waters. Parks and woodlands line much of its banks (which lie entirely within Spartanburg County), and rocky shoals and natural waterfalls can be found throughout its course. It stretches from the northern end of the county to the eastern end, where it empties into the Pacolet.</li>
<li>The <b>Cottonwood Trail</b> is a walking trail located in the Edwin M. Griffin Nature Preserve that runs along part of Lawson’s Fork Creek. The trail includes picnic areas, a raised path over an extensive wetlands area and access to sporadic sandbars. Located just east of downtown, it is used frequently by cyclists, joggers and walkers. Since the Lawson's Fork floodplain is not suitable for development, wildlife populate the area. Larger animals that can be found here include <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_deer" title="White-tailed deer">white-tailed deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_turkey" title="Wild turkey">wild turkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pileated_woodpecker" title="Pileated woodpecker">pileated woodpeckers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mallard_ducks" title="Mallard ducks">mallard ducks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_geese" title="Canadian geese">Canadian geese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_snapping_turtle" title="Common snapping turtle">snapping turtles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hatcher_Garden_and_Woodland_Preserve" title="Hatcher Garden and Woodland Preserve">Hatcher Garden and Woodland Preserve</a></b>, located in the midst of an urban environment, is a welcome oasis of natural beauty. Retired social activist Harold Hatcher and his wife Josephine transformed an eroding gully into a thick woods and flower garden which now provides a haven for birds and other wildlife.</li>
</ul>
<p>Early European settlers to this area included French fur trappers, English woodsmen, and Scots-Irish farmers. Few remnants survive from these early pioneering days, but traces can be found in the more rural areas of the county.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Walnut_Grove_Plantation" title="Walnut Grove Plantation">Walnut Grove Plantation</a></b>, an 18th-century farmhouse, has been preserved by The Spartanburg County Historical Association. The site of a locally famous skirmish during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, it was the home of the Moore family. The plantation lies south of Spartanburg near the town of <a href="/wiki/Roebuck,_South_Carolina" title="Roebuck, South Carolina">Roebuck</a>, and is open to the public for tours and during annual festivals.</li>
<li><b>The Seay House</b>, another 18th-century home, is a more typical representative of a pioneer home. Its single stone fireplace and simple construction were common traits of farmsteads from this period.</li>
<li><b>The Price House</b>, the third 18th-century home maintained by the Historical Association, is unique. Its sturdy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_bond" title="Flemish bond">Flemish-bond</a> brick construction and three stories are less common in this area. By carefully examining the original inventory lists of the house, the Historical Association has been able to retrieve period pieces that approximate the original contents of the house.</li>
</ul>
<p>First established in the 1780s as a courthouse village, Spartanburg may have been named for the Spartan regiment of the South Carolina militia. The city was incorporated in 1831, at the time of the 50th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowpens" title="Battle of Cowpens">Battle of Cowpens</a>, a pivotal fight of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> that took place only a few miles away. The city’s streets and architectural record reflect the changes of the 19th and 20th centuries.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Morgan Square</b>, the city’s primary downtown hub, is the original courthouse village. It was founded adjacent to a small spring (now underground) on the western slope of a ridge, which forms the border of the <a href="/wiki/Tyger_River" title="Tyger River">Tyger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacolet_River" title="Pacolet River">Pacolet River</a> watersheds. The square's name derives from <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Morgan" title="Daniel Morgan">Daniel Morgan</a>, the general who commanded the American forces at Cowpens. A statue of Morgan was placed in the square in 1881. The oldest existing buildings on Morgan Square date to the 1880s.</li>
<li>The <b>Magnolia Street Train Depot</b> is one of the older buildings in Spartanburg and stands as a reminder of Spartanburg’s old nickname "the Hub City," referring to the many transportation routes that connected Spartanburg with cities throughout the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is now the home of the <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> station, the Hub City Railroad Museum, and the Hub City Farmers' Market.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hampton_Heights" title="Hampton Heights">Hampton Heights Historic District</a></b> is the city's oldest intact downtown neighborhood, located a couple of blocks south of Morgan Square. Architectural styles in this neighborhood range from large <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne_style_architecture" title="Queen Anne style architecture">Queen Anne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical</a> homes to cozy early 20th-century bungalows.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cotton_mill" title="Cotton mill">Cotton mills</a> have abounded in the Spartanburg area since 1816, earning Spartanburg the reputation as the "<a href="/wiki/Lowell,_Massachusetts" title="Lowell, Massachusetts">Lowell</a> of the South." Although there were few mills in the area before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, technological advances, northern capital, and out-migration from the poor farms that made white labor available, created a wave of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction Era of the United States">postbellum</a> mill development here and in much of the Piedmont <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_USA" title="Southern USA">South</a>. Additionally, the abundant streams and rivers in the area are just beginning their descent towards the lower-lying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midlands_(South_Carolina)" title="Midlands (South Carolina)">Midlands</a> region. In many places, these waterways descend abruptly, providing a source for plentiful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterpower" title="Waterpower">waterpower</a>. Cotton mills were built along these rivers to harness this power, and so began the region’s servitude to <a href="/wiki/King_Cotton" title="King Cotton">King Cotton</a>. These mills, their owners and their laborers dominated the politics and economy of the region for nearly a century. Although nearly all abandoned, many mills remain along the riverbanks, the Piedmont equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> ruins.</p>

<ul>
<li><b>Glendale Mill</b> is located off Lawson’s Fork Creek southeast of the city. Although gutted by fire in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> a few towers and smokestacks remain, providing a dramatic backdrop to the dam, shoals and waterfalls of the creek below. The former company store now serves as the home of the <a href="/wiki/Wofford_College" title="Wofford College">Wofford College</a> Environmental Studies Center. The Glendale Shoals bridge will be getting a $600,00 makeover, and will ultimately connect to the state's <a href="/wiki/Palmetto_Trail" title="Palmetto Trail">Palmetto Trail</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Beaumont Mill</b>,north of the downtown, has recently been purchased by Spartanburg Regional Healthcare who moved their billing, human resources, and medical records to the 180,000 square foot mill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The adjacent mill village has been designated as a local historic district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Converse Mill</b> is located east of the city along the Pacolet River. It has recently been purchased by a developer. The mill was reconstructed in 1903 after a huge flood washed away the original mill. The dam is still in use by Converse Energy Inc as a hydroelectric plant.</li>
</ul>
<p>When the United States entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in 1917, one of the sixteen divisional cantonments for the training of National Guard troops was Camp Wadsworth, which is located in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Westgate_Mall_(Spartanburg)" title="Westgate Mall (Spartanburg)">Westgate Mall</a>. Large numbers of New York National Guardsmen trained there in addition to many southern troops. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Camp Croft south of the city trained Army recruits. This is now a South Carolina state park with the same name. Some portions of the park contain the original <a href="/wiki/Quonset_hut" title="Quonset hut">quonset huts</a> (1/2 metal tube structures).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current mayor, Junie White, was elected in 2009. Spartanburg operates under a <a href="/wiki/City_manager" title="City manager">city manager</a> form of government in which the mayor and six city council members have equal votes. Council members represent districts within the city and the mayor is elected at large. The council appoints a city manager, who is responsible for the daily administration of city governmental affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> City Hall is located at 145 West Broad Street.</p>
<p>The Spartanburg County Administration Building (this is the old Sears building which was vacated in the mid-1970s when Sears moved to <a href="/wiki/Westgate_Mall_(Spartanburg)" title="Westgate Mall (Spartanburg)">Westgate Mall</a> and renovated in the late 1980s or early 1990s) is located at 366 North Church Street. It is across the street from the <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Memorial_Auditorium" title="Spartanburg Memorial Auditorium">Spartanburg Memorial Auditorium</a>.</p>

<p>Within the past decade, developers and community leaders have spearheaded an effort to revitalize Spartanburg's downtown commercial district. This has resulted in a remodeling of Morgan Square, the restoration of a number of historic structures and the relocation of several businesses and company headquarters to the downtown vicinity. Some of these new developments include a nine-floor, 240-room Marriott, the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Advance_America_Cash_Advance" title="Advance America Cash Advance">Advance America</a>, and the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/QS/1_Data_Systems" title="QS/1 Data Systems">QS/1 Data Systems</a>. The world headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Denny%27s" title="Denny's">Denny's</a> restaurants is also located in downtown Spartanburg. Numerous other smaller businesses such as <a href="/wiki/RJ_Rockers_Brewing_Company" title="RJ Rockers Brewing Company">RJ Rockers Brewing Company</a> have also moved downtown as a result of this community-wide effort.</p>
<p>The economy of Spartanburg benefits from the <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_Manufacturing_Co._Spartanburg" title="BMW Manufacturing Co. Spartanburg">manufacturing facility</a> located in the western end of Spartanburg County. Manufacturing began in 1996 with certain types of the <a href="/wiki/BMW_3_Series" title="BMW 3 Series">3 Series</a> (from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_E36" title="BMW E36">E36</a> platform) and with the <a href="/wiki/BMW_Z3" title="BMW Z3">Z3</a> roadster. However, a year later when the newer 3 Series (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_E46" title="BMW E46">E46</a>) platform emerged, BMW decided not to build it at the Spartanburg plant, but instead exclusively manufacture variants of the popular Z3. The plant currently manufactures the <a href="/wiki/BMW_X5_(F15)" title="BMW X5 (F15)">X5</a> SAV and <a href="/wiki/BMW_X6" title="BMW X6">X6</a> SAC for the world market. As part of an expansion project announced in March 2008, the plant will add about 1,200,000 square feet (110,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of assembly space, and it will become the home of the next-generation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_X3_(F25)" title="BMW X3 (F25)">X3</a> SAV.</p>
<p>Spartanburg is also home to the world headquarters and research facility for <a href="/wiki/Milliken_%26_Company" title="Milliken &amp; Company">Milliken &amp; Company</a>, a textile and chemical manufacturer.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colleges">Colleges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Colleges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spartanburg is a college town, with eight institutions of higher learning:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Carolina_Upstate" title="University of South Carolina Upstate">University of South Carolina Upstate</a> (formerly known as University of South Carolina Spartanburg, or USCS).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Converse_College" title="Converse College">Converse College</a> – Founded in 1889, Converse is a comprehensive masters institution with a co-ed graduate school and an undergraduate women's liberal arts college.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sherman_College_of_Chiropractic" title="Sherman College of Chiropractic">Sherman College of Chiropractic</a> – South Carolina's only chiropractic college</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Community_College" title="Spartanburg Community College">Spartanburg Community College</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Methodist_College" title="Spartanburg Methodist College">Spartanburg Methodist College</a> – The only 2-year, private, residential college in the state.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_College_of_Osteopathic_Medicine" title="Virginia College of Osteopathic Medicine">Edward Via College of Osteopathic Medicine (VCOM)</a> – Carolinas Campus. First classes began in Fall 2011.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Virginia_College" title="Virginia College">Virginia College</a> – Private career college opened in January 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wofford_College" title="Wofford College">Wofford College</a> – Founded in 1854, Wofford is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">Phi Beta Kappa</a> liberal arts college with an enrollment of approximately 1,500 students.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_and_private_schools">Public and private schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Public and private schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the City of Spartanburg's public schools are run by <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_County_School_District_7" title="Spartanburg County School District 7">Spartanburg County School District 7</a>, one of seven loosely affiliated districts located in <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_County,_South_Carolina#Education" title="Spartanburg County, South Carolina">Spartanburg County</a>. However, the westernmost part of the city is served by Spartanburg County School District 6, which has two elementary schools within city limits. Spartanburg is home to the main campus of the South Carolina School for the Deaf and Blind. It has five Regional Outreach Centers throughout the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The city is also home to Spartanburg Preparatory School, a K-8 public charter school that is the only "brick and mortar" charter school in the Upstate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spartanburg is also home to Spartanburg Christian Academy, a K-12 private school in North Spartanburg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Day_School" title="Spartanburg Day School">Spartanburg Day School</a>, a K-12 private school offering the International Baccalaureate in grades K-4, and to Oakbrook Preparatory and Westgate Christian schools, both K-12 private schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Located in <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Heights" title="Hampton Heights">Hampton Heights</a>, the Montessori Academy of Spartanburg is a PreK-8 private school providing a <a href="/wiki/Montessori_education" title="Montessori education">Montessori</a> educational approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Meeting Street Academy in downtown Spartanburg is a branch of a <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston</a>-based private school and currently offers PreK and Kindergarten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>St. Paul the Apostle Catholic School is located in downtown Spartanburg. It is affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Charleston" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Charleston">Diocese of Charleston</a> and is K-8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spartanburg County’s healthcare is mainly provided by the Spartanburg Regional Healthcare System. <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Regional" title="Spartanburg Regional">Spartanburg Regional</a> is a public, not-for-profit, integrated health care delivery system based in the Spartanburg Regional Medical Center, a 588-bed teaching and research hospital. SRHS provides healthcare services to a five-county area in North and South Carolina, serving an Upstate medical population of more than 300,000 people.</p>
<p>In 1925, Dr. Hugh Ratchford Black opened a 35-bed facility named in honor of his wife, Mary Black. The current Skylyn Drive facility opened in 1968, and today, the campus features a 353,690-square-foot (32,859 m<sup>2</sup>) modern medical facility. The medical staff consists of more than 350 physicians representing all specialties. Mary Black Physician Group has over 100 employed physicians in more than 30 locations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The Hub City Farmers' Market, an outdoor market held Saturday mornings from 6:00am – 12:00pm during the summer and fall on the grounds of the historic Magnolia Street Train Station, showcasing local (often organic) produce and goods.</li>
<li>Retrofest, the Southeast's largest disco festival held at <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Memorial_Auditorium" title="Spartanburg Memorial Auditorium">Spartanburg Memorial Auditorium</a> on the first Saturday in February.</li>
<li>Spring Fling, a weekend festival typically held the first Saturday of May, has many live artists, rides, and other assorted attractions.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/SouthEast_LinuxFest" title="SouthEast LinuxFest">SouthEast LinuxFest</a> – annual, well attended Linux and open source software conference for the southeast – held in Spartanburg in 2010 and 2011.</li>
<li>Red, White and Boom, a Fourth of July event held at Barnet Park featuring patriotic music and a fireworks display.</li>
<li>The Annual Sidewalk Arts Show, an open, juried art exhibition held concurrently with The International Festival on the first Saturday in October.</li>
<li>The International Festival, an event showcasing culture and cuisine from countries around the globe held at Barnet Park on the first Saturday in October.</li>
<li>Music on Main, a street concert event held every Thursday (April through August) downtown.</li>
<li>The Greek Festival, a major street festival that is held in September by the local Greek community at St. Nicholas Greek Orthodox Church. It offers Greek food and cultural activities, and is the sister festival to the Greek Festival held every spring in <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_South_Carolina" title="Greenville, South Carolina">Greenville</a>.</li>
<li>Dickens of a Christmas, a Victorian holiday event held annually in downtown Spartanburg on the Tuesday after Thanksgiving.</li>
<li>Festifall, an historical celebration held on the grounds of the 18th-century Walnut Grove Plantation in October, featuring demonstrations and reenactments.</li>
<li>Taste of the Backcountry, a historical celebration held on the grounds of the 18th-century Price House in April, featuring food samples and demonstrations.</li>
<li>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.hubcityhogfest.com/" rel="nofollow">Hub City Hog Fest</a> is an annual barbecue festival and competition that benefits Mobile Meals. It is held in the heart of downtown Spartanburg with food trucks, over 40 BBQ teams, and two days of live music.</li>
</ul>

<p>Historic <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Park" title="Duncan Park">Duncan Park Stadium</a> was once home to the Spartanburg Stingers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Coastal_Plain_League" title="Coastal Plain League">Coastal Plain League</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and the Spartanburg Crickets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Collegiate_Baseball_League" title="Southern Collegiate Baseball League">Southern Collegiate Baseball League</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and is the oldest minor league baseball stadium in the country. It was also once home to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Phillies" title="Spartanburg Phillies">Spartanburg Phillies</a>, a minor league team of the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a>. It now is the home stadium for the baseball teams of <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_High_School" title="Spartanburg High School">Spartanburg High School</a>.</p>
<p>The Shrine Bowl of the Carolinas is held each year at Wofford's Gibbs Stadium. It is a high school football all-star game played between the top players from South Carolina and the top players from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/BMW_Charity_Pro-Am" title="BMW Charity Pro-Am">BMW Charity Pro-Am</a> is a golf tournament on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationwide_Tour" title="Nationwide Tour">Nationwide Tour</a> held each year in May at three courses in the Upstate, including the Carolina Country Club in Spartanburg.</p>
<p>The USC Upstate Spartans, Spartanburg Methodist College Pioneers, and the <a href="/wiki/Wofford_Terriers" title="Wofford Terriers">Wofford College Terriers</a> offer a variety of sports for both men and women. <a href="/wiki/Converse_College" title="Converse College">Converse College</a> also offers NCAA Division II women's sports teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Conference" title="Southern Conference">Southern Conference</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA">NCAA</a> is headquartered in Spartanburg.</p>
<p>Spartanburg is located an hour away from the <a href="/wiki/Clemson_University" title="Clemson University">Clemson</a> <a href="/wiki/Clemson_Tigers" title="Clemson Tigers">Tigers</a> and about an hour and a half away from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Carolina" title="University of South Carolina">University of South Carolina</a> <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Gamecocks" title="South Carolina Gamecocks">Gamecocks</a>.</p>
<p>The Spartanburg Parks Commission hosts several travel baseball and softball tournaments each year, helping brand the city as one of the Southeast's most reputable tournament locations. Tyger River Park, a 13-field mega baseball/softball complex, opened in 2012.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Upward_Sports" title="Upward Sports">Upward Sports</a>, a Christian-based sports organization for kids, is headquartered in Spartanburg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Spartanburg has, throughout its history, been a fruitful home to a creative community. Cultural events and institutions abound in the city and county and consistently draw large crowds.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <b>Chapman Cultural Center</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Spartanburg's cultural anchor for history, art, theatre, dance, music, and science, is located in a three-building complex on the northern edge of downtown. Opened in October 2007, the Center was designed by <a href="/wiki/David_M._Schwarz" title="David M. Schwarz">David M. Schwarz</a> Architects of Washington, D.C. It houses the Spartanburg Art Museum, Spartanburg County Regional History Museum, Science Center, Little Theatre, Ballet, Music Foundation, and other groups that were formerly located in The Arts Center<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> on South Spring Street. It is owned and operated by The Arts Partnership of Greater Spartanburg, a non-profit organization dedicated to supporting arts and cultural agencies in Spartanburg County.</li>
<li><b>The Showroom</b>, the home of Hub-Bub,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> is a new center for progressive arts in the community. It houses a gallery, film screen, stage, and concert venue and is home to a nationally recognized artist residency program. Hub-Bub is the creative effort responsible for the founding of The Showroom and, as a project goal, seeks to attract and retain creative talent in the community. It was launched in March 2005 by the <a href="/wiki/Hub_City_Writers_Project" title="Hub City Writers Project">Hub City Writers Project</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> with assistance from private donors and the city of Spartanburg. The Hub City Writers Project, located a block away, serves the community as a local publishing company and independent bookstore. The Showroom also housed the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TEDx" title="TEDx">TEDx</a> Spartanburg event on September 10, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Converse_College" title="Converse College">Converse College</a></b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> is a nationally known four-year liberal arts institution recognized for its strong music and visual art programs. It hosts events open to the community throughout the year. Twitchell Auditorium is located on the campus of Converse College. Home of the Greater Spartanburg Philharmonic Orchestra, Twitchell Auditorium has served as hosts to other groups such as the Spartanburg All-County High School Band and Boston Brass. Twitchell Auditorium was built in 1899 and renovated for the school's centennial celebration in 1989. Famous for its acoustics, the 1500-seat auditorium is home to a 57-rank <a href="/wiki/Casavant_Fr%C3%A8res" title="Casavant Frères">Casavant</a> organ with 2,600+ pipes. Theatre Converse puts on several plays a year, and Converse puts on an opera annually, as well as opera scenes. The college has had major concerts in recent years with such artists as <a href="/wiki/Caedmon%27s_Call" title="Caedmon's Call">Caedmon's Call</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Mraz" title="Jason Mraz">Jason Mraz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corey_Smith_(musician)" title="Corey Smith (musician)">Corey Smith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colbie_Caillat" title="Colbie Caillat">Colbie Caillat</a>.</li>
<li><b>Wofford College</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> is a prestigious liberal arts college. Not traditionally known for its arts and cultural strength, Wofford has made notable strides to develop arts programs in recent years. Poet and environmental writer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_E._Lane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John E. Lane (page does not exist)">John E. Lane</a>, theater artist Mark Ferguson, and visual artist <a href="/wiki/Kris_Neely" title="Kris Neely">Kris Neely</a> are all graduates of Wofford. Each returned to their alma mater to develop curricular and co-curricular opportunities in the Arts, including a Creative Writing program, a Theatre Major, and a minor in Studio Art. John Lane was also a critical visionary in the creation of the Goodall Environmental Studies Center in Glendale, SC.</li>
<li>The <b>Spartanburg County Public Library</b> headquarters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> housed in an innovative building on South Church Street, is home to a voluminous collection of fiction, nonfiction, children’s literature, A/V materials and items relating to local history and genealogy. The library hosts many meetings, concerts and presentations.</li>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Memorial_Auditorium" title="Spartanburg Memorial Auditorium">Spartanburg Memorial Auditorium</a></b> is located on N. Church Street, across from the municipal building in the northwest end of the city of Spartanburg. The "SMA" has hosted various famous acts such as <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crosby_Stills_and_Nash" title="Crosby Stills and Nash">Crosby Stills and Nash</a>, <a href="/wiki/B.B._King" title="B.B. King">B.B. King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Joel" title="Billy Joel">Billy Joel</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Copperfield_(illusionist)" title="David Copperfield (illusionist)">David Copperfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Grizzard" title="Lewis Grizzard">Lewis Grizzard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_Connick,_Jr." title="Harry Connick, Jr.">Harry Connick, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Levert" title="Gerald Levert">Gerald Levert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle" title="Dave Chappelle">Dave Chappelle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Seinfeld" title="Jerry Seinfeld">Jerry Seinfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phish" title="Phish">Phish</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Prairie_Home_Companion" title="A Prairie Home Companion">A Prairie Home Companion</a></i>, and many others. Originally built in an <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> style and was renovated c. 2002 including a new facade and backstage with loading area.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Families_Advocating_for_Campus_Equality" title="Families Advocating for Campus Equality">Families Advocating for Campus Equality</a>, an organization that fights accusations of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_misconduct" title="Sexual misconduct">sexual misconduct</a> on college campuses<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> is based in Spartanburg.<a class="external free" href="http://www.facecampusequality.org/contact.html" rel="nofollow">http://www.facecampusequality.org/contact.html</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Spartanburg's primary newspaper is the <i><a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Herald-Journal" title="Spartanburg Herald-Journal">Spartanburg Herald-Journal</a></i>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Media_Group" title="Halifax Media Group">Halifax Media Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>The Spartanburg Journal</i> is a weekly newspaper that is part of a Community Journal group that also has editions in <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_South_Carolina" title="Greenville, South Carolina">Greenville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anderson,_South_Carolina" title="Anderson, South Carolina">Anderson</a>.</li>
<li>The <i>Spartan Weekly News</i> is a weekly newspaper with offices located in downtown Spartanburg. The paper covers all of Spartanburg County with an emphasis on the city of Spartanburg, and its coverage focuses on items of community interest and well as news from around the upstate of South Carolina.</li>
<li>The award-winning Hometown News Group has several newspapers throughout the county and upstate region: <i>The Boiling Springs Sentry</i>, <i>The Blacksburg Times</i>, <i>The Inman Times</i>, <i>Spartanburg County News</i>, <i>The Chesnee Tribune</i>, <i>The Middle Tyger Times</i>, <i>The Whitmire News</i>, <i>The Woodruff News</i>, and <i>The Greer Citizen</i>. Aside from local news and sports coverage, the newspapers offer free wedding and social announcements. All of these papers, save "The Greer Citizen" ceased publication at the end of 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Upstate Link</i> magazine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> is a young reader (20s–30s) newsweekly in the Upstate of South Carolina, which includes Greenville, Spartanburg and Anderson. The weekly publication began in January 2004. <i>Link</i> continues to be a print publication, but its website ceased operation in 2008. Its new website is run by Chicago-based <a href="/wiki/Metromix" title="Metromix">Metromix</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Spartanburg is served by the Spartanburg Area Regional Transit Agency (SPARTA), covering the city of Spartanburg and the surrounding urbanized area with 8 routes leading to a wide variety of destinations. All SPARTA buses are equipped with bicycle racks. Two hybrid-electric buses were added to the fleet in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The SPARTA Passenger Center is located at 100 North Liberty Street and also serves <a href="/wiki/Greyhound_Lines" title="Greyhound Lines">Greyhound</a> buses.</p>
<p>Mass Transit is provided to all citizens of Spartanburg County through Spartanburg County Dial-A-Ride. It is a door to door service that operates six days a week.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airports">Airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenville-Spartanburg_International_Airport" title="Greenville-Spartanburg International Airport">Greenville-Spartanburg International Airport</a> (GSP) lies mostly in Spartanburg County in suburban <a href="/wiki/Greer,_South_Carolina" title="Greer, South Carolina">Greer</a>, and it serves <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_South_Carolina" title="Greenville, South Carolina">Greenville</a> as well as Spartanburg. It has become one of the busiest airports in South Carolina.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg_Downtown_Memorial_Airport" title="Spartanburg Downtown Memorial Airport">Spartanburg Downtown Memorial Airport</a> (SPA) is a general aviation airport owned and operated by the City, which lies southwest of town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railroad_station">Railroad station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spartanburg,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Railroad station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Crescent_(train)" title="Crescent (train)"><i>Crescent</i> train</a> connects Spartanburg with the cities of New York, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spartanburg_(Amtrak_station)" title="Spartanburg (Amtrak station)">Amtrak station</a> is situated at 290 Magnolia Street.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> of 2000, there were 39,673 people, 15,989 households, and 9,721 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 2,066.3 people per square mile (799.9/km²). There were 17,696 housing units at an average density of 923.9 per square mile (356.8/km²). The racial makeup was 49.55% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 47.15% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 0.18% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 1.33% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.06% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.76% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.96% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 1.78% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 15,989 households out of which 28.9% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 34.0% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 23.0% had a female householder with no husband present, and 39.2% were non-families. 34.0% of all households were made up of individuals and 13.2% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.33 and the average family size was 3.00.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 25.2% under the age of 18, 12.2% from 18 to 24, 26.6% from 25 to 44, 20.6% from 45 to 64, and 15.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 35 years. For every 100 females there were 79.6 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $28,735, and the median income for a family was $36,108. Males had a median income of $30,587 versus $23,256 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $18,136. About 19.4% of families and 23.3% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 34.6% of those under age 18 and 15.4% of those age 65 or over.</p>
