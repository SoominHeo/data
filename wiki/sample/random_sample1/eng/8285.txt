<p><b>Spam</b> (stylized <b>SPAM</b>) is a brand of <a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">canned</a> precooked meat made by <a href="/wiki/Hormel" title="Hormel">Hormel Foods Corporation</a>. It was first introduced in 1937 and gained popularity worldwide after its use during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeriousSpam_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SeriousSpam-1">[1]</a></sup> By 2003, Spam was sold in 41 countries on six continents and trademarked in over 100 countries (except in <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hormelgoddess_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hormelgoddess-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2007, the seven billionth can of Spam was sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to its label, Spam's basic ingredients are <a href="/wiki/Boston_butt" title="Boston butt">pork shoulder</a> meat, with <a href="/wiki/Ham" title="Ham">ham</a> meat added, salt, water, <a href="/wiki/Modified_starch" title="Modified starch">modified</a> <a href="/wiki/Potato_starch" title="Potato starch">potato starch</a> as a binder, sugar, and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">sodium nitrite</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservative</a>. Natural <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a> forms during cooking in its <a href="/wiki/Tin_can" title="Tin can">tins</a> on the production line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many have raised concerns over Spam's nutritional attributes, in large part due to its high content of fat, sodium, and preservatives.</p>
<p>By the early 1970s the name "Spam" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">was often misused to describe any canned meat product</a> containing pork, such as pork <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luncheon_meat" title="Luncheon meat">luncheon meat</a>. With expansion in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications_technology" title="Communications technology">communications technology</a>, it became the subject of urban legends about <a href="/wiki/Mystery_meat" title="Mystery meat">mystery meat</a> and other appearances in pop culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Most notable was a <a href="/wiki/Spam_(Monty_Python)" title="Spam (Monty Python)">Monty Python sketch</a> portraying Spam as tasting horrible, ubiquitous and inescapable, characteristics which led to its name being borrowed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" title="Spam (electronic)">unsolicited electronic messages</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(email)" title="Spam (email)">spam email</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tools.ietf.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-tools.ietf.org-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spam was introduced by Hormel in 1937. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ken_Daigneau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ken Daigneau (page does not exist)">Ken Daigneau</a>, brother of a company executive, won a $100 prize that year in a competition to name the new item.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> Hormel claims that the meaning of the name "is known by only a small circle of former Hormel Foods executives", but popular beliefs are that the name is an abbreviation of "spiced ham", "spare meat", or "shoulders of pork and ham".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Another popular explanation is that Spam is an acronym standing for "Specially Processed American Meat" or "Specially Processed Army Meat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The difficulty of delivering fresh meat to the front during World War II saw Spam become a ubiquitous part of the U.S. soldier's diet. It became variously referred to as "ham that didn't pass its physical", "meatloaf without basic training",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeriousSpam_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SeriousSpam-1">[1]</a></sup> and "Special Army Meat". Over 150 million pounds of Spam were purchased by the military before the war's end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxfordSpam_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordSpam-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War II and the occupations which followed, Spam was introduced into Guam, Hawaii, Okinawa, the Philippines, and other islands in the Pacific. Immediately absorbed into native diets, it has become a unique part of the history and effects of U.S. influence in the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BurgerKingSpam_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BurgerKingSpam-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a consequence of World War II rationing and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lend-Lease_Act" title="Lend-Lease Act">Lend-Lease Act</a>, Spam also gained prominence in the United Kingdom. British prime minister during the 1980s <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> later referred to it as a "wartime delicacy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr.org_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr.org-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition to increasing production for the U.K., Hormel expanded Spam output as part of Allied aid to the similarly beleaguered Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> declared: "Without Spam we wouldn't have been able to feed our army".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Throughout the war, countries ravaged by the conflict and faced with strict food rations came to appreciate Spam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_and_territories">United States and territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States and territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Domestically, Spam's chief advantages were affordability, accessibility, and extended shelf life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxfordSpam_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordSpam-9">[9]</a></sup> Statistics from the 1990s say that 3.8 cans of Spam are consumed every second in the United States, totaling nearly 122 million cans annually. It became part of the diet of almost 30% of American households, it is perceived differently in various regions of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is also sometimes associated with economic hardship because of its relatively low cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeriousSpam_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-SeriousSpam-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam that is sold in North America, South America, and Australia is produced in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Minnesota" title="Austin, Minnesota">Austin, Minnesota</a> (also known as "Spam Town USA") and in <a href="/wiki/Fremont,_Nebraska" title="Fremont, Nebraska">Fremont, Nebraska</a>. Austin, Minnesota also has a restaurant with a menu devoted exclusively to Spam, called "Johnny's SPAMarama Menu".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, Spam was introduced to various private and public schools in South Florida as cheap food and even for art sculptures. Due to the success of the introduction, Hormel Foods also introduced school "color-themed" spam, the first being a blue and green variety which is still traditionally used in some private schools of South Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hawaii">Hawaii</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hawaii">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg/220px-Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg/330px-Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg/440px-Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spam_musubi_at_Ninja_Sushi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Spam_musubi" title="Spam musubi">Spam musubi</a> is a popular snack and lunch food in Hawaii</div>

<p>Spam is especially popular in the state of Hawaii, where residents have the highest per capita consumption in the United States. Its perception there is very different from on the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>A popular native sushi dish in Hawaii is <a href="/wiki/Spam_musubi" title="Spam musubi">Spam musubi</a>, where cooked Spam is placed atop rice and wrapped in a band of <a href="/wiki/Nori" title="Nori">nori</a>, a form of <i><a href="/wiki/Onigiri" title="Onigiri">onigiri</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Varieties of Spam are found in Hawaii that are unavailable in other markets, including Honey Spam, Spam with Bacon, and Hot and Spicy Spam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Star_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Star-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hawaiian <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger King</a> restaurants began serving Spam in 2007 to compete with the local <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> chains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BurgerKingSpam_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BurgerKingSpam-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In Hawaii, Spam is so popular that it is sometimes referred to as "The Hawaiian Steak".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Guam_and_the_Northern_Marianas">Guam and the Northern Marianas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Guam and the Northern Marianas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, average per capita consumption is 16 tins per year. It is also found on McDonald's menus there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">Northern Mariana Islands</a>, lawyers from Hormel have threatened legal action against the local press for running articles alleging ill-effects of high Spam consumption on the health of the local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sandwich de Mezcla is a party staple in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> containing Spam, <a href="/wiki/Velveeta" title="Velveeta">Velveeta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pimiento" title="Pimiento">pimientos</a> between two slices of <a href="/wiki/Wonder_Bread" title="Wonder Bread">Wonder Bread</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After World War II, Newforge Foods, part of the Fitch Lovell group, was awarded the license to produce the product in the UK at its Gateacre factory, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> where it stayed until production switched to the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Crown_AmbA" title="Danish Crown AmbA">Danish Crown Group</a> (owners of the Tulip Food Company) in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United Kingdom has adopted Spam into various recipes. For example, recipes include Spam Yorkshire Breakfast, Spamish Omelette, and Spam Hash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Spam can also be sliced, battered and deep-fried into <a href="/wiki/Spam_fritter" title="Spam fritter">Spam fritters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spam_served_with_rice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Spam_served_with_rice.jpg/170px-Spam_served_with_rice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Spam_served_with_rice.jpg/255px-Spam_served_with_rice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Spam_served_with_rice.jpg/340px-Spam_served_with_rice.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spam_served_with_rice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spam is often served with rice in Asia.</div>

</div>
<p>In China, Hormel decided to adopt a different strategy to market Spam, promoting it as a foreign, premium food product and changing the Spam formula to be meatier in order to accommodate local Chinese tastes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-china_businessweek_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-china_businessweek-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Okinawan_cuisine" title="Okinawan cuisine">Okinawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japan</a>, the product is added into <a href="/wiki/Onigiri" title="Onigiri">onigiri</a> alongside eggs and used as a staple ingredient in the traditional Okinawan dish <i><a href="/wiki/Chanpur%C5%AB" title="Chanpurū">chanpurū</a></i>, and a Spam burger is sold by local fast food chain <a href="/wiki/Jef" title="Jef">Jef</a>. For the 70th anniversary of Spam in 2007, cans with special designs were sold in Japan due to its popularity, primarily in Okinawa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_33-0"><a href="#cite_note--33">[33]</a></sup> Following the March 2011 earthquake, Spam sales in Japan declined and Hormel shifted its focus to China<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-china_businessweek_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-china_businessweek-32">[32]</a></sup> although Hormel did pledge to donate $100,000 along with cans of Spam for relief efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In the summer of 2011, Burger King introduced its own version of a burger made of Spam, called 'BK Shot' Spam Burgers. These small burgers are filled with slices of the canned meat and were an attempt by Burger King to capitalize on Spam's popularity in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In early 2014, Burger King also introduced the Spam and Cheese burger as a breakfast menu item.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_Hong_Kong" title="Cuisine of Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> after World War II, meat was scarce and expensive, so Spam was an accessible, affordable alternative. The luncheon meat has been incorporated into dishes such as macaroni with fried egg and spam in chicken soup, as well as <a href="/wiki/Ramen" title="Ramen">ramen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Philippines, Spam is a popular food item and seen as a cultural symbol. It is prepared and used in a variety of ways, including being fried, served alongside condiments, or used in sandwiches. The canned meat's popularity transcends economic class, and Spam gift sets are even used as homecoming gifts. There are more than 9 different varieties of Spam currently available in the country and an estimated 1.25 million kilos of the meat is sold every year in the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> During the rescue efforts after <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Ketsana" title="Typhoon Ketsana">Typhoon Ondoy</a> (Ketsana) in 2009, Hormel Foods donated over 30,000 pounds of Spam to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_National_Red_Cross" title="Philippine National Red Cross">Philippine National Red Cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In later years, the surfeit of Spam in both North and South Korea during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> led to the establishment of the Spam <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kimbap" title="Kimbap">kimbap</a></i> (rice and vegetable filled seaweed roll). Because of a scarcity of fish and other traditional kimbap products such as kimchi or fermented cabbage, Spam was added to a rice roll with kimchi and cucumber and wrapped in seaweed. Spam was also used by US soldiers in Korea as a means of trading for items, services or information around their bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">South Korea</a>, Spam (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" xml:lang="ko-Hang">스팸</span>; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Korean transliteration">seupaem</span></i>, licensed from Hormel by <a href="/wiki/CJ_CheilJedang" title="CJ CheilJedang">CJ CheilJedang</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>) is popular with a majority of the population, and outranks <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> and <a href="/wiki/KFC" title="KFC">KFC</a> in status as a foodstuff. Today, South Korea produces and consumes more Spam than any other country except the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam is also an original ingredient in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budae_jjigae" title="Budae jjigae">budae jjigae</a></i> ("army base stew"), a spicy stew with different types of preserved meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a>, demand for kosher canned meats increased as Spam became more popular during World War II. Canned meat was briefly mentioned during wartime from 1939–43, but the true boom in kosher canned meat came in 1945. This is when kosher canned meat became the key item in Europe's Jewish war victim relief packages. Then in 1946, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Kosher_Sausage_Manufacturing_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chicago Kosher Sausage Manufacturing Company (page does not exist)">Chicago Kosher Sausage Manufacturing Company</a> registered a patent for a kosher canned meat product called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Breef&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Breef (page does not exist)">Breef</a>. Made of beef, Breef has a similar texture to Spam but tastes like corned beef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Also, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher">kosher</a> variant of Spam, known as <a href="/wiki/Loof" title="Loof">Loof</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">לוף</span>‎‎, a Hebrew linguistic play on <a href="/wiki/Meatloaf" title="Meatloaf">meatloaf</a>), was produced by Richard Levi, and mostly used as part of <a href="/wiki/Field_ration" title="Field ration">field rations</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glatt_kosher" title="Glatt kosher">Glatt kosher</a> version was also produced. It was phased out of field rations during the early 2000s and was finally removed from rations when production ceased in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>Beginning in 1940, Spam sponsored <a href="/wiki/George_Burns" title="George Burns">George Burns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gracie_Allen" title="Gracie Allen">Gracie Allen</a> on their <a href="/wiki/George_Burns#Radio_stars" title="George Burns">radio program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>During WWII, Spam was not only eaten but was also incorporated into many other aspects of the war (grease for guns, can for scrap metal, etc.); it was so prominent that Uncle Sam was nicknamed "Uncle Spam".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Other terms influenced by the product’s name include the European invasion fleet, or the "Spam Fleet". Furthermore, the United Service Organizations (USO) toured the "Spam Circuit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxfordSpam_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordSpam-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States in the aftermath of World War II, a troupe of former servicewomen was assembled by Hormel Foods to promote Spam from coast to coast. The group was known as the Hormel Girls and associated the food with being patriotic. In 1948, two years after its formation, the troupe had grown to 60 women with 16 forming an orchestra. The show went on to become a radio program where the main selling point was Spam. The Hormel Girls were disbanded in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam gained a reputation in the United Kingdom and the United States as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_food" title="Poverty food">poverty food</a>. The image of Spam as a low cost meat product gave rise to the Scottish colloquial term "Spam valley" to describe certain affluent housing areas where residents appear to be wealthy but in reality may be living at poverty levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam was featured in an iconic 1970 <a href="/wiki/Monty_Python" title="Monty Python">Monty Python</a> sketch called "<a href="/wiki/Spam_(Monty_Python)" title="Spam (Monty Python)">Spam</a>". Set in a café which only served dishes containing Spam, including "Spam, Spam, Spam, Spam, Spam, Spam, baked beans, Spam, Spam, Spam and Spam", the piece also featured a companion song. By the 1990s, Spam's perceived ubiquity led to its name being adopted for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" title="Spam (electronic)">unsolicited electronic messages</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(email)" title="Spam (email)">spam email</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Because of its being used in a line of a song in <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="Monty Python and the Holy Grail">Monty Python and the Holy Grail</a></i>, the title of the musical version of the film became <i><a href="/wiki/Spamalot" title="Spamalot">Spamalot</a></i>.</p>
<p>Spam was also referenced in the parody song "Spam" by <a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic" title='"Weird Al" Yankovic'>"Weird Al" Yankovic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAMlyrics_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPAMlyrics-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other offshoots of Spam in popular culture include a book of <a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haikus</a> about spam titled <i>Spam-Ku: Tranquil Reflections on Luncheon Loaf</i>. There is also a mock Church of Spam, and a Spam Cam which is a webcam trained on a can of decaying spam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam is referenced in <i><a href="/wiki/Island_of_the_Sequined_Love_Nun" title="Island of the Sequined Love Nun">Island of the Sequined Love Nun</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Moore_(author)" title="Christopher Moore (author)">Christopher Moore</a>, where SPAM is explained as <i>Shaped Pork Approximating Man</i>, which was used to explain its popularity amongst Pacific Island Cannibals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam has also incorporated social media as part of its marketing; for example, it has official Twitter accounts in both the US and UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spam has also been mentioned in the online point and click game "Escape from Plastic Beach" from 2010 on <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">gorillaz.com</a>. In the game it's used as bait for a talking fish.</p>

<p>Spam is celebrated in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Minnesota" title="Austin, Minnesota">Austin, Minnesota</a>, home to the <a href="/wiki/Spam_Museum" title="Spam Museum">Spam Museum</a>. The museum tells the history of the Hormel company, the origin of Spam, and its place in world culture, including Hawaii where Spam is eaten daily by locals.</p>
<p>Austin is also the location of final judging in the national Spam recipe competition. Competing recipes are collected from winning submissions at the top 40 state fairs in the nation. The Spamettes are a quartet from Austin who only sing about Spam in parodies of popular songs. They first performed at the first Spam Jam in 1990 and continue to perform at various events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hawaii holds an annual <a href="/wiki/Spam_Jam" title="Spam Jam">Spam Jam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Waikiki" title="Waikiki">Waikiki</a> during the last week of April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The small town of <a href="/wiki/Shady_Cove,_Oregon" title="Shady Cove, Oregon">Shady Cove, Oregon</a> is home to the annual Spam Parade and Festival, with the city allocating US$1,500 for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spamarama" title="Spamarama">Spamarama</a> was a yearly festival held around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_Fool%27s_Day" title="April Fool's Day">April Fool's Day</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>. The theme of Spamarama was a gentle parody of Spam, rather than a straightforward celebration: the event at the heart of the festival was a Spam <a href="/wiki/Cook-off" title="Cook-off">cook-off</a> that originated as a challenge to produce the most appetizing recipe for the meat. A rule of the event was that contestants had to be prepared to eat the Spam dish if requested by a judge. The festival included light sporting activities and musical acts, in addition to the cook-off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spamarama_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spamarama-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As listed on the official Spam website, there are numerous different flavors of Spam products, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Spam Classic – original flavor</li>
<li>Spam Hot &amp; Spicy – with <a href="/wiki/Tabasco_sauce" title="Tabasco sauce">Tabasco</a> flavor</li>
<li>Jalapeño Spam</li>
<li>Spam with Black Pepper</li>
<li>Spam Low Sodium – "25% less sodium"</li>
<li>Spam Lite – "33% fewer calories, 25% less sodium, and 50% less fat" – made from pork shoulder meat, ham, and mechanically separated chicken</li>
<li>Spam Oven Roasted Turkey</li>
<li>Spam Hickory Smoked</li>
<li>Spam Spread – "if you're a spreader, not a slicer ... just like Spam Classic, but in a spreadable form"</li>
<li>Spam Bacon</li>
<li>Spam Cheese</li>
<li>Spam Garlic</li>
<li>Spam Teriyaki</li>
<li>Spam Chorizo</li>
<li>Spam Macadamia Nuts – Partnered with Hamakua Plantation</li>
<li>Spam Turkey</li>
<li>Spam Tocino</li>
<li>Spam Portuguese Sausage</li>
<li>In addition to the variety of flavors, Spam is sold in tins smaller than the twelve-ounce standard size. Spam Singles are also available, which are single sandwich-sized slices of Spam Classic or Lite, sealed in <a href="/wiki/Retort_pouch" title="Retort pouch">retort pouches</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varieties_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-varieties-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
