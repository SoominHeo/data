<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Space marine</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>space marine</b>, an <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetype</a> of <a href="/wiki/Military_science_fiction" title="Military science fiction">military science fiction</a>, is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldier</a> that operates in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> or on alien worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prucher2007_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-prucher2007-1">[1]</a></sup> Historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(military)" title="Marine (military)">marines</a> fulfill multiple roles: ship defence, landing parties, and general-purpose high-mobility land deployments that operate within a fixed distance of shore. By analogy, hypothetical space marines would defend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spaceships" title="Spaceships">spaceships</a>, land on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a>, and satisfy rapid-deployment needs throughout <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_marine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest known use of the term "space marine" was by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Olsen" title="Bob Olsen">Bob Olsen</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short story</a> "Captain Brink of the Space Marines" (<i><a href="/wiki/Amazing_Stories" title="Amazing Stories">Amazing Stories</a></i>, Volume 7, Number 8, November 1932), a light-hearted work whose title is a <a href="/wiki/Word_play" title="Word play">play</a> on the song "<a href="/wiki/Captain_Jinks_of_the_Horse_Marines" title="Captain Jinks of the Horse Marines">Captain Jinks of the Horse Marines</a>", and in which the protagonists were marines of the "Earth Republic Space Navy" on mission to rescue celebrity twins from aliens on <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>. Olsen published a <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a> sequel four years later, "The Space Marines and the Slavers" (<i>Amazing Stories</i>, Volume 10, Number 13, December 1936), featuring the same characters using a spaceship with <a href="/wiki/Active_camouflage" title="Active camouflage">active camouflage</a> to free hostages from <a href="/wiki/Martian" title="Martian">Martian</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_pirate" title="Space pirate">space pirates</a> on <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A more widely known early example was <a href="/wiki/E._E._Smith" title="E. E. Smith">E. E. Smith</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lensman_series" title="Lensman series"><i>Lensman</i> series</a>. While the first story, <i><a href="/wiki/Triplanetary_(novel)" title="Triplanetary (novel)">Triplanetary</a></i> and most later sequels (<i><a href="/wiki/Second_Stage_Lensmen" title="Second Stage Lensmen">Second Stage Lensmen</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Lens_(novel)" title="Children of the Lens (novel)">Children of the Lens</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vortex_Blaster" title="The Vortex Blaster">The Vortex Blaster</a></i>) do not mention them, passing mentions of marines are made in <i><a href="/wiki/Galactic_Patrol_(novel)" title="Galactic Patrol (novel)">Galactic Patrol</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astounding_Stories" title="Astounding Stories">Astounding Stories</a></i>, September 1937–February 1938) and <i><a href="/wiki/Gray_Lensman" title="Gray Lensman">Gray Lensman</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[c]</a></sup> (<i>Astounding Stories</i>, October 1939–January 1940), and a more direct mention is made in <i><a href="/wiki/First_Lensman" title="First Lensman">First Lensman</a></i> (1950): "Dronvire of Rigel Four in the lead, closely followed by Costigan, Northrop, Kinnison the Younger, and a platoon of armed and armored Space Marines!".</p>
<p>The phrase "space marines" appears in <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Misfit_(short_story)" title="Misfit (short story)">Misfit</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[d]</a></sup> (1939) and is again used in "<a href="/wiki/The_Long_Watch" title="The Long Watch">The Long Watch</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[e]</a></sup> (1941) which is referenced in his later novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Cadet_(novel)" title="Space Cadet (novel)">Space Cadet</a></i> (1948), in all cases before Smith had used the phrase. Heinlein's <i><a href="/wiki/Starship_Troopers" title="Starship Troopers">Starship Troopers</a></i> (1959) is considered the defining work for the concept, although it does not use the term "space marine". The actors playing the Colonial Marines in <i><a href="/wiki/Aliens_(film)" title="Aliens (film)">Aliens</a></i> (1986) were required to read <i>Starship Troopers</i> as part of their training prior to filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[3]</a></sup> Heinlein intended for the capsule troopers of the Mobile Infantry to be an amalgam of the shipborne aspect of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marine_Corps" title="US Marine Corps">US Marine Corps</a> relocated to space and coupled with the battlefield delivery and mission profile of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army" title="US Army">US Army</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paratroopers" title="Paratroopers">paratroopers</a>.</p>
<p>As a gaming concept, space marines play a major role in the <i><a href="/wiki/Warhammer_40,000" title="Warhammer 40,000">Warhammer 40,000</a></i> miniatures wargame, in which they are genetically altered super-soldiers and the most powerful fighting forces available to the Imperium of Man. In computer games, playing a space marine in action games was popularized by <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(1993_video_game)" title="Doom (1993 video game)">Doom</a></i> series, first published in 1993. It is a convenient game <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back-story" title="Back-story">back-story</a> as it excuses the presence of the character on a hostile alien world with little <a href="/wiki/Military_logistics" title="Military logistics">support</a> and heavy weaponry. Some critics have suggested it has been overused to the point of being an action game <a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Cliché">cliché</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trademark_controversy">Trademark controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_marine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trademark controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2012, online retailer <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> removed the <a href="/wiki/E-book" title="E-book">e-book</a> <i>Spots the Space Marine</i> by <a href="/wiki/M.C.A._Hogarth" title="M.C.A. Hogarth">M.C.A. Hogarth</a> at the request of games company <a href="/wiki/Games_Workshop" title="Games Workshop">Games Workshop</a>. They claimed the use of the phrase "space marine" infringed on their <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> of the term for their game <i>Warhammer 40,000</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[5]</a></sup> In February 2013, the row received a lot of publicity, with authors such as <a href="/wiki/Cory_Doctorow" title="Cory Doctorow">Cory Doctorow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stross" title="Charles Stross">Charles Stross</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Scalzi" title="John Scalzi">John Scalzi</a> supporting Hogarth, and Amazon.com then restored the e-book for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_marine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In film and television space marines often appear in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squads" title="Squads">squads</a>, while in video games the protagonist Marine is usually alone or in very small squads.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Depending on the mission, they may be deployed via <a href="/wiki/Dropship_(science_fiction)" title="Dropship (science fiction)">dropship</a> or another specialised insertion craft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Their <a href="/wiki/Battledress" title="Battledress">battledress</a> varies between media, ranging from equipment comparable to modern-day fatigues (or just being contemporary, such as the equipment of Colonial Marines in the <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(2004_TV_series)" title="Battlestar Galactica (2004 TV series)">re-imaged <i>Battlestar Galactica</i></a>) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesuit" title="Spacesuit">environmentally sealed</a> <a href="/wiki/Powered_exoskeleton" title="Powered exoskeleton">suits of powered armour</a>. Equipment and weaponry is similarly varied, often incorporating various fictional technologies. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directed-energy_weapons" title="Directed-energy weapons">Directed-energy weapons</a> are common, though conventional firearms are also used, like the M41A Pulse Rifles the Colonial Marines in the <a href="/wiki/Aliens_(film)" title="Aliens (film)">Aliens</a> movie use (which are projectile weapons that use an electric pulse to shoot <a href="/wiki/Caseless_ammunition" title="Caseless ammunition">caseless ammunition</a>). If the marines' armour is particularly bulky, their weapons may be similarly scaled up such as in Warhammer 40,000 where Space Marines carry "boltguns," effectively rocket-propelled grenade launchers, as a standard firearm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-fiction_aspects">Non-fiction aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_marine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Non-fiction aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Hot_Eagle" title="Project Hot Eagle">Project Hot Eagle</a> considers the use of <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> to deliver Marines to a target on the ground. "Within minutes of bursting into the atmosphere beyond the speed of sound – and dispatching that ominous sonic boom – a small squad of Marines could be on the ground and ready for action within 2 hours."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[8]</a></sup></p>
