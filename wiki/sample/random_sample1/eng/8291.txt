<p>The <b>Spanish Navy</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es"><i>Armada Española</i></span>), is the <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">maritime branch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armed_Forces" title="Spanish Armed Forces">Spanish Armed Forces</a> and one of the oldest active naval forces in the world. The Spanish navy was responsible for a number of major historic achievements in navigation, the most famous being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_America" title="Discovery of America">Discovery of America</a> and the first global <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Magellan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Sebasti%C3%A1n_Elcano" title="Juan Sebastián Elcano">Elcano</a>. For several centuries, it played a crucial logistical role in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> and defended a vast trade network across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_treasure_fleet" title="Spanish treasure fleet">Americas and Europe</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> between <a href="/wiki/Manila_galleon" title="Manila galleon">Asia and the Americas</a>.</p>
<p>The Spanish Navy was the most powerful maritime force in the world in the 16th and early 17th centuries. Reform under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon dynasty</a> improved its logistical and military capacity in the 18th century, for most of which Spain possessed the world's third largest navy. In the 19th century, the Spanish Navy built and operated the <a href="/wiki/Peral_Submarine" title="Peral Submarine">first military submarine</a>, made <a href="/wiki/Spanish_warship_Destructor" title="Spanish warship Destructor">important contributions</a> in the development of <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a> warships, and achieved the first global circumnavigation by an <a href="/wiki/Spanish_ironclad_Numancia" title="Spanish ironclad Numancia">ironclad vessel</a>. Currently, the Spanish 'Armada' is the sixth largest navy in the world.</p>
<p>The main bases of the Spanish Navy are located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rota,_C%C3%A1diz" title="Rota, Cádiz">Rota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrol,_Galicia" title="Ferrol, Galicia">Ferrol</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando,_C%C3%A1diz" title="San Fernando, Cádiz">San Fernando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Spain" title="Cartagena, Spain">Cartagena</a>.</p>

<p>The roots of the modern Spanish navy date back to before the unification of Spain. By the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the two principal kingdoms that would later combine to form Spain, <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Aragon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a>, had developed powerful fleets. Aragon possessed the third largest navy in the late medieval Mediterranean, although its capabilities were exceeded by those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetian_Republic" title="Venetian Republic">Venice</a> and (until overtaken in the 15th century by those of Aragon) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genoese_Republic" title="Genoese Republic">Genoa</a>. In the 14th and 15th centuries, these naval capabilities enabled Aragon to assemble the largest collection of territories of any European power in the Mediterranean, encompassing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balearics" title="Balearics">Balearics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, southern Italy and, briefly, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Athens" title="Duchy of Athens">Duchy of Athens</a>. Castile meanwhile used its naval capacities to conduct its <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">reconquista</a> operations against the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>, capturing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">Cadiz</a> in 1232 and also to help the French Crown against its enemies in the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a>. In 1402, a Castilian expedition led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_de_Bethencourt" title="Juan de Bethencourt">Juan de Bethencourt</a> conquered the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> for <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">Henry III of Castile</a>.</p>
<p>In the 15th century, Castile entered into a race of exploration with <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, the country that inaugurated the European <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>. In 1492, two <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">caravels</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">carrack</a>, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, arrived in America, on an expedition that sought a westward oceanic passage across the Atlantic, to the Far East. This began the era of trans-oceanic trade routes, pioneered by the Spanish in the seas to the west of Europe and the Portuguese to the east.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Habsburg_era">The Habsburg era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Navy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Habsburg era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the discovery of America and the settlement of certain Caribbean islands, such as <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, Spanish <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadors</a> <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a> were carried by the Spanish navy to the mainland, where they conquered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_New_Spain" title="Viceroyalty of New Spain">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Peru</a> respectively. The navy also carried explorers to the North American mainland, including <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de León">Juan Ponce de León</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%81lvarez_de_Pineda" title="Álvarez de Pineda">Álvarez de Pineda</a>, who discovered <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> (1519) and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> (1521) respectively. In 1519, Spain sent out the first expedition of <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">world circumnavigation</a> in history, which was put in the charge of the Portuguese Commander <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a>. Following the death of Magellan in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, the expedition was completed under the command of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Sebasti%C3%A1n_Elcano" title="Juan Sebastián Elcano">Juan Sebastián Elcano</a> in 1522. In 1565, a follow-on expedition by <a href="/wiki/Miguel_L%C3%B3pez_de_Legazpi" title="Miguel López de Legazpi">Miguel López de Legazpi</a> was carried by the navy from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_New_Spain" title="Viceroyalty of New Spain">New Spain</a> (Mexico) to the Philippines via <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> in order to establish the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish East Indies</a>, a base for trade with the Orient. For two and a half centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Manila_galleon" title="Manila galleon">Manila galleons</a> operated across the Pacific linking <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a>. Until the early 17th century, the Pacific Ocean. Aside from the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Marianas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Islands" title="Caroline Islands">Caroline Islands</a>, several naval expeditions also discovered the <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu archipelago</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marquesas_Islands" title="Marquesas Islands">Marquesas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South" title="South">South</a> Pacific. In the quest for <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a>, Spanish explorers in the 17th century also discovered the <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> archipelagos. Most significantly, from 1565 Spanish fleets explored and colonised the Philippine archipelago, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish East Indies</a>.</p>

<p>After the unification of its kingdoms under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a>, Spain maintained two largely separate fleets, one consisting chiefly of galleys for use in the Mediterranean and the other of sailing ships for the Atlantic, successors to the Aragonese and Castilian navies respectively. This arrangement continued until superseded by the decline of galley warfare during the 17th century. The completion of the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> with the conquest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Granada" title="Kingdom of Granada">Kingdom of Granada</a> in 1492 had been followed by naval expansion in the Mediterranean, where Spain seized control of almost every significant port along the coast of North Africa west of <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a>, notably <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a> (captured 1497), <a href="/wiki/Mers_El_K%C3%A9bir" title="Mers El Kébir">Mers El Kébir</a> (1505), <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">Oran</a> (1509), <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a> (1510) and <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a> (1511), which marked the furthest point of this advance. However, the hinterlands of these ports remained under the control of their Muslim and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a> inhabitants, and the expanding naval power of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> brought about a major Islamic counter-offensive, which embroiled Spain in decades of intense warfare for control of the western Mediterranean. (Algiers and Tripoli would be lost to the Ottomans later in the 16th century.) From the 1570s, the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Dutch Revolt</a> increasingly challenged Spanish sea power, producing powerful rebel naval forces that attacked Spanish shipping and in time made Spain's sea communications with its possessions in the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> difficult. Most notable of these attacks was the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gibraltar_(1607)" title="Battle of Gibraltar (1607)">Battle of Gibraltar</a> in 1607, in which a Dutch squadron destroyed a fleet of <a href="/wiki/Galleon" title="Galleon">galleons</a> at anchor in the confines of the bay. This naval war took on a global dimension with actions in the Caribbean and the Far East, notably around the Philippines. Spain's response to its problems included the encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a> based in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a> and known from their main base as <a href="/wiki/Dunkirkers" title="Dunkirkers">Dunkirkers</a>, who preyed on Dutch merchant ships and fishing trawlers.</p>

<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto" title="Battle of Lepanto">Battle of Lepanto</a> (1571), the Holy League, formed by Spain, Venice, the Papal States and other Christian allies, inflicted a great defeat on the Ottoman Navy, stopping Muslim forces from gaining uncontested control of the Mediterranean.</p>
<p>In the 1580s, the conflict in the Netherlands drew England into war with Spain, creating a further menace to Spanish shipping. The effort to neutralise this threat led to a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">disastrous attempt to invade England</a> in 1588. This defeat led to a reform of fleet operations. The navy at this time was not a single operation but consisted of various fleets, made up mainly of armed merchantmen with escorts of royal ships. The Armada fiasco marked a turning point in naval warfare, where gunnery was now more important than ramming and boarding and so Spanish ships were equipped with purpose built naval guns. During the 1590s, the expansion of these fleets allowed a great increase in the overseas trade and massive increase in the importation of luxuries and silver. Nevertheless, inadequate port defences allowed an Anglo-Dutch force to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capture_of_Cadiz" title="Capture of Cadiz">raid Cadiz</a> in 1596, and though unsuccessful in its objective of capturing the silver from the just returned convoy, was able to inflict great damage upon the city. Port defences at Cadiz were upgraded and all attempts to repeat the attack in the following centuries would fail.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Spanish ships were able to step up operations in the English Channel, the North Sea and towards Ireland. They were able to capture many enemy ships, merchant and military, in the early decades of the 17th century and provide military supplies to Spanish armies in France and the Low Countries and to Irish rebels in Ireland.</p>
<p>By the middle of the 17th century, Spain had been drained by the vast strains of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years'</a> and related wars and began to slip into a slow decline. During the middle to late decades of the century, the Dutch, English and French were able to take advantage of Spain's shrinking, run-down and increasingly underequipped fleets. Military priorities in continental Europe meant that naval affairs were increasingly neglected. The Dutch took control of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">smaller islands of the Caribbean</a>, while England conquered <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and France the western part of <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>. These territories became bases for raids on Spanish New World ports and shipping by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirates" title="Pirates">pirates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Privateers" title="Privateers">privateers</a>. The Spanish concentrated their efforts in keeping the most important islands, such as <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the majority of Santo Domingo, while the system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_treasure_fleets" title="Spanish treasure fleets">treasure fleets</a>, despite being greatly diminished, was rarely defeated in safely conveying its freight of silver and Asian luxeries across the Atlantic to Europe. Only two such convoys were ever lost to enemy action with their cargo, one to a Dutch fleet in 1628 and another to an English fleet in 1656. A third convoy was destroyed at anchor by another English attack in 1657, but it had already unloaded its treasure.</p>
<p>By the time of the wars of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Grand_Alliance" title="War of the Grand Alliance">Grand Alliance (1688-97)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">Spanish Succession (1702-14)</a>, the Habsburg regime had decided that it was more cost effective to rely on allied fleets, Anglo-Dutch and French respectively, than to invest in its own fleets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bourbon_era">The Bourbon era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Navy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Bourbon era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> arose after the establishment on the Spanish throne of a <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> king, following the extinction of the Spanish Habsburg line. The internal division between supporters of a Hapsburg and those of a Bourbon king led to a civil war and ultimately to the loss of Sicily, Sardinia, <a href="/wiki/Minorca" title="Minorca">Minorca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>. Gibraltar and Minorca were occupied by British forces fighting under the Spanish flag of Hapsburg contender <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles VI</a>. Minorca was ultimately surrendered to Spain years later. During the War of Spanish Succession, Spain's possessions in the Netherlands and mainland Italy were also ceded.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo,_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="795" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo%2C_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg/220px-Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo%2C_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo%2C_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg/330px-Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo%2C_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7e/Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo%2C_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg/440px-Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo%2C_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Defensa_de_Cartagena_de_Indias_por_la_escuadra_de_D._Blas_de_Lezo,_a%C3%B1o_1741.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Battle of Cartagena de Indias, 1741</div>
</div>

<p>Attempting to reverse the losses of the previous war, in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Quadruple_Alliance" title="War of the Quadruple Alliance">War of the Quadruple Alliance</a> (1718–20) the Spanish navy successfully convoyed armies to invade Sicily and Sardinia, but the escort fleet was destroyed by the British in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_Passaro" title="Battle of Cape Passaro">Battle of Cape Passaro</a> and the Spanish invasion army was defeated in Italy by the Austrians. A major program to renovate and reorganise the run-down navy was begun. A <i>secretaría</i> (ministry) of the army and navy had been established by the Bourbon regime as early as 1714; which centralized the command and administration of the different fleets. Following the war of Quadruple Alliance, a program of rigorous standardization was introduced in ships, operations, and administration. Given the needs of its empire, Spanish warship designs tended to be more orientated towards long-range escort and patrol duties than for battle. A major reform of the Spanish navy was initiated, updating its ships and administration, which was helped by French and Italian experts, although Spaniards, most notably <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Castaneta" title="Antonio Castaneta">Antonio Castaneta</a>, soon rose to prominence in this work, which made Spain a leader in warship design and quality again, as was demonstrated by ships like the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Princess_(1740)" title="HMS Princess (1740)">Princesa</a>. A major naval yard was established at <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, enabling the navy to maintain a permanent force in the Americas for the defence of the colonies and the suppression of piracy and smuggling.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="593" data-file-width="799" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg/220px-Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg/330px-Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg/440px-Nav%C3%ADo_santa_ana_de_112_ca%C3%B1ones.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<i>Navio</i> or ship-of-the-line, <i>Santa Anna</i>, 1784-1814</div>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Polish_Succession" title="War of the Polish Succession">War of the Polish Succession</a> (1733–38), a renewed attempt to regain the lost Italian territories for the Bourbon dynasty was successful; with the French as allies and the British and Dutch neutral, Spain launched a campaign by sea and retook Sicily and southern Italy from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_monarchy" title="Austrian monarchy">Austria</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Jenkins%27_Ear" title="War of Jenkins' Ear">War of Jenkins' Ear</a>, the navy showed it was able to maintain communications with the American colonies and resupply Spanish forces in Italy in the face of British naval opposition. The navy played an important part in the decisive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Battle of Cartagena de Indias">Battle of Cartagena de Indias</a> in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, where a massive British invasion fleet and army were defeated by a smaller Spanish force commanded by able strategist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_Blas_de_Lezo" title="Admiral Blas de Lezo">Blas de Lezo</a>. This Spanish victory prolonged Spain's supremacy in the Americas until the early 19th century. The program of naval renovation was continued and by the 1750s the Spanish navy had outstripped the Dutch to become the third most powerful in the world, behind only those of Britain and France.</p>
<p>Joining France against Britain near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> (1756–63), the navy failed to prevent the British <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Havana_(1762)" title="Battle of Havana (1762)">capturing Havana</a>, during which the Spanish squadron present was also captured. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a> (1775–83), the Spanish navy was essential to the establishment, in combination with the French and Dutch navies, of a numerical advantage that stretched British naval resources. They played a vital role, along with the French and Dutch, in maintaining military supplies to the American rebels. The navy also played a key role in the Spanish army led operations that defeated the British in Florida. The bulk of the purely naval combat on the allied side fell to the French navy, although Spain achieved lucrative successes with the capture of two great British convoys meant for the resupply of British forces and loyalists in North America. Joint operations with France resulted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Minorca,_1781" title="Invasion of Minorca, 1781">capture of Minorca</a> but failed in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Siege_of_Gibraltar" title="Great Siege of Gibraltar">siege of Gibraltar</a>.</p>
<p>Having initially opposed France in the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a> (1792–1802), Spain changed sides in 1796, but defeat by the British a few months later in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_St_Vincent_(1797)" title="Battle of Cape St Vincent (1797)">Battle of Cape St Vincent</a> was followed by the blockade of the main Spanish fleet in Cadiz. The run down of naval operations had as much to do with the confused political situation in Spain as it had to do with the blockade. The British blockade of Spain's ports was of limited success and an attempt to <a href="/wiki/Assault_on_C%C3%A1diz_(1797)" title="Assault on Cádiz (1797)">attack Cadiz</a> was defeated; ships on special missions and convoys successfully evaded the Cadiz blockade and other ports continued to operate with little difficulty, but the main battle fleets were largely inactive. The blockade was lifted with the Peace of Amiens 1802. <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1796%E2%80%931808)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1796–1808)">The war</a> recommenced in 1804 and ended in 1808 when the Spain and the United Kingdom became allied against Napoleon. As in the first part, Cadiz was blockaded and Spanish naval activity was minimal. The most notable event was Spanish involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Battle of Trafalgar</a> under French leadership. This resulted in the Spanish navy losing eleven ships-of-the-line or over a quarter of its line-of-battle ships. After Spain became allied with the United Kingdom in 1808 in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peninsular_war" title="Peninsular war">war of independence</a>, the Spanish navy <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_the_Rosily_Squadron" title="Capture of the Rosily Squadron">joined the war effort</a> against Napoleon.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_navy_in_decline">The navy in decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Navy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The navy in decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peral1888.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="193" data-file-width="608" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Peral1888.jpg/220px-Peral1888.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Peral1888.jpg/330px-Peral1888.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Peral1888.jpg/440px-Peral1888.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peral1888.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The experimental <i><a href="/wiki/Peral_Submarine" title="Peral Submarine">Peral Submarine</a></i>, 1888</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The 1820s saw the loss of most of the Spanish Empire in the Americas. With the empire greatly reduced in size and Spain divided and unstable after its own war of independence, the navy lost its importance and shrank greatly.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> in 1898, a badly supported and equipped Spanish fleet of four armored cruisers and two destroyers was overwhelmed by numerically and technically superior forces (three new battleships, one new second class battleship, and one large armored cruiser) as it tried to break out of an American blockade in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Santiago_de_Cuba" title="Battle of Santiago de Cuba">Battle of Santiago de Cuba</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_Cervera" title="Admiral Cervera">Admiral Cervera</a>'s squadron was overrun in an attempt to break a powerful American blockade off <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="570" data-file-width="740" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg/220px-USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg/330px-USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg/440px-USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USS_Olympia_art_NH_91881-KN_cropped.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Battle of Manila Bay, 1898</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the Philippines, a squadron, made up of ageing ships, including some obsolete cruisers, had already been sacrificed in a token gesture in Manila Bay. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Manila_Bay" title="Battle of Manila Bay">Battle of Manila Bay</a> took place on 1 May 1898, during the Spanish–American War. The American Asiatic Squadron under Commodore George Dewey engaged and destroyed the Spanish Pacific Squadron under Admiral Patricio Montojo y Pasarón. The engagement took place in Manila Bay in the Philippines, and was the first major engagement of the Spanish–American War. This war marked the end for the Spanish Navy as a global maritime force.</p>
<p>At the end of the 19th century, the Spanish Navy adopted the <i><a href="/wiki/Salve_Marinera" title="Salve Marinera">Salve Marinera</a></i>, a hymn to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a> as <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady,_Star_of_the_Sea" title="Our Lady, Star of the Sea">Stella Maris</a>, as its official <a href="/wiki/Anthem" title="Anthem">anthem</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_20th_and_21st_centuries">The 20th and 21st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Navy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The 20th and 21st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3637" data-file-width="5104" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg/220px-Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg/330px-Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg/440px-Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isaac_Peral_submarine_LOC_ggbain_22650.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
US-built Spanish submarine <i>Isaac Peral</i> (launched in 1916)</div>

<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rif_War_(1920)" title="Rif War (1920)">Rif War</a> in Morocco, the Spanish navy conducted operations along the coast, including the <a href="/wiki/Alhucemas_landing" title="Alhucemas landing">Alhucemas landing</a> in 1925, the first air-naval landing of the world. At that time, the Navy developed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Aviation" title="Naval Aviation">Naval Aviation</a> branch, the <i>Aeronáutica naval</i>. In 1931, following the proclamation of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">Second Spanish Republic</a>, the Navy of the Spanish Kingdom became the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Republican_Navy" title="Spanish Republican Navy">Spanish Republican Navy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Aznar-Caba%C3%B1as" title="Juan Bautista Aznar-Cabañas">Admiral Aznar</a>'s casual comment: <i>"Do you think it was a little thing what happened yesterday, that Spain went to bed as a monarchy and rose as a republic"</i> became instantly famous, going quickly around Madrid and around Spain, making people accept the fact and setting a more relaxed mood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Spanish Republican Navy introduced a few changes in the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Flag of the Second Spanish Republic">flags and ensigns</a>, as well as in the Navy officer rank insignia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Executive_curl" title="Executive curl">executive curl</a> <i>(La coca)</i> was replaced by a golden five-pointed star and the royal crown of the brass buttons and of the officers' breastplates <i>(La gola)</i> became a <a href="/wiki/Mural_crown" title="Mural crown">mural crown</a>. The Spanish Republican Navy became divided after the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_coup_of_July_1936" title="Spanish coup of July 1936">coup of July 1936</a> that led to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> (1936–39). The fleet's two <a href="/wiki/Espa%C3%B1a-class_battleship" title="España-class battleship">small dreadnoughts</a>, one <a href="/wiki/Spanish_cruiser_Baleares" title="Spanish cruiser Baleares">heavy cruiser</a>, one <a href="/wiki/Spanish_destroyer_Almirante_Ferr%C3%A1ndiz" title="Spanish destroyer Almirante Ferrándiz">large destroyer</a> and half a dozen submarines and auxiliary vessels were lost in the course of the conflict.</p>
<p>Since the mid-20th century, the Spanish Navy began a process of reorganization to once again become one of the major navies of the world. After the development of the <a href="/wiki/Baleares-class_frigate" title="Baleares-class frigate"><i>Baleares</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> based on the US Navy's <a href="/wiki/Knox-class_frigate" title="Knox-class frigate"><i>Knox</i> class</a>, the Spanish Navy embraced the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_doctrine" title="Naval doctrine">naval doctrine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Spain is a member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> in 1982 and the Armada Española has taken part in many coalition peacekeeping operations, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SFOR" title="SFOR">SFOR</a> to <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> and other locations around the world. Today's Armada is a modern navy with a carrier group, a modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_ship_Juan_Carlos_I_(L61)" title="Spanish ship Juan Carlos I (L61)">strategic amphibious ship</a> (which has recently replaced a dedicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_aircraft_carrier_Principe_de_Asturias" title="Spanish aircraft carrier Principe de Asturias">aircraft carrier</a>), modern <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> (F-100 class) with the <a href="/wiki/Aegis_Combat_System" title="Aegis Combat System">Aegis Combat System</a>, F-80-class frigates, minesweepers, new <a href="/wiki/S-80-class_submarine" title="S-80-class submarine"><i>S-80</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>, amphibious ships and various other ships, including oceanographic research ships.</p>
<p>The Armada's <a href="/wiki/Special_operations" title="Special operations">special operations</a> and unconventional warfare capability is embodied in the Naval Special Warfare Command (<b>Mando de Guerra Naval Especial</b>), which is under the direct control of the Admiral of the Fleet. Two units operate under this command:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Special Operations Unit</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unidad_de_Operaciones_Especiales_(UOE)" title="Unidad de Operaciones Especiales (UOE)">Unidad de Operaciones Especiales (UOE)</a>): Special operations unit trained in maritime counter-terrorism, combat diving and swimming, coastal infiltration, ship boarding, direct action, and special reconnaissance.</li>
<li>The <i>Combat Diver Unit</i> (<b>Unidad Especial de Buceadores de Combate (UEBC)</b>): Specialized combat diving unit trained in underwater demolitions and hydrographic reconnaissance.</li>
</ul>
<p>Armada officers receive their education at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escuela_Naval_Militar" title="Escuela Naval Militar">Spanish Naval Academy</a> (ENM). They are recruited through two different methods:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Militar de Complemento:</b> Similar to the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROTC" title="ROTC">ROTC</a> program, students are college graduates who enroll in the Navy. They spend a year at the Naval Academy and then are commissioned as Ensigns and Marine Second Lieutenants. This path is growing in prestige. Their career stops at the rank of Commander (for the Navy) and for the Marines, Lieutenant Colonel.</li>
<li><b>Militar de Carrera:</b> Students spend one year in the Naval Academy if they apply to the Supply Branch or the Engineering Branch, and five years if they apply as General Branch or Marines, receiving a university degree-equivalent upon graduation and being commissioned as Ensigns and Marine Second Lieutenants.</li>
</ul>

<p>Subordinate to the Spanish Chief of Naval Staff, stationed in Madrid, are four area commands: the Cantabrian Maritime Zone with its headquarters at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrol,_A_Coru%C3%B1a" title="Ferrol, A Coruña">Ferrol</a> on the Atlantic coast; the Straits Maritime Zone with its headquarters at <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando,_C%C3%A1diz" title="San Fernando, Cádiz">San Fernando</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">Cadiz</a>; the Mediterranean Maritime Zone with its headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Spain" title="Cartagena, Spain">Cartagena</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> Maritime Zone with its headquarters at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Palmas_de_Gran_Canaria" title="Las Palmas de Gran Canaria">Las Palmas de Gran Canaria</a>. Operational naval units are classified by mission and assigned to either the combat forces, the protective forces, or the auxiliary forces. Combat forces are given the tasks of conducting offensive and defensive operations against potential enemies and for assuring maritime communications. Their principal vessels included two carrier groups, naval aircraft, transports, landing vessels, <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>, and missile-armed fast attack craft. Protective forces have the mission of securing maritime communications over both ocean and coastal routes, securing the approaches to ports and maritime terminals. Their principal components are <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvette" title="Corvette">corvettes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minesweeper_(ship)" title="Minesweeper (ship)">minesweepers</a>. It also has marine units for the defense of naval installations. Auxiliary forces are responsible for transportation and provisioning at sea and has diverse tasks like coast guard operations, scientific work, and maintenance of training vessels. In addition to supply ships and tankers, the force included destroyers and a large number of patrol craft.</p>
<p>Until February 2013, when it was decommissioned because of budget cuts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the second largest vessel of the <i>Armada</i> was the aircraft carrier <a href="/wiki/Spanish_aircraft_carrier_Pr%C3%ADncipe_de_Asturias" title="Spanish aircraft carrier Príncipe de Asturias"><i>Principe de Asturias</i></a>, which entered service in 1988 after completing sea trials. Built in Spain, it was designed with a "ski-jump" takeoff deck. Its complement was 29 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AV-8_Harrier_II" title="AV-8 Harrier II">AV-8 Harrier II</a> vertical (or short) takeoff and landing (V/STOL) aircraft or 16 helicopters designed for antisubmarine warfare and to support marine landings.</p>
<p>As of 2012, the Armada has a strength of 20,800 personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infanter.C3.ADa_de_Marina">Infantería de Marina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Navy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Infantería de Marina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Navy_Marines" title="Spanish Navy Marines">Spanish Navy Marines</a></div>
<p>The Infantería de Marina is the marine infantry of the Spanish Navy. It has a strength of 11,500 troops and is divided into base defense forces and landing forces. One of the three base defense battalions is stationed with each of the Navy headquarters. "Groups" (midway between battalions and regiments) are stationed in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Palmas_de_Gran_Canaria" title="Las Palmas de Gran Canaria">Las Palmas de Gran Canaria</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Tercio" title="Tercio">Tercio</a></i> (fleet — regiment equivalent) is available for immediate embarkation and based out of San Fernando. Its principal weapons include light tanks, armored personnel vehicles, self-propelled artillery, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TOW_missile" title="TOW missile">TOW</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_missile" title="Dragon missile">Dragon</a> antitank missiles.</p>

<p>As of 2016, there are approximately 78 vessels in service within the Navy, including minor auxiliary vessels. A breakdown includes; one <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_assault_ship" title="Amphibious assault ship">amphibious assault ship</a> (also used as an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a>), two <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_transport_dock" title="Amphibious transport dock">amphibious transport docks</a>, 11 <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>, six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mine_countermeasure_vessel" title="Mine countermeasure vessel">mine countermeasure vessels</a>, 23 <a href="/wiki/Patrol_boat" title="Patrol boat">patrol vessels</a> and a number of <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_ship" title="Auxiliary ship">auxiliary ships</a>. The total displacement of the Spanish Navy is approximately 220,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Navy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>The Fleet</b> (Headquarters located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rota,_C%C3%A1diz" title="Rota, Cádiz">Rota</a>)
<ul>
<li><b>Projection Group "Alfa" located at Rota</b>
<ul>
<li>1 LHD / multi-purpose warship and aircraft carrier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_ship_Juan_Carlos_I_(L61)" title="Spanish ship Juan Carlos I (L61)"><i>Juan Carlos I</i> (L61)</a>. 27,079 Tons.</li>
<li>2 LPD <a href="/wiki/Galicia-class_landing_platform_dock" title="Galicia-class landing platform dock"><i>Galicia</i>-class</a> landing platform docks. 13,818 tons.</li>
<li>1 Replenishment ship <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPS_Pati%C3%B1o_(A14)" title="SPS Patiño (A14)"><i>Patiño</i> (A14)</a> (located at Ferrol). 17,045 tons.</li>
<li>1 Replenishment ship <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPS_Cantabria_(A15)" title="SPS Cantabria (A15)"><i>Cantabria</i> (A15)</a> (located at Ferrol). 19,500 tons.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>41st Escort Squadron located at Rota</b>
<ul>
<li>6 Frigates (<a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa-class_frigate" title="Santa María-class frigate"><i>Santa María</i> class</a>). 4,017 tons.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>31st Escort Squadron located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrol,_Spain" title="Ferrol, Spain">Ferrol</a></b>
<ul>
<li>5 AEGIS Frigates (<a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Baz%C3%A1n-class_frigate" title="Álvaro de Bazán-class frigate"><i>Álvaro de Bazán</i> class</a>). 6,250 tons.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Submarine flotilla located at Cartagena</b>.
<ul>
<li>3 Submarines S-70 <i>Galerna</i> (<a href="/wiki/Agosta-class_submarine" title="Agosta-class submarine"><i>Agosta</i> class</a>). 1,740 tons.</li>
<li>4 AIP submarines (<a href="/wiki/S-80-class_submarine" title="S-80-class submarine"><i>S-80</i> class</a>). (Under construction) 2,426 tons.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>MCM flotilla located at Cartagena</b>
<ul>
<li>6 Minehunters M-30 (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Segura-class_minehunter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Segura-class minehunter (page does not exist)"><i>Segura</i> class</a>). 585 tons.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Patrol craft flotilla</b>
<ul>
<li>3 Corvettes (<a href="/wiki/Descubierta-class_corvette" title="Descubierta-class corvette"><i>Descubierta</i> class</a>). 1,666 tons.</li>
<li>7 Patrol ships (<a href="/wiki/Buque_de_Acci%C3%B3n_Mar%C3%ADtima" title="Buque de Acción Marítima"><i>Meteoro</i> class</a>) (3 ordered.) 2,500 tons.</li>
<li>4 Patrol ships (<a href="/wiki/Serviola-class_patrol_boat" title="Serviola-class patrol boat"><i>Serviola</i> class</a>). 1,276 tons.</li>
<li>3 Patrol ships (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chilreu-class_patrol_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chilreu-class patrol boat (page does not exist)"><i>Chilreu</i> class</a>) (These vessels differ in detail). c1,500 tons.</li>
<li>Various small patrol boats (being deleted as the <i>Meteoro</i>s commission).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>TOTAL Tons Main Vessels: 233,596 Tons</b></li>
</ul>
