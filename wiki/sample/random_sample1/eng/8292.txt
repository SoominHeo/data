<p><b>Spanish settlement in Argentina</b>, that is the arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> emigrants in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, took place firstly in the period before Argentina's independence from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and again in large numbers during the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Between the 15th and 19th centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> was the sole colonial power in the territories that became Argentina after the 1816 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine_declaration_of_independence" title="Argentine declaration of independence">Argentine declaration of independence</a>. Thus, before 1850, the vast majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_peoples" title="European peoples">European</a> settlers in Argentina were from Spain, and they carried the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonial</a> administration, including religious affairs, government, and commercial business. A substantial Spanish-descended <a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people">Criollo</a> population gradually built up in the new cities, while some mixed with the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Argentina" title="Indigenous peoples in Argentina">indigenous populations</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mestizos" title="Mestizos">Mestizos</a></i>), with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Argentine" title="Afro-Argentine">Black slave population</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mulattoes" title="Mulattoes">Mulattoes</a></i>), or with other European immigrants.</p>
<p>Since a great part of the immigrants to Argentina before the mid-19th century were of Spanish descent, and the fact that a significative part of the late-19th century/early-20th century immigrants to Argentina were Spaniards, the large majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine_people" title="Argentine people">Argentines</a> are of at least part Spanish ancestry. Indeed, the 20 most common surnames in Argentina are Spanish. However this prevalence, and the numerous shared cultural aspects between Argentina and Spain (the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish language</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people">Criollo</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> traditions) has been mitigated by massive <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Argentina" title="Immigration to Argentina">Immigration to Argentina</a> at the turn of the 20th century involving an overall majority of non-Spanish peoples from all over Europe. This has led to a hybrid <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Argentina" title="Culture of Argentina">Argentine culture</a> which is among the most distinct from traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_culture" title="Spanish culture">Spanish culture</a> in Latin America. Furthermore, a large proportion of Spanish immigration to Argentina during the 20th century, was from the North Western region of <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, which has a separate language and distinct culture from other parts of Spain.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Argentines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The interplay between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine" title="Argentine">Argentine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_culture" title="Spanish culture">Spanish culture</a> goes back a long way, and has historically been quite complex. Spanish settlements date back to 16th century, and from then on, many <a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people">criollo</a> Spaniards would populate the area of Argentina, with some intermarrying with non-Spaniards. <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> established a permanent colony on the site of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> in 1580, although initial settlement was primarily overland from <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. The Spanish further integrated <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> into their vast empire by establishing the Vice Royalty of Rio de la Plata in 1776, and <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> became a flourishing port. <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> would ]]become a crucial part of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.</p>
<p>The Argentine independence movement would however drastically change <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine-Spanish_relations" title="Argentine-Spanish relations">Argentine-Spanish relations</a>. The Argentine movement for independence from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> would in fact begin in the powerful city of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> on May 25, 1810, and the whole new country formally declaring independence from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> on July 9, 1816 in the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="San Miguel de Tucumán">San Miguel de Tucumán</a>. Following the defeat of the Spanish, centralist and federalist groups waged a lengthy conflict between themselves to determine the future of the nation of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. Prior to its independence, Spaniards in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> who were against the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> and desired their independence came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Argentines" title="Argentines">Argentines</a>, and those who were opposed to independence continued to be identified as <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a>. But a few generations after independence, and particularly after recent immigration, most Argentines began to see themselves as purely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine" title="Argentine">Argentine</a> out of pride in their new developing nation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_Immigration">Spanish Immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Argentines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spanish Immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>During the colonial period (1492 -1832) a total of 2.4 million Spaniards emigrated to Virreinato of Buenos Aires, forming the basis of modern-day Argentine population. In the post-colonial period (1832-1950), there would be a further influx of Spanish immigrants to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> from all over Spain during the <a href="/wiki/Great_European_immigration_wave_to_Argentina" title="Great European immigration wave to Argentina">Great European immigration wave to Argentina</a>, after the creation of the modern Argentine state. Between 1857 and 1960, more than 2.5 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish people</a> emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">Cantabria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, while significantly smaller numbers of immigrants also arrived from <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.</p>
<p>Galicians make up 70% of the Spanish post-colonial immigrant population in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. The city with the world's second largest number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galician_people" title="Galician people">Galician people</a> is <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, where immigration from <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> was so profound that today all Spaniards, regardless of their origin within <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, are referred to as <i>gallegos</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galician_people" title="Galician people">Galicians</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.</p>
<p>Roughly 10-15% of the Argentine population are descended from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people">Basque people</a>, both Spanish and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a>, and are described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_Argentine" title="Basque Argentine">Basque Argentines</a>. They gather in several <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque</a> cultural centres in most of the large cities in the country.</p>
<p>In 2013, there were 92.453 Spanish citizens born in Spain living in Argentina and another 288.494 Spanish citizens born in Argentina .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Argentines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While there continues to be strong interest among the population in European affairs and their European heritage, the Argentine culture today varies considerably from the Spanish just like the American or Australian cultures vary from the British.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Figures">Figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Argentines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yale university report says 2,080,000 Spanish immigrants entered Argentina between 1857-1940. Spain forms 31.4% (Italy 44.9%) of all immigrants in that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yale-3">[3]</a></sup> Nevertheless, due to prior Spanish immigration occurring throughout the colonial period, the modern day Argentine population is overwhelmingly Spanish in origin, with the 20 most common surnames in the country being all from Spain. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
