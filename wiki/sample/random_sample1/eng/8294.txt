<p>Archduchess <b>Maria of Austria</b> (21 June 1528 – 26 February 1603) was the spouse of <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian II, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> and King of Bohemia and Hungary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She was the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles V</a> and twice served as regent of Spain.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_of_Austria,_Holy_Roman_Empress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maria was born in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> to <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a> and King of Spain, and <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_Portugal" title="Isabella of Portugal">Isabella of Portugal</a>. She grew up mostly between <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a> with her siblings, <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Austria,_Princess_of_Portugal" title="Joanna of Austria, Princess of Portugal">Joanna</a>. They built a strong family bond despite their father's regular absences. Maria and her brother, Philip, shared similar strong personal views and policies which they kept during the rest of their lives.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Married_life">Married life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_of_Austria,_Holy_Roman_Empress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Married life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 15 September 1548, aged twenty, she married her first cousin <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian II, Holy Roman Emperor">Archduke Maximilian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The couple had sixteen children during the course of a twenty-eight-year marriage.</p>
<p>While her father was occupied with German affairs, Maria and Maximilian acted as <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_regents" title="List of Spanish regents">regents of Spain</a> from 1548 to 1551 during the absence of Prince Philip. Maria stayed at the Spanish court until August 1551,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and in 1552 the couple moved to live at the court of Maximilian's father in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. During another absence of her brother, now King Philip II, from 1558 to 1561, Maria was again regent of Spain and returned to Madrid during that time.</p>
<p>After her return to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, her husband gradually succeeded his father <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand I</a> as ruler of Germany, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a>, which he ruled from 1564 to his death in 1576. Maria was a devout Catholic and frequently disagreed with her religiously ambiguous husband. She had great influence over her sons, the future emperors <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthias,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Matthias, Holy Roman Emperor">Matthias</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Spain">Return to Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_of_Austria,_Holy_Roman_Empress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Return to Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maria returned to Spain in 1582, taking her youngest surviving child <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Margaret_of_Austria_(1567%E2%80%931633)" title="Archduchess Margaret of Austria (1567–1633)">Margaret</a> with her, promised to marry <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a>, who had lost his fourth wife <a href="/wiki/Anna_of_Austria,_Queen_of_Spain" title="Anna of Austria, Queen of Spain">Anna of Austria</a> in 1580. Margaret finally refused and took the veil as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poor_Clare" title="Poor Clare">Poor Clare</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Commenting that she was very happy to live in "a country without <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretics</a>", Maria settled in the <a href="/wiki/Convent_of_Las_Descalzas_Reales" title="Convent of Las Descalzas Reales">Convent of Las Descalzas Reales</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, where she lived until her death in 1603.</p>
<p>She was the patron of the noted Spanish composer <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Luis_de_Victoria" title="Tomás Luis de Victoria">Tomás Luis de Victoria</a>, and the great <a href="/wiki/Officium_Defunctorum" title="Officium Defunctorum">Requiem</a> Mass he wrote in 1603 for her funeral is considered among the finest and most refined of his works.</p>
<p>Maria exerted some influence together with Queen <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Austria,_Queen_of_Spain" title="Margaret of Austria, Queen of Spain">Margaret</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III of Spain</a>. Margaret, the sister of the future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Ferdinand_II" title="Emperor Ferdinand II">Emperor Ferdinand II</a>, would be one of three women at Philip's court who would apply considerable influence over the king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.91_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.91-5">[5]</a></sup> Margaret was considered by contemporaries to be extremely pious – in some cases, excessively pious, and too influenced by the Church,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.98_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.98-6">[6]</a></sup> and 'astute and very skillful' in her political dealings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> although 'melancholic' and unhappy over the influence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Lerma" title="Duke of Lerma">Duke of Lerma</a> over her husband at court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.98_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.98-6">[6]</a></sup> Margaret continued to fight an ongoing battle with Lerma for influence until her death in 1611. Philip had an 'affectionate, close relationship' with Margaret, and paid her additional attention after she bore him a son, also named <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip</a>, in 1605.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.100_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.100-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maria, the Austrian representative to the Spanish court – and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archduchess_Margaret_of_Austria_(1567-1633)" title="Archduchess Margaret of Austria (1567-1633)">Margaret of the Cross</a>, Maria's daughter – along with queen Margaret, were a powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> and pro-Austrian faction in the court of <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III of Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.91_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.91-5">[5]</a></sup> They were successful, for example, in convincing Philip to provide financial support to Ferdinand from 1600 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.100_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-S.C3.A1nchez.2C_p.100-8">[8]</a></sup> Philip steadily acquired other religious advisors. Father Juan de Santa Maria, the <a href="/wiki/Confessor" title="Confessor">confessor</a> to Philip's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Anna_of_Spain" title="Maria Anna of Spain">Maria Anna</a>, was felt by contemporaries to have an excessive influence over Philip at the end of his life, and both he and Luis de Aliaga, Philip's own confessor, were credited with the overthrow of Lerma in 1618. Similarly Mariana de San Jose, a favoured nun of Queen Margaret's, was also criticised for her later influence over the King's actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Children">Children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_of_Austria,_Holy_Roman_Empress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maria and Maximilian had sixteen children of which only five were still alive in the time of her death:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anna_of_Austria_(1549-1580)" title="Anna of Austria (1549-1580)">Anne of Austria</a> (2 November 1549 – 26 October 1580), married her uncle <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a></li>
<li>Ferdinand of Austria (28 March 1551 – 16 June 1552 Vienna)</li>
