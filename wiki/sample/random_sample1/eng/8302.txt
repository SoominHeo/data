<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sforza" title="Sforza">Sforza</a> Castle</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it"><i>Castello Sforzesco</i></span>) is a castle in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. It was built in the 15th century by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Milan" title="Duke of Milan">Duke of Milan</a>, on the remains of a 14th-century fortification. Later renovated and enlarged, in the 16th and 17th centuries it was one of the largest citadels in Europe. Extensively rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Beltrami" title="Luca Beltrami">Luca Beltrami</a> in 1891â1905, it now houses several of the city's museums and art collections.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sforza_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original construction was ordered by local lord <a href="/wiki/Galeazzo_II_Visconti" title="Galeazzo II Visconti">Galeazzo II Visconti</a> in 1358âc. 1370;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> this castle was known as <i>Castello di Porta Giova</i> (or <i>Porta Zubia</i>), from the name of a gate in walls located nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tour_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tour-2">[2]</a></sup> His successors <a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Visconti" title="Gian Galeazzo Visconti">Gian Galeazzo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Maria_Visconti" title="Giovanni Maria Visconti">Giovanni Maria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Maria_Visconti" title="Filippo Maria Visconti">Filippo Maria Visconti</a> enlarged it, until it became a square-plan castle with 200Â m-long sides, four towers at the corners and up to 7-metre-thick (23Â ft) walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tour_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tour-2">[2]</a></sup> The castle was the main residence in the city of its <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a> lords, and was destroyed by the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Golden_Ambrosian_Republic" title="Golden Ambrosian Republic">Golden Ambrosian Republic</a> which ousted them in 1447.</p>
<p>In 1450, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" title="Francesco Sforza">Francesco Sforza</a>, once he shattered the republicans, began reconstruction of the castle to turn it into his princely residence. In 1452 he hired sculptor and architect <a href="/wiki/Filarete" title="Filarete">Filarete</a> to design and decorate the central tower, which is still known as <i>Torre del Filarete</i>. After Francesco's death, the construction was continued by his son <a href="/wiki/Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza" title="Galeazzo Maria Sforza">Galeazzo Maria</a>, under architect Benedetto Ferrini. The decoration was executed by local painters. In 1476, during the regency of <a href="/wiki/Bona_of_Savoy" title="Bona of Savoy">Bona of Savoy</a>, the tower with her name was built.</p>

<p>In 1494 <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Sforza" title="Ludovico Sforza">Ludovico Sforza</a> became lord of Milan, and called numerous artists to decorate the castle. These include <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> (who frescoed several rooms, in collaboration with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernardino_Zenale" title="Bernardino Zenale">Bernardino Zenale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_Butinone" title="Bernardino Butinone">Bernardino Butinone</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bramante" title="Bramante">Bramante</a>, who painted frescoes in the <i>Sala del Tesoro</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the <i>Sala della Balla</i> was decorated with Francesco Sforza's deeds. Around 1498, Leonardo worked at the ceiling of the <i>Sala delle Asse</i>, painting decorations of vegetable motifs. In the following years, however, the castle was damaged by assaults from Italian, French and German troops; a bastion, known as <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tenaglia" title="wiktionary:tenaglia">tenaglia</a></i> was added, perhaps designed by <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Cesariano" title="Cesare Cesariano">Cesare Cesariano</a>. After the French victory in the 1515 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marignano" title="Battle of Marignano">Battle of Marignano</a>, the defeated <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_Sforza" title="Maximilian Sforza">Maximilian Sforza</a>, his Swiss mercenaries, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Sion" title="Bishop of Sion">bishop of Sion</a> retreated into the castle. However, King <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a> followed them into Milan, and his <a href="/wiki/Sapper" title="Sapper">sappers</a> placed mines under the castle's foundations, whereupon the defenders capitulated. In 1521, in a period in which it was used as a weapons depot, the Torre del Filarete exploded. When <a href="/wiki/Francesco_II_Sforza" title="Francesco II Sforza">Francesco II Sforza</a> returned briefly to power in Milan, he had the fortress restored and enlarged, and a part of it adapted as residence for his wife, <a href="/wiki/Christina_of_Denmark" title="Christina of Denmark">Christina of Denmark</a>.</p>

</div>
<p>Under the Spanish domination which followed, the castle became a <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">citadel</a>, as the governor's seat was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_Milan" title="Royal Palace of Milan">Ducal Palace</a> (1535). Its garrison varied from 1,000 to 3,000 men, led by a Spanish <a href="/wiki/Castellan" title="Castellan">castellan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tour_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tour-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1550 works began to adapt the castle to modern fortification style, as an hexagon (originally pentagon)-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_fort" title="Star fort">star fort</a>, following the addition of 12 bastions. The external fortifications reached 3Â km in length and covered an area of 25.9 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCI438_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCI438-4">[4]</a></sup> The castle remained in use as a fort also after the Spaniards were replaced by the Austrians in Lombardy.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99,_d.3_-_(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="845" data-file-width="1172" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99%2C_d.3_-_%281%29.jpg/220px-Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99%2C_d.3_-_%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99%2C_d.3_-_%281%29.jpg/330px-Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99%2C_d.3_-_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99%2C_d.3_-_%281%29.jpg/440px-Journal_de_Jean%E2%80%93Claude_Locquin_-_Archives_Nationales_-_607AP-99%2C_d.3_-_%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Most of the outer fortifications were demolished during the period of Napoleonic rule in Milan under the <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Republic" title="Cisalpine Republic">Cisalpine Republic</a>. The semi-circular Piazza Castello was constructed around the city side of the castle, surrounded by a radial street layout of new urban blocks bounded by the Foro Buonoparte. The area on the "country" side of the castle was laid out as a 700-by-700-metre (2,300 by 2,300Â ft) square parade ground known as Piazza d'Armi.</p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unification_of_Italy" title="Unification of Italy">unification of Italy</a> in the 19th century, the castle was transferred from military use to the city of Milan. <a href="/wiki/Parco_Sempione" title="Parco Sempione">Parco Sempione</a>, one of the largest parks in the city, was created on the former parade grounds.</p>
<p>The government of Milan undertook restoration works, directed by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Beltrami" title="Luca Beltrami">Luca Beltrami</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Via_Dante" title="Via Dante">Via Dante</a> was cut through the medieval street layout in the 1880s to provide a direct promenade between the castle and <a href="/wiki/Milan_Cathedral" title="Milan Cathedral">the Duomo</a> on axis with the main gate. The central tower, known as the Torre Filarete, above the main city entrance was rebuilt, on the basis of 16th-century drawings, between 1900 and 1905 as a monument to King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umberto_I" title="Umberto I">Umberto I</a>.</p>
<p>Allied bombardment of Milan in 1943 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> severely damaged the castle. The post-war reconstruction of the building for museum purposes was undertaken by the <a href="/wiki/BBPR" title="BBPR">BBPR</a> architectural partnership.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sforza_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The castle has a quadrangular plan, site across the city's walls. The wall which once faced the countryside north to Milan has square towers and has an ogival gate. This was once accessed through a <a href="/wiki/Drawbridge" title="Drawbridge">drawbridge</a>. The northern tower is known as <i>Torre della Corte</i>, while the western one is called ''<i>Torre del <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tesoro" title="wiktionary:tesoro">Tesoro</a></i>: both received wide windows during the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Sforza" title="House of Sforza">Sforza</a> age.</p>
<p>The corner defended by the <i>Torre Ducale</i> is characterized by a <a href="/wiki/Loggia" title="Loggia">loggia</a> bridge, attributed to Bramante, and commissioned by Ludovico Sforza in the late 15th century to connect the <i>Corte Ducale</i> (the court in the area used as ducal residence) and the <i>Cortile della Ghirlanda</i>. This <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ghirlanda" title="wiktionary:ghirlanda">ghirlanda</a></i> refers to a wall, protected by a ditch filled by water, built under Francesco Sforza, of which few traces remain today, including the <i>Porta del Soccorso</i>. Remains of two later <a href="/wiki/Ravelin" title="Ravelin">ravelins</a> can be seen in correspondence of the point in which the castle was joined by the city walls (near the <i>Porta Comasina</i> gate) and the <i>Porta del Carmine</i>. The <i>Porta della Ghirlanda</i> gate was entered through a ravelin (now lost) and had two entrances accessed through runways, on which lead to an underground passage which continued along the walls.</p>
<p>The external side which once faced the walled city has two round towers, commissioned by Francesco Sforza to replace the former square ones, which had become less suitable to defend against fire weapons. The central tower, called <i>Torre del Filarete</i>, is a modern reconstruction. The round towers lost the upper parts under the Austrians, which needed open space for their artillery. Their upper sector existing today is also a modern restoration. The <i>Torre del Filarete</i> and the <i>Porta del Santo Spirito</i>, located more southwards, are both preceded by a ravelin.</p>
<p>The main gate leads to a large court from which several internal features can be seen. These include the Tower of Bona of Savoy (1476) and the <i>Rocchetta</i>, a sort of internal defensive <a href="/wiki/Ridotto" title="Ridotto">ridotto</a> with a gate of its own. At the right of the <i>Porta del Carmine</i> are remains of two 15th century courts. The <i>Rocchetta</i>, whose access gate from the main court (a modern addition) features the coat of arms of the Sforza, has an internal courts with, on three sides, a <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">portico</a> with 15th century arcades. The <i>Corte Ducale</i> is the wing of the castle originally used as ducal residence; it features a court with two <a href="/wiki/Loggia" title="Loggia">loggias</a>, a smaller one at left and a larger one at its end, called <i>Loggiato dell'Elefante</i> due to the presence of a fresco of an elephant.</p>

<p>The Castello complex includes some museums:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Sforza_Castle_Pinacoteca" title="Sforza Castle Pinacoteca">Pinacoteca del Castello Sforzesco</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with an art collection which includes <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Andrea Mantegna</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Trivulzio_Madonna" title="Trivulzio Madonna">Trivulzio Madonna</a></i> and masterpieces by <a href="/wiki/Canaletto" title="Canaletto">Canaletto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Tiepolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Foppa" title="Vincenzo Foppa">Vincenzo Foppa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>.</li>

<li>The Achille Bertarelli Print Collection.</li>
<li>The Museum of Rondanini PietÃ  which includes <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>'s last sculpture (the <i><a href="/wiki/Rondanini_Piet%C3%A0" title="Rondanini PietÃ ">Rondanini PietÃ </a></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The Trivulziana Library holds <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>'s '<a href="/wiki/Codex_Trivulzianus" title="Codex Trivulzianus">Codex Trivulzianus</a>' manuscript. Also in 2012 new paintings attributable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Merisi_da_Caravaggio" title="Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio">Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio</a> have been discovered at the castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
