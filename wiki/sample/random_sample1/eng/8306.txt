<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>spork</b> is a hybrid form of <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">cutlery</a> taking the form of a <a href="/wiki/Spoon" title="Spoon">spoon</a>-like shallow scoop with two to four <a href="/wiki/Fork" title="Fork">fork</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tine" title="wikt:tine">tines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Spork-like utensils, such as the terrapin fork or ice cream fork,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> have been manufactured since the late 19th century;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> for spork-like designs date back to at least 1874, and the word "spork" was registered as a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> in the US and the UK decades later. They are used by fast food restaurants, schools, prisons, the military, backpackers and in <a href="/wiki/Airline_meal" title="Airline meal">airline meals</a>.</p>
<p>The word <i>spork</i> combines <i>spoon</i> and <i>fork</i>. Similarly, the word <i>foon</i> is a blend of <i>fork</i> and <i>spoon</i>. The word <i>"spork"</i> appeared in the 1909 supplement to the <i><a href="/wiki/Century_Dictionary" title="Century Dictionary">Century Dictionary</a>,</i> where it was described as a <a href="/wiki/Trade_name" title="Trade name">trade name</a> and "a '<a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a>-word' applied to a long, slender spoon having, at the end of the bowl, projections resembling the tines of a fork".</p>

<p>In the US, patents for sporks and proto-sporks have been issued. A combined spoon, fork, and knife closely resembling the modern spork was invented by Samuel W. Francis and issued <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US147119" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 147,119</a></span> in February 1874.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Other early patents predating the modern spork include <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US904553" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 904,553</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> for a "cutting spoon", granted on November 24, 1908 to Harry L. McCoy and <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US1044869" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 1,044,869</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> for a spoon with a tined edge, granted to Frank Emmenegger in November 1912. Many of these inventions predated the use of the term "spork". Given this significant prior art, the basic concept of combining aspects of a spoon and fork is well established; more modern patents have limited themselves to the specific implementation and appearance of the spork. These <a href="/wiki/Design_patent" title="Design patent">design patents</a> do not prevent anyone from designing and manufacturing a different version of a spork. Examples of modern US design patents for sporks include patent number D247,153 issued in February 1978<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and patent D388,664 issued in January 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>word</i> spork <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">originated</a> in the early 20th century to describe such devices. In 1951, Hyde W. Ballard of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westtown_Township,_Pennsylvania" title="Westtown Township, Pennsylvania">Westtown, Pennsylvania</a> filed an application with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_Office" title="United States Patent Office">United States Patent Office</a> (USPO; now the United States Patent and Trademark Office) to register "Spork" as a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> for a combination spoon and fork made of stainless steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Van Brode Milling Company subsequently registered SPORK for a combination plastic spoon, fork and knife at the USPO on October 27, 1970, but the registration expired 20 years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The word Spork accompanied by a stylised design is registered in the U.S. in relation to hand tools, in the name of a UK-based individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK, Plastico Limited registered Spork as a trademark in relation to cutlery with effect from September 18, 1975 (reg. no. 1052291). The registration is now in the name of another company and remains in force. The trademark is also registered in the UK in relation to gardening tools in the name of the same UK based individual who owns US trademark registration no. 2514381. Another British company, Lifeventure, sells <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> and plastic versions using the name "Forkspoon".</p>
<p>In an unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> in 1999 where the company Regalzone sought to invalidate Plastico Limited's UK registration for Spork, Justice Neuberger wrote:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I accept that the word <i>Spork</i> involves a clever idea of making a single word by eliding beginning of the word spoon and the end of the word fork. The fact that it is clever and the fact that the meaning of <i>Spork</i> could be said to be obvious once it is explained does not mean that it is obvious what it is. Indeed, I would have thought that if one asked a person in 1975 what a Spork was, he or she would not know. If one then explained what it was and how the word came about, one might then be told that it was obvious or that it was clever.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_and_uses">Materials and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Materials and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CodazziSugakiya1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="398" data-file-width="800" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/CodazziSugakiya1.jpg/200px-CodazziSugakiya1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/CodazziSugakiya1.jpg/300px-CodazziSugakiya1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/CodazziSugakiya1.jpg/400px-CodazziSugakiya1.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Materials such as <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate plastic</a> have been used in spork manufacture. Plastic sporks are common in prisons in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> because they are difficult to form into <a href="/wiki/Shiv_(weapon)" title="Shiv (weapon)">shiv</a>-type <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Prepackaged meals may come with a disposable plastic spork. Sporks are also frequently used by <a href="/wiki/Backpacking_(wilderness)" title="Backpacking (wilderness)">backpackers</a> and other outdoorsmen as they are a lightweight and space-saving alternative to carrying both a fork and spoon.</p>
