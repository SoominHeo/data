<p><i><b>Sputnik 1</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Спутник-1</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Russian" title="Help:IPA for Russian">[ˈsputnʲɪk]</a></span> "Satellite-1", or ПС-1 ["PS-1", i.e., <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Простейший Спутник-1</span>, <i>Prosteyshiy Sputnik-1</i>, "Elementary Satellite 1"])<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> was the first <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">artificial Earth satellite</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> launched it into an elliptical <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> on 4 October 1957. It was a 58 cm (23 in) diameter polished metal sphere, with four external radio antennae to broadcast radio pulses. It was visible all around the Earth and its radio pulses were detectable. This surprise success precipitated the American <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_crisis" title="Sputnik crisis">Sputnik crisis</a> and triggered the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>, a part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. The launch ushered in new political, military, technological, and scientific developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tracking and studying <i>Sputnik 1</i> from Earth provided scientists with valuable information, even though the satellite itself wasn't equipped with sensors. The density of the upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere#Temperature_and_the_atmospheric_layers" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> could be deduced from its <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">drag</a> on the orbit, and the propagation of its radio signals gave information about the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>.</p>
<p><i>Sputnik 1</i> was launched during the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a> from <a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin's Start">Site No.1/5</a>, at the 5th <a href="/wiki/Tyuratam" title="Tyuratam">Tyuratam</a> range, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kazakh_SSR" title="Kazakh SSR">Kazakh SSR</a> (now known as the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>). The satellite travelled at about 29,000 kilometres per hour (18,000 mph; 8,100 m/s), taking 96.2 minutes to complete each orbit. It transmitted on 20.005 and 40.002 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHz" title="MHz">MHz</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WJJorden_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-WJJorden-8">[8]</a></sup> which were monitored by <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> operators throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The signals continued for 21 days until the transmitter batteries ran out on 26 October 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vibdata_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-vibdata-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Sputnik</i> burned up on 4 January 1958 whilst reentering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, after travelling about 70 million km (43 million mi) and spending three months in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_the_launch">Before the launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Before the launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_construction_project">Satellite construction project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Satellite construction project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 December 1954, chief Soviet rocket scientist <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a> addressed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimitri_Ustinov" title="Dimitri Ustinov">Dimitri Ustinov</a> and proposed a developmental plan for an artificial satellite. Korolev forwarded a report by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikhail_Klavdievich_Tikhonravov" title="Mikhail Klavdievich Tikhonravov">Mikhail Tikhonravov</a> with an overview of similar projects abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Tikhonravov had emphasized that the launch of an orbital satellite was an inevitable stage in the development of rocket technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZE_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZE-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 July 1955, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> announced through his press secretary that the United States would launch an artificial satellite during the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a> (IGY).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A week later, on 8 August, the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Politburo of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> approved the proposal to create an artificial satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On 30 August <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vasily_Ryabikov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vasily Ryabikov (page does not exist)">Vasily Ryabikov</a> – the head of the State Commission on <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 rocket</a> test launches – held a meeting where Korolev presented calculation data for a spaceflight trajectory to the Moon. They decided to develop a three-stage version of the R-7 rocket for satellite launches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 30 January 1956 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Soviet_Union)" title="Council of Ministers (Soviet Union)">Council of Ministers</a> approved practical work on an artificial Earth-orbiting satellite. This satellite, named <i>Object D</i>, was planned to be completed in 1957–58; it would have a mass of 1,000 to 1,400 kg (2,200 to 3,100 lb) and would carry 200 to 300 kg (440 to 660 lb) of scientific instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The first test launch of "Object D" was scheduled for 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZE_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZE-12">[12]</a></sup> Work on the satellite was to be divided between institutions as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NL_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NL-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR_Academy_of_Sciences" title="USSR Academy of Sciences">USSR Academy of Sciences</a> was responsible for the general scientific leadership and research instruments supply</li>
<li>the Ministry of Defense Industry and its primary design bureau <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-1" title="OKB-1">OKB-1</a> were assigned the task of building the satellite</li>
<li>the Ministry of Radiotechnical Industry would develop the control system, radio/technical instruments and the <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> system</li>
<li>the Ministry of Ship Building Industry would develop <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscope</a> devices</li>
<li>the Ministry of Machine Building would develop ground launching, refueling and transportation means</li>
<li>the Ministry of Defense was responsible for conducting launches</li>
</ul>
<p>Preliminary design work was completed by July 1956 and the scientific tasks to be carried out by the satellite were defined. These included measuring the density of the atmosphere and its <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> composition, the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. These data would be valuable in the creation of future artificial satellites. A system of ground stations was to be developed to collect data transmitted by the satellite, observe the satellite's orbit, and transmit commands to the satellite. Because of the limited time frame, observations were planned for only 7 to 10 days and orbit calculations were not expected to be extremely accurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of 1956 it became clear that the complexity of the ambitious design meant that 'Object D' could not be launched in time because of difficulties creating scientific instruments and the low <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> produced by the completed R-7 engines (304 sec instead of the planned 309 to 310 sec). Consequently, the government rescheduled the launch for April 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZE_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ZE-12">[12]</a></sup> Object D would later fly as <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnik_3" title="Sputnik 3">Sputnik 3</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fearing the U.S. would launch a satellite before the USSR, OKB-1 suggested the creation and launch of a satellite in April–May 1957, before the IGY began in July 1957. The new satellite would be simple, light (100 kg or 220 lb), and easy to construct, forgoing the complex, heavy scientific equipment in favour of a simple radio transmitter. On 15 February 1957 the Council of Ministers of the USSR approved this simple satellite, designated 'Object PS'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1957sat_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-1957sat-20">[20]</a></sup> This version allowed the satellite to be tracked visually by Earth-based observers, and it could transmit tracking signals to ground-based receiving stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1957sat_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-1957sat-20">[20]</a></sup> The launch of two satellites, PS-1 and PS-2, with two R-7 rockets (8K71) was approved, provided that the R-7 completed at least two successful test flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1957sat_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-1957sat-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_vehicle_preparation_and_launch_site_selection">Launch vehicle preparation and launch site selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch vehicle preparation and launch site selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_(rocket)" title="Sputnik (rocket)">Sputnik (rocket)</a> and <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a> was initially designed as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBM</a> by OKB-1. The decision to build it was made by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> and the Council of Ministers of the USSR on 20 May 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The R-7 was also known by its GRAU (later GURVO) designation 8K71.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> At the time, the R-7 was known to NATO sources as the T-3 or M-104,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoxStoiko69_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoxStoiko69-23">[23]</a></sup> and Type A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A special <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> commission selected Tyuratam for the construction of a rocket <a href="/wiki/Proving_ground" title="Proving ground">proving ground</a> (the 5th Tyuratam range, usually referred to as "NIIP-5", or "GIK-5" in the post-Soviet time). The selection was approved on 12 February 1955 by the Council of Ministers of the USSR, but the site would not be completed until 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Actual work on the construction of the site began on 20 July by military building units. On 14 June 1956 Sergei Korolev decided to adapt the R-7 rocket to the 'Object D',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> that would later be replaced by the much lighter 'Object PS'.</p>
<p>The first launch of an R-7 rocket (8K71 No.5L) occurred on 15 May 1957. A fire began in the Blok D strap-on almost immediately at liftoff, but the booster continued flying until T+98 seconds when the strap-on broke away and the vehicle crashed some 400 km downrange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR7_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-AR7-27">[27]</a></sup> Three attempts to launch the second rocket (8K71 No.6) were made on 10–11 June, but an assembly defect prevented launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The unsuccessful launch of the third R-7 rocket (8K71 No.7) took place on 12 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR7_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-AR7-27">[27]</a></sup> An electrical short caused the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernier_engine" title="Vernier engine">vernier engines</a> to put the missile into an uncontrolled roll which resulted in all of the strap-ons separating 33 seconds into the launch. The R-7 crashed about 7 km from the pad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RR7_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-RR7-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The launch of the fourth rocket (8K71 No.8), on 21 August at 15:25 <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Time" title="Moscow Time">Moscow Time</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR7_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-AR7-27">[27]</a></sup> was successful. The rocket's core boosted the dummy warhead to the target altitude and velocity, reentered the atmosphere, and broke apart at a height of 10 km (6.2 mi) after traveling 6,000 km. On 27 August <a href="/wiki/Telegraph_Agency_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Telegraph Agency of the Soviet Union">TASS</a> issued a statement on the successful launch of a long-distance multistage ICBM. The launch of the fifth R-7 rocket (8K71 No.9), on 7 September<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR7_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-AR7-27">[27]</a></sup> was also successful, but the dummy was also destroyed on atmospheric re-entry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RR7_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-RR7-29">[29]</a></sup> and hence needed a redesign to completely fulfill its military purpose. The rocket, however, was deemed suitable for satellite launches, and Korolev was able to convince the State Commission to allow the use of the next R-7 to launch PS-1,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> allowing the delay in the rocket's military exploitation to launch the PS-1 and PS-2 satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 September a modified R-7 rocket, named <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_(rocket)" title="Sputnik (rocket)">Sputnik</a> and indexed as 8K71PS,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi.2C_p._163_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi.2C_p._163-33">[33]</a></sup> arrived at the proving ground and preparations for the launch of PS-1 began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Compared to the military R-7 test vehicles, the mass of 8K71PS was reduced from 280 tonnes to 272 tonnes; its length with PS-1 was 29.167 metres (95 ft 8.3 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> at lift off was 3.90 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">MN</a> (880,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force" title="Pound-force">lb<sub>f</sub></a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> These weight reductions were accomplished by deleting the inertial guidance system, several telemetry measurements, and assorted hardware designed to support a warhead.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_complex">Observation complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Observation complex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PS-1 was not designed to be controlled, it could only be observed. Initial data at the launch site would be collected at six separate observatories and telegraphed to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NII-4&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NII-4 (page does not exist)">NII-4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup> Located back in Moscow (at <a href="/wiki/Yubileyny,_Moscow_Oblast" title="Yubileyny, Moscow Oblast">Bolshevo</a>), NII-4 was a scientific research arm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(Soviet_Union)" title="Ministry of Defence (Soviet Union)">Ministry of Defence</a> dedicated to missile development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi39_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi39-36">[36]</a></sup> The six observatories, designated IP-1 through IP-6, were clustered around the launch site, with the closest (IP-1) situated at a distance of 1 km (0.62 mi) from the launch pad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second, nationwide observation complex was established to track the satellite after its separation from the rocket. Called the Command-Measurement Complex, it consisted of the coordination center in NII-4 and seven distant stations situated along the line of the satellite's <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">ground track</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi162_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi162-37">[37]</a></sup> These tracking stations were located at <a href="/wiki/Tyuratam" title="Tyuratam">Tyuratam</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sary-Shagan" title="Sary-Shagan">Sary-Shagan</a>; <a href="/wiki/Yeniseysk" title="Yeniseysk">Yeniseysk</a>; <a href="/wiki/Klyuchi,_Kamchatka_Krai" title="Klyuchi, Kamchatka Krai">Klyuchi</a>; <a href="/wiki/Yelizovo" title="Yelizovo">Yelizovo</a>; <a href="/wiki/Makat" title="Makat">Makat</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atyrau_Province" title="Atyrau Province">Guryev Oblast</a>; and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ishkup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ishkup (page does not exist)">Ishkup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Krasnoyarsk_Krai" title="Krasnoyarsk Krai">Krasnoyarsk Krai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi162_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi162-37">[37]</a></sup> Stations were equipped with <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, optical instruments, and communications systems. Data from stations were transmitted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraphs</a> into NII-4 where <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a> specialists calculated orbital parameters. The complex became an early prototype of the Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control_Center" title="Mission Control Center">Mission Control Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The observatories used a trajectory measurement system called "Tral," developed by <a href="/wiki/OKB" title="OKB">OKB</a> MEI, by which they received and monitored data from <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponders</a> mounted on the R-7 rocket's core stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The data was useful even after the satellite's separation from the second stage of the rocket; <i>Sputnik</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> location was calculated from the data on the second stage's location which followed <i>Sputnik</i> at a known distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Tracking of the booster during launch had to be accomplished through purely passive means such as visual coverage and radar detection. R-7 test launches demonstrated that the tracking cameras were only good up to an altitude of 200 km (120 mi) but radar could track it for almost 400 km (250 mi).</p>
<p>Outside the Soviet Union, the satellite was tracked by <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> operators in many countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovell196_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lovell196-41">[41]</a></sup> The US government followed it from the Central Radio Propagation Laboratory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" title="National Bureau of Standards">National Bureau of Standards</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and the booster rocket was located and tracked by the <a href="/wiki/Lovell_Telescope" title="Lovell Telescope">Lovell Telescope</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank_Observatory" title="Jodrell Bank Observatory">Jodrell Bank Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovell196_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lovell196-41">[41]</a></sup> Canada's <a href="/wiki/Newbrook_Observatory" title="Newbrook Observatory">Newbrook Observatory</a> was the first facility in North America to photograph <i>Sputnik 1</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sputnik_asm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="896" data-file-width="1094" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Sputnik_asm.jpg/220px-Sputnik_asm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Sputnik_asm.jpg/330px-Sputnik_asm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Sputnik_asm.jpg/440px-Sputnik_asm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

A replica of <i>Sputnik 1</i> at the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a></div>

<p>The chief constructor of <i>Sputnik 1</i> at OKB-1 was Mikhail S. Khomyakov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NK2002_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-NK2002-44">[44]</a></sup> The satellite was a 585-millimetre (23.0 in) diameter sphere, assembled from two hemispheres that were <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_seal" title="Hermetic seal">hermetically</a> sealed with <a href="/wiki/O-ring" title="O-ring">o-rings</a> and connected by 36 <a href="/wiki/Bolted_joint" title="Bolted joint">bolts</a>. It had a mass of 83.6 kilograms (184 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The hemispheres were 2 mm thick,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and were covered with a highly polished 1 mm-thick <a href="/wiki/Heat_shield" title="Heat shield">heat shield</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NK97_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NK97-47">[47]</a></sup> made of aluminium-<a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMG6T" title="AMG6T">AMG6T</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> ("AMG" is an abbreviation for "aluminium-magnesium" and "T" stands for "titanium"; the alloy is 6% magnesium and 0.2% titanium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>). The satellite carried two pairs of antennas designed by the Antenna Laboratory of OKB-1 led by Mikhail V. Krayushkin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NL_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NL-17">[17]</a></sup> Each antenna was made up of two whip-like parts: 2.4 and 2.9 meters (7.9 and 9.5 ft) in length,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and had an almost spherical <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pattern" title="Radiation pattern">radiation pattern</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS-50">[50]</a></sup> so that the satellite beeps were transmitted with equal power in all directions, making reception of the transmitted signal independent of the satellite's rotation.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Power_supply" title="Power supply">power supply</a>, with a mass of 51 kg (112 lb),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhel_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhel-51">[51]</a></sup> was in the shape of an octagonal <a href="/wiki/Nut_(hardware)" title="Nut (hardware)">nut</a> with the radio transmitter in its hole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> It consisted of three <a href="/wiki/Silver-oxide_battery" title="Silver-oxide battery">silver-zinc batteries</a>, developed at the All-Union Research Institute of Current Sources (VNIIT) under the leadership of Nikolai S. Lidorenko. Two of these batteries powered the radio transmitter and one powered the temperature regulation system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhel_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zhel-51">[51]</a></sup> The batteries had an expected lifetime of two weeks, and operated for 22 days. The power supply was turned on automatically at the moment of the satellite's separation from the second stage of the rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>The satellite had a one-watt, 3.5 kg (7.7 lb)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup> radio transmitting unit inside, developed by Vyacheslav I. Lappo from <i>NII-885,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-53">[53]</a></sup> that worked on two frequencies, 20.005 and 40.002 MHz. Signals on the first frequency were transmitted in 0.3 sec pulses (under normal temperature and pressure conditions on-board), with pauses of the same duration filled by pulses on the second frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Analysis of the radio signals was used to gather information about the electron density of the ionosphere. Temperature and pressure were encoded in the duration of radio beeps. A temperature regulation system contained a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fan_(mechanical)" title="Fan (mechanical)">fan</a>, a dual <a href="/wiki/Thermal_cutoff" title="Thermal cutoff">thermal switch</a>, and a control thermal switch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-53">[53]</a></sup> If the temperature inside the satellite exceeded 36 °C (97 °F) the fan was turned on and when it fell below 20 °C (68 °F) the fan was turned off by the dual thermal switch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-RS-50">[50]</a></sup> If the temperature exceeded 50 °C (122 °F) or fell below 0 °C (32 °F), another control thermal switch was activated, changing the duration of the radio signal pulses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-53">[53]</a></sup> <i>Sputnik 1</i> was filled with dry <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, pressurized to 1.3 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi.2C_p._163_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi.2C_p._163-33">[33]</a></sup> The satellite had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric" title="Barometric">barometric</a> switch, activated if the pressure inside the satellite fell below 130 kPa, which would have indicated failure of the pressure vessel or puncture by a meteor, and would have changed the duration of radio signal impulse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-AR-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>While attached to the rocket, <i>Sputnik 1</i> was protected by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cone_(geometry)" title="Cone (geometry)">cone</a>-shaped <a href="/wiki/Payload_fairing" title="Payload fairing">payload fairing</a>, with a height of 80 cm (31.5 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-5"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup> The fairing separated from both <i>Sputnik</i> and the spent R-7 second stage at the same time as the satellite was ejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_53-4"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-53">[53]</a></sup> Tests of the satellite were conducted at OKB-1 under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Oleg_Ivanovsky" title="Oleg Ivanovsky">Oleg G. Ivanovsky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NK2002_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-NK2002-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_and_mission">Launch and mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Launch and mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The control system of the Sputnik rocket was adjusted to an intended orbit of 223 by 1,450 km (139 by 901 mi), with an orbital period of 101.5 min.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MainR_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-MainR-56">[56]</a></sup> The trajectory had been calculated earlier by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgi_Grechko" title="Georgi Grechko">Georgi Grechko</a>, using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR_Academy_of_Sciences" title="USSR Academy of Sciences">USSR Academy of Sciences</a>' <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe computer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-6"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="1000" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg/200px-Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg/300px-Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg/400px-Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dawn_of_the_Space_Age.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Artist's impression of <i>Sputnik 1</i> in orbit</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Sputnik rocket was launched on 4 October 1957 at 19:28:34 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> (5 October at the launch site<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rswSM_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-rswSM-1">[1]</a></sup>) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Site_No.1" title="Site No.1">Site No.1</a> at NIIP-5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Telemetry indicated that the strap-ons separated 116 seconds into the flight and the core stage engine shut down 295.4 seconds into the flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MainR_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-MainR-56">[56]</a></sup> At shut down, the 7.5 tonne core stage with PS-1 attached had attained an altitude of 223 km (139 mi) above sea level, a velocity of 7,780 m/s (25,500 ft/s) and velocity vector inclination to the local horizon of 0 degrees 24 minutes. This resulted in an initial orbit of 223 kilometres (139 mi) by 950 kilometres (590 mi), with an apogee approximately 500 kilometres (310 mi) lower than intended, and an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclination</a> of 65.1 degrees and a period of 96.2 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MainR_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-MainR-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The launch came very close to failure—a postflight examination of telemetry data found that the Blok G strap-on had not attained full power at ignition and the resulting imbalanced thrust caused the booster to pitch over about 2° six seconds after liftoff. Two seconds later, the flight control system tried to compensate by rapidly moving the vernier engines and stabilizer fins. The Blok G strap-on finally reached 100% thrust only one second before the pitch angle would have been sufficient enough to trigger an automatic shutdown command, which would have terminated the launch and sent the R-7 and <i>Sputnik 1</i> crashing to the ground in a fireball only a short distance from the pad.</p>
<p>A fuel regulator in the booster also failed around 16 seconds into launch, which resulted in excessive RP-1 consumption for most of powered flight and engine thrust 4% above nominal. Core stage cutoff was intended for T+296 seconds, but the premature propellant depletion caused thrust termination to occur one second earlier when a sensor detected overspeed of the empty RP-1 turbopump. There were 375 kilograms (827 lb) of LOX remaining at cutoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 19.9 seconds after engine cut-off, PS-1 separated from the second stage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rswSM_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-rswSM-1">[1]</a></sup> and the satellite's transmitter was activated. These signals were detected at the IP-1 station by Junior Engineer-Lieutenant V.G. Borisov, where reception of <i>Sputnik 1</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> "beep-beep-beep" tones confirmed the satellite's successful deployment. Reception lasted for two minutes, until PS-1 fell below the horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_31-7"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The Tral telemetry system on the R-7 core stage continued to transmit and was detected on its second orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rswSM_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-rswSM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The designers, engineers and technicians who developed the rocket and satellite watched the launch from the range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VR_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-VR-61">[61]</a></sup> After the launch they drove to the mobile radio station to listen for signals from the satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VR_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-VR-61">[61]</a></sup> They waited about 90 minutes to ensure that the satellite had made one orbit and was transmitting, before Korolev called Soviet premier <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redmoonPN_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-redmoonPN-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the first orbit the <a href="/wiki/Telegraph_Agency_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Telegraph Agency of the Soviet Union">Telegraph Agency of the Soviet Union</a> (TASS) transmitted: "As result of great, intense work of scientific institutes and design bureaus the first artificial Earth satellite has been built".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The R-7 core stage, with a mass of 7.5 tonnes and a length of 26 meters, also reached Earth orbit and was visible from the ground at night as a <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">first magnitude</a> object following the satellite. Deployable reflective panels were placed on the booster in order to increase its visibility for tracking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redmoonPN_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-redmoonPN-62">[62]</a></sup> The satellite itself, a small, highly polished sphere, was barely visible at sixth magnitude, and thus more difficult to follow optically. A third object, the payload fairing, also achieved orbit.</p>
<p>The core stage of the R-7 remained in orbit for two months until 2 December 1957, while <i>Sputnik 1</i> orbited until 4 January 1958, having completed 1,440 orbits of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rswSM_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-rswSM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_crisis" title="Sputnik crisis">Sputnik crisis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a></div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Our movies and television programs in the fifties were full of the idea of going into space. What came as a surprise was that it was the Soviet Union that launched the first satellite. It is hard to recall the atmosphere of the time.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/John_Logsdon" title="John Logsdon">John Logsdon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The Soviets provided details of <i>Sputnik 1</i> before the launch but few outside the Soviet Union noticed. After reviewing information publicly available before the launch, the science writer <a href="/wiki/Willy_Ley" title="Willy Ley">Willy Ley</a> wrote in 1958:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If somebody tells me that he has the rockets to shoot — which we know from other sources, anyway — and tells me what he will shoot, how he will shoot it, and in general says virtually everything except for the precise date — well, what should I feel like if I'm surprised when the man shoots?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley195811_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley195811-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Teams of visual observers at 150 stations in the United States and other countries were alerted during the night to watch for the Soviet sphere at dawn and during the evening twilight. They had been organized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Moonwatch" title="Project Moonwatch">Project Moonwatch</a> to sight the satellite through binoculars or telescopes as it passed overhead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-CR-66">[66]</a></sup> The USSR asked radio amateurs and commercial stations to record the sound of the satellite on <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-CR-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

Listeners were both thrilled and terrified to hear <i>Sputnik 1</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> steady beep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>News reports at the time pointed out that "anyone possessing a short wave receiver can hear the new Russian earth satellite as it hurtles over this area of the globe".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:MOSQUOTE" title="Wikipedia:MOSQUOTE"><span title="The text near this tag needs citation. (December 2015)">this quote needs a citation</span></a></i>]</sup> Directions, provided by the <a href="/wiki/American_Radio_Relay_League" title="American Radio Relay League">American Radio Relay League</a> were to "Tune in 20 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">megacycles</a> sharply, by the time signals, given on that frequency. Then tune to slightly higher frequencies. The 'beep, beep' sound of the satellite can be heard each time it rounds the globe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The first recording of <i>Sputnik 1</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> signal was made by <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">RCA</a> engineers near Riverhead, Long Island. They then drove the tape recording into Manhattan for broadcast to the public over <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> radio. However, as <i>Sputnik</i> rose higher over the East Coast, its signal was picked up by ham station W2AEE, the ham radio station of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>. Students working in the university's FM station, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WKCR" title="WKCR">WKCR</a>, made a tape of this, and were the first to rebroadcast the <i>Sputnik</i> signal to the American public (or whoever could receive the FM station).</p>
<p>At first the Soviet Union agreed to use equipment "compatible" with that of the United States, but later announced the lower frequencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-CR-66">[66]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> declined to comment on military aspects of the launch, but said "it did not come as a surprise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> On 5 October the Naval Research Laboratory announced it had recorded four crossings of <i>Sputnik 1</i> over the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR_66-3"><a href="#cite_note-CR-66">[66]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Cambridge_Research_Center" title="Air Force Cambridge Research Center">USAF Cambridge Research Center</a> collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Bendix_Aviation" title="Bendix Aviation">Bendix-Friez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Broadcasting" title="Westinghouse Broadcasting">Westinghouse Broadcasting Co.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_M.I.T._Computation_Center_and_Operation_Moonwatch" title="The M.I.T. Computation Center and Operation Moonwatch">MIT</a>, to obtain a motion picture of <i>Sputnik</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> rocket body crossing the pre-dawn sky of Baltimore, broadcast on 12 October by <a href="/wiki/WBZ-TV" title="WBZ-TV">WBZ-TV</a> in Boston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> U.S. President Eisenhower obtained photographs of the Soviet facilities from <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">Lockheed U-2</a> flights conducted since 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BN_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-BN-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The success of <i>Sputnik 1</i> seemed to have changed minds around the world regarding a shift in power to the Soviets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>'s launch of <i>Sputnik 1</i> spurred the United States to create the <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (ARPA, later DARPA) in February 1958 to regain a technological lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NetValley_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-NetValley-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Britain the media and population initially reacted with a mixture of fear for the future, but also amazement about humankind's progress. Many newspapers and magazines heralded the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">Space Age</a>. However, when the Soviet Union launched <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">a second craft</a> containing the dog <a href="/wiki/Laika" title="Laika">Laika</a>, the media narrative returned to one of anti-communism and many people sent protests to the Russian embassy and the RSPCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propaganda">Propaganda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Propaganda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1836" data-file-width="1267" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg/220px-Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg/330px-Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg/440px-Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Soviet 40 kopeks stamp, showing satellite's orbit</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Sputnik 1</i> was not immediately used for Soviet propaganda. The Soviets had kept quiet about their earlier accomplishments in rocketry, fearing that it would lead to secrets being revealed and failures being exploited by the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bessonov_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-bessonov-77">[77]</a></sup> When the Soviets began using <i>Sputnik</i> in their propaganda, they emphasized pride in the achievement of Soviet technology, arguing that it demonstrated the Soviets' superiority over the West. People were encouraged to listen to <i>Sputnik</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> signals on the radio<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bessonov_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-bessonov-77">[77]</a></sup> and to look out for <i>Sputnik</i> in the night sky. While <i>Sputnik</i> itself had been highly polished, its small size made it barely visible to the naked eye. What most watchers actually saw was the much more visible 26 meter core stage of the R-7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bessonov_77-2"><a href="#cite_note-bessonov-77">[77]</a></sup> Shortly after the launch of PS-1, Khrushchev pressed Korolev to launch another satellite in time for the 40th anniversary of the October Revolution on 7 November 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>The launch of <i>Sputnik 1</i> surprised the American public and shattered the perception, furthered by American propaganda, of the United States as the technological superpower and the Soviet Union as a backward country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-79">[79]</a></sup> Privately, however, the CIA and President Eisenhower were aware of progress being made by the Soviets on <i>Sputnik</i> from secret spy plane imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Together with the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> (JPL), the <a href="/wiki/Army_Ballistic_Missile_Agency" title="Army Ballistic Missile Agency">Army Ballistic Missile Agency</a> built <i><a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a></i>, and launched it on 31 January 1958. Before work was completed, however, the Soviet Union launched a second satellite, <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">Sputnik 2</a></i>, on 3 November 1957. Meanwhile, the televised failure of <i><a href="/wiki/Vanguard_TV3" title="Vanguard TV3">Vanguard TV3</a></i> on 6 December 1957 deepened American dismay over the country's position in the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>. The Americans took a more aggressive stance in the emerging space race,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-81">[81]</a></sup> resulting in an emphasis on science and technological research and reforms in many areas from the military to education systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morring_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-morring-82">[82]</a></sup> The federal government began investing in science, engineering and mathematics at all levels of education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peoples_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-peoples-83">[83]</a></sup> An advanced research group was assembled for military purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-79">[79]</a></sup> These research groups developed weapons such as ICBMs and missile defense systems, as well as spy satellites for the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_79-3"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>On Friday, 4 October 1957, the Soviets had orbited the world's first artificial satellite. Anyone who doubted its existence could walk into the backyard just after sunset and see it.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Mike Gray, <i>Angle of Attack</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Initially U.S. President Eisenhower was not surprised by <i>Sputnik 1</i>. He had been forewarned of the R-7s capabilities by information derived from <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2 spy plane</a> overflight photos as well as signals and telemetry intercepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The Eisenhower administration's first response was low-key and almost dismissive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Eisenhower was even pleased that the USSR, not the U.S., would be the first to test the waters of the still-uncertain <a href="/wiki/Space_law" title="Space law">legal status of orbital satellite overflights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Eisenhower had suffered the Soviet protests and shoot-downs of <a href="/wiki/Project_Genetrix" title="Project Genetrix">Project Genetrix</a> (Moby Dick) balloons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and was concerned about the probability of a U-2 being shot down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> To set a precedent for "freedom of space" before the launch of America's secret WS-117L spy satellites,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> the U.S. had launched <a href="/wiki/Project_Vanguard" title="Project Vanguard">Project Vanguard</a> as its own "civilian" satellite entry for the International Geophysical Year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Eisenhower greatly underestimated the reaction of the American public, who were shocked by the launch of <i>Sputnik</i> and by the televised failure of the Vanguard Test Vehicle 3 launch attempt. The sense of fear was inflamed by Democratic politicians and professional cold warriors, who portrayed the United States as woefully behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> One of the many books that suddenly appeared for the lay-audience noted seven points of "impact" upon the nation: Western leadership, Western strategy and tactics, missile production, applied research, basic research, education, and democratic culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoxStoiko69_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-CoxStoiko69-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. soon had a number of successful satellites, including <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_SCORE" title="Project SCORE">Project SCORE</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Courier_1B" title="Courier 1B">Courier 1B</a>. However, public reaction to the <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_crisis" title="Sputnik crisis">Sputnik crisis</a> led to the creation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Advanced Research Projects Agency">Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (renamed the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency or <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a> in 1972),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> and an increase in U.S. government spending on scientific research and education. Not only did the launch of <i>Sputnik</i> spur America to action in the space race, it also led directly to the creation of NASA through the space act bill.</p>
<p><i>Sputnik</i> also contributed directly to a new emphasis on science and technology in American schools. With a sense of urgency, Congress enacted the 1958 <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Education_Act" title="National Defense Education Act">National Defense Education Act</a>, which provided low-interest loans for college tuition to students majoring in math and science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> After the launch of <i>Sputnik</i>, a poll conducted and published by the University of Michigan showed that 26% of Americans surveyed thought that Russian sciences and engineering were superior to that of the United States. (A year later, however, that figure had dropped to 10% as the U.S. began launching its own satellites into space.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p>One consequence of the <i>Sputnik</i> shock was the perception of a "<a href="/wiki/Missile_gap" title="Missile gap">missile gap</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> This became a dominant issue in the 1960 Presidential campaign.</p>
<p>One irony of the <i>Sputnik</i> event was the initially low-key response of the Soviet Union. The Communist Party newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">Pravda</a></i> only printed a few paragraphs about <i>Sputnik 1</i> on 4 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> In the days following the world's startled response, the Soviets started celebrating their great accomplishment.</p>
<p><i>Sputnik</i> also inspired a generation of engineers and scientists. Harrison Storms, the North American designer who was responsible for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-15" title="X-15">X-15</a> rocket plane, and went on to head the effort to design the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Apollo Command/Service Module</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> launch vehicle's second stage was moved by the launch of <i>Sputnik</i> to think of space as being the next step for America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Astronauts <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a>, who was the first American in space, and <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a> later wrote of how the sight of <i>Sputnik I</i> passing overhead inspired them to their new careers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Homer_Hickam" title="Homer Hickam">Homer Hickam</a>'s memoir <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_Boys" title="Rocket Boys">Rocket Boys</a></i> and the movie <i><a href="/wiki/October_Sky" title="October Sky">October Sky</a></i> tell the story of how a coal miner's son, inspired by <i>Sputnik</i>, started building rockets in the mining town where he lived.</p>
<p>The launch of <i>Sputnik 1</i> inspired United States writer <a href="/wiki/Herb_Caen" title="Herb Caen">Herb Caen</a> to coin the term "<a href="/wiki/Beatnik" title="Beatnik">beatnik</a>" in an article about the <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">Beat Generation</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> on 2 April 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamlin_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamlin-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Backup_units_and_replicas">Backup units and replicas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputnik_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Backup units and replicas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1207" data-file-width="1600" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg/220px-Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg/330px-Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg/440px-Kansas_Cosmosphere_Sputnik_1_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<i>Sputnik</i> replica at the <a href="/wiki/Cosmosphere" title="Cosmosphere">Cosmosphere</a> in Hutchinson, Kansas</div>

<p>At least two vintage duplicates of <i>Sputnik 1</i> exist, built apparently as backup units. One resides just outside Moscow in the corporate museum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia">Energia</a>, the modern descendant of Korolev's design bureau, where it is on display by appointment only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSTopTen_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSTopTen-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPOI_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPOI-105">[105]</a></sup> Another is in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Flight" title="Museum of Flight">Museum of Flight</a> in Seattle, Washington. Unlike Energia's unit, it has no internal components, but it does have casings and molded fittings inside (as well as evidence of battery wear), which suggest it was built as more than just a model. Authenticated by the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Museum_of_Cosmonautics" title="Memorial Museum of Cosmonautics">Memorial Museum of Cosmonautics</a> in Moscow, the unit was auctioned in 2001 and purchased by an anonymous private buyer who donated it to the museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSTopTen_104-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSTopTen-104">[104]</a></sup> Two more <i>Sputnik</i> backups are said to be in the personal collections of American entrepreneurs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Garriot" title="Richard Garriot">Richard Garriot</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSTopTen_104-2"><a href="#cite_note-CSTopTen-104">[104]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jay_S._Walker" title="Jay S. Walker">Jay S. Walker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired2008_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired2008-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the Soviets' donation to the UN, there are dozens of other full-size <i>Sputnik</i> replicas, more or less accurate, on display in locations around the world, including the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a> in Washington, D.C.;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSTopTen_104-3"><a href="#cite_note-CSTopTen-104">[104]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Frontiers_of_Flight_Museum" title="Frontiers of Flight Museum">Frontiers of Flight Museum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth_Museum_of_Science_and_History" title="Fort Worth Museum of Science and History">Fort Worth Museum of Science and History</a>, both in Texas;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSTopTen_104-4"><a href="#cite_note-CSTopTen-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="Armstrong Air and Space Museum">Armstrong Air and Space Museum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force" title="National Museum of the United States Air Force">National Museum of the United States Air Force</a>, both in Ohio; the <a href="/wiki/Cosmosphere" title="Cosmosphere">Cosmosphere</a> in Hutchinson, Kansas; the <a href="/wiki/California_Science_Center" title="California Science Center">California Science Center</a> in Los Angeles; the <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum,_London" title="Science Museum, London">Science Museum, London</a>; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Museum_Liverpool" title="World Museum Liverpool">World Museum</a> in Liverpool; Powerhouse Museum in Sydney, Australia and outside the Russian embassy in Madrid, Spain.</p>
<p>Three one-third scale student-built replicas of <i>Sputnik 1</i> were deployed from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mir_space_station" title="Mir space station">Mir space station</a> between 1997 and 1999.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The first, named <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnik_40" title="Sputnik 40">Sputnik 40</a></i> to commemorate the fortieth anniversary of the launch of <i>Sputnik 1</i>, was deployed in November 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BN_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-BN-71">[71]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnik_41" title="Sputnik 41">Sputnik 41</a></i> was launched a year later, and <i>Sputnik 99</i> was deployed in February 1999. A fourth replica was launched but never deployed, and was destroyed when Mir was <a href="/wiki/Deorbit_of_Mir" title="Deorbit of Mir">deorbited</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSTopTen_104-5"><a href="#cite_note-CSTopTen-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSP_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSP-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
