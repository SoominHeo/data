<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Spring training</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, <b>spring training</b> is a series of practices and exhibition games preceding the start of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_season" title="Regular season">regular season</a>. Spring training allows new players to try out for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roster" title="Roster">roster</a> and position spots, and gives existing players practice time prior to competitive play. Spring training has always attracted fan attention, drawing crowds who travel to the warmer climates to enjoy the weather and watch their favorite teams play, and spring training usually coincides with <a href="/wiki/Spring_break" title="Spring break">spring break</a> for many US college students.</p>
<p>Spring training typically starts in mid-February and continues until just before <a href="/wiki/Opening_Day" title="Opening Day">Opening Day</a> of the regular season, traditionally the first week of April. In some years, teams not scheduled to play on Opening Day will play spring training games that day. Pitchers and catchers report to spring training first because pitchers benefit from a longer training period. A few days later, position players arrive and team practice begins. Team members normally wear their batting practice uniforms for the duration of spring training and only wear their normal jerseys beginning on Opening Day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg/300px-SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg/450px-SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg/600px-SpringTrainingHoHoKamPark.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>Spring training by major league teams in sites other than their regular season game sites first became popular in the 1890s and by 1910 was in wide use. <a href="/wiki/Hot_Springs,_Arkansas" title="Hot Springs, Arkansas">Hot Springs, Arkansas</a> has been called the original "birthplace" of Spring Training baseball. The location of Hot Springs and the concept of getting the players ready for the upcoming season was the brainchild of Chicago White Stockings (today's <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a>) team President <a href="/wiki/Albert_Spalding" title="Albert Spalding">Albert Spalding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cap_Anson" title="Cap Anson">Cap Anson</a>. In 1886, the White Stockings traveled to Hot Springs to prepare for the upcoming season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopediaofarkansas.net_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopediaofarkansas.net-2">[2]</a></sup> Practicing at the <b>Hot Springs Baseball Grounds,</b> the White Stockings had a successful season and other teams took notice and began holding spring training in Hot Springs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopediaofarkansas.net_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopediaofarkansas.net-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Spiders" title="Cleveland Spiders">Cleveland Spiders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> followed the White Stockings to Hot Springs. Whittington Park/<a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson_Park" title="Ban Johnson Park">Ban Johnson Park</a> (1894), Majestic Park (1909) and Fogel Field (1912) were all built in Hot Springs to host Major League teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Famously, a young pitcher named <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> was playing an emergency game at first base on St. Patrick's Day, 1918, his first time playing the field. Ruth would hit two home runs that day in Hot Springs, and the second was a 573-foot shot that landed across the street from Whittington Park in a pond of the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Alligator_Farm_and_Petting_Zoo" title="Arkansas Alligator Farm and Petting Zoo">Arkansas Alligator Farm and Petting Zoo</a>. Soon he was playing the field more often.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buddbailey_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-buddbailey-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Over 130 Major League Baseball Hall of Famers, including such names as Ruth, <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young" title="Cy Young">Cy Young</a>, Cap Anson, <a href="/wiki/Honus_Wagner" title="Honus Wagner">Honus Wagner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Ty Cobb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tris_Speaker" title="Tris Speaker">Tris Speaker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Johnson" title="Walter Johnson">Walter Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Hornsby" title="Rogers Hornsby">Rogers Hornsby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Foxx" title="Jimmie Foxx">Jimmie Foxx</a> were involved in Hot Springs Spring Training games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finance.yahoo.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-finance.yahoo.com-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>The First Boys of Spring</i> is a 2015 documentary about Hot Springs Spring Training. The film was narrated by area native, actor <a href="/wiki/Billy_Bob_Thornton" title="Billy Bob Thornton">Billy Bob Thornton</a>, and produced by filmmaker Larry Foley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>The documentary began airing nationally on the <a href="/wiki/MLB_Network" title="MLB Network">MLB Network</a> in February, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Early training sites include the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hot_Springs,_Arkansas" title="Hot Springs, Arkansas">Hot Springs, Arkansas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">Tulsa, Oklahoma</a>; the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>, when the team was owned by <a href="/wiki/Del_Webb" title="Del Webb">Del Webb</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> in Los Angeles when owned by <a href="/wiki/William_Wrigley_Jr." title="William Wrigley Jr.">William Wrigley Jr.</a>; the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_St._Louis_Browns" title="History of the St. Louis Browns">St. Louis Browns</a> and later the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Athletics" title="Kansas City Athletics">Kansas City Athletics</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> then in <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach, Florida</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a> and other teams joined in by the early 1940s. The <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> are credited with being the first team to conduct spring training camp in Arizona. They trained in Phoenix at Riverside Park at Central Avenue and the Salt River in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Phillies" title="Philadelphia Phillies">Philadelphia Phillies</a> were the first of the current major-league teams to train in Florida, when they spent two weeks in <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a> in 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Spring training in Florida began in earnest in 1913, when the Chicago Cubs played in Tampa, and the Cleveland Indians in Pensacola. One year later, two other teams moved to Florida for spring training, the real start of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapefruit_League" title="Grapefruit League">Grapefruit League</a>. Except for a couple of years during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when travel restrictions prevented teams training south of the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_river" title="Ohio river">Ohio rivers</a>, Florida hosted more than half of the spring training teams through 2009. Since 2010, major league teams have been equally divided during spring training, with 15 teams in Florida and 15 teams in Arizona.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> All but six of the major league teams have gone to spring training in Florida at one time or another. Many of the most famous players in baseball history (<a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Ruth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Gehrig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stan_Musial" title="Stan Musial">Musial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ty_Cobb" title="Ty Cobb">Cobb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Mays" title="Willie Mays">Mays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">DiMaggio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Berra" title="Yogi Berra">Berra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mantle</a>, and many more) have called Florida home for 4–6 weeks every spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the autobiography of former Cleveland Indians owner <a href="/wiki/Bill_Veeck" title="Bill Veeck">Bill Veeck</a>, the avoidance of racism was one reason the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cactus_League" title="Cactus League">Cactus League</a> was established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1947, Veeck was the owner of the minor league <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers_(minor_league_baseball_team)" title="Milwaukee Brewers (minor league baseball team)">Milwaukee Brewers</a> and the team trained in <a href="/wiki/Ocala,_Florida" title="Ocala, Florida">Ocala, Florida</a>. Veeck inadvertently sat in the Black section of the segregated stands and engaged in conversation with a couple of fans. According to Veeck's book, the local law enforcement told Veeck he could not sit in that section, and then called the Ocala mayor when Veeck argued back. The mayor finally backed down when Veeck threatened to take his team elsewhere for spring training and promised to let the country know why.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baseball_color_line" title="Baseball color line">Baseball color line</a></div>
<p>Veeck sold the Brewers in 1945 and retired to his ranch in <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">Tucson, Arizona</a>, but soon purchased the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> in 1946. He decided to buck tradition and train the Indians in Tucson and convinced the New York Giants to give Phoenix a try. Thus the Cactus League was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Veeck then signed <a href="/wiki/Larry_Doby" title="Larry Doby">Larry Doby</a> to the Indians. Doby was the second African-American to play MLB in the 20th century, and the first for the American League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Florida and Arizona now host all Major League Baseball teams for spring training, this has not always been the case. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="History of the Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> trained in <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> in 1947 and 1949, and in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Havana_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Havana-20">[20]</a></sup> The New York Yankees also trained in the early 1950s in Cuba and the Dominican Republic. Spring training camps and games were also held in Hawaii, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and various cities of northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, sometimes by visiting major league teams in the 1950s and 1960s.</p>

<p>During World War II, most teams held an abbreviated spring training within easy reach of their cities. In order to conserve rail transport during the war, 1943's Spring Training was limited to an area east of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> and north of the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> held camp in <a href="/wiki/French_Lick,_Indiana" title="French Lick, Indiana">French Lick, Indiana</a>; the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901–60)">Washington Senators</a> in <a href="/wiki/College_Park,_Maryland" title="College Park, Maryland">College Park, Maryland</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> in <a href="/wiki/Asbury_Park,_New_Jersey" title="Asbury Park, New Jersey">Asbury Park, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before and shortly after big league baseball reached the West Coast, a number of teams trained in the state of California or along the state boundary. The Chicago Cubs trained on <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catalina_Island,_California" title="Santa Catalina Island, California">Catalina Island</a> in the 1920s, '30s, and '40s. For example, early in their history, the then-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_Angels" title="California Angels">California Angels</a> held spring training in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs,_California" title="Palm Springs, California">Palm Springs, California</a> from 1961 to 1993, the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Padres" title="San Diego Padres">San Diego Padres</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yuma,_Arizona" title="Yuma, Arizona">Yuma, Arizona</a> from 1969 to 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a> in the 1970s, and various major league teams had trained in <a href="/wiki/Riverside,_California" title="Riverside, California">Riverside</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Bernardino,_California" title="San Bernardino, California">San Bernardino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/El_Centro,_California" title="El Centro, California">El Centro</a> near the Mexican border.</p>
<p>The concept of spring training is not limited to North America; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_baseball" title="Japanese baseball">Japanese professional baseball</a> leagues' teams adopted spring training and preseason game sites across East Asia such as <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>; the Pacific Islands (most notably in Hawaii); and two cities in the United States: <a href="/wiki/Salinas,_California" title="Salinas, California">Salinas, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yuma,_Arizona" title="Yuma, Arizona">Yuma, Arizona</a> on the Mexican border.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_training_locations_by_team">Spring training locations by team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spring training locations by team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Major_League_Baseball_spring_training_ballparks" title="List of Major League Baseball spring training ballparks">List of Major League Baseball spring training ballparks</a></div>
<p>Generally, teams train in either Florida or Arizona based on their geographic location in the U.S., with eastern teams playing in Florida and western teams training in Arizona; the exceptions being the Cleveland Indians, Cincinnati Reds, Milwaukee Brewers, and the two Chicago-based teams all training in Arizona. The last west-coast team to train in Florida was the Los Angeles Dodgers, who moved to Arizona in 2009.</p>
<p>In modern training, teams that train in Florida will play other Florida-training teams in their exhibition games, regardless of regular-season league affiliations. Likewise, Arizona-training teams will play other Arizona teams. These have been nicknamed the Grapefruit League and Cactus League, respectively, after plants typical of the respective states. The teams can play colleges, minor league baseball clubs, intra-squad games (members of the same team play against each other), split-squad games (games when one team is scheduled for two games in one day, so the team splits into two squads and each squad plays in one of the games), and B Games (unofficial Spring Training games where statistics and standings are not counted).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In years when the <a href="/wiki/World_Baseball_Classic" title="World Baseball Classic">World Baseball Classic</a> occurs, the national teams in the tournament prepare by playing major league teams.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grapefruit_League">Grapefruit League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grapefruit League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The origin of the name "Grapefruit League" has several versions. One popular myth was that <a href="/wiki/Casey_Stengel" title="Casey Stengel">Casey Stengel</a> threw a <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">grapefruit</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> manager <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Robinson" title="Wilbert Robinson">Wilbert Robinson</a>. The accepted version is that aviator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruth_Law" title="Ruth Law">Ruth Law</a> threw the grapefruit. In 1915, Law had been throwing <a href="/wiki/Golf_ball" title="Golf ball">golf balls</a> from her airplane to advertise a golf course. Someone suggested throwing a baseball from her airplane. Robinson, whose team was in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach" title="Daytona Beach">Daytona Beach</a> area for spring training, agreed to try to catch the baseball. Flying 525 feet above Robinson, Law realized she had forgotten her baseball and threw a grapefruit that she had. When Robinson tried to catch it, the grapefruit exploded in his face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cactus_League">Cactus League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cactus League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike the Grapefruit League, teams in the Cactus League often share stadiums; of the 15 teams who train in Arizona, only the Cubs, Angels, Brewers, Giants and A's have their own home stadiums.</p>
<p>The newest stadium built for MLB spring training is <a href="/wiki/Sloan_Park" title="Sloan Park">Sloan Park</a>, the spring training home for the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mesa,_Arizona" title="Mesa, Arizona">Mesa, Arizona</a>, which opened in February 2014.</p>
<p>According to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arizona_Republic" title="Arizona Republic">Arizona Republic</a></i>, the Cactus League generates more than $300 million a year in economic impact to the greater Phoenix metropolitan area economy. The <i>Arizona Republic</i> newspaper reports that more than $500 million has been spent on "building eight new stadiums and renovating two others for the 15 teams in the Valley."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Attendance set a new record at 2011 Cactus League games with 1.59 million attending games at the various stadiums in the Phoenix metro area. Much of the attendance surge is attributed to the Salt River Fields at Talking Stick venue that accounted for 22 percent of the Cactus League attendance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Baseball_statistics" title="Baseball statistics">Statistics</a> are recorded during spring training games, but they are not combined with the listed statistics for regular season games, and unusual performances which would have broken records if accomplished during the regular season are considered to be unofficial.</p>
<p>For example, on March 14, 2000, the Red Sox used six pitchers to achieve a 5–0 <a href="/wiki/Perfect_game" title="Perfect game">perfect game</a> victory over the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Blue_Jays" title="Toronto Blue Jays">Toronto Blue Jays</a>. A perfect game is considered a crowning accomplishment during the regular season or postseason, but in spring training it attracts little notice. Starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Mart%C3%ADnez" title="Pedro Martínez">Pedro Martínez</a>, who lost a perfect game in extra innings in 1995 while pitching for the former <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a>, was talking to reporters at the conclusion of the game, rather than watching the final pitches. Reliever <a href="/wiki/Rod_Beck" title="Rod Beck">Rod Beck</a>, who finished the game, did not realize the nature of his accomplishment until informed by catcher Joe Sidall. Many fans also left before the game's conclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although spring training statistics are unofficial, teams frequently use players' spring training performances as a way of assigning starting roles and roster spots on the club.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extended_spring_training">Extended spring training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_training&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Extended spring training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_sox_orioles_xst.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="600" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/83/Red_sox_orioles_xst.jpg/300px-Red_sox_orioles_xst.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/83/Red_sox_orioles_xst.jpg/450px-Red_sox_orioles_xst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/83/Red_sox_orioles_xst.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

An extended spring training game between the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Red Sox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Orioles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sarasota,_Florida" title="Sarasota, Florida">Sarasota, Florida</a> during the 2008 season.</div>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball">Minor league</a> players participate in spring training following a telescoped schedule that generally lasts from March 1–31. At its conclusion, most players are assigned to full-season Class A, AA, or AAA <a href="/wiki/Farm_team" title="Farm team">farm team</a> rosters to begin the regular minor league season. However, those players deemed unready for a full-season campaign—through inexperience or injury—are assigned to "extended spring training", a structured program of workouts, rehabilitation sessions, simulated games, and exhibition games based in the major league parent team's minor league training complex. If a player is deemed ready to participate in full-season league action, he is promoted to an appropriate-level farm club. When the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_season" title="Short season">short season</a>" Class A and rookie leagues begin play in late June, extended spring training players are assigned to those rosters, placed on the <a href="/wiki/Disabled_list" title="Disabled list">disabled list</a>, or released.</p>
