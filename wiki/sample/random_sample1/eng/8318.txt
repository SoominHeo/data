<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Split leap</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>split leap</b> or <b>split jump</b> is a sequence of body movements in which a person assumes a <a href="/wiki/Split_(gymnastics)" title="Split (gymnastics)">split</a> position after leaping or <a href="/wiki/Jumping" title="Jumping">jumping</a> from the floor, respectively, while still in the air. Split leaps and split jumps are both found in various genres of <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dance</a> including <a href="/wiki/Acro_dance" title="Acro dance">acro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jazz_dance" title="Jazz dance">jazz dance</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a>. Split jumps may also serve as a form of <a href="/wiki/Split_jump_(exercise)" title="Split jump (exercise)">exercise</a>, and the term <i>split jump</i> is also commonly used to describe similar body movements in <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">figure skating</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Split_leap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some types of split leaps and jumps are named according to the type of split that is performed, while others may use nomenclature associated with specific dance genres. For example, a <i>straddle</i> (sometimes called <i>side</i>) split leap incorporates a straddle split, with legs extended symmetrically to the sides, whereas a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_jet%C3%A9" title="Grand jeté">grand jeté</a>, which involves a front split, derives its name from ballet terminology. A <i>stag split leap</i> is a split leap in which one knee is bent, whereas both knees are bent in a <i>double stag split leap</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Split_leap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Split leaps and split jumps require significant flexibility and strength. Flexibility and strength are both needed to attain a split position without the aid of external leg support. Also, in order to remain airborne while in the split position, strength is needed to propel the body upward with sufficient <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> to compensate for the loss of vertical <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> that results from raising the legs into a split position while airborne.</p>
<p>In dance, the perceived quality of a split leap or split jump depends in large part on the application of various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dance_technique" title="Dance technique">dance techniques</a>. In particular, emphasis is often placed on pointing the feet while airborne, especially during the split, so as to extend the leg lines. Also, proper technique (<i>i.e.,</i> <a href="/wiki/Best_practice" title="Best practice">best practice</a>) typically calls for straight legs (except in <i>stag</i> variations) and a full split position at the <a href="/wiki/Apex_(geometry)" title="Apex (geometry)">apex</a> of the leap or jump. <a href="/wiki/Ballon_(ballet)" title="Ballon (ballet)">Ballon</a>, which is the appearance of effortless and weightless movement, is another important aesthetic. There are, however, variations that run counter to traditional dance techniques. For example, the feet may be intentionally oriented at right angles to the legs to produce a notable, unusual aesthetic.</p>
