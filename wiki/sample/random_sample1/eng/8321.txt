<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">World record progression 1000 m speed skating women</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

On a 400 m rink it is the only distance that uses another finish line than other distances, while on the 333.3 m rink the distance has a common start- and finish line.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s:_Recognition">1930s: Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1930s: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of the world record 1000 m speed skating for women starts in 1931,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> when the <a href="/wiki/International_Skating_Union" title="International Skating Union">ISU</a> (then still called <a href="/wiki/International_Skating_Union" title="International Skating Union">IEV, Internationale Eislauf Vereinigung</a>) decides to recognize records for women. The first <i>official</i> world record to be recognized was the 2:16.4 of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zofia_Nehringowa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zofia Nehringowa (page does not exist)">Zofia Nehringowa</a> from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, skated on 26 January 1929, in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, though at least two female <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> skaters are known to have skated faster before 1929, Ljudmila Aleksejeva skated 1:57.6 in 1928, and later a 1:56.5 in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, because the Soviet Union was not a member of the ISU, their results could not be ratified.</p>
<p>The next record to be recognised by the ISU was the 2:08.8 of Austrian figure- and speed skater <a href="/wiki/Liselotte_Landbeck" title="Liselotte Landbeck">Liselotte Landbeck</a>, skated on 10 January 1932, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eisstadion_Davos" title="Eisstadion Davos">Davos</a>, which Zofia Nehringowa a week later in <a href="/wiki/Engelberg" title="Engelberg">Engelberg</a> improved to 2:03.4, still above the results of Aleksejeva. The first skater that set a world's best performance together with her official world record was the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Synn%C3%B8ve_Lie" title="Synnøve Lie">Synnøve Lie</a>, who skated 1:51.2 at the end of the 1931/32 season in <a href="/wiki/Brandbu" title="Brandbu">Brandbu</a>.</p>
<p>The first woman below 1:50.0 was Liselotte Landbeck, who skated a 1:48.5 on 22 January 1933 in Davos, which Synnøve Lie a year later improved on the same rink to 1:48.1. This soon proved not fast enough, as <a href="/wiki/Vern%C3%A9_Lesche" title="Verné Lesche">Verné Lesche</a> from <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> set the record to 1:45.7 a month later, during the <a href="/wiki/World_Allround_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Allround Speed Skating Championships for Women">unofficial Women's World Allround Speed Skating Championships of 1934</a> in Oslo. At the end of the 1934/35 season that time fell to U.S. speed skater <a href="/wiki/Kit_Klein" title="Kit Klein">Kit Klein</a>, who skated a 1:42.3 in <a href="/wiki/Kongsberg" title="Kongsberg">Kongsberg</a>. The next woman to improve the world record, <a href="/wiki/Laila_Schou_Nilsen" title="Laila Schou Nilsen">Laila Schou Nilsen</a>, brought the record down to 1:38.8 during the <a href="/wiki/World_Allround_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Allround Speed Skating Championships for Women">Women's World Allround Speed Skating Championships of 1937</a> in Davos.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s:_Slow_improvement">1950s: Slow improvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1950s: Slow improvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1:38.8 proved to be a though nut to crack, and it took till 5 February 1951 before Soviet speed skater <a href="/wiki/Sofya_Kondakova" title="Sofya Kondakova">Sofya Kondakova</a> managed to skate 1:36.8 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medeo" title="Medeo">Medeo</a> rink in <a href="/wiki/Almaty" title="Almaty">Alma Ata</a>, the Soviet Union meanwhile being a member of the ISU. In the years that followed three other Soviet skaters brought the record lower: <a href="/wiki/Rimma_Zhukova" title="Rimma Zhukova">Rimma Zhukova</a> 1:36.6 in 1952, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lidia_Selikova&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lidia Selikova (page does not exist)">Lidia Selikova</a> 1:36.4 in 1953 and <a href="/wiki/Tamara_Rylova" title="Tamara Rylova">Tamara Rylova</a> 1:33.4 in 1955.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s:_Artificial_ice">1960s: Artificial ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1960s: Artificial ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1960s saw a first technological breakthrough in speed skating: the 400 m artificial ice rink. The first world record 1000 m speed skating women on artificial ice was skated by Soviet speed skater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lidia_Skoblikova" title="Lidia Skoblikova">Lidia Skoblikova</a>, who skated a 1:31.8 during the <a href="/wiki/World_Allround_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Allround Speed Skating Championships for Women">Women's World Allround Speed Skating Championships of 1963</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karuizawa" title="Karuizawa">Karuizawa</a>. The next records would bring the 1:30.0 limit in sight, as <a href="/wiki/Stien_Kaiser" title="Stien Kaiser">Stien Kaiser</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> skated a 1.31.0 on the artificial ice of <a href="/wiki/Eisstadion_Inzell" title="Eisstadion Inzell">Inzell</a> on 3 March 1968 and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elly_van_der_Brom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elly van der Brom (page does not exist)">Elly van der Brom</a> of the Netherlands skated a 1:30.0 on the natural ice of Davos on 9 February 1969.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s:_Acceleration">1970s: Acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1970s: Acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1970s saw a second technological breakthrough in the form of the skin suit, though that came only into general use by 1976. The 1970s saw a faster improvement than the decades before, beginning with Soviet skater <a href="/wiki/Lyudmila_Titova" title="Lyudmila Titova">Lyudmila Titova</a>, who was the first woman below 1:30.0 with her 1:29.5 on 9 January 1970 on the Medeo rink, a record that stood only a day because <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lyudmila_Fechina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lyudmila Fechina (page does not exist)">Lyudmila Fechina</a> skated 1:29.0 the next day of the same event. This result was equalled next year by Stien Kaiser on 16 January 1971 in Davos and by Lyudmila Titova during day one of the <a href="/wiki/World_Sprint_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women">World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women 1971</a> in Inzell. A day later however Lyudmila Titova made the record her own again when she skated a 1:27.7. The last skater who set the world record on natural ice was U.S. skater <a href="/wiki/Anne_Henning" title="Anne Henning">Anne Henning</a>, who managed a 1:27.3 on 8 January 1972 in Davos. Medeo by now had an artificial rink, and the next records of the 1970s would be skated here by Soviet skater <a href="/wiki/Tatyana_Averina" title="Tatyana Averina">Tatyana Averina</a>: 1:26.40 in 1974, and 1:26.12, 1:25.28 and 1:23.46 in 1975.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s:_Indoor_rinks">1980s: Indoor rinks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1980s: Indoor rinks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1980s saw a third technological breakthrough in the form of the indoor 400 m rink, though the first major races were not on indoor ice before 1987. The first 1000 m record of the decade, 1:23.01, was skated by Soviet skater <a href="/wiki/Natalya_Petrusyova" title="Natalya Petrusyova">Natalya Petrusyova</a> on the Medeo rink at the end of the 1979/80 season. A year later <a href="/wiki/Christa_Luding-Rothenburger" title="Christa Luding-Rothenburger">Christa Rothenburger</a> of <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> improved that to 1:20.95, also on the Medeo rink, only to lose it a day later again to Natalya Petrusyova, when she managed a 1:20.81.</p>
<p>At the end of the 1982/83 season Natalya Petrusyova became the first woman to skate below 1:20.00, when she skated a 1:19.31 on the Medeo rink. The last woman to set a world record on outdoor ice was <a href="/wiki/Karin_Enke" title="Karin Enke">Karin Kania</a> of East Germany, when she skated a 1:18.84 during the <a href="/wiki/World_Sprint_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women">World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women of 1986</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karuizawa" title="Karuizawa">Karuizawa</a>. She would also be the first to skate a 1000 m world record indoor: 1:18.11 on 5 December 1987 in <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Oval" title="Olympic Oval">Calgary</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Speed_skating_at_the_1988_Winter_Olympics#1.2C000_m_2" title="Speed skating at the 1988 Winter Olympics">1988 Winter Olympics</a>, Christa Rothenburger brought the record lower still with 1:17.65.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s:_The_clap_skate">1990s: The clap skate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s: The clap skate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1990s saw a fourth technological breakthrough, the clap skate, though that only came into general use at the end of the decade. But when it arrived, faster times became possible. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catriona_Le_May_Doan" title="Catriona Le May Doan">Catriona Le May Doan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> was the first woman to skate a world record 1000 m for women on clap skates when she skated a 1:16.07 on 22 November 1997 in Calgary. U.S. skater <a href="/wiki/Chris_Witty" title="Chris Witty">Chris Witty</a> would improve that twice during that season, from 1:15.43 to 1:14.96, while <a href="/wiki/Monique_Garbrecht-Enfeldt" title="Monique Garbrecht-Enfeldt">Monique Garbrecht</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> skated 1:14.61 on 21 February 1999 during the <a href="/wiki/World_Sprint_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women">World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women of 1999</a> in Calgary.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_2000:_Higher_and_faster">From 2000: Higher and faster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_record_progression_1000_m_speed_skating_women&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: From 2000: Higher and faster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the last decades it has become evident that world records on the 1000 m for women can only be broken on (indoor) highland rinks. The last lowland record was set in 1935, the last outdoor record in 1986. Later world records were set at altitude in Calgary, and from 2002 the even higher indoor rink of <a href="/wiki/Utah_Olympic_Oval" title="Utah Olympic Oval">Salt Lake City</a> came into use. Monique Garbrecht was the first to skate a world record 1000 m for women there with 1:14.13, followed by <a href="/wiki/Sabine_V%C3%B6lker" title="Sabine Völker">Sabine Völker</a> from Germany with 1:14.06 and Chris Witty with 1:13.83. The role of Calgary was not over yet, the next records were for <a href="/wiki/Cindy_Klassen" title="Cindy Klassen">Cindy Klassen</a> of Canada with 1:13.46 and 1:13.11 during the 24/25 March 2006 Calgary Olympic Oval final and <a href="/wiki/Christine_Nesbitt" title="Christine Nesbitt">Christine Nesbitt</a> of Canada skated 1:12.68 during the <a href="/wiki/World_Sprint_Speed_Skating_Championships_for_Women" title="World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women">World Sprint Speed Skating Championships for Women of 2012</a> in Calgary.</p>
