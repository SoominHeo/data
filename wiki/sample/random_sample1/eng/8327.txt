<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Slide guitar</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Slide guitar</b> is a particular method or technique for playing the <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>. Instead of altering the <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/String_(music)" title="String (music)">strings</a> in the normal manner (by pressing the string against <a href="/wiki/Fret" title="Fret">frets</a>), an object called a "<a href="/wiki/Steel_bar" title="Steel bar">slide</a>" is placed upon the string to vary its vibrating length, and pitch. This slide can then be moved along the string without lifting (hence the name), creating smooth transitions in pitch and allowing wide, expressive <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a>.</p>
<p>Slide guitar is most often played (assuming a right-handed player and guitar):</p>
<ul>
<li>With the guitar in the normal position, using a slide on one of the fingers of the left hand.</li>
<li>With the guitar held horizontally, belly-up, using a metal bar called a "steel" ("slides" generally fit around a finger) held with the hand and wrist above the frets, fingers pointing away from the player's body; this is known as "<a href="/wiki/Lap_steel_guitar" title="Lap steel guitar">lap steel guitar</a>". This same technique is used to play <a href="/wiki/Pedal_steel_guitar" title="Pedal steel guitar">pedal steel guitar</a> and the "<a href="/wiki/Dobro" title="Dobro">Dobro</a>" <a href="/wiki/Resonator_guitar" title="Resonator guitar">resonator guitar</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">Bluegrass music</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The technique of using a slide on a string has been traced to one-stringed African instruments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Payne1-1">[1]</a></sup> similar to a "<a href="/wiki/Diddley_bow" title="Diddley bow">Diddley bow</a>".</p>
<p>The technique was made popular by African American <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> artists. The first musician recorded using the style was <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Weaver_(musician)" title="Sylvester Weaver (musician)">Sylvester Weaver</a>, who recorded two solo pieces "Guitar Blues" and "Guitar Rag" in 1923. Some of the blues artists who most prominently used the slide include gospel singer <a href="/wiki/Blind_Willie_Johnson" title="Blind Willie Johnson">Blind Willie Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blind_Willie_McTell" title="Blind Willie McTell">Blind Willie McTell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Fred_McDowell" title="Mississippi Fred McDowell">Mississippi Fred McDowell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charley_Patton" title="Charley Patton">Charley Patton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Son_House" title="Son House">Son House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Johnson" title="Robert Johnson">Robert Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Casey_Bill_Weldon" title="Casey Bill Weldon">Casey Bill Weldon</a>.</p>
<p>The first influential classic <a href="/wiki/Electric_blues" title="Electric blues">electric blues</a> slide guitarist is arguably <a href="/wiki/Elmore_James" title="Elmore James">Elmore James</a>, who in 1951 created a slide guitar interpretation of Robert Johnson's 1936 "<a href="/wiki/Dust_My_Broom" title="Dust My Broom">Dust My Broom</a>" riff,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oliver_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oliver-2">[2]</a></sup> and is held in particularly high regard. His slide and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottleneck_guitar" title="Bottleneck guitar">bottleneck guitar</a> techniques were later widely adopted by blues and rock guitarists—including <a href="/wiki/British_blues" title="British blues">British blues</a> bands such as <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Animals" title="The Animals">The Animals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Yardbirds" title="The Yardbirds">The Yardbirds</a>, and rock guitarists such as <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck" title="Jeff Beck">Jeff Beck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dicaire_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dicaire-3">[3]</a></sup> Blues legend <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">Muddy Waters</a> was also very influential, particularly in developing the electric Chicago blues slide guitar from the acoustic <a href="/wiki/Delta_blues" title="Delta blues">Mississippi Delta</a> slide guitar. Texas blues musician <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Winter" title="Johnny Winter">Johnny Winter</a> developed his distinctive style through years of touring with Waters. Slide player <a href="/wiki/Roy_Rogers_(guitarist)" title="Roy Rogers (guitarist)">Roy Rogers</a> honed his slide skills by touring with blues artist <a href="/wiki/John_Lee_Hooker" title="John Lee Hooker">John Lee Hooker</a>. John Lee's cousin <a href="/wiki/Earl_Hooker" title="Earl Hooker">Earl Hooker</a> may have been the first to use <a href="/wiki/Wah-wah_(music)" title="Wah-wah (music)">wah-wah</a> and slide together.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The sound has since become commonplace in <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiian_music" title="Hawaiian music">Hawaiian music</a>. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, by bands and artists such as <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canned_Heat" title="Canned Heat">Canned Heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Allman_Brothers_Band" title="The Allman Brothers Band">The Allman Brothers Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ry_Cooder" title="Ry Cooder">Ry Cooder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rea" title="Chris Rea">Chris Rea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Raitt" title="Bonnie Raitt">Bonnie Raitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynyrd_Skynyrd" title="Lynyrd Skynyrd">Lynyrd Skynyrd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blackfoot_(band)" title="Blackfoot (band)">Blackfoot (band)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foghat" title="Foghat">Foghat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molly_Hatchet" title="Molly Hatchet">Molly Hatchet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Feat" title="Little Feat">Little Feat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Petty_%26_The_Heartbreakers" title="Tom Petty &amp; The Heartbreakers">Tom Petty &amp; The Heartbreakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">Eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faces_(band)" title="Faces (band)">Faces</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZZ_Top" title="ZZ Top">ZZ Top</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitesnake" title="Whitesnake">Whitesnake</a>, <a href="/wiki/AC/DC" title="AC/DC">AC/DC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cinderella" title="Cinderella">Cinderella</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a>. <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a> featured a slide guitar as early as their 1963 recording of the <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>/<a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> song "<a href="/wiki/I_Wanna_Be_Your_Man" title="I Wanna Be Your Man">I Wanna Be Your Man</a>". Guitarist <a href="/wiki/Brian_Jones" title="Brian Jones">Brian Jones</a> played slide in a very blues-oriented style. Jones was also one of the first English guitarists to play slide and during the band's early years, he was considered one of the best slide guitarists in the music world. His successors <a href="/wiki/Mick_Taylor" title="Mick Taylor">Mick Taylor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Wood" title="Ronnie Wood">Ronnie Wood</a> also displayed their own slide guitar skills while with the band. The album <i><a href="/wiki/Let_It_Bleed" title="Let It Bleed">Let It Bleed</a></i> features <a href="/wiki/Keith_Richards" title="Keith Richards">Keith Richards</a> on slide guitar for the majority of the album, since the band were in-between guitarists during the making of the album. Rolling Stones vocalist <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a> has also played slide guitar on occasion (both in the Stones and his solo career).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many early <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> songs such as "<a href="/wiki/See_Emily_Play" title="See Emily Play">See Emily Play</a>" feature <a href="/wiki/Syd_Barrett" title="Syd Barrett">Syd Barrett</a>'s slide guitar performances, reflecting the band's original Chicago urban blues repertoire from musicians such as <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">Bo Diddley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slim_Harpo" title="Slim Harpo">Slim Harpo</a>; <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a>, who is better known as the primary lead guitarist for the band, has also been known to use the slide technique in a few of <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>'s songs, mainly after Syd's departure, and has been also known to use the technique on many of the bands tours although using a lap steel guitar for his slide parts.</p>
<p>Canned Heat's <a href="/wiki/Alan_Wilson_(musician)" title="Alan Wilson (musician)">Alan Wilson</a> also helped bring slide guitar to rock music in the late 1960s. He used it frequently to create a buzzing delta blues boogie that can be heard on tracks such as <i>London Blues</i>, <i>I Love My Baby</i>, <i>Sandy Blues</i>, and others, and can also be seen during their performances at the Monterey Pop Festival on Rollin and Tumblin' and at Woodstock During Woodstock Boogie and On The Road Again.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a> experimented with slide guitar during the latter half of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' career, first using the technique on an early <a href="/wiki/Outtake" title="Outtake">outtake</a> recording of <i><a href="/wiki/Strawberry_Fields_Forever" title="Strawberry Fields Forever">Strawberry Fields Forever</a></i>, in 1966. The 1965 songs <i><a href="/wiki/Drive_My_Car" title="Drive My Car">Drive My Car</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Run_for_Your_Life_(Beatles_song)" title="Run for Your Life (Beatles song)">Run For Your Life</a></i> have slide guitar, but may have been played by Paul McCartney and John Lennon, respectively). Harrison later used slide extensively in his solo career, on songs such as <i><a href="/wiki/My_Sweet_Lord" title="My Sweet Lord">My Sweet Lord</a></i>, <i>Give Me Love (Give Me Peace On Earth)</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Love_(George_Harrison_song)" title="This Is Love (George Harrison song)">This Is Love</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Cheer_Down" title="Cheer Down">Cheer Down</a></i>. He played slide in the <a href="/wiki/Traveling_Wilburys" title="Traveling Wilburys">Traveling Wilburys</a> as well as on The Beatles' 1995 and 1996 reunion singles <i><a href="/wiki/Free_as_a_Bird" title="Free as a Bird">Free as a Bird</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Love_(The_Beatles_song)" title="Real Love (The Beatles song)">Real Love</a></i>.</p>
<p>Like Alan Wilson, <a href="/wiki/Duane_Allman" title="Duane Allman">Duane Allman</a> played a key role in bringing slide guitar into rock music, through his work with <a href="/wiki/The_Allman_Brothers_Band" title="The Allman Brothers Band">The Allman Brothers Band</a>, specifically on the 1971 live album <i><a href="/wiki/At_Fillmore_East" title="At Fillmore East">At Fillmore East</a></i> and with <a href="/wiki/Derek_and_the_Dominos" title="Derek and the Dominos">Derek and the Dominos</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Layla_and_Other_Assorted_Love_Songs" title="Layla and Other Assorted Love Songs">Layla and Other Assorted Love Songs</a></i> album. Other slide guitarists such as <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck" title="Jeff Beck">Jeff Beck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Raitt" title="Bonnie Raitt">Bonnie Raitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rory_Gallagher" title="Rory Gallagher">Rory Gallagher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Wood" title="Ronnie Wood">Ronnie Wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rod_Price" title="Rod Price">Rod Price</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Gibbons" title="Billy Gibbons">Billy Gibbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Rossington" title="Gary Rossington">Gary Rossington</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lynyrd_Skynyrd" title="Lynyrd Skynyrd">Lynyrd Skynyrd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Walsh" title="Joe Walsh">Joe Walsh</a> used middle finger and later in the mid 80s, used a brass slide.</p>
<p>Allman extended the expressive range of the slide guitar by incorporating the <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">harmonica</a> effects of <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Boy_Williamson_II" title="Sonny Boy Williamson II">Sonny Boy Williamson II</a>, most clearly in the Allman Brothers' <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover version</a> of Sonny Boy's <a href="/wiki/One_Way_Out_(song)" title="One Way Out (song)">"One Way Out"</a>, heard on their album <i><a href="/wiki/Eat_a_Peach" title="Eat a Peach">Eat a Peach</a></i>. Allman's recordings were a seminal influence on <a href="/wiki/Derek_Trucks" title="Derek Trucks">Derek Trucks</a>, a skilled slide player who has risen to international prominence as a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allman_Brothers_Band" title="Allman Brothers Band">Allman Brothers Band</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derek_Trucks_Band" title="Derek Trucks Band">Derek Trucks Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tedeschi_Trucks_Band" title="Tedeschi Trucks Band">Tedeschi Trucks Band</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>'s touring band.</p>
<p>Most recently lap style slide has been reborn via artists like <a href="/wiki/Ben_Harper" title="Ben Harper">Ben Harper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_White" title="Jack White">Jack White</a>, Sean Kirkwood and <a href="/wiki/Xavier_Rudd" title="Xavier Rudd">Xavier Rudd</a> - both Harper and Rudd are players of <a href="/wiki/Weissenborn" title="Weissenborn">Weissenborn</a> slide guitars, the former using original early 1900s instruments long with modern-day variations such as his own co-designed Asher signature model, the latter using modern reproductions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Slides may be used on any guitar, but slides generally and steels in particular are often used on instruments specifically made to play in this manner. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steel_guitar" title="Steel guitar">Steel guitars</a></li>
<li>Many <a href="/wiki/Resonator_guitar" title="Resonator guitar">resonator guitars</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Dobro" title="Dobro">Dobros</a> and their descendants</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lap_slide_guitar" title="Lap slide guitar">Lap slide guitars</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Weissenborn" title="Weissenborn">Weissenborns</a> and their descendants</li>
<li>Conventional <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitars</a></li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lapstyle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="296" data-file-width="400" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Lapstyle.jpg/250px-Lapstyle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Lapstyle.jpg/375px-Lapstyle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Lapstyle.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lapstyle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Wooden resonator guitar played with a steel, angled to form a chord unavailable from straight open tuning.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An ordinary guitar, either electric or acoustic, can be used for playing slide. Often, the strings are raised a little higher off the fingerboard than they would be for conventional guitar playing—especially if the player isn't going to use the free fingers for fretting. An <i>extension nut</i> may be used to achieve the higher string height at the peghead end of the fingerboard. This is just a normal nut, with the slots filed less deeply, and often in a straight line rather than following the radius of the fretboard. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lap_steel" title="Lap steel">lap steel</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedal_steel" title="Pedal steel">pedal steel</a> are guitars that have evolved especially for playing slide in the horizontal position. Resophonic or <a href="/wiki/Resonator_guitar" title="Resonator guitar">resonator guitars</a> have often been employed for slide playing, typically held horizontally. They are sometimes known as <a href="/wiki/Dobro" title="Dobro">Dobros</a> after the Dopyera brothers, whose company first made them. <i><a href="/wiki/National_String_Instrument_Corporation" title="National String Instrument Corporation">National</a></i> is another brand. In resonator guitars, rather than the sound being produced by the body's hollow, a special bridge transfers the vibrations from the strings to a metal cone placed inside the body.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slides">Slides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Slides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CARB%2BIDE.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/CARB%2BIDE.png/220px-CARB%2BIDE.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/CARB%2BIDE.png/330px-CARB%2BIDE.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/CARB%2BIDE.png/440px-CARB%2BIDE.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CARB%2BIDE.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of a carbon fiber guitar slide made by PickHeaven.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A slide can be made with any type of smooth hard material that allows tones to resonate. The slide's <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> (in terms of <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and wall thickness) cause differences in <a href="/wiki/Sustain" title="Sustain">sustain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">loudness</a>, while the surface structure and material affect tonal clarity and timbre. Square, <a href="/wiki/Bevel" title="Bevel">beveled</a> or rounded edges may allow a player to apply different techniques, while <a href="/wiki/Ground_glass_joint" title="Ground glass joint">tapered</a> rather than straight sides may help improve control and cause less damping. Pedal steel players may prefer using tonebars, which have one capped end. One recent development is the rise of hybrid slides. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_Fiber" title="Carbon Fiber">Carbon Fiber</a> is a great material for a slide due to its unique clarity and sustain, while maintaining comfort. Glass Moonshine slides are made of glass, but have a porous ceramic interior that helps prevent slipping; other slides have been designed to reduce the weight of brass or porcelain slides by using a lightweight interior, while still others are made of glass on the front and of metal on the back to allow easy switching. One can use a solid metal bar or rod, laid across the strings of the guitar and held by the fingers of the fretting hand being laid on it to either side, parallel to it. Pipes, and stones have also been used to good effect, as have rings and spoons. Even a knife can reportedly be used: "As he played, he pressed a knife on the strings of the guitar in a manner popularised by Hawaiian guitarists who used steel bars. The effect was unforgettable." ―<a href="/wiki/W._C._Handy" title="W. C. Handy">W. C. Handy</a> on his first hearing slide guitar, a blues player in the <a href="/wiki/Tutwiler,_Mississippi" title="Tutwiler, Mississippi">Tutwiler, Mississippi</a> train station.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Makers">Makers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Makers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nick_Manoloff" title="Nick Manoloff">Nick Manoloff</a> was a pioneer in the making, patenting and commercially marketing slides as early as 1937. In 1989, Terrie Lambert invented the Moonshine (<a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>) slide that produces a timbre in between that of brass and glass, and the Mudslide (<a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>) slide, which like <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> slides is quite heavy, producing richer, fuller and resonating tones with more <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">harmonics</a>. As a result, they are often used in blues music. The Moonshine and Mudslide slides are glazed on the outside but porous on the inside so that finger moisture is absorbed, preventing slippage. Metals such as <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chrome_plating" title="Chrome plating">chrome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> cause a bright penetrating sound and are mostly used with electric guitars, among others for rock music. Necks cut from bottles, segments of copper or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PVC" title="PVC">PVC</a> plumbing pipe, small glass medicine bottles, and even deep length wrench sockets are commonly used by those who do not choose to buy a slide.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">Technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>
</td>

<p>The slide is pressed against the strings—lightly, so as not to touch the strings to the fretboard, and parallel to the frets. The pitch of the strings can then be continuously varied by moving the slide up and down the fingerboard. The usual limitation in fretted guitar playing of twelve pitches per octave does not apply. The technique lends itself to <a href="/wiki/Glissando" title="Glissando">glissandi</a> (swoops up or down to a note); in addition it has the ability to evoke sounds of the human voice or natural noises. Another strength of the technique is its vibrato, which is easily achieved by oscillating the hand so that the slide goes quickly back and forth.</p>
<p>The major limitation of slide playing is of course that only one <a href="/wiki/Guitar_chord" title="Guitar chord">chord shape</a> is easily available: whatever the strings are tuned to going straight across. Other two-note intervals can be played by angling the bar. Many slide <a href="/wiki/Guitarist" title="Guitarist">guitarists</a> also use their free fingers to fret the strings to add that sound. Using the free fingers opens up the possibility of playing chord shapes other than the straight line given by the slide. One strategy is to use the free fingers for <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_guitar" title="Rhythm guitar">rhythm work</a>, and intersperse this with lead phrases played with the slide.</p>
<p>The guitar may be held in the normal guitar-playing position (that is, with the face of the guitar more-or-less vertical) or it may be held <a href="/wiki/Lap_slide_guitar" title="Lap slide guitar">flat</a>, with the face of the guitar horizontal. In the latter case the guitar may sit flat in one's lap or on a stool, face up, or held in this position by a strap, and played standing up. If holding the guitar in the normal vertical position, it is more common to use the tube type of slide. In the horizontal approach, solid bars are more commonly used, and the grip is overhand: the hand is not wrapped around the slide, the index finger is nearest the bridge, the little finger nearest the nut, fingers pointing away from the chest.</p>
<p>Usually, a slide player will use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_tuning" title="Open tuning">open tuning</a>, although standard tuning is sometimes used. In open tuning the strings are tuned to sound a <a href="/wiki/Chord_(music)" title="Chord (music)">chord</a> when not fretted. The chord tuned to is most often major. Open tunings commonly used with slide include Open D or "Vestapol" tuning: D-A-d-f#-a-d; and Open G or "Spanish" tuning: D-G-d-g-b-d. Open E and Open A, formed by raising each of those tunings a whole tone, are also common. These tunings can be traced back to the 19th century through the banjo, predating the Hawaiian guitar. Other tunings are used as well, in particular the 'drop D' tuning is used by many electric slide players as this tuning allows for power chords in the bass strings and conventional tuning 'navigation' for the rest.</p>
<p>Slide guitar is most often fingerpicked, with or without plastic or metal picks on the thumb and fingers. However some players use a flatpick (<a href="/wiki/Plectrum" title="Plectrum">plectrum</a>).</p>
<p>The bottleneck or tube type of slide is usually worn over the ring (3rd) or little (4th) finger, while others wear it over the middle (2nd) finger. Wearing it on the 4th finger has the advantage of leaving one more finger free to fret notes if desired. However some players feel that they get better control using the ring or middle fingers. Most instructors recommend letting one or more of the fingers behind the slide rest lightly on the strings to help mute unwanted vibrations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_slide_guitar_system">Double slide guitar system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Double slide guitar system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="685" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg/220px-Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg/330px-Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg/440px-Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Double_Slide_Guitar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brian Cober's double slide technique in action</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A relatively new technique,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> expanding the musical range and sonic capabilities of slide guitar, is the system of double slide guitar. It was invented by Brian Cober,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (August 2012)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> a Canadian blues musician. In double slide, the first slide is placed on the middle finger (usually a modified steel bar that can be put on the finger), and a modified thumb slide is put on the thumb that is able to cover two strings. Double slide is meant to be played on a six-string lap guitar (or a regular six-string guitar modified with the strings raised for high action like a lap guitar), usually tuned to open E tuning. The double slide guitar system enables the player to play chords not heard in open tunings, such as minor chords, dominant seventh chords, etc. and provides a greater use of technique in soloing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Will_Ray" title="Will Ray">Will Ray</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hellecasters" title="Hellecasters">Hellecasters</a> uses a similar technique, wearing "stealth" pinky-type slides on either hand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years, some guitarists have developed the bottleneck technique further by introducing other guitar effects. Commonly overdrive from tube amplifiers, and distortion effects pedals are commonly employed to produce bluesy and swampy tones.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bass">Bass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slide_guitar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John-Paul-Jones1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3143" data-file-width="2848" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/John-Paul-Jones1.jpg/220px-John-Paul-Jones1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/John-Paul-Jones1.jpg/330px-John-Paul-Jones1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/John-Paul-Jones1.jpg/440px-John-Paul-Jones1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John-Paul-Jones1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Jones_(musician)" title="John Paul Jones (musician)">John Paul Jones</a> playing slide</div>

<p>A few musicians have used slides with <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass guitar</a>: <b>slide bass</b>. <a href="/wiki/Mark_Sandman" title="Mark Sandman">Mark Sandman</a> was probably the best known proponent (with <a href="/wiki/Morphine_(band)" title="Morphine (band)">Morphine</a>, he performed primarily on a custom two-string slide bass guitar). <a href="/wiki/Bill_Laswell" title="Bill Laswell">Bill Laswell</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Weaver_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Weaver (musician) (page does not exist)">Robert Weaver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Rutmanis" title="Kevin Rutmanis">Kevin Rutmanis</a>, Marc Sloan, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Wood_(jazz_musician)" title="Chris Wood (jazz musician)">Chris Wood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Lessard" title="Stefan Lessard">Stefan Lessard</a> have also played slide bass. <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Jones_(musician)" title="John Paul Jones (musician)">John Paul Jones</a> of <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a> has performed on a custom-made bass <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lap_steel" title="Lap steel">lap steel</a>. Timo Shanko of G. Love &amp; Special Sauce, incorporates slide playing on electric bass. Jazz bassist <a href="/wiki/Victor_Wooten" title="Victor Wooten">Victor Wooten</a> occasionally uses a slide for soloing during his live performances. Similarly to Jones, Mark Robbins, bass player and song writer from Joan, plays a number of songs in the standing lap slide style .</p>
