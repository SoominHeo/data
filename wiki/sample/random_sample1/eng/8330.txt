<p><b>Sludge metal</b> (sometimes referred to simply as "<b>sludge</b>") is an <a href="/wiki/Extreme_metal" title="Extreme metal">extreme</a> genre of music that melds elements of <a href="/wiki/Doom_metal" title="Doom metal">doom metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">Southern rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Eyehategod_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Eyehategod-2">[2]</a></sup> Sludge metal is typically harsh and abrasive, often featuring shouted or screamed vocals, heavily <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(music)" title="Distortion (music)">distorted</a> instruments and sharply contrasting <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempos</a>. While the style was anticipated by the <a href="/wiki/Melvins" title="Melvins">Melvins</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>, many of its earliest pioneers were from the city of <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sludge_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sludge metal generally combines the slow <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempos</a>, heavy rhythms and dark, pessimistic atmosphere of doom metal with the aggression, shouted vocals and occasional fast tempos of hardcore punk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Eyehategod_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Eyehategod-2">[2]</a></sup> As <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> put it, "The shorthand term for the kind of rock descending from early <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a> and late <a href="/wiki/Black_Flag_(band)" title="Black Flag (band)">Black Flag</a> is <i>sludge</i>, because it's so slow and dense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Hammer" title="Metal Hammer">Metal Hammer</a></i>, sludge metal "[s]pawned from a messy collision of Black Sabbath’s downcast <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">metal</a>, Black Flag’s tortured hardcore and the sub/dom grind of early <a href="/wiki/Swans_(band)" title="Swans (band)">Swans</a>, shaken up with lashings of cheap whisky and bad pharmaceuticals".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many sludge bands compose slow-paced songs that contain brief hardcore passages<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering-5">[5]</a></sup> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Eyehategod" title="Eyehategod">Eyehategod</a>'s "Depress" and "My Name Is God"). <a href="/wiki/Mike_Williams_(singer)" title="Mike Williams (singer)">Mike Williams</a>, a founder of the sludge style and member of Eyehategod, suggests that "the moniker of sludge apparently has to do with the slowness, the dirtiness, the filth and general feel of decadence the tunes convey".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t43_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-t43-6">[6]</a></sup> However, some bands emphasize fast tempos throughout their music. The string instruments (<a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass guitar</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Guitar_tunings" title="Guitar tunings">down-tuned</a> and heavily <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(music)" title="Distortion (music)">distorted</a> and are often played with large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Audio_feedback" title="Audio feedback">feedback</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Dopesick_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Dopesick-7">[7]</a></sup> to produce a thick yet abrasive sound. Additionally, guitar solos are often absent. Drumming is often performed in typical doom metal fashion. Drummers may employ hardcore <a href="/wiki/D-beat" title="D-beat">d-beat</a> or double-kick drumming during faster passages, or through the thick breakdowns (which are characteristic of the sludge sound). Vocals are usually shouted or screamed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Dopesick_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Dopesick-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-TakeasNeededforPain_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-TakeasNeededforPain-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-SewnMouthSecrets_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-SewnMouthSecrets-9">[9]</a></sup> and lyrics are generally pessimistic in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Crowbar-Crowbar_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Crowbar-Crowbar-10">[10]</a></sup> Suffering, drug abuse,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-ToaFrown_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-ToaFrown-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Sore_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Sore-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-WhentheKiteStringPops_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-WhentheKiteStringPops-13">[13]</a></sup> politics and anger towards society are common lyrical themes.</p>
<p>Many sludge metal bands from the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a> incorporate <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">Southern rock</a> influences,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-IntheNameofSuffering-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Dopesick_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Dopesick-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-TakeasNeededforPain_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-TakeasNeededforPain-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-CoC_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-CoC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-SoilentGreen_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-SoilentGreen-15">[15]</a></sup> although not all sludge bands share this style. There is some controversy as to whether the term refers to only the style emerging from <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> and later the American South more broadly, or to "a complete consciousness in the heads of like-minded Black Flag/Black Sabbath influenced scenes and individuals all over the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t43_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-t43-6">[6]</a></sup> So-called "atmospheric" sludge bands adopt a more experimental approach and compose music with an <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient</a> atmosphere, reduced aggression and philosophical lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Isis_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Isis-16">[16]</a></sup> Due to the similarities between sludge and <a href="/wiki/Stoner_rock" title="Stoner rock">stoner metal</a>, there is often a crossover between the two genres,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Gateway_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Gateway-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Kylesa_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Kylesa-18">[18]</a></sup> but sludge metal generally avoids stoner metal's usage of <a href="/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">psychedelia</a>. Sludge metal also bears some musical and lyrical resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Crust_punk" title="Crust punk">crust punk</a>, due to the usage of political lyrics and thick, "dirty" guitar sounds.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sludge_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors">Precursors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sludge_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Precursors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Flag_(band)" title="Black Flag (band)">Black Flag</a>, musicians cited by pioneers of the style as influential include <a href="/wiki/Lynyrd_Skynyrd" title="Lynyrd Skynyrd">Lynyrd Skynyrd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Frost" title="Celtic Frost">Celtic Frost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Ginn" title="Greg Ginn">Greg Ginn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trouble_(band)" title="Trouble (band)">Trouble</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnivore_(band)" title="Carnivore (band)">Carnivore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vitus_(band)" title="Saint Vitus (band)">Saint Vitus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gore_(band)" title="Gore (band)">Gore</a>, Righteous Pigs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t43_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-t43-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amebix" title="Amebix">Amebix</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t44_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-t44-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Swans_(band)" title="Swans (band)">Swans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t44_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-t44-19">[19]</a></sup> Early sludge metal groups also borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_music" title="Industrial music">industrial music</a> of <a href="/wiki/SPK_(band)" title="SPK (band)">SPK</a>, <a href="/wiki/Throbbing_Gristle" title="Throbbing Gristle">Throbbing Gristle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chrome_(band)" title="Chrome (band)">Chrome</a> and Swans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t43_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-t43-6">[6]</a></sup> The beginnings of sludge have been traced to the "slow punk" of <a href="/wiki/Flipper_(band)" title="Flipper (band)">Flipper</a>, Swans' 1984 album <i><a href="/wiki/Cop_(album)" title="Cop (album)">Cop</a></i>, and Black Flag's album <i><a href="/wiki/My_War" title="My War">My War</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t44_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-t44-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Perhaps the most significant influence was <a href="/wiki/Melvins" title="Melvins">Melvins</a>, a band from the state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>. Their earliest releases, <i><a href="/wiki/Melvins!_(album)" title="Melvins! (album)">Six Songs</a></i> (1986) and <i><a href="/wiki/Gluey_Porch_Treatments" title="Gluey Porch Treatments">Gluey Porch Treatments</a></i> (1987), are often regarded as the first sludge records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t44_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-t44-19">[19]</a></sup> At this time, the band was also an important member of the Washington <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> scene. Another prominent band from the Washington grunge scene, <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Chains" title="Alice in Chains">Alice in Chains</a> have also been influential to early sludge metal with their second album <i><a href="/wiki/Dirt_(Alice_in_Chains_album)" title="Dirt (Alice in Chains album)">Dirt</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VultureHound_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-VultureHound-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMatters_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopMatters-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Neurosis_(band)" title="Neurosis (band)">Neurosis</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>, were also significant early practitioners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the beginning of the 1990s, a number of bands from <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> with <a href="/wiki/Music_of_New_Orleans#Heavy_metal" title="Music of New Orleans">its metal scene</a>) took these influences and developed the style that would be known as sludge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eyehategod" title="Eyehategod">Eyehategod</a> (formed in 1988),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Eyehategod_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Eyehategod-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crowbar_(US_band)" title="Crowbar (US band)">Crowbar</a> (formed in 1989 as The Slugs)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Crowbar_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Crowbar-23">[23]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Acid_Bath" title="Acid Bath">Acid Bath</a> (formed in 1991)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-AcidBath_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-AcidBath-24">[24]</a></sup> pioneered this movement. On the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buzzov*en" title="Buzzov*en">Buzzov*en</a> (formed in 1989),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Buzzov-en_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Buzzov-en-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/16_(band)" title="16 (band)">16</a> (formed in 1990) and <a href="/wiki/Grief_(band)" title="Grief (band)">Grief</a> (formed in 1991)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> adopted a slower-paced approach to the emerging genre.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Phil_Anselmo" title="Phil Anselmo">Phil Anselmo</a>,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Back in those days, everything in the underground was fast, fast, fast. It was the rule of the day. But when the Melvins came out with their first record, <i>Gluey Porch Treatments</i>, it really broke the mold, especially in New Orleans. People began to appreciate playing slower. With that, all the old Black Sabbath came back around and then you start digging and you come to your <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vitus_(band)" title="Saint Vitus (band)">Saint Vitus</a>, your <a href="/wiki/Witchfinder_General_(band)" title="Witchfinder General (band)">Witchfinder General</a>, your <a href="/wiki/Pentagram_(band)" title="Pentagram (band)">Pentagram</a>, etc.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_developments">Subsequent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sludge_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subsequent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sludge metal subsequently spread throughout the Southern and Eastern United States.</p>
<p>Jose Carlos Santos notes a focus shift as a result of the impact of the British group <a href="/wiki/Iron_Monkey_(band)" title="Iron Monkey (band)">Iron Monkey</a>'s first album in 1997:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Coincidence or not, it seemed like the sludge floodgates opened to the rest of the world, and in the past decade small pockets, or mini-scenes, can be spotted in just about any country you'd care to mention.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssp2_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssp2-28">[28]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>These include the Japanese group <a href="/wiki/Corrupted_(band)" title="Corrupted (band)">Corrupted</a> and contemporary American groups such as <a href="/wiki/Dumb_Numbers" title="Dumb Numbers">Dumb Numbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lair_of_the_Minotaur" title="Lair of the Minotaur">Lair of the Minotaur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Man_Gloom" title="Old Man Gloom">Old Man Gloom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kylesa" title="Kylesa">Kylesa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssp2_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ssp2-28">[28]</a></sup> In addition, the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> has been identified as a major source of new sludge groups such as <a href="/wiki/Mastodon_(band)" title="Mastodon (band)">Mastodon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baroness_(band)" title="Baroness (band)">Baroness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Tusk_(band)" title="Black Tusk (band)">Black Tusk</a>, and Kylesa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssp2_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-ssp2-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the late 1990s, many sludge metal bands began to incorporate <a href="/wiki/Post-rock" title="Post-rock">post-rock</a> elements into their music. This post-rock/sludge crossover was greatly inspired by the experimental style of <a href="/wiki/Neurosis_(band)" title="Neurosis (band)">Neurosis</a> during the early to mid 1990s, and is performed by prominent bands such as <a href="/wiki/Isis_(band)" title="Isis (band)">Isis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Isis_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Isis-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_Luna" title="Cult of Luna">Cult of Luna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelican_(band)" title="Pelican (band)">Pelican</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burst_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-burst-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
