<p><b>Sadness</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Psychological_pain" title="Psychological pain">emotional pain</a> associated with, or characterized by, feelings of disadvantage, loss, despair, grief, helplessness, disappointment and sorrow. An individual experiencing sadness may become quiet or lethargic, and withdraw themselves from others. An example of severe sadness is depression. <a href="/wiki/Crying" title="Crying">Crying</a> is often an indication of sadness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sadness is one of the "six basic emotions" described by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ekman" title="Paul Ekman">Paul Ekman</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anger" title="Anger">anger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surprise_(emotion)" title="Surprise (emotion)">surprise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">fear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disgust" title="Disgust">disgust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sadness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg/220px-Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg/330px-Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg/440px-Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sadness_at_the_beach.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Profoundly depressed man on beach in morning gloom with head in hands.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sadness is a common experience in childhood. Some families may have a (conscious or unconscious) rule that sadness is "not allowed",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Robin_Skynner" title="Robin Skynner">Robin Skynner</a> has suggested that this may cause problems, arguing that with sadness "screened off", people can become shallow and <a href="/wiki/Mania" title="Mania">manic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Pediatrician <a href="/wiki/T._Berry_Brazelton" title="T. Berry Brazelton">T. Berry Brazelton</a> suggests that acknowledging sadness can make it easier for families to address more serious emotional problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sadness is part of the normal process of the child separating from an early symbiosis with the mother and becoming more independent. Every time a child separates a little more, he or she will have to cope with a small loss. If the mother cannot allow the minor distress involved, the child may never learn how to deal with sadness by themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Brazelton argues that too much cheering a child up devalues the emotion of sadness for them;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Selma_Fraiberg" title="Selma Fraiberg">Selma Fraiberg</a> suggests that it is important to respect a child's right to experience a loss fully and deeply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mahler" title="Margaret Mahler">Margaret Mahler</a> also saw the ability to feel sadness as an emotional achievement, as opposed for example to warding it off through restless hyperactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D._W._Winnicott" title="D. W. Winnicott">D. W. Winnicott</a> similarly saw in sad crying the psychological root of valuable musical experiences in later life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neuroanatomy">Neuroanatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sadness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Neuroanatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Journal_of_Psychiatry" title="American Journal of Psychiatry">American Journal of Psychiatry</a></i>, sadness was found to be associated with "increases in bilateral activity within the vicinity of the middle and posterior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temporal_cortex" title="Temporal cortex">temporal cortex</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateral_cerebellum" title="Lateral cerebellum">lateral cerebellum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerebellar_vermis" title="Cerebellar vermis">cerebellar vermis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midbrain" title="Midbrain">midbrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Putamen" title="Putamen">putamen</a>, and caudate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Jose V. Pardo has his M.D and Ph.D and leads a research program in cognitive neuroscience. Using positron emission tomography (PET) Pardo and his colleagues were able to provoke sadness among seven normal men and women by asking them to think about sad things. They observed increased brain activity in the bilateral inferior and orbitofrontal cortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In a study that induced sadness in subjects by showing emotional film clips, the feeling was correlated with significant increases in regional brain activity, especially in the prefrontal cortex, in the region called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brodmann%27s_area_9" title="Brodmann's area 9">Brodmann's area 9</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>. A significant increase in activity was also observed in the bilateral anterior temporal structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coping_mechanisms">Coping mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sadness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coping mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coping_(psychology)" title="Coping (psychology)">Coping (psychology)</a></div>
<p>People deal with sadness in different ways, and it is an important emotion because it helps to motivate people to deal with their situation. Some coping mechanisms include: getting social support and/or spending time with a pet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> creating a list, or engaging in some activity to express sadness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some individuals, when feeling sad, may exclude themselves from a social setting, so as to take the time to recover from the feeling.</p>
<p>While being one of the moods people most want to shake, sadness can sometimes be perpetuated by the very coping strategies chosen, such as ruminating, "drowning one's sorrows", or permanently isolating oneself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As alternative ways of coping with sadness to the above, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_behavioral_therapy" title="Cognitive behavioral therapy">cognitive behavioral therapy</a> suggests instead either challenging one's negative thoughts, or scheduling some positive event as a distraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Being attentive to, and patient with, one's sadness may also be a way for people to learn through solitude;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> while emotional support to help people stay with their sadness can be further helpful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Such an approach is fueled by the <a href="/wiki/Object_relations_theory" title="Object relations theory">underlying belief</a> that loss (when felt wholeheartedly) can lead to a new sense of aliveness, and to a re-engagement with the outside world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pupil_empathy">Pupil empathy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sadness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pupil empathy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pupil" title="Pupil">Pupil</a> size may be an indicator of sadness. A sad <a href="/wiki/Facial_expression" title="Facial expression">facial expression</a> with small pupils is judged to be more intensely sad as the pupil size decreases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A person's own pupil size also <a href="/wiki/Mirror_neuron" title="Mirror neuron">mirrors</a> this and becomes smaller when viewing sad faces with small pupils. No parallel effect exists when people look at neutral, happy or angry expressions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The greater degree to which a person's pupils mirror another predicts a person's greater score on <a href="/wiki/Empathy" title="Empathy">empathy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_Wilson_Critchley_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_Wilson_Critchley-23">[23]</a></sup> However, in disorders such as autism and psychopathy facial expressions that represent sadness may be subtle, which may show a need for a more non-linguistic situation to affect their level of empathy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_Wilson_Critchley_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_Wilson_Critchley-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the Renaissance, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Spenser" title="Edmund Spenser">Edmund Spenser</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Faerie_Queene" title="The Faerie Queene">The Faerie Queene</a></i> endorsed sadness as a marker of spiritual commitment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>, sadness is distinguished from unhappiness,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> to exemplify <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>'s preference for a sad, but settled determination, as opposed to what he saw as the shallower temptations of either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Despair" title="Despair">despair</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hope" title="Hope">hope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Julia_Kristeva" title="Julia Kristeva">Julia Kristeva</a> considered that "a diversification of moods, variety in sadness, refinement in <a href="/wiki/Sorrow_(emotion)" title="Sorrow (emotion)">sorrow</a> or mourning are the imprint of a humanity that is surely not triumphant but subtle, ready to fight and creative".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
