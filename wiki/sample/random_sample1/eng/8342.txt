<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Win–loss record (pitching)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Softball" title="Softball">softball</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a>'s <b>win–loss record</b> (also referred to simply as their <b>record</b>) indicates the number of wins (denoted "W") and losses (denoted "L") they have been credited with. For example, a 20–10 win–loss record would represent 20 wins and 10 losses.</p>
<p>In each game, one pitcher on the winning team is awarded a win (the "winning pitcher") and one pitcher on the losing team is given a loss (the "losing pitcher") in their respective statistics. These pitchers are collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Pitchers_of_record" title="Pitchers of record">pitchers of record</a>. The designation of win or loss for a pitcher is known as a <i>decision</i>, and only one pitcher for each team receives a decision. A <a href="/wiki/Starting_pitcher" title="Starting pitcher">starting pitcher</a> who does not receive credit for a win or loss is said to have <i>no decision</i>. In certain situations, another pitcher on the winning team who pitched in <a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">relief</a> of the winning pitcher can be credited with a <a href="/wiki/Save_(baseball)" title="Save (baseball)">save</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hold_(baseball)" title="Hold (baseball)">holds</a> can be awarded to relief pitchers on both sides, but these are never awarded to the same pitcher who is awarded the win.</p>
<p>The decisions are awarded by the <a href="/wiki/Official_scorer" title="Official scorer">official scorer</a> of the game in accordance with the league's rules. The official scorer does not assign a winning or losing pitcher in some games which are <a href="/wiki/Forfeit_(baseball)" title="Forfeit (baseball)">forfeited</a>, such as those that are tied at the time of forfeiture. If the game is tied (a rare event), no pitcher is awarded any decision. A pitcher's winning percentage is calculated by dividing the number of wins by the number of decisions (wins plus losses), and it is commonly expressed with three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decimal_places" title="Decimal places">decimal places</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Winning_pitcher">Winning pitcher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Win%E2%80%93loss_record_(pitching)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Winning pitcher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, the winning pitcher is defined as the pitcher who last pitched prior to the half-inning when the winning team took the lead for the last time.</p>
<p>There are two exceptions to this rule. The first is that a starting pitcher must complete five innings to earn a win (four innings for a game that lasts five innings on defense). If he fails to do so, he is ineligible to be the winning pitcher even if he last pitched prior to the half-inning when his team took the lead for the last time, and the <a href="/wiki/Official_scorer" title="Official scorer">official scorer</a> awards the win to the <a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">relief pitcher</a> who, in the official scorer's judgment, was the most effective.</p>
<p>The second exception applies if the relief pitcher who last pitched prior to the half-inning when the winning team took the lead for the last time was "ineffective in a brief appearance" in the official scorer's judgment, in which case the win is awarded to the succeeding relief pitcher who, in the official scorer's judgment, was the most effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">Major League Baseball All-Star Game</a>, every pitcher is considered as a relief pitcher for the purpose of this rule. For example, a starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Matt_Cain" title="Matt Cain">Matt Cain</a> was awarded the win in the <a href="/wiki/2012_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="2012 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">2012 All-Star Game</a> despite throwing only two innings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Losing_pitcher">Losing pitcher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Win%E2%80%93loss_record_(pitching)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Losing pitcher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The losing pitcher is the pitcher who allows the go-ahead run to reach base for a lead that the winning team never relinquishes. If a pitcher allows a run which gives the opposing team the lead, his team comes back to lead or tie the game, and then the opposing team regains the lead against a subsequent pitcher, the earlier pitcher does not get the loss.</p>
<p>If a pitcher leaves the game with his team in the lead or with the score tied, but with the go-ahead run on base, and this runner subsequently scores the go-ahead run, the pitcher who allowed this runner to reach base is responsible for the loss. This is true regardless of the manner in which this batter originally reached base, and how he subsequently scored. If the relief pitching successfully completes the half-inning without surrendering the go-ahead run, the departed pitcher cannot receive a loss.</p>
<p>For example, on April 13, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Zambrano" title="Carlos Zambrano">Carlos Zambrano</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> was facing the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> in the top of the 5th inning. He was taken out of the game with the Cubs leading 5–4 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bases_loaded" title="Bases loaded">bases loaded</a>. The pitcher who replaced him, <a href="/wiki/Will_Ohman" title="Will Ohman">Will Ohman</a>, proceeded to allow two of the runners on base to score, giving the Reds a 6–5 lead. Although Zambrano was not pitching at the time the runs were scored, he was charged with the loss, as the base runners who scored were his responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Win%E2%80%93loss_record_(pitching)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pitchers who receive the win and the loss are known, collectively, as the <a href="/wiki/Pitchers_of_record" title="Pitchers of record">pitchers of record</a>. A pitcher who starts a game but leaves without earning either a win or a loss (that is, before either team gains or surrenders the ultimate lead) is said to have received a no decision, regardless of his individual performance. A pitcher's total wins and losses are commonly noted together; for instance, a pitching record of 12–10 indicates 12 wins and 10 losses.</p>
<p>In the early years of <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> before 1900 it was common for an exceptional pitcher to win 30 or more games in one season with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Hoss_Radbourn" title="Old Hoss Radbourn">Old Hoss Radbourn</a> of the defunct <a href="/wiki/Providence_Grays" title="Providence Grays">Providence Grays</a> holding the record with 59 wins in 1884. Since 1900, however, pitchers have made fewer and fewer starts and the standard has changed. Gradually, as hitting improved, better pitching was needed. This meant, among other things, throwing the ball much harder, and it became unrealistic to ask a pitcher to throw nearly as hard as he could for over 100 pitches a game without giving him several days to recover.</p>
<p>In the first third of the 20th century (especially after the <a href="/wiki/Live-ball_era" title="Live-ball era">live-ball era</a>), winning 30 games became the rare mark of excellent achievement; this standard diminished to 25 games during the 1940s through 1980s (the only pitcher to win 30 or more games during that time was <a href="/wiki/Denny_McLain" title="Denny McLain">Denny McLain</a> in 1968, in what was an anomalous <a href="/wiki/1968_Major_League_Baseball_season#The_Year_of_the_Pitcher" title="1968 Major League Baseball season">pitching-dominated season</a>).</p>
<p>Since 1990, this has changed even further, as winning 20 or more games in a single season is now achieved by only a handful of pitchers each season. For example, in 2004 only three of the more than five hundred major league pitchers did so. In 2006 and again in 2009, no pitcher in either league won 20 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The last pitcher to win 25 games was <a href="/wiki/Bob_Welch_(baseball)" title="Bob Welch (baseball)">Bob Welch</a> in 1990.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote in 2011 that as advanced statistics have expanded, a pitcher's win-loss record has decreased in importance. For example, <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Félix Hernández">Félix Hernández</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a> in 2010 in spite of a 13–12 record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Many times a win is substantially out of the pitcher's control; even a dominant pitcher cannot record a win if his team does not score any <a href="/wiki/Run_(baseball)" title="Run (baseball)">runs</a> for him. For instance, in 2004, <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a> starting pitcher <a href="/wiki/Ben_Sheets" title="Ben Sheets">Ben Sheets</a> had a losing record of 12–14, despite displaying a league-best 8:1 <a href="/wiki/Strikeout" title="Strikeout">strikeout</a>-to-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walk_(baseball)" title="Walk (baseball)">walk</a> ratio and was among the top 5 pitchers in <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">ERA</a> (2.70) and <a href="/wiki/Walks_plus_hits_per_inning_pitched" title="Walks plus hits per inning pitched">WHIP</a> (0.98).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In addition to their dependence on run support, wins for a starting pitcher are also dependent on <a href="/wiki/Bullpen" title="Bullpen">bullpen</a> support. A starting pitcher can pitch brilliantly, leaving the game with the lead, and then watch helplessly from the dugout as the bullpen blows the save and gives up the lead. That would entitle the starting pitcher to a no-decision instead of a win despite the strong performance, regardless of whether or not the team ends up winning. Starting pitchers on teams with a weak bullpen tend to have fewer wins because of this. Likewise, a pitcher can give a poor performance and give up many runs and leave the game earlier than desired, but still win because his team scored even more runs. Some prefer the <a href="/wiki/Quality_start" title="Quality start">quality start</a> statistic as an indication of how many times a starting pitcher gave his team a realistic chance to win.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
