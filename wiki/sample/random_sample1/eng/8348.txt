<p><b>Shiga Prefecture</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">滋賀県</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shiga-ken</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_Japan" title="Prefectures of Japan">prefecture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, which forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Kansai_region" title="Kansai region">Kansai region</a> in the western part of <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It encircles <a href="/wiki/Lake_Biwa" title="Lake Biwa">Lake Biwa</a>, the largest freshwater lake in Japan. The capital is <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctsu" title="Ōtsu">Ōtsu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_Sites_of_Shiga_Prefecture" title="Historic Sites of Shiga Prefecture">Historic Sites of Shiga Prefecture</a></div>
<p>Shiga was known as <b><a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cmi_Province" title="Ōmi Province">Ōmi Province</a></b> or <b>Gōshū</b> before the prefectural system was established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Omi was a neighbor of <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, at the junction of western and eastern Japan. During the period 667 to 672, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Tenji" title="Emperor Tenji">Emperor Tenji</a> founded a palace in Otsu. In 742, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Sh%C5%8Dmu" title="Emperor Shōmu">Emperor Shōmu</a> founded a palace in <a href="/wiki/Shigaraki,_Shiga" title="Shigaraki, Shiga">Shigaraki</a>. In the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">Heian Period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saich%C5%8D" title="Saichō">Saichō</a> was born in the north of Otsu and founded <a href="/wiki/Enryaku-ji" title="Enryaku-ji">Enryaku-ji</a>, the center of <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">Tendai</a> and one a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> <a href="/wiki/Historic_Monuments_of_Ancient_Kyoto_(Kyoto,_Uji_and_Otsu_Cities)" title="Historic Monuments of Ancient Kyoto (Kyoto, Uji and Otsu Cities)">and monument of Ancient Kyoto</a> now.</p>
<p>In the Medieval Period, the <a href="/wiki/Sasaki_clan" title="Sasaki clan">Sasaki clan</a> ruled Omi, and afterward the <a href="/wiki/Rokkaku_clan" title="Rokkaku clan">Rokkaku clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ky%C5%8Dgoku_clan" title="Kyōgoku clan">Kyōgoku clan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azai_clan" title="Azai clan">Azai clans</a> ruled Omi. In the 1570s, <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a> subjugated Omi and built <a href="/wiki/Azuchi_Castle" title="Azuchi Castle">Azuchi Castle</a> on the eastern shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Biwa" title="Lake Biwa">Lake Biwa</a> in 1579. <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dd%C5%8D_Takatora" title="Tōdō Takatora">Tōdō Takatora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gam%C5%8D_Ujisato" title="Gamō Ujisato">Gamō Ujisato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oichi" title="Oichi">Oichi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yodo-dono" title="Yodo-dono">Yodo-dono</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohatsu" title="Ohatsu">Ohatsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oeyo" title="Oeyo">Oeyo</a> were Omi notables in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sengoku_Period" title="Sengoku Period">Sengoku Period</a>. In those times, <a href="/wiki/Ninja" title="Ninja">Ninja</a> were active in Kōka (See also <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dga-ry%C5%AB" title="Kōga-ryū">Kōga-ryū</a>).</p>
<p>In 1600, <a href="/wiki/Ishida_Mitsunari" title="Ishida Mitsunari">Ishida Mitsunari</a>, born in the east of Nagahama and based in <a href="/wiki/Sawayama_Castle" title="Sawayama Castle">Sawayama Castle</a>, made war against <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu" title="Tokugawa Ieyasu">Tokugawa Ieyasu</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sekigahara,_Gifu" title="Sekigahara, Gifu">Sekigahara, Gifu</a>. After the battle, Ieyasu made <a href="/wiki/Ii_Naomasa" title="Ii Naomasa">Ii Naomasa</a> a new lord of Sawayama. Naomasa established the <a href="/wiki/Hikone_Domain" title="Hikone Domain">Hikone Domain</a>, later famous for <a href="/wiki/Ii_Naosuke" title="Ii Naosuke">Ii Naosuke</a>. Ii Naosuke became the Tokugawa shogunate's <a href="/wiki/Tair%C5%8D" title="Tairō">Tairō</a> and concluded commercial treaties with the Western powers and thus ended Japan's isolation from the world in the 19th century. Besides the Hikone Domain, many domains ruled Omi such as <a href="/wiki/Zeze_Domain" title="Zeze Domain">Zeze</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_the_han_system" title="Abolition of the han system">abolition of the han system</a>, eight prefectures were formed in Omi. They were unified into Shiga Prefecture in September 1872. "Shiga Prefecture" was named after "<a href="/wiki/Shiga_District,_Shiga" title="Shiga District, Shiga">Shiga District</a>" because Otsu belonged to the district until 1898. From August 1876 to February 1881, southern Fukui Prefecture had been incorporated into Shiga Prefecture.</p>
<p>In 2015, Shiga Governor <a href="/wiki/Taiz%C5%8D_Mikazuki" title="Taizō Mikazuki">Taizō Mikazuki</a> conducted a survey asking citizens whether they felt it necessary to change the name of the prefecture, partly to raise its profile as a destination for domestic tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

Lake Biwa in Shiga Prefecture viewed from space</div>

<p>Shiga borders <a href="/wiki/Fukui_Prefecture" title="Fukui Prefecture">Fukui Prefecture</a> in the north, <a href="/wiki/Gifu_Prefecture" title="Gifu Prefecture">Gifu Prefecture</a> in the east, <a href="/wiki/Mie_Prefecture" title="Mie Prefecture">Mie Prefecture</a> in the southeast, and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Prefecture" title="Kyoto Prefecture">Kyoto Prefecture</a> in the west.</p>
<p>Lake Biwa, Japan's largest, is located at the center of this prefecture. It occupies one-sixth of its area. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seta_River" title="Seta River">Seta River</a> flows from Lake Biwa to <a href="/wiki/Osaka_Bay" title="Osaka Bay">Osaka Bay</a> through <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>. This is the only natural river that flows out from the lake. Most other natural rivers flow into the lake. There were many lagoons around Lake Biwa, but most of them were reclaimed in 1940s. One of the preserved lagoons is the wetland <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">水郷</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>suigō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in Omihachiman, and it was selected as the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_Landscapes_of_Japan" title="Cultural Landscapes of Japan">Important Cultural Landscapes</a> in 2006.</p>
<p>The lake divides the prefecture into four different areas: <i>Kohoku</i> (湖北, north of lake) centered Nagahama, <i>Kosei</i> (湖西, west of lake) centered <a href="/wiki/Imazu,_Shiga" title="Imazu, Shiga">Imazu</a>, <i>Kotō</i> (湖東, east of lake) centered Hikone and <i>Konan</i> (湖南, south of lake) centered Otsu.</p>
<p>Plains stretch to the eastern shore of Lake Biwa. The prefecture is enclosed by mountain ranges with the <a href="/wiki/Hira_Mountains" title="Hira Mountains">Hira Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hiei" title="Mount Hiei">Mount Hiei</a> in the west, the <a href="/wiki/Ibuki_Mountains" title="Ibuki Mountains">Ibuki Mountains</a> in the northeast, and the <a href="/wiki/Suzuka_Mountains" title="Suzuka Mountains">Suzuka Mountains</a> in the southeast. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ibuki" title="Mount Ibuki">Mount Ibuki</a> is the highest mountain in Shiga. In <a href="/wiki/Yogo,_Shiga" title="Yogo, Shiga">Yogo</a>, a small lake is famous for the legend of the heavenly robe of an angel <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">天女の羽衣</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Tennin" title="Tennin">tennyo</a> no hagoromo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which is similar to a western <a href="/wiki/Swan_maiden" title="Swan maiden">Swan maiden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shiga's climate sharply varies between north and south. Southern Shiga is usually warm, but northern Shiga is typically cold with high snowfall and hosts many skiing grounds. In Nakanokawachi, the northernmost village of Shiga, snow reached a depth of 5.6 metres (18 ft) in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 1 April 2014, 37% of the total land area of the prefecture was designated as <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_Japan#History" title="List of national parks of Japan">Natural Parks</a> (the highest total of any prefecture), namely the <a href="/wiki/Biwako_Quasi-National_Park" title="Biwako Quasi-National Park">Biwako</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suzuka_Quasi-National_Park" title="Suzuka Quasi-National Park">Suzuka</a> Quasi-National Parks; and <a href="/wiki/Kot%C5%8D_Prefectural_Natural_Park" title="Kotō Prefectural Natural Park">Kotō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kutsuki-Katsuragawa_Prefectural_Natural_Park" title="Kutsuki-Katsuragawa Prefectural Natural Park">Kutsuki-Katsuragawa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mikami-Tanakami-Shigaraki_Prefectural_Natural_Park" title="Mikami-Tanakami-Shigaraki Prefectural Natural Park">Mikami-Tanakami-Shigaraki</a> Prefectural Natural Parks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The current governor of Shiga is <a href="/wiki/Taiz%C5%8D_Mikazuki" title="Taizō Mikazuki">Taizō Mikazuki</a>, a former member of the House of Representatives from Shiga (DPJ, 3rd district), who was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_gubernatorial_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shiga gubernatorial election, 2014 (page does not exist)">elected in July 2014</a> to succeed governor <a href="/wiki/Yukiko_Kada" title="Yukiko Kada">Yukiko Kada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefectural_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shiga Prefectural Assembly (page does not exist)">prefectural assembly</a> has 47 members from 16 electoral districts, and is still elected in unified local elections (last round: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_unified_local_elections,_2011" title="Japanese unified local elections, 2011">2011</a>). As of July 2014, the assembly was composed as follows: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_Democratic_Party_of_Japan" title="Liberal Democratic Party of Japan">LDP</a> 21 members, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Japan" title="Democratic Party of Japan">DPJ</a>/kenmin network 12, Taiwa no kai/Shiga net 5, LDP sasshin no kai 5, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dmeit%C5%8D" title="Kōmeitō">Kōmeitō</a> 2, <a href="/wiki/Your_Party" title="Your Party">Your Party</a> 1, independent 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2500" data-file-width="3750" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg/220px-Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg/330px-Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg/440px-Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Omihachiman_shimmatidori01s3200.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Cultivated areas occupy nearly one-sixth of the prefecture. Rice is the principal crop: over 90 percent of the farmlands are rice fields. Most farms are small, producing only a slight income. Most farmers depend on income from other sources. Eastern Shiga is famous for cattle breeding and southeastern Shiga is famous for <a href="/wiki/Green_tea" title="Green tea">green tea</a>. On Lake Biwa, some people are engaged in fishery and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater_pearl" title="Freshwater pearl">freshwater pearl</a> farming.</p>
<p>Since the Medieval Period, especially in <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, many Shiga people were active in <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a> and were called Ōmi merchants <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">近江商人</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ōmi shōnin, Ōmi akindo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, sometimes Ōmi thieves <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">近江泥棒</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ōmi dorobō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> by other envious merchants. For example, <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Life" title="Nippon Life">Nippon Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Itochu" title="Itochu">Itochu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marubeni" title="Marubeni">Marubeni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takashimaya" title="Takashimaya">Takashimaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wacoal" title="Wacoal">Wacoal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yanmar" title="Yanmar">Yanmar</a> were founded by people from Shiga. In their home towns such as Omihachiman, Hino, <a href="/wiki/Gokash%C5%8D,_Shiga" title="Gokashō, Shiga">Gokashō</a> and Toyosato, their mansions were preserved as tourist attractions.</p>
<p>Beginning in the 1960s, Shiga developed industry, supporting major factories owned by companies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Japan" title="IBM Japan">IBM Japan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_(company)" title="Canon (company)">Canon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yanmar_Diesel" title="Yanmar Diesel">Yanmar Diesel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi" title="Mitsubishi">Mitsubishi</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toray" title="Toray">Toray</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office_(Japan)" title="Cabinet Office (Japan)">Cabinet Office</a>'s statistics in 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary sector of the economy</a> accounted for 44.8% of Gross Shiga Product, the highest proportion in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_scores_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-best_scores-10">[10]</a></sup> Traditional industries include textiles, <a href="/wiki/Shigaraki_ware" title="Shigaraki ware">Shigaraki ware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butsudan" title="Butsudan">Butsudan</a> in Hikone and Nagahama, medicines in Koka, and fan ribs in <a href="/wiki/Adogawa,_Shiga" title="Adogawa, Shiga">Adogawa</a>.</p>

<p>The population is concentrated along the southern shore of Lake Biwa in Otsu city (adjacent to Kyoto) and along the lake's eastern shore in cities such as Kusatsu and Moriyama, which are within commuting distance to Kyoto. The lake's western and northern shores are more rural and resort-oriented with white sand beaches. In recent years, many Brazilians settled in Shiga to work in nearby factories. 26,471 foreigners live in Shiga and 36% of foreigners were Brazilians as of December 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Biwa,_Shiga" title="Biwa, Shiga">Biwa Town</a> (now a part of Nagahama) is a home of The <a href="/wiki/Tonda_Traditional_Bunraku_Puppet_Troupe" title="Tonda Traditional Bunraku Puppet Troupe">Tonda Traditional Bunraku Puppet Troupe</a>. Founded in the 1830s, the group is one of the most active traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Bunraku" title="Bunraku">Bunraku</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_puppet_theater" title="Japanese puppet theater">puppet theaters</a> in Japan outside the National Theater in Osaka. Toyosato and Higashiomi are known to a mecca of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goshu_ondo" title="Goshu ondo">Goshu ondo</a>.</p>
<p>Museums include the Sagawa Art Museum in Moriyama, the Lake Biwa Museum in Kusatsu and the <a href="/wiki/Miho_Museum" title="Miho Museum">Miho Museum</a> in Kōka. In Kōka, a ninja house is preserved as a visitor center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since ancient times, Shigans have eaten fish from Lake Biwa. The most famous lake-food is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narezushi" title="Narezushi">fermented sushi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crucian_carp" title="Crucian carp">crucian carp</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">鮒寿司</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>funa-zushi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. It retains the ancient style of sushi and has a pungent odor. Shiga is also famous for high quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wagy%C5%AB" title="Wagyū">wagyū</a>, Omi beef. The Hikone Domain presented beef as medicinal food to shoguns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition, <i><a href="/wiki/Tsukemono" title="Tsukemono">tsukemono</a></i> of root crops, <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a> <a href="/wiki/Nabemono" title="Nabemono">nabe</a> or mallard <a href="/wiki/Sukiyaki" title="Sukiyaki">sukiyaki</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%B4%A8%E9%8D%8B" title="ja:鴨鍋">鴨鍋</a> or 鴨すき</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kamo-nabe or kamo-suki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in northern Shiga, red colored <a href="/wiki/Konjac" title="Konjac">konjac</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">赤こんにゃく</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>aka konnyaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in Omihachiman, <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dmen" title="Sōmen">sōmen</a> with grilled <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">mackerel</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%84%BC%E9%AF%96%E7%B4%A0%E9%BA%BA" title="ja:焼鯖素麺">焼鯖素麺</a></span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>yaki-saba sōmen</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in Nagahama, and lightly seasoned <a href="/wiki/Champon" title="Champon">champon</a> in Hikone are examples of specific cuisine in Shiga.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Biwako_Broadcasting" title="Biwako Broadcasting">Biwako Broadcasting</a> broadcasts local TV programs. <a href="/wiki/NHK" title="NHK">NHK</a> has a broadcasting station in Otsu. <p>Shiga has many <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> resources, but Shiga is overshadowed by its much more famous neighbor Kyoto. Over four million foreign tourists visited Japan in 2000, but only sixty-five thousand visited Shiga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main gateways to Shiga are the <a href="/wiki/Maibara_Station" title="Maibara Station">Maibara Station</a> in northern Shiga and the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otsu,_Shiga" title="Otsu, Shiga">Otsu</a> in the south. The Maibara Station is about 2 hours and 20 minutes away from the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Station" title="Tokyo Station">Tokyo Station</a> by the Tokaido Shinkansen. It is easy to go to Otsu from Kyoto and Osaka by high-speed trains.</p>
<p>Shiga's most prominent feature is Lake Biwa. The northern shore is especially scenic, such as the cherry blossoms of Kaizu Osaki in spring and the sacred island <a href="/wiki/Chikubu_Island" title="Chikubu Island">Chikubu-shima</a>. The western shore has white sand beaches, popular among Kyotoites during the summer. The scenery of the southern shore, particularly around Otsu, was selected as <i>Ōmi Hakkei</i> or <a href="/wiki/Eight_Views_of_%C5%8Cmi" title="Eight Views of Ōmi">Eight Views of Ōmi</a>, popularized by <a href="/wiki/Hiroshige" title="Hiroshige">Hiroshige</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">ukiyo-e</a>. Most of the original eight views are now almost gone or changed from centuries ago. One remaining view is the Ukimidō "floating temple" building at <a href="/wiki/Mangetsu-ji" title="Mangetsu-ji">Mangetsu-ji</a> temple in Katata, northern Otsu. It was reconstructed with concrete in 1937, but a small temple still stands on the lake near the shore, accessible by a short bridge. Another scene features <a href="/wiki/Ishiyama-dera" title="Ishiyama-dera">Ishiyama-dera</a> temple in southern Otsu, which is also renowned for having a room where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lady_Murasaki" title="Lady Murasaki">Lady Murasaki</a> thought up the plan for some chapters of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tale_of_Genji" title="Tale of Genji">Tale of Genji</a></i>.</p>
<p>The many mountains around the lake offer beautiful views. Mount Hira is a popular picnic spot. Mountain roads like the Oku-Biwako Parkway road up north and the Hiei-zan Driveway and Oku-Hiei Driveway overlooking the southwestern shore. In Otsu, the Otsu Prince Hotel's Top of Otsu restaurant provides a panoramic view of the lake and city. The <i>Michigan</i> paddlewheel boat offers lake cruises.</p>
<p>Besides natural beauty, historical buildings and festivals persons rank among those of national importance. Shiga has 807 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Treasures_of_Japan" title="National Treasures of Japan">National Treasures</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Important_Cultural_Properties_of_Japan" title="Important Cultural Properties of Japan">Important Cultural Properties</a>, that ranks the fourth large number in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_scores_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-best_scores-10">[10]</a></sup> Shiga's most famous historical building is <a href="/wiki/Hikone_Castle" title="Hikone Castle">Hikone Castle</a>, one of four <a href="/wiki/List_of_National_Treasures_of_Japan_(castles)" title="List of National Treasures of Japan (castles)">national treasure castles</a> in the country. The castle tower is well preserved and has many cherry trees. The neighboring city of <a href="/wiki/Nagahama,_Shiga" title="Nagahama, Shiga">Nagahama</a> has <a href="/wiki/Nagahama,_Shiga#Sightseeing" title="Nagahama, Shiga">tourism</a> in addition to its hikiyama festival. Notable shrines include <a href="/wiki/Hiyoshi_Taisha" title="Hiyoshi Taisha">Hiyoshi Taisha</a> in Otsu and <a href="/wiki/Taga-taisha" title="Taga-taisha">Taga-taisha</a> in Taga, which respectively head the seventh and twentieth largest <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine#Shrine_networks" title="Shinto shrine">shrine networks</a> in Japan, at about 4,000 shrines and 260 shrines, respectively.</p>
<p>Unique festivals include the hikiyama festival (floats parade festival), held in ten areas such as <a href="/wiki/Nagahama,_Shiga" title="Nagahama, Shiga">Nagahama</a>, Otsu, Maibara, Hino and Minakuchi. The Nagahama hikiyama festival held each April is one of the three major hikiyama festivals in Japan and was designated an Important Intangible Cultural Property in 1979. During this festival ornate floats are mounted with miniature stages on which boys (playing both male and female roles) act in <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> plays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Higashiomi (formerly <a href="/wiki/Y%C5%8Dkaichi,_Shiga" title="Yōkaichi, Shiga">Yōkaichi</a>) city holds a Giant Kite Festival every May along the riverbank. Ordinary people are invited to pull the rope that sends the kites aloft.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boats">Boats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shiga_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Boats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With development of land transportation in the 20th century, waterborne transportation in Lake Biwa was disused except for steamer services to islands on the lake and pleasure boats.</p>
