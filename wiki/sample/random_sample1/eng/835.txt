<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._Jay_Cox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cox grew up in Eastern Nevada and made his first film when he was eight years old (a two-minute film noir called <i>Vampire Cave</i>). Growing up, he continued writing and creating short films. He graduated with a BA in Journalism from <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a>. He then moved to Los Angeles (where he currently lives) and worked as an actor, performance artist, photographer and other jobs. He began making video shorts, industrial films and documentaries. After he wrote his screenplay for <i>The Thing in Bob's Garage</i> in 1998, he was given a number of jobs rewriting other people's screenplays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steven_Houseman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steven_Houseman-1">[1]</a></sup> His own screenplay, <i><a href="/wiki/Sweet_Home_Alabama_(film)" title="Sweet Home Alabama (film)">Sweet Home Alabama</a></i>, became a box office hit when the film, starring <a href="/wiki/Reese_Witherspoon" title="Reese Witherspoon">Reese Witherspoon</a>, was released in 2002. His next film <i><a href="/wiki/Latter_Days" title="Latter Days">Latter Days</a></i>, which he wrote, produced and directed, won him several audience awards at film festivals. The title <i>Latter Days</i> refers to <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>. His familiarity with the subject matter came from his upbringing as a fifth-generation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mormon" title="Mormon">Mormon</a>. When he moved to Los Angeles he came out as gay. The film is not autobiographical, but it is deeply personal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rebecca_Phillips_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rebecca_Phillips-2">[2]</a></sup> He directed and produced the 2008 film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiss_the_Bride_(2008_film)" title="Kiss the Bride (2008 film)">Kiss the Bride</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Tori_Spelling" title="Tori Spelling">Tori Spelling</a>, and is a screenwriter for the 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/New_in_Town" title="New in Town">New in Town</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9e_Zellweger" title="Renée Zellweger">Renée Zellweger</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_Connick,_Jr" title="Harry Connick, Jr">Harry Connick, Jr</a>. He mentioned in a 2004 interview that some of his idols are <a href="/wiki/James_L._Brooks" title="James L. Brooks">James L. Brooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Pollack" title="Sydney Pollack">Sydney Pollack</a> and <a href="/wiki/Billy_Wilder" title="Billy Wilder">Billy Wilder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steven_Houseman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steven_Houseman-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
