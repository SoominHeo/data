<p><b>Seagate Technology PLC</b> (commonly referred to as <b>Seagate</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">data storage</a> company. It was incorporated in 1978, as <b>Shugart Technology.</b> Since 2010, the company is incorporated in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, with operational headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Cupertino,_California" title="Cupertino, California">Cupertino, California</a>, United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seagate developed the first 5.25-inch <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a> (HDD), the 5-megabyte ST-506, in 1980. They were a major supplier in the <a href="/wiki/Microcomputer" title="Microcomputer">microcomputer</a> market during the 1980s, especially after the introduction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_XT" title="IBM XT">IBM XT</a> in 1983. Today Seagate along with <a href="/wiki/Western_Digital" title="Western Digital">Western Digital</a> dominates the HDD market. Much of their growth has come through acquisition of competitors. In 1989, Seagate acquired <a href="/wiki/Control_Data_Corporation" title="Control Data Corporation">Control Data Corporation</a>'s Imprimis division, makers of CDC's HDD products. Seagate acquired <a href="/wiki/Conner_Peripherals" title="Conner Peripherals">Conner Peripherals</a> in 1996, <a href="/wiki/Maxtor" title="Maxtor">Maxtor</a> in 2006, and <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a>'s HDD business in 2011.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_as_Shugart_Technology">Founding as Shugart Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding as Shugart Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Seagate Technology (then called Shugart Technology) was incorporated on November 1, 1978, and commenced operations with co-founders <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shugart" title="Alan Shugart">Al Shugart</a>, Tom Mitchell, Doug Mahon, Finis Conner and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syed_Iftikar" title="Syed Iftikar">Syed Iftikar</a> in October 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The company came into being when Conner approached Shugart with the idea of starting a new company to develop 5.25-inch HDDs which Conner predicted would be a coming economic boom in the disk drive market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-4">[4]</a></sup> The name was changed to Seagate Technology to avoid a lawsuit from Xerox's subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Shugart_Associates" title="Shugart Associates">Shugart Associates</a> (also founded by Shugart).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_and_Tom_Mitchell_era">Early history and Tom Mitchell era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history and Tom Mitchell era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seagate_ST-225.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1600" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Seagate_ST-225.jpg/220px-Seagate_ST-225.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Seagate_ST-225.jpg/330px-Seagate_ST-225.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Seagate_ST-225.jpg/440px-Seagate_ST-225.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The company's first product, the 5-<a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">megabyte</a> <a href="/wiki/ST-506" title="ST-506">ST-506</a>, was released in 1980. It was the first hard disk to fit the 5.25-inch form factor of the Shugart "mini-floppy" drive. The hard disk, which used a <a href="/wiki/Modified_Frequency_Modulation" title="Modified Frequency Modulation">Modified Frequency Modulation</a> (MFM) encoding, was a hit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and was later released in a 10-megabyte version, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ST-412" title="ST-412">ST-412</a>. With this Seagate secured a contract as a major <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">OEM</a> supplier for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_XT" title="IBM XT">IBM XT</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s first personal computer to contain a hard disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The large volumes of units sold to IBM, the then-dominant supplier of PCs, fueled Seagate's early growth. In their first year, Seagate shipped $10 million of units to consumers. By 1983, the company shipped over 200,000 units for revenues of $110 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 20-megabyte version, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ST-225" title="ST-225">ST-225</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the 30-megabyte version, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ST-238" title="ST-238">ST-238</a> (physically similar but using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Run_Length_Limited#.282.2C7.29_RLL" title="Run Length Limited">Run Length Limited</a> ((2,7) RLL) encoding to improve storage capacity), were popular aftermarket additions for the IBM XT and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_AT" title="IBM AT">AT</a> and compatible microcomputers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These were also made in SCSI versions. In 1983, Al Shugart was replaced as president by then chief operating officer, Tom Mitchell, in order to move forward with corporate restructuring in the face of a changing market. Shugart continued to oversee corporate planning. By this point the company had a 45% market share of the single-user hard drive market, with IBM purchasing 60% of the total business Seagate was doing at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, Seagate acquired <a href="/wiki/Control_Data_Corporation" title="Control Data Corporation">Control Data</a>'s (CDC) Imprimis Technology, CDC's disk storage division, resulting in a combined market share of 43 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imprimis_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imprimis-11">[11]</a></sup> The acquisition was synergistic with little overlap in products or markets;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Seagate benefited from Imprimis' head technology and quality reputation while Imprimis gained access to Seagate's lower component and manufacturing costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imprimis_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Imprimis-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Al_Shugart_era_.281990s.29">Second Al Shugart era (1990s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second Al Shugart era (1990s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1991, Tom Mitchell resigned as president under pressure from the board of directors, with Al Shugart reassuming presidency of the company. Shugart refocused the company on its more lucrative markets, and on mainframe drives instead of external drives. He also pulled away from the practice of outsourcing the production of components overseas, which allowed Seagate to better keep up with demand as the demand for PCs increased exponentially in 1993, across the market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This included a domestic partnership with <a href="/wiki/Corning_Inc." title="Corning Inc.">Corning Inc.</a>, which began using a new glass ceramic compound to manufacture disk substrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1991, Seagate also introduced the <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Barracuda" title="Seagate Barracuda">Barracuda</a> HDDs, the industry's first hard disk with a 7200-RPM spindle speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1993, Seagate became the first company to cumulatively ship 50 million HDDs over its firm's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The following year Seagate Technology Inc moved from the Nasdaq exchange to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>, trading under the ticker symbol SEG. Upon leaving, the company was the 17th-largest company in terms of trading volume on the Nasdaq exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1996, Seagate merged with <a href="/wiki/Conner_Peripherals" title="Conner Peripherals">Conner Peripherals</a> to form world's largest independent hard-drive manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Following the merger, the company began a system of consolidating the components and production methods within its production chain of factories in order to streamline how products were built between plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, Seagate introduced the industry's first hard disk with a 10,000-RPM spindle speed, the Cheetah 4LP. The product increased to a speed of 15,000-RPM by 2000 with the release of the Cheetah 15X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In May 1997 the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> in England awarded <a href="/wiki/Amstrad" title="Amstrad">Amstrad</a> PLC $93 million in a lawsuit over reportedly faulty disk drives Seagate sold to Amstrad, a British manufacturer and marketer of personal computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> That year Seagate also introduced the first <a href="/wiki/Fibre_Channel" title="Fibre Channel">Fibre Channel</a> interface hard drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In July 1998, Shugart resigned his positions with the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> That year the firm Seagate Research was also established in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> a $30 million investment focused on future technologies and prototypes. Technology developed by the center would include devices like the hard drive disk for Microsoft's first <a href="/wiki/Xbox" title="Xbox">Xbox</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 1999, Seagate shipped its 250 millionth hard drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gomes_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gomes-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming_a_private_company_in_2000">Becoming a private company in 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Becoming a private company in 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Seagate became a private company again, with a management buy-out and stock transfer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veritas_Software_Corp" title="Veritas Software Corp">Veritas Software Corp</a>. The total deal, worth about $20 billion, included the sale of its disk-drive operations for $2 billion to an investor group led by <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake_Partners" title="Silver Lake Partners">Silver Lake Partners</a>. The goal of the deal was to unlock the value of the 33% ownership stake Seagate had in Veritas, which had put the value of Seagate's stock at around $33 billion even though its market cap was only $15 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gomes_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gomes-29">[29]</a></sup> The company was <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(business)" title="Incorporation (business)">incorporated</a> in <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a> and stayed private until it re-entered the <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public market</a> in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orfes_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-orfes-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-emerging_as_a_public_company_.282002.E2.80.932010.29">Re-emerging as a public company (2002–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Re-emerging as a public company (2002–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, Seagate re-entered the HDD market for <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">notebook computers</a> and provided the 1-inch disk hard drives for the new first <a href="/wiki/IPod" title="IPod">iPods</a>. This led to a trend of digital devices being created with progressively more and more memory, especially in cameras and music devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The following year <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> called Seagate "the nation's top maker of hard drives used to store data in computers", as its revenue forecast outpaced Wall Street estimates towards the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the beginning of 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine named Seagate its Company of the Year as the best managed company in the United States. <i>Forbes</i> wrote that, "Seagate is riding the world's gadget boom. Its 1-inch drives are the archives for cameras and MP3 players." It also credited Seagate as being the company that "sparked the personal computer revolution 25 years ago with the first 5.25-inch hard drive for the PC".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Seagate created the <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_drive" title="Hybrid drive">hybrid drive</a> concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2008, Seagate was the first to ship 1 billion HDDs. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNet" title="CNet">CNet</a>, it took 17 years to ship the first 100 million and 15 years to ship the next 900 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2009, Bill Watkins was released from employment as CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_to_present">2010 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>2010: Seagate announced that it was moving its headquarters and most staff from Scotts Valley to Cupertino, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>June 2010: Seagate released the world's first 3 TB hard drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In September Seagate released the first portable 1.5 TB hard drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>July 2011: the company changed its country of incorporation from the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> to <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2011, Seagate acquired Samsung's HDD business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>2012: In March Seagate demonstrated the first 1 TB/square inch density hard drive, with the possibility of scaling up to 60 TB by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>2013: Seagate was the first HDD company to begin shipment of <a href="/wiki/Shingled_magnetic_recording" title="Shingled magnetic recording">shingled magnetic recording</a> drives, announcing in September that they had already shipped over 1 million such drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2016, Seagate was hit with class action lawsuit over the defective hard drives they sell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2016, Seagate demonstrated the 60 TB SSD, claimed to be "the largest SSD ever demonstrated", at the Flash Memory Summit in Santa Clara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2017, Seagate announced the shut down of one of its largest HDD assembly plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Seagate was traded for most of its existence as a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> under the symbol "SGAT" on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> system, then moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYSE" title="NYSE">NYSE</a> under the symbol "SEG" in the 1990s. In 2000, Seagate incorporated in the Cayman Islands in order to reduce income taxes. In 2000, the company was taken private by an investment group composed of Seagate management, <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake_Partners" title="Silver Lake Partners">Silver Lake Partners</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_Pacific_Group" title="Texas Pacific Group">Texas Pacific Group</a> and others in a three-way merger-spinoff with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veritas_Software" title="Veritas Software">Veritas Software</a>; Veritas merged with Seagate, which was bought by the investment group. Veritas was then immediately spun off to shareholders, gaining rights to Seagate Software Network and Storage Management Group (with products such as <a href="/wiki/Backup_Exec" title="Backup Exec">Backup Exec</a>), as well as Seagate's shares in <a href="/wiki/SanDisk" title="SanDisk">SanDisk</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_Systems" title="Dragon Systems">Dragon Systems</a>. Seagate Software Information Management Group was renamed <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Decisions" title="Crystal Decisions">Crystal Decisions</a> in May 2001. Seagate re-entered the public market in December 2002 on the NASDAQ as "STX."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gomes_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gomes-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orfes_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-orfes-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partnerships_and_acquisitions">Partnerships and acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Partnerships and acquisitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Finis Conner left Seagate in early 1985 and founded <a href="/wiki/Conner_Peripherals" title="Conner Peripherals">Conner Peripherals</a>, which originally specialized in small-form-factor drives for portable computers. Conner Peripherals also entered the tape drive business with its purchase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archive_Corporation" title="Archive Corporation">Archive Corporation</a>. After ten years as an independent company, Conner Peripherals was acquired by Seagate in a 1996 merger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, Seagate acquired Mirra, Inc., a producer of personal servers for data recovery. It also acquired ActionFront Data Recovery Labs, which provides data recovery services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-49">[49]</a></sup> In 2006, Seagate acquired <a href="/wiki/Maxtor" title="Maxtor">Maxtor</a> in an all-stock deal worth $1.9 billion, and continued to market the separate Maxtor brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The following year, Seagate acquired <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Evault&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Evault (page does not exist)">EVault</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-49">[49]</a></sup> and MetaLINCS, later rebranded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I365" title="I365">i365</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Seagate acquired Xyratex, a storage systems company, for approximately $375 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The same year, it acquired LSI's flash enterprise PCIe flash and SSD controller products, and its engineering capabilities, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avago" title="Avago">Avago</a> for $450 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2015, Seagate acquired Dot Hill Systems Corp., a supplier of software and hardware storage systems, for approximately $696 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seagate_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of Seagate's wireless storage devices was found to have an undocumented hardcoded password.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 21, 2014, numerous tech articles around the globe published findings from the cloud storage provider <a href="/wiki/Backblaze" title="Backblaze">Backblaze</a> that Seagate <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disks</a> are least reliable among prominent hard disk manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> However, the Backblaze tests have been criticized for having a flawed methodology that has inconsistent environment variables, such as ambient temperatures and vibration, and disk usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
