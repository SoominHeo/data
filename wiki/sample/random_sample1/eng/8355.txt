<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sigma-algebra</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"Σ-algebra" redirects here. For an algebraic structure admitting a given signature Σ of operations, see <a href="/wiki/Universal_algebra" title="Universal algebra">Universal algebra</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">mathematical analysis</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>, a <b>σ-algebra</b> (also <b>σ-field</b>) on a set <i>X</i> is a collection Σ of <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subsets</a> of <i>X</i> that includes the empty subset, is <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">closed</a> under <a href="/wiki/Complement_(set_theory)" title="Complement (set theory)">complement</a>, and is closed under countable <a href="/wiki/Union_(set_theory)" title="Union (set theory)">unions</a> and countable <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory)" title="Intersection (set theory)">intersections</a>. The pair (<i>X</i>, Σ) is called a <b>measurable space</b>.</p>
<p>A σ-algebra specializes the concept of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_algebra" title="Set algebra">set algebra</a>. An algebra of sets needs only to be closed under the union or intersection of <i>finitely</i> many subsets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main use of σ-algebras is in the definition of <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measures</a>; specifically, the collection of those subsets for which a given measure is defined is necessarily a σ-algebra. This concept is important in <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">mathematical analysis</a> as the foundation for <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_integration" title="Lebesgue integration">Lebesgue integration</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>, where it is interpreted as the collection of events which can be assigned probabilities. Also, in probability, σ-algebras are pivotal in the definition of <a href="/wiki/Conditional_expectation" title="Conditional expectation">conditional expectation</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, (sub) σ-algebras are needed for the formal mathematical definition of a <a href="/wiki/Sufficient_statistic" title="Sufficient statistic">sufficient statistic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sufficiency_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sufficiency-2">[2]</a></sup> particularly when the statistic is a function or a random process and the notion of <a href="/wiki/Conditional_probability_distribution" title="Conditional probability distribution">conditional density</a> is not applicable.</p>
<p>If <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i> = {<i>a</i>, <i>b</i>, <i>c</i>, <i>d</i>},</span> one possible σ-algebra on <i>X</i> is <span class="texhtml">Σ = { ∅, {<i>a</i>, <i>b</i>}, {<i>c</i>, <i>d</i>}, {<i>a</i>, <i>b</i>, <i>c</i>, <i>d</i>} },</span> where ∅ is the <a href="/wiki/Empty_set" title="Empty set">empty set</a>. In general, a finite algebra is always a σ-algebra.</p>
<p>If {<i>A</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>A</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>A</i><sub>3</sub>, …} is a countable <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_a_set" title="Partition of a set">partition</a> of <i>X</i> then the collection of all unions of sets in the partition (including the empty set) is a σ-algebra.</p>
<p>A more useful example is the set of subsets of the <a href="/wiki/Real_line" title="Real line">real line</a> formed by starting with all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_interval" title="Open interval">open intervals</a> and adding in all countable unions, countable intersections, and relative complements and continuing this process (by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transfinite_iteration" title="Transfinite iteration">transfinite iteration</a> through all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countable_ordinal" title="Countable ordinal">countable ordinals</a>) until the relevant closure properties are achieved (a construction known as the <a href="/wiki/Borel_hierarchy" title="Borel hierarchy">Borel hierarchy</a>).</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are at least three key motivators for σ-algebras: defining measures, manipulating limits of sets, and managing partial information characterized by sets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measure">Measure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measure</a> on <i>X</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a> that assigns a non-negative <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real number</a> to subsets of <i>X</i>; this can be thought of as making precise a notion of "size" or "volume" for sets. We want the size of the union of disjoint sets to be the sum of their individual sizes, even for an infinite sequence of <a href="/wiki/Disjoint_sets" title="Disjoint sets">disjoint sets</a>.</p>
<p>One would like to assign a size to <i>every</i> subset of <i>X</i>, but in many natural settings, this is not possible. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Axiom_of_choice" title="Axiom of choice">axiom of choice</a> implies that when the size under consideration is the ordinary notion of length for subsets of the real line, then there exist sets for which no size exists, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Vitali_set" title="Vitali set">Vitali sets</a>. For this reason, one considers instead a smaller collection of privileged subsets of <i>X</i>. These subsets will be called the measurable sets. They are closed under operations that one would expect for measurable sets, that is, the complement of a measurable set is a measurable set and the countable union of measurable sets is a measurable set. Non-empty collections of sets with these properties are called σ-algebras.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits_of_sets">Limits of sets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Limits of sets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub_.CF.83-algebras">Sub σ-algebras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sub σ-algebras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In much of probability, especially when <a href="/wiki/Conditional_expectation" title="Conditional expectation">conditional expectation</a> is involved, one is concerned with sets that represent only part of all the possible information that can be observed. This partial information can be characterized with a smaller σ-algebra which is a subset of the principal σ-algebra; it consists of the collection of subsets relevant only to and determined only by the partial information. A simple example suffices to illustrate this idea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_properties">Definition and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Definition and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Let <i>X</i> be some set, and let 2<sup><i>X</i></sup> represent its <a href="/wiki/Power_set" title="Power set">power set</a>. Then a subset <span class="nowrap">Σ ⊂ 2<sup><i>X</i></sup></span> is called a <b><i>σ</i>-algebra</b> if it satisfies the following three properties:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><i>X</i> is in Σ, and <i>X</i> is considered to be the <a href="/wiki/Universal_set" title="Universal set">universal set</a> in the following context.</li>
<li>Σ is <i>closed under complementation</i>: If <i>A</i> is in Σ, then so is its <a href="/wiki/Complement_(set_theory)" title="Complement (set theory)">complement</a>, <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i>\<i>A</i></span>.</li>
<li>Σ is <i>closed under countable unions</i>: If <i>A</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>A</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>A</i><sub>3</sub>, ... are in Σ, then so is <i>A</i> = <i>A</i><sub>1</sub> ∪ <i>A</i><sub>2</sub> ∪ <i>A</i><sub>3</sub> ∪ … .</li>
</ol>
<p>From these properties, it follows that the σ-algebra is also closed under countable <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory)" title="Intersection (set theory)">intersections</a> (by applying <a href="/wiki/De_Morgan%27s_laws" title="De Morgan's laws">De Morgan's laws</a>).</p>
<p>It also follows that the <a href="/wiki/Empty_set" title="Empty set">empty set</a> ∅ is in Σ, since by <b>(1)</b> <i>X</i> is in Σ and <b>(2)</b> asserts that its complement, the empty set, is also in Σ. Moreover, since <span class="texhtml">{<i>X</i>, ∅</span>} satisfies condition <b>(3)</b> as well, it follows that <span class="texhtml">{<i>X</i>, ∅</span>} is the smallest possible σ-algebra on <i>X</i>. The largest possible σ-algebra on <i>X</i> is 2<sup><i>X</i></sup>.</p>
<p>Elements of the <i>σ</i>-algebra are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measurable_set" title="Measurable set">measurable sets</a>. An ordered pair <span class="texhtml">(<i>X</i>, Σ)</span>, where <i>X</i> is a set and Σ is a <i>σ</i>-algebra over <i>X</i>, is called a <b>measurable space</b>. A function between two measurable spaces is called a <a href="/wiki/Measurable_function" title="Measurable function">measurable function</a> if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preimage" title="Preimage">preimage</a> of every measurable set is measurable. The collection of measurable spaces forms a <a href="/wiki/Category_(mathematics)" title="Category (mathematics)">category</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Measurable_function" title="Measurable function">measurable functions</a> as <a href="/wiki/Morphism" title="Morphism">morphisms</a>. <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">Measures</a> are defined as certain types of functions from a <i>σ</i>-algebra to [0, ∞].</p>
<p>A σ-algebra is both a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pi-system" title="Pi-system">π-system</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Dynkin_system" title="Dynkin system">Dynkin system</a> (λ-system). The converse is true as well, by Dynkin's theorem (below).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynkin.27s_.CF.80-.CE.BB_theorem">Dynkin's π-λ theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dynkin's π-λ theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This theorem (or the related <a href="/wiki/Monotone_class_theorem" title="Monotone class theorem">monotone class theorem</a>) is an essential tool for proving many results about properties of specific σ-algebras. It capitalizes on the nature of two simpler classes of sets, namely the following.</p>
<dl>
<dd>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pi-system" title="Pi-system">π-system</a> <i>P</i> is a collection of subsets of X that is closed under finitely many intersections, and</dd>
<dd>a <a href="/wiki/Dynkin_system" title="Dynkin system">Dynkin system</a> (or λ-system) <i>D</i> is a collection of subsets of X that contains X and is closed under complement and under countable unions of <i>disjoint</i> subsets.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Dynkin's π-λ theorem says, if <i>P</i> is a π-system and <i>D</i> is a Dynkin system that contains <i>P</i> then the σ-algebra σ(<i>P</i>) <a href="/wiki/Sigma-algebra#.CF.83-algebra_generated_by_an_arbitrary_family" title="Sigma-algebra">generated</a> by <i>P</i> is contained in <i>D</i>. Since certain π-systems are relatively simple classes, it may not be hard to verify that all sets in <i>P</i> enjoy the property under consideration while, on the other hand, showing that the collection <i>D</i> of all subsets with the property is a Dynkin system can also be straightforward. Dynkin's π-λ Theorem then implies that all sets in σ(<i>P</i>) enjoy the property, avoiding the task of checking it for an arbitrary set in σ(<i>P</i>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CF.83-algebras_for_subspaces">σ-algebras for subspaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: σ-algebras for subspaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Suppose <i>Y</i> is a subset of <i>X</i> and let (<i>X</i>, Σ) be a measurable space.</p>
<ul>
<li>The collection {<i>Y</i> ∩ <i>B</i>: <i>B</i> ∈ Σ} is a σ-algebra of subsets of <i>Y</i>.</li>
<li>Suppose (<i>Y</i>, Λ) is a measurable space. The collection {<i>A</i> ⊂ <i>X</i> : <i>A</i> ∩ <i>Y</i> ∈ Λ} is a σ-algebra of subsets of <i>X</i>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_.CF.83-ring">Relation to σ-ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relation to σ-ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i>σ</i>-algebra Σ is just a <a href="/wiki/Sigma-ring" title="Sigma-ring"><i>σ</i>-ring</a> that contains the universal set <i>X</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vestrup2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vestrup2009-4">[4]</a></sup> A <i>σ</i>-ring need not be a <i>σ</i>-algebra, as for example measurable subsets of zero Lebesgue measure in the real line are a <i>σ</i>-ring, but not a <i>σ</i>-algebra since the real line has infinite measure and thus cannot be obtained by their countable union. If, instead of zero measure, one takes measurable subsets of finite Lebesgue measure, those are a <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_sets" title="Ring of sets">ring</a> but not a <i>σ</i>-ring, since the real line can be obtained by their countable union yet its measure is not finite.</p>

<p>A separable measure space has a natural <a href="/wiki/Pseudometric_space" title="Pseudometric space">pseudometric</a> that renders it <a href="/wiki/Separable_space" title="Separable space">separable</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Pseudometric_space" title="Pseudometric space">pseudometric space</a>. The distance between two sets is defined as the measure of the <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_difference" title="Symmetric difference">symmetric difference</a> of the two sets. Note that the symmetric difference of two distinct sets can have measure zero; hence the pseudometric as defined above need not to be a true metric. However, if sets whose symmetric difference has measure zero are identified into a single <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_class" title="Equivalence class">equivalence class</a>, the resulting <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_class" title="Equivalence class">quotient set</a> can be properly metrized by the induced metric. If the measure space is separable, it can be shown that the corresponding metric space is, too.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_set-based_examples">Simple set-based examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Simple set-based examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Let <i>X</i> be any set.</p>
<ul>
<li>The family consisting only of the empty set and the set <i>X</i>, called the minimal or <b>trivial σ-algebra</b> over <i>X</i>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Power_set" title="Power set">power set</a> of <i>X</i>, called the <b>discrete σ-algebra</b>.</li>
<li>The collection {∅, <i>A</i>, <i>A</i><sup>c</sup>, <i>X</i>} is a simple σ-algebra generated by the subset <i>A</i>.</li>
<li>The collection of subsets of <i>X</i> which are countable or whose complements are countable is a σ-algebra (which is distinct from the power set of <i>X</i> if and only if <i>X</i> is uncountable). This is the σ-algebra generated by the <a href="/wiki/Singleton_(mathematics)" title="Singleton (mathematics)">singletons</a> of <i>X</i>. Note: "countable" includes finite or empty.</li>
<li>The collection of all unions of sets in a countable <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_a_set" title="Partition of a set">partition</a> of <i>X</i> is a σ-algebra.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".CF.83-algebras_generated_by_families_of_sets">σ-algebras generated by families of sets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: σ-algebras generated by families of sets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CF.83-algebra_generated_by_an_arbitrary_family">σ-algebra generated by an arbitrary family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: σ-algebra generated by an arbitrary family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Let <i>F</i> be an arbitrary family of subsets of <i>X</i>. Then there exists a unique smallest σ-algebra which contains every set in <i>F</i> (even though <i>F</i> may or may not itself be a σ-algebra). It is, in fact, the intersection of all σ-algebras containing <i>F</i>. (See intersections of σ-algebras above.) This σ-algebra is denoted σ(<i>F</i>) and is called <b>the σ-algebra generated by <i>F</i></b>.</p>
<p>If <i>F</i> is empty, then σ(<i>F</i>)=<span class="texhtml">{<i>X</i>, ∅</span>}. Otherwise σ(<i>F</i>) consists of all the subsets of <i>X</i> that can be made from elements of <i>F</i> by a countable number of complement, union and intersection operations.</p>
<p>For a simple example, consider the set <i>X</i> = {1, 2, 3}. Then the σ-algebra generated by the single subset {1} is <span class="texhtml">σ({{1}}) = {∅, {1}, {2, 3}, {1, 2, 3}}</span>. By an <a href="/wiki/Abuse_of_notation" title="Abuse of notation">abuse of notation</a>, when a collection of subsets contains only one element, <i>A</i>, one may write σ(<i>A</i>) instead of σ({<i>A</i>}); in the prior example σ({1}) instead of σ({{1}}). Indeed, using <span class="texhtml">σ(<i>A</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>A</i><sub>2</sub>, ...)</span> to mean <span class="texhtml">σ({<i>A</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>A</i><sub>2</sub>, ...})</span> is also quite common.</p>
<p>There are many families of subsets that generate useful σ-algebras. Some of these are presented here.</p>

If <i>S</i> is finite or countably infinite or, more generally, (<i>S</i>, Σ<sub><i>S</i></sub>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borel_space" title="Borel space">standard Borel space</a> (e.g., a separable complete metric space with its associated Borel sets), then the converse is also true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Examples of standard Borel spaces include <b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup> with its Borel sets and <b>R</b><sup>∞</sup> with the cylinder σ-algebra described below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Borel_and_Lebesgue_.CF.83-algebras">Borel and Lebesgue σ-algebras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma-algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Borel and Lebesgue σ-algebras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An important example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borel_algebra" title="Borel algebra">Borel algebra</a> over any <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a>: the σ-algebra generated by the <a href="/wiki/Open_set" title="Open set">open sets</a> (or, equivalently, by the <a href="/wiki/Closed_set" title="Closed set">closed sets</a>). Note that this σ-algebra is not, in general, the whole power set. For a non-trivial example that is not a Borel set, see the <a href="/wiki/Vitali_set" title="Vitali set">Vitali set</a> or <a href="/wiki/Borel_set#Non-Borel_sets" title="Borel set">Non-Borel sets</a>.</p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a> <b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup>, another σ-algebra is of importance: that of all <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">Lebesgue measurable</a> sets. This σ-algebra contains more sets than the Borel σ-algebra on <b>R</b><sup><i>n</i></sup> and is preferred in <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integration</a> theory, as it gives a <a href="/wiki/Complete_measure" title="Complete measure">complete measure space</a>.</p>
