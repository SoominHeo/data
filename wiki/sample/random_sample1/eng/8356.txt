<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, <b>Sigmund</b> is a hero whose story is told in the <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lsunga_saga" title="Völsunga saga">Völsunga saga</a>. He and his sister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sign%C3%BD" title="Signý">Signý</a>, are the children of <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lsung" title="Völsung">Völsung</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Hljod" title="Hljod">Hljod</a>. Sigmund is best known as the father of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigur%C3%B0" title="Sigurð">Sigurð</a> the dragon-slayer, though Sigurð's tale has almost no connections to the Völsung cycle.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="V.C3.B6lsunga_saga"><i>Völsunga saga</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigmund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Völsunga saga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sigmunds_Schwert_(1889)_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="639" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Sigmunds_Schwert_%281889%29_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg/220px-Sigmunds_Schwert_%281889%29_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Sigmunds_Schwert_%281889%29_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg/330px-Sigmunds_Schwert_%281889%29_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Sigmunds_Schwert_%281889%29_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg/440px-Sigmunds_Schwert_%281889%29_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sigmunds_Schwert_(1889)_by_Johannes_Gehrts.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Sigmund's Sword" (1889) by Johannes Gehrts.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <i>Völsunga saga</i>, Signý marries <a href="/wiki/Siggeir" title="Siggeir">Siggeir</a>, the king of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gautland" title="Gautland">Gautland</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4sterg%C3%B6tland" title="Västergötland">Västergötland</a>). Völsung and Sigmund are attending the wedding feast (which lasted for some time before and after the marriage), when <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>, disguised as a beggar, plunges a sword (Gram) into the living tree <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnstokk" title="Barnstokk">Barnstokk</a> ("offspring-trunk"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORCHARD14_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORCHARD14-1">[1]</a></sup>) around which Völsung's hall is built. The disguised Odin announces that the man who can remove the sword will have it as a gift. Only Sigmund is able to free the sword from the tree.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Siggeir" title="Siggeir">Siggeir</a> is smitten with envy and desire for the sword. He tries to buy it but Sigmund refuses. Siggeir invites Sigmund, his father Völsung and Sigmund's <a href="/wiki/Numbers_in_Norse_mythology" title="Numbers in Norse mythology">nine</a> brothers to visit him in Gautland to see the newlyweds three months later. When the Völsung clan arrive, they are attacked by the Gauts; King Völsung is killed and his sons captured. Signý beseeches her husband to spare her brothers and to put them in <a href="/wiki/Stocks" title="Stocks">stocks</a> instead of killing them. As Siggeir thinks that the brothers deserve to be tortured before they are killed, he agrees.</p>
<p>He then lets his <a href="/wiki/Shapeshifting" title="Shapeshifting">shapeshifting</a> mother turn into a wolf and devour one of the brothers each night. During that time, Signý tries various ruses but fails every time until only Sigmund remains. On the ninth night, she has a servant smear <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> on Sigmund's face and when the she-wolf arrives, she starts licking the honey off and sticks her tongue into Sigmund's mouth, whereupon Sigmund bites her tongue off, killing her. Sigmund then escapes his bonds and hides in the forest.</p>
<p>Signý brings Sigmund everything he needs. Bent on revenge for their father's death, she also sends her sons to him in the wilderness, one by one, to be tested. As each fails, she urges Sigmund to kill them, until one day when he refuses to continue killing innocent children. Finally, in despair, she comes to him in the guise of a <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lva" title="Völva">völva</a> and conceives a child by him, <a href="/wiki/Sinfj%C3%B6tli" title="Sinfjötli">Sinfjötli</a> (the <i>Fitela</i> of <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i>). Sinfjötli, born of their <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">incest</a>, passes the test.</p>
<p>Sigmund and his son/nephew, Sinfjötli, grow wealthy as outlaws. In their wanderings, they come upon men sleeping in cursed wolf skins. Upon killing the men and putting on the wolf skins, they are cursed with a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lycanthropy" title="Lycanthropy">lycanthropy</a>. Eventually, they avenge the death of Völsung.</p>
<p>After Signý dies, Sigmund and Sinfjötli go harrying together. Sigmund marries a woman named <a href="/wiki/Borghild" title="Borghild">Borghild</a> and has two sons, one of them named <a href="/wiki/Helgi_Hundingsbane" title="Helgi Hundingsbane">Helgi</a>. Sinfjötli slays Borghild's brother while vying for a woman they both want. Borghild avenges her brother by poisoning Sinfjötli.</p>
<p>Later, Sigmund marries a woman named Hjördís. After a short time of peace, Sigmund's lands are attacked by King Lyngi. In battle, Sigmund matches up against an old man who is <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> in disguise. Odin shatters Sigmund's sword, and Sigmund falls at the hands of others. Dying, he tells Hjördís that she is pregnant and that her son will one day make a great weapon out of the fragments of his sword. That son was to be <a href="/wiki/Sigurd" title="Sigurd">Sigurd</a>. Sigurd himself had a son named Sigmund, who was killed when he was three years old by a vengeful Brynhild.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_other_Germanic_heroes">Relation to other Germanic heroes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigmund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to other Germanic heroes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sigmund/Siegmund is also the name of Sigurd/Siegfried's father in other versions of the Sigurd story, but without any of the details about his life or family that appear in Norse Völsung tales and poems. On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i> includes Sigemund the Wælsing and his nephew Fitela in a tale of dragon slaying told within the main story. Herein the story of Sigemund is told to Beowulf, a warrior also from <a href="/wiki/Geats" title="Geats">Gautland</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parallels">Parallels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigmund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parallels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parallels to Sigmund's pulling the sword from the tree can be found in other mythologies (notably in the <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">Arthurian</a> legends). Also, <a href="/wiki/Sinfj%C3%B6tli" title="Sinfjötli">Sinfjötli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mordred" title="Mordred">Mordred</a> share the characteristic of being nephew and son to the main characters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_modern_fiction">In modern fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigmund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In modern fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The story of Sigmund, beginning with the marriage of Signy to Siggeir and ending with Sigmund's vengeance on Siggeir, was retold in the novelette "Vengeance" by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Gilchrist_Brodeur" title="Arthur Gilchrist Brodeur">Arthur Gilchrist Brodeur</a>, which appeared in the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Adventure_(magazine)" title="Adventure (magazine)">Adventure</a></i>, June 30, 1925. Brodeur was a professor at Berkeley and became well known for his scholarship on <i>Beowulf</i> and Norse sagas.</p>
<p>Siegmund (ジークムント Jīkumunto) is a sacred Lance that appears in Fire Emblem: The Sacred Stones. Known as the Flame Lance (炎槍, Ensō lit. Flame Spear) Siegmund, alongside the Thunder Sword Sieglinde,is one of the Sacred Twins of Renais, and is usable only by Ephraim. Like most of its other counterparts, Siegmund has the ability to inflict effective damage against Monster units.</p>
