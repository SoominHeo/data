<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cinematograph</h1>

<p>A <b>cinematograph</b> is a motion picture film <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a>, which also serves as a <a href="/wiki/Movie_projector" title="Movie projector">film projector</a> and printer. It was invented in the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[notes 1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematograph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The device was first invented and patented as the "Cinématographe Léon Bouly" by French inventor <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Bouly" title="Léon Bouly">Léon Bouly</a> on February 12, 1892. Bouly coined the term “cinematograph”, from the Greek for “writing in movement”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-abel-2">[1]</a></sup> Due to a lack of money, Bouly was unable to develop his ideas properly and maintain his patent fees, so he sold his rights to the device and its name to the <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Lumière Brothers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> In 1895, they applied the name to a device that was largely their own creation.</p>
<p>They made their first film, <i><a href="/wiki/Workers_Leaving_the_Lumi%C3%A8re_Factory" title="Workers Leaving the Lumière Factory">Sortie de l'usine Lumière de Lyon</a></i>, that same year. The film was publicly screened at L'Eden, the world's first and oldest <i>cinéma</i>, located in <a href="/wiki/La_Ciotat" title="La Ciotat">La Ciotat</a> in southeastern France, on September 28, 1895. The first commercial, public screening of <a href="/wiki/Cinematography" title="Cinematography">cinematographic</a> films happened in Paris on 28 December 1895 and was organised by the <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Lumière brothers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> The cinematograph was also exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1900)" title="Exposition Universelle (1900)">Paris Exposition of 1900</a>. At the Exposition, films made by the Lumière Brothers were projected onto a large screen measuring 16 by 21 meters (approximately 52.5 x 69 feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-louis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-louis-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several versions of cinématographes were developed, including ones by the inventor Robert Royou Beard (1856-1932),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> the electrical engineer Cecil Wray (1866-1944),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> the optician Alfred Wrench (?-?),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Georges_Demen%C3%BF" title="Georges Demenÿ">Georges Demenÿ</a> and, of course, the Lumière brothers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention">Invention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematograph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Invention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Louis Lumière</a> worked with his brother <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Auguste</a> to create a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion-picture" title="Motion-picture">motion-picture</a> camera superior to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinetograph" title="Kinetograph">kinetograph</a>, which did not have a projector. The <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Lumières</a> endeavored to correct the flaws they perceived in the kinetograph and the kinetoscope, to develop a machine with both sharper images and better <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illumination_(lighting)" title="Illumination (lighting)">illumination</a>. The Cinématographe weighed only 16 lbs., which allowed for ease of transportation and placement. As well, the Cinématographe was manually operated by a hand-crank, as opposed to Edison's electrically powered camera, which was not readily portable. Furthermore, while only one person at a time could use Edison's kinetoscope for viewing - through an eyepiece, <a href="/wiki/Peep_show" title="Peep show">peep show</a> style - the Cinématographe could project an image onto a screen so a large audience of people could view images simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gomery_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-gomery-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cinématographe produced a sharper projected image than had been seen before due to its design, in which a kind of fork held frame behind the lens in place using the perforations in the sides of the film strip.</p>
<p>In 1897, the Lumières further added to their invention by using a glass flask of water as the condenser to concentrate the light onto the film frame and to absorb heat. The flask also acted as a safety feature, as the light would no longer focus on the flammable film if the glass were to break due to overheating or accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-louis_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-louis-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematograph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cinematograph became a popular attraction for people all over the world. The Lumière Brothers took their machine as far as China and India<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gomery_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-gomery-10">[9]</a></sup> and it was enjoyed by people of all classes and social standings. The cinematograph was used to show films in <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_(movie_theater)" title="Nickelodeon (movie theater)">nickelodeons</a>, where even the poorest classes could pay the entry fee. It was exhibited at fairs and used as entertainment in <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">vaudeville</a> houses in both Europe and the United States. While vaudeville is typically associated with the middle class, the machine also found its way into more sophisticated venues, where it appealed to the artistic tastes of high society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abel_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-abel-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Lumières</a> held their first private screening of projected motion pictures on March 22, 1895. Their first public screening of films at which admission was charged was held on December 28, 1895, at <a href="/wiki/Salon_Indien_du_Grand_Caf%C3%A9" title="Salon Indien du Grand Café">Salon Indien du Grand Café</a> in Paris. This history-making presentation featured ten short films, including their first film, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workers_Leaving_the_Lumiere_Factory" title="Workers Leaving the Lumiere Factory">Sortie des Usines Lumière à Lyon</a></i> (<i>Workers Leaving the Lumière Factory in Lyon</i>).[6] Each film is 17 meters long, which, when hand cranked through a projector, runs approximately 50 seconds.</p>
