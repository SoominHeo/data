<p><i><b>Sinosauropteryx</b></i> (meaning "Chinese reptilian wing", <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">中华龙鸟</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Zhōnghuá lóng niǎo</span></i>; literally: "China <a href="/wiki/Chinese_dragon" title="Chinese dragon">dragon</a> bird") is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compsognathid" title="Compsognathid">compsognathid</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaur</a>. Described in 1996, it was the first dinosaur taxon outside of <a href="/wiki/Avialae" title="Avialae">Avialae</a> (birds and their immediate relatives) to be found with evidence of <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>. It was covered with a coat of very simple <a href="/wiki/Protein_filament" title="Protein filament">filament</a>-like feathers. Structures that indicate colouration have also been preserved in some of its feathers, which makes <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> the first non-avialian dinosaurs where colouration has been determined. The colouration includes a reddish and light banded tail. Some contention has arisen with an alternative interpretation of the filamentous impression as remains of <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> fibres, but this has not been widely accepted.</p>
<p><i>Sinosauropteryx</i> was a small theropod with an unusually long tail and short arms. The longest known specimen reaches up to 1.07 metres (3.5 feet) in length, with an estimated weight of 0.55 kilograms (1.2 pounds) It was a close relative of the similar but older genus <i><a href="/wiki/Compsognathus" title="Compsognathus">Compsognathus</a></i>, both genera belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> Compsognathidae. Only one <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> has been named: <i>S. prima</i>, meaning "first" in reference to its status as the first feathered non-avialian dinosaur species discovered. Three specimens have been described. The third specimen previously assigned to this genus represents either a second, as-yet unnamed species or a distinct, related genus.</p>
<p><i>Sinosauropteryx</i> lived in what is now northeastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> during the early <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale#Terminology" title="Geologic time scale">period</a>. It was among the first dinosaurs discovered from the <a href="/wiki/Yixian_Formation" title="Yixian Formation">Yixian Formation</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liaoning_Province" title="Liaoning Province">Liaoning Province</a>, and was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Jehol_Biota" title="Jehol Biota">Jehol Biota</a>. Well-preserved fossils of this species illustrate many aspects of its biology, such as its diet and reproduction.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_scale.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="291" data-file-width="290" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sinosauropteryx_scale.png/170px-Sinosauropteryx_scale.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sinosauropteryx_scale.png/255px-Sinosauropteryx_scale.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/Sinosauropteryx_scale.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_scale.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Size of adult and sub-adult specimens, compared with a human</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Sinosauropteryx</i> was a small <a href="/wiki/Bipedalism" title="Bipedalism">bipedal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a>, noted for its short arms, large first finger (thumbs), and long tail. The taxon includes some of the smallest known adult non-avian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a> specimens, with the <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> specimen measuring only 68 cm (27 in) in length, including the tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> However, this individual was relatively young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup> The longest known specimen reaches up to 1.07 m (3.5 ft) in length, with an estimated weight of 0.55 kg (1.2 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TH07_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TH07-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Sinosauropteryx</i> was anatomically similar to <i>Compsognathus</i>, differing from its European relatives in its proportions. The skull of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> was 15% longer than its <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">thigh bones</a>, unlike in <i>Compsognathus</i>, where the skull and thigh bones are approximately equivalent in length. The arms of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> (<a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radius_(bone)" title="Radius (bone)">radius</a>) were only 30% the length of its legs (thigh bone and <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">shin</a>), compared to 40% in <i>Compsognathus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> had several features unique among all other theropods. It had 64 <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a> in its tail. This high number made its tail the longest relative to body length of any theropod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> Its hands were long compared to its arms, about 84% to 91% of the length of the rest of the arm (humerus and radius), and half the length of the foot. The first and second digits were about the same length, with a large claw on the first digit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup> The first fingers were large, being both longer and thicker than either of the bones of the forearm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> The teeth differed slightly (they were <a href="/wiki/Heterodont" title="Heterodont">heterodont</a>) based on position: those near the tips of the upper jaws (on the <a href="/wiki/Premaxilla" title="Premaxilla">premaxillae</a>) were slender and lacked serrations, while those behind them (on the <a href="/wiki/Maxilla" title="Maxilla">maxillae</a>) were serrated and laterally compressed. The teeth of the lower jaws were similarly differentiated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A pigmented area in the abdomen of the holotype has been suggested as possible traces of organs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> and was interpreted as the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> by John Ruben and colleagues, which they described as part of a <a href="/wiki/Crocodilia" title="Crocodilia">crocodilian</a>-like "<a href="/wiki/Lung#Reptilian_lungs" title="Lung">hepatic piston</a>" respiratory system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JRetal97_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-JRetal97-4">[4]</a></sup> A later study, while agreeing that the pigmented area represented something originally inside the body, found no defined structure and noted that any organs would have been distorted by the processes that flattened the skeleton into an essentially two-dimensional form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Dark pigment is also present in the eye region of the holotype and another specimen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feathers">Feathers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Feathers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feathered_dinosaurs" title="Feathered dinosaurs">Feathered dinosaurs</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="958" data-file-width="897" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg/170px-Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg/255px-Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg/340px-Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_prima_slab.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Counter slab of the holotype, on temporary display at Yale University's Peabody Museum of Natural History</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All described specimens of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> preserve <a href="/wiki/Integument" title="Integument">integumentary structures</a> (filaments arising from the skin) which most palaeontologists interpret as very primitive type of <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>. These short, down-like filaments are preserved along the back half of the skull, the arms, neck, back, and top and bottom of the tail. Additional patches of feathers have been identified on the sides of the body, and palaeontologists Chen, Dong and Zheng proposed that the density of the feathers on the back and the randomness of the patches elsewhere on the body indicated the animals would have been fully feathered in life, with the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#dorsal_and_ventral" title="Anatomical terms of location">ventral</a> feathers having been removed by decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The filaments are preserved with a gap between the bones, which several authors have noted corresponds closely to the expected amount of skin and muscle tissue that would have been present in life. The feathers are closest to the bone on the skull and end of the tail, where little to no muscle was present, and the gap increases over the back vertebrae, where more musculature would be expected, indicating that the filaments were external to the skin and do not correspond with <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue" title="Subcutaneous tissue">subcutaneous</a> structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="727" data-file-width="568" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg/170px-Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg/255px-Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg/340px-Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fossil_of_Sinosauropteryx.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Image showing the entire holotype specimen, on exhibit in Japan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The filaments exhibit random orientations and are often wavy, which has been interpreted as evidence that they were soft and pliable in life. Microscopic examination shows that individual filaments appear dark along the edges and light internally, suggesting that they were hollow, like modern feathers. Compared to modern mammals the filaments were quite coarse, with each individual strand much larger and thicker than the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hairs</a> of similarly sized mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The length of the filaments varies across the body. On the type specimen, they are shortest just in front of the eyes, with a length of 13 mm (0.51 in). Going further along the body, the filaments rapidly increase in length until reaching lengths of 35 mm (1.4 in) over the <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">shoulder blades</a>. The length remains uniform over the back, until beyond the hips, when the filaments lengthen again and reach their maximum length midway down the tail at 40 mm (1.6 in). The filaments on the underside of the tail are shorter overall and decrease in length more rapidly than those on the dorsal surface. By the 25th tail vertebrae, the filaments on the underside reach a length of only 35 mm (1.4 in). The longest feathers present on the forearm measured 14 mm (0.55 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the feathers are too dense to isolate a single structure for examination, several studies have suggested the presence of two distinct filament types (thick and thin) interspersed with each other. The thick filaments tend to appear 'stiffer' than thin filaments, and the thin filaments tend to lie parallel to each other but at angles to nearby thick filaments. These properties suggest that the individual feathers consisted of a central quill (<i>rachis</i>) with thinner <i>barbs</i> branching off from it, similar to but more primitive in structure than modern bird feathers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Overall, the filaments most closely resemble the "plumules" or down-like feathers of some modern birds, with a thick central quill and long, thin barbs. The same structures are seen in other fossils from the Yixian Formation, including <i><a href="/wiki/Confuciusornis" title="Confuciusornis">Confuciusornis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>While <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> had feather-like structures, it was not very closely related to the previous "first bird" <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeopteryx" title="Archaeopteryx">Archaeopteryx</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> There are many dinosaur <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> that were more closely related to <i>Archaeopteryx</i> than <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> was, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deinonychosauria" title="Deinonychosauria">deinonychosaurians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Oviraptorosauria" title="Oviraptorosauria">oviraptorosaurians</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Therizinosaur" title="Therizinosaur">therizinosauroids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-padian2004_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-padian2004-5">[5]</a></sup> This indicates that feathers may have been a characteristic of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">theropod</a> dinosaurs, not just the obviously bird-like ones, making it possible that equally distant animals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Compsognathus" title="Compsognathus">Compsognathus</a></i> had feathers as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-padian2004_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-padian2004-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colouration">Colouration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Colouration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_colouration" title="Animal colouration">Animal colouration</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="428" data-file-width="700" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png/220px-Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png/330px-Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png/440px-Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_mmartyniuk_solosml.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reconstruction illustrating colouration as suggested by the study of preserved melanosomes. Black regions and most of the sides lack fossilised feathers.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Sinosauropteryx</i> was the first dinosaur to have its life colouration described by scientists based on physical evidence. Some fossils of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> show an alternation of lighter and darker bands preserved on the tail. Chen and colleagues initially interpreted this banding pattern as an artifact of the splitting between the main slab and counter-slab in which the original specimen was preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> However, Longrich suggested in his 2002 presentation for the Society of Vertebrate Paleontology that these specimens actually preserve remnants of the colouration pattern the animal would have exhibited in life. He argued that the dark, banded areas on the tail were too evenly spaced to have been caused by random separation of the fossil slabs, and that they represent fossilized pigments present in the feathers. Additionally, rather than an artifact of preservation or decomposition, the presence of dark feathers along only the top of the body may also reflect the colour pattern in life, indicating that <i>Sinosauropteryx prima</i> was <a href="/wiki/Countershading" title="Countershading">countershaded</a> with dark colouration on its back and lighter colouration on its underside, with bands or stripes on the tail for camouflage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longrich2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-longrich2002-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Longrich's conclusions were supported in a paper first published online in the journal <i>Nature</i> in January 2010. Fucheng Zhang and colleagues examined the fossilized feathers of several dinosaurs and early birds, and found evidence that they preserved <a href="/wiki/Melanosome" title="Melanosome">melanosomes</a>, the cells that give the feathers of modern birds their colour. Among the specimens studied was a previously undescribed specimen of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i>, IVPP V14202. By examining melanosome structure and distribution, Zhang and colleagues were able to confirm the presence of light and dark bands of colour in the tail feathers of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i>. Furthermore, the team was able to compare melanosome types to those of modern birds to determine a general range of colour. From the presence of phaeomelanosomes, spherical melanosomes that make and store red pigment, they concluded that the darker feathers of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> were chestnut or reddish brown in colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhangetal2010_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-zhangetal2010-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="974" data-file-width="1024" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG/220px-Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG/330px-Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG/440px-Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_prima.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Undescribed fossil specimen at the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Science_Museum" title="Hong Kong Science Museum">Hong Kong Science Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Despite its feathers, most palaeontologists do not consider <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> to be birds. <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">Phylogenetically</a>, the genus is only distantly related to the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aves" title="Aves">Aves</a>, usually defined as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeopteryx_lithographica" title="Archaeopteryx lithographica">Archaeopteryx lithographica</a></i> plus modern birds. The scientists who described <i>Sinosauropteryx</i>, however, used a character-based, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apomorphy" title="Apomorphy">apomorphic</a>, definition of the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">Class</a> Aves, in which any animal with feathers is considered to be a bird. They argued that the filamentous plumes of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> represent true feathers with a <a href="/wiki/Rachis" title="Rachis">rachis</a> and barbs, and thus that <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> should be considered a true bird.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiji1997_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-jiji1997-8">[8]</a></sup> They classified the genus as belonging to a new biological <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a>, <b>Sinosauropterygiformes</b>, family <b>Sinosauropterygidae</b>, within the subclass <a href="/wiki/Sauriurae" title="Sauriurae">Sauriurae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiji1996_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-jiji1996-9">[9]</a></sup> These proposals have not been accepted, and <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> is generally classified in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Compsognathidae" title="Compsognathidae">Compsognathidae</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jietal2007_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jietal2007-10">[10]</a></sup> a group of small-bodied long-tailed <a href="/wiki/Coelurosauria" title="Coelurosauria">coelurosaurian</a> theropods known from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Jurassic" title="Late Jurassic">Late Jurassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a> of Asia, Europe, and South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Below is a cladogram showing the placement of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> within Coelurosauria by Senter <i>et al.</i> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senter10_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-senter10-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

</div>

<p>There is only one named species of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i>, <i>S. prima</i>. A possible second species is represented by the specimen GMV 2124 (aka NGMC 2124), which was described as a third, larger specimen of <i>S. prima</i> by Ji and Ji in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiji1997_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-jiji1997-8">[8]</a></sup> However, in a 2002 presentation and abstract for the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Vertebrate_Paleontology" title="Society of Vertebrate Paleontology">Society of Vertebrate Paleontology</a>, Nick Longrich showed that this specimen differs in several anatomical aspects from the others, including its relatively large size, proportionally longer shins, and shorter tail. Longrich suggested that GMV 2124 was a compsognathid coelurosaurian, while <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> proper was a more primitive kind of coelurosaurian or even a <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> <a href="/wiki/Carnosauria" title="Carnosauria">carnosaurian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-longrich2002_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-longrich2002-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2007, Gishlick and Gauthier concurred that this specimen was probably a new <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>, and tentatively re-classified it as <i>Sinosauropteryx?</i> sp., though they suggested it may belong in a new genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gishlick.26gauthier2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gishlick.26gauthier2007-13">[13]</a></sup> Also in 2007, Ji, Ji and colleagues wrote that GMV 2124 is probably a new genus, noting the differences in tail length and hindlimb proportions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jietal2007_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jietal2007-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinguishing_anatomical_features">Distinguishing anatomical features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distinguishing anatomical features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ji and Ji (1996) identified many features that set <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> apart from other birds and dinosaurs. They found that it was a small primitive bird with a relatively high skull, blunt rostrum and a slightly high premaxilla; that the antorbital fenestra was elliptical but not enlarged, the dentary was robust, the surangular was narrow and elongated, and the dentition is extremely well developed and acute; that there are over 50 extremely elongated caudals, constituting 60% of the body length, and the forelimb is extremely short with a short and thick humerus; the pubis was elongated and extremely inflated at its distal end and the ischium is broad; the hind limb was long and robust, the tibia is only slightly longer than the femur, the tarsals are separated, and the metatarsals are relatively robust with unfused proximal ends; the feathers are short, small, and uniform; many ornament the top of the skull, cervical, and dorsal regions, in addition to the dorsal and ventral caudal region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiji1996_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-jiji1996-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_discovery">History of discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History of discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_birds" title="Origin of birds">Origin of birds</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="756" data-file-width="1200" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Sinosauropteryx.jpg/220px-Sinosauropteryx.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Sinosauropteryx.jpg/330px-Sinosauropteryx.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Sinosauropteryx.jpg/440px-Sinosauropteryx.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skeletal restoration of two specimens</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first fossil specimen of the dinosaur later named <i>Sinosauropteryx prima</i> was uncovered in August 1996 by Li Yumin. Yumin was a farmer and part-time fossil hunter who often prospected around Liaoning Province to acquire fossils to sell to individuals and museums. Yumin recognized the unique quality of the specimen, which was separated into two slabs, and sold the slabs to two separate museums in China: the National Geological Museum in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a> Institute of Geology and Paleontology. The director of the Beijing museum, Ji Qiang, recognized the importance of the find, as did visiting Canadian palaeontologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phil_Currie" title="Phil Currie">Phil Currie</a> and artist Michael Skrepnick, who became aware of the fossil by chance as they explored the Beijing museum's collections after leading a fossil tour of the area during the first week of October, 1996. Currie recognized the significance of the fossil immediately. As <i>The New York Times</i> quoted him, "When I saw this slab of <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">silt stone</a> mixed with volcanic ash in which the creature is embedded, I was bowled over."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2006_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2006-14">[14]</a></sup> When originally described, the authors named <i>Sinosauropteryx</i>, meaning "Chinese Reptilian Wing<i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiji1996_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-jiji1996-9">[9]</a></sup></i></p>
<p>Chinese authorities initially barred photographs of the specimen from publication. However, Currie brought a photograph to the 1996 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Vertebrate_Paleontology" title="Society of Vertebrate Paleontology">Society of Vertebrate Paleontology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a> in New York, causing crowds of palaeontologists to gather and discuss the new discovery. The news reportedly left palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/John_Ostrom" title="John Ostrom">John Ostrom</a>, who in the 1970s had pioneered the theory that birds evolved from dinosaurs, "in a state of shock."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2006_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2006-14">[14]</a></sup> Ostrom later joined an international team of researchers who gathered in Beijing to examine the fossils; other team members included feather expert Alan Brush, fossil bird expert <a href="/wiki/Larry_Martin" title="Larry Martin">Larry Martin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Wellnhofer" title="Peter Wellnhofer">Peter Wellnhofer</a>, an expert on the early bird <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeopteryx" title="Archaeopteryx">Archaeopteryx</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alanfeduccia1999_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-alanfeduccia1999-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three specimens have been assigned to <i>Sinosauropteryx prima</i>: the holotype <a href="/wiki/Geological_Museum_of_China" title="Geological Museum of China">GMV</a> 2123 (and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_slab" title="Counter slab">counter slab</a> [opposite face], <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nanjing_Institute_of_Geology_and_Palaeontology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nanjing Institute of Geology and Palaeontology (page does not exist)">NIGP</a> 127586), NIGP 127587, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dalian_Natural_History_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dalian Natural History Museum (page does not exist)">D</a> 2141.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jietal2007_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jietal2007-10">[10]</a></sup> Another specimen, <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Vertebrate_Paleontology_and_Paleoanthropology" title="Institute of Vertebrate Paleontology and Paleoanthropology">IVPP</a> V14202, was assigned to the genus but not to the only species by Zhang and colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhangetal2010_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-zhangetal2010-7">[7]</a></sup> The assignment of an additional larger specimen to <i>S. prima</i>, <a href="/wiki/Geological_Museum_of_China" title="Geological Museum of China">GMV</a> 2124, was later found to be in error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jietal2007_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jietal2007-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gishlick.26gauthier2007_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-gishlick.26gauthier2007-13">[13]</a></sup> All of the fossils were found in the Jianshangou or Dawangzhangzi Beds of the <a href="/wiki/Yixian_Formation" title="Yixian Formation">Yixian Formation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Beipiao" title="Beipiao">Beipiao</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lingyuan,_Liaoning" title="Lingyuan, Liaoning">Lingyuan</a> regions of <a href="/wiki/Liaoning" title="Liaoning">Liaoning</a>, China. These fossil beds have been dated to 124.6–122 million years ago, during the late <a href="/wiki/Barremian" title="Barremian">Barremian</a> to early <a href="/wiki/Aptian" title="Aptian">Aptian</a> stages of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhou2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-zhou2006-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_of_filaments">Identity of filaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Identity of filaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1475" data-file-width="2103" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg/220px-Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg/330px-Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg/440px-Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_Prima.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of the head</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Controversy regarding the identity of the filaments preserved in the first <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> specimen began almost immediately, as the team of scientists spent three days in Beijing examining the specimen under a microscope. The results of their studies (reported during a press conference at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Natural_Sciences" title="Academy of Natural Sciences">Philadelphia Academy of Natural Sciences</a> on Thursday, April 24, 1997) were inconclusive; the team agreed that the structures preserved on <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> were not modern feathers, but suggested further research was required to discover their exact nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pressconference1997_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pressconference1997-17">[17]</a></sup> Palaeontologist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Feduccia" title="Alan Feduccia">Alan Feduccia</a>, who had not yet examined the specimen, wrote in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audubon_Magazine" title="Audubon Magazine">Audubon Magazine</a></i> that the structures of <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> (which he considered at the time to be a synonym of <i>Compsognathus</i>, as <i>Compsognathus prima</i>) were stiffening structures from a frill running along the back, and that dinosaur palaeontologists were engaging in wishful thinking when equating the structures with feathers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audubon1997_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-audubon1997-18">[18]</a></sup> Subsequent publications saw some of the team members disagreeing over the identity of the structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-currie.26chen2001_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-currie.26chen2001-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LMSC00_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-LMSC00-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Feduccia's frill argument was followed up in several other publications, in which researchers interpreted the filamentous impressions around <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> fossils as remains of <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> fibres rather than primitive feathers. Since the structures are clearly external to the body, these researchers have proposed that the fibres formed a frill on the back of the animal and underside of its tail, similar to some modern aquatic lizards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geistetal1997_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-geistetal1997-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubenjones2000_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubenjones2000-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linghan-soliaretal2007_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-linghan-soliaretal2007-23">[23]</a></sup> The absence of feathers would refute the proposal that <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> is the most <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> known theropod genus with feathers, and also raise questions about the current theory of feather origins itself. It calls into question the idea that the first feathers evolved not for <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> but for insulation, and that they made their first appearance in relatively basal dinosaur lineages that later evolved into modern birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-balddino_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-balddino-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most researchers have disagreed with the identification of the structures as collagen or other structural fibres. Notably, the team of scientists that reported the presence of pigmentation cells in the structures argued that their presence proved the structures were feathers, not collagen, because collagen does not contain pigment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhangetal2010_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-zhangetal2010-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gregory_S._Paul" title="Gregory S. Paul">Gregory S. Paul</a> reidentified what the collagen hypothesis's proponents consider a body outline outside of the fibres as an artefact of preparation: breakage and brushed-on sealant have been misidentified as the outline of the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSP02_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSP02-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeobiology">Palaeobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Palaeobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7386" data-file-width="4297" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg/170px-Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg/255px-Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg/340px-Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinosauropteryx_GMV_2124.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cast of the skeleton belonging to GMV 2124, which is probably not a <i>Sinosauropteryx</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The specimen NIGP 127587 was preserved with the remains of a <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizard</a> in its gut region, indicating that small, fast-moving animals made up part of the diet of <i>Sinosauropteryx prima</i>. Numerous lizards of this type have been found in the same rocks as <i>Sinosauropteryx</i>, but have yet to be described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The possible <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> specimen GMV 2124 (<i>Sinosauropteryx?</i> sp.) was found with three mammal jaws in its gut region. Hurum, Luo, and Kielan-Jaworowska (2006) identified two of these jaws as belonging to <i><a href="/wiki/Zhangheotherium" title="Zhangheotherium">Zhangheotherium</a></i> and the third to <i><a href="/wiki/Sinobaatar" title="Sinobaatar">Sinobaatar</a></i>, showing that these two mammals were part of the animal's diet. <i>Zhangheotherium</i> is known to have had a spur on the ankle, like the modern <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a>, which would indicate that <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> fed on possibly venomous mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurumetal2006_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-hurumetal2006-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The same specimen of <i>S. prima</i> which had preserved a lizard in its stomach contents (NIGP 127587) also had several small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in its abdomen. Two eggs were preserved just in front of and above the <a href="/wiki/Pubis_(bone)" title="Pubis (bone)">pubic</a> boot, and several more may lie underneath them on the slab. It is unlikely that they were eaten by the animal, as they are in the wrong part of the body cavity for the egg shells to have remained intact. It is more likely that they are unlaid eggs produced by the animal itself. Each egg measured 36 mm (1.4 in) long by 26 mm (1.0 in) wide. The presence of two developed eggs suggests that <i>Sinosauropteryx</i> had dual <a href="/wiki/Oviduct" title="Oviduct">oviducts</a> and laid eggs in pairs, like other theropods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeoecology">Palaeoecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinosauropteryx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Palaeoecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Sinosauropteryx</i>, as a <a href="/wiki/Yixian_Formation" title="Yixian Formation">Yixian Formation</a> dinosaur, is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Jehol_Biota" title="Jehol Biota">Jehol Biota</a>, the assemblage of organisms found in the Yixian Formation and overlying <a href="/wiki/Jiufotang_Formation" title="Jiufotang Formation">Jiufotang Formation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhou2006_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-zhou2006-16">[16]</a></sup> The Yixian Formation is composed largely of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rocks</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>. Between the volcanic layers are several beds of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> representing deposition in a <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenetal1998_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-chenetal1998-1">[1]</a></sup> The freshwater lake strata of the Yixian Formation have preserved a wide variety of plants, invertebrates, and vertebrates. <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">Gymnosperm</a> forests were extensive, with a few early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">flowering plants</a> as well. <a href="/wiki/Ostracod" title="Ostracod">Ostracods</a> and insects were diverse, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropods</a> were abundant. Mammals and birds are also well-known from the formation. The setting was subject to periodic mortality events including volcanic eruptions, <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">noxious gases erupting from the lakes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhou2006_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-zhou2006-16">[16]</a></sup> The climate has been interpreted as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a>, with distinct wet and dry seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wangetal2006conifer_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-wangetal2006conifer-28">[28]</a></sup> The yearly temperature during this time period averaged about 10 degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">celsius</a> (50 degrees <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a>), indicating a temperate climate with unusually cold winters for the generally warm Mesozoic era, possibly due to northern China's high latitude during this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amoitetal2011_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-amoitetal2011-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
