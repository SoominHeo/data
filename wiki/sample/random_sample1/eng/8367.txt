<p><b>Île aux Cygnes</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[il o siɲ]</a></span>; English: Isle of the Swans) is a small <a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">artificial island</a> on the river <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">Seine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, France, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/15th_arrondissement" title="15th arrondissement">15th arrondissement</a>. It was created in 1827 to protect the port of <a href="/wiki/Grenelle" title="Grenelle">Grenelle</a>. It should not be confused with an earlier <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_des_Cygnes_(former_island)" title="Île des Cygnes (former island)">Île des Cygnes</a> that was attached to the <a href="/wiki/Champ_de_Mars" title="Champ de Mars">Champ de Mars</a> in the late 18th century.</p>
<p>The uninhabited island is 850 metres (2,789 ft) long and 11 metres (36 ft) at its widest point, making it the third-largest island in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michelin_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michelin-1">[1]</a></sup> A tree-lined walkway, named L'Allée des Cygnes (Path of Swans), runs the length of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since 2012, there has been a public workout space with bicycles and a climbing wall underneath the <a href="/wiki/Pont_de_Grenelle" title="Pont de Grenelle">Pont de Grenelle</a>, close to a <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a> replica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michelin_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Michelin-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island is crossed by three bridges: the Pont de Grenelle, the <a href="/wiki/Pont_Rouelle" title="Pont Rouelle">Pont Rouelle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pont_de_Bir-Hakeim" title="Pont de Bir-Hakeim">Pont de Bir-Hakeim</a>. It is served by the <a href="/wiki/Passy_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Passy (Paris Métro)">Passy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bir-Hakeim_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Bir-Hakeim (Paris Métro)">Bir-Hakeim</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_stations_of_the_Paris_M%C3%A9tro" title="List of stations of the Paris Métro">Métro stations</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statue_of_Liberty_replica">Statue of Liberty replica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8Ele_aux_Cygnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statue of Liberty replica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A notable feature is a quarter scale replica of <i>Liberty Enlightening the World</i>, commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a>, 22 metres tall and facing west in the direction of its larger sibling in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Inaugurated by President <a href="/wiki/Marie_Fran%C3%A7ois_Sadi_Carnot" title="Marie François Sadi Carnot">Marie François Sadi Carnot</a> on 4 July 1889,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> nearly three years after its US counterpart, it was given to the city of Paris by the Parisian community in America to mark the <a href="/wiki/Centennial" title="Centennial">centennial</a> of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>.</p>
<p>The statue originally faced east, toward the <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a>, but it was turned west in 1937 for the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_fair" title="World's fair">world's fair</a> hosted in Paris that year. At its base is a commemorative plaque, and the tablet in its left hand bears the inscription <i>IV Juillet 1776 = XIV Juillet 1789</i>, recognizing the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(U.S.)" title="Independence Day (U.S.)">Independence Day</a> and the French <a href="/wiki/Bastille_Day" title="Bastille Day">Bastille Day</a>.</p>
<p>In 1998, on the occasion of the "Year of France in Japan", the 14-ton statue was transported to Japan and displayed on <a href="/wiki/Odaiba" title="Odaiba">Odaiba</a>, an artificial island in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Bay" title="Tokyo Bay">Tokyo Bay</a>, before returning to Paris the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A smaller statue is located in the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Orsay" title="Musée d'Orsay">Musée d'Orsay</a> and another can be seen at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Arts_et_M%C3%A9tiers" title="Musée des Arts et Métiers">Musée des Arts et Métiers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
