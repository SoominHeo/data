<p>The <b>Cathedral Church and Minor Basilica of the Immaculate Mother of God, Help of Christians</b> (colloquially, <b>St Mary's Cathedral</b>) is the cathedral church of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Sydney" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Sydney">Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Sydney</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cathedra" title="Cathedra">seat</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Bishops_and_Archbishops_of_Sydney" title="Catholic Bishops and Archbishops of Sydney">Archbishop of Sydney</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Fisher" title="Anthony Fisher">Anthony Fisher</a> <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_Preachers" title="Order of Preachers">OP</a></small>. It is dedicated to the "Immaculate Mother of God, Help of Christians", Patroness of Australia and holds the title and dignity of a <a href="/wiki/Minor_basilica" title="Minor basilica">minor basilica</a>, bestowed upon it by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">Pope Pius XI</a> on 4 August 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>St Mary's has the greatest length of any church in Australia (although it is neither the tallest nor largest overall). is located on <a href="/wiki/College_Street,_Sydney" title="College Street, Sydney">College Street</a> in the heart of the City of Sydney where, despite the high-rise development of the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_central_business_district" title="Sydney central business district">Sydney central business district</a>, its imposing structure and twin spires make it a landmark from every direction. In 2008, St Mary's Cathedral became the focus of <a href="/wiki/World_Youth_Day_2008" title="World Youth Day 2008">World Youth Day 2008</a> and was visited by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> who consecrated the new forward altar.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sydney was established as a penal settlement on <a href="/wiki/Australia_Day" title="Australia Day">26 January</a> 1788 in the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_George_III" title="King George III">King George III</a> by Captain <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Phillip" title="Arthur Phillip">Arthur Phillip</a>, for prisoners transported from Britain. Many of the people to arrive in Sydney at that time were military, some with wives and family, there were also some free settlers. The first chaplain of the colony was the Reverend <a href="/wiki/Richard_Johnson_(chaplain)" title="Richard Johnson (chaplain)">Richard Johnson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. No specific provision was made for the religious needs of those many convicts and settlers who were Roman Catholics. To redress this, an <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">Irish Catholic</a> priest, a Father O'Flynn, travelled to the colony of New South Wales but, as he arrived without government sanction, he was sent home.</p>
<p>It was not until 1820 that two priests, Father Conolly and Father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Therry" title="John Therry">John Therry</a>, arrived to officially minister to the Roman Catholics in Australia. Father Conolly went to Tasmania and Father Therry remained in Sydney. Therry claimed that on the day of his arrival, he had a vision of a mighty church of golden stone dedicated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Blessed Virgin Mary</a> raising its twin spires above the city of Sydney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This vision came to pass, but not until after 180 years and three intermediate buildings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="One_church_after_another">One church after another</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: One church after another">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="449" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg/220px-Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg/330px-Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg/440px-Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sydney_Hyde_Park_and_St_Marys.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
St Mary's Cathedral from the west with Hyde Park in the foreground. 1940s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Father Therry applied for a grant of land on which to build a church. He asked for land on the western side of Sydney, towards <a href="/wiki/Darling_Harbour" title="Darling Harbour">Darling Harbour</a>. But the land allocated to him was towards the East, adjacent to a number of Governor <a href="/wiki/Lachlan_Macquarie" title="Lachlan Macquarie">Lachlan Macquarie</a>'s building projects, the hospital of 1811, the <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park_Barracks,_Sydney" title="Hyde Park Barracks, Sydney">Hyde Park Barracks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._James_Church,_Sydney" title="St. James Church, Sydney">St James'</a> Anglican church which was also used as a law court. The site for the Catholic church overlooked a barren area upon which the bricks for Macquarie's buildings were made. The area is now Hyde Park, with avenues of trees and the Archibald Fountain.</p>
<p>The foundation stone for the first St Mary's was laid on 29 October 1821 by Governor Macquarie. Built by <a href="/wiki/James_Dempsey_(builder)" title="James Dempsey (builder)">James Dempsey</a>, it was a simple cruciform stone structure which paid homage to the rising fashion for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo_Gothic" title="Neo Gothic">Gothic</a> style in its pointed windows and pinnacles. In 1835, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Polding" title="John Polding">John Polding</a> became the first archbishop of the Roman Catholic Church in Australia. In 1851 the church was modified to the designs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Welby_Pugin" title="Augustus Welby Pugin">Augustus Welby Pugin</a>. Father Therry died on 25 May 1864. On 29 June 1865, the church caught fire and was destroyed.</p>
<p>The then archdeacon, Father McEnroe, immediately set about planning and fund raising in order to build the present cathedral, based upon a plan drawn up by Archbishop Polding. Polding wrote to <a href="/wiki/William_Wardell" title="William Wardell">William Wardell</a>, a pupil of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Welby_Pugin" title="Augustus Welby Pugin">Augustus Welby Pugin</a>, the most prominent architect of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a> movement. Polding was impressed with Wardell's building of <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_University_of_Sydney" title="St John's College, University of Sydney">St John's College</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sydney" title="University of Sydney">University of Sydney</a>. In his letter, Polding gives Wardell a completely free hand in the design, saying <i>"Any plan, any style, anything that is beautiful and grand, to the extent of our power."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Wardell had also designed and commenced work on <a href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_Cathedral,_Melbourne" title="St Patrick's Cathedral, Melbourne">St Patrick's Cathedral, Melbourne</a> in 1858.</p>
<p>There were to be two intermediate stages. A temporary wooden church was constructed, which was also destroyed by fire in the summer of 1869. The third temporary provision was a sturdy brick building on the site, not of the cathedral but of St Mary's School, which it was to serve long after the present structure was in use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney_HDR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3517" data-file-width="2527" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral%2C_Sydney_HDR.jpg/220px-St_Mary%27s_Cathedral%2C_Sydney_HDR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral%2C_Sydney_HDR.jpg/330px-St_Mary%27s_Cathedral%2C_Sydney_HDR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral%2C_Sydney_HDR.jpg/440px-St_Mary%27s_Cathedral%2C_Sydney_HDR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Main front, facing south</div>

<p>Archbishop Polding laid the foundation stone for the present cathedral in 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was to be a huge and ambitious structure with a wide nave and aisle and three towers. Polding did not live to see it in use as he died in 1877. Five years later, on 8 September 1882, his successor, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Vaughan" title="Roger Vaughan">Archbishop Vaughan</a>, presided at the dedication Mass. Archbishop Vaughan gave the peal of bells which were rung for the first time on that day. Vaughan died while in England in 1883.</p>
<p>The cathedral's builder (for this stage) was <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(building_contractor)" title="John Young (building contractor)">John Young</a>, who also built a large sandstone house in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a> style, known as "The Abbey", in <a href="/wiki/Annandale,_New_South_Wales" title="Annandale, New South Wales">Annandale, New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>But St Mary's was still far from finished, the work proceeding under <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Francis_Moran" title="Patrick Francis Moran">Cardinal Moran</a>. In 1913 <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kelly_(bishop)" title="Michael Kelly (bishop)">Archbishop Kelly</a> laid the foundation stone for the nave, which continued under the architects Hennessy, Hennessy and Co. In 1928 Kelly dedicated the nave in time for the commencement of the 29th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Eucharistic_Congress" title="International Eucharistic Congress">International Eucharistic Congress</a>. A slight difference of colour and texture of the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_sandstone" title="Sydney sandstone">sandstone</a> on the internal walls marks the division between the first and second stage of building.</p>
<p>The decoration and enrichment of the cathedral continued with the remains of <a href="/wiki/Roger_Vaughan" title="Roger Vaughan">Archbishop Vaughan</a> being returned to Sydney and buried within the Chapel of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Patrick" title="St Patrick">Irish Saints</a>. The richly decorated crypt which enshrines the bodies of many of the early priests and bishops was not completed until 1961 when it was dedicated by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Thomas_Gilroy" title="Norman Thomas Gilroy">Cardinal Gilroy</a>.</p>
<p>For many years the two squared-off towers of the façade gave a disappointing appearance to an otherwise-elegant building. It was from time to time suggested pinnacles should be put up to match the central tower as it appeared plain that William Wardell's proposed spires would never be built. But with the assistance of a grant from the Government to mark the new millennium, the spires were eventually built in 2000.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_2000">Since 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Since 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, St Mary's Cathedral became the focus of <a href="/wiki/World_Youth_Day_2008" title="World Youth Day 2008">World Youth Day 2008</a> and was visited by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> who, in his homily on 19 July, made the historic full apology for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_sex_abuse" title="Child sex abuse">child sex abuse</a> by Catholic priests in Australia, of whom 107 have been committed by the courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>On 16 December 2014, Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Fisher" title="Anthony Fisher">Anthony Fisher</a> invoked the special prayers in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Missal" title="Roman Missal">Roman Missal</a> from the "mass in times of civil disturbance" for the victims of the <a href="/wiki/2014_Sydney_hostage_crisis" title="2014 Sydney hostage crisis">2014 Sydney hostage crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A memorial service was held at the cathedral on the morning of 19 December for Tori Johnson and Katrina Dawson, in attendance were the Governor-General <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cosgrove" title="Peter Cosgrove">Peter Cosgrove</a>, then-Minister of Communications <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Turnbull" title="Malcolm Turnbull">Malcolm Turnbull</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sinodinos" title="Arthur Sinodinos">Arthur Sinodinos</a>, Premier of New South Wales <a href="/wiki/Mike_Baird" title="Mike Baird">Mike Baird</a>, and then-Minister of Transport <a href="/wiki/Gladys_Berejiklian" title="Gladys Berejiklian">Gladys Berejiklian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plan">Plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St Mary's Cathedral is unusual among large cathedrals in that, because of its size, the plan of the city around it and the fall of the land, it is oriented in a north-south direction rather than the usual east-west. The liturgical East End is at the north and the West Front is to the south.</p>
<p>The plan of the cathedral is a conventional English cathedral plan, cruciform in shape, with a tower over the crossing of the nave and transepts and twin towers at the West Front (in this case, the south). The chancel is square-ended, like the chancels of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Cathedral" title="Lincoln Cathedral">Lincoln</a>, <a href="/wiki/York_Minster" title="York Minster">York</a> and several other English cathedrals. There are three processional doors in the south with additional entrances conveniently placed in the transept facades so that they lead from <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_Sydney" title="Hyde Park, Sydney">Hyde Park</a> and from the presbytery buildings and school adjacent the cathedral.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The architecture is typical of the Gothic Revival of the 19th century, inspired by the journals of the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Camden_Society" title="Cambridge Camden Society">Cambridge Camden Society</a>, the writings of <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> and the architecture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Welby_Pugin" title="Augustus Welby Pugin">Augustus Welby Pugin</a>. At the time that the foundation stone was laid, the architect <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Blacket" title="Edmund Blacket">Edmund Blacket</a> had just completed Sydney's very much smaller Anglican cathedral in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perpendicular_Gothic" title="Perpendicular Gothic">Perpendicular Gothic</a> style and the Main Building of Sydney University. St Mary's, when William Wardell's plan was realised, was to be a much larger, more imposing and more sombre structure than the smaller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Andrew%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney" title="St. Andrew's Cathedral, Sydney">St Andrew's</a> and, because of its fortuitous siting, still dominates many views of the city despite the high-rise buildings.</p>
<p>The style of the cathedral is <a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture#Decorated_Gothic" title="English Gothic architecture">Geometric Decorated Gothic</a>, the archaeological antecedent being the ecclesiastical architecture of late 13th century England. It is based fairly closely on the style of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Cathedral" title="Lincoln Cathedral">Lincoln Cathedral</a>, the tracery of the huge chancel window being almost a replica of that at Lincoln.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior">Exterior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exterior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:392px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2263" data-file-width="3395" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg/390px-St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg/585px-St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg/780px-St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg 2x" width="390"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_from_the_north-west.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The cathedral seen from the north-west</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lateral view of the building from Hyde Park is marked by the regular progression of Gothic windows with pointed arches and simple tracery. The upper roofline is finished with a pierced parapet, broken by decorative gables above the clerestorey windows, above which rises a steeply pitched slate roof with many small dormers in the French manner. The roofline of the aisles is decorated with carved bosses between the sturdy buttresses which support flying <a href="/wiki/Buttress" title="Buttress">buttresss</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clerestorey" title="Clerestorey">clerestorey</a>.</p>
<p>Facing Hyde Park, the transept provides the usual mode of public entrance, as is common in many French cathedrals, and has richly decorated doors which, unlike those of the main front, have had their carved details completed and demonstrate the skills of local craftsmen in both designing and carving in the Gothic style. Included in the foliate bosses are Australian native plants such as the <a href="/wiki/Waratah" title="Waratah">waratah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floral_emblem" title="Floral emblem">floral emblem</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>.</p>
<p>St Mary's Cathedral is generally approached on foot from the city through Hyde Park, where the transept front and central tower rise up behind the <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Fountain" title="Archibald Fountain">Archibald Fountain</a>. During the 20th century the gardeners of Hyde Park have further enhanced the vista by laying out a garden on the cathedral side of the park in which the plantings have often taken the form of a cross.</p>
<p>Despite the many English features of the architecture including its interior and chancel termination, the entrance façade is not English at all. It is a design loosely based on the most famous of all Gothic west fronts, that of <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_de_Paris" title="Notre Dame de Paris">Notre Dame de Paris</a> with its balance of vertical and horizontal features, its three huge portals and its central rose window. There are two more large <a href="/wiki/Rose_window" title="Rose window">rose windows</a>, one in each of the transepts. The French façade was, however, intended to have twin stone spires like those of <a href="/wiki/Lichfield_Cathedral" title="Lichfield Cathedral">Lichfield Cathedral</a>, but they were not to be put in place until 132 years after the building was commenced.</p>
<p>The crossing tower, which holds the bells, is quite stocky but its silhouette is made elegant by the provision of tall crocketted pinnacles. The completed spires of the main front enhance the view of the cathedral along College Street and particularly the ceremonial approach from the flight of stairs in front of the cathedral. Standing at 74.6 metres (245 ft), they make St Mary's the fourth tallest church in Australia, after the triple-spired <a href="/wiki/St_Patrick%27s_Cathedral,_Melbourne" title="St Patrick's Cathedral, Melbourne">St Patrick's Cathedral, Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral,_Melbourne" title="St Paul's Cathedral, Melbourne">St Paul's Cathedral, Melbourne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Heart_Cathedral,_Bendigo" title="Sacred Heart Cathedral, Bendigo">Sacred Heart Cathedral, Bendigo</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

Interior during a service in 2009</div>

<p>In cross-section the cathedral is typical of most large churches in having a high central nave and an aisle on either side, which serve to buttress the nave and provide passage around the interior. The interior of the nave thus rises in three stages, the arcade, the gallery and the clerestory which has windows to light the nave. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_diagram" title="Cathedral diagram">cathedral diagram</a> The building is of golden-coloured sandstone which has weathered externally to golden-brown. The roof is of red cedar, that of the nave being of an open arch-braced construction enlivened by decorative pierced carvings. The chancel is vaulted with timber, which was probably intended to be richly decorated in red, blue and gold after the manner of the wooden roof at Peterborough, but this did not eventuate, and the warm colour of the timber contrasts well with the stonework.</p>
<p>The side aisles are vaulted in stone, with a large round boss at the centre of each ribbed vault. Children who, over the years, have crawled into the arched space beneath the pulpit have discovered another such beautiful carved boss, in miniature and usually unseen. On all the terminals of arches within the buildings are carved heads of saints. Those that are near the <a href="/wiki/Confessional" title="Confessional">confessionals</a> are at eye-level and may be examined for their details.</p>
<p>The screen behind the high altar is delicately carved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oamaru_limestone" title="Oamaru limestone">Oamaru limestone</a> from New Zealand. It contains niches filled with statues like the similar niches in the <a href="/wiki/Altar_of_Our_Lady" title="Altar of Our Lady">altar of Our Lady</a> located directly behind it. There are two large chapels and two smaller ones, the larger being the Chapel of the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Heart" title="Sacred Heart">Sacred Heart</a> and the Chapel of the Irish Saints. On either side of the Lady Chapel are the Chapels of Ss <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Joseph" title="St Joseph">Joseph</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peter" title="St Peter">Peter</a> all with ornately carved altars and a small statue in each niche. The embellished mosaics in the Kelly Chapel floor were laid by Melocco Co in 1937 approximately the same time as the mosaic floor in the Lady Chapel of <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_University_of_Sydney" title="St John's College, University of Sydney">St John's College</a> also designed by Wardell.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting">Lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of the brightness of Australian sunlight, it was decided at the time of construction to glaze the clerestorey with yellow glass. This glazing has darkened over the years, permitting little light to enter. The yellow glazing contrasts with the predominantly blue stained glass of the lower windows. To counteract the darkness, the cathedral installed extensive lighting in the 1970s, designed to give more-or-less equal illumination to all parts of the buildings. The interior is lit with a diffuse yellow glow, which, like the upper windows, is in contrast to the effects of the natural light which penetrates through the white areas of the stained glass. The installed lighting counteracts the pattern of light and shade that would normally exists in a cathedral of the Gothic style by illuminating most brightly those parts of the structure which would normally be subdued.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stained_glass">Stained glass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Stained glass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The glory of St Mary's Cathedral is the stained glass, all the work of <a href="/wiki/Hardman_%26_Co." title="Hardman &amp; Co.">Hardman &amp; Co.</a> and covering a period of about 50 years.</p>
<p>There are about 40 pictorial windows representing several themes and culminating in the chancel window showing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_Man" title="Fall of Man">Downfall of Humanity</a> and Mary, crowned and enthroned beside her Son as he sits in Judgement, pleading Jesus' mercy upon Christians.</p>
<p>Other windows include the Mysteries of the <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">Rosary</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Christ" title="Nativity of Christ">Birth</a> and Childhood of Jesus and <a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Saints" title="Lives of the Saints">Lives of the Saints</a>. Stylistically, the windows move from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a> of the 19th century to a more painterly and lavish style of the early 20th century. The three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rose_windows" title="Rose windows">rose windows</a> above the entrances, in particular, excite a lot of admiration. Many of the windows are of exceptional quality but the masterpiece is the huge window of the chancel, which has few, if any, rivals among the world's 19th century windows for beauty of design. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stained_glass_-_British_glass,_1811-1918" title="Stained glass - British glass, 1811-1918">Stained glass - British glass, 1811-1918</a></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treasures">Treasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Treasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>St Mary's is full of treasures and devotional objects. Around the walls of the aisles are located the <a href="/wiki/Stations_of_the_Cross" title="Stations of the Cross">Stations of the Cross</a>, painted in oils by L. Chovet of Paris and selected for St Mary's by Cardinal Moran in 1885. In the western transept is a marble replica of Michelangelo's <a href="/wiki/Piet%C3%A0_(Michelangelo)" title="Pietà (Michelangelo)">Pietà</a>, the original of which is in <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>, Rome. This sculpture was brought to Australia for display in David Jones Ltd. department store and was later donated to the cathedral.</p>
<p>Located previously in the crypt, where it was touched in the evening by the setting sun, was the Grave of the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldier" title="Tomb of the Unknown Soldier">Unknown Soldier</a>, a realistic depiction of a dead soldier sculpted by <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Lambert" title="George Washington Lambert">George Washington Lambert</a>. Although previously visible to the public from above, the tomb has now been moved into the aisle of the cathedral, to give the public greater access to it.</p>
<p>The crypt has an extensive mosaic floor, the achievement of Peter Melocco and his firm. This design has as its foundation a cross elaborately decorated like a vast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_Art" title="Celtic Art">Celtic</a> <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscript</a>, with rondels showing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_myth" title="Genesis creation myth">Days of Creation</a> and the titles of the Virgin Mary.</p>

<li>The first pipe organ installed in the original cathedral was built by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Bevington" title="Henry Bevington">Henry Bevington</a> of London and installed in June 1841. Of two manuals and 23 stops, it was the largest organ in Australia until its destruction by fire in 1865.</li>
<li>In 1942 a pipe organ built by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Howell_Whitehouse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joseph Howell Whitehouse (page does not exist)">Joseph Howell Whitehouse</a> of Brisbane, (1874–1954), was installed in the gallery at the end of the nave, above the main door.</li>
<li>Between 1959 and 1971 <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Sharp" title="Ronald Sharp">Ronald Sharp</a> (b. 1929) installed an electric-action pipe organ in the triforium gallery of the sanctuary, but this was never completed. This is only used for the weekly Novena Mass in the Lady Chapel.</li>
<li>In 1997 a new <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">pipe organ</a> was built by <a href="/wiki/Orgues_L%C3%A9tourneau" title="Orgues Létourneau">Orgues Létourneau</a> of Saint Hyacinthe, Quebec, and installed on a new gallery built around the rose window in the western transept. It was completed in 1999 and dedicated by Cardinal Clancy. The Létourneau organ is of three manuals and 46 stops. In addition to being played from the gallery, it may be played with the Whitehouse organ, from a four-manual mobile electronic console located at floor level.</li>
<li>There is a Rodgers digital organ in the quire, which accompanies the daily choral liturgies.</li>
<li>There is an organ in the crypt, made by Bellsham Pipe Organs (1985), which is used for liturgies within the crypt.</li>
</ul>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Choirs">Choirs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Choirs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two choirs that sing liturgically at the cathedral. The Cathedral Choir sings almost daily. There is also a voluntary adult choir, the St Mary's Singers, which sings regularly for services and occasional concerts.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="St_Mary.27s_Cathedral_Choir">St Mary's Cathedral Choir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: St Mary's Cathedral Choir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Begun in the 19th century as a mixed voice choir, the <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Cathedral_Choir,_Sydney" title="St Mary's Cathedral Choir, Sydney">St Mary's Cathedral Choir</a> has been a traditional English-style cathedral choir of men and boys since 1955. There are usually 24 choristers (boy trebles and altos) and 12 lay clerks (professional countertenors, tenors and basses). The choir sings Mass and Vespers daily (excluding Friday) and is the oldest continuous musical institution in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They perform works ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_Chant" title="Gregorian Chant">Gregorian Chant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyphony#Western_Europe_and_Roman_Catholicism" title="Polyphony">Sacred Polyphony</a> and Renaissance compositions to 21st century compositions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cathedral_Scholars">Cathedral Scholars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cathedral Scholars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Choristers from the Cathedral Choir whose voices have broken continue their training as part of this special ensemble. The Scholars sing Vespers and Mass on Wednesdays and Saturdays.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="St_Mary.27s_Singers">St Mary's Singers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: St Mary's Singers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The St Mary's Singers is an adult choir of volunteer mixed voices which sings for Vespers and Mass on the third Saturday of every month, as well as at other liturgical occasions, and gives regular concerts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bells">Bells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Mary%27s_Cathedral,_Sydney&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Bells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bells of St Mary's Cathedral have a unique place in Australian history. There have been three separate <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_bells" title="Ring of bells">rings of bells</a> at the cathedral, all cast by the <a href="/wiki/Whitechapel_Bell_Foundry" title="Whitechapel Bell Foundry">Whitechapel Bell Foundry</a> of London. The first, of eight bells, arrived in Sydney in August 1843, and were installed in a wooden campanile located away from the main building (approximately where the pulpit is today). They were the first bells hung for <a href="/wiki/Change_ringing" title="Change ringing">change ringing</a> in Australia and rang for the first time on New Year's Day <a href="/wiki/1844_in_Australia" title="1844 in Australia">1844</a>.</p>
<p>When the cathedral was destroyed by fire in June 1865, the bells escaped damage. Construction of new cathedral began in 1866, and during 1868 the bells were removed from the campanile and installed in the new tower, which was situated where the south-western tower now stands.</p>
<p>In 1881 the bells were traded in for a new ring of eight which were installed the following year (the Whitechapel Foundry was then using the name Mears &amp; Stainbank). In 1885 a contract was signed for the construction of the Central Tower (the Moran Tower) to which the bells were transferred in 1898.</p>
<p>A century later an entirely new ring was ordered. Today the Central Tower houses a ring of twelve bells, plus two additional bells which allow alternative groups of bells to be rung, depending on the number and skill of the ringers available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> They were rung for the first time in 1986.</p>
<p>Seven bells from the 1881 peal now form part of a ring of twelve bells at St Francis Xavier Cathedral in Adelaide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The old tenor bell (the Moran Bell) became the Angelus bell and is located in St Mary's south-eastern tower.</p>
<p>The bells are rung regularly before Solemn Mass on Sundays and on major feast days. They are also rung by arrangement for weddings and funerals and to mark important civic occasions. The bells of St Mary's were heard leading the ringing which marked the centenary of Australian Federation. They are also rung as part of the finale to Sydney's <a href="/wiki/Symphony_in_the_Domain" title="Symphony in the Domain">Symphony in the Domain</a> concert in January, in unison with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tchaikovsky" title="Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky</a>'s <a href="/wiki/1812_Overture" title="1812 Overture">1812 Overture</a>.</p>
<p>The ringing and care of the bells is entrusted to the St Mary's Basilica Society of Change Ringers, members of <a href="/wiki/The_Australian_and_New_Zealand_Association_of_Bellringers" title="The Australian and New Zealand Association of Bellringers">The Australian and New Zealand Association of Bellringers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
