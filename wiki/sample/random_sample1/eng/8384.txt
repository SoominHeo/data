<p><b>Noboru Okamoto</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">岡本 登</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Okamoto Noboru</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born February 15, 1932 in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, Japan)</span>, known by the pen name <b>Sanpei Shirato</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">白土 三平</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shirato Sanpei</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, is a Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manga_artist" title="Manga artist">manga artist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essayist" title="Essayist">essayist</a> known for his <a href="/wiki/Social_criticism" title="Social criticism">social criticism</a> as well as his realistic drawing style and the characters in his scenarios. He is considered a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Gekiga" title="Gekiga">gekiga</a>. The son of the Japanese proletarian painter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Toki_Okamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Toki Okamoto (page does not exist)">Toki Okamoto</a>, his dream to become an artist equal with his father started when he became a <a href="/wiki/Kamishibai" title="Kamishibai">Kamishibai</a> artist. He is also known for his work published in the early issues of the manga anthology magazine <a href="/wiki/Garo_(magazine)" title="Garo (magazine)"><i>Garo</i></a> in 1964, which he began publishing so as to serialize his comic <i>Kamui</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanpei_Shirato&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his childhood Shirato's father was active in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proletarian_culture" title="Proletarian culture">proletarian culture</a> movement, being one of the few people to be photographed with the tortured corpse of proletarian leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kobayashi_Takiji" title="Kobayashi Takiji">Kobayashi Takiji</a>. As he grew up he experienced the rancor of the war years, and it is said that these grim emotions come out in the <a href="/wiki/Nihilism" title="Nihilism">nihilistic</a> society portrayed in his works.</p>
<p>Shirato developed his artistic style through painting picture-card shows (<a href="/wiki/Kamishibai" title="Kamishibai">kamishibai</a>) after finishing middle school at 18. He was influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Ukiyo-e" title="Ukiyo-e">Ukiyo-e</a> of the pre-Meiji period, but differed in his portrayal of action in a multi-panel "slow motion" style unique to his manga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> His style of action portrayal came from the tension building techniques inherent in the performance aspect of Kami-shibai. Shirato started his career as a professional manga artist in 1957 with <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ninja_bugeich%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ninja bugeichō (page does not exist)">Ninja bugeichō</a></i>, a historically-based ninja-themed manga that captured the attention of students and intellectuals of the time. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Kamui" title="The Legend of Kamui">The Legend of Kamui</a></i>, the first series published in <i>Garo</i>, can be considered his most important manga work. It is the story of Kamui, a ninja who leaves an organization that pursues him and clearly sees the true nature of the <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> period and the discrimination that existed in the feudal system. Shirato's works are primarily historical dramas that focus on <a href="/wiki/Ninja" title="Ninja">ninja</a>, present a historical record of Japan, and criticize oppression, discrimination, and exploitation.</p>
<p>Many of Shirato's works have been adapted as anime series and films, including <i>Ninja bugeichō</i>, adapted by <a href="/wiki/Nagisa_Oshima" title="Nagisa Oshima">Nagisa Oshima</a> as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Band_of_Ninja&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Band of Ninja (page does not exist)">Band of Ninja</a></i> in 1967, an unusual film consisting only of images from the manga and voiceovers with no animation. Some works have received attention in the United States, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Kamui" title="The Legend of Kamui">The Legend of Kamui</a></i>, which was released in 1987 by <a href="/wiki/Viz_Media" title="Viz Media">Viz Media</a>, but others remain relatively unknown.</p>
