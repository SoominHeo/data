<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Shirkuh</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For places in Iran, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Shirkuh,_Iran_(disambiguation)" title="Shirkuh, Iran (disambiguation)">Shirkuh, Iran</a>.</div>
<p><b>Asad ad-Dīn Shīrkūh bin Shādhī</b> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">أسد الدين شيركوه بن شاذي</span>‎‎), also known as <b>Shirkuh</b>, <b>Shêrkoh</b>, or <b>Shêrko</b> (meaning "lion of the mountains" both in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_language" title="Kurdish language">Kurdish</a>) (died 22 February 1169) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_people" title="Kurdish people">Kurdish</a> military commander, and uncle of <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a>. His military and diplomatic efforts in Egypt were a key factor in establishing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayyubid" title="Ayyubid">Ayyubid</a> family in that country.</p>
<p>He was originally from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_people" title="Kurdish people">Kurdish</a> village in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> near the town of <a href="/wiki/Dvin_(ancient_city)" title="Dvin (ancient city)">Dvin</a>. He was the son of Shadhi ibn Marwan, a Kurdish ruler, and was the brother of <a href="/wiki/Najm_ad-Din_Ayyub" title="Najm ad-Din Ayyub">Najm ad-Din Ayyub</a>, the ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/Ayyubid_dynasty" title="Ayyubid dynasty">Ayyubid dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The family was closely connected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaddadid" title="Shaddadid">Shaddadid</a> dynasty, and when the last Shaddadid was deposed in Dvin in 1130, Shahdi moved the family first to <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> and then to <a href="/wiki/Tikrit" title="Tikrit">Tikrit</a>, where he was appointed governor by the regional administrator Bihruz. Ayyub succeeded his father as governor of Tikrit when Shahdi died soon after. When Shirkuh killed a Christian with whom he was quarrelling in Tikrit in 1138,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the brothers were exiled (Shirkuh's nephew Yusuf, later known as Saladin, was supposedly born the night they left). They joined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nur_ad-Din_Zengi" title="Nur ad-Din Zengi">Nur ad-Din Zengi</a>'s army, and Shirkuh served under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nur_ad-Din_Zengi" title="Nur ad-Din Zengi">Nur ad-Din Zengi</a> who succeeded Zengi in <a href="/wiki/Mosul" title="Mosul">Mosul</a>. Shirkuh was later given <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ar-Rahba" title="Ar-Rahba">ar-Rahba</a> and other appandages by Nur ad Din Zengi as his vassal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ayyub served as governor of <a href="/wiki/Baalbek" title="Baalbek">Baalbek</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, and the two brothers negotiated the surrender of Damascus to Nur ad-Din in 1154.</p>
<p>In 1163 Nur ad-Din was asked by <a href="/wiki/Shawar" title="Shawar">Shawar</a> to intervene in Egypt in a dispute between him and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dirgham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dirgham (page does not exist)">Dirgham</a> over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">Fatimid</a> <a href="/wiki/Vizier" title="Vizier">vizierate</a>. Nur ad-Din sent Shirkuh, and this was to be the first of three ventures Shirkuh made into Egypt, nominally on Nur ad-Din's behalf. On this first occasion, his nephew Saladin accompanied him as an advisor. Shawar was restored and Dirgham was killed, but after quarrelling with Shirkuh, Shawar allied with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amalric_I_of_Jerusalem" title="Amalric I of Jerusalem">Amalric I of Jerusalem</a>, who marched into Egypt in 1164 and besieged Shirkuh at <a href="/wiki/Bilbeis" title="Bilbeis">Bilbeis</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusader_invasion_of_Egypt" title="Crusader invasion of Egypt">Crusader invasion of Egypt</a>). In response Nur ad-Din attacked the <a href="/wiki/Crusader_states" title="Crusader states">Crusader states</a> and almost captured the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Antioch" title="Principality of Antioch">Principality of Antioch</a>.</p>
<p>Shirkuh was invited back into Egypt by the Fatimid Caliph <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-%27%C4%80%E1%B8%8Did" title="Al-'Āḍid">Al-'Āḍid</a> in 1167, to help defeat the Crusaders who were attacking Cairo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Shawar once again allied with Amalric, who besieged Shirkuh in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> until he agreed to leave; however, a Crusader garrison remained in Egypt and Amalric allied with the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, planning to conquer it entirely. To destroy the garrison, Shawar switched alliances, from Amalric to Shirkuh. The Muslims fought a pitched battle with the Crusaders, who did not have the resources to conquer Egypt and were forced to retreat.</p>
<p>Shirkuh and his associates enjoyed widespread support among the civil elite In Egypt for religious reasons. Although the Fatimid rulers were Shiite, the majority of people remained Sunni Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In January 1169 Shirkuh entered <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> and had the untrustworthy Shawar executed. When he reached Cairo with his armies he was welcomed by the Fatimid Caliph <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-%27%C4%80%E1%B8%8Did" title="Al-'Āḍid">Al-'Āḍid</a> and treated with great honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He accepted the office of vizier, but died two months later on March 22; as <a href="/wiki/Baha_ad-Din_ibn_Shaddad" title="Baha ad-Din ibn Shaddad">Baha ad-Din ibn Shaddad</a> describes, "it was the case that Asad ad-Din was a great eater, excessively given to partaking of rich meats. He suffered many bouts of indigestion and from <a href="/wiki/Peritonsillar_abscess" title="Peritonsillar abscess">quinsy</a>, from which he would recover after putting up with great discomfort. He was taken severely ill, afflicted with a serious quinsy, which killed him on 22 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jumada_al-thani" title="Jumada al-thani">Jumada II</a> 564 [22 February 1169]."</p>
<p>He was succeeded as vizier by his nephew <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a>, who had served with him on his campaigns in Egypt. Saladin eventually succeeded Nur ad-Din as well, uniting Egypt and Syria, which enabled him to almost completely drive out the crusaders from Syria and Palestine. A number of historians have offered the view that Shirkuh's death was an important factor in allowing Saladin to consolidate his position as Sultan and as undisputed head of the Ayyubid family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shirkuh is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_language" title="Kurdish language">Kurdish</a>-<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> name which literally means "the lion (of the) mountain". His Arabic honorific <i>Asad ad-Din</i> similarly means "the lion of faith". In <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, his name was rendered as "Siraconus"; <a href="/wiki/William_of_Tyre" title="William of Tyre">William of Tyre</a>, referring to the expedition of 1163, describes him as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"an able and energetic warrior, eager for glory and of wide experience in military affairs. Generous far beyond the resources of his patrimony, Shirkuh was beloved by his followers because of this munificence. He was small of stature, very stout and fat and already advanced in years. Though of lowly origin, he had become rich and risen by merit from his humble estate to the rank of prince. He was afflicted with <a href="/wiki/Cataract" title="Cataract">cataract</a> in one eye. He was a man of great endurance under hardships, one who bore hunger and thirst with an equanimity quite unusual for that time of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Although Nur ad-Din Zengi took back the domain of Homs on Shirkuh's death, in 1193 Saladin gave Homs to Shirkuh's son <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Shirkuh" title="Muhammad ibn Shirkuh">Muhammad ibn Shirkuh</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and his descendants continued to rule in Homs thereafter until the death in 1263 of his last descendant the emir, <a href="/wiki/Al-Ashraf_Musa,_Emir_of_Homs" title="Al-Ashraf Musa, Emir of Homs">Al-Ashraf Musa, Emir of Homs</a>. After this Homs was ruled directly as part of the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk" title="Mamluk">Mamluk</a> empire.</p>
