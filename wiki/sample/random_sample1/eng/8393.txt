<p><b>Syria</b> Is a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary state</a>, but for administrative purposes, it is divided into fourteen <b>governorates</b> (<i>muhafazat</i>, singular <i><a href="/wiki/Muhafazah" title="Muhafazah">muhafazah</a></i>). The <a href="/wiki/Governorate" title="Governorate">governorates</a> are divided into sixty <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Syria" title="Districts of Syria">districts</a> (<i>manatiq</i>, singular <i><a href="/wiki/Mintaqah" title="Mintaqah">mintaqah</a></i>), which are further divided into subdistricts (<i>nawahi</i>, singular <i><a href="/wiki/Nahiyah" title="Nahiyah">nahia</a></i>). The <i>nawahi</i> contain villages, which are the smallest administrative units.</p>
<p>Each governorate is headed by a governor, appointed by the president, subject to cabinet approval. The governor is responsible for administration, health, social services, education, tourism, public works, transportation, domestic trade, agriculture, industry, civil defense, and maintenance of law and order in the governorate. The minister of local administration works closely with each governor to coordinate and supervise local development projects. The governor is assisted by a provincial council, all of whose members are popularly elected for four-year terms. In addition, each council elects from among its member an executive bureau which administers the day to day issues between provincial council sessions. Each executive officer is charged with specific functions.</p>
<p>Districts and subdistricts are administered by officials appointed by the governor. These officials work on local matters with elected district councils and serve as intermediaries between the central government and traditional local leaders, such as village chiefs, clan leaders, and councils of elders.</p>
