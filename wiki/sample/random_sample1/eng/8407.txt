<p><b>Simone Signoret</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[simɔn siɲɔʁɛ]</a></span>; 25 March 1921 – 30 September 1985) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_cinema" title="French cinema">French cinema</a> actress often hailed as one of France's greatest film stars. She became the first French person to win an <a href="/wiki/32nd_Academy_Awards" title="32nd Academy Awards">Academy Award</a>, for her role in <i><a href="/wiki/Room_at_the_Top_(1959_film)" title="Room at the Top (1959 film)">Room at the Top</a></i> (1959).</p>
<p>In her lifetime she also received two <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="César Award for Best Actress">Césars</a>, three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTAs</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy" title="Emmy">Emmy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Cannes Film Festival Award for Best Actress">Cannes Film Festival Award</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Bear_for_Best_Actress" title="Silver Bear for Best Actress">Silver Bear for Best Actress</a> awards, a <a href="/wiki/National_Board_of_Review_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="National Board of Review Award for Best Actress">NBR Award</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> nomination.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simone_Signoret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Signoret was born <b>Simone Henriette Charlotte Kaminker</b> in <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a>, Germany, to André and Georgette (Signoret) Kaminker, as the eldest of three children, with two younger brothers. Her father, a pioneering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreter" title="Interpreter">interpreter</a> who worked in the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>, was a French-born army officer from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_Jewish" title="Polish Jewish">Polish Jewish</a> family,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> who brought the family to <a href="/wiki/Neuilly-sur-Seine" title="Neuilly-sur-Seine">Neuilly-sur-Seine</a> on the outskirts of Paris. Her mother, Georgette, from whom she acquired her stage name, was a <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Signoret grew up in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in an intellectual atmosphere and studied English, German and Latin. After completing secondary school during the Nazi Occupation, Simone was responsible for supporting her family and forced to take work as a typist for a French collaborationist newspaper, <i>Les nouveaux temps</i>, run by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Luchaire" title="Jean Luchaire">Jean Luchaire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simone_Signoret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_France_during_World_War_II" title="German occupation of France during World War II">German occupation of France</a>, Signoret mixed with an artistic group of writers and actors who met at a café in the <a href="/wiki/Saint-Germain-des-Pr%C3%A9s" title="Saint-Germain-des-Prés">Saint-Germain-des-Prés</a> quarter, <i><a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_de_Flore" title="Café de Flore">Café de Flore</a></i>. By this time, she had developed an interest in acting and was encouraged by her friends, including her lover, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_G%C3%A9lin" title="Daniel Gélin">Daniel Gélin</a>, to follow her ambition. In 1942, she began appearing in bit parts and was able to earn enough money to support her mother and two brothers as her father, who was a French patriot, had fled the country in 1940 to join General <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">De Gaulle</a> in England. She took her mother's maiden name for the screen to help hide her Jewish roots.</p>
<p>Signoret's sensual features and earthy nature led to type-casting and she was often seen in roles as a prostitute. She won considerable attention in <i><a href="/wiki/La_Ronde_(1950_film)" title="La Ronde (1950 film)">La Ronde</a></i> (1950), a film which was banned briefly in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> as immoral. She won further acclaim, including an acting award from the British Film Academy, for her portrayal of another prostitute in <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Becker" title="Jacques Becker">Jacques Becker</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casque_d%27or" title="Casque d'or">Casque d'or</a></i> (1951). She appeared in many notable films in France during the 1950s, including <i><a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_Raquin_(1953_film)" title="Thérèse Raquin (1953 film)">Thérèse Raquin</a></i> (1953), directed by <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Carn%C3%A9" title="Marcel Carné">Marcel Carné</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Diaboliques_(film)" title="Les Diaboliques (film)">Les Diaboliques</a></i> (1954), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crucible_(1957_film)" title="The Crucible (1957 film)">The Crucible</a></i> (<i>Les Sorcières de Salem</i>; 1956), based on <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Miller" title="Arthur Miller">Arthur Miller</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crucible" title="The Crucible">The Crucible</a></i>.</p>

<p>In 1958, Signoret acted in the English independent film, <i><a href="/wiki/Room_at_the_Top_(1959_film)" title="Room at the Top (1959 film)">Room at the Top</a></i> (1959), which won her numerous awards including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prix_d%27interpr%C3%A9tation_f%C3%A9minine" title="Prix d'interprétation féminine">Best Female Performance Prize</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a>. She was the only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_cinema" title="French cinema">French cinema</a> actress to receive an Oscar until <a href="/wiki/Juliette_Binoche" title="Juliette Binoche">Juliette Binoche</a> in 1997 (Supporting Actress) and <a href="/wiki/Marion_Cotillard" title="Marion Cotillard">Marion Cotillard</a> in 2008 (Best Actress), and the first woman to win the award appearing in a foreign film. She was offered films in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, but turned them down, continuing to work in France and England—notably opposite <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Term_of_Trial" title="Term of Trial">Term of Trial</a></i> (1962)—until 1965. Earning another Oscar nomination for her work on what would be <a href="/wiki/Vivien_Leigh" title="Vivien Leigh">Vivien Leigh</a>'s final film—<a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Ship_of_Fools_(film)" title="Ship of Fools (film)">Ship of Fools</a></i>, also starring <a href="/wiki/Lee_Marvin" title="Lee Marvin">Lee Marvin</a>—Signoret appeared in a few other Hollywood films before returning to France in 1969.</p>
<p>In 1962, Signoret translated Lillian Hellman's play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Foxes" title="The Little Foxes">The Little Foxes</a></i> into French for a production in Paris that ran for six months at the Theatre Sarah-Bernhardt. She played the Regina role as well. Hellman was displeased with the production, although the translation was approved by scholars selected by Hellman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Signoret's one attempt at Shakespeare, performing <a href="/wiki/Macbeth" title="Macbeth">Lady Macbeth</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Court_Theatre" title="Royal Court Theatre">Royal Court Theatre</a> in London in 1966 proved to be ill-advised, with some harsh critics; one referred to her English as "impossibly Gallic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her later years, Signoret was often criticized for gaining weight and letting her looks go, but she was never concerned with glamour, ignored the insults and continued giving finely etched performances. She won more acclaim for her portrayal of a weary madam in <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Rosa" title="Madame Rosa">Madame Rosa</a></i> (1977) and as an unmarried sister who unknowingly falls in love with her paralyzed brother via anonymous correspondence in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A8re_inconnue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chère inconnue (page does not exist)">I Sent a Letter to my Love</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%C3%A8re_inconnue" title="fr:Chère inconnue">fr</a>)</span></i> (1980).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simone_Signoret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Signoret's memoirs, <i>Nostalgia Isn't What It Used To Be</i>, were published in 1978. She also wrote a novel, <i>Adieu Volodya</i>, published in 1985, the year of her death.</p>
<p>Signoret first married filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Yves_All%C3%A9gret" title="Yves Allégret">Yves Allégret</a> (1944–49), with whom she had a daughter <a href="/wiki/Catherine_All%C3%A9gret" title="Catherine Allégret">Catherine Allégret</a>, herself an actress. Her second marriage was to the Italian-born French actor <a href="/wiki/Yves_Montand" title="Yves Montand">Yves Montand</a> in 1951, a union which lasted until her death.</p>
<p>Signoret died of <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic cancer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Autheuil-Authouillet" title="Autheuil-Authouillet">Autheuil-Authouillet</a>, France, aged 64. She was buried in <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_Lachaise_Cemetery" title="Père Lachaise Cemetery">Père Lachaise Cemetery</a> in Paris and Yves Montand was later buried next to her.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simone_Signoret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i>Marilyn</i> (2011) by Sue Glover, premiered at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens%27_Theatre" title="Citizens' Theatre">Citizens' Theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> on 17 February 2011. The play charted the deteriorating relationship between Signoret and <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a> during the filming of <i><a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Make_Love" title="Let's Make Love">Let's Make Love</a></i>. Unable to achieve the recognition of Oscar-winning Signoret, Monroe begins an affair with Signoret's husband, <a href="/wiki/Yves_Montand" title="Yves Montand">Yves Montand</a>.</li>
<li>Singer <a href="/wiki/Nina_Simone" title="Nina Simone">Nina Simone</a> (Born Eunice Waymon) took her last name from Simone Signoret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
