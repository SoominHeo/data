<p><b>Sivas</b> (<a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>: <span lang="hy">Սեբաստիա</span>; <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Sebastia</i>, <i>Sebastea</i>, <i>Sebasteia</i>, <i>Sebaste</i>) is a city in central Turkey and the seat of <a href="/wiki/Sivas_Province" title="Sivas Province">Sivas Province</a>. According to a 2011 estimate, its urban population is 425,297.</p>
<p>The city, which lies at an elevation of 4,193 feet (1,278 m) in the broad valley of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C4%B1z%C4%B1l%C4%B1rmak_(river)" title="Kızılırmak (river)">Kızılırmak</a> river, is a moderately-sized trade center and industrial city, although the economy has traditionally been based on agriculture. Rail repair shops and a thriving manufacturing industry of rugs, bricks, cement, and cotton and woolen textiles form the mainstays of the city's economy. The surrounding region is a cereal-producing area with large deposits of iron ore which are worked at <a href="/wiki/Divri%C4%9Fi" title="Divriği">Divriği</a>.</p>
<p>Sivas is also a communications hub for the north-south and east-west trade routes to Iraq and Iran, respectively. With the development of railways, the city gained new economic importance as junction of important rail lines linking the cities of <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">Ankara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kayseri" title="Kayseri">Kayseri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samsun" title="Samsun">Samsun</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Erzurum" title="Erzurum">Erzurum</a>. The city is linked by <a href="/wiki/Sivas_Airport" title="Sivas Airport">air</a> to <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>.</p>
<p>The popular name <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_(name)" title="Sebastian (name)">Sebastian</a> derives from the Latin <i>Sebastianus</i>, meaning someone from the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cresswell2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cresswell2007-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis1995_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis1995-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval">Ancient and medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sivas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Excavations at a mound known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Topraktepe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Topraktepe (page does not exist)">Topraktepe</a> indicate <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> settlement in the area as early as 2600 BC,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> though little is known of Sivas' history prior to its emergence in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> period. In 64 BC as part of his reorganization of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Third_Mithridatic_War" title="Third Mithridatic War">Third Mithridatic War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey the Great</a> founded a city on the site called "Megalopolis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Numismatic evidence suggests that Megalopolis changed its name in the last years of the 1st century BC to "Sebaste", which is the feminine form of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> name corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>. The name "Sivas" is the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> version deriving from the name Sebasteia, as the city was known during the late <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Roman (Byzantine) empire</a>. Sebasteia became the capital of the province of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenia_Minor" title="Armenia Minor">Armenia Minor</a> under the emperor <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a>, was a town of some importance in the early history of the Christian Church; in the 4th century it was the home of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Blaise" title="Saint Blaise">Saint Blaise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Sebaste" title="Peter of Sebaste">Saint Peter of Sebaste</a>, bishops of the town, and of <a href="/wiki/Eustathius_of_Sebaste" title="Eustathius of Sebaste">Eustathius</a>, one of the early founders of <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monasticism</a> in Asia Minor. It was also the place of martyrdom of the <a href="/wiki/Forty_Martyrs_of_Sebaste" title="Forty Martyrs of Sebaste">Forty Martyrs of Sebaste</a>, also 4th century. <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> had a fortified wall around it rebuilt in the 6th century.</p>
<p>Sebasteia was the first important city to be plundered by Turkish tribes in 1059.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the August of that year the troops of various emirs gathered before the unwalled city. Initially they hesitated to sack it, mistaking that the domes of the several Christian churches were tends of military camps. As soon as they realized that the city was defenceless they burned it for eight days, slaughtered large part of its population and took many prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vryonis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vryonis-7">[7]</a></sup> The city came under the domain of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkmen_people" title="Turkmen people">Turkmen</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danishmend" title="Danishmend">Danishmend</a> dynasty (1071–1174) after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Manzikert" title="Battle of Manzikert">Battle of Manzikert</a> in 1071. After the death of Danişmend Gazi, Sivas passed to Nizamettin <a href="/wiki/Ya%C4%9F%C4%B1basan" title="Yağıbasan">Yağıbasan</a> who won it after a struggle with Danişmend Gazi's successors. In 1174, the city was captured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Sultanate_of_R%C3%BBm" title="Seljuk Sultanate of Rûm">Seljuk</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Kilij_Arslan_II" title="Kilij Arslan II">Kilij Arslan II</a> and periodically served as capital of the Seljuk empire along with <a href="/wiki/Konya" title="Konya">Konya</a>. Under Seljuk rule, Sivas was an important center of trade along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silk_road" title="Silk road">silk road</a> and site of a citadel, along with mosques and madrasahs (Islamic educational institutions), four of which survive today and one of which houses the Sivas Museum. Then it passed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilkhanids" title="Ilkhanids">Ilkhanids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eretna" title="Eretna">Eretna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kadi_Burhan_al-Din" title="Kadi Burhan al-Din">Kadı Burhanettin</a>.</p>

<p>The city was acquired by Ottoman Sultan <a href="/wiki/Bayezid_I" title="Bayezid I">Bayezid I</a> (1389–1402). In 1398, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamerlane" title="Tamerlane">Tamerlane</a> swept into the area and his forces destroyed the city in 1400, after which it was recaptured by the Ottomans in 1408.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howorth_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howorth-8">[8]</a></sup> Under the Ottomans, Sivas served as the administrative center of the province of Rum until about the late 19th century. The <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a> maintained six Armenian churches in Sivas, being the Meryemana, Surp Sarkis, Surp Minas, Surp Prgitsh, Surp Hagop, Surp Kevork, four monasteries Surp Nschan, Surp Hreshdagabed, Surp Anabad, Surp Hntragadar, an Armenian Apostolic orphanage and several schools. The <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Catholic_Church" title="Armenian Catholic Church">Armenian Catholic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latins</a> also had one church and a metropolitan of Sebastea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Two Protestant churches and eight, mostly <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a>- and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_people" title="American people">American</a>-staffed, schools. During the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">genocide against Armenians</a> as well as against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Genocide" title="Greek Genocide">Greek Christians</a> from July 5, 1915 onwards, the Christian community of Sivas was exterminated by deportations and mass executions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sivas_Congress" title="Sivas Congress">Sivas Congress</a> (<i>Heyet-i Temiliye</i>) was held in this city 4–11 September 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halil87_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halil87-11">[11]</a></sup> With the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Atatürk</a> (1881–1938), the founder of the Turkish Republic, from <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasya</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_Sivas" title="Congress of Sivas">Congress of Sivas</a> is considered a turning point in the formation of the Turkish Republic. It was at this congress that Atatürk's position as chair of the executive committee of the national resistance was confirmed (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">Turkish War of Independence</a></i>). Sivas was depicted on the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">reverse</a> of the Turkish 500 <a href="/wiki/Turkish_lira" title="Turkish lira">lira</a> banknote of 1927-1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmb.gov.tr_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcmb.gov.tr-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 2 July 1993, 37 participants in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alevi" title="Alevi">Alevi</a> cultural and literary festival were killed when a mob of demonstrators set fire to the Madimak hotel in Sivas during a violent protest by some 15,000 members of various radical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist" title="Islamist">Islamist</a> groups against the presence of <a href="/wiki/Aziz_Nesin" title="Aziz Nesin">Aziz Nesin</a>. The deaths resulted in the Turkish government taking a harder stance against religious fanaticism, militant Islam, and antisecularism. In late 2006, there was a campaign by the <a href="/wiki/Pir_Sultan_Abdal" title="Pir Sultan Abdal">Pir Sultan Abdal</a> Cultural Institute to convert the former hotel into a museum to commemorate the tragedy, now known as the <a href="/wiki/Sivas_massacre" title="Sivas massacre">Sivas massacre</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sivas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sivas has a dry-summer <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>: <i>Dsb</i>), with warm and dry summers and cold and snowy winters. The driest months are July and August and the wettest are April and May.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sivas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, Sivas was known for producing <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a>. As of 1913, Sivas produced 79,000 tons of cereal, making it a major, and successful, industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero60_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero60-16">[16]</a></sup> Sivas also had <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchards</a>, exporting produce to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero62_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero62-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">Alfalfa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> was also produced in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero63_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero63-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero74_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero74-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">Sericulture</a> was seen in Sivas before 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero64_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero64-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sivas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
<p>A cultural hub as well as an industrial one, Sivas contains many examples of 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Seljuq_dynasty" title="Seljuq dynasty">Seljuk</a> architecture. The <i>Mavi Medrese</i> from 1271, the <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Eifaiye_Medrese" title="Şifaiye Medrese">Şifaiye Medresesi</a> from 1218 and the <i>Çifte Minare Medresesi</i> from 1271, with its intricately carved façade and minarets, are among the most noteworthy monuments. The oldest surviving mosque is the Grand Mosque (Ulu Camii) completed in 1196 is famous for its simplicity. The city is also famous for its <i>Medrese</i>s (Islamic seminaries). <i>Gök Medresesi</i> (the Celestial Madrasa; depicted on the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">obverse</a> of the Turkish 500 <a href="/wiki/Turkish_lira" title="Turkish lira">lira</a> banknote of 1927-1939<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmb.gov.tr_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcmb.gov.tr-12">[12]</a></sup>) and <i>Mavi Medrese</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sifaiye_Medresesi" title="Sifaiye Medresesi">Sifaiye Medresesi</a>, on the other hand, was completed earlier, on the eve of the second wave of Turkic immigration to Anatolia, in 1218 and with its intricately carved façade and minarets are among the most noteworthy edifices carries on the traditional Seljuk Medrese plan.</p>
<p>The city also contains some fine examples of the Ottoman architectural style. <i>Kurşunlu Hamamı</i> (Leaden Bath) which was completed in 1576, is the largest Turkish bath in the city and it contains many details from the classical Ottoman bath building. <i>Behrampaşa Hanı</i> (Caravansaray), was completed in 1573 and it is famous for the lion motifs around its windows.</p>
<p>Atatürk Congress and Ethnography Museum (<i>Atatürk Kongre ve Etnografya Müzesi</i>) is a museum with two sections. One is a dedicated to the Ottoman heritage of Sivas. The other is to the Sivas Congress, one of the pivotal moments in the Turkish national movement.</p>

<p>The modern heart of the city is Hükümet Square (Hükümet Meydanı, also called Konak Meydanı) located just next to the Governor's mansion. This area is also home to many of the city's high end hotels and restaurants. The city's shoppers usually head to Atatürk Avenue.</p>
<p>Sivas is also famous for its thermal springs which have a respectable percentage in the city's income. People believe that the water of these thermal springs can cure many illnesses. The most famous thermal areas are, <i>Sıcak Çermik</i>, <i>Soğuk Çermik</i> and <i>Kangal Balıklı Kaplıca</i>.</p>

<p>Football is the most popular sport: in the older districts above the city center children often kick balls around in the evenings in the smallest streets. The city's <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> club is <a href="/wiki/Sivasspor" title="Sivasspor">Sivasspor</a>, which plays its games at the <a href="/wiki/Sivas_4_Eyl%C3%BCl_Stadium" title="Sivas 4 Eylül Stadium">Sivas 4 Eylül Stadium</a>. The club played in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCper_Lig" title="Süper Lig">Süper Lig</a> between 2005 and 2016.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sivas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specialties of Sivas are Tarhana (a soup made using sour yogurt), Kelecos (a sour potato soup made with yoghurt) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Katmer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Katmer (page does not exist)">Katmer</a>, a kind of flatbread. One distinct feature of Sivas cooking is the use of Madimak which is a local herb similar to Spinach. Sivas kebabı is a variety of Kebab originating from Sivas.</p>
