<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Astronomical seeing</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Astronomical seeing</b> refers to the blurring and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scintillation_(astrophysics)" title="Scintillation (astrophysics)">twinkling</a> of astronomical objects such as stars caused by <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent mixing</a> in the Earth's atmosphere that causes variations of the optical <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>. The <i>astronomical seeing</i> conditions on a given night at a given location describe how much the Earth's atmosphere perturbs the images of stars as seen through a telescope.</p>
<p>The most common seeing measurement is the diameter (or more correctly the <a href="/wiki/Full_width_at_half_maximum" title="Full width at half maximum">full width at half maximum</a> or FWHM) of the optical intensity across the <i>seeing disc</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Point_spread_function" title="Point spread function">point spread function</a> for imaging through the atmosphere). The FWHM of the point spread function (loosely called seeing disc diameter or "<i>seeing</i>") is a reference to the best possible <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">angular resolution</a> which can be achieved by an <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">optical telescope</a> in a long photographic exposure, and corresponds to the FWHM of the fuzzy blob seen when observing a point-like source (such as a star) through the atmosphere. The size of the seeing disc is determined by the <i>astronomical seeing</i> conditions at the time of the observation. The best conditions give a seeing disk diameter of ~0.4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">arcseconds</a> and are found at high-altitude observatories on small islands such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea_Observatory" title="Mauna Kea Observatory">Mauna Kea</a> or <a href="/wiki/Roque_de_los_Muchachos_Observatory" title="Roque de los Muchachos Observatory">La Palma</a>.</p>
<p>Seeing is one of the biggest problems for Earth-based astronomy: while large <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> have theoretically milli-arcsecond resolution, the real image will never be better than the average seeing disc during the observation. This can easily mean a factor of 100 between the potential and practical resolution. Starting in the 1990s, new <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a> have been introduced that can help correct for these effects, dramatically improving the resolution of ground based telescopes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_seeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Astronomical seeing has several effects:</p>
<ol>
<li>It causes the images of <a href="/wiki/Point_source" title="Point source">point sources</a> (such as stars), which in the absence of atmospheric turbulence would be steady <a href="/wiki/Airy_disk" title="Airy disk">Airy patterns</a> produced by diffraction, to break up into speckle patterns, which change very rapidly with time (the resulting speckled images can be processed using <a href="/wiki/Speckle_imaging" title="Speckle imaging">speckle imaging</a>)</li>
<li>Long exposure images of these changing speckle patterns result in a blurred image of the point source, called a <i>seeing disc</i></li>
<li>The brightness of stars appears to fluctuate in a process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scintillation_(astronomy)" title="Scintillation (astronomy)">scintillation</a> or twinkling</li>
<li>Atmospheric seeing causes the fringes in an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_interferometer" title="Astronomical interferometer">astronomical interferometer</a> to move rapidly</li>
<li>The distribution of atmospheric seeing through the atmosphere (the C<sub>N</sub><sup>2</sup> profile described below) causes the image quality in <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a> systems to degrade the further you look from the location of reference star</li>
</ol>
<p>The effects of atmospheric seeing were indirectly responsible for the belief that there were <a href="/wiki/Martian_canal" title="Martian canal">canals on Mars</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In viewing a bright object such as Mars, occasionally a still patch of air will come in front of the planet, resulting in a brief moment of clarity. Before the use of <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">charge-coupled devices</a>, there was no way of recording the image of the planet in the brief moment other than having the observer remember the image and draw it later. This had the effect of having the image of the planet be dependent on the observer's memory and preconceptions which led the belief that Mars had linear features.</p>
<p>The effects of atmospheric seeing are qualitatively similar throughout the visible and near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infra-red" title="Infra-red">infra-red</a> wavebands. At large telescopes the long exposure image resolution is generally slightly higher at longer wavelengths, and the timescale (t<sub>0</sub> - see below) for the changes in the dancing speckle patterns is substantially lower.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures">Measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_seeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three common descriptions of the astronomical seeing conditions at an observatory:</p>
<ul>
<li>The full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the seeing disc</li>
<li>r<sub>0</sub> (the size of a typical "lump" of uniform air within the turbulent atmosphere<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) and t<sub>0</sub> (the time-scale over which the changes in the turbulence become significant)</li>
<li>The C<sub>N</sub><sup>2</sup> profile</li>
</ul>
<p>These are described in the sub-sections below:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_full_width_at_half_maximum_.28FWHM.29_of_the_seeing_disc">The full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the seeing disc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_seeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the seeing disc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Without an atmosphere, a small star would have an apparent size, an "<a href="/wiki/Airy_disk" title="Airy disk">Airy disk</a>", in a telescope image determined by <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> and would be inversely proportional to the diameter of the telescope. However, when light enters the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, the different temperature layers and different wind speeds distort the light waves, leading to distortions in the image of a star. The effects of the atmosphere can be modeled as rotating cells of air moving turbulently. At most observatories, the turbulence is only significant on scales larger than r<sub>0</sub> (see below—the seeing parameter r<sub>0</sub> is 10–20 cm at visible wavelengths under the best conditions) and this limits the resolution of telescopes to be about the same as given by a space-based 10–20 cm telescope.</p>
<p>The distortion changes at a high rate, typically more frequently than 100 times a second. In a typical astronomical image of a star with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exposure_time" title="Exposure time">exposure time</a> of seconds or even minutes, the different distortions average out as a filled disc called the point spread function or "seeing disc". The <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> of the seeing disk, most often defined as the <a href="/wiki/Full_width_at_half_maximum" title="Full width at half maximum">full width at half maximum</a> (FWHM), is a measure of the astronomical seeing conditions.</p>
<p>It follows from this definition that seeing is always a variable quantity, different from place to place, from night to night, and even variable on a scale of minutes. Astronomers often talk about "good" nights with a low average seeing disc diameter, and "bad" nights where the seeing diameter was so high that all observations were worthless.</p>

<p>The FWHM of the seeing disc (or just "seeing") is usually measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a>, abbreviated with the symbol (″). A 1.0″ seeing is a good one for average astronomical sites. The seeing of an urban environment is usually much worse. Good seeing nights tend to be clear, cold nights without wind gusts. Warm air rises (<a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>), degrading the seeing, as do wind and clouds. At the best high-altitude mountaintop <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatories</a>, the wind brings in stable air which has not previously been in contact with the ground, sometimes providing seeing as good as 0.4".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="r0_and_t0">r<sub>0</sub> and t<sub>0</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_seeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: r0 and t0">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The astronomical seeing conditions at an observatory can be conveniently described by the parameters r<sub>0</sub> and t<sub>0</sub>. For telescopes with diameters smaller than r<sub>0</sub>, the resolution of long-exposure images is determined primarily by diffraction and the size of the Airy pattern and thus is inversely proportional to the telescope diameter. For telescopes with diameters larger than r<sub>0</sub>, the image resolution is determined primarily by the atmosphere and is independent of telescope diameter, remaining constant at the value given by a telescope of diameter equal to r<sub>0</sub>. r<sub>0</sub> also corresponds to the length-scale over which the turbulence becomes significant (10–20 cm at visible wavelengths at good observatories), and t<sub>0</sub> corresponds to the time-scale over which the changes in the turbulence become significant. r<sub>0</sub> determines the spacing of the actuators needed in an <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a> system, and t<sub>0</sub> determines the correction speed required to compensate for the effects of the atmosphere.</p>
<p>The parameters r<sub>0</sub> and t<sub>0</sub> vary with the wavelength used for the astronomical imaging, allowing slightly higher resolution imaging at longer wavelengths using large telescopes.</p>
<p>The seeing parameter r<sub>0</sub> is often known as the <a href="/wiki/Fried_parameter" title="Fried parameter">Fried parameter</a> (pronounced "freed"), named after <a href="/wiki/David_L._Fried" title="David L. Fried">David L. Fried</a>. The atmospheric time constant t<sub>0</sub> is often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Greenwood_frequency" title="Greenwood frequency">Greenwood time constant</a>, after <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Darryl_Greenwood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Darryl Greenwood (page does not exist)">Darryl Greenwood</a>.</p>

<p>In reality, the pattern of blobs (<i>speckles</i>) in the images changes very rapidly, so that long-exposure photographs would just show a single large blurred blob in the center for each telescope diameter. The diameter (FWHM) of the large blurred blob in long-exposure images is called the seeing disc diameter, and is independent of the telescope diameter used (as long as adaptive optics correction is not applied).</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Kolmogorov_model_of_turbulence">The Kolmogorov model of turbulence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_seeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Kolmogorov model of turbulence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A description of the nature of the wavefront perturbations introduced by the atmosphere is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence#Kolmogorov.27s_theory" title="Turbulence">Kolmogorov model</a> developed by Tatarski,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> based partly on the studies of <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> by the Russian mathematician <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Kolmogorov" title="Andrey Kolmogorov">Andreï Kolmogorov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This model is supported by a variety of experimental measurements<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and is widely used in simulations of astronomical imaging. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overcoming_atmospheric_seeing">Overcoming atmospheric seeing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_seeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Overcoming atmospheric seeing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seeing_Moon.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="150" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ef/Seeing_Moon.gif" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">An animated image of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>'s surface showing the effects of Earth's atmosphere on the view</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first answer to this problem was <a href="/wiki/Speckle_imaging" title="Speckle imaging">speckle imaging</a>, which allowed bright objects with simple morphology to be observed with diffraction-limited angular resolution. Later came <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, working outside the atmosphere and thus not having any seeing problems and allowing observations of faint targets for the first time (although with poorer resolution than speckle observations of bright sources from ground-based telescopes because of Hubble's smaller telescope diameter). The highest resolution visible and infrared images currently come from imaging optical <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_interferometer" title="Astronomical interferometer">interferometers</a> such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navy_Prototype_Optical_Interferometer" title="Navy Prototype Optical Interferometer">Navy Prototype Optical Interferometer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Optical_Aperture_Synthesis_Telescope" title="Cambridge Optical Aperture Synthesis Telescope">Cambridge Optical Aperture Synthesis Telescope</a>, but those can only be used on very bright stars.</p>
<p>Starting in the 1990s, many telescopes have developed <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a> systems that partially solve the seeing problem. The best systems so far built, such as <a href="/wiki/Spectro-Polarimetric_High-Contrast_Exoplanet_Research" title="Spectro-Polarimetric High-Contrast Exoplanet Research">SPHERE</a> on the ESO <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">VLT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gemini_Planet_Imager" title="Gemini Planet Imager">GPI</a> on the Gemini telescope, achieve a <a href="/wiki/Strehl_ratio" title="Strehl ratio">Strehl ratio</a> of 90% at a wavelength of 2.2 micrometers, but only within a very small region of the sky at a time.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="608" data-file-width="1280" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg/220px-A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg/330px-A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg/440px-A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Mix_of_Colours_and_Wonder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Astronomers can make use of an artificial star by shining a powerful laser to correct for the blurring caused by the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A wider field of view can be obtained by using multiple deformable mirrors conjugated to several atmospheric heights and measuring the vertical structure of the turbulence, in a technique known as Multiconjugate Adaptive Optics.</p>
<p>Another cheaper technique, <a href="/wiki/Lucky_imaging" title="Lucky imaging">lucky imaging</a>, has had good results on smaller telescopes. This idea dates back to pre-war naked-eye observations of moments of good seeing, which were followed by observations of the planets on cine film after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The technique relies on the fact that every so often the effects of the atmosphere will be negligible, and hence by recording large numbers of images in real-time, a 'lucky' excellent image can be picked out. This happens more often when the number of r0-size patches over the telescope pupil is not too large, and the technique consequently breaks down for very large telescopes. It can nonetheless outperform adaptive optics in some cases and is accessible to amateurs. It does require very much longer observation times than <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">adaptive optics</a> for imaging faint targets, and is limited in its maximum resolution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
