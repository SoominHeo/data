<p><b>Sisoridae</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfishes</a>. These Asian catfishes live in fast-moving waters and often have adaptations that allow them to adhere to objects in their habitats. The family includes about 235 species.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sisoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The family Sisoridae is recognized as a natural, <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a> group based on morphological and molecular evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guo_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guo-2">[2]</a></sup> It is divided into two subfamilies, <b>Sisorinae</b> and <b>Glyptosterninae</b> (<b>glyptosternoids</b>). The Sisorinae contain the five genera <i><a href="/wiki/Bagarius" title="Bagarius">Bagarius</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gagata" title="Gagata">Gagata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gogangra" title="Gogangra">Gogangra</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nangra" title="Nangra">Nangra</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sisor" title="Sisor">Sisor</a></i>. The Glyptosterninae contain three tribes. <b>Glyptothoracini</b> contains only the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Glyptothorax" title="Glyptothorax">Glyptothorax</a></i> and <b>Pseudecheneidina</b> contains only the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudecheneis" title="Pseudecheneis">Pseudecheneis</a></i>. The remaining genera, <i><a href="/wiki/Euchiloglanis" title="Euchiloglanis">Euchiloglanis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Exostoma" title="Exostoma">Exostoma</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaridoglanis" title="Glaridoglanis">Glaridoglanis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Glyptosternon" title="Glyptosternon">Glyptosternon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Myersglanis" title="Myersglanis">Myersglanis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Oreoglanis" title="Oreoglanis">Oreoglanis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Parachiloglanis" title="Parachiloglanis">Parachiloglanis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pareuchiloglanis" title="Pareuchiloglanis">Pareuchiloglanis</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudexostoma" title="Pseudexostoma">Pseudexostoma</a></i>, are contained in the tribe <b>Glyptosternina</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyly</a> of the entire family and the tribe Glyptosterninae are well supported by osteological morphology and molecular data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the genera <i>Glyptothorax</i> (tribe Glyptothoracini) and <i>Pseudecheneis</i> (tribe Pseudecheneidina), the species have thoracic adhesive apparatuses to attach to objects in the stream bed; in <i>Glyptothorax</i>, grooves of this apparatus run parallel or oblique to the axis of the body, while in <i>Pseudecheneis</i> grooves run transverse to the axis of the body. The thoracic adhesive apparatus is not present in the other sisorid genera. The paired fins may be <a href="/wiki/Skin_fold" title="Skin fold">plaited</a> to form an adhesive apparatus in <i>Pseudecheneis</i>, glyptosternoids, and variably in <i>Glyptothorax</i>. Thus, glyptosternoids lack a thoracic adhesive apparatus, but do have plaited paired fins, and members of the subfamily Sisorinae lack either a thoracic adhesive apparatus or plaited paired fins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The monophyly of certain glyptosternoid genera is doubtful. The <a href="/wiki/Paraphyly" title="Paraphyly">paraphyly</a> of <i>Pareuchiloglanis</i>, <i>Oreoglanis</i>, and <i>Pseudexostoma</i> (with the possible inclusion of <i>Myersglanis</i> and <i>Parachiloglanis</i>) has been demonstrated and a rediagnosis of glyptosternine genera is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ng2004_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ng2004-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence from a 2007 molecular analysis supports polyphyly of <i>Pareuchiloglanis</i>. <i>Glaridoglanis</i> might be a <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> member of the tribe Glyptosternina. <i>Pseudecheneis</i> may be placed in the tribe Glyptosternina, but its sister-group relationship between it and the monophyletic glyptosternoids cannot be rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guo_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been proposed to move the genera of <a href="/wiki/Erethistidae" title="Erethistidae">Erethistidae</a> into Sisoridae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sisoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sisorids inhabit freshwater and originate from southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> to South <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, primarily in the Oriental region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-5">[5]</a></sup> Glyptosterninae is distributed from the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> to China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ng2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ng2005-6">[6]</a></sup> Most glyptosternine genera are found in China, with the exception of <i>Myersglanis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guo_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guo-2">[2]</a></sup> Glyptosternoid catfish species have restricted distributions, and many apparently wide-ranging species have been shown to consist of more than one species, each with restricted distributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ng2006_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ng2006-7">[7]</a></sup> Sisorids are mostly small forms inhabit mountain streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record_and_biogeography">Fossil record and biogeography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sisoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil record and biogeography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oldest known sisorid fossil is <i>B. bagarius</i> found in Sumatra and India of the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhou_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhou-8">[8]</a></sup> The origin of glyptosternoid fishes could be in the later Pliocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhou_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zhou-8">[8]</a></sup> Another study proposes glyptosternoids possibly originated in the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>-<a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> boundary (19–24 Mya) and radiated from the Miocene to <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> along with several rapid speciation events in a relatively short time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guo_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guo-2">[2]</a></sup> The three great uplifts of the <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>/<a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">Plateau</a> destroyed the pattern of river systems in the late Pliocene to the early Pleistocene. The ancestor of <i>Euchiloglanis</i> originated from the allied <i>Glyptosternon</i> in the second uplift and <i>Pareuchiloglanis</i>, <i>Pseudexostoma</i>, <i>Oreoglanis</i>, <i>Exostoma</i>, and <i>Glaridoglanis</i> originated with the third uplift. The <i>Exostoma</i> group (<i>Exostoma</i>, <i>Pseudexostoma</i>, and <i>Oreoglanis</i>) originated after the outline of the Qinghai/Tibet Plateau was formed. The speciation of this group was not strong and the distribution limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhou_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zhou-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sisoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of these fish have four pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbels" title="Barbels">barbels</a> and a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adipose_fin" title="Adipose fin">adipose fin</a>. The maximum size is 2 metres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-5">[5]</a></sup> In all fish except those of the subfamily Sisorinae, some sort of adhesive apparatus, either in the form of a thoracic adhesive apparatus or in plaited paired fins, allow the fish to adhere to objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
