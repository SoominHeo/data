<p><b>Xishuangbanna</b>, <b>Sibsongbanna</b>, or <b>Sipsong Panna</b>, abbreviated to <b>Banna</b> (full name: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tai_L%C3%BC" title="Tai Lü">Tai Lü</a>: <span style="font-family:'Dai Banna SIL', 'Noto Sans New Tai Lue', 'Microsoft New Tai Lue';">ᦈᦹᧈᦈᦹᧈᦋᦵᦲᧁᦘᦱᦉᦱᦑᦺ᧑᧒ᦗᧃᦓᦱ</span>; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">西双版纳傣族自治州</span>; <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">สิบสองปันนา</span>; <a href="/wiki/Lao_language" title="Lao language">Lao</a>: <span lang="lo">ສິບສອງພັນນາ</span>; <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a>: စစ်ဆောင်ပန်းနား) is a <a href="/wiki/Lu_people" title="Lu people">Tai Lü</a> <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_prefectures_of_China" title="Autonomous prefectures of China">autonomous prefecture</a> in the extreme south of <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> Province, China. The prefectural seat is <a href="/wiki/Jinghong" title="Jinghong">Jinghong</a>, the largest settlement in the area and one that straddles the <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong River</a>, called the Lancang River in Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hyde_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hyde-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sipsongpanna (cognate to the <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> <span lang="th">สิบสองปันนา</span>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><i>Sipsong Panna</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Tai_L%C3%BC_language" title="Tai Lü language">Tai Lü</a> compound consisting of <i>sipsong</i> "twelve", <i>pan</i> "township" and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B8%99%E0%B8%B2" title="wikt:นา"><i>na</i></a> "rice paddy". The name refers to the traditional division of the <i><a href="/wiki/Mueang" title="Mueang">mueang</a></i> into twelve districts that were called <i>panna</i> (literally "township rice-fields")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The etymology is parallel to the autonomous Tai-speaking region in <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a> from 1890 to 1945 called <a href="/wiki/Sip_Song_Chau_Tai" title="Sip Song Chau Tai">Sip Song Chau Tai</a> meaning "twelve Tai cantons".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the chaos of the <a href="/wiki/Xinhai_Revolution" title="Xinhai Revolution">Xinhai Revolution</a> that overthrew the Qing government in 1911 in favor of a Chinese republican government, a local official, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B9%80%E0%B8%88%E0%B9%89%E0%B8%B2" title="wikt:เจ้า">Chao</a> Meeng Jie, staged a rebellion against the Qing remnant officials. The <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> provincial government of the newly established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_(1912-1949)" title="Republic of China (1912-1949)">Republic of China</a> sent troops in 1913 to oust the rebels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:137–138</sup> Ke Shuxun remained in Xishuangbanna to govern with his "13 Principles of Governing the Frontier", which emphasized equality between Han and Dai in areas such as land ownership and taxation, allowed intermarriage between the ethnic groups and promoted education in secular and technical subjects, rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burmese</a>-based monastic education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:156–158</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a> (1931-1945) saw the heavy bombardment of Xishuangbanna by Japanese troops and a simultaneous influx of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Taiism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pan-Taiism (page does not exist)">Pan-Taiist</a> propaganda from Japan's ally, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Thailand_(1932%E2%80%931973)" title="History of Thailand (1932–1973)">Thailand</a>. According to Hsieh,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:173–174</sup> this reduced the appeal of a broad pan-<a href="/wiki/Tai_peoples" title="Tai peoples">Tai</a> identity among the Dai Lue.</p>
<p>During the final phase of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War" title="Chinese Civil War">Chinese Civil War</a>, many remnants of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guomindang" title="Guomindang">Guomindang</a> fled from <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communists</a> forces into Burma's <a href="/wiki/Shan_State" title="Shan State">Shan State</a> from Xishuangbanna. The new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC) sent various non-military expeditions to Xishuangbanna from 1949 to provide services such as schools and hospitals to replace those from Christian western missionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:193–194</sup></p>
<p>The Communists took control of the prefecture from Guomindang loyalists in 1952. On January 23, 1953, the PRC established the Xishuangbanna Dai Autonomous Region and ended the <a href="/wiki/Tusi" title="Tusi">native-chieftain system</a>. That year, the People's Congress of Xishuangbanna created the <a href="/wiki/New_Tai_Lue_alphabet" title="New Tai Lue alphabet">New Dai Lue alphabet</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Tai_Tham_alphabet" title="Tai Tham alphabet">Tai Tham alphabet</a>, for printing material in the <a href="/wiki/Tai_L%C3%BC_language" title="Tai Lü language">Tai Lü language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:243–244</sup></p>
<p>Xishuangbanna was made an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_prefecture" title="Autonomous prefecture">autonomous prefecture</a> in 1955, but lost some territory on the creation of the autonomous counties of <a href="/wiki/Jingdong_Yi_Autonomous_County" title="Jingdong Yi Autonomous County">Jingdong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jiangcheng_Hani_and_Yi_Autonomous_County" title="Jiangcheng Hani and Yi Autonomous County">Jiangcheng</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:40</sup> <a href="/wiki/Land_reform" title="Land reform">Land reform</a> started in earnest in January 1956, destroying the power of the village headmen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:188–189, 211</sup> State-owned rubber plantations accounted for most of the region's wealth during the early PRC period.</p>
<p>Xishuangbanna also received an influx of educated youth during the <a href="/wiki/Down_to_the_Countryside_Movement" title="Down to the Countryside Movement">Down to the Countryside Movement</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a> (1966-1976). During this period Buddhist temples in Xishuangbanna were used as barns, only being restored to their original purpose in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:239</sup></p>
<p>In 1987, the Xishuangbanna government promulgated the <i>Law of the Xishuangbanna Dai Nationality Autonomous Prefecture for Self-government</i> to bring local laws into line with the national <i>Law of the People's Republic of China for Regional National Autonomy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:68</sup></p>
<p>Shao Cunxin (<span lang="zh">召存信</span>, 1922-2015), former head of the Chieftain's outer council (1944-1950) and chief of Meng Peng (1938-1950), was the chief of the autonomous prefecture from 1955 to 1992.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prefecture has an area of 19,700 km<sup>2</sup> (7,600 sq mi). Xishuangbanna is the home of the <a href="/wiki/Dai_people" title="Dai people">Dai people</a>. The region sits at a lower altitude than most of Yunnan, and borders closely on <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a>. It is fast becoming a sought after tourist destination.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg/250px-Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg/375px-Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg/500px-Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Passiflora xishuangbannaensis</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Xishuangbanna harbors much of the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> of Yunnan Province, which harbors much of the biodiversity of China. Its tropical climate and its remoteness until recent times accounts for this. In addition to an abundance of plants, Xishuangbanna is home to the last few <a href="/wiki/Asian_elephant" title="Asian elephant">Asian elephants</a> still in China; the species roamed over a large part of the country even as late as a few hundred years ago. The elephants are protected in a reserve, but the plant diversity is threatened by, and has for five decades been threatened by, the proliferation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> which completely destroy the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> and replace it with a <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> of trees originally from <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Passiflora_xishuangbannaensis" title="Passiflora xishuangbannaensis">Passiflora xishuangbannaensis</a></i> is a recently discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passionflower" title="Passionflower">passionflower</a> species that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> to Xishuangbanna.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With censuses in the year 2000 Xishuangbanna had 993,397 inhabitants with a population density of 50.43 inhabitants per km². According to the 2000 national census, <a href="/wiki/Dai_people" title="Dai people">Dai people</a> make up the plurality at 29.89%, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_(people)" title="Han (people)">Han people</a> coming in at a close second at 29.11%. At the time of the 1977 census, however, Han people made up the largest single ethnic group in Xishuangbanna, making up 36.53% of a population of 627,089, while the Dai made up 33.15%, and others 30.32%. The Xishuangbanna government has strived to maintain this ethnic balance of around 33% of each group: Han, Dai, and other; this policy is known as "the plan of three-three-three" (<span lang="zh-hans">三三三计划</span>, <i>sān-sān-sān jìhuà</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62–64</sup></p>
<p>Before the increasing social mobility of the 1940s, inhabitants of Xishuangbanna called each other "basin people" (<span lang="zh-hant">壩區民族</span>, <i>bàqū mínzú</i>) or "mountain people" (<span lang="zh-hant">山區民族</span>, <i>shānqū mínzú</i>) in reference to the groups' stereotyped location. The Han and Dai lived mostly around the mountains and played a socially dominant role, while the non-Dai ethnic minorities lived in the basins and were politically disenfranchised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44, 52</sup> The Dai used to be called the <i>Baiyi</i> (<span lang="zh">摆夷</span>), and until a 1936 Guomindang reform, the <i>bai</i> part was written with the dog radical (<span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%8A%AD" title="wikt:犭">犭</a></span>). The PRC government decided that regardless of radical, the term <i>Baiyi</i> is pejorative, and adopted <i>Dai</i> (<span lang="zh">傣</span>) instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:257</sup> Historically, some ethnic minorities adapted some Dai characteristics in order to alleviate discrimination and increase their social status, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulang_people" title="Bulang people">Bulang people</a> adopting the <a href="/wiki/Sarong" title="Sarong">sarong</a> dress, practicing <a href="/wiki/Matrilocal_residence" title="Matrilocal residence">matrilocal residence</a>, and learning the <a href="/wiki/Tai_Tham_alphabet" title="Tai Tham alphabet">Tai Tham alphabet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54</sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jinghong" title="Jinghong">Jinghong</a> City and <a href="/wiki/Menghai_County" title="Menghai County">Menghai County</a>, the two major <a href="/wiki/Hani_people" title="Hani people">Hani</a> subgroups are Jiuwei 鸠为 and Jizuo 吉坐.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Jizuo 吉坐 are the largest Hani ethnic subgroup in Jinghong.</p>
<p>The Jiuwei claim to have migrated from Honghe and Mojiang. The Jiuwei live in various villages in Jinghong, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Mengbozhai 勐波寨, Menghan Town 勐罕寨, Jinghong City</li>
<li>Agupu 阿古普 (also called Manwoke 曼窝科) in Leiwu 类吴, Mengsong Township 勐宋, Jinghong City</li>
<li>Napazhai 那帕寨 in Damenglong 大勐笼, Jinghong City</li>
<li>Baiya village 拜牙村 in Menghun 勐混, Menghai County (The <a href="/wiki/Akeu_language" title="Akeu language">Ake</a> 阿克 subgroup live in Lougu 楼固村, located in Menghun 勐混 as well.)</li>
<li>Babingzhai 坝丙寨, Xidingshan 西定山, Menghai County</li>
</ul>
<p>There are also ethnic Hani that are locally called Aini 爱尼 living in 7 villages on Nanlin Mountain 南林山 of southwestern <a href="/wiki/Jinghong" title="Jinghong">Jinghong</a>, namely Manbage 曼八阁, Manjinglong 曼景龙, Manjingnan 曼景囡, Mangudu 曼固独, Manbaqi 曼把奇, Manbasan 曼巴伞, and Manjingmai 曼景卖.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Six_famous_tea_mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Six famous tea mountains (page does not exist)">Six famous tea mountains</a> region (Chinese: <span lang="zh">六大茶山</span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Liù Dà Chá Shān</span></i>) located in the prefecture produce some of the most highly regarded <a href="/wiki/Pu-erh_tea" title="Pu-erh tea">Pu-erh tea</a> in the 20th century.</p>
<p>Xishuangbanna is rich in nature, historical and cultural resources, noted for its folklore, rain forests, rare plants and wildlife. Its major tourist attractions include Menglun Tropical Botanical Garden, Manfeilong Pagodas (Tanuozhuanglong), Jingzhen Pavilion, Wild Elephant Gully, Dai people's village at Ganlanba.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The well-known traditional festival is the ethnic Dai's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water-Splashing_Festival" title="Water-Splashing Festival">Water-Splashing Festival</a>. It lasts for three days from April 13 to 15. Besides the water festival event it also consists of some other events such as <a href="/wiki/Dragon_boat" title="Dragon boat">Dragon boat</a> races, firing of indigenous missiles, flying Kongming Lamps.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xishuangbanna_Dai_Autonomous_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Xishuangbanna_Gasa_Airport" title="Xishuangbanna Gasa Airport">Xishuangbanna Gasa Airport</a> (formerly "Jinghong International Airport") in 1990, traveling to Xishuangbanna by air has become more popular and convenient and there are daily flights connecting Xishuangbanna with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kunming_City" title="Kunming City">Kunming City</a>. The area also has air connections with <a href="/wiki/Dali_City" title="Dali City">Dali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>. The Xishuangbanna Airport is 6 km (3.7 mi) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jinghong_City" title="Jinghong City">Jinghong City</a>.</p>
<p>There are also bus routes to places all over <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> and neighboring provinces. It is 590 kilometers from Kunming to Jinghong. Long-distances buses depart from Kunming South Station and arrive at Jinghong Bus Station, costing CNY 210-250, which is about 8-10hours' duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xishuangbanna_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Xishuangbanna-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2010, plans were announced for a 530 km (330 mi) railway linking Xishuangbanna to <a href="/wiki/Vientiane" title="Vientiane">Vientiane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> connections to <a href="/wiki/State_Railway_of_Thailand" title="State Railway of Thailand">Thailand</a> are also possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
