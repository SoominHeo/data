<p>A <b>stack</b> or <b>sea stack</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landform</a> consisting of a steep and often vertical column or columns of rock in the sea near a coast, formed by <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion#Wave_action" title="Coastal erosion">wave erosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Stacks are formed over time by wind and water, processes of coastal geomorphology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They are formed when part of a <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">headland</a> is <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">eroded</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_action" title="Hydraulic action">hydraulic action</a>, which is the force of the sea or water crashing against the rock. The force of the water weakens cracks in the headland, causing them to later collapse, forming free-standing stacks and even a small island. Without the constant presence of water, stacks also form when a <a href="/wiki/Natural_arch" title="Natural arch">natural arch</a> collapses under <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>, due to <a href="/wiki/Subaerial" title="Subaerial">sub-aerial</a> processes like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_erosion" title="Wind erosion">wind erosion</a>. Erosion causes the arch to collapse, leaving the pillar of hard rock standing away from the coast—the stack. Eventually, erosion will cause the stack to collapse, leaving a <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Stump" title="Stump">stump</a>. Stacks can provide important nesting locations for <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>, and many are popular for <a href="/wiki/Rock_climbing" title="Rock climbing">rock climbing</a>.</p>

<p>Isolated steep-sided, rocky oceanic islets, typically of volcanic origin, are also loosely called "stacks" or "volcanic stacks".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stack_(geology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stacks typically form in horizontally-bedded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rocks" title="Sedimentary rocks">sedimentary</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_rocks" title="Volcanic rocks">volcanic rocks</a>, particularly on <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> cliffs. These rock types' medium hardness means medium resistance to abrasive and attritive <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. A more resistant layer may form a <a href="/wiki/Caprock" title="Caprock">capstone</a>. (Cliffs with weaker rock—such as <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>—tend to <a href="/wiki/Slump_(geology)" title="Slump (geology)">slump</a> and erode too quickly to form stacks, while harder rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> erode in different ways.)</p>
<p>The formation process usually begins when the sea attacks small cracks in a headland and opens them. The cracks then gradually get larger and turn into a small cave. When the cave wears through the headland, an arch forms. Further erosion causes the arch to collapse, leaving the pillar of hard rock standing away from the coast—the stack. Eventually, erosion will cause the stack to collapse, leaving a stump. This stump usually forms a small rock island, low enough for a high tide to submerge.</p>
<p>Some stacks last for a very long time—possibly because they do not get submerged easily.</p>
