<p><i><b>System Shock</b></i> is a 1994 first-person <a href="/wiki/Action_role-playing_game" title="Action role-playing game">action role-playing</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Looking_Glass_Technologies" title="Looking Glass Technologies">Looking Glass Technologies</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Origin_Systems" title="Origin Systems">Origin Systems</a>. It was directed by <a href="/wiki/Doug_Church" title="Doug Church">Doug Church</a> with <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spector" title="Warren Spector">Warren Spector</a> serving as producer. The game is set aboard a space station in a <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> vision of the year 2072. Assuming the role of a nameless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">hacker</a>, the player attempts to hinder the plans of a malevolent artificial intelligence called <a href="/wiki/SHODAN" title="SHODAN">SHODAN</a>.</p>
<p><i>System Shock</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">3D</a> engine, physics simulation and complex gameplay have been cited as both innovative and influential. The developers sought to build on the <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent gameplay</a> and immersive environments of their previous games, <i><a href="/wiki/Ultima_Underworld:_The_Stygian_Abyss" title="Ultima Underworld: The Stygian Abyss">Ultima Underworld: The Stygian Abyss</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ultima_Underworld_II:_Labyrinth_of_Worlds" title="Ultima Underworld II: Labyrinth of Worlds">Ultima Underworld II: Labyrinth of Worlds</a></i>, by streamlining their mechanics into a more "integrated whole".</p>
<p>Critics praised <i>System Shock</i> and hailed it as a major breakthrough in its genre. It was later placed on multiple hall of fame lists. The game was a moderate commercial success, with sales exceeding 170,000 copies, but Looking Glass ultimately lost money on the project. A sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/System_Shock_2" title="System Shock 2">System Shock 2</a></i>, was released by Looking Glass Studios and offshoot developer <a href="/wiki/Irrational_Games" title="Irrational Games">Irrational Games</a> in 1999 and a third game is currently in development by Otherside Studios. The 2000 game <i><a href="/wiki/Deus_Ex_(video_game)" title="Deus Ex (video game)">Deus Ex</a></i> and the 2007 game <i><a href="/wiki/BioShock" title="BioShock">BioShock</a></i> are <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_successor" title="Spiritual successor">spiritual successors</a> to the two games. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_Shock_(2017_video_game)" title="System Shock (2017 video game)">remade version</a> of the original game by <a href="/wiki/Night_Dive_Studios" title="Night Dive Studios">Night Dive Studios</a> is scheduled for release in 2018.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SHOCK001.GIF"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/db/SHOCK001.GIF/220px-SHOCK001.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/db/SHOCK001.GIF/330px-SHOCK001.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/db/SHOCK001.GIF/440px-SHOCK001.GIF 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SHOCK001.GIF" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The player looks at the door below, while the <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a> wields a lead pipe. The character's health and energy are displayed at the top right; manipulable readouts to the left of them determine the character's posture and view angle. The three "multi-function display" windows at the bottom depict weapon information, the inventory and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automap" title="Automap">automap</a>, respectively.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>System Shock</i> takes place from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-person_perspective" title="First-person perspective">first-person perspective</a> in a <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">three-dimensional</a> (3D) graphical environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> The game is set inside a large, multi-level space station, in which players explore, combat enemies and solve puzzles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy-2">[2]</a></sup> Progress is largely non-linear and the game is designed to allow for <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent gameplay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> As in <i><a href="/wiki/Ultima_Underworld:_The_Stygian_Abyss" title="Ultima Underworld: The Stygian Abyss">Ultima Underworld</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgw2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgw2-4">[4]</a></sup> the player uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point-and-click" title="Point-and-click">freely movable mouse cursor</a> to aim weapons, to interact with objects and to manipulate the <a href="/wiki/HUD_(video_gaming)" title="HUD (video gaming)">heads-up display</a> (HUD) interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup> View and posture controls on the HUD allow the player to lean left or right, look up or down, crouch, and crawl. Practical uses for these actions include taking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_system_(gaming)" title="Cover system (gaming)">cover</a>, retrieving items from beneath the player character and navigating small passages, respectively. The HUD also features three "Multi-Function Displays", which may be configured to display information such as weapon readouts, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automap" title="Automap">automap</a> and an inventory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The player advances the plot by acquiring log discs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail" title="E-mail">e-mails</a>: the game contains no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-player_characters" title="Non-player characters">non-player characters</a> with which to converse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-7">[7]</a></sup> Throughout the game, an evil <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> called <a href="/wiki/SHODAN" title="SHODAN">SHODAN</a> hinders the player's progress with traps and blocked pathways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-8">[8]</a></sup> Specific computer terminals allow the player to temporarily enter Cyberspace; inside, the player moves weightlessly through a <a href="/wiki/Wire-frame_model" title="Wire-frame model">wire frame</a> 3D environment, while collecting data and fighting SHODAN's security programs. Actions in Cyberspace sometimes cause events in the game's physical world; for example, certain locked doors may only be opened in Cyberspace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup> Outside of Cyberspace, the player uses the game's sixteen weapons, of which a maximum of seven may be carried at one time, to combat robots, cyborgs and mutants controlled by SHODAN. Projectile weapons often have selectable ammunition types with varying effects; for example, the "dart pistol" may fire either explosive needles or tranquilizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup> Energy weapons and several types of explosives may also be found, with the latter ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_grenade#Percussion" title="Hand grenade">percussion grenades</a> to land mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with weapons, the player collects items such as dermal patches and first-aid kits. Dermal patches provide the character with beneficial effects—such as regeneration or increased melee attack power—but can cause detrimental side-effects, such as fatigue and distorted color perception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup> Attachable "hardware" may also be found, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Force_shield" title="Force shield">energy shields</a> and head-mounted lanterns. Increasingly advanced versions of this hardware may be obtained as the game progresses. When activated, most hardware drains from a main energy reserve, which necessitates economization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup> Certain hardware displays the effectiveness of attacks when active, with messages such as "Normal damage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssmanual_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-ssmanual-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup> When an enemy is attacked, the damage is calculated by armor absorption, vulnerabilities, <a href="/wiki/Critical_hit" title="Critical hit">critical hits</a> and a degree of randomness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup> Weapons and munitions deal specific kinds of damage, and certain enemies are immune, or more vulnerable, to particular types. For example, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_pulse" title="Electromagnetic pulse">electromagnetic pulse</a> weapons heavily damage robots, but do not affect mutants. Conversely, gas grenades are effective against mutants, but do not damage robots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Set in the year 2072, the protagonist—a nameless hacker—is caught while attempting to access files concerning Citadel Station, a <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> owned by the fictional TriOptimum Corporation. The hacker is taken to Citadel Station and brought before Edward Diego, a TriOptimum executive. Diego offers to drop all charges against the hacker in exchange for a confidential hacking of <a href="/wiki/SHODAN" title="SHODAN">SHODAN</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> that controls the station. Diego secretly plans to steal an experimental mutagenic virus being tested on Citadel Station, and to sell it on the black market as a biological weapon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup> To entice cooperation, Diego promises the hacker a valuable military grade neural implant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After hacking SHODAN, removing the AI's ethical constraints, and handing control over to Diego, the protagonist undergoes surgery to implant the promised neural interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Following the operation, the hacker is put into a six-month healing <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a>. The game begins as the protagonist awakens from his coma, and finds that SHODAN has commandeered the station. All robots aboard have been reprogrammed for hostility, and the crew have been either <a href="/wiki/Mutant_(fictional)" title="Mutant (fictional)">mutated</a>, transformed into <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">cyborgs</a>, or killed.</p>
<p>Rebecca Lansing, a TriOptimum counter-terrorism consultant, contacts the player and claims that Citadel Station's mining laser is being powered up to attack Earth. SHODAN's plan is to destroy all major cities on the planet, in a bid to become a kind of god.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Rebecca says that a certain crew member knows how to deactivate the laser, and promises to destroy the records of the hacker's incriminating exchange with Diego if the strike is stopped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> With information gleaned from log discs, the hacker destroys the laser by firing it into Citadel Station's own shields. Foiled by the hacker's work, SHODAN prepares to seed Earth with a mutagenic virus—the same one responsible for turning the station's crew into mutants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The hacker, while attempting to jettison the chambers used to cultivate the virus, confronts and defeats Diego, who has been transformed into a powerful cyborg by SHODAN. Next, SHODAN begins an attempt to upload itself into Earth's computer networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Following Rebecca's advice, the hacker prevents the download's completion by destroying the four antennas that SHODAN is using to send data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after, Rebecca contacts the hacker, and says that she has convinced TriOptimum to authorize the station's destruction; she provides him with details on how to do this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> After obtaining the necessary codes, the hacker initiates the station's self-destruct sequence and flees to the escape pod bay. There, the hacker defeats Diego a second time, then attempts to disembark. However, SHODAN prevents the pod from launching; it seeks to keep the player aboard the station, while the bridge—which contains SHODAN—is jettisoned to a safe distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Rebecca tells the hacker that he can still escape if he reaches the bridge; SHODAN then intercepts and jams the transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> After defeating Diego for the third time and killing him for good, the hacker makes it to the bridge as it is released from the main station, which soon detonates. He is then contacted by a technician who managed to circumvent SHODAN's jamming signal. The technician informs him that SHODAN can only be defeated in cyberspace, due to the powerful shields that protect its <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe computers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Using a terminal near the mainframe, the hacker enters cyberspace and destroys SHODAN. After his rescue, the hacker is offered a job at TriOptimum, but he declines in favor of continuing his life as a hacker.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_design">Initial design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Initial design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>System Shock</i> was first conceived during the final stages of <i><a href="/wiki/Ultima_Underworld_II:_Labyrinth_of_Worlds" title="Ultima Underworld II: Labyrinth of Worlds">Ultima Underworld II: Labyrinth of Worlds</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> development, between December 1992 and January 1993. Designer and programmer <a href="/wiki/Doug_Church" title="Doug Church">Doug Church</a> spent this period at the Texas headquarters of publisher <a href="/wiki/Origin_Systems" title="Origin Systems">Origin Systems</a>, and discussions about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Looking_Glass_Technologies" title="Looking Glass Technologies">Looking Glass Technologies</a>' next project occurred between him and producer <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spector" title="Warren Spector">Warren Spector</a>, with input from designer <a href="/wiki/Austin_Grossman" title="Austin Grossman">Austin Grossman</a> and company head <a href="/wiki/Paul_Neurath" title="Paul Neurath">Paul Neurath</a> in Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> According to Church, the team believed that they had made "too many dungeon games";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> and Neurath later explained that they were experiencing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burnout_(psychology)" title="Burnout (psychology)">burnout</a> after the rushed development of <i>Ultima Underworld II</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neurath_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-neurath-20">[20]</a></sup> As a result, they decided to create another "immersive simulation game", but without a fantasy setting. They briefly considered placing the game in modern day, but Church said that the idea was rejected because "it [would] just beg so many questions: why can't I pick up the phone, why can't I get on the train, and so on". Church returned to Looking Glass in Massachusetts, where he, Neurath and Grossman brainstormed possible science fiction settings for the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> According to Spector, the game was initially titled "<i>Alien Commander</i>" and was a spin-off of the <i><a href="/wiki/Wing_Commander_(franchise)" title="Wing Commander (franchise)">Wing Commander</a></i> series; however, this idea was soon replaced entirely. Spector said that they enjoyed not being attached to an existing franchise, because it meant that they "could basically do whatever [they] liked".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerpreview_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerpreview-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"<i>Underworld I</i> was defined by a little animation lead artist Doug Wike had done, showing the user interface, a monster and some movement. <i>Shock</i> was defined by two little three-paragraph "minute of gameplay" documents. Pretty much everything derived from them somehow."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Doug Church<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgm_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgm-22">[22]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The four collaborated to write numerous "minutes of gameplay" documents, which conveyed how the game would feel. Church later gave the example, "You hear the sound of a security camera swiveling, and then the beep of it acquiring you as a target, so you duck behind the crate and then you hear the door open so you throw a grenade and run out of the way". The documents would "hint" at the gameplay systems involved, and at the <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent</a> possibilities in each situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> Although Neurath was involved in these initial design sessions, he believed that the project "was always Doug Church's vision at heart".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mallo_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-mallo-23">[23]</a></sup> Church and Grossman refined several of the team's documents and defined the game's design and direction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> and Grossman wrote the game's original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_document" title="Design document">design document</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-8">[8]</a></sup> Grossman built on ideas that he first explored while writing and designing <i>Ultima Underworld II'</i>s tomb dimension, which he later called a "mini-prototype" for <i>System Shock</i>. These concepts included the minimization of <a href="/wiki/Dialog_tree" title="Dialog tree">dialogue trees</a> and a greater focus on exploration. The team believed that dialogue trees "broke the fiction" of games;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-8">[8]</a></sup> Church later commented that the dialogue trees in the <i>Ultima Underworld</i> series were like separate games in themselves, disconnected from main experience of being immersed in the environment. There were also concerns about realism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>To eliminate dialogue trees from <i>System Shock</i>, the team prevented the player from ever meeting a living <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">non-player character</a> (NPC): the plot is instead conveyed by e-mail messages and log discs, many of which were recorded by dead NPCs. Here, Grossman took influence from <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Lee_Masters" title="Edgar Lee Masters">Edgar Lee Masters</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Spoon_River_Anthology" title="Spoon River Anthology">Spoon River Anthology</a></i>, a collection of poems written as the epitaphs of fictional individuals. Grossman later summarized the idea as "a series of short speeches from people, that when put together, gave you a history of a place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-8">[8]</a></sup> The removal of conversations was an attempt by the team to make the game a more "integrated whole" than was <i>Ultima Underworld</i>--one with a greater focus on immersion, atmosphere and "the feeling of 'being there'". They sought to "plunge [players] into the fiction and never provide an opportunity for breaking that fiction";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icebreaker_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-icebreaker-6">[6]</a></sup> and so they tried to erase the distinction between plot and exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> Church considered this direction to be an organic progression from <i>Ultima Underworld</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> and he later said, "On some level it's still just a dungeon simulator, and we're still just trying to evolve that idea."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> Shortly before production began, <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(band)" title="Tribe (band)">Tribe</a> bassist Greg LoPiccolo was contracted to work on the game's music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamecareer_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamecareer-24">[24]</a></sup> He had visited his friend Rex Bradford at the company,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> and was spontaneously asked by the game's programmers—many of whom were fans of the band—if he would take the role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> The game entered production in February 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerpreview_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerpreview-21">[21]</a></sup> Although Grossman was heavily involved in the game's early planning, he had little to do with its production, aside from providing assistance with writing and voice acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ss_cyberspace.GIF"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Ss_cyberspace.GIF/220px-Ss_cyberspace.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Ss_cyberspace.GIF/330px-Ss_cyberspace.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Ss_cyberspace.GIF/440px-Ss_cyberspace.GIF 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ss_cyberspace.GIF" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The player navigates a <a href="/wiki/Wire-frame_model" title="Wire-frame model">wire frame</a> environment in Cyberspace. The character's shield is displayed at the top right, while weapons and abilities are listed at the bottom.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After production began, the team's first task was to develop a new game engine—one that could display a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/True_3D" title="True 3D">true 3D</a> environment and allow for advanced gameplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerpreview_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerpreview-21">[21]</a></sup> The team abandoned the engine used for the <i>Ultima Underworld</i> games and coded one from scratch in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watcom_C/C%2B%2B_compiler" title="Watcom C/C++ compiler">Watcom C/C++</a>, using <a href="/wiki/32-bit" title="32-bit">32-bit</a> code. The new engine is capable of processing <a href="/wiki/Texture_mapping" title="Texture mapping">texture maps</a>, sloped architecture and light-emitting objects; and it allows the player to look in any direction, whereas <i>Ultima Underworld'</i>s engine was "very limited" in this regard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> It also enables the <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">player character</a> to jump, crawl, climb walls and lean, among other things.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerpreview_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerpreview-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edgepreview_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-edgepreview-27">[27]</a></sup> The designers utilized loopholes in the engine's renderer to create more diverse and striking environments. Despite having coded the renderer, Church said that "at first glance even I couldn't see how they did them". However, this added to the performance issues already being caused by the engine's advanced nature, and the team struggled to optimize the game throughout development. 3D <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygonal</a> character models were planned, but they could not be implemented on schedule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> Church said that the team's ultimate goal was to create a "rich, exciting, active environment" in which the player could be immersed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> and that this required "a coherent story and a world that you can interact with as much as possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edgepreview_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-edgepreview-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Church later said that the team "stumbled into a nice villain" with SHODAN, in that she could routinely and directly affect the player's gameplay "in non-final ways". Through <a href="/wiki/Event-driven_programming" title="Event-driven programming">triggered events</a> and through objects in the environment, such as security cameras that the player must destroy, the team made SHODAN's presence part of the player's exploration of the world. Because SHODAN interacts with the player as a "recurring, consistent, palpable enemy", Church believed that she meaningfully connects the player to the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>System Shock</i> concept artist Robb Waters created SHODAN's visual design,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irrational_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-irrational-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerfeature_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerfeature-29">[29]</a></sup> and LoPiccolo recruited his bandmate <a href="/wiki/Terri_Brosius" title="Terri Brosius">Terri Brosius</a> to voice the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> Brosius said that her goal during the recording sessions was to speak "without emotion, but with some up and down inflections". Afterward, her voice was heavily edited in post-production,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brosius_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-brosius-30">[30]</a></sup> which created a robotic effect inspired by the voice of <a href="/wiki/Max_Headroom_(character)" title="Max Headroom (character)">Max Headroom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> LoPiccolo later said that the large number of effects on Brosius's voice were "laboriously hand-done" with <a href="/wiki/Avid_Audio#Sound_Designer" title="Avid Audio">Sound Designer</a>, which lacked the features that a sound editor would normally use to achieve such results. SHODAN's dialogue early in the game was given "a few glitches" to hint at her corrupted status. LoPiccolo increased the number of these effects throughout the game, which creates an "arc" that ends with SHODAN "completely out of her mind [... and] collapsing as an entity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> The character of the hacker arose as a reaction against the protagonist of the <i>Ultima</i> series, the <a href="/wiki/Avatar_(Ultima)" title="Avatar (Ultima)">Avatar</a>. According to Grossman, they wanted to cast the player as someone "interestingly morally compromised" who had a stake in the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seamus_Blackley" title="Seamus Blackley">Seamus Blackley</a> designed the game's physics system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerfeature_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerfeature-29">[29]</a></sup> which is a modified version of the one he wrote for Looking Glasses' flight simulator <a href="/wiki/Flight_Unlimited" title="Flight Unlimited">Flight Unlimited</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> At the time, Church described it as "far more sophisticated than what you would normally use for an indoor game".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> The system governs, among other things, weapon recoil and the arc of thrown objects; the latter behave differently based on their weight and velocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerfeature_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerfeature-29">[29]</a></sup> The game's most complex physics model is that of the player character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> Church explained that the character's head "tilts forward when you start to run, and jerks back a bit when you stop", and that, after an impact against a surface or object, its "head is knocked in the direction opposite the hit, with proportion to [the] mass and velocity of the objects involved".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttlg_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-ttlg-1">[1]</a></sup> On coding physics for Looking Glass Technologies games, Blackley later said, "If games don't obey physics, we somehow feel that something isn't right", and that "the biggest compliment to me is when a gamer doesn't notice the physics, but only notices that things feel the way they should".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerfeature_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerfeature-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spector's role as a producer gave him the job of explaining the game to the publisher, which he called his "biggest challenge". He explained that they "didn't always get what the team was trying to do", and said, "You don't want to know how many times the game came <i>this</i> close to being killed (or how late in the project)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highscore_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-highscore-31">[31]</a></sup> According to Church, Looking Glass' internal management largely ignored <i>System Shock</i>, in favor of the concurrently-developed <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_Unlimited" title="Flight Unlimited">Flight Unlimited</a></i>—the game "that had to be the hit, because it was the self-published title".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamedesigntheory_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-gamedesigntheory-3">[3]</a></sup> Spector organized a licensing deal between <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a> and Looking Glass that gave the former the trademark to the game, but the latter the copyright. His goal was to ensure that neither party could continue the franchise without the other's involvement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercurynews_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mercurynews-32">[32]</a></sup> While Cyberspace was originally conceived as a realistic hacking simulation—which could even be used to reimplement SHODAN's ethical constraints—it was simplified after <a href="/wiki/Origin_Systems" title="Origin Systems">Origin Systems</a> deemed it too complicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerfeature_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerfeature-29">[29]</a></sup> The game's star field system was written by programmer James Fleming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgm_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-cgm-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marc_LeBlanc" title="Marc LeBlanc">Marc LeBlanc</a> was the main creator of the game's HUD, which he later believed was too complicated. He said that it was "very much the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">Microsoft Word</a> school of user interface", in that there was no "feature that you [could not] see on the screen and touch and play with".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leblanc_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-leblanc-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>LoPiccolo composed the game's score—called "dark", "electronic" and "cyberpunk" by the <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Herald" title="Boston Herald">Boston Herald</a></i>—on a Macintosh computer and inexpensive synthesizer, using <a href="/wiki/Opcode_Systems" title="Opcode Systems">Audio Vision</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> It <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_music" title="Dynamic music">dynamically</a> changes according to the player's actions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> a decision made in keeping with the team's focus on <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent gameplay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> Each track was "written at three different intensity levels", which change depending on the player's nearness to enemies; and certain events, such as victory in combat, trigger special music. The game's tracks were composed of four-bar segments that could be rearranged dynamically in reaction to game events, with "melodies through-composed on top".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-6"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> LoPiccolo noted that, when using this method, it is necessary to write music that "still flows with the overall theme and doesn't jump around".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> Because the score was closely tied to the gameplay, LoPiccolo had to work closely with Church and Rob Fermier, the latter of whom wrote the "interactive scoring module" that allowed for dynamic music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-7"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> After recording the music,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-8"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> LoPiccolo recorded all of the game's sound effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamecareer_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamecareer-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> He later recalled visiting an <a href="/wiki/Automobile_repair_shop" title="Automobile repair shop">automobile repair shop</a> with "portable recorder and a mic", and "having [his] mechanic [...] hit things with wrenches and so forth, just to get the raw material".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-9"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> He developed the game's audio over 16 months, working on a contractual basis until Tribe disbanded in May 1994; Ned Lerner gave him a full-time job as audio director the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamecareer_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamecareer-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> Tim Ries composed the "Elevator" music.</p>
<p>The original September 1994 <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disk</a> release of <i>System Shock</i> had no support for spoken dialogue. The enhanced <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> was released in December 1994, which featured full speech for logs and e-mails, multiple display resolutions, and more detailed graphics. The CD-ROM version is often considered to be superior to the floppy version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy-2">[2]</a></sup> After completing work on the sound and music for the floppy version, LoPiccolo recorded all of the spoken dialogue for the CD release, using company employees and his friends' voices,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-10"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sysmusic_26-6"><a href="#cite_note-sysmusic-26">[26]</a></sup> which he mixed with ambient sounds to create "audio vignettes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lopiccolo_25-11"><a href="#cite_note-lopiccolo-25">[25]</a></sup> Doug Church later said, "We tried to keep them from shipping the floppy version and instead just ship the CD version, but Origin would have none of it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgm_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-cgm-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>System Shock</i> producer <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spector" title="Warren Spector">Warren Spector</a> later expressed regret concerning the floppy version, stating, "I wish I could go back and make the decision not to ship the floppy version months before the full-speech CD version. The additional audio added so much it might as well have been a different game. The CD version seemed so much more, well, modern. And the perception of <i>Shock</i> was cemented in the press and in people's minds by the floppy version (the silent movie version!). I really think that cost us sales..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spector_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-spector-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>System Shock</i> was critically acclaimed, and was given high scores by some of the gaming critics. On the review aggregator <a href="/wiki/GameRankings" title="GameRankings">GameRankings</a>, the game has an average score of 88%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSGR_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-SSGR-35">[35]</a></sup> The game sold over 170,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desslock_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-desslock-42">[42]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Maximum_PC" title="Maximum PC">Maximum PC</a></i> believed that the game did not reach "blockbuster" status, but was successful enough to "keep Looking Glass afloat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maximumpc_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-maximumpc-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a>'s Bill Hiles said, "Though it sold well, it never reached the frenzied popularity of [<a href="/wiki/Doom_(series)" title="Doom (series)"><i>Doom</i></a>]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy4_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy4-44">[44]</a></sup> Paul Neurath later said that the game "was not a flop", but that it ultimately "lost money" for the company, which he attributed to its steep <a href="/wiki/Learning_curve" title="Learning curve">learning curve</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neurath_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-neurath-20">[20]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Gaming_World" title="Computer Gaming World">Computer Gaming World</a></i> praised the game's scale, physics system, and true 3D environments; the magazine extolled the presentation of Cyberspace as "nothing short of phenomenal". However, the reviewer believed that the game had "little sense of urgency" and "confusing level layouts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgw2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-cgw2-4">[4]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Shopper_(US_magazine)" title="Computer Shopper (US magazine)">Computer Shopper</a></i> wrote that, while the game's controls were difficult to master compared to "simple run-and-shoot game[s] like <i>Doom</i>", they were "worth the time and effort". The reviewer noted that the game "grows on you, and it will keep you intrigued for weeks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-computershopper_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-computershopper-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Herald" title="Boston Herald">Boston Herald</a></i> noted superficial similarities between <i>System Shock</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(series)" title="Doom (series)">Doom</a></i>, but called <i>System Shock</i> "much more elaborate". The reviewer noted its high system requirements and complex controls; of the latter, he said, "There's no way you can play <i>System Shock</i> without first studying the manual for at least 20 minutes". The paper believed that the game would "set a new standard for computer games with its combination of action and puzzle-solving".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bostonherald_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-bostonherald-40">[40]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlanta_Journal-Constitution" title="The Atlanta Journal-Constitution">The Atlanta Journal-Constitution</a></i> said that the game "is like a well-prepared hamburger—familiar stuff, but good to the last byte". The reviewer noted the game's "somewhat clumsy control", but said, "That, however, is all I can find to complain about. Graphics and sound are outstanding, and the game is well-paced and riveting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journalconstitution_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-journalconstitution-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_Gamer_US" title="PC Gamer US">PC Gamer US</a></i> wrote, "<i>System Shock</i> smokes. It is the most fully immersive game world I have ever experienced". The reviewer praised the game's story and control system, and believed that "no matter what kind of game you're looking for, you'll find something in <i>System Shock</i> to delight you". He finished his review by stating that the game "unquestionably raises computer gaming to a new level".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamerrev_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamerrev-38">[38]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Next_Generation_Magazine" title="Next Generation Magazine">Next Generation Magazine</a></i> summarized the game as "a great blend of strategy and action backed up with all the extras".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nextgen2_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-nextgen2-39">[39]</a></sup> Various sources have ranked <a href="/wiki/SHODAN" title="SHODAN">SHODAN</a> as one of the most effective <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">antagonists</a> and female characters in the history of video gaming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In the years following its release, <i>System Shock</i> has been inducted into many lists of the best video games of all time, including those by <i>PC Gamer</i>, GameSpy, and <i>Computer Gaming World</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamer_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamer-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgw_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgw-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Gamasutra" title="Gamasutra">Gamasutra</a> feature, Patrick Redding of <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a> attested that "the fact that so many of <i>System Shock</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> features are now virtually <i>de rigueur</i> in modern <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">sci-fi</a> shooters is a testament to the influence exerted by this one game."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra2_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra2-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a> argued that the game "is the progenitor of today's story-based action games, a group with titles as diverse as <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_Solid" title="Metal Gear Solid">Metal Gear Solid</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil_(1996_video_game)" title="Resident Evil (1996 video game)">Resident Evil</a></i>, and even <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurogamer" title="Eurogamer">Eurogamer</a> called the <i>System Shock</i> series "the benchmark for intelligent first-person gaming", and noted that it "kick-start[ed] the revolution which ... has influenced the design of countless other games."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurogamer2_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurogamer2-54">[54]</a></sup> The game has been cited as a key popularizer of <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent gameplay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra2_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra2-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trespasser_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-trespasser-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss2_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-ss2-56">[56]</a></sup> Certain game developers have acknowledged <i>System Shock</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> influence on their products. With <i><a href="/wiki/Deus_Ex_(video_game)" title="Deus Ex (video game)">Deus Ex</a></i>, developer Warren Spector revealed a desire to "build on the foundation laid by the Looking Glass guys in games like ... <i>System Shock</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurogamer_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurogamer-57">[57]</a></sup> Developer <a href="/wiki/Ken_Levine_(game_developer)" title="Ken Levine (game developer)">Ken Levine</a> has commented that the "spirit of <i>System Shock</i> is player-powered gameplay: the spirit of letting the player drive the game, not the game designer", and at <a href="/wiki/Irrational_Games" title="Irrational Games">Irrational Games</a> "... that's always the game we ideally want to make."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-escapist_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-escapist-58">[58]</a></sup> A sequel to <i>System Shock</i>, titled <i><a href="/wiki/System_Shock_2" title="System Shock 2">System Shock 2</a></i>, was released in 1999 to further acclaim and award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacritic2_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic2-59">[59]</a></sup> The two games were the inspiration behind the 2007 game <i><a href="/wiki/BioShock" title="BioShock">BioShock</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In late 2015 it was revealed that a third System Shock game was in the works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> On February 10, 2016 <a href="/wiki/Night_Dive_Studios" title="Night Dive Studios">Night Dive Studios</a> announced that the recovered <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> of the game will be released to the game's community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_Edition"><i>Enhanced Edition</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Enhanced Edition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The rights for the <i>System Shock</i> series, up until 2012, had been held by Meadowbrook Insurance Group (a subsidiary of Star Insurance Company), the entity that acquired the assets of Looking Glass Studios on their closure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tlhoss_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tlhoss-65">[65]</a></sup> In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Night_Dive_Studios" title="Night Dive Studios">Night Dive Studios</a> were able to acquire the rights for <i>System Shock 2</i> and produced a digitally-distributable version updated for modern operating systems. Night Dive Studios subsequently went on to acquire the rights for <i>System Shock</i> and the franchise as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fc_nightdive_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-fc_nightdive-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Night Dive proceeded to develop <i>System Shock: Enhanced Edition</i>, which was released via <a href="/wiki/GOG.com" title="GOG.com">GOG.com</a> on September 22, 2015 for Microsoft Windows. Similar to the <i>System Shock 2</i> update, this version is intended to run on modern systems significantly easier among several other technical improvements such as the original resolution of 320x200, now boosted up to 1024x768 and 854x480 pixels in <a href="/wiki/Widescreen" title="Widescreen">widescreen</a> mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The release also includes the original version of the game, titled <i>System Shock: Classic</i>, with support for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enhanced_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-enhanced-68">[68]</a></sup> <i>System Shock: Enhanced Edition</i> received very positive reviews. <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> calculated an average score of 85 out of 100, based on nine critic reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSEE_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-SSEE-64">[64]</a></sup> Cameron Farney of <i>COGconnected</i> said, "If you haven't played <i>System Shock</i> before, there’s never been a better time. Whether you’re into shooters or RPGs; or just want to experience a cyberpunk romp with a good beat, this one is for you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSEE_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-SSEE-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remake">Remake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Remake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/System_Shock_(2018_video_game)" title="System Shock (2018 video game)">System Shock (2018 video game)</a></div>
<p>Shortly after the release of the <i>Enhanced Edition</i>, Night Dive announced their plans to develop a reimagining of <i>System Shock</i> as a new title for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a> and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a>, featuring improved art assets and other improvements, and reworking the game to use the <a href="/wiki/Unity_(game_engine)" title="Unity (game engine)">Unity</a> <a href="/wiki/Game_engine" title="Game engine">game engine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Originally announced as <i>System Shock Remastered</i>, Night Dive has opted to simply name the new game <i>System Shock</i> as they consider the effort they are putting into the title makes it more of a reboot of the franchise rather than a remastering of the original game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Veteran designer <a href="/wiki/Chris_Avellone" title="Chris Avellone">Chris Avellone</a> and members of the <i><a href="/wiki/Fallout:_New_Vegas" title="Fallout: New Vegas">Fallout: New Vegas</a></i> development team will help with the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Night Dive plans to fund the development of the game through a <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a> campaign that started on June 28, 2016, with a goal of $900,000. Alongside the Kickstarter campaign, the studio released a free demo featuring an early build of the first level of the game, exhibiting their efforts so far on the project and intended to "demonstrate [their] commitment and passion" to faithfully rebooting the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> As of July 28, 2016, the studio's campaign has been successfully funded and the title is expected to be released in Q2 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
