<p>For some, <b>systems thinking</b> is the cognitive process of studying and understanding <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">systems</a> of every kind. For others, the focus is on social organizations in particular.</p>
<p>A system may be defined in general as a set of interrelated or interacting elements. This definition accommodates both passive structures (e.g. a necklace, or the Dewey Decimal System) and active structures. However, most system theorists focus on activity systems in which structures/components interact in behaviors/processes.</p>
<p>In biology, a living organism is seen as a set of organs, muscles etc. that interact in processes to sustain the organism. Each cell is seen as a collection of organelles that interact in processes to sustain both the cell and the wider organism. In business, the organization is seen as a set of people and machines that interact in processes to achieve business goals.</p>
<p>The term general system theory was coined by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy" title="Ludwig von Bertalanffy">Ludwig von Bertalanffy</a> in the middle of the 20th century. General systems theory is about broadly applicable concepts and principles, as opposed to concepts and principles applicable to one domain of knowledge. Bertalanffy's ideas were picked by others including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Ross_Ashby" title="William Ross Ashby">Ross Ashby</a> and Anatol Rapoport, working in the fields of mathematics, psychology, biology, game theory and social network analysis.</p>
<p>An early focus of general system theory was on homeostatic or self-regulating systems that maintain themselves in a consistent or viable state through input/output feedback loops.</p>
<p>Sociological systems thinking started much earlier, in the 19th century. It may be now seen as the specialism of general system theory focused on social and business systems. Such systems are often described in terms of inputs, transformations and outputs, and feedback loops that operate (in the light of goals, stakeholders, and external influences) to make an organization healthy or unhealthy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_overview">History and overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Systems thinking has roots in a diverse range of sources from <a href="/wiki/Jan_Smuts" title="Jan Smuts">Jan Smuts</a>' <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holism</a> in the 1920s, to the general <a href="/wiki/Systems_theory" title="Systems theory">systems theory</a> that was advanced by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy" title="Ludwig von Bertalanffy">Ludwig von Bertalanffy</a> in the 1940s and <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a> advanced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Ross_Ashby" title="William Ross Ashby">Ross Ashby</a> in the 1950s. The field was further developed by <a href="/wiki/Jay_Wright_Forrester" title="Jay Wright Forrester">Jay Forrester</a> and members of the Society for Organizational Learning at MIT, which culminated in the popular book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifth_Discipline" title="The Fifth Discipline">The Fifth Discipline</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Senge" title="Peter Senge">Peter Senge</a>, which defined systems thinking as the capstone for true organizational learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Derek_Cabrera" title="Derek Cabrera">Derek Cabrera</a>'s self-published book <i>Systems Thinking Made Simple</i> claimed that systems thinking itself is the emergent property of <a href="/wiki/Complex_adaptive_system" title="Complex adaptive system">complex adaptive system</a> behavior that results from <a href="/wiki/DSRP" title="DSRP">four simple rules of thought</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CabreraCabrera_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CabreraCabrera-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Systems thinking has been defined as an approach to <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a> that attempts to balance holistic thinking and reductionistic thinking. By taking the overall system as well as its parts into account systems thinking is designed to avoid potentially contributing to further development of <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">unintended consequences</a>. There are many methods and approaches to systems thinking. For example, the Waters Foundation presents systems thinking as a set of habits or practices within a framework that is based on the belief that the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/component" title="wikt:component">component</a> parts of a system can best be understood in the context of relationships with each other and with other systems, rather than in isolation; and that systems thinking focuses on cyclical rather than linear cause and effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other models characterize systems thinking differently. Recent scholars, however, are focused on the "patterns that connect" this diversity or <a href="/wiki/Epistemological_pluralism" title="Epistemological pluralism">pluralism</a> of methods and approaches.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Systems_science" title="Systems science">systems science</a>, it is argued that the only way to fully understand why a problem or element occurs and persists is to understand the parts in relation to the whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Standing in contrast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descartes" title="Descartes">Descartes</a>'s scientific <a href="/wiki/Reductionism" title="Reductionism">reductionism</a>, it proposes to view systems in a <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> manner. Consistent with <a href="/wiki/Systems_philosophy" title="Systems philosophy">systems philosophy</a>, systems thinking concerns an understanding of a system by examining the linkages and interactions between the elements that compose the entire system.</p>
<p>Systems science thinking attempts to illustrate how small catalytic events that are separated by distance and time can be the cause of significant changes in <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex systems</a>. Acknowledging that an improvement in one area of a system can adversely affect another area of the system, it promotes <a href="/wiki/Organizational_communication" title="Organizational communication">organizational communication</a> at all levels to avoid the <a href="/wiki/Information_silo" title="Information silo">silo effect</a>. Systems thinking techniques may be used to study any kind of system – <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">social</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineered</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">conceptual</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_concept_of_a_system">The concept of a system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The concept of a system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several ways to think of and define a system include:</p>
<ul>
<li>a system is composed of parts</li>
<li>a system is other than the sum of its parts</li>
<li>all the parts of a system must be related (directly or indirectly), else there are really two or more distinct systems</li>
<li>a system is encapsulated (has a boundary)</li>
<li>a system can be nested inside another system</li>
<li>a system can overlap with another system</li>
<li>a system is bounded in time, but may be intermittently operational</li>
<li>a system is bounded in space, though the parts are not necessarily co-located</li>
<li>a system receives input from, and sends output into, the wider environment</li>
<li>a system consists of processes that transform inputs into outputs</li>
<li>a system is autonomous in fulfilling its purpose (a car is not a system. A car with a driver is a system)</li>
</ul>
<p>Systems science thinkers consider that:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> is a dynamic and complex whole, interacting as a structured functional unit <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circuit" title="wikt:circuit">circuit</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a>, material and <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> flow among the different elements that compose a system (see <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)"><i>open system</i></a>).</li>
<li>A system is a community within an environment.</li>
<li>Energy, material, and information flow from and to the surrounding environment via semi-permeable membranes or boundaries that may include negotiable limits.</li>
<li>Systems are often composed of entities that seek equilibrium but can exhibit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patterns" title="Patterns">patterns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillation</a>, randomness, or chaos (see <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos theory</a>), or <a href="/wiki/Exponential_function" title="Exponential function">exponential</a> behavior (see Exponential Function).</li>
</ul>
<p>A holistic system is any set (group) of interdependent or <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">temporally</a> interacting parts. <i>Parts</i> are generally systems themselves and composed of other parts, just as systems are generally parts or <i><a href="/wiki/Holon_(philosophy)" title="Holon (philosophy)">holons</a></i> of other systems.</p>
<p>Systems science and the application of systems science thinking has been grouped into the following three categories based on the techniques or methodologies used to design, analyze, modify, or manage a system:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hard_systems" title="Hard systems">Hard systems</a> – involving <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulations</a>, hard systems approaches to system thinking often use <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> and the techniques of <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">operations research</a>/<a href="/wiki/Management_science" title="Management science">management science</a>. Hard systems approaches are useful for problems that can be justifiably quantified. However, hard systems cannot easily account for unquantifiable variables such as opinions, culture, or politics, etc.,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and may treat people as passive elements, rather than as beings with complex motivations.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_systems" title="Soft systems">Soft systems</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Soft_systems_methodology" title="Soft systems methodology">soft systems methodology</a>) – is a methodology for systems that cannot easily be quantified, especially systems that involve people holding multiple and conflicting frames of reference. Soft systems methods are useful for understanding motivations, viewpoints, and interactions, and for addressing qualitative as well as quantitative dimensions of problem situations. Soft systems approaches to system thinking may use foundation methodological work developed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Checkland" title="Peter Checkland">Peter Checkland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson_(systems_scientist)" title="Brian Wilson (systems scientist)">Brian Wilson</a>, and their colleagues at <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_University" title="Lancaster University">Lancaster University</a>. This approach may include <a href="/wiki/Morphological_analysis_(problem-solving)" title="Morphological analysis (problem-solving)">morphological analysis</a>, which is a complementary method for structuring and analyzing non-quantifiable problem complexes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_systems" title="Evolutionary systems">Evolutionary systems</a> – <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_H._B%C3%A1n%C3%A1thy" title="Béla H. Bánáthy">Béla H. Bánáthy</a> developed a methodology that is applicable to the design of complex social systems. This technique integrates critical systems inquiry with <a href="/wiki/Soft_systems_methodology" title="Soft systems methodology">soft systems methodologies</a>. Evolutionary systems, similar to <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamic systems</a> are understood as open, complex systems, but with the capacity to evolve over time. Bánáthy uniquely integrated the <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinarity" title="Interdisciplinarity">interdisciplinary</a> perspectives of systems research (including <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">cultural anthropology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_evolutionary_synthesis" title="Modern evolutionary synthesis">evolutionary theory</a>, and others.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_systems_approach">The systems approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The systems approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The systems thinking approach incorporates several tenets:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Interdependence" title="Interdependence">Interdependence</a> of objects and their attributes – independent elements can never constitute a system</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">Holism</a> – <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergent properties</a> not possible to detect by <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_analysis" title="Philosophical analysis">analysis</a> should be possible to define by a holistic approach</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Global_optimization" title="Global optimization">Goal seeking</a> – systemic interaction must result in some goal or final state</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Input/output" title="Input/output">Inputs</a> and outputs – in a <a href="/wiki/Closed_system" title="Closed system">closed system</a> inputs are determined once and constant; in an <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">open system</a> additional inputs are admitted from the environment</li>
<li>Transformation of inputs into outputs – the process by which the goals are obtained</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">Entropy</a> – the amount of disorder or randomness present in any system</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Regulator_(automatic_control)" title="Regulator (automatic control)">Regulation</a> – a method of <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> is necessary for the system to operate predictably</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">Hierarchy</a> – complex wholes are made up of smaller subsystems</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Differentiation_(sociology)" title="Differentiation (sociology)">Differentiation</a> – specialized units perform specialized functions</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Equifinality" title="Equifinality">Equifinality</a> – alternative ways of attaining the same objectives (convergence)</li>
<li>Multifinality – attaining alternative objectives from the same inputs (divergence)</li>
</ul>
<p>A treatise on systems thinking ought to address many issues including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Encapsulation of a system in space and/or in time</li>
<li>Active and passive systems (or structures)</li>
<li>Transformation by an activity system of inputs into outputs</li>
<li>Persistent and transient systems</li>
<li>Evolution, the effects of time passing, the life histories of systems and their parts.</li>
<li>Design and designers.</li>
</ul>
<p>Using the tenet of "multifinality", a supermarket could be considered a:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Profit making system" from the perspective of management and owners</li>
<li>"Distribution system" from the perspective of the suppliers</li>
<li>"Employment system" from the perspective of employees</li>
<li>"Materials supply system" from the perspective of customers</li>
<li>"Entertainment system" from the perspective of loiterers</li>
<li>"Social system" from the perspective of local residents</li>
<li>"Dating system" from the perspective of single customers</li>
</ul>
<p>As a result of such thinking, new insights may be gained into how the supermarket works, why it has problems, how it can be improved or how changes made to one component of the system affects other components.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hierarchical_frameworks">Hierarchical frameworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hierarchical frameworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Model_of_hierarchical_complexity" title="Model of hierarchical complexity">Model of hierarchical complexity</a></div>
<p>A number of hierarchical frameworks have been developed, but such hierarchical frameworks have also been criticized in an article in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_Science_(journal)" title="Philosophy of Science (journal)">Philosophy of Science</a></i>, which noted that they are plagued with conceptual deficiencies; the authors concluded that "the concept of discrete ontological levels does not succeed and has been used to motivate several problematic claims about the natural world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PotochnikMcGill_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PotochnikMcGill-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenneth_Boulding.27s_hierarchy_of_systems">Kenneth Boulding's hierarchy of systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kenneth Boulding's hierarchy of systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The following table shows <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_E._Boulding" title="Kenneth E. Boulding">Kenneth E. Boulding</a>'s 1956 hierarchy of systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:126</sup></p>
<pre>
 Level  Characteristic unit    Summary description
 (1)    framework              static systems
 (2)    clockwork              simple dynamic systems
 (3)    thermostat             control mechanisms and cybernetic systems
 (4)    cell                   open systems, or self-maintaining structures
 (5)    plant                  genetic/societal systems
 (6)    animal                 mobile, teleological systems with self-awareness
 (7)    human individual       animal systems with self-consciousness
 (8)    human society          social systems with self-consciousness
 (9)    transcendental idea    ultimate, absolutes, and inescapeable knowledges
</pre>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stephen_Haines.27_seven_levels_of_living_systems">Stephen Haines' seven levels of living systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Stephen Haines' seven levels of living systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

They begin with Earth as the largest living system and extend all the way down to cells, the smallest living system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haines2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haines2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

 (2)    Organ
 (3)    Individual organism
 (4)    Group, team or department

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Katz_.26_Kahn.27s_open_system_model">Katz &amp; Kahn's open system model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Systems_thinking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Katz &amp; Kahn's open system model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i>The Social Psychology of Organizations</i> (1966), psychologists <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Katz_(psychologist)" title="Daniel Katz (psychologist)">Daniel Katz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Kahn" title="Robert L. Kahn">Robert L. Kahn</a> developed their <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">open system</a> theory for the interpretation of organizational actions in terms of input, throughput, output, environment, and other characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Their open system model built upon Bertalanffy's general systems theory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and has been adapted into various business process models, such as the "five phases of systems thinking" framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haines2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haines2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<table class="wikitable">
<caption>Some definitions from Katz &amp; Kahn's open system model</caption>
<tr>
<td>Input</td>
<td>Resources are taken or received from the external environment</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Throughput</td>
<td>The process of conversion or transformation of resources within a system</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Output</td>
<td>The work of the system, exported back into the environment</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Feedback</td>
<td>A continuing source of information concerning the relationship with the external environment used to make the necessary changes in order to survive and grow</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Environment</td>
<td>All the elements outside the system that have the potential to affect all or part of the system</td>
</tr>
</table>
