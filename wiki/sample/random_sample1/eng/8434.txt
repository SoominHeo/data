<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Syagrius</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reame_di_Siagrio_(486).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1344" data-file-width="1440" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Reame_di_Siagrio_%28486%29.png/300px-Reame_di_Siagrio_%28486%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Reame_di_Siagrio_%28486%29.png/450px-Reame_di_Siagrio_%28486%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Reame_di_Siagrio_%28486%29.png/600px-Reame_di_Siagrio_%28486%29.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p><b>Syagrius</b> (430 – 486 or 487) was the last Roman military commander in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, whose defeat by king <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a> of the Franks is considered the end of Western Roman rule outside of Italy. He came to this position through inheritance, for his father was <a href="/wiki/Aegidius" title="Aegidius">Aegidius</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the last Roman <i><a href="/wiki/Magister_militum" title="Magister militum">magister militum</a> per <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gallias</a></i>. Syagrius preserved his father's <a href="/wiki/Rump_state" title="Rump state">rump state</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Somme_(river)" title="Somme (river)">Somme</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Loire" title="Loire">Loire</a> around <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">Soissons</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">collapse of central rule in the Western Empire</a>, a domain <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">Gregory of Tours</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Soissons" title="Kingdom of Soissons">"Kingdom" of Soissons</a>. Syagrius governed this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman" title="Gallo-Roman">Gallo-Roman</a> enclave from the death of his father in 464 until 486, when he was defeated in battle by <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a>.</p>
<p>Historians have mistrusted the title "rex Romanorum" that Gregory of Tours gave him, at least as early as <a href="/wiki/Godefroid_Kurth" title="Godefroid Kurth">Godefroid Kurth</a>, who dismissed it as a gross error in 1893. The common consensus has been to follow Kurth, based on the historical truism that Romans hated kingship from the days of the expulsion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarquin_the_Proud" title="Tarquin the Proud">Tarquin the Proud</a>; for example, Syagrius' article in the <i><a href="/wiki/Prosopography_of_the_Later_Roman_Empire" title="Prosopography of the Later Roman Empire">Prosopography of the Later Roman Empire</a></i> omits this title, preferring to refer to him as a "Roman ruler (in North Gaul)". However, S. Fanning has assembled a number of examples of <i>rex</i> being used in a neutral, if not favorable, context, and argues that "the phrase <i>Romanorum rex</i> is not peculiar to Gregory of Tours or to Frankish sources", and that Gregory's usage may indeed show "that they were, or were seen to be, claiming to be Roman emperors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_End_of_Roman_Gaul">The End of Roman Gaul</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syagrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The End of Roman Gaul">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="356" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg/220px-Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg/330px-Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Syagrius_brought_before_Clovis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The captured Syagrius is brought before Alaric II who orders him sent to Clovis I</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Despite being isolated from the surviving portions of the Roman Empire, Syagrius managed to maintain a degree of Roman authority in northern Gaul for twenty years, and his state outlived the end of the Western Empire itself, the last Emperors being overthrown or killed in 476 and 480. Syagrius managed to hold off the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Salian_Franks" title="Salian Franks">Salian Franks</a>, who were internally divided under kings including <a href="/wiki/Childeric_I" title="Childeric I">Childeric</a>. However, it is known that Childeric had previously come to the aid of the Gallo-Romans, joining a certain officer named <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_(Gallo-Roman)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul (Gallo-Roman) (page does not exist)">Paul</a> in operations against <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> who at one point seized <a href="/wiki/Angers" title="Angers">Angers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon Childeric's death in 481 his son Clovis succeeded him. While Childeric had seen no need to overthrow the last Roman foothold in the west, Clovis assembled an army, issued a challenge, and met Syagrius's forces. Few details are known of the subsequent clash, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Soissons_(486)" title="Battle of Soissons (486)">Battle of Soissons</a>, but Syagrius was decisively defeated and fled. His domain passed to the Franks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon" title="Edward Gibbon">Edward Gibbon</a> later wrote, "It would be ungenerous, without some more accurate knowledge of his strength and resources, to condemn the rapid flight of Syagrius, who escaped after the loss of a battle to the distant court of <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Toulouse was the capital of <a href="/wiki/Alaric_II" title="Alaric II">Alaric II</a>, king of the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>. Intimidated by the victorious Franks, the Visigoths imprisoned Syagrius, then surrendered him to Clovis. He died not long after, stabbed in secret according to <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">Gregory of Tours</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants">Descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syagrius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite the assassination of Syagrius, the family evidently prospered under Frankish rule. King <a href="/wiki/Guntram" title="Guntram">Guntram</a> sent a Count Syagrius on a diplomatic mission to the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> in 585. A descendant, Syagria, made a large donation of land to the monks of <a href="/wiki/Novalesa_Abbey" title="Novalesa Abbey">Novalesa Abbey</a> in 739. "The last known member of the Syagrii was an abbot of <a href="/wiki/Nantua" title="Nantua">Nantua</a> who was mentioned in 757."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
