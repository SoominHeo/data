<p>The <i><b>Seattle Post-Intelligencer</b></i> (popularly known as the <i><b>Seattle P-I</b></i>, the <i><b>Post-Intelligencer</b></i>, or simply the <i><b>P-I</b></i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Online_newspaper" title="Online newspaper">online newspaper</a> and former print <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> covering <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and the surrounding metropolitan area.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> was founded in 1863 as the weekly <i>Seattle Gazette</i>, and was later published daily in <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a> format. It was long one of the city's two daily newspapers, along with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seattle_Times" title="The Seattle Times">The Seattle Times</a></i>, until it became an online-only publication on March 18, 2009.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seattle_P-I_final_cover20090317.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="218" height="336" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/de/Seattle_P-I_final_cover20090317.jpg/160px-Seattle_P-I_final_cover20090317.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/de/Seattle_P-I_final_cover20090317.jpg 1.5x" width="160"/></a>

<p>J.R. Watson founded the <i>P-I</i>, Seattle's first newspaper, on December 10, 1863, as the <i>Seattle Gazette</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-history_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pi-history-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-historylink_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pi-historylink-2">[2]</a></sup> The paper failed after a few years and was renamed the <i>Weekly Intelligencer</i> in 1867 by the new owner, Sam Maxwell.</p>
<p>In 1878, after publishing the <i>Intelligencer</i> as a morning daily, <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_Hanford" title="Thaddeus Hanford">Thaddeus Hanford</a> bought the <i>Daily Intelligencer</i> for $8,000. Hanford also acquired the daily <i>Puget Sound Dispatch</i> and the weekly <i>Pacific Tribune</i> and folded both papers into the <i>Intelligencer</i>. In 1881, the <i>Intelligencer</i> merged with the <i>Seattle Post</i>. The names were combined to form the present-day name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-historylink_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pi-historylink-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1886, Indiana businessman <a href="/wiki/Leigh_S._J._Hunt" title="Leigh S. J. Hunt">Leigh S. J. Hunt</a> came to Seattle and purchased the <i>Seattle Post-Intelligencer</i>, which he owned and published until he was forced to sell in the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1893" title="Panic of 1893">Panic of 1893</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historylink8956_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Historylink8956-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Circulation stood at 31,000 in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-history_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pi-history-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1912, editor Eric W. Allen left the paper to found the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon" title="University of Oregon">University of Oregon</a> School of Journalism, which he ran until his death in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a> took over the paper in 1921, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst Corporation</a> owns the <i>P-I</i> to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-historylink_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-pi-historylink-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a> had a special relationship with the <i>P-I</i>.</p>
<p>In 1936, 35 P-I writers and members of <a href="/wiki/The_Newspaper_Guild" title="The Newspaper Guild">The Newspaper Guild</a> went on <a href="/wiki/1936_Seattle_Post-Intelligencer_strike" title="1936 Seattle Post-Intelligencer strike">three-month strike</a> against "arbitrary dismissals and assignment changes and other 'efficiency' moves by the newspaper." The <a href="/wiki/International_Brotherhood_of_Teamsters" title="International Brotherhood of Teamsters">International Brotherhood of Teamsters</a> joined the strike in solidarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Roger Simpson and William Ames co-wrote their book <i>Unionism or Hearst: the Seattle Post-Intelligencer Strike of 1936</i> on the topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Also in 1936, their son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Clarence_John_Boettiger" title="Clarence John Boettiger">Clarence John Boettiger</a> took over as publisher. He brought his wife <a href="/wiki/Anna_Roosevelt_Halsted" title="Anna Roosevelt Halsted">Anna</a>, the Roosevelts' daughter, to also work at the paper. Anna became editor of the women's page. Boettiger left Seattle to enter the U.S. Army in April 1943, while Anna stayed at the paper to help keep a liberal voice in the running of the paper. After Boettiger's absence, the paper increasingly turned conservative with Hearst's new acting publisher. Anna left Seattle in December 1943 to live in the White House with her youngest child, Johnny. This effectively ended the Roosevelt-Boettiger ties with the <i>P-I</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Love_in_Shadow_by_John_R._Boettiger_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_Love_in_Shadow_by_John_R._Boettiger-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 15, 2006, no copies were printed as a result of a power outage caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/December_2006_Pacific_Northwest_storms" title="December 2006 Pacific Northwest storms">December 2006 Pacific Northwest storms</a>. It was the first time in 70 years that publication had been suspended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dec2006-no-print_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dec2006-no-print-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 9, 2009, the Hearst Corporation announced that after losing money on it every year since 2000, Hearst was putting the <i>P-I</i> up for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes-2009jan_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2009jan-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-2009jan9_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pi-2009jan9-10">[10]</a></sup> The paper would be put on the market for 60 days, and if a buyer could not be found within that time, the paper would either be turned into an Internet-only publication with a drastically reduced staff, or closed outright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes-2009jan_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2009jan-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pi-2009jan9_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-pi-2009jan9-10">[10]</a></sup> The news of the paper's impending sale was initially broken by local station <a href="/wiki/KING-TV" title="KING-TV">KING-TV</a> the night prior to the official announcement, and came as a surprise to the <i>P-I</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s staff and the owners of rival newspaper the <i>Seattle Times</i>. Analysts did not expect a buyer to be found, in view of declining circulation in the U.S. newspaper industry and other newspapers on the market going unsold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes-2009jan_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2009jan-9">[9]</a></sup> Five days before the 60-day deadline, the <i>P-I</i> reported that the Hearst Corporation had given several <i>P-I</i> reporters provisional job offers for an online edition of the <i>P-I</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 16, 2009, the newspaper posted a headline on its front page, followed shortly after by a short news story, that explained that the following day's edition would be its final one in print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pifrontpage_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pifrontpage-12">[12]</a></sup> The newspaper's publisher, Roger Oglesby, was quoted saying that the <i>P-I</i> would continue as an online-only operation. Print subscribers had their subscriptions automatically transferred to the <i>Seattle Times</i> on March 18.</p>
<p>As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <i>P-I</i> continues as an online-only newspaper. In September 2010, the site had an estimated 2.8 million unique visitors and 208,000 visitors per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_operating_agreement">Joint operating agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Joint operating agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1983 to 2009, the <i>P-I</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seattle_Times" title="The Seattle Times">The Seattle Times</a></i> had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_operating_agreement" title="Joint operating agreement">joint operating agreement</a> (JOA) whereby <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Production,_costs,_and_pricing" title="Production, costs, and pricing">production</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a>, and circulation were run for both papers by the Seattle Times Company. They maintained separate <a href="/wiki/News" title="News">news</a> and <a href="/wiki/Editorial" title="Editorial">editorial</a> departments. The papers published a combined Sunday edition, although the <i>Times</i> handled the majority of the editorial content while the <i>P-I</i> only provided a small editorial/opinions section.</p>
<p>In 2003 <i>Times</i> tried to cancel the JOA, citing a clause in it that three consecutive years of <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">losses</a> were cause for cancelling the agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JOA_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-JOA-14">[14]</a></sup> Hearst disagreed and immediately filed suit to prevent the <i>Times</i> from cancelling the agreement. Hearst argued that a <i><a href="/wiki/Force_majeure" title="Force majeure">force majeure</a></i> clause prevented the <i>Times</i> from claiming losses in 2000 and 2001 as reason to end the JOA, because they resulted from extraordinary events (in this case, a seven-week newspaper <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strike</a>).</p>
<p>Each side publicly accused the other of attempting to put its rival out of business. The trial judge granted a summary judgment in Hearst's favor on the <i>force majeure</i> issue. But after two appeals, the <a href="/wiki/State_supreme_court" title="State supreme court">Washington State Supreme Court</a> ruled in favor of the <i>Times</i> on June 30, 2005, on the <i>force majeure</i> clause, reversing the trial-court judge. The two papers settled the issue on April 16, 2007.</p>
<p>The JOA ended in 2009 with the cessation of the <i>P-I</i> print edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pifrontpage_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-pifrontpage-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>P-I</i> was notable for its two-time <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning <a href="/wiki/Editorial_cartoonist" title="Editorial cartoonist">editorial cartoonist</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Horsey" title="David Horsey">David Horsey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Report_on_Judge_Gary_Little">Report on Judge Gary Little</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Report on Judge Gary Little">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Investigative reporting on <a href="/wiki/King_County_Superior_Court" title="King County Superior Court">King County Superior Court</a> Judge <a href="/wiki/Gary_Little" title="Gary Little">Gary Little</a>'s out-of-court contact with juvenile defendants revealed accusations that Little <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse#Child_sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">molested</a> young boys while he was a teacher at Seattle's exclusive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lakeside_School_(Seattle,_Washington)" title="Lakeside School (Seattle, Washington)">Lakeside School</a> between 1968 and 1971. It also revealed inappropriate contact between Little and juveniles appearing before him after he became a judge. On August 19, 1988, after reporter Duff Wilson called the judge to advise him the newspaper was publishing the story, Little shot himself in the <a href="/wiki/King_County_Courthouse" title="King County Courthouse">King County Courthouse</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethical debates</a> surrounding the publication of the story – and the network of connections that protected Little – are taught in <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a> classes, and led to reforms in the way judges are disciplined in Washington state.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conduct_Unbecoming_series">Conduct Unbecoming series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conduct Unbecoming series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006 the <i>P-I</i> became the subject of a complaint to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Washington_News_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Washington News Council (page does not exist)">Washington News Council</a> for its reporting on the <a href="/wiki/King_County,_Washington" title="King County, Washington">King County</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_County,_Washington_Sheriff%27s_Office" title="King County, Washington Sheriff's Office">Sheriff's Office</a>. The media watch-dog group ruled against the <i>P-I</i>, agreeing with Sheriff Sue Rahr's complaint that the newspaper had unfairly disparaged the Sheriff's Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <i>P-I</i> declined to participate in the proceedings, and opted instead to give a detailed reply on its website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_P-I_Globe">The P-I Globe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The P-I Globe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_(2014).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2504" data-file-width="2785" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_%282014%29.jpg/220px-Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_%282014%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_%282014%29.jpg/330px-Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_%282014%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_%282014%29.jpg/440px-Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_%282014%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seattle_P-I_Globe_2_(2014).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The P-I Globe is an official Seattle Landmark.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>P-I</i> is known for the 13.5-ton, 30-foot (9.1 m) <a href="/wiki/Neon_sign" title="Neon sign">neon</a> globe atop its headquarters on the <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Bay" title="Elliott Bay">Elliott Bay</a> waterfront, which features the words "It's in the P-I" rotating around the globe and an 18-foot (5.5 m) eagle perched atop with wings stretched upwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keep_globe_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-keep_globe-18">[18]</a></sup> The globe originated from a 1947 readers' contest to determine a new symbol for the paper. Out of 350 entrants, the winner was by Jack (known as Jakk) C. Corsaw, a University of Washington art student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landmark-designation_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-landmark-designation-19">[19]</a></sup> The globe was manufactured in 1948<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landmark-designation_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-landmark-designation-19">[19]</a></sup> and was placed atop the paper's then-new headquarters building at 6th Avenue and Wall Street. When the newspaper moved its headquarters again in 1986, to its current location on the waterfront, the globe was relocated to the new building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keep_globe_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-keep_globe-18">[18]</a></sup> Over the decades since its first installation, the globe has become a city landmark that, to locals, is as iconic as the <a href="/wiki/Space_Needle" title="Space Needle">Space Needle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A stylized rendering of the globe appeared on the masthead of the newspaper in its latter years and continues to feature on its website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2012, it was designated a Seattle Landmark by the city's <a href="/wiki/City_of_Seattle_Landmarks_Preservation_Board" title="City of Seattle Landmarks Preservation Board">Landmarks Preservation Board</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landmark-designation_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-landmark-designation-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landmark-komo_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-landmark-komo-21">[21]</a></sup> Mayor <a href="/wiki/Ed_Murray_(Washington_politician)" title="Ed Murray (Washington politician)">Ed Murray</a> signed a city ordinance that had been passed by the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_City_Council" title="Seattle City Council">Seattle City Council</a> on December 17, 2015 that designated the globe as an official city landmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2012, the globe was donated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_History_and_Industry" title="Museum of History and Industry">Museum of History and Industry</a>, which planned to refurbish and relocate it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but as of late-2016 this had not occurred.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_employees">Notable employees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Post-Intelligencer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notable employees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notable employees of the <i>P-I</i> have included the novelists <a href="/wiki/E._B._White" title="E. B. White">E. B. White</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Robbins" title="Tom Robbins">Tom Robbins</a>.</p>
