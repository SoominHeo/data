<p>The <b>field of view</b> is the extent of the observable world that is <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">seen</a> at any given moment. In case of <a href="/wiki/Optical_instrument" title="Optical instrument">optical instruments</a> or sensors it is a <a href="/wiki/Solid_angle" title="Solid angle">solid angle</a> through which a detector is sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans_and_animals">Humans and animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Humans and animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the context of human vision, the term "field of view" is typically used in the sense of a restriction to what is visible by external apparatus, like spectacles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfano_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alfano-2">[2]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> goggles. Note that eye movements do not change the field of view.</p>
<p>If the analogy of the eye's retina working as a sensor is drawn upon, the corresponding concept in human (and much of animal vision) is the <i>visual field</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strasburger_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strasburger-3">[3]</a></sup> It is defined as “the number of degrees of visual angle during stable fixation of the eyes”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strasburger_Poeppel_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strasburger_Poeppel-4">[4]</a></sup> Note that eye movements are excluded in the definition. Different animals have different visual fields, depending, among others, on the placement of the eyes. <a href="/wiki/Human_eye#Field_of_view" title="Human eye">Humans</a> have an almost 180-degree forward-facing horizontal diameter of their visual field, while some <a href="/wiki/Bird_vision" title="Bird vision">birds</a> have a complete or nearly complete 180-degree visual field. The vertical range of the visual field in humans is typically around 135 degrees.</p>
<p>The range of visual abilities is not uniform across the visual field, and varies from animal to animal. For example, <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular vision</a>, which is the basis for <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis" title="Stereopsis">stereopsis</a> and is important for <a href="/wiki/Depth_perception" title="Depth perception">depth perception</a>, covers 114 degrees (horizontally) of the visual field in humans;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the remaining peripheral 60–70 degrees have no binocular vision (because only one <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">eye</a> can see those parts of the visual field). Some birds have a scant 10 or 20 degrees of binocular vision.</p>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Color_vision" title="Color vision">color vision</a> and the ability to perceive shape and motion vary across the visual field; in humans the former is concentrated in the center of the visual field, while the latter tends to be much stronger in the periphery. The physiological basis for that is the much higher concentration of color-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a> and color-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Parvocellular_cell" title="Parvocellular cell">parvocellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">retinal ganglion cells</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fovea_centralis" title="Fovea centralis">fovea</a> – the central region of the retina – in comparison to the higher concentration of color-insensitive <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod cells</a> and motion-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Magnocellular_cell" title="Magnocellular cell">magnocellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">retinal ganglion cells</a> in the visual periphery. Since cone cells require considerably brighter light sources to be activated, the result of this distribution is further that peripheral vision is much more sensitive at night relative to foveal vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strasburger_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strasburger-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversions">Conversions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conversions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many optical instruments, particularly <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> or spotting scopes, are advertised with their field of view specified in one of two ways: angular field of view, and linear field of view. Angular field of view is typically specified in degrees, while linear field of view is a ratio of lengths. For example, binoculars with a 5.8 <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degree (angular)</a> field of view might be advertised as having a (linear) field of view of 102 mm per meter. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Machine_vision">Machine vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Machine vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Machine_vision" title="Machine vision">machine vision</a> the lens <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a> and <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">image sensor</a> size sets up the fixed relationship between the field of view and the working distance. Field of view is the area of the inspection captured on the camera’s imager. The size of the field of view and the size of the camera’s imager directly affect the image resolution (one determining factor in accuracy). Working distance is the distance between the back of the lens and the target object.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remote_sensing">Remote sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Remote sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Solid_angle" title="Solid angle">solid angle</a> through which a detector element (a pixel sensor) is sensitive to electromagnetic radiation at any one time, is called <i>instantaneous field of view</i> or IFOV. A measure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spatial_resolution" title="Spatial resolution">spatial resolution</a> of a remote sensing imaging system, it is often expressed as dimensions of visible ground area, for some known sensor <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Single pixel IFOV is closely related to concept of <i>resolved pixel size</i>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_resolved_distance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ground resolved distance (page does not exist)">ground resolved distance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_sample_distance" title="Ground sample distance">ground sample distance</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modulation_transfer_function" title="Modulation transfer function">modulation transfer function</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In astronomy the field of view is usually expressed as an <a href="/wiki/Solid_angle" title="Solid angle">angular area</a> viewed by the instrument, in <a href="/wiki/Square_degree" title="Square degree">square degrees</a>, or for higher magnification instruments, in square <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc-minute" title="Arc-minute">arc-minutes</a>. For reference the Wide Field Channel on the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Camera_for_Surveys" title="Advanced Camera for Surveys">Advanced Camera for Surveys</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> has a field of view of 10 sq. arc-minutes, and the High Resolution Channel of the same instrument has a field of view of 0.15 sq. arc-minutes. Ground based survey telescopes have much wider fields of view. The photographic plates used by the <a href="/wiki/UK_Schmidt_Telescope" title="UK Schmidt Telescope">UK Schmidt Telescope</a> had a field of view of 30 sq. degrees. The 1.8 m (71 in) <a href="/wiki/Pan-STARRS" title="Pan-STARRS">Pan-STARRS</a> telescope, with the most advanced digital camera to date has a field of view of 7 sq. degrees. In the near infra-red WFCAM on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UKIRT" title="UKIRT">UKIRT</a> has a field of view of 0.2 sq. degrees and the forthcoming <a href="/wiki/VISTA_(telescope)" title="VISTA (telescope)">VISTA</a> telescope will have a field of view of 0.6 sq. degrees. Until recently digital cameras could only cover a small field of view compared to <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plates</a>, although they beat photographic plates in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_efficiency" title="Quantum efficiency">quantum efficiency</a>, linearity and dynamic range, as well as being much easier to process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_view" title="Angle of view">Angle of view</a></div>
<p>In photography, the field of view is that part of the world that is visible through the camera at a particular position and orientation in space; objects outside the FOV when the picture is taken are not recorded in the photograph. It is most often expressed as the angular size of the view cone, as an <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_view" title="Angle of view">angle of view</a>. For normal lens, field of view can be calculated FOV = 2 arctan(SensorSize/2f), where <i>f</i> is <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">focal length</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_of_view&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view_in_video_games" title="Field of view in video games">Field of view in video games</a></div>
<p>The field of view in <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> refers to the part you see of a game world, which is dependent on the scaling method used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
