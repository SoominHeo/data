<p><b>Theodore Meir Bikel</b> (pronounced <i>bih-KEL</i>; May 2, 1924 – July 21, 2015) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_American" title="Austrian American">Austrian-American</a> <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> stage, television and film actor, <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk singer</a>, musician, composer, unionist and political activist.</p>
<p>He made his stage debut in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tevye_the_Milkman" title="Tevye the Milkman">Tevye the Milkman</a></i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv,_Israel" title="Tel Aviv, Israel">Tel Aviv, Israel</a>, when he was in his teens. He later studied acting at Britain's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Dramatic_Art" title="Royal Academy of Dramatic Art">Royal Academy of Dramatic Art</a> and made his London stage debut in 1948 and in New York in 1955. He was also a widely recognized and recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_singer" title="Folk singer">folk singer</a> and guitarist. In 1959 he co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Newport_Folk_Festival" title="Newport Folk Festival">Newport Folk Festival</a> and created the role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_von_Trapp" title="Captain von Trapp">Captain von Trapp</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Mary_Martin" title="Mary Martin">Mary Martin</a> as Maria in the original <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodgers_%26_Hammerstein" title="Rodgers &amp; Hammerstein">Rodgers &amp; Hammerstein</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Music" title="The Sound of Music">The Sound of Music</a></i>. In 1969 Bikel began acting and singing on stage as Tevye in the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Fiddler_on_the_Roof" title="Fiddler on the Roof">Fiddler on the Roof</a></i>, a role he performed more often than any other actor to date. The production won nine Tony Awards and was one of the <a href="/wiki/Long-running_musical_theatre_productions" title="Long-running musical theatre productions">longest-running musicals in Broadway history</a>.</p>
<p>Bikel was president of the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Actors_and_Artistes_of_America" title="Associated Actors and Artistes of America">Associated Actors and Artistes of America</a> and was president of <a href="/wiki/Actors%27_Equity_Association" title="Actors' Equity Association">Actors' Equity</a> in the late 1970s and early 1980s. He served as the Chair of the Board of Directors of <a href="/wiki/Partners_for_Progressive_Israel" title="Partners for Progressive Israel">Partners for Progressive Israel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> where he also lectured.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Theodore Bikel was born into a <a href="/wiki/American_Jews" title="American Jews">Jewish family</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Austrian_Republic" title="First Austrian Republic">Austria</a>, the son of Miriam (née Riegler) and Josef Bikel, from <a href="/wiki/Bukovina" title="Bukovina">Bukovina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As an active <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionist</a>, his father named him after <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Herzl" title="Theodor Herzl">Theodor Herzl</a>, the founder of modern Zionism. Following <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">the German union with Austria</a> in 1938, Bikel's family fled to <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a>, where his father's contacts helped the family obtain British passports. Bikel studied at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikve_Yisrael" title="Mikve Yisrael">Mikve Yisrael</a> agricultural school and joined <a href="/wiki/Kibbutz" title="Kibbutz">Kibbutz</a> <a href="/wiki/Kfar_HaMaccabi" title="Kfar HaMaccabi">Kfar HaMaccabi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haaretz_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-haaretz-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bikel started acting while in his teens. He performed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habimah_Theater" title="Habimah Theater">Habimah Theater</a> in 1943 and was one of the founding members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cameri_Theatre" title="Cameri Theatre">Cameri Theatre</a>, which became a leading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israeli" title="Israeli">Israeli</a> theater company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haaretz_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-haaretz-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fox-7">[7]</a></sup> He described his acting experience there as similar, if not better, than the <a href="/wiki/Method_acting" title="Method acting">Method acting</a> techniques taught at the <a href="/wiki/Actors_Studio" title="Actors Studio">Actors Studio</a> in New York. "The Habimah people were much closer to the Method, indeed, than <a href="/wiki/Lee_Strasberg" title="Lee Strasberg">Lee Strasberg</a> was, because they were direct disciples of <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Stanislavski" title="Konstantin Stanislavski">Stanislavski</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staggs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-staggs-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1945, he moved to London to study at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Dramatic_Art" title="Royal Academy of Dramatic Art">Royal Academy of Dramatic Art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Bikel moved to the United States in 1954 and became a naturalized citizen in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Actor">Actor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Actor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1948, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Redgrave" title="Michael Redgrave">Michael Redgrave</a> recommended Bikel to his friend <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a> as understudy for the parts of both <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kowalski" title="Stanley Kowalski">Stanley Kowalski</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_Mitchell_(A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_character)" title="Harold Mitchell (A Streetcar Named Desire character)">Mitch</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">West End</a> premiere of <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(play)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (play)">A Streetcar Named Desire</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Bikel graduated from understudy to star opposite the director's wife, <a href="/wiki/Vivien_Leigh" title="Vivien Leigh">Vivien Leigh</a>, with a sudden unplanned performance when a co-star, playing the role of Mitch, came down with a case of flu. Bikel showed up backstage and went directly to Leigh's dressing room to ask if she wanted to rehearse with him, to make sure he was right for the part. She replied that she did not need to: "Go and do it," she said. "You are a professional, and Larry gave you this job because he trusted you to do it well." After the show, Leigh told him, "Well done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staggs_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-staggs-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>For most of his acting career, he became known for his versatility in playing characters of different nationalities, claiming he took on those different personalities so his acting would "never get stale."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup> On television he played an Armenian merchant on <i><a href="/wiki/Ironside_(1967_TV_series)" title="Ironside (1967 TV series)">Ironside</a></i>, a Polish professor on <i><a href="/wiki/Charlie%27s_Angels" title="Charlie's Angels">Charlie's Angels</a></i>, and American professor on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Paper_Chase_(TV_series)" title="The Paper Chase (TV series)">The Paper Chase</a></i>, a Bulgarian villain on <i><a href="/wiki/Falcon_Crest" title="Falcon Crest">Falcon Crest</a></i>, a Russian on <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i>, and an Italian on <i><a href="/wiki/Murder,_She_Wrote" title="Murder, She Wrote">Murder, She Wrote</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In movies, he played a German officer in <i><a href="/wiki/The_African_Queen_(film)" title="The African Queen (film)">The African Queen</a></i> (1951) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Enemy_Below" title="The Enemy Below">The Enemy Below</a></i> (1957), a Southern sheriff in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Defiant_Ones" title="The Defiant Ones">The Defiant Ones</a></i>, and a Russian submarine captain in the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Russians_Are_Coming,_the_Russians_Are_Coming" title="The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming">The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming</a></i> (1966). He also portrayed the sadistic General Jouvet in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pride_and_the_Passion" title="The Pride and the Passion">The Pride and the Passion</a></i> (1957), and was screentested for the role of <a href="/wiki/Auric_Goldfinger" title="Auric Goldfinger">Auric Goldfinger</a> in the <a href="/wiki/James_Bond_in_film" title="James Bond in film">James Bond</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Goldfinger_(film)" title="Goldfinger (film)">Goldfinger</a></i> (1964).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady_(film)" title="My Fair Lady (film)">My Fair Lady</a></i> (1964), he played an overbearing Hungarian linguist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>He made his Broadway debut in 1955 in "Tonight in Samarkind" and in 1958 was nominated for a Tony for "The Rope Dancers." In 1959, he created the role of <a href="/wiki/Georg_von_Trapp" title="Georg von Trapp">Captain von Trapp</a> in the original production of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Music" title="The Sound of Music">The Sound of Music</a></i>, which earned him a second Tony nomination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-fox-7">[7]</a></sup> Bikel did not like his role, however, because his ability to sing was underutilized, nor did he like performing the same role of the Captain repeatedly. When the composers, <a href="/wiki/Rodgers_and_Hammerstein" title="Rodgers and Hammerstein">Rodgers and Hammerstein</a>, realized Bikel was an accomplished folksinger, they wrote the song "<a href="/wiki/Edelweiss_(song)" title="Edelweiss (song)">Edelweiss</a>" specifically for him to sing and accompany himself on the guitar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1964, he played Zoltan Karpathy, the dialect expert, in the film version of <i><a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady_(film)" title="My Fair Lady (film)">My Fair Lady</a></i>. Since his first appearance as <a href="/wiki/Tevye" title="Tevye">Tevye</a> in the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Fiddler_on_the_Roof" title="Fiddler on the Roof">Fiddler on the Roof</a></i> in 1967, Bikel had performed the role more often than any other actor (more than 2,000 times). When an injury required 74-year-old fellow Israeli performer <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Topol" title="Chaim Topol">Chaim Topol</a> (veteran of many productions of the stage show and star of the motion picture of Fiddler on the Roof) to withdraw from a high-budget, much-promoted 2009 North American tour of the musical, Bikel substituted for him in several appearances in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bikel was a guest star on many popular television shows. He appeared in an episode of the 1954 <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> <a href="/wiki/Legal_drama" title="Legal drama">legal drama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Justice_(1954_TV_series)" title="Justice (1954 TV series)">Justice</a></i> based on cases from the Legal Aid Society of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He also appeared in the episode entitled "The Faithful Pilgrimage" of CBS's <i><a href="/wiki/Appointment_with_Adventure" title="Appointment with Adventure">Appointment with Adventure</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Anthology_series" title="Anthology series">anthology series</a>. The particular episode was written by <a href="/wiki/Rod_Serling" title="Rod Serling">Rod Serling</a>. He also appeared in a second episode of <i>Appointment with Adventure</i> entitled "Return of the Stranger." Bikel also appeared in <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a>'s 1971 film <i><a href="/wiki/200_Motels" title="200 Motels">200 Motels</a></i>.</p>
<p>Later, Bikel guest starred on <a href="/wiki/Rod_Serling" title="Rod Serling">Rod Serling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)">The Twilight Zone</a></i> (episode "<a href="/wiki/Four_O%27Clock" title="Four O'Clock">Four O'Clock</a>" as Oliver Crangle). He appeared on episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/Wagon_Train" title="Wagon Train">Wagon Train</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Five-O" title="Hawaii Five-O">Hawaii Five-O</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Columbo" title="Columbo">Columbo</a></i> (1977, "The Bye-Bye Sky High I.Q. Murder Case"), <i><a href="/wiki/Charlie%27s_Angels" title="Charlie's Angels">Charlie's Angels</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_San_Pedro_Beach_Bums" title="The San Pedro Beach Bums">The San Pedro Beach Bums</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">Cannon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Little_House_on_the_Prairie_(TV_series)" title="Little House on the Prairie (TV series)">Little House on the Prairie</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mission:_Impossible" title="Mission: Impossible">Mission: Impossible</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gunsmoke" title="Gunsmoke">Gunsmoke</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dynasty_(TV_series)" title="Dynasty (TV series)">Dynasty</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/All_in_the_Family" title="All in the Family">All in the Family</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Knight_Rider_(1982_TV_series)" title="Knight Rider (1982 TV series)">Knight Rider</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murder_She_Wrote" title="Murder She Wrote">Murder She Wrote</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasy_Island" title="Fantasy Island">Fantasy Island</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Mickey_Spillane%27s_Mike_Hammer" title="Mickey Spillane's Mike Hammer">Mickey Spillane's Mike Hammer</a></i> (episode "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elegy_for_a_Tramp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elegy for a Tramp (page does not exist)">Elegy for a Tramp</a>" as Gerringer and aired on January 28, 1987).</p>
<p>In the early 1990s, he appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i>, in the episode "<a href="/wiki/Family_(Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation)" title="Family (Star Trek: The Next Generation)">Family</a>", playing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Rozhenko" title="Sergey Rozhenko">Sergey Rozhenko</a>, the Russian-born adoptive father of <a href="/wiki/Worf" title="Worf">Worf</a>. Bikel performed two roles in the <i><a href="/wiki/Babylon_5" title="Babylon 5">Babylon 5</a></i> universe, in 1994 as Rabbi Koslov in the first-season episode "<a href="/wiki/TKO_(Babylon_5)" title="TKO (Babylon 5)">TKO</a>" and in 1998, as <a href="/wiki/Ranger_(Babylon_5)" title="Ranger (Babylon 5)">Ranger</a> leader Lenonn in the TV movie <i><a href="/wiki/Babylon_5:_In_the_Beginning" title="Babylon 5: In the Beginning">Babylon 5: In the Beginning</a></i>.</p>
<p>Bikel was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award" title="Drama Desk Award">Drama Desk Award</a> in 2010 for outstanding solo performance for “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sholom_Aleichem" title="Sholom Aleichem">Sholom Aleichem</a>: Laughter Through Tears,” an off-Broadway play which he also wrote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-variety-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2012, Bikel played the title role in <i><a href="/wiki/Visiting_Mr._Green" title="Visiting Mr. Green">Visiting Mr. Green</a></i> with the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company in Toronto, Ontario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2013, Bikel starred in <i>Journey 4 Artists</i>, a documentary which celebrates the power of music and religious diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folksinger_and_composer">Folksinger and composer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Folksinger and composer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1955, at the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Jac_Holzman" title="Jac Holzman">Jac Holzman</a> of <a href="/wiki/Elektra_Records" title="Elektra Records">Elektra Records</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Bikel began recording songs, including several albums of Jewish folk songs and songs from Russia and other countries, making over 20 contemporary and folk music albums during his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> For those, he played acoustic guitar alone or accompanied by other musicians. He was able to sing in 21 different languages, including Yiddish, Hebrew, German, Russian, Hungarian, Romanian, French, medieval Spanish, Zulu and English. His early albums included <i>Israeli Folk Songs</i> (1955) and <i>Songs of Russian Old &amp; New</i> (1960).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup> One of his Russian albums was entitled <i>Songs of a Russian Gypsy</i>, in 1958, where he introduced the folk tune, "Kretchma."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Bikel's live performances were issued on two albums: <i>Bravo Bikel</i> (1959), and <i>Bikel on Tour</i>(1963).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strong_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-strong-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1959 Bikel co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Newport_Folk_Festival" title="Newport Folk Festival">Newport Folk Festival</a> (together with <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>, Harold Leventhal, <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Brand" title="Oscar Brand">Oscar Brand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Wein" title="George Wein">George Wein</a>). He performed a number of recorded duets with <a href="/wiki/Judy_Collins" title="Judy Collins">Judy Collins</a> at various festivals and on television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> During an interview, when asked what inspired him to become involved in organizing a folk festival, he said that music was "one of the few answers to the chaos that we have," one of the only recourses to avoid social strife, and a means of giving youth hope for a better world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newport_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-newport-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bikel viewed then 21-year-old <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> as one of those young performers expressing emotional and social messages through song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newport_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-newport-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1963, Bikel joined Dylan, Seeger, <a href="/wiki/Peter,_Paul_and_Mary" title="Peter, Paul and Mary">Peter, Paul and Mary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Baez" title="Joan Baez">Joan Baez</a> for the festival grand finale as they sang "Blowin' in the Wind" and "We Shall Overcome."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacAdams_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacAdams-27">[27]</a></sup> Following the festival, Bikel, Seeger and Dylan traveled to a planned rally in Greenwood, Mississippi to perform Dylan's newly-written song, "<a href="/wiki/Only_a_Pawn_in_Their_Game" title="Only a Pawn in Their Game">Only a Pawn in Their Game</a>," about the man who murdered <a href="/wiki/Medgar_Evers" title="Medgar Evers">Medgar Evers</a>, head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacAdams_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-MacAdams-27">[27]</a></sup> Originally, only Bikel and Seeger were scheduled to perform, but Bikel wanted Dylan to go with them. He told Dylan's manager, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Grossman" title="Albert Grossman">Albert Grossman</a>, "I'll tell you what. Buy him a ticket. Don't tell him where it came from. Tell him it's time to go down and experience the South."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sounes_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sounes-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bikel's close friendship with Seeger was sometimes tested as a result of the festival's choice of performers. On one occasion, Seeger became infuriated and wished he had an ax to cut the electrical cables because of the poor audio during <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>'s legendary performance accompanied by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Butterfield_Blues_Band" title="Paul Butterfield Blues Band">Paul Butterfield Blues Band</a>. Seeger expected Bikel to support him: "Theo, for Chrissake—tell them. Set them straight!" Bikel stepped forward and told Seeger, "Peter, this band, these rebels—they are us. They are what we were twenty years ago. Remember?" Seeger stared at him "like a trauma victim", as Bikel succeeded in calming Seeger down enough to let the group finish their songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitz_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitz-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1965, Bikel, as well as Seeger, was shocked when Bob Dylan turned electric at the Festival, an event some call "Dylan's declaration of musical independence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1962, Bikel became the first singer besides Dylan to perform "<a href="/wiki/Blowin%27_in_the_Wind" title="Blowin' in the Wind">Blowin' in the Wind</a>" in public. His 1964 album <i>A Folksinger's Choice</i> featured <a href="/wiki/Roger_McGuinn" title="Roger McGuinn">Jim McGuinn</a> (as he was then known) on banjo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strong_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-strong-23">[23]</a></sup> Bikel (with business partner <a href="/wiki/Herb_Cohen" title="Herb Cohen">Herb Cohen</a>) opened the first folk music coffeehouse in Los Angeles, <i>The Unicorn</i>. Its popularity led to the two opening a second club, Cosmo Alley, which in addition to folk music presented poets such as <a href="/wiki/Maya_Angelou" title="Maya Angelou">Maya Angelou</a> and comics including <a href="/wiki/Lenny_Bruce" title="Lenny Bruce">Lenny Bruce</a>. Bikel became increasingly involved with <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil rights</a> issues and <a href="/wiki/Progressivism_in_the_United_States" title="Progressivism in the United States">progressive</a> causes, and was a <a href="/wiki/Delegate" title="Delegate">delegate</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1968_Democratic_Convention" title="1968 Democratic Convention">1968 Democratic Convention</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bikel was married four times. He married Ofra Ichilov in 1942. They divorced the following year. His second marriage was in 1967 to Rita Weinberg Call with whom he has two children. They divorced in 2008. He married conductor <a href="/wiki/Tamara_Brooks" title="Tamara Brooks">Tamara Brooks</a> later that year. She died in 2012. He married Aimee Ginsburg on December 29, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bikel died on July 21, 2015, at <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan_UCLA_Medical_Center" title="Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center">Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center</a> in Los Angeles of natural causes, according to publicist Harlan Boll. Bikel is survived by his wife, Aimee Ginsburg, his sons from his second marriage, Robert and Daniel, and three grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup> He was buried at <a href="/wiki/Hillside_Memorial_Park_Cemetery" title="Hillside Memorial Park Cemetery">Hillside Memorial Park Cemetery</a> in Culver City, California.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_activism">Political activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bikel was a longtime activist in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil rights</a> and human rights movements, participating as a fundraiser with performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup> He co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Actors_Federal_Credit_Union" title="Actors Federal Credit Union">Actors Federal Credit Union</a> in 1962, and in 1968 he was a delegate to the Democratic National Convention in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup> He was president of <a href="/wiki/Actors%27_Equity_Association" title="Actors' Equity Association">Actors' Equity</a> from 1977 to 1982 in which office he supported human rights causes. Since 1988 he had been president of the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Actors_and_Artistes_of_America" title="Associated Actors and Artistes of America">Associated Actors and Artistes of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon hearing of his death, Actors’ Equity wrote: "From the time he joined Equity in 1954, Bikel has been an advocate for the members of our union and his extraordinary achievements paved the way for so many. No one loved theater more, his union better or cherished actors like Theo did. He has left an indelible mark on generations of members past and generations of members to come. We thank you, Theo, for all you have done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-variety-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bikel was an active supporter and campaigner for <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>. He did some of his campaigning during the run of <i>Sound of Music</i> which got him into trouble with the producers, who did not think it was becoming for an actor. He recalls, "I would go out sometimes between matinee and evening performances, go to a rally and speak from a flat-bed truck, and then come back to the theater." The producers stopped complaining, however, when after one show he was picked up backstage by a limousine carrying <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a>, and he accompanied her to a Democratic rally as her special guest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the 1977 <a href="/wiki/AFL%E2%80%93CIO" title="AFL–CIO">AFL–CIO</a> Convention, Bikel welcomed the Russian dissident <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Bukovsky" title="Vladimir Bukovsky">Vladimir Bukovsky</a> upon his release from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chenoweth4_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chenoweth4-35">[35]</a></sup> He was arrested in front of the Soviet Embassy in Washington in 1986 while protesting the plight of <a href="/wiki/Refusenik" title="Refusenik">Soviet Jews</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> appointed him to serve on the National Council for the Arts in 1977 for a six-year term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2007 he served as chair of the Board of Directors of <a href="/wiki/Meretz" title="Meretz">Meretz</a> USA (now <a href="/wiki/Partners_for_Progressive_Israel" title="Partners for Progressive Israel">Partners for Progressive Israel</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was a member of the High IQ collective <a href="/wiki/Mensa_International" title="Mensa International">Mensa International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quiz83_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-quiz83-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_recognition">Awards and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Bikel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awards and recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1959 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nomination for <i>The Defiant Ones</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_10-11"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>1992 – Honorary Doctorate of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hartford" title="University of Hartford">University of Hartford</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
