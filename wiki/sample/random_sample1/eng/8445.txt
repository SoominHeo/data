<p>The <b>Sierpinski triangle</b> (also with the original orthography <i>Sierpiński</i>), also called the <b>Sierpinski gasket</b> or the <b>Sierpinski Sieve</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attractive_fixed_set" title="Attractive fixed set">attractive fixed set</a> with the overall shape of an <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">equilateral triangle</a>, subdivided <a href="/wiki/Recursion" title="Recursion">recursively</a> into smaller equilateral triangles. Originally constructed as a curve, this is one of the basic examples of <a href="/wiki/Self-similarity" title="Self-similarity">self-similar</a> sets, i.e., it is a mathematically generated pattern that can be reproducible at any magnification or reduction. It is named after the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> <a href="/wiki/Wac%C5%82aw_Sierpi%C5%84ski" title="Wacław Sierpiński">Wacław Sierpiński</a>, but appeared as a decorative pattern many centuries prior to the work of Sierpiński.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constructions">Constructions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Constructions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many different ways of constructing the Sierpinski triangle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Removing_triangles">Removing triangles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Removing triangles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sierpinski triangle may be constructed from an <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">equilateral triangle</a> by repeated removal of triangular subsets:</p>
<ol>
<li>Start with an equilateral triangle.</li>
<li>Subdivide it into four smaller congruent equilateral triangles and remove the central one.</li>
<li>Repeat step 2 with each of the remaining smaller triangles</li>
</ol>
<dl>
<dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg" title="The evolution of the Sierpinski triangle"><img alt="The evolution of the Sierpinski triangle" data-file-height="111" data-file-width="680" height="84" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg/512px-Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg/768px-Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg/1024px-Sierpinski_triangle_evolution.svg.png 2x" width="512"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>Each removed triangle (a <i>trema</i>) is <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topologically</a> an <a href="/wiki/Open_set" title="Open set">open set</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This process of recursively removing triangles is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Finite_subdivision_rule" title="Finite subdivision rule">finite subdivision rule</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shrinking_and_duplication">Shrinking and duplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shrinking and duplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The same sequence of shapes, converging to the Sierpinski triangle, can alternatively be generated by the following steps:</p>
<ol>
<li>Start with any triangle in a plane (any closed, bounded region in the plane will actually work). The canonical Sierpinski triangle uses an <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">equilateral triangle</a> with a base parallel to the horizontal axis (first image).</li>
<li>Shrink the triangle to <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span> height and <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span> width, make three copies, and position the three shrunken triangles so that each triangle touches the two other triangles at a corner (image 2). Note the emergence of the central hole—because the three shrunken triangles can between them cover only <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">3</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">4</span></span> of the area of the original. (Holes are an important feature of Sierpinski's triangle.)</li>
<li>Repeat step 2 with each of the smaller triangles (image 3 and so on).</li>
</ol>
<p>Note that this infinite process is not dependent upon the starting shape being a triangle—it is just clearer that way. The first few steps starting, for example, from a square also tend towards a Sierpinski triangle. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Barnsley" title="Michael Barnsley">Michael Barnsley</a> used an image of a fish to illustrate this in his paper "V-variable fractals and superfractals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg" title="Iterating from a square"><img alt="Iterating from a square" data-file-height="128" data-file-width="680" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg/512px-Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg/768px-Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg/1024px-Sierpinski_triangle_evolution_square.svg.png 2x" width="512"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>The actual fractal is what would be obtained after an infinite number of iterations. More formally, one describes it in terms of functions on closed sets of points. If we let <i>d</i><sub>A</sub> denote the dilation by a factor of <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span> about a point A, then the Sierpinski triangle with corners A, B, and C is the fixed set of the transformation <i>d</i><sub>A</sub> ∪ <i>d</i><sub>B</sub> ∪ <i>d</i><sub>C</sub>.</p>
<p>This is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attractive_fixed_set" title="Attractive fixed set">attractive fixed set</a>, so that when the operation is applied to any other set repeatedly, the images converge on the Sierpinski triangle. This is what is happening with the triangle above, but any other set would suffice.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chaos_game">Chaos game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chaos game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="607" data-file-width="700" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif/200px-Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif/300px-Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif/400px-Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sierpinski_chaos_animated.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Animated creation of a Sierpinski triangle using the chaos game</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>If one takes a point and applies each of the transformations <i>d</i><sub>A</sub>, <i>d</i><sub>B</sub>, and <i>d</i><sub>C</sub> to it randomly, the resulting points will be dense in the Sierpinski triangle, so the following algorithm will again generate arbitrarily close approximations to it:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Start by labeling <b>p</b><sub>1</sub>, <b>p</b><sub>2</sub> and <b>p</b><sub>3</sub> as the corners of the Sierpinski triangle, and a random point <b>v</b><sub>1</sub>. Set <span class="nowrap"><b>v</b><sub><i>n</i>+1</sub> = <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>(<b>v</b><sub><i>n</i></sub> + <b>p</b><sub><i>r<sub>n</sub></i></sub>)</span>, where <i>r<sub>n</sub></i> is a random number 1, 2 or 3. Draw the points <b>v</b><sub>1</sub> to <b>v</b><sub>∞</sub>. If the first point <b>v</b><sub>1</sub> was a point on the Sierpiński triangle, then all the points <b>v</b><sub><i>n</i></sub> lie on the Sierpinski triangle. If the first point <b>v</b><sub>1</sub> to lie within the perimeter of the triangle is not a point on the Sierpinski triangle, none of the points <b>v</b><sub><i>n</i></sub> will lie on the Sierpinski triangle, however they will converge on the triangle. If <b>v</b><sub>1</sub> is outside the triangle, the only way <b>v</b><sub><i>n</i></sub> will land on the actual triangle, is if <b>v</b><sub><i>n</i></sub> is on what would be part of the triangle, if the triangle was infinitely large.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Or more simply:</p>
<ol>
<li>Take 3 points in a plane to form a triangle, you need not draw it.</li>
<li>Randomly select any point inside the triangle and consider that your current position.</li>
<li>Randomly select any one of the 3 vertex points.</li>
<li>Move half the distance from your current position to the selected vertex.</li>
<li>Plot the current position.</li>
<li>Repeat from step 3.</li>
</ol>
<p><i>Note:</i> This method is also called the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_game" title="Chaos game">chaos game</a>, and is an example of an <a href="/wiki/Iterated_function_system" title="Iterated function system">iterated function system</a>. You can start from any point outside or inside the triangle, and it would eventually form the Sierpinski Gasket with a few leftover points (if the starting point lies on the outline of the triangle, there are no leftover points). It is interesting to do this with pencil and paper. A brief outline is formed after placing approximately one hundred points, and detail begins to appear after a few hundred.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrowhead_curve">Arrowhead curve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arrowhead curve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="345" data-file-width="593" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png/360px-Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png/540px-Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png 2x" width="360"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arrowhead_curve_1_through_6.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Construction of the <a href="/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_arrowhead_curve" title="Sierpiński arrowhead curve">Sierpiński arrowhead curve</a></div>

<p>Another construction for the Sierpinski triangle shows that it can be constructed as a <a href="/wiki/Curve" title="Curve">curve</a> in the plane. It is formed by a process of repeated modification of simpler curves, analogous to the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Koch_snowflake" title="Koch snowflake">Koch snowflake</a>:</p>
<ol>
<li>Start with a single line segment in the plane</li>
<li>Repeatedly replace each line segment of the curve with three shorter segments, forming 120° angles at each junction between two consecutive segments, with the first and last segments of the curve either parallel to the original line segment or forming a 60° angle with it.</li>
</ol>
<p>The resulting fractal curve is called the <a href="/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_arrowhead_curve" title="Sierpiński arrowhead curve">Sierpiński arrowhead curve</a>, and its limiting shape is the Sierpinski triangle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellular_automata">Cellular automata</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cellular automata">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sierpinski triangle also appears in certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellular_automata" title="Cellular automata">cellular automata</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Rule_90" title="Rule 90">Rule 90</a>), including those relating to <a href="/wiki/Conway%27s_Game_of_Life" title="Conway's Game of Life">Conway's Game of Life</a>. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Life-like_cellular_automaton" title="Life-like cellular automaton">Life-like cellular automaton</a> B1/S12 when applied to a single cell will generate four approximations of the Sierpinski triangle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The time-space diagram of a replicator pattern in a cellular automaton also often resembles a Sierpinski triangle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pascal.27s_triangle">Pascal's triangle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pascal's triangle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If one takes <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_triangle" title="Pascal's triangle">Pascal's triangle</a> with 2<sup><i>n</i></sup> rows and colors the even numbers white, and the odd numbers black, the result is an approximation to the Sierpinski triangle. More precisely, the <a href="/wiki/Limit_of_a_sequence" title="Limit of a sequence">limit</a> as <i>n</i> approaches infinity of this <a href="/wiki/Parity_(mathematics)" title="Parity (mathematics)">parity</a>-colored 2<sup><i>n</i></sup>-row Pascal triangle is the Sierpinski triangle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Towers_of_Hanoi">Towers of Hanoi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Towers of Hanoi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Towers_of_Hanoi" title="Towers of Hanoi">Towers of Hanoi</a> puzzle involves moving disks of different sizes between three pegs, maintaining the property that no disk is ever placed on top of a smaller disk. The states of an <i>n</i>-disk puzzle, and the allowable moves from one state to another, form an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undirected_graph" title="Undirected graph">undirected graph</a> that can be represented geometrically as the <a href="/wiki/Intersection_graph" title="Intersection graph">intersection graph</a> of the set of triangles remaining after the <i>n</i>th step in the construction of the Sierpinski triangle. Thus, in the limit as <i>n</i> goes to infinity, this sequence of graphs can be interpreted as a discrete analogue of the Sierpinski triangle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For integer number of dimensions <i>d</i>, when doubling a side of an object, 2<sup><i>d</i></sup> copies of it are created, i.e. 2 copies for 1-dimensional object, 4 copies for 2-dimensional object and 8 copies for 3-dimensional object. For the Sierpinski triangle, doubling its side creates 3 copies of itself. Thus the Sierpinski triangle has <a href="/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension" title="Hausdorff dimension">Hausdorff dimension</a> <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><a href="/wiki/Natural_logarithm" title="Natural logarithm">log</a>(3)</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">log(2)</span></span> ≈ 1.585, which follows from solving 2<sup><i>d</i></sup> = 3 for <i>d</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FFG120_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FFG120-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area of a Sierpinski triangle is zero (in <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">Lebesgue measure</a>). The area remaining after each iteration is clearly <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">3</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">4</span></span> of the area from the previous iteration, and an infinite number of iterations results in zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The points of a Sierpinski triangle have a simple characterization in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(mathematics)#Barycentric_coordinates_on_triangles" title="Barycentric coordinates (mathematics)">Barycentric coordinates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> If a point has coordinates (0.<i>u</i><sub>1</sub><i>u</i><sub>2</sub><i>u</i><sub>3</sub>…, 0.<i>v</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sub>3</sub>…, 0.<i>w</i><sub>1</sub><i>w</i><sub>2</sub><i>w</i><sub>3</sub>…), expressed as <a href="/wiki/Binary_number" title="Binary number">Binary numbers</a>, then the point is in Sierpinski's triangle if and only if <span class="nowrap"><i>u</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> + <i>v</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> + <i>w</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> = 1</span> for all <i>i</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalization_to_other_Moduli">Generalization to other Moduli</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Generalization to other Moduli">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A generalization of the Sierpinski triangle can also be generated using <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_triangle" title="Pascal's triangle">Pascal's triangle</a> if a different Modulo is used. Iteration <i>n</i> can be generated by taking a <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_triangle" title="Pascal's triangle">Pascal's triangle</a> with <i>P</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> rows and coloring numbers by their value for <i>x</i> mod <i>P</i>. As <i>n</i> approaches infinity, a fractal is generated.</p>
<p>The same fractal can be achieved by dividing a triangle into a tessellation of <i>P</i><sup>2</sup> similar triangles and removing the triangles that are upside-down from the original, then iterating this step with each smaller triangle.</p>
<p>Conversely, the fractal can also be generated by beginning with a triangle and duplicating it and arranging <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>n</i>(<i>n</i> + 1)</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span> of the new figures in the same orientation into a larger similar triangle with the vertices of the previous figures touching, then iterating that step.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <b>Sierpinski tetrahedron</b> or <b>tetrix</b> is the three-dimensional analogue of the Sierpinski triangle, formed by repeatedly shrinking a regular <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron" title="Tetrahedron">tetrahedron</a> to one half its original height, putting together four copies of this tetrahedron with corners touching, and then repeating the process. This can also be done with a square <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry)" title="Pyramid (geometry)">pyramid</a> and five copies instead. (A Hausdorff dimension strictly between 2 and 3 would indicate zero volume and infinite area.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wac%C5%82aw_Sierpi%C5%84ski" title="Wacław Sierpiński">Wacław Sierpiński</a> described the Sierpinski triangle in 1915. However, similar patterns appear already in the 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Cosmati" title="Cosmati">Cosmati</a> <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaics</a> in the cathedral of <a href="/wiki/Anagni" title="Anagni">Anagni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and other places of central Italy, for carpets in many places such as the nave of the Roman Basilica of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_in_Cosmedin" title="Santa Maria in Cosmedin">Santa Maria in Cosmedin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and for isolated triangles positioned in rotae in several churches and Basiliche.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the case of the isolated triangle, it is interesting to notice that the iteration is at least of three levels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierpinski_triangle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The usage of the word "gasket" to refer to the Sierpinski triangle refers to <a href="/wiki/Gasket" title="Gasket">gaskets</a> such as are found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor" title="Motor">motors</a>, and which sometimes feature a series of holes of decreasing size, similar to the fractal; this usage was coined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Mandelbrot" title="Benoît Mandelbrot">Benoît Mandelbrot</a>, who thought the fractal looked similar to "the part that prevents leaks in motors".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
