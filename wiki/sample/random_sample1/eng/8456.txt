<p>In <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>, <b>tense</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">category</a> that expresses time reference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ELL_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ELL-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comrie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comrie-3">[3]</a></sup> Tenses are usually manifested by the use of specific forms of <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>, particularly in their <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">conjugation</a> patterns.</p>
<p>Basic tenses found in many languages include the <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">past</a>, <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">future</a>. Some languages have only two distinct tenses, such as past and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonpast" title="Nonpast">nonpast</a>, or future and <a href="/wiki/Nonfuture_tense" title="Nonfuture tense">nonfuture</a>. There are also <a href="/wiki/Tenseless_language" title="Tenseless language">tenseless languages</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, which is traditionally thought to have no tense at all, although recent research suggests that it may possess a future and <a href="/wiki/Nonfuture_tense" title="Nonfuture tense">nonfuture</a> system, which is typical of Sino-Tibetan languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On the other hand, some languages make finer tense distinctions, such as remote vs. recent past, or near vs. remote future.</p>
<p>Tenses generally express time relative to the <a href="/wiki/TUTT_(linguistics)" title="TUTT (linguistics)">moment of speaking</a>. In some contexts, however, their meaning may be relativized to a point in the past or future which is established in the discourse (the moment being spoken about). This is called <a href="/wiki/Relative_and_absolute_tense" title="Relative and absolute tense"><i>relative</i></a> (as opposed to <i>absolute</i>) tense. Some languages have different verb forms or constructions which manifest relative tense, such as <a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">pluperfect</a> ("past-in-the-past") and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future-in-the-past" title="Future-in-the-past">future-in-the-past</a>".</p>
<p>Expressions of tense are often closely connected with expressions of the category of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspect</a>; sometimes what are traditionally called tenses (in languages such as <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>) may in modern analysis be regarded as combinations of tense with aspect. Verbs are also often conjugated for <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">mood</a>, and since in many cases the three categories are not manifested separately, some languages may be described in terms of a combined <a href="/wiki/Tense%E2%80%93aspect%E2%80%93mood" title="Tense–aspect–mood">tense–aspect–mood</a> (TAM) system.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English noun <i>tense</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>tens</i> "time" (spelled <i>temps</i> in modern French through deliberate archaisation), from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>tempus</i></span> "time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is not related to the adjective <i>tense</i>, which comes from Latin <i>tensus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Latin_conjugation#Participles" title="Latin conjugation">perfect passive participle</a> of <i>tendere</i> "stretch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_the_term">Uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In modern linguistic theory, tense is understood as a category that expresses (<a href="/wiki/Grammaticalization" title="Grammaticalization">grammaticalizes</a>) time reference; namely one which, using <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammatical</a> means, places a state or action in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ELL_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ELL-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comrie_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Comrie-3">[3]</a></sup> Nonetheless, in many descriptions of languages, particularly in traditional European grammar, the term "tense" is applied to series of verb forms or constructions that express not merely position in time, but also additional properties of the state or action – particularly aspectual or modal properties.</p>
<p>The category of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspect</a> expresses how a state or action relates to time – whether it is seen as a complete event, an ongoing or repeated situation, etc. Many languages make a distinction between <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">perfective aspect</a> (denoting complete events) and <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">imperfective aspect</a> (denoting ongoing or repeated situations); some also have other aspects, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_aspect" title="Perfect aspect">perfect aspect</a>, denoting a state following a prior event. Some of the traditional "tenses" express time reference together with aspectual information. In <a href="/wiki/Latin_grammar" title="Latin grammar">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_grammar" title="French grammar">French</a>, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">imperfect</a> denotes past time in combination with imperfective aspect, while other verb forms (the Latin perfect, and the French <i><a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_compos%C3%A9" title="Passé composé">passé composé</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_simple" title="Passé simple">passé simple</a></i>) are used for past time reference with perfective aspect.</p>
<p>The category of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">mood</a> is used to express <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_modality" title="Linguistic modality">modality</a>, which includes such properties as uncertainty, <a href="/wiki/Evidentiality" title="Evidentiality">evidentiality</a>, and obligation. Commonly encountered moods include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicative_mood" title="Indicative mood">indicative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subjunctive_mood" title="Subjunctive mood">subjunctive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mood" title="Conditional mood">conditional</a>. Mood can be bound up with tense, aspect, or both, in particular verb forms. Hence certain languages are sometimes analysed as having a single <a href="/wiki/Tense%E2%80%93aspect%E2%80%93mood" title="Tense–aspect–mood">tense–aspect–mood</a> (TAM) system, without separate manifestation of the three categories.</p>
<p>The term <i>tense</i>, then, particularly in less formal contexts, is sometimes used to denote any combination of tense proper, aspect, and mood. As regards <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, there are many <a href="/wiki/Uses_of_English_verb_forms" title="Uses of English verb forms">verb forms and constructions</a> which combine time reference with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_aspect" title="Continuous aspect">continuous</a> and/or perfect aspect, and with indicative, subjunctive or conditional mood. Particularly in some <a href="/wiki/English_language_teaching" title="English language teaching">English language teaching</a> materials, some or all of these forms can be referred to simply as tenses (see <a href="#English">below</a>).</p>
<p>Particular tense forms need not always carry their basic time-referential meaning in every case. A present tense form may sometimes refer to the past (as in the <a href="/wiki/Historical_present" title="Historical present">historical present</a>), a past tense form may sometimes refer to the non-past (as in some <a href="/wiki/English_conditional_sentences" title="English conditional sentences">English conditional sentences</a>), and so on.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_tenses">Possible tenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Possible tenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Not all languages have tense: <a href="/wiki/Tenseless_language" title="Tenseless language">tenseless languages</a> include <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dyirbal_language" title="Dyirbal language">Dyirbal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEComrie198550-53_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEComrie198550-53-7">[7]</a></sup> Some languages have all three basic tenses (the <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">past</a>, <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">future</a>), while others have only two: some have past and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonpast" title="Nonpast">nonpast</a> tenses, the latter covering both present and future times (as in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> in some analyses), whereas others such as <a href="/wiki/Greenlandic_language" title="Greenlandic language">Greenlandic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">Quechua</a> have future and <a href="/wiki/Nonfuture_tense" title="Nonfuture tense">nonfuture</a>. Some languages have four or more tenses, making finer distinctions either in the past (e.g. remote vs. recent past) or in the future (e.g. near vs. remote future). The six-tense language <a href="/wiki/Kalaw_Lagaw_Ya" title="Kalaw Lagaw Ya">Kalaw Lagaw Ya</a> of Australia has the remote past, the recent past, the today past, the present, the today/near future and the remote future.</p>
<p>A historical past tense, used for events perceived as historical, is found in, for example, the Amazonian <a href="/wiki/Cubeo_language" title="Cubeo language">Cubeo language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tenses that refer specifically to "today" are called <a href="/wiki/Hodiernal_tense" title="Hodiernal tense">hodiernal tenses</a>; these can be either past or future. Apart from Kalaw Lagaw Ya, another language which features such tenses is <a href="/wiki/Mwera_language" title="Mwera language">Mwera</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bantu_language" title="Bantu language">Bantu language</a> of Tanzania. It is also suggested that in 17th-century French, the <i><a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_compos%C3%A9" title="Passé composé">passé composé</a></i> served as a hodiernal past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Tenses which contrast with hodiernals, by referring to the past before today or the future after today, are called pre-hodiernal and post-hodiernal respectively. Some languages also have a <a href="/wiki/Crastinal_tense" title="Crastinal tense">crastinal tense</a>, a future tense referring specifically to tomorrow (found in some Bantu languages); or a <a href="/wiki/Hesternal_tense" title="Hesternal tense">hesternal tense</a>, a past tense referring specifically to yesterday<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (although this name is also sometimes used to mean pre-hodiernal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>). A tense for after tomorrow is thus called post-crastinal, and one for before yesterday is called pre-hesternal.</p>
<p>Another tense found in some languages, including <a href="/wiki/Luganda" title="Luganda">Luganda</a>, is the persistive tense, used to indicate that a state or ongoing action is still the case (or, in the negative, is no longer the case). Luganda also has tenses meaning "so far" and "not yet".</p>
<p>Some languages have special tense forms that are used to express <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_tense" title="Relative tense">relative tense</a>. Tenses that refer to the past relative to the time under consideration are called <i>anterior</i>; these include the <a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">pluperfect</a> (for the past relative to a past time) and the <a href="/wiki/Future_perfect" title="Future perfect">future perfect</a> (for the past relative to a future time). Similarly, <i>posterior</i> tenses refer to the future relative to the time under consideration, as with the English "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future-in-the-past" title="Future-in-the-past">future-in-the-past</a>": <i>(he said that) he <b>would go</b>.</i> Relative tense forms are also sometimes analysed as combinations of tense with aspect: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_aspect" title="Perfect aspect">perfect aspect</a> in the anterior case, or the <a href="/wiki/Prospective_aspect" title="Prospective aspect">prospective aspect</a> in the posterior case.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense_marking">Tense marking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tense marking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tense is normally indicated by the use of a particular verb form – either an <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflected</a> form of the main verb, or a <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">multi-word construction</a>, or both in combination. Inflection may involve the use of <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a>, such as the <i>-ed</i> ending that marks the past tense of <a href="/wiki/English_verbs" title="English verbs">English regular verbs</a>, but can also entail <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">stem</a> modifications, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablaut" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a>, as found in the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_strong_verb" title="Germanic strong verb">strong verbs</a> in English and other Germanic languages, or <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a>. Multi-word tense constructions often involve <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">auxiliary verbs</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clitics" title="Clitics">clitics</a>. Examples which combine both types of tense marking include the French <i><a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_compos%C3%A9" title="Passé composé">passé composé</a></i>, which has an auxiliary verb together with the inflected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">past participle</a> form of the main verb; and the <a href="/wiki/Irish_grammar#Verbs" title="Irish grammar">Irish past tense</a>, where the proclitic <i>do</i> (in various surface forms) appears in conjunction with the affixed or ablaut-modified past tense form of the main verb.</p>
<p>As has already been mentioned, indications of tense are often bound up with indications of other verbal categories, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tense-aspect-mood" title="Tense-aspect-mood">aspect and mood</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">conjugation</a> patterns of verbs often also reflect <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">agreement</a> with categories pertaining to the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">person</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">gender</a>. It is consequently not always possible to identify elements that mark any specific category, such as tense, separately from the others.</p>
<p>A few languages have been shown to mark tense information (as well as aspect and mood) on <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>. This may be called <a href="/wiki/Nominal_TAM" title="Nominal TAM">nominal TAM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Languages that do not have grammatical tense, such as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, express time reference chiefly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_(semiotics)" title="Lexical (semiotics)">lexical</a> means – through <a href="/wiki/Adverbial" title="Adverbial">adverbials</a>, time phrases, and so on. (The same is done in tensed languages, to supplement or reinforce the time information conveyed by the choice of tense.) Time information is also sometimes conveyed as a secondary feature by markers of other categories, as with the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_grammar#Aspect" title="Chinese grammar">Chinese aspect markers</a> <i>le</i> and <i>guo</i>, which in most cases place an action in past time. However, much time information is conveyed implicitly by context – it is therefore not always necessary, when <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translating</a> from a tensed to a tenseless language, say, to express explicitly in the target language all of the information conveyed by the tenses in the source.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_particular_languages">In particular languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In particular languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_and_Ancient_Greek">Latin and Ancient Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Latin and Ancient Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> is traditionally described as having six tenses (the Latin for "tense" being <i>tempus</i>, plural <i>tempora</i>):</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">Present</a> <i>(praesens)</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">Imperfect</a> <i>(praeteritum imperfectum)</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">Perfect</a> <i>(praeteritum perfectum)</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">Future</a> <i>(futurus)</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">Pluperfect</a> <i>(plus quam perfectum)</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Future_perfect" title="Future perfect">Future perfect</a> <i>(anterior futurus)</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Of these, the imperfect and perfect can be considered to represent a past tense combined with <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">imperfective</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">perfective</a> aspect respectively (the first is used for habitual or ongoing past actions or states, and the second for completed actions). The pluperfect and future perfect are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_tense" title="Relative tense">relative tenses</a>, referring to the past relative to a past time or relative to a future time.</p>
<p>Latin verbs are conjugated for tense (and aspect) together with <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">mood</a> (indicative, subjunctive, and sometimes imperative) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_voice" title="Grammatical voice">voice</a> (active or passive). Most forms are produced by inflecting the verb stem, with endings that also depend on the person and number of the subject. Some of the passive forms are produced using a <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">participle</a> together with a conjugated <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">auxiliary verb</a>. For details of the forms, see <a href="/wiki/Latin_conjugation" title="Latin conjugation">Latin conjugation</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_verbs#Tenses" title="Ancient Greek verbs">tenses of Ancient Greek</a> are similar, but with a three-way aspect contrast in the past: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aorist_tense" title="Aorist tense">aorist</a>, the perfect and the imperfect. The aorist was the "simple past", while the imperfective denoted uncompleted action in the past, and the perfect was used for past events having relevance to the present.</p>
<p>The study of modern languages has been <a href="/wiki/History_of_English_grammars" title="History of English grammars">greatly influenced</a> by the grammar of the Classical languages, since early grammarians, often monks, had no other reference point to describe their language. Latin terminology is often used to describe modern languages, sometimes with a change of meaning, as with the application of "perfect" to forms in English that do not necessarily have perfective meaning, or the words <i>Imperfekt</i> and <i>Perfekt</i> to <a href="/wiki/German_verbs" title="German verbs">German past tense forms</a> that mostly lack any relationship to the aspects implied by those terms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>English has only two <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphological</a> tenses: the <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-past_tense" title="Non-past tense">non-past</a>, as in <i>he <b>goes</b></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">past</a> or <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">preterite</a>, as in <i>he <b>went</b></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuddlestonPullum200251_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuddlestonPullum200251-13">[13]</a></sup> The non-past usually references the present, but sometimes references the future (as in <i>the bus <b>leaves</b> tomorrow</i>). (It also sometimes references the past, however, in what is called the <a href="/wiki/Historical_present" title="Historical present">historical present</a>.)</p>
<p>Constructions with the <a href="/wiki/English_modal_verbs" title="English modal verbs">modal auxiliary</a> verbs <a href="/wiki/Shall_and_will" title="Shall and will"><i>will</i> and <i>shall</i></a> also frequently reference the future (although they have other uses as well); these are often described as the English <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">future tense</a>. Less commonly, forms with the auxiliaries <i>would</i> and (rarely) <i>should</i> are described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_tense" title="Relative tense">relative tense</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future-in-the-past" title="Future-in-the-past">future-in-the-past</a>. (The same forms are used for the <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mood" title="Conditional mood">conditional mood</a>, and for various other meanings.)</p>
<p>The present and past are distinguished by verb form, using either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablaut" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a> (<i>sing(s)</i> ~ <i>sang</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> (<i>walk(s)</i> ~ <i>walked</i>). For details, see <a href="/wiki/English_verbs" title="English verbs">English verbs</a>.</p>

Each of these can combine with the tenses to produce a large set of different constructions, mostly involving one or more <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">auxiliary verbs</a> together with a <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">participle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">infinitive</a>:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Indo-European_languages">Other Indo-European languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other Indo-European languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_verbs" title="Proto-Indo-European verbs">Proto-Indo-European verbs</a> had present, perfect (<a href="/wiki/Stative_verb" title="Stative verb">stative</a>), imperfect and aorist forms – these can be considered as representing two tenses (present and past) with different <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspects</a>. Most languages in the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> family have developed systems either with two morphological tenses (present or "non-past", and past) or with three (present, past and future). The tenses often form part of entangled <a href="/wiki/Tense%E2%80%93aspect%E2%80%93mood" title="Tense–aspect–mood">tense–aspect–mood</a> conjugation systems. Additional tenses, tense–aspect combinations, etc. can be provided by compound constructions containing auxiliary verbs.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> (which include English) have present (non-past) and past tenses formed morphologically, with future and other additional forms made using auxiliaries. In standard <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, the compound past <i>(<a href="/wiki/German_verbs" title="German verbs">Perfekt</a>)</i> has replaced the simple morphological past in most contexts.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> (descendants of Latin) have past, present and future morphological tenses, with additional aspectual distinction in the past. <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> is an example of a language where, as in German, the simple morphological perfective past <i>(<a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_simple" title="Passé simple">passé simple</a>)</i> has mostly given way to a compound form <i>(<a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_compos%C3%A9" title="Passé composé">passé composé</a>)</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_language" title="Celtic language">Celtic language</a>, has past, present and future tenses (see <a href="/wiki/Irish_conjugation" title="Irish conjugation">Irish conjugation</a>). The past contrasts perfective and imperfective aspect, and some verbs retain such a contrast in the present. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Irish" title="Classical Irish">Classical Irish</a> had a three-way aspectual contrast of simple–perfective–imperfective in the past and present tenses.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_language" title="Indo-Iranian language">Indo-Iranian language</a>, has past and non-past forms, with additional aspectual distinctions. Future can be expressed using an auxiliary, but almost never in non-formal context.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, verbs are <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect#Slavic_languages" title="Grammatical aspect">intrinsically</a> perfective or imperfective. In <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> and some other languages in the group, perfective verbs have past and future tenses, while imperfective verbs have past, present and future, the imperfective future being a compound tense in most cases. The future tense of perfective verbs is formed in the same way as the present tense of imperfective verbs. However, in <a href="/wiki/South_Slavic_languages" title="South Slavic languages">South Slavic languages</a>, there may be a greater variety of forms – <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, for example, has present, past (both "imperfect" and "aorist") and future tenses, for both perfective and imperfective verbs, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_aspect" title="Perfect aspect">perfect</a> forms made with an auxiliary (see <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_verbs" title="Bulgarian verbs">Bulgarian verbs</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_tense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, both members of the <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic</a> language family, have morphological present (non-past) and past tenses. The <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_verbs" title="Hungarian verbs">Hungarian verb</a> <i>van</i> ("to be") also has a future form.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Turkish_grammar" title="Turkish grammar">Turkish verbs</a> conjugate for past, present and future, with a variety of aspects and moods.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arabic_verbs" title="Arabic verbs">Arabic verbs</a> have past and non-past; future can be indicated by a prefix.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Korean_verbs" title="Korean verbs">Korean verbs</a> have a variety of affixed forms which can be described as representing present, past and future tenses, although they can alternatively be considered to be aspectual. Similarly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_verbs" title="Japanese verbs">Japanese verbs</a> are described as having present and past tenses, although they may be analysed as aspects. <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> and many other East Asian languages generally lack inflection and are considered to be <a href="/wiki/Tenseless_language" title="Tenseless language">tenseless languages</a>, although they may have aspect markers which convey certain information about time reference.</p>
<p>For examples of languages with a greater variety of tenses, see the section on <a href="#Possible_tenses">possible tenses</a>, above. Fuller information on tense formation and usage in particular languages can be found in the articles on those languages and their grammars.</p>
