<p>The <b>Sicilian Vespers</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Vespri siciliani</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a>: <span lang="scn" xml:lang="scn"><i>Vespiri siciliani</i></span>) is the name given to the successful rebellion on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> that broke out at <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, 1282 against the rule of the French-born king <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Naples" title="Charles I of Naples">Charles I</a>, who had ruled the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a> since 1266. Within six weeks, three thousand French men and women were slain by the rebels, and the government of King Charles lost control of the island. It was the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Sicilian_Vespers" title="War of the Sicilian Vespers">War of the Sicilian Vespers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Papacy_versus_the_House_of_Hohenstaufen">The Papacy versus the House of Hohenstaufen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Papacy versus the House of Hohenstaufen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rising had its origin in the struggle between the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen" title="Hohenstaufen">Hohenstaufen dynasty,</a> <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperors</a> for control over <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, especially the Church's private <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">demesne</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a>. These lay between Hohenstaufen lands in northern Italy and the Hohenstaufen <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a> in the south; the Hohenstaufen also at the time ruled Germany.</p>
<p>In 1245 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IV" title="Pope Innocent IV">Pope Innocent IV</a> declared <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor</a> deposed, and roused opposition to him in Germany and Italy. When Frederick died in 1250, his dominion was inherited by his son, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_IV_of_Germany" title="Conrad IV of Germany">Conrad IV of Germany</a>. A period of turmoil followed Conrad's death in 1254, and the Kingdom of Sicily was seized by <a href="/wiki/Manfred,_King_of_Sicily" title="Manfred, King of Sicily">Manfred, King of Sicily</a>, Frederick's natural son, who reigned from 1258 to 1266.</p>
<p>Manfred had no involvement in German politics, where the interregnum lasted longer and there was no emperor until 1274. He first styled himself as vicar of his nephew <a href="/wiki/Conradin" title="Conradin">Conradin</a>, Conrad's son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, following a false rumour that Conradin was dead, Manfred later had himself crowned as king. He wished for a reconciliation with the papacy, which may have explained his support for the landless <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_II,_Latin_Emperor" title="Baldwin II, Latin Emperor">Baldwin II, Latin Emperor</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_IV" title="Pope Urban IV">Pope Urban IV</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_IV" title="Pope Clement IV">Pope Clement IV</a> were not prepared to recognize Manfred as lawful ruler of Sicily and first excommunicated then sought to depose him by force of arms.</p>
<p>After abortive attempts to enlist England as the champion of the Papacy against Manfred,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Urban IV settled on <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Naples" title="Charles I of Naples">Charles I of Naples</a> as his candidate for the Sicilian throne. Charles invaded Italy and defeated and killed Manfred in 1266 at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Benevento" title="Battle of Benevento">Battle of Benevento</a>, becoming King of Sicily. In 1268 Conradin, who had meanwhile come of age, invaded Italy to press his claim to the throne, but he was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tagliacozzo" title="Battle of Tagliacozzo">Battle of Tagliacozzo</a> and executed afterwards. Charles was now undisputed master of the Kingdom of Sicily.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_of_Anjou_and_Sicilian_unrest">Charles of Anjou and Sicilian unrest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Charles of Anjou and Sicilian unrest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Charles regarded his Sicilian territories as a springboard for his Mediterranean ambitions, which included the overthrow of <a href="/wiki/Michael_VIII_Palaiologos" title="Michael VIII Palaiologos">Michael VIII Palaiologos</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, and the capture of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, then the richest city in the western world. Unrest simmered in Sicily because of its very subordinate role in Charles's empire — its nobles had no share in the government of their own island and were not compensated by lucrative posts abroad, as were Charles's French, Provençal and Neapolitan subjects; also Charles spent the heavy taxes he imposed on wars outside Sicily, making Sicily somewhat of a donor economy to Charles' nascent empire. As <a href="/wiki/Steven_Runciman" title="Steven Runciman">Steven Runciman</a> put it, "[The Sicilians] saw themselves now being ruled to enable an alien tyrant make conquests from which they would have no benefit" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Runciman.2C_p._112_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Runciman.2C_p._112-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The unrest was also fomented by Byzantine agents to thwart Charles's projected invasion, and by King <a href="/wiki/Peter_III_of_Aragon" title="Peter III of Aragon">Peter III of Aragon</a>, Manfred's son-in-law, who saw his wife <a href="/wiki/Constance_of_Sicily,_Queen_of_Aragon" title="Constance of Sicily, Queen of Aragon">Constance</a> as rightful heir to the Sicilian throne.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_uprising">The uprising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The uprising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vespro22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Vespro22.jpg/220px-Vespro22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Vespro22.jpg/330px-Vespro22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Vespro22.jpg/440px-Vespro22.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vespro22.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The church of the Holy Spirit in Palermo.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The event takes its name from an insurrection which began at the start of <a href="/wiki/Vespers" title="Vespers">Vespers</a>, the sunset prayer marking the beginning of the night vigil on Easter Monday, March 30, 1282, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Spirit_(Sicily)" title="Church of the Holy Spirit (Sicily)">Church of the Holy Spirit</a> just outside <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Beginning on that night, thousands of Sicily's French inhabitants were massacred within six weeks. The events that started the uprising are not known for certain, but the various retellings have common elements. The only town in Sicily not to join the rebellion was a small village called <a href="/wiki/Sperlinga" title="Sperlinga">Sperlinga</a>, which protected French soldiers in a castle excavated in sandstone.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Steven_Runciman" title="Steven Runciman">Steven Runciman</a>, the Sicilians at the church were engaged in holiday festivities and a group of French officials came by to join in and began to drink. A sergeant named Drouet dragged a young married woman from the crowd, pestering her with his advances. Her husband then attacked Drouet with a knife, killing him. When the other Frenchmen tried to avenge their comrade, the Sicilian crowd fell upon them, killing them all. At that moment all the church bells in Palermo began to ring for Vespers. Runciman describes the mood of the night:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>To the sound of the bells messengers ran through the city calling on the men of Palermo to rise against the oppressor. At once the streets were filled with angry armed men, crying "Death to the French" ("<i>moranu li Franchiski</i>" in <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian language</a>). Every Frenchman they met was struck down. They poured into the inns frequented by the French and the houses where they dwelt, sparing neither man, woman nor child. Sicilian girls who had married Frenchmen perished with their husbands. The rioters broke into the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> convents; and all the foreign friars were dragged out and told to pronounce the word "ciciri", whose sound the French tongue could <a href="/wiki/Shibboleth" title="Shibboleth">never accurately reproduce</a>. Anyone who failed the test was slain… By the next morning some two thousand French men and women lay dead; and the rebels were in complete control of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Runciman.2C_p._115_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Runciman.2C_p._115-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Bruni" title="Leonardo Bruni">Leonardo Bruni</a> (1416), the Palermitans were holding a festival outside the city when the French came up to check for weapons, and on that pretext began to fondle the breasts of their women. This then began a riot. The French were attacked, first with rocks, then weapons, and all were killed. The news spread to other cities leading to revolt throughout Sicily. "By the time the furious anger at their insolence had drunk its fill of blood, the French had given up to the Sicilians not only their ill-gotten riches but their lives as well."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There is also a third version of the events that is quite close to Runciman's, varying only in the minor details. This story is part of the oral tradition on the island up to the present time. This oral tradition cannot be verified, but is of interest to sociologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the legend, <a href="/wiki/John_of_Procida" title="John of Procida">John of Procida</a> was the mastermind behind the conspiracy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Immediate_aftermath">Immediate aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Immediate aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After leaders were elected in Palermo, messengers spread word across the island for the rebels to strike before the French had time to organise resistance. In a fortnight the rebels gained control over most of the island, and within six weeks it was all under rebel control, except for <a href="/wiki/Messina" title="Messina">Messina</a> which was well fortified, and whose leading family, the Riso, remained faithful to Charles. But on 28 April it too broke into open revolt and, most significantly, the islanders' first act was to set fire to Charles's fleet in the harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It is reported that upon hearing of the fleet's destruction, King Charles exclaimed "Lord God, since it has pleased You to ruin my fortune, let me only go down in small steps."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles' Vicar Herbert and his family were safely within castle <a href="/wiki/Mategriffon" title="Mategriffon">Mategriffon</a>, but after negotiations the rebels granted Herbert and his family safe conduct to leave the island upon a promise that they never return. After the restoration of order in the city, the townsmen announced themselves a <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">free commune</a> answerable only to the pope. They elected leaders, one of whom was <a href="/wiki/Bartholomaeus_of_Neocastro" title="Bartholomaeus of Neocastro">Bartholomaeus of Neocastro</a> who was prominent in the unfolding events and would later chronicle much of the revolt in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%27%27Historia_Sicula%27%27&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="''Historia Sicula'' (page does not exist)">''Historia Sicula''</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historia_Sicula" title="it:Historia Sicula">it</a>)</span>, an important if sometimes contradictory source of information for historians. Again significantly, the leaders' next act was to send word, via a <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> merchant named Alafranco Cassano, to the Emperor Michael advising him that his nemesis Charles had been crippled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Runciman.2C_p._219_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Runciman.2C_p._219-13">[13]</a></sup> Only thereafter were ambassadors sent to <a href="/wiki/Pope_Martin_IV" title="Pope Martin IV">Pope Martin IV</a> pleading for each city on the island to be recognised as a free commune under the sole suzerainty of the Holy Church. The islanders were hoping for status such as enjoyed by <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a>, Genoa, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Pisa" title="Republic of Pisa">Pisa</a> and other cities, free to form their own government, but morally answerable only to the pope who would hold a vague and unstable suzerainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, the French pope was firmly in Charles' camp and he directed the Sicilians to recognize Charles as their rightful king.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> But Martin underestimated the Sicilians' hatred of the French, and especially of Charles, who ruled from Naples rather than Palermo, where he could have seen the suffering caused by his officials. Charles' island officials were far removed from his oversight; he did not see the avarice, the rape, theft and murder, nor did he see the high taxes levied against the meager possessions of the peasants, which kept them impoverished, but made no improvement in their lives.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aragonese_intervention">Aragonese intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aragonese intervention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nespory.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="498" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Nespory.jpg/275px-Nespory.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Nespory.jpg/413px-Nespory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5f/Nespory.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nespory.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Peter III Aragon, in Sicily during Vespri siciliani (1282). We can see him accompanied by his wife and her royal maids, to claim her legal rights to the throne. "Arrivo Aragonesi" (Biblioteca Vaticana)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the pope refused the rebels' pleas to allow the status of free communes, the Sicilians sent for <a href="/wiki/Peter_III_of_Aragon" title="Peter III of Aragon">Peter III of Aragon</a>, whose wife <a href="/wiki/Constance_of_Sicily,_Queen_of_Aragon" title="Constance of Sicily, Queen of Aragon">Constance</a> was <a href="/wiki/Manfred,_King_of_Sicily" title="Manfred, King of Sicily">Manfred, King of Sicily</a>'s daughter, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor</a>'s great-granddaughter and the sole surviving heir of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, who was not in captivity and was in a position to assert her rights. Pedro III championed his wife's claim to the entirety of the Kingdom of Sicily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Runciman.2C_p._201_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Runciman.2C_p._201-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the Vespers, Peter III constructed and outfitted a fleet for war. When the pope asked why he needed such a great war fleet, Pedro stated that it would be used against the followers of Islam along the northern coast of Africa, because he had legitimate trade interests there and needed to protect them. So when Peter received a request for help from the Sicilians he was conveniently on the north coast of Africa in Tunis just 200 miles across the sea from the island. At first, Peter feigned indifference to the request of the Sicilians and their plight, but after several days to allow a proper showing of deference made for the pope's consumption, he took advantage of the revolt. Peter ordered his fleet to sail for Sicily, landed at <a href="/wiki/Trapani" title="Trapani">Trapani</a> on August 30, 1282. While he marched towards Palermo, his fleet followed close by the coastal road. Peter III of Aragon's involvement changed the character of the uprising from a local revolt into a European War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Peter arrived at Palermo on September 2 and initially he was received by the populace with indifference, as merely one foreign king replacing another. However, after Pope Martin made plain his orders for the populace to accept Charles, Peter promised the islanders that they would enjoy the ancient privileges they had had under the Norman king, <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_Sicily" title="William II of Sicily">William II of Sicily</a>. Thereafter, he was accepted as a satisfactory second choice and was crowned by acclamation of the people at the cathedral in Palermo on September 4, thus becoming also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Sicily" title="Peter I of Sicily">Peter I of Sicily</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the pope's blessing, the counterattack from Charles was not long in coming; his fleet from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a> arrived and blockaded the port of Messina and made several attempts to land troops on the island, but all were repulsed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Michael_Palaeologus.27_Commentary">Michael Palaeologus' Commentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Michael Palaeologus' Commentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Years later, in his autobiography, <a href="/wiki/Michael_VIII_Palaiologos" title="Michael VIII Palaiologos">Michael VIII</a> wrote: "Should I dare to claim that I was God's instrument to bring freedom to the Sicilians, then I should only be stating the truth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPal537_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPal537-19">[19]</a></sup> But as Runciman observes, with or without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> gold, it was the proud people of Sicily alone who fought against their armed oppressor; and "However it may have been plotted and prepared, it was that one March evening of the Vespers at Palermo that brought down King Charles' empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Runciman, Steven, <i>The Sicilian Vespers</i>, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1958, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0521437741">ISBN 0-521-43774-1</a>.</li>
<li><i>Lu Rebellamentu di Sichilia, lu quale Hordinau e Fichi pari Misser Iohanni in Procita contra Re Carlu</i> is still located in the Central Library in Palermo. Whether it is a contemporary narrative or not hinges on the interpretation of one word in the text. Runciman (p. 329) describes these words as "putirini", the first person plural, vs "putirisi" the impersonal tense.</li>
<li>The earliest narrative source for the Vespers is the Sicilian language <i><a href="/wiki/Lu_rebellamentu_di_Sichilia" title="Lu rebellamentu di Sichilia">Lu rebellamentu di Sichilia</a></i>, written perhaps as early as 1287. It credits <a href="/wiki/John_of_Procida" title="John of Procida">John of Procida</a> with organising the overthrow of the French and portrays him in a positive light. Two later Tuscan <a href="/wiki/Guelphs_and_Ghibellines" title="Guelphs and Ghibellines">Guelph</a> histories, the <a href="/wiki/Liber_Jani_de_Procida_et_Palialoco" title="Liber Jani de Procida et Palialoco">Liber Jani de Procida et Palialoco</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Leggenda_di_Messer_Gianni_di_Procida" title="Leggenda di Messer Gianni di Procida">Leggenda di Messer Gianni di Procida</a></i>, possibly relying on the <i>Rebellamentu</i> or the <i>Rebellamentu</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s lost source, follow it in stressing John's involvement, but they portray him in a more critical light. The <i>Liber</i>, as its title suggests, emphasises John's negotiations with Michael VIII ("Palioloco").</li>
<li>Besides these there are two <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> chronicles of importance. The <i>Leggenda</i> was once thought to be a source for the <i><a href="/wiki/Nuova_Cronica" title="Nuova Cronica">Nuova Cronica</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Villani" title="Giovanni Villani">Giovanni Villani</a>, itself a source for the Vespers. <a href="/wiki/Brunetto_Latini" title="Brunetto Latini">Brunetto Latini</a>, in his <i>Tesoro</i>, similarly adopts the Sicilian version of events, which includes the earliest version of the rape. The Tuscan <i>Liber</i> turns the rape story around, suggesting the Sicilian woman had pulled a knife on her French suitor when his friends came to aid him.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References_in_culture">References in culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References in culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The massacre of the French garrison of <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> by the city's populace in 1302 was termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruges_Matins_(massacre)" title="Bruges Matins (massacre)">Bruges Matins</a> in tribute to the Sicilian Vespers.</li>
<li>The present (but composed in 1847 and set to music in 1848) Italian <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">National anthem</a>, "Il Canto degli Italiani", popularly known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fratelli_d%27Italia" title="Fratelli d'Italia">Fratelli d'Italia</a>" ("Brothers of Italy"): "Il suon d'ogni squilla / i vespri sonò" (with reference to the past uprisings of the Italian people against foreign rulers, occurring again in these years).</li>
<li>Reflecting the dual significance of the events to both France and Italy, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Giuseppe Verdi</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Les_v%C3%AApres_siciliennes" title="Les vêpres siciliennes">Les vêpres siciliennes</a></i> was originally written for the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Opera" title="Paris Opera">Paris Opera</a> but is usually performed in the Italian version, <i><a href="/wiki/I_vespri_siciliani" title="I vespri siciliani">I vespri siciliani</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Francesco_Hayez" title="Francesco Hayez">Francesco Hayez</a> painted a series on the Sicilian Vespers, beginning in 1821.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_term">Other uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sicilian_Vespers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In 1594, when the French King <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV</a> was taking some tedious peace negotiations with the Spanish ambassador in France, bored with the unwillingness of the Spaniards to accept his terms, he stated that the King of Spain should behave with more humility, for if not, he could easily invade Spanish territories in Italy, stating that <i>"My armies could move so fast that I would have breakfast in Milan and dine in Rome."</i> Whereupon the Spanish ambassador replied <i>"Now then, if that is so, Your Majesty would surely make it to Sicily in time for Vespers"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Having previously arranged the murder of mafia boss <a href="/wiki/Joe_Masseria" title="Joe Masseria">Joe Masseria</a> on 15 April 1931 in order to consolidate organized crime in New York City under <a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Maranzano" title="Salvatore Maranzano">Salvatore Maranzano</a>, mafia boss <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Luciano" title="Lucky Luciano">Lucky Luciano</a> then ordered the murders of Maranzano and those cappos of Maranzano and Masseria whom Luciano saw as threats. These murders allegedly occurred on September 10, 1931 which marked the end of the <a href="/wiki/Castellammarese_War" title="Castellammarese War">Castellammarese War</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and in mafia parlance is known as the <i>Night of the Sicilian Vespers</i>. This was later proved to be mostly a myth in mafia culture as no hard evidence exists that all these murders - outside of Maranzano and a few others - actually occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sicilian-born brothers David and Francis Rifugiato named their short-lived band "The Sicilian Vespers" after this event. They released one album on <a href="/wiki/Profile_Records" title="Profile Records">Profile Records</a> in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Operation_Sicilian_Vespers_(1992%E2%80%9398)" title="Operation Sicilian Vespers (1992–98)">Operation Sicilian Vespers (1992–98)</a>, an internal security operation involving the collaborative forces of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Armed_Forces" title="Italian Armed Forces">Italian Armed Forces</a> and local police in the fight against the mafia in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a></li>
</ul>
