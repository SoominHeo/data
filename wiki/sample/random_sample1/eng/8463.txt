<p>The <b>Emirate of Sicily</b> was an Islamic state (<a href="/wiki/Emirate" title="Emirate">emirate</a>) on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> which existed from 831 to 1072.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-stan-1">[1]</a></sup> Its capital was <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a>.</p>
<p>Muslims, who first invaded in 652, seized control of the entire island from the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Sicily" title="Muslim conquest of Sicily">prolonged series of conflicts</a> from 827 to 902. An Arab-Byzantine culture developed, producing a multiconfessional and multilingual state. The Emirate was conquered by Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_people" title="Norman people">Norman</a> mercenaries under <a href="/wiki/Roger_I_of_Sicily" title="Roger I of Sicily">Roger I of Sicily</a>, who founded the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Sicily" title="County of Sicily">County of Sicily</a> in 1071.</p>
<p>Sicilian Muslims remained citizens of the multi-ethnic County and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a>, until those who had not already converted were expelled in the 1240s. Until the late 12th century, and probably as late as the 1220s, Muslims formed the majority of the island's population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Their influence remains in some elements of the <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian language</a>, as well as surnames and locations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Arab_invasions_of_Sicily">First Arab invasions of Sicily</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: First Arab invasions of Sicily">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 535, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Byzantine_Emperors" title="List of Byzantine Emperors">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> returned <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, now ruled from <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> exclusively. As the power of what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> waned in the West, Sicily was invaded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> forces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uthman_Ibn_Affan" title="Uthman Ibn Affan">Caliph Uthman</a> in the year 652. However, this first invasion was short-lived, and the Arabs left soon after. By the end of the 7th century, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_North_Africa" title="Umayyad conquest of North Africa">Umayyad conquest of North Africa</a>, the Arabs had captured the nearby port city of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, allowing them to build shipyards and a permanent base from which to launch more sustained attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmsmi_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dmsmi-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 700, the island of <a href="/wiki/Pantelleria" title="Pantelleria">Pantelleria</a> was captured by Arabs, and it was only discord among the Arabs that prevented an attempted invasion of Sicily coming next. Instead, trading agreements were arranged with the Byzantines, and Arab merchants were allowed to trade goods at the Sicilian ports. Attacks from Muslim fleets were repeated in 703, 728, 729, 730, 731, 733 and 734, the last two meeting with a substantial Byzantine resistance.</p>
<p>The first true conquest expedition was launched in 740: in that year the Muslim prince <a href="/wiki/Habib" title="Habib">Habib</a><sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (April 2015)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, who had participated on the 728 attack, successfully captured <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Sicily" title="Syracuse, Sicily">Syracuse</a>. Ready to conquer the whole island, they were however forced to return to <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_revolt" title="Berber revolt">Berber revolt</a>. A second attack in 752 aimed only to sack the same city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolt_of_Euphemius_and_gradual_Muslim_conquest_of_the_island">Revolt of Euphemius and gradual Muslim conquest of the island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Revolt of Euphemius and gradual Muslim conquest of the island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Sicily" title="Muslim conquest of Sicily">Muslim conquest of Sicily</a></div>
<p>In 826 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphemius_(King_of_Sicily)" title="Euphemius (King of Sicily)">Euphemius</a>, the commander of the Byzantine fleet of Sicily, forced a nun to marry him. Emperor <a href="/wiki/Michael_II" title="Michael II">Michael II</a> caught wind of the matter and ordered that General Constantine end the marriage and cut off Euphemius' nose. Euphemius rose up, killed Constantine and then occupied Syracuse; he in turn was defeated and driven out to <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stan_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-stan-1">[1]</a></sup> He offered rule of Sicily over to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ziyadat_Allah_I_of_Aghlabids" title="Ziyadat Allah I of Aghlabids">Ziyadat Allah</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aghlabid" title="Aghlabid">Aghlabid</a> Emir of <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> in return for a place as a general and safety; an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> army was sent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stan_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-stan-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The latter agreed to conquer Sicily, promising to give it to Euphemius in exchange for a yearly tribute, and entrusted its conquest to the 70-year-old qadi <a href="/wiki/Asad_ibn_al-Furat" title="Asad ibn al-Furat">Asad ibn al-Furat</a>. The Muslim force counted 10,000 infantry, 700 cavalry and 100 ships, reinforced by Euphemius' ships and, after the landing at <a href="/wiki/Mazara_del_Vallo" title="Mazara del Vallo">Mazara del Vallo</a>. A first battle against the loyal Byzantine troops occurred on July 15, 827, near Mazara, resulting in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aghlabid" title="Aghlabid">Aghlabid</a> victory.</p>
<p>Asad subsequently conquered the southern shore of the island and laid siege to <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Sicily" title="Syracuse, Sicily">Syracuse</a>. After <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Syracuse_(827%E2%80%93828)" title="Siege of Syracuse (827–828)">a year of siege</a>, and an attempted mutiny, his troops were however able to defeat a large army sent from <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a>, also backed by a Venetian fleet led by Doge <a href="/wiki/Giustiniano_Participazio" title="Giustiniano Participazio">Giustiniano Participazio</a>. But when a plague killed many of the Muslim troops, as well as Asad himself, the Muslims retreated to the castle of <a href="/wiki/Mineo" title="Mineo">Mineo</a>. Later they returned to the offensive, but failed to conquer <a href="/wiki/Enna" title="Enna">Castrogiovanni</a> (the modern Enna, where Euphemius died) and retreated back to Mazara.</p>
<p>In 830 they received a strong reinforcement of 30,000 <a href="/wiki/Ifriqiya" title="Ifriqiya">Ifriqiyan</a> and Andalusian troops. The Iberian Muslims defeated the Byzantine commander Teodotus in July–August of that year, but again a plague forced them to return to Mazara and then to <a href="/wiki/Ifriqiya" title="Ifriqiya">Ifriqiya</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ifriqiya" title="Ifriqiya">Ifriqiyan</a> units sent to besiege <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> managed to capture it after a year long siege in September 831.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Palermo became the Muslim capital of Sicily, renamed al-Madinah ("The City").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alatas_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-alatas-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The conquest was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See-saw" title="See-saw">see-saw</a> affair; with considerable resistance and many internal struggles, it took over a century for Byzantine Sicily to be conquered. Syracuse held out for a long time but <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Syracuse_(877%E2%80%93878)" title="Siege of Syracuse (877–878)">fell</a> in 878, <a href="/wiki/Taormina" title="Taormina">Taormina</a> fell in 902, and the last Byzantine outpost was taken in 965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stan_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-stan-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Period_as_an_Emirate">Period as an Emirate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Period as an Emirate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1474" data-file-width="1874" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG/250px-Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG/375px-Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG/500px-Arabo-NormanArchitecture.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>

<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab-Norman_culture" title="Arab-Norman culture">Arab-Norman</a> art and architecture combined Occidental features (such as the Classical pillars and friezes) with typical <a href="/wiki/Islamic_art" title="Islamic art">Islamic decorations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calligraphy" title="Islamic calligraphy">calligraphy</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In succession Sicily was ruled by the Sunni <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aghlabid" title="Aghlabid">Aghlabid</a> dynasty in <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and the Shiite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">Fatimids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. However, throughout this period, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> Muslims formed the majority of the Muslim community in Sicily,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with most (if not all) of the people of Palermo being Sunni,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> leading to their hostility to the Shia Kalbids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Sunni population of the island was replenished following sectarian rebellions across north Africa from 943-47 against the Fatimids harsh religious policies, leading to several waves of refugees fleeing to Sicily in an attempt to escape Fatimid retaliation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Byzantines took advantage of temporary discord to occupy the eastern end of the island for several years.</p>
<p>After suppressing a revolt the Fatimid caliph <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismail_al-Mansur" title="Ismail al-Mansur">Ismail al-Mansur</a> appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Hasan_al-Kalbi" title="Al-Hasan al-Kalbi">al-Hasan al-Kalbi</a> (948–964) as Emir of Sicily. He successfully managed to control the continuously revolting Byzantines and founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalbid" title="Kalbid">Kalbid</a> dynasty. Raids into <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italy</a> continued under the Kalbids into the 11th century, and in 982 a German army under <a href="/wiki/Otto_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto II, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto II, Holy Roman Emperor</a> was defeated near Crotone in <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>. With Emir <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yusuf_al-Kalbi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yusuf al-Kalbi (page does not exist)">Yusuf al-Kalbi</a> (986–998) a period of steady decline began. Under al-Akhal (1017–1037) the dynastic conflict intensified, with factions within the ruling family allying themselves variously with the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirid" title="Zirid">Zirids</a>. After this period, <a href="/wiki/Al-Mu%27izz_ibn_Badis" title="Al-Mu'izz ibn Badis">Al-Mu'izz ibn Badis</a> attempted to annex the island for the Zirids, while intervening in the affairs of the feuding Muslims; however, the attempt ultimately failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sicily_under_Muslim_rule">Sicily under Muslim rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sicily under Muslim rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The new Muslim rulers initiated land reforms which in turn, increased productivity and encouraged the growth of smallholdings, a dent to the dominance of the landed estates. The Arabs further improved irrigation systems through <a href="/wiki/Qanat" title="Qanat">qanats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pistachio" title="Pistachio">pistachio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> were introduced to Sicily. A description of <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> was given by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Hawqal" title="Ibn Hawqal">Ibn Hawqal</a>, a Baghdad merchant who visited Sicily in 950. A walled suburb called the Kasr (the palace) is the center of Palermo until today, with the great Friday mosque on the site of the later Roman cathedral. The suburb of Al-Khalisa (<a href="/wiki/Kalsa" title="Kalsa">Kalsa</a>) contained the Sultan's palace, baths, a mosque, government offices, and a private prison. Ibn Hawqual reckoned 7,000 individual butchers trading in 150 shops. By 1050, Palermo had a population of 350,000, making it one of the largest cities in Europe, second only to <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Islamic Spain</a>'s capital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Córdoba, Spain">Cordova</a>, which had a population of 450,000. In contrast, under the succeeding Christian <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a>, Palermo's population dropped to 150,000, though it was still the largest city in Europe due to the greater decline in Cordova's population; by 1330 Palermo's population had declined to 51,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Long_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Long-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arab traveler, geographer, and poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Jubair" title="Ibn Jubair">Ibn Jubair</a> visited the area in the end of the 12th century and described Al-Kasr and Al-Khalisa (Kalsa):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The capital is endowed with two gifts, splendor and wealth. It contains all the real and imagined beauty that anyone could wish. Splendor and grace adorn the piazzas and the countryside; the streets and highways are wide, and the eye is dazzled by the beauty of its situation. It is a city full of marvels, with buildings similar to those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Córdoba, Spain">Cordoba</a> [sic], built of limestone. A permanent stream of water from four springs runs through the city. There are so many mosques that they are impossible to count. Most of them also serve as schools. The eye is dazzled by all this splendor.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Throughout this reign, continued revolts by Byzantine Sicilians occurred, especially in the east, and part of the lands were even re-occupied before being quashed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpriv_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpriv-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The local population conquered by the Muslims were Romanized Catholic Sicilians in Western Sicily and Greek speaking Christians mainly in the eastern half of the island, but there were also a significant number of Jews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Christians and Jews were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_tolerance" title="Religious tolerance">tolerated</a> under Muslim rule as <a href="/wiki/Dhimmi" title="Dhimmi">dhimmi</a>, but were subject to some restrictions. The dhimmi were also required to pay the <a href="/wiki/Jizya" title="Jizya">jizya</a>, or poll tax, and the <a href="/wiki/Kharaj" title="Kharaj">kharaj</a> or land tax, but were exempt from the tax that Muslims had to pay (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zakaat" title="Zakaat">Zakaat</a>). Under Arab rule there were different categories of Jizya payers, but their common denominator was the payment of the Jizya as a mark of subjection to Muslim rule in exchange for protection against foreign and internal aggression. The conquered population could avoid this subservient status simply by converting to Islam. Whether by honest religious conviction or societal compulsion large numbers of native Sicilians converted to Islam. However, even after 100 years of Islamic rule, numerous Greek speaking Christian communities prospered, especially in north-eastern Sicily, as dhimmi. This was largely a result of the Jizya system which allowed co-existence. This co-existence with the conquered population fell apart after the reconquest of Sicily, particularly following the death of King <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_Sicily" title="William II of Sicily">William II of Sicily</a> in 1189.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Norman Kingdom of Sicily">Norman Kingdom of Sicily</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Emirate of Sicily began to fragment as intra-dynastic quarrels took place within the Muslim regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stan_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-stan-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1044, under emir <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hasan_al-Samsam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hasan al-Samsam (page does not exist)">Hasan al-Samsam</a>, the island fragmented into four <a href="/wiki/Qadi" title="Qadi">qadits</a>, or small fiefdoms: the qadit of Trapani, Marsala, Mazara and Sciacca; that of Girgenti, Castrogiovanni and Castronuovo; that of Palermo and Catania; and that of Syracuse. By 1065, all of them had been unified by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ayyub_ibn_Tamim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ayyub ibn Tamim (page does not exist)">Ayyub ibn Tamim</a>, the son of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirid" title="Zirid">Zirid</a> emir of Ifriqiyya. In 1068 he left Sicily, and what remained under Muslim control fell under two qadits: one, led by Ibn Abbad (known as Benavert in western chronicles) in Syracuse, and the other under Hammud in <a href="/wiki/Enna" title="Enna">Qas'r Ianni</a> (modern Enna).</p>
<p>By the 11th century mainland southern Italian powers were hiring <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> mercenaries, who were Christian descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>; it was the Normans under <a href="/wiki/Roger_I_of_Sicily" title="Roger I of Sicily">Roger I</a> who captured Sicily from the Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stan_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-stan-1">[1]</a></sup> The Norman <a href="/wiki/Robert_Guiscard" title="Robert Guiscard">Robert Guiscard</a>, son of Tancred, <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">invaded Sicily</a> in 1060. The island was split between three Arab <a href="/wiki/Emirate" title="Emirate">emirs</a>, and the sizable Christian population rose up against the ruling Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> After taking <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, Roger I occupied <a href="/wiki/Messina" title="Messina">Messina</a> with an army of 700 knights. In 1068, <a href="/wiki/Roger_I_of_Sicily" title="Roger I of Sicily">Roger de Hauteville</a> and his men defeated the Muslims at <a href="/wiki/Misilmeri" title="Misilmeri">Misilmeri</a>. Catania fell in 1071, followed, after one year of siege, by Palermo in 1072. The Normans captured Syracuse in 1086.</p>
<p>The loss of the main port cities dealt a severe blow to Muslim power on the island. The city of Qas'r Ianni was still ruled by its emir, Ibn Al-Hawas, who held out for years. His successor, Hamud, surrendered, and converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, only in 1087. After his conversion, Ibn Hamud subsequently became part of the Christian nobility and retired with his family to an estate in <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a> provided by Roger I. In 1091, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butera,_Italy" title="Butera, Italy">Butera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noto" title="Noto">Noto</a> in the southern tip of Sicily and the island of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, the last Arab strongholds, fell to the Christians with ease. After the conquest of Sicily, the Normans removed the local emir, Yusuf Ibn Abdallah from power, but did so by respecting Arab customs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-initalymag_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-initalymag-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Norman-Arab-Byzantine_culture" title="Norman-Arab-Byzantine culture">Norman-Arab-Byzantine culture</a></div>
<p>The Norman <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_II" title="Roger II">Roger II</a> has been characterized as multi-ethnic in nature and religiously tolerant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Normans, Jews, Muslim Arabs, Byzantine Greeks, <a href="/wiki/Lombards_of_Sicily" title="Lombards of Sicily">Lombards</a> and native Sicilians lived in relative harmony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> remained a language of government and administration for at least a century into Norman rule, and traces remain in the <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">language of Sicily</a> and evidently more in the <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">language of Malta</a> today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dmsmi_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dmsmi-7">[7]</a></sup> The Muslims also maintained their domination of industry, retailing and production, while Muslim artisans and expert knowledge in government and administration were highly sought after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, once the Normans had conquered the island, the Muslims were faced with the choice of voluntary departure or subjection to Christian rule. Many Muslims chose to leave, provided they had the means to do so. “The transformation of Sicily into a Christian island”, remarks Abulafia, “was also, paradoxically, the work of those whose culture was under threat”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Despite the presence of an Arab-speaking Christian population, Muslim peasants received baptism from the Roman and Greek Christians and adopted even Greek Christian names; in several instances, Christian serfs with Greek names listed in the Monreale registers had living Muslim parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the Norman rulers followed a policy of steady Latinization. Some Muslims chose the option of feigning conversion, but such a remedy could only provide individual protection and could not sustain a community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>‘Lombard’ pogroms against Muslims started in the 1160s. Muslim and Christian communities in Sicily became increasingly geographically separated. The island’s Muslim communities were mainly isolated beyond an internal frontier which divided the south-western half of the island from the Christian north-east. Sicilian Muslims, a subject population, were dependent on the mercy of their Christian masters and, ultimately, on royal protection. When <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_Sicily" title="William II of Sicily">King William the Good</a> died in 1189, this royal protection was lifted, and the door was opened for widespread attacks against the island’s Muslims. This destroyed any lingering hope of coexistence, however unequal the respective populations might have been. <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry VI</a>’s death in 1197, and that of his wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constance_of_Sicily" title="Constance of Sicily">Constance</a> a year later, plunged Sicily into political turmoil. With the loss of royal protection and with <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> still an infant in papal custody, Sicily became a battleground for rival German and papal forces. The island’s Muslim rebels sided with German warlords like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markward_von_Anweiler" title="Markward von Anweiler">Markward von Anweiler</a>. In response, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_III" title="Pope Innocent III">Innocent III</a> declared a crusade against Markward, alleging that he had made an unholy alliance with the Saracens of Sicily. Nevertheless, in 1206 that same pope attempted to convince the Muslim leaders to remain loyal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> By this time, the Muslim rebellion was critical, with Muslims in control of Jato, Entella, Platani, Celso, Calatrasi, Corleone (taken in 1208), Guastanella and Cinisi. In other words, the Muslim revolt extended throughout a whole stretch of western Sicily. The rebels were led by Muhammad Ibn Abbād. He called himself the ‘prince of believers’, struck his own coins, and attempted to find Muslim support from other parts of the Muslim world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cliohres.net_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-cliohres.net-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a>, no longer a child, responded by launching a series of campaigns against the Muslim rebels in 1221. The <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen" title="Hohenstaufen">Hohenstaufen</a> forces rooted out the defenders of Jato, Entella, and the other fortresses. Rather than exterminate the Muslims, in 1223, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> and the Christians began the first deportations of Muslims to <a href="/wiki/Lucera" title="Lucera">Lucera</a> in Apulia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> A year later, expeditions were sent against Malta and Djerba, to establish royal control and prevent their Muslim populations from helping the rebels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cliohres.net_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-cliohres.net-30">[30]</a></sup> Paradoxically, Saracen archers were a common component of these “Christian” armies from this era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenstaufen" title="House of Hohenstaufen">House of Hohenstaufen</a> and their successors (<a href="/wiki/Capetian_House_of_Anjou" title="Capetian House of Anjou">Capetian House of Anjou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Aragonese</a> <a href="/wiki/House_of_Barcelona" title="House of Barcelona">House of Barcelona</a>) gradually "Latinized" Sicily over the course of two centuries, and this social process laid the groundwork for the introduction of Latin (as opposed to Byzantine) Catholicism. The process of Latinization was fostered largely by the Roman Church and its liturgy. The annihilation of Islam in Sicily was completed by the late 1240s, when the final deportations to <a href="/wiki/Lucera" title="Lucera">Lucera</a> took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
