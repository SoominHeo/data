<p>The <b>City of Sunderland</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_government_district" title="Local government district">local government district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a>, in <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, with the status of a <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a>. It is named after its largest settlement, <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a>, but covers a far larger area which includes the towns of <a href="/wiki/Hetton-le-Hole" title="Hetton-le-Hole">Hetton-le-Hole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houghton-le-Spring" title="Houghton-le-Spring">Houghton-le-Spring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Washington, Tyne and Wear">Washington</a>, and a range of suburban villages.</p>
<p>The district was formed in 1973, titled the <i>Metropolitan Borough of Sunderland</i>, as part of the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> and is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amalgamation_(politics)" title="Amalgamation (politics)">amalgamation</a> of four former local government districts of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>. It was granted city status in 1991, the 40th anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen Elizabeth II's</a> accession. The city had a population of 275,300 at the time of the 2011 census, with the majority of the population (174,286) residing in <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BUA-2">[2]</a></sup> The 'Sunderland Built-up Area' (including <a href="/wiki/Whitburn,_South_Tyneside" title="Whitburn, South Tyneside">Whitburn</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Tyneside" title="South Tyneside">South Tyneside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chester-le-Street" title="Chester-le-Street">Chester-le-Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ouston,_County_Durham" title="Ouston, County Durham">Ouston</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Hetton" title="South Hetton">South Hetton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelton,_County_Durham" title="Pelton, County Durham">Pelton</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>) is quoted alternatively as having a population of around 335,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BUA-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also known by its nickname 'Sunny D'.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The metropolitan borough was formed in 1973 under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> by the merger of several districts of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> - <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Washington, Tyne and Wear">Washington Urban District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houghton-le-Spring" title="Houghton-le-Spring">Houghton-le-Spring Urban District</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hetton-le-Hole" title="Hetton-le-Hole">Hetton Urban District</a> - with the <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">County Borough of Sunderland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The metropolitan borough was granted <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city status</a> in 1991, the 40th anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen's</a> accession. At the Queen's Golden Jubilee the city petitioned to be allowed a <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor" title="Lord Mayor">Lord Mayor</a>, but was unsuccessful. Although the city does not have a <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">Cathedral</a>, as it is located in the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Durham" title="Diocese of Durham">Diocese of Durham</a>, it does have <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_Minster" title="Sunderland Minster">Sunderland Minster</a>.</p>
<p>Between 1939 and 1945 the Wear yards launched 245 merchant ships totalling 1.5 million tons, a quarter of the merchant tonnage produced in the UK at this period. Competition from overseas caused a downturn in demand for Sunderland built ships toward the end of the 20th century. The last shipyard in Sunderland closed in 1988.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/HMS_Ocean_(L12)" title="HMS Ocean (L12)">HMS Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s biggest <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warship</a>, is Sunderland's adopted ship. In March 2004 it was granted the freedom of the City. <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Biscop" title="Benedict Biscop">St Benedict Biscop</a> was adopted as the City's <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">Patron</a> <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">Saint</a> in March 2004.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="City_government">City government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: City government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_review_2004">Boundary review 2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Boundary review 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like all metropolitan authorities, the city is divided into a number of wards or <i>electoral districts</i>. Each has three councillors elected for a four-year term. One third of councillors face re-election each year, and no city elections are held in the fourth year of the election cycle - this was when each ward's sole metropolitan county councillor was elected, prior to the county council's abolition in 1986.</p>
<p>The City has 25 such wards. When the boundaries of these wards were set in 1982, each ward had a roughly equal population. By 2004 there had been a considerable shift in population. In particular, the east and south east – the old parish of Sunderland and the mining village of Ryhope – had lost population to the "New Town" of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Washington, Tyne and Wear">Washington</a>. As a result, the boundaries were redrawn; Sunderland lost one ward and Washington gained one. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Council_election,_2004" title="Sunderland Council election, 2004">2004 election</a> for all 75 councillors was held on 10 June 2004.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> created two different two-tier systems for local administration, with different division of functions. As a <i>metropolitan</i> authority, Sunderland retained responsibility for waste collection, although disposal of the garbage was a county function, and for education. In 1986, when <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a> Metropolitan County Council was abolished, most county functions became the responsibility of the constituent districts, whilst new <i><a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom#Joint-boards" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">joint boards</a></i> assumed the co-ordinating roles in the county. Thus control over economic development, of the Museums and Archive Service, of the Tyne and Wear Fire and Civil Defence Authority and of the Passenger Transport Authority is exercised not by committees of directly elected county councillors but by nominees chosen by the elected members of the five district authorities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sunderland has not had a separate police force since 1967, when the Borough of Sunderland Police merged with Durham Constabulary. The City is now part of the <a href="/wiki/Northumbria_Police" title="Northumbria Police">Northumbria Police</a> Force area. This force was set up in 1973, and covers the whole of Tyne and Wear plus the much larger but much less densely populated county of <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>.</p>
<p>The city is unparished, except for <a href="/wiki/Hetton-le-Hole" title="Hetton-le-Hole">Hetton-le-Hole</a> which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a>, and which has a <a href="/wiki/Town_council" title="Town council">town council</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_structure">Political structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_local_elections" title="Sunderland local elections">Sunderland local elections</a></div>
<p>The City has had a <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> controlled council since 1973, and often before that. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Council_election,_2004" title="Sunderland Council election, 2004">elections of May 2003</a> the political structure was 63 Labour, 9 <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a>, and 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" title="Independent (politician)">independent</a>. The only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(modern)" title="Liberal Party (modern)">Liberal Party</a> councillor sat with the only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> as a "Liberal/Democrat" group. Three Councillors resigned from the Labour Party following disputes over the selection of candidates for the 2004 elections. Two became independent members; one joined the Liberal Democrat party, becoming group leader after the 2004 municipal elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reorganisation of electoral areas saw major changes in all but two wards, but the elections of 10 June 2004, the first fought under the new boundaries, saw little change in the political representation of the City as a whole, with 61 Labour, 12 Conservative, and 2 Liberal Democrat councillors elected.</p>
<p>The Conservative party won all three seats in a new ward whose boundaries spanned several old wards. The Liberal Democrats stronghold ward was abolished entirely, and became part of four new wards, and the Liberal Democrat councillors elected in 2004 were from two very different areas.</p>
<p>Following further disputes within the Labour Party, 3 councillors resigned the whip at the end of 2006 and, joined by one of the liberal democrat members formed an independent group, which is the minority party in opposition.</p>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Council_election,_2007" title="Sunderland Council election, 2007">elections of May 2007</a>, the Labour Party continued their decline. Labour lost three seats to the Conservatives, whilst former Labour Mayor Mrs Julianna Heron lost to an independent: the same person who had defeated her husband in 2003 and who was in turn defeated by him in 2004. The Conservative party made further significant gains in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Council_election,_2008" title="Sunderland Council election, 2008">2008 election</a>, gaining five additional seats and strengthening their support in others.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Council_election,_2010" title="Sunderland Council election, 2010">election in 2010</a>, held at the same time as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">general election</a> saw Labour gain 4 seats to increase their majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-change-5">[5]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Council_election,_2011" title="Sunderland Council election, 2011">2011 local elections</a> Labour gained a further four seats from the Conservatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hold_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-hold-6">[6]</a></sup> After the 2015 General election the composition of the council was Labour 66, Conservative 6 and independent 3 .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hold_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-hold-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_constituencies">Parliamentary constituencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Parliamentary constituencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since major boundary changes in 2010, there are three constituencies covering the City of Sunderland, all currently regarded as safe for the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houghton_and_Sunderland_South" title="Houghton and Sunderland South">Houghton and Sunderland South</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_Central" title="Sunderland Central">Sunderland Central</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_and_Sunderland_West" title="Washington and Sunderland West">Washington and Sunderland West</a></li>
</ul>
<p>In the 1991, 1997, 2001 and 2005 General Elections, the former Sunderland South constituency was the first to declare the results, and the tradition was continued by the new Houghton and Sunderland South seat in 2010.</p>
<p>The whole City of Sunderland is within the <a href="/wiki/North_East_England_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="North East England (European Parliament constituency)">North East England</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliamentary</a> constituency.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twin_towns">Twin towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Twin towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sunderland is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_twinning" title="Town twinning">twinned</a> with: <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a>, UK, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essen,_Germany" title="Essen, Germany">Essen, Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Nazaire" title="Saint-Nazaire">Saint-Nazaire</a>, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twin_towns_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-twin_towns-7">[7]</a></sup> It also has Friendship Agreements with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>, USA; and <a href="/wiki/Harbin" title="Harbin">Harbin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, both in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twin_towns_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-twin_towns-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sunderland and Washington share historical links, as the ancestors of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidents_of_the_USA" title="Presidents of the USA">President</a> of the United States of America, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, lived in <a href="/wiki/Washington_Old_Hall" title="Washington Old Hall">Washington Old Hall</a>, which is within the <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipal</a> boundaries of Sunderland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialect">Dialect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dialect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mackem" title="Mackem">Mackem</a></div>
<p>The Spackem accent and dialect is often mistaken for <a href="/wiki/Geordie" title="Geordie">Geordie</a> by people not from the region, as the two tongues sound exactly the same in pronunciation and diction. The dialect also has several variations between different areas of the City, as demonstrated when the hoax tapes purporting to be of the Yorkshire Ripper were analysed. The tapes were made by a man who came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Wearside_Jack" title="Wearside Jack">Wearside Jack</a>, and were thought by linguistic experts to be made by someone specifically from the Castletown area of Sunderland. When the perpetrator, <a href="/wiki/Wearside_Jack" title="Wearside Jack">John Humble</a> was eventually caught he confirmed that he did indeed attend school in the Castletown area of Sunderland in his childhood.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_literature">Art and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Art and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Walrussundcolour1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="533" data-file-width="800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7b/Walrussundcolour1.jpg/220px-Walrussundcolour1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7b/Walrussundcolour1.jpg/330px-Walrussundcolour1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7b/Walrussundcolour1.jpg/440px-Walrussundcolour1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Walrussundcolour1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a> was a frequent visitor to the area. He wrote most of <i><a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">Jabberwocky</a></i> at <a href="/wiki/Whitburn,_South_Tyneside" title="Whitburn, South Tyneside">Whitburn</a> as well as "<a href="/wiki/The_Walrus_and_the_Carpenter" title="The Walrus and the Carpenter">The Walrus and the Carpenter</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some parts of the area are also widely believed to be the inspiration for his Alice in Wonderland stories, such as Hylton Castle and Backhouse Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There is a statue to Carroll in Whitburn library. Lewis Carroll was also a visitor to the Rectory of Holy Trinity Church, Southwick; then a township independent of Sunderland. Carroll's connection with Sunderland, and the area's history, is documented in <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Talbot" title="Bryan Talbot">Bryan Talbot</a>'s 2007 graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Sunderland" title="Alice in Sunderland">Alice in Sunderland</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> More recently, Sunderland-born <a href="/wiki/Terry_Deary" title="Terry Deary">Terry Deary</a>, writer of the series of <i><a href="/wiki/Horrible_Histories" title="Horrible Histories">Horrible Histories</a></i> books, has achieved fame and success, and many others such as thriller writer <a href="/wiki/Sheila_Quigley" title="Sheila Quigley">Sheila Quigley</a>, are following his lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Salford-born painter, <a href="/wiki/L._S._Lowry" title="L. S. Lowry">L. S. Lowry</a>, was a frequent visitor, staying in the Seaburn Hotel in Sunderland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Many of his paintings of seascapes and shipbuilding are based on Wearside scenes. The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Gallery_for_Contemporary_Art" title="Northern Gallery for Contemporary Art">Northern Gallery for Contemporary Art</a> on Fawcett Street and <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_Museum_and_Winter_Gardens" title="Sunderland Museum and Winter Gardens">Sunderland Museum and Winter Gardens</a> showcase exhibitions and installations from up-and-coming and established artists alike, with the latter holding an extensive collection of Lowry's work. The National Glass Centre on Liberty Way also exhibits a number of glass sculptures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>-based writer, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dan_W.Griffin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dan W.Griffin (page does not exist)">Dan W.Griffin</a> was a student at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_University" title="Sunderland University">Sunderland University</a> between 1991 and 1995. His experiences are described in his book, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=No_stranger_to_the_P45&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="No stranger to the P45 (page does not exist)">No stranger to the P45</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bands_and_musicians_from_Yorkshire_and_North_East_England" title="Bands and musicians from Yorkshire and North East England">Bands and musicians from Yorkshire and North East England</a></div>
<p>Sunderland has produced a modest number of musicians that have gone on to reach international fame, most notably <a href="/wiki/David_A._Stewart" title="David A. Stewart">Dave Stewart</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eurythmics" title="Eurythmics">Eurythmics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kenickie" title="Kenickie">Kenickie</a>, which featured <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Laverne" title="Lauren Laverne">Lauren Laverne</a> on vocals, also achieved a top ten album and wide critical acclaim in the mid-to-late-1990s. In recent years, a thriving <a href="/wiki/Underground_music" title="Underground music">underground music</a> scene in Sunderland has helped the likes of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Smalltown_Heroes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Smalltown Heroes (page does not exist)">Smalltown Heroes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_futureheads" title="The futureheads">The futureheads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_Music" title="Field Music">Field Music</a> and more recently <a href="/wiki/Frankie_%26_The_Heartstrings" title="Frankie &amp; The Heartstrings">Frankie &amp; The Heartstrings</a> gain national recognition.</p>
<p>Other famous Sunderland musicians include punk rockers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Toy_Dolls" title="The Toy Dolls">The Toy Dolls</a>, who broke the top five of the charts with "Nellie the Elephant" in December 1984; the punk rock band <a href="/wiki/Leatherface" title="Leatherface">Leatherface</a>; the lead singer of dance outfit <a href="/wiki/Olive_(band)" title="Olive (band)">Olive</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruth_Ann_Boyle" title="Ruth Ann Boyle">Ruth Ann Boyle</a>, who achieved a UK chart-topper with "You're Not Alone" in May 1997, and has gone on to work with fellow chart-toppers <a href="/wiki/Enigma_(UK_band)" title="Enigma (UK band)">Enigma</a>; <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_of_Toffs" title="A Tribe of Toffs">A Tribe of Toffs</a> made number 21 with their cult hit "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Kettley_is_a_Weatherman" title="John Kettley is a Weatherman">John Kettley is a Weatherman</a>" in December 1988.</p>
<p>On 7 and 8 May 2005, Sunderland played host to <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Weekend" title="Big Weekend">Big Weekend</a> concert - the UK's largest free music festival. The event, held at <a href="/wiki/Herrington" title="Herrington">Herrington Country Park</a> in the shadow of <a href="/wiki/Penshaw_Monument" title="Penshaw Monument">Penshaw Monument</a>, was attended by 30,000 visitors and featured <a href="/wiki/Foo_Fighters" title="Foo Fighters">Foo Fighters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kasabian" title="Kasabian">Kasabian</a>, <a href="/wiki/KT_Tunstall" title="KT Tunstall">KT Tunstall</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_Brothers" title="Chemical Brothers">Chemical Brothers</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Eyed_Peas" title="The Black Eyed Peas">The Black Eyed Peas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Empire Theatre sometimes plays host to music acts. In 2009, it hosts <a href="/wiki/Jane_McDonald" title="Jane McDonald">Jane McDonald</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Drifters" title="The Drifters">The Drifters</a> among others.</p>
<p>The Sunderland <a href="/wiki/Stadium_of_Light" title="Stadium of Light">Stadium of Light</a> has hosted regular concerts since 2009, including the likes of <a href="/wiki/Oasis_(band)" title="Oasis (band)">Oasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Take_That" title="Take That">Take That</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Leon" title="Kings of Leon">Kings of Leon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunderland_Empire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="800" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Sunderland_Empire.jpg/220px-Sunderland_Empire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Sunderland_Empire.jpg/330px-Sunderland_Empire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Sunderland_Empire.jpg/440px-Sunderland_Empire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The Sunderland Empire theatre.</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_Empire_Theatre" title="Sunderland Empire Theatre">Sunderland Empire Theatre</a>, opened in 1907, is the largest theatre in the North East, reopened in December 2004 following a major redevelopment allowing it to stage <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">West End</a> shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Saigon" title="Miss Saigon">Miss Saigon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Starlight_Express" title="Starlight Express">Starlight Express</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady" title="My Fair Lady">My Fair Lady</a></i>, all of which have been performed at the Empire. The Empire is the only theatre between Leeds and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> large enough to accommodate such shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It has also played host to an annual season from the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Royal_Ballet" title="Birmingham Royal Ballet">Birmingham Royal Ballet</a> for over ten years.</p>
<p>The Royalty Theatre is the home of the amateur Royalty Theatre group who also put on a number of low-budget productions throughout the year. Renowned film producer <a href="/wiki/David_Parfitt" title="David Parfitt">David Parfitt</a> belonged to this company before achieving worldwide fame and is now a patron of the theatre.</p>
<p>The Empire also played host to the final performance of comic actor <a href="/wiki/Sid_James" title="Sid James">Sid James</a>, who died of a heart attack whilst on stage in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sunderland is home to a <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Motor_Manufacturing_UK" title="Nissan Motor Manufacturing UK">Nissan car manufacturing plant</a>.</p>
<p>Over recent years Sunderland city centre has seen a re-development of the <a href="/wiki/Sunniside,_Sunderland" title="Sunniside, Sunderland">Sunniside</a> area taking place, with new bars, cafes and retailers opening their doors on the Eastern side of the city. The Sunniside area now includes an Empire Cinema, Gala Casino and many surrounding eateries.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Bridges" title="The Bridges">The Bridges</a> Shopping Centre covers much of the City Centre and was opened in 1988. It is currently owned by Land Securities Group after they purchased the site in 1990. The centre receives over 26 million shopping visits every year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media.2C_internet.2C_film_and_television">Media, internet, film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Media, internet, film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sunderland has two local newspapers: the daily evening tabloid <i>The <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_Echo" title="Sunderland Echo">Sunderland Echo</a></i>, founded in 1873, and the <i>Sunderland Star</i> - a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_daily_newspaper" title="Free daily newspaper">free newspaper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It also has its own <a href="/wiki/Independent_Local_Radio" title="Independent Local Radio">local radio</a> station <a href="/wiki/Sun_FM" title="Sun FM">Sun FM</a>, a community radio station <a href="/wiki/Spark_FM" title="Spark FM">Spark FM</a> and a hospital radio station - <a href="/wiki/Radio_Sunderland_for_Hospitals" title="Radio Sunderland for Hospitals">Radio Sunderland for Hospitals</a>, and can receive other north-eastern independent radio stations <a href="/wiki/Metro_Radio" title="Metro Radio">Metro Radio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_1152" title="Magic 1152">Magic 1152</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxy_North_East" title="Galaxy North East">Galaxy North East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Century_Radio" title="Century Radio">Century Radio</a>. The current regional BBC radio station is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Newcastle" title="BBC Radio Newcastle">BBC Radio Newcastle</a>. The regional DAB multiplex for the Sunderland area is operated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bauer_Digital_Radio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bauer Digital Radio (page does not exist)">Bauer Digital Radio</a> plc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The city is covered by <a href="/wiki/BBC_North_East_and_Cumbria" title="BBC North East and Cumbria">BBC North East and Cumbria</a> and <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyne_Tees_Television" title="Tyne Tees Television">Tyne Tees</a> franchise, which has a regional office in the University's Media Centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sunderland's first film company was established in 2008; and is known as "Tanner Films Ltd" and is based in the <a href="/wiki/Sunniside,_Sunderland" title="Sunniside, Sunderland">Sunniside</a> area of the city. The companies first film, "King of the North" starring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angus_MacFadyen" title="Angus MacFadyen">Angus MacFadyen</a> and set in the <a href="/wiki/Hetton-le-Hole" title="Hetton-le-Hole">Hetton-le-Hole</a> area of the city; is currently under production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sunderland was named "The Facebook Capital of Britain" by the BBC in February 2010. The statistics showed that people in Sunderland were more likely to log on to the social networking site than anywhere else in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Sunderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The only professional sporting team in Sunderland is the <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team, <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_A.F.C." title="Sunderland A.F.C.">Sunderland A.F.C.</a>, which was formed in 1879, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and plays home games at the 49,000 seat capacity <a href="/wiki/Stadium_of_Light" title="Stadium of Light">Stadium of Light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sunderland also has the north-east's top women's football team, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunderland_A.F.C._Women" title="Sunderland A.F.C. Women">Sunderland A.F.C. Women</a>, who have been financially separated from the men's team since summer 2005. They currently play in the top tier of English women's football - <a href="/wiki/FA_Women%27s_Premier_League_National_Division" title="FA Women's Premier League National Division">FA Women's Premier League National Division</a>, despite their financial struggles. 2008 Olympic boxing silver medalist Tony Jeffries hails from Sunderland. He now owns and operates California's #1 ranked gym, BoxNBurn. Sunderland's longest stadium occupancy so far was of <a href="/wiki/Roker_Park" title="Roker Park">Roker Park</a> for 99 years beginning in 1898, with relocation taking place due to the stadium's confined location and the need to build an all-seater stadium. The initial relocation plan had been for a stadium to be situated alongside the Nissan factory, but these were abandoned in favour of the Stadium of Light at <a href="/wiki/Monkwearmouth" title="Monkwearmouth">Monkwearmouth</a> on the site of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colliery" title="Colliery">colliery</a> that had closed at the end of 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_Nissan_F.C." title="Sunderland Nissan F.C.">Sunderland Nissan F.C.</a> the City now has only one non-league side, <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_Ryhope_Community_Association_F.C." title="Sunderland Ryhope Community Association F.C.">Sunderland Ryhope Community Association F.C.</a> who now play in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_League_Division_One" title="Northern League Division One">Northern League Division One</a> after a successful promotion campaign in the 2009/10 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> MMA fighters Ross "The Real Deal" Pearson and Ian "The Machine" Freeman also hail from Sunderland.</p>
