<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Trial and error</h1>

<p><b>Trial and error</b> is a fundamental method of <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is characterised by repeated, varied attempts which are continued until success,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or until the agent stops trying.</p>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W.H._Thorpe" title="W.H. Thorpe">W.H. Thorpe</a>, the term was <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">devised</a> by <a href="/wiki/C._Lloyd_Morgan" title="C. Lloyd Morgan">C. Lloyd Morgan</a> (1852–1936) after trying out similar phrases "trial and failure" and "trial and practice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/Morgan%27s_Canon" title="Morgan's Canon">Morgan's Canon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">animal behaviour</a> should be explained in the simplest possible way. Where behaviour seems to imply higher mental processes, it might be explained by trial-and-error learning. An example is the skillful way in which his terrier Tony opened the garden gate, easily misunderstood as an insightful act by someone seeing the final behaviour. Lloyd Morgan, however, had watched and recorded the series of approximations by which the dog had gradually learned the response, and could demonstrate that no insight was required to explain it.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edward_Thorndike" title="Edward Thorndike">Edward Thorndike</a> showed how to manage a trial-and-error experiment in the laboratory. In his famous experiment, a cat was placed in a series of puzzle boxes in order to study the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_effect" title="Law of effect">law of effect</a> in learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He plotted learning curves which recorded the timing for each trial. Thorndike's key observation was that learning was promoted by positive results, which was later refined and extended by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B.F._Skinner" title="B.F. Skinner">B.F. Skinner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Operant_conditioning" title="Operant conditioning">operant conditioning</a>.</p>
<p>Trial and error is also a <a href="/wiki/Heuristic" title="Heuristic">heuristic</a> method of problem solving, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Repair" title="Repair">repair</a>, tuning, or obtaining <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>. In the field of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, the method is called <b>generate and test</b>. In elementary algebra, when solving equations, it is <b>"guess and check"</b>.</p>
<p>This approach can be seen as one of the two basic approaches to problem solving, contrasted with an approach using <a href="/wiki/Insight" title="Insight">insight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a>. However, there are intermediate methods which for example, use theory to guide the method, an approach known as <i>guided empiricism</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This approach is far more successful with simple problems and in games, and is often resorted to when no apparent rule applies. This does not mean that the approach need be careless, for an individual can be methodical in manipulating the variables in an attempt to sort through possibilities that may result in success. Nevertheless, this method is often used by people who have little knowledge in the problem area. The trial-and-error approach has been studied from its natural computational point of view <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simplest_applications">Simplest applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Simplest applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Ross_Ashby" title="William Ross Ashby">Ashby</a> (1960, section 11/5) offers three simple strategies for dealing with the same basic exercise-problem; and they have very different efficiencies: Suppose there are 1000 on/off switches which have to be set to a particular combination by random-based testing, each test to take one second. [This is also discussed in Traill (1978/2006, section C1.2]. The strategies are:</p>
<ul>
<li>the perfectionist all-or-nothing method, with no attempt at holding partial successes. This would be expected to take more than 10^301 seconds, [i.e. 2^1000 seconds, or 3·5×(10^291) centuries!];</li>
<li>a serial-test of switches, holding on to the partial successes (assuming that these are manifest) would take 500 seconds on average; while</li>
<li>a parallel-but-individual testing of all switches simultaneously would take only one second.</li>
</ul>
<p>Note the tacit assumption here that no intelligence or insight is brought to bear on the problem. However, the existence of different available strategies allows us to consider a separate ("superior") domain of processing — a <i>"meta-level"</i> above the mechanics of switch handling — where the various available strategies can be randomly chosen. Once again this is "trial and error", but of a different type. This leads us to:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hierarchies">Hierarchies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hierarchies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ashby's book develops this "meta-level" idea, and extends it into a whole <a href="/wiki/Recursion" title="Recursion">recursive</a> sequence of levels, successively above each other in a systematic hierarchy. On this basis he argues that human intelligence emerges from such organization: relying heavily on trial-and-error (at least initially at each new stage), but emerging with what we would call "intelligence" at the end of it all. Thus presumably the topmost level of the hierarchy (at any stage) will still depend on simple trial-and-error.</p>
<p>Traill (1978/2006) suggests that this Ashby-hierarchy probably coincides with <a href="/wiki/Jean_Piaget" title="Jean Piaget">Piaget</a>'s well-known theory of developmental stages. [This work also discusses Ashby's 1000-switch example; see §C1.2]. After all, it is part of Piagetian doctrine that children learn by first <i>actively doing</i> in a more-or-less random way, and then hopefully learn from the consequences — which all has a certain to Ashby's random "trial-and-error".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traill (2008, <i>espec. Table "S" on p.31</i>) follows <a href="/wiki/Niels_Kaj_Jerne" title="Niels Kaj Jerne">Jerne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Popper</a> in seeing this strategy as probably underlying <i>all</i> knowledge-gathering systems — <i>at least in their initial phase</i>.</p>
<p>Four such systems are identified:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">Natural selection</a> which "educates" the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of the species,</li>
<li>The brain of the individual (just discussed);</li>
<li>The "brain" of society-as-such (including the publicly held body of science); and</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_immune_system" title="Adaptive immune system">adaptive immune system</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intention">Intention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Intention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Ashby-and-<a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">Cybernetics</a> tradition, the word "trial" usually implies <i>random-or-arbitrary</i>, without any <i>deliberate</i> choice.</p>
<p>However amongst non-cyberneticians, "trial" will often imply a deliberate subjective act by some adult human agent; (e.g. in a court-room, or laboratory). So that has sometimes led to confusion.</p>
<p>Of course the situation becomes even more confusing if one accepts Ashby's hierarchical explanation of intelligence, and its implied ability to be deliberate and to creatively <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> — all based ultimately on <i>non-deliberate</i> actions. The lesson here seems to be that one must simply be careful to clarify the meaning of one's own words, and indeed the words of others. [Incidentally it seems that <i>consciousness</i> is not an essential ingredient for <i>intelligence</i> as discussed above.]</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trial and error has a number of features:</p>
<ul>
<li>solution-oriented: trial and error makes no attempt to discover <i>why</i> a solution works, merely that it <i>is</i> a solution.</li>
<li>problem-specific: trial and error makes no attempt to generalize a solution to other problems.</li>
<li>non-optimal: trial and error is generally an attempt to find <i>a</i> solution, not <i>all</i> solutions, and not the <i>best</i> solution.</li>
<li>needs little knowledge: trials and error can proceed where there is little or no knowledge of the subject.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is possible to use trial and error to find all solutions or the best solution, when a testably finite number of possible solutions exist. To find all solutions, one simply makes a note and continues, rather than ending the process, when a solution is found, until all solutions have been tried. To find the best solution, one finds all solutions by the method just described and then comparatively evaluates them based upon some predefined set of criteria, the existence of which is a condition for the possibility of finding a best solution. (Also, when only one solution can exist, as in assembling a jigsaw puzzle, then any solution found is the only solution and so is necessarily the best.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trial_and_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trial and error has traditionally been the main method of finding new drugs, such as <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">Chemists</a> simply try chemicals at random until they find one with the desired effect. In a more sophisticated version, chemists select a narrow range of chemicals it is thought may have some effect using a technique called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structure-activity_relationship" title="Structure-activity relationship">structure-activity relationship</a>. (The latter case can be alternatively considered as a changing of the problem rather than of the solution strategy: instead of "What chemical will work well as an antibiotic?" the problem in the sophisticated approach is "Which, if any, of the chemicals in this narrow range will work well as an antibiotic?") The method is used widely in many disciplines, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymer_technology" title="Polymer technology">polymer technology</a> to find new polymer types or families.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> can be regarded as containing an element of trial and error in its formulation and testing of hypotheses. Also compare <a href="/wiki/Genetic_algorithm" title="Genetic algorithm">genetic algorithms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simulated_annealing" title="Simulated annealing">simulated annealing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reinforcement_learning" title="Reinforcement learning">reinforcement learning</a> – all varieties for search which apply the basic idea of trial and error.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">Biological evolution</a> can be considered as a form of trial and error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Random mutations and sexual genetic variations can be viewed as trials and poor reproductive fitness, or lack of improved fitness, as the error. Thus after a long time 'knowledge' of well-adapted genomes accumulates simply by virtue of them being <i>able</i> to reproduce.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bogosort" title="Bogosort">Bogosort</a>, a conceptual sorting algorithm (that is extremely inefficient and impractical), can be viewed as a trial and error approach to sorting a list. However, typical simple examples of bogosort do not track which orders of the list have been tried and may try the same order any number of times, which violates one of the basic principles of trial and error. Trial and error is actually more efficient and practical than bogosort; unlike bogosort, it is guaranteed to halt in finite time on a finite list, and might even be a reasonable way to sort extremely short lists under some conditions <a href="/wiki/Jumping_spider" title="Jumping spider">Jumping spiders</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genus_(biology)" title="Genus (biology)">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Portia_(spider)" title="Portia (spider)">Portia</a></i> use trial and error to find new tactics against unfamiliar prey or in unusual situations, and remember the new tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HarlandJackson2000Cats_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HarlandJackson2000Cats-7">[7]</a></sup> Tests show that <i><a href="/wiki/Portia_fimbriata" title="Portia fimbriata">Portia fimbriata</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Portia_labiata" title="Portia labiata">Portia labiata</a></i> can use trial and error in an artificial environment, where the spider's objective is to cross a miniature <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoon</a> that is too wide for a simple jump, and must either jump then swim or only swim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JacksonEtc2006ConfineProb_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JacksonEtc2006ConfineProb-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JacksonEtc2001ConfinProb_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-JacksonEtc2001ConfinProb-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
