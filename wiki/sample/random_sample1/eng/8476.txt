<p>A <b>test pilot</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">aviator</a> who flies new and modified <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> in specific maneuvers, known as flight test techniques<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or FTTs, allowing the results to be measured and the design to be evaluated.</p>
<p>In the 1950s, test pilots were being killed at the rate of about one a week, but the risks have shrunk to a fraction of that, thanks to the maturation of aircraft technology, better ground-testing and simulation of aircraft performance, fly by wire technology and lately, the use of <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> to test experimental aircraft features. Still, piloting experimental aircraft remains more dangerous than most other types of flying.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_Pilot_Qualifications">Test Pilot Qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Test_pilot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Test Pilot Qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A test pilot must be able to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Understand a test plan;</li>
<li>Stick to a test plan, flying a plane in a highly specific way;</li>
<li>Carefully document the results of each test;</li>
<li>Have an excellent feel for the aircraft, and sense exactly how it is behaving oddly if it is doing so;</li>
<li>Solve problems quickly if anything goes wrong with the aircraft during a test;</li>
<li>Cope with many different things going wrong at once.</li>
<li>Effectively communicate flight test observations to engineers and relate engineering results to the pilot community, thus bridging the gap between those who design and build aircraft with those who employ the aircraft to accomplish a mission.</li>
</ul>
<p>Test pilots must have an excellent knowledge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_engineering" title="Aeronautical engineering">aeronautical engineering</a>, in order to understand how and why planes are tested. They must be above-average pilots with excellent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytical_skills" title="Analytical skills">analytical skills</a> and the ability to fly accurately whilst following a flight plan.</p>
<p>Test pilots can be <b>experimental</b> and <b>engineering</b> test pilots (investigating the characteristics of new types of aircraft during development) or <b>production</b> test pilots (the more mundane role of confirming the characteristics of new aircraft as they come off the production line); many test pilots would perform both roles during their careers. Modern test pilots often receive formal training from highly-selective military test pilot schools, although other test pilots receive training and experience from civilian institutions and/or manufacturers' test pilot development programs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Test_pilot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Test flying as a systematic activity started during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aircraft_Establishment" title="Royal Aircraft Establishment">Royal Aircraft Establishment</a> (RAE) in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. An "Experimental Flight" was formed at the <a href="/wiki/Central_Flying_School" title="Central Flying School">Central Flying School</a>. During the 1920s, test flying was further developed by the RAE in the UK, and by the <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a> (NACA) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In the 1950s, NACA was transformed into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a>, or NASA. During these years, as work was done into aircraft stability and handling qualities, test flying evolved towards a more qualitative scientific profession.</p>
<p>The world's oldest test pilot school is what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Empire_Test_Pilots%27_School" title="Empire Test Pilots' School">Empire Test Pilots' School</a> (motto "Learn to Test - Test to Learn"), at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAF_Boscombe_Down" title="RAF Boscombe Down">RAF Boscombe Down</a> in the UK. In America, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force_Test_Pilot_School" title="United States Air Force Test Pilot School">United States Air Force Test Pilot School</a> is located at <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Test_Pilot_School" title="United States Naval Test Pilot School">United States Naval Test Pilot School</a> is located at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Patuxent_River" title="Naval Air Station Patuxent River">Naval Air Station Patuxent River</a>, Maryland and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPNER" title="EPNER">EPNER</a> (<i>Ecole du Personnel Navigant d'Essai et de Reception</i> - "School for flight test and acceptance personnel"), the French test pilot school, is located in <a href="/wiki/Istres" title="Istres">Istres</a>, France. The only civilian school in the United States is the <a href="/wiki/National_Test_Pilot_School" title="National Test Pilot School">National Test Pilot School</a>, a not-for-profit educational institute located in <a href="/wiki/Mojave,_California" title="Mojave, California">Mojave</a>, California.</p>
