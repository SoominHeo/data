<p>In <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">physical theories</a>, a <b>test particle</b> is an idealized model of an object whose physical properties (usually <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charge_(physics)" title="Charge (physics)">charge</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">size</a>) are assumed to be negligible except for the property being studied, which is considered to be insufficient to alter the behavior of the rest of the system. The concept of a test particle often simplifies problems, and can provide a good approximation for physical phenomena. In addition to its uses in the simplification of the dynamics of a system in particular limits, it is also used as a diagnostic in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_simulations" title="Computer simulations">computer simulations</a> of physical processes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_particles_in_general_relativity">Test particles in general relativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Test_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Test particles in general relativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In metric theories of gravitation, particularly <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, a test particle is an idealized model of a small object whose mass is so small that it does not appreciably disturb the ambient <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">gravitational field</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equation" title="Einstein field equation">Einstein field equation</a>, the gravitational field is locally coupled not only to the distribution of non-gravitational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass-energy" title="Mass-energy">mass-energy</a>, but also to the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> (e.g. pressure, viscous stresses in a <a href="/wiki/Fluid_solution" title="Fluid solution">perfect fluid</a>).</p>
<p>In the case of test particles in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_solution" title="Vacuum solution">vacuum solution</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electrovacuum_solution" title="Electrovacuum solution">electrovacuum solution</a>, this turns out to imply that in addition to the tidal acceleration experienced by small clouds of test particles (spinning or not), <i>spinning</i> test particles may experience additional <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">accelerations</a> due to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spin-spin_force&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spin-spin force (page does not exist)">spin-spin forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poisson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-poisson-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_particles_in_plasma_physics_or_electrodynamics">Test particles in plasma physics or electrodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Test_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Test particles in plasma physics or electrodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In simulations with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_fields" title="Electromagnetic fields">electromagnetic fields</a> the most important characteristics of a <b>test particle</b> is its <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>. In this situation it is often referred to as a <b>test charge</b>.</p>

<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>, the behavior of a test charge is determined by effects of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> described by the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force</a>. In this case, a positive test charge will be deflected clockwise if moving perpendicular to a magnetic field pointing toward you, and counterclockwise if moving perpendicular to a magnetic field directed away from you.</p>
