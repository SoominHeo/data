<p><i><b>Zelda's Adventure</b></i> is an <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">action-adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> developed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Viridis_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Viridis Corporation (page does not exist)">Viridis Corporation</a> and released for the <a href="/wiki/Philips_CD-i" title="Philips CD-i">Philips CD-i</a> console system. Set in the land of Tolemac, the game follows a non-traditional Zelda-saves-Link storyline, in which Link has been captured by the evil lord <a href="/wiki/Ganon" title="Ganon">Ganon</a>, and Zelda must collect the seven celestial signs in order to rescue him.</p>
<p>Released nearly 8 months after the first two <a href="/wiki/CD-i_games_from_The_Legend_of_Zelda_series" title="CD-i games from The Legend of Zelda series"><i>Zelda</i> CD-i games</a>, <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> uses a different game engine from <a href="/wiki/Link:_The_Faces_of_Evil_and_Zelda:_The_Wand_of_Gamelon" title="Link: The Faces of Evil and Zelda: The Wand of Gamelon"><i>Faces of Evil</i> and <i>Wand of Gamelon</i></a>. Whereas the first two CD-i games were patterned on the side-scrolling <i><a href="/wiki/Zelda_II:_The_Adventure_of_Link" title="Zelda II: The Adventure of Link">Zelda II: The Adventure of Link</a></i>, <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> took the top-down <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda_(1986_video_game)" title="The Legend of Zelda (1986 video game)">The Legend of Zelda</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_to_the_Past" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past">A Link to the Past</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening" title="The Legend of Zelda: Link's Awakening">Link's Awakening</a> as its models. <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> is the only <i>Legend of Zelda</i> game to feature live-action cutscenes. Reception for the game was poor, and whereas some modern critics have given more nuanced reviews of the first two games, modern criticism for <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> is unanimously negative.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zelda%27s_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zeldasadventure1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="612" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Zeldasadventure1.jpg/220px-Zeldasadventure1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Zeldasadventure1.jpg/330px-Zeldasadventure1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Zeldasadventure1.jpg/440px-Zeldasadventure1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

A screenshot from <i>Zelda's Adventure</i></div>

<p>Unlike the previous two CD-i <i>Zelda</i> games, which take the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side-scrolling_game" title="Side-scrolling game">side-scrolling</a> view from <i><a href="/wiki/Zelda_II:_The_Adventure_of_Link" title="Zelda II: The Adventure of Link">Zelda II</a></i>, <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> is played with the overhead view found in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda_(1986_video_game)" title="The Legend of Zelda (1986 video game)">The Legend of Zelda</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNZA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGNZA-2">[2]</a></sup> Playing as Princess Zelda, the aim is to fight through the Seven Shrines of the Underworld to collect the celestial signs, and bring the land of Tolemac to an Age of Lightness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNZA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGNZA-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MGZA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MGZA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the other two games, <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> was created by Viridis, an entirely different company, with a change in style and gameplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGNZA_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-IGNZA-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MGZA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MGZA-3">[3]</a></sup> Level design is very much like the original <i>The Legend of Zelda</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_to_the_Past" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past">The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past</a></i>, with an overworld that allows access to individual dungeons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MGZA_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MGZA-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldasadvent_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-zeldasadvent-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Full_motion_video" title="Full motion video">FMV</a> sequences that present the plot are <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> instead of animated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldasadvent_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-zeldasadvent-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zelda%27s_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ganon" title="Ganon">Ganon</a> has kidnapped Link and stolen the seven celestial signs, creating an "Age of Darkness" in the kingdom of Tolemac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Princess_Zelda" title="Princess Zelda">Princess Zelda</a> is recruited by the court <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrologer" title="Astrologer">astrologer</a> Gaspra (played by Mark Andrade) to collect the signs to defeat Ganon and save Link.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guided by the words of Shurmak, Zelda must first travel through the forest to the Shrine of Rock,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> where she encounters Llort, a greedy minion of Ganon who protects the first celestial sign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Gaspra appears to congratulate Zelda and direct her to the Shrine of Illusion where she faces Pasquinade to earn the second celestial sign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Guided by the inhabitants of Tolemac, Zelda then makes her way to the mountains to conquer the Shrines of Air<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and Destiny<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> before crossing the great south sea to challenge Agwanda at the Shrine of Water for the fifth sign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Gaspra directs Zelda once more to the Shrine of Power in the southeast where her strength is tested,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> before travelling to the Shrine of Fire where she will face Warbane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> As Zelda reaches to collect the final celestial sign Ganon's claw stops her, and she is drawn into his lair for the final battle.</p>
<p>In the game's final scenes, peace returns to Tolemac. Link is revealed to be safe, holding hands with Zelda where the entrance to Ganon's lair once stood, the land now thriving with new growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zelda%27s_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1989, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> signed a deal with <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> to begin development of a <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a>-based system known as the "Nintendo PlayStation" or the SNES CD to be an <a href="/wiki/Peripheral" title="Peripheral">add-on</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Super Nintendo Entertainment System">Super Nintendo Entertainment System</a> that would allow for <a href="/wiki/Full_motion_video" title="Full motion video">FMV</a> and larger games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdioverview_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdioverview-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trailers_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-trailers-20">[20]</a></sup> However, Nintendo broke the agreement and instead signed with <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a> to make the add-on, which caused <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> to spin off their add-on into its own console called the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">PlayStation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdioverview_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdioverview-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dumbest_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dumbest-22">[22]</a></sup> Witnessing the poor reception of the <a href="/wiki/Sega_CD" title="Sega CD">Sega Mega-CD</a>, Nintendo scrapped the idea of making an add-on entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdioverview_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-cdioverview-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trailers_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-trailers-20">[20]</a></sup> As part of dissolving the agreement with Philips, Nintendo gave them the license to use five of their characters, including <a href="/wiki/Link_(The_Legend_of_Zelda)" title="Link (The Legend of Zelda)">Link</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Zelda" title="Princess Zelda">Princess Zelda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ganon" title="Ganon">Ganon</a>, for games on Philips's console called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CD-i" title="CD-i">CD-i</a>, after the partnership's dissolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trailers_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-trailers-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kot_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-kot-23">[23]</a></sup> Contracting out to independent studios, Philips subsequently used the characters to create three games for the CD-i, with Nintendo taking no part in their development except to give input on the look of the characters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trailers_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-trailers-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faceevil_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-faceevil-24">[24]</a></sup> based on the artwork from Nintendo's original two titles and that of their respective instruction booklets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrogamer27_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-retrogamer27-25">[25]</a></sup> Philips insisted that the development studios utilize all aspects of the CD-i's capabilities including <a href="/wiki/Full_motion_video" title="Full motion video">FMV</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamelon_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamelon-26">[26]</a></sup> high-resolution graphics, and CD-quality music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrogamer27_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-retrogamer27-25">[25]</a></sup> Because the system had not been designed as a dedicated video game console, there were several technical limitations, such as laggy controls (especially for the standard infrared controller),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamelon_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamelon-26">[26]</a></sup> and numerous problems in streaming-audio, memory, disc access, and graphics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrogamer27_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-retrogamer27-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The backgrounds for <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> were created from videos of scenery near <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_2" title="California State Route 2">Santa Monica Boulevard</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Los_Angeles,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="West Los Angeles, Los Angeles, California">West L.A.</a>, footage of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> taken from a helicopter, and the developers' vacation photos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> This decision was responsible for much of the game's RAM usage, causing backgrounds to scroll slowly and causing extreme frustration to the game's developers. The CD-i's technical abilities were so limited that the use of one or two kilobytes of system RAM caused arguments amongst the developers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> Photos of the characters were shot using mirrors mounted on the ceiling, which was so low it precluded mounting the camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdii_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdii-28">[28]</a></sup> The composer for <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> also played the part of Gaspra in the game's cutscenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> The houses and interiors built for the cut scenes were built as scale models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> The model artist was Jason Bakutis, who had worked in Hollywood on movies like <i><a href="/wiki/Critters_3" title="Critters 3">Critters 3</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Freddy%27s_Dead:_The_Final_Nightmare" title="Freddy's Dead: The Final Nightmare">Freddy's Dead: The Final Nightmare</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jason_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-jason-29">[29]</a></sup> Developers have stated they were not influenced by the first two CD-i Zelda games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> spent two years in <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">testing</a>, longer than it took to develop the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> Much more music was composed for the game than was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> Developers had difficulty making sure all the areas of the game had proper background masking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-developing_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-developing-27">[27]</a></sup> There were plans at one point to hire <a href="/wiki/Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen" title="Echo &amp; the Bunnymen">Echo &amp; the Bunnymen</a> to do the music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jason_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-jason-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Intending to push the capacities of the CD-i to its limits, development initially progressed with a goal of 600 screens and 160 <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">NPCs</a>. At this early stage, Viridis president Lee Barnes suggested that playthrough time might take as much as 300 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> These development figures were reduced in the final product which had only a handful of NPCs and whose playthrough time has been placed by one commentator at only 12 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of the game's programming was done by one person - Randy Casey. Randy was responsible for programming all of the game and all associated tools. Additional programming for the inventory system and game progress tracking, dubbed "FRP engine" was done by Gavin James. There is conflicting information about the game's budget—one developer claims there was "no budget at all"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdii_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdii-28">[28]</a></sup> while Bakutis claims (possibly facetiously) it had "the biggest budget <i>ever</i> for a video game".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jason_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-jason-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zelda%27s_Adventure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like the other two CD-i Zelda games, <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> was widely panned by critics. The graphics of <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> were called "blurry and digitized".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-up-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)"><i>Wired</i> magazine</a> said that the graphics were some of the worst ever encountered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-21">[21]</a></sup> The game's acting was criticized as unprofessional. Another flaw that has been identified is that the game could not produce both sound effects and music at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-up-32">[32]</a></sup> Scott Sharkey of <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a> called the box art of <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> one of the 15 worst ever made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> was released as the Philips CD-i was being discontinued and has become very rare over time, as have the first two Philips Zelda games; <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> is regularly sold for over $100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-up-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> appeared in a bracket poll of "The Greatest <i>Legend of Zelda</i> Game" along with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zelda:_The_Wand_of_Gamelon" title="Zelda: The Wand of Gamelon">Zelda: The Wand of Gamelon</a></i>. It lost in the first set of rounds to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Ocarina_of_Time" title="The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time">The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite giving positive reviews for <i>Faces of Evil</i> and <i>Wand of Gamelon</i> neither Danny Cowan of <i><a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a></i> nor <i>RetroGamer</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> John Szczepaniak would extend them to <i>Zelda's Adventure</i>, which Szczepaniak described as demonstrating arbitrary and illogical design, sloppy visuals, nearly non-existent music, excruciatingly high difficulty and cumbersome loading and controlling. Gameplay for <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> has also been portrayed as a trial-and-error effort to guess which items can be used to defeat which enemy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deservdamn_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-deservdamn-36">[36]</a></sup> Cowan called <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> "practically unplayable" due to the jerky frame rate, unresponsive controls and long load times, summarizing his review with a warning to "avoid this game at all costs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-up-32">[32]</a></sup> In discussing the popular online conception that <i>Zelda's Adventure</i> is superior to <i>Wand of Gamelon</i> and <i>Faces of Evil</i>, <i>RetroGamer</i> pointed to the top-down perspective as fomenting misinformation regarding the game's similarities to the original <i>Zelda</i> when, according to <i>RetroGamer</i>, the game is actually not worth playing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deservdamn_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-deservdamn-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
