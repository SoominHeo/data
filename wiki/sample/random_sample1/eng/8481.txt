<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Operation Freakout</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Operation Freakout</b>, also known as <b>Operation PC Freakout</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">Church of Scientology</a> covert plan intended to have the U.S. author and journalist <a href="/wiki/Paulette_Cooper" title="Paulette Cooper">Paulette Cooper</a> imprisoned or committed to a <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric_hospital" title="Psychiatric hospital">psychiatric hospital</a>. The plan, undertaken in 1976 following years of church-initiated lawsuits and covert harassment, was meant to eliminate the perceived threat that Cooper posed to the church and obtain revenge for her publication in 1971 of a highly critical book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scandal_of_Scientology" title="The Scandal of Scientology">The Scandal of Scientology</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> discovered documentary evidence of the plot and the preceding campaign of harassment during an investigation into the Church of Scientology in 1977, eventually leading to the church compensating Cooper in an out-of-court settlement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span></h2>
<p>Cooper, a freelance journalist and author, had begun researching <a href="/wiki/Scientology" title="Scientology">Scientology</a> in 1968 and wrote a critical article on the church for the British magazine <i>Queen</i> (now <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Bazaar" title="Harper's Bazaar">Harpers Bazaar</a></i>) in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The church promptly sued for libel, adding <i>Queen</i> to the dozens of British publications that it had already sued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Undeterred, Cooper expanded her article into a full-length book, <i>The Scandal of Scientology</i> (subtitled "A chilling examination of the nature, beliefs and practices of the "now religion.'"). It was published by Tower Publications, Inc. of New York in the summer of 1971. The church responded by suing her in December 1971, demanding $300,000 for "untrue, libelous and defamatory statements about the Church."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1972.E2.80.931976:_Operations_Daniel_and_Dynamite">1972–1976: Operations Daniel and Dynamite</span></h2>
<p>Cooper was seen as a high-priority target by the church's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardian%27s_Office" title="Guardian's Office">Guardian's Office</a>, which acted as a combination of <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">intelligence agency</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal" title="Legal">legal</a> office and <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> bureau for the church. As early as February 29, 1972, the church's third most senior official, Jane Kember, sent a directive to Terry Milner, the Deputy Guardian for Intelligence United States (DGIUS), instructing him to collect information about Paulette Cooper so that she could be "handled."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup> In response, Milner ordered his subordinates to "attack her in as many ways as possible" and undertake "wide-scale exposure of PC's sex life", a plan which was named <a href="/wiki/List_of_Guardian%27s_Office_operations#Operations_Daniel_and_Dynamite" title="List of Guardian's Office operations">Operation Daniel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cooper counter-sued on March 30, 1972, demanding $15.4 million in damages for the ongoing harassment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, the church stepped up the harassment, for instance painting her name and phone number on street walls so that she would receive obscene phone calls, and subscribing her to pornographic mailing lists. She also received anonymous death threats and her neighbors received letters claiming that she had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venereal_disease" title="Venereal disease">venereal disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cooper_bomb_threat.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="813" data-file-width="1371" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cooper_bomb_threat.gif/220px-Cooper_bomb_threat.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cooper_bomb_threat.gif/330px-Cooper_bomb_threat.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cooper_bomb_threat.gif/440px-Cooper_bomb_threat.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cooper_bomb_threat.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The second of the two forged <a href="/wiki/Bomb_threat" title="Bomb threat">bomb threats</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In December 1972, the church launched a new attack called <a href="/wiki/List_of_Guardian%27s_Office_operations#Operations_Daniel_and_Dynamite" title="List of Guardian's Office operations">Operation Dynamite</a>. That month, a woman ostensibly soliciting funds for <a href="/wiki/United_Farm_Workers" title="United Farm Workers">United Farm Workers</a> stole a quantity of stationery from Cooper's apartment. A few days later, the New York Church of Scientology "received" two anonymous bomb threats. The following May, Cooper was indicted for making the bomb threats and arraigned for a federal <a href="/wiki/Grand_jury" title="Grand jury">grand jury</a>. The threats had been written on her stationery, which was marked with her fingerprints.</p>
<p>The charges were eventually dropped in 1975 with the filing of a <a href="/wiki/Nolle_prosequi" title="Nolle prosequi">Nolle prosequi</a> order by the local <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney" title="United States Attorney">US Attorney's office</a>, but it was not until the fall of 1977 that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> discovered that the bomb threats had been staged by the Guardian's Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup> A contemporary memorandum sent between two Guardian's Office staff noted on a list of jobs successfully accomplished: "Conspired to entrap Mrs. Lovely into being arrested for a felony which she did not commit. She was arraigned for the crime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The church sued Cooper again in 1975 in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The church itself was reported to have imported Cooper's books into foreign countries for the express purpose of <a href="/wiki/Libel_tourism" title="Libel tourism">suing her in jurisdictions where the libel laws were stricter</a> than in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian09_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian09-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1976:_Operation_Freakout">1976: Operation Freakout</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cooper_freakout.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="926" data-file-width="599" height="340" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Cooper_freakout.gif/220px-Cooper_freakout.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Cooper_freakout.gif/330px-Cooper_freakout.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Cooper_freakout.gif/440px-Cooper_freakout.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cooper_freakout.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Part of the planning document for Operation Freakout, April 1976</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the spring of 1976, the Guardian Office leadership decided to initiate an operation with the aim "To get P.C. incarcerated in a mental institution or jail, or at least to hit her so hard that she drops her attacks." The planning document, dated April 1, 1976, declared the aim to be "[t]o remove PC from her position of power so that she cannot attack the C of S [Church of Scientology]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its initial form Operation Freakout consisted of three different plans (or "channels", as the Guardian's Office termed them), tailored to implicate her by her Jewish descent:</p>
<ol>
<li>First, a woman was to imitate Paulette Cooper's voice and make telephone threats to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consulate" title="Consulate">consulates</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</li>
<li>Second, a threatening letter was to be mailed to an Arab consulate in such a fashion that it would appear to have been done by Paulette Cooper (who is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a>).</li>
<li>Third, a Scientologist volunteer was to impersonate Paulette Cooper at a laundromat and threaten the current president <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> and then <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a>. A second Scientologist would thereafter inform the FBI of the threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Two additional plans were added to Operation Freakout on April 13, 1976. The fourth plan called for Scientologist agents to gather information from Cooper so that the success of the first three plans could be assessed. The fifth plan was for a Scientologist to warn an Arab consulate by telephone that Paulette Cooper had been talking about bombing it. A sixth and final plan was added subsequently. It was effectively a re-run of the 1972 plot, requiring Scientologists to obtain Paulette Cooper's fingerprints on a blank piece of paper, type a threatening letter to Kissinger on that paper, and mail it. Guardian's Office staff member Bruce Raymond noted in an internal memo: "This additional channel [the sixth plan] should really have put her away. Worked with all the other channels. The F.B.I. already think she did the bomb threats on the C of S [in 1972]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 31, 1976, Jane Kember telexed Henning Heldt, the Deputy Guardian U.S., to update him on the situation:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"PC [Paulette Cooper] is still resisting paying the money but the judgement stands in PT [present time] ... Have her lawyer contacted and also arrange for PC to get the data that we can slap the writs on her. If you want legal docs, from here on we will provide. Then if she still declines to come we slap the writs on her before she reaches CW [Clearwater] as we don't want to be seen publically  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] being brutal to such a pathetic victim from a concentration camp."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_and_aftermath">Exposure and aftermath</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1754" data-file-width="1275" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg/180px-1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg/270px-1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg/360px-1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1979_GrandJuryCover_US_v_Hubbard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grand Jury Charges, Introduction, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America_v._Mary_Sue_Hubbard" title="United States of America v. Mary Sue Hubbard">United States of America v. Mary Sue Hubbard</a>", <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_Columbia" title="United States District Court for the District of Columbia">United States District Court for the District of Columbia</a>, 1979.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ultimately, Operation Freakout was never put into effect. On June 11, 1976, two Scientology agents—Michael Meisner and Gerald Bennett Wolfe—were caught in the act of committing attempted <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">burglary</a> at a courthouse in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> as part of the Guardian's Office's ongoing <a href="/wiki/Operation_Snow_White" title="Operation Snow White">Operation Snow White</a>. The Guardian's Office was preoccupied for the next year with attempts to hush up the scandal, even going to the lengths of kidnapping Meisner and holding him <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/incommunicado" title="wikt:incommunicado">incommunicado</a> to prevent him from testifying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup> The church sought to bring a quick end to the dispute with Cooper in December 1976 when it proposed to settle with her, on condition that she was not to republish or comment on <i>The Scandal of Scientology</i> and agree to assign the book's copyright to the Church of Scientology of California.</p>
<p>On July 8, 1977, however, the FBI raided Scientology offices in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and Washington, D.C., seizing over 48,000 documents. They revealed the extent to which the Church had committed "criminal campaigns of vilification, burglaries and thefts ... against private and public individuals and organizations," as the U.S. Government prosecutor put it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup> The documents were later released to the public, enabling Cooper and the world at large to learn about the details of Operation Freakout.</p>
<p>Sometime during late 1977, according to an affidavit given by Margery Wakefield, a secret meeting was held at the Guardian's Office where two murders were planned. The first of which was of a young man who had defected from Scientology and had been recaptured, and the second was an assassination of Cooper in which they were planning to shoot her dead. It is unknown whether or not these plans were attempted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although in the end nobody was indicted for the harassment of Cooper, the wider campaign of criminal activity was successfully prosecuted by the United States Government. <a href="/wiki/Mary_Sue_Hubbard" title="Mary Sue Hubbard">Mary Sue Hubbard</a>, Jane Kember, Henning Heldt, Morris Budlong, Duke Snider, Dick Weigand, Greg Willardson, Mitchell Hermann and Cindy Raymond were indicted by a federal grand jury on charges of theft, burglary, conspiracy, and other crimes. With the exception of Kember and Budlong, the defendants agreed to uncontested <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stipulation_(law)" title="Stipulation (law)">stipulation</a> of the evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Kember and Budlong were convicted separately after being extradited from the United Kingdom. All of the defendants were imprisoned, serving up to four years in jail. Coincidentally, they were tried, convicted and sentenced in the same courthouse that their agents had been caught robbing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sentencing_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-sentencing-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Church of Scientology filed at least 19 lawsuits against Cooper throughout the 1970s and 1980s, which Cooper considered part of "a typical Scientology dirty-tricks campaign" and which Cooper's attorney Michael Flynn said was motivated by L. Ron Hubbard's declaration that the purpose of a lawsuit was to "<a href="/wiki/Vexatious_litigation" title="Vexatious litigation">harass and discourage</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breeze_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Breeze-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Cooper discontinued her legal actions against Scientology in 1985 after receiving an out-of-court settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
