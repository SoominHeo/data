<p><i><b>Six Feet Under</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> television series created and <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer#Motion_pictures_and_television" title="Executive producer">produced</a> by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ball_(screenwriter)" title="Alan Ball (screenwriter)">Alan Ball</a>. It premiered on the premium cable network <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> in the United States on June 3, 2001 and ended on August 21, 2005, spanning <a href="/wiki/List_of_Six_Feet_Under_episodes" title="List of Six Feet Under episodes">five seasons and 63 episodes</a>. The show depicts members of the Fisher family, who run a <a href="/wiki/Funeral_home" title="Funeral home">funeral home</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, and their friends and lovers. The series traces these characters' lives over the course of five years. The <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble</a> drama stars <a href="/wiki/Peter_Krause" title="Peter Krause">Peter Krause</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_C._Hall" title="Michael C. Hall">Michael C. Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frances_Conroy" title="Frances Conroy">Frances Conroy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Ambrose" title="Lauren Ambrose">Lauren Ambrose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freddy_Rodriguez_(actor)" title="Freddy Rodriguez (actor)">Freddy Rodriguez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathew_St._Patrick" title="Mathew St. Patrick">Mathew St. Patrick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Griffiths" title="Rachel Griffiths">Rachel Griffiths</a> as the show's seven central characters. The series was produced by Actual Size Films and The Greenblatt/Janollari Studio, and was shot on location in Los Angeles and in Hollywood studios.</p>
<p><i>Six Feet Under</i> received widespread critical acclaim, particularly for its writing and acting, and consistently drew high ratings for the HBO network. Regarded by many as one of the greatest TV dramas of all time, it has since been included on <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">TIME</a></i> magazine's "All-TIME 100 TV Shows",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine's "50 Greatest TV Shows of All Time" list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It has also been described as having one of the finest series <a href="/wiki/Everyone%27s_Waiting" title="Everyone's Waiting">finales</a> in the history of television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It won numerous awards, including nine <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Awards</a>, three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" title="Screen Actors Guild Awards">Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Awards</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Show_synopsis">Show synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Show synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Six_Feet_Under_episodes" title="List of Six Feet Under episodes">List of Six Feet Under episodes</a></div>
<p>The show stars <a href="/wiki/Peter_Krause" title="Peter Krause">Peter Krause</a> as <a href="/wiki/Nate_Fisher" title="Nate Fisher">Nathaniel Samuel "Nate" Fisher, Jr.</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Funeral_director" title="Funeral director">funeral director</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Fisher" title="Nathaniel Fisher">father</a> (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Jenkins" title="Richard Jenkins">Richard Jenkins</a>) dies and bequeaths to him and his brother, <a href="/wiki/David_Fisher_(Six_Feet_Under)" title="David Fisher (Six Feet Under)">David</a> (<a href="/wiki/Michael_C._Hall" title="Michael C. Hall">Michael C. Hall</a>), co-ownership of the family funeral business. The Fisher clan also includes widow, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruth_Fisher" title="Ruth Fisher">Ruth</a> (<a href="/wiki/Frances_Conroy" title="Frances Conroy">Frances Conroy</a>), and daughter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claire_Fisher" title="Claire Fisher">Claire</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lauren_Ambrose" title="Lauren Ambrose">Lauren Ambrose</a>). Other regulars include mortician and family friend, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federico_Diaz" title="Federico Diaz">Federico Diaz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Freddy_Rodriguez_(actor)" title="Freddy Rodriguez (actor)">Freddy Rodriguez</a>), Nate's on-again/off-again girlfriend, <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Chenowith" title="Brenda Chenowith">Brenda Chenowith</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rachel_Griffiths" title="Rachel Griffiths">Rachel Griffiths</a>), and David's long-term boyfriend, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keith_Charles_(Six_Feet_Under)" title="Keith Charles (Six Feet Under)">Keith Charles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mathew_St._Patrick" title="Mathew St. Patrick">Mathew St. Patrick</a>).</p>
<p>On one level, the show is a conventional family drama, dealing with such issues as interpersonal relationships, <a href="/wiki/Infidelity" title="Infidelity">infidelity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>. At the same time, the show is distinguished by its unblinking focus on the topic of <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>, which it explores on multiple levels (personal, religious, and philosophical). Each episode begins with a death – the cause of which ranges from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> or <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sudden_infant_death_syndrome" title="Sudden infant death syndrome">sudden infant death syndrome</a> – and that death usually sets the thematic tone for each episode, allowing the characters to reflect on their current fortunes and misfortunes in a way that is illuminated by the death and its aftermath. The show also utilises <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_humor" title="Dark humor">dark humor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a> running throughout.</p>
<p>A recurring plot device consists of a character having an imaginary conversation with the deceased; for example, Nate, David, and Federico sometimes "converse" with the decedent at the beginning of the episode, while the corpse is being embalmed, or during funeral planning or the funeral itself. Sometimes, the characters converse with other, recurring deceased characters, most notably Nathaniel Fisher, Sr. The show's creator, Alan Ball, avers that this represents the living characters' internal dialogues expressed in the form of external conversations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although overall plots and characters were created by Alan Ball, there are conflicting reports on how the series was conceived. In one instance, Ball stated that he came up with the premise of the show after the deaths of his sister and father. However, in an interview,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-In_Memoriam_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-In_Memoriam-5">[5]</a></sup> he intimates that HBO entertainment president Carolyn Strauss proposed the idea to him. In a copyright-infringement lawsuit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> screenwriter Gwen O'Donnell asserted that she was the original source of the idea which later passed through Strauss to Ball; the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, proceeding on the assumption that this assertion was true, rejected her claim. Ball stated in an interview,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">When I went to HBO and they had read my first draft and Carolyn Strauss said, 'You know, this is really, really good. I love these characters, I love these situations, but it feels a little safe. Could you just make it just a little more fucked up?' which is not a note that you get in Hollywood very often. And I thought, 'Wow!' And that gave me free range to go a little deeper, go a little darker, go a little more complicated.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_themes">Major themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The show focuses on human mortality, the symbiotic nature of life and death feeding off of each other and the lives of those who deal with it on a daily basis. When discussing the concept of the show, creator Alan Ball elaborates on the foremost questions the show's pilot targeted:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-In_Memoriam_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-In_Memoriam-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Who are these people who are funeral directors that we hire to face death for us? What does that do to their own lives – to grow up in a home where there are dead bodies in the basement, to be a child and walk in on your father with a body lying on a table opened up and him working on it? What does that do to you?</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p><i>Six Feet Under</i> introduces the Fisher family as the basis on which to explore these questions. Throughout its five-season, 63-episode run, major characters experience crises which are in direct relation to their environment and the grief they have experienced. Alan Ball again relates these experiences as well as the choice of the series' title, to the persistent subtext of the program:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><i>Six Feet Under</i> refers not only to being buried as a dead body is buried, but to primal emotions and feelings running under the surface. When one is surrounded by death – to counterbalance that, there needs to be a certain intensity of experience, of needing to escape. It's Nate with his womanizing – it's Claire and her sexual experimentation – it's Brenda's sexual compulsiveness – it's David having sex with a male hooker in public – it's Ruth having several affairs – it's the life force trying to push up through all of that suffering and grief and depression.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Exteriors for the Fisher home were shot at 2302 West 25th Street and the intersection of Arlington Avenue, in the <a href="/wiki/West_Adams,_Los_Angeles" title="West Adams, Los Angeles">West Adams</a> neighborhood of Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In Season 5, episode 1, Rico mentions that he grew up "in West Adams, near where I work."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Episodes">Episodes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Episodes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Six_Feet_Under_episodes" title="List of Six Feet Under episodes">List of Six Feet Under episodes</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crew">Crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The series was created by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ball_(screenwriter)" title="Alan Ball (screenwriter)">Alan Ball</a>, who also served as executive producer and <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">showrunner</a> for the entire series run. Robert Greenblatt and David Janollari executive produced the series, as The Greenblatt Janollari Studio was one of the production companies. The other producers were Lori Jo Nemhauser and Robert Del Valle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-crew-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The writing staff consisted of creator Alan Ball, who wrote nine episodes over the series run, including the pilot episode and the series finale. Writers who were on staff for the entire series run included <a href="/wiki/Rick_Cleveland" title="Rick Cleveland">Rick Cleveland</a>, who wrote eight episodes and became an executive producer in the fifth season; <a href="/wiki/Kate_Robin" title="Kate Robin">Kate Robin</a>, who wrote eight episodes and became a supervising producer in the fifth season; and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Eric_Kaplan" title="Bruce Eric Kaplan">Bruce Eric Kaplan</a>, who wrote seven episodes and became an executive producer in the fourth season. Christian Williams was just on staff for the first season, writing two episodes. Both Laurence Andries and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Taylor_(screenwriter)" title="Christian Taylor (screenwriter)">Christian Taylor</a> wrote three episodes each during their run on the series for the first two seasons, and they also served as producers. <a href="/wiki/Scott_Buck" title="Scott Buck">Scott Buck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jill_Soloway" title="Jill Soloway">Jill Soloway</a> joined in the second season staying on staff for the rest of the series, and each wrote seven episodes. Buck became a co-executive producer in the fourth season, and Soloway became a co-executive producer in the fifth season. The last set of writers to join the staff were <a href="/wiki/Craig_Wright_(playwright)" title="Craig Wright (playwright)">Craig Wright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Oliver" title="Nancy Oliver">Nancy Oliver</a> in the third season. Wright wrote six episodes and became a producer in the fifth season and Oliver wrote five episodes and became a co-producer in the fifth season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-crew-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Creator Alan Ball also directed the most episodes, directing the pilot and each of the season finales. <a href="/wiki/Dan_Attias" title="Dan Attias">Dan Attias</a> also directed six episodes, from season two to five. <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Bates" title="Kathy Bates">Kathy Bates</a> (who also played <a href="/wiki/List_of_Six_Feet_Under_characters#Bettina" title="List of Six Feet Under characters">Bettina</a> on the series), <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cuesta" title="Michael Cuesta">Michael Cuesta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_Garc%C3%ADa_(director)" title="Rodrigo García (director)">Rodrigo García</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Podeswa" title="Jeremy Podeswa">Jeremy Podeswa</a> each directed five episodes. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Engler" title="Michael Engler">Michael Engler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Minahan" title="Daniel Minahan">Daniel Minahan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alan_Poul" title="Alan Poul">Alan Poul</a> (who also served as an executive producer for the series) each directed four episodes. <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Arteta" title="Miguel Arteta">Miguel Arteta</a> directed three episodes and <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Holofcener" title="Nicole Holofcener">Nicole Holofcener</a> directed two episodes. Single-episode directors included <a href="/wiki/Peter_Care" title="Peter Care">Peter Care</a>, Alan Caso, <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Cholodenko" title="Lisa Cholodenko">Lisa Cholodenko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Coulter" title="Allen Coulter">Allen Coulter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Davidson_(director)" title="Adam Davidson (director)">Adam Davidson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Harron" title="Mary Harron">Mary Harron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Marston" title="Joshua Marston">Joshua Marston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_McBride" title="Jim McBride">Jim McBride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karen_Moncrieff" title="Karen Moncrieff">Karen Moncrieff</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Patterson_(director)" title="John Patterson (director)">John Patterson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Shakman" title="Matt Shakman">Matt Shakman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Taylor_(director)" title="Alan Taylor (director)">Alan Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rose_Troche" title="Rose Troche">Rose Troche</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Webber" title="Peter Webber">Peter Webber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-crew-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The series' main theme, written by composer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newman" title="Thomas Newman">Thomas Newman</a>, won a 2002 Emmy Award for <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Main_Title_Theme_Music" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Main Title Theme Music">Outstanding Main Title Theme Music</a> and two Grammy Awards in 2003 for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Instrumental_Composition" title="Grammy Award for Best Instrumental Composition">Best Instrumental Composition</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Instrumental_Arrangement" title="Grammy Award for Best Instrumental Arrangement">Best Instrumental Arrangement</a>.</p>
<p>The production sound from seasons three through five was mixed by <a href="/wiki/Bo_Harwood" title="Bo Harwood">Bo Harwood</a>, and was nominated in 2004 for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_Audio_Society_Award" title="Cinema Audio Society Award">Cinema Audio Society Award</a>.</p>
<p>Seasons two through five featured a promotional teaser trailer prior to the premiere of that season. The songs featured in each seasons trailer were "Heaven" by <a href="/wiki/Lamb_(band)" title="Lamb (band)">Lamb</a> for season two; "<a href="/wiki/A_Rush_of_Blood_to_the_Head" title="A Rush of Blood to the Head">A Rush of Blood to the Head</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a> for season three; "<a href="/wiki/Feeling_Good" title="Feeling Good">Feeling Good</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Nina_Simone" title="Nina Simone">Nina Simone</a> for season four; and "<a href="/wiki/Breathe_Me" title="Breathe Me">Breathe Me</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Sia_Furler" title="Sia Furler">Sia Furler</a> for season five, which is also used for montage in the series finale. All these songs are included in either of two soundtracks for the show.</p>
<p>The episode recaps for the first two seasons feature the song "Nothing Lies Still Long" by <a href="/wiki/Pell_Mell_(band)" title="Pell Mell (band)">Pell Mell</a>. The episode previews for the first and fifth seasons feature the <i>Six Feet Under</i> title theme, while the other seasons feature the <a href="/wiki/Rae_%26_Christian" title="Rae &amp; Christian">Rae &amp; Christian</a> remix version of the title theme.</p>
<p>Music supervision for the entire run of the series was provided by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Calamar" title="Gary Calamar">Gary Calamar</a> and Thomas Golubic, who were also credited as producers for the two soundtrack albums.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">Awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Six_Feet_Under_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_awards_and_nominations_received_by_Six_Feet_Under" title="List of awards and nominations received by Six Feet Under">List of awards and nominations received by Six Feet Under</a></div>
<p>At the 2002 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Awards" title="Primetime Emmy Awards">Primetime Emmy Awards</a>, the series received 23 nominations for its first two seasons, including a nomination for Outstanding Drama Series. Series creator <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ball_(screenwriter)" title="Alan Ball (screenwriter)">Alan Ball</a> won for Outstanding Directing for a Drama Series for the pilot episode and <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Clarkson" title="Patricia Clarkson">Patricia Clarkson</a> won for Outstanding Guest Actress in a Drama Series. The rest of the ensemble cast, including <a href="/wiki/Michael_C._Hall" title="Michael C. Hall">Michael C. Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Krause" title="Peter Krause">Peter Krause</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frances_Conroy" title="Frances Conroy">Frances Conroy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Griffiths" title="Rachel Griffiths">Rachel Griffiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freddy_Rodriguez_(actor)" title="Freddy Rodriguez (actor)">Freddy Rodriguez</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Ambrose" title="Lauren Ambrose">Lauren Ambrose</a> all received acting nominations. Guest actors <a href="/wiki/Lili_Taylor" title="Lili Taylor">Lili Taylor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illeana_Douglas" title="Illeana Douglas">Illeana Douglas</a> received nominations in the guest acting category. The series received 16 nominations at the 2003 Primetime Emmy Awards for its third season, including a nomination for Outstanding Drama Series. Peter Krause, Frances Conroy, Lauren Ambrose, Rachel Griffiths, <a href="/wiki/James_Cromwell" title="James Cromwell">James Cromwell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Bates" title="Kathy Bates">Kathy Bates</a> all received acting nominations. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Poul" title="Alan Poul">Alan Poul</a> was nominated for directing for the episode "Nobody Sleeps", and <a href="/wiki/Craig_Wright_(playwright)" title="Craig Wright (playwright)">Craig Wright</a> was nominated for writing for the episode "Twilight". The series received 5 nominations at the 2005 Primetime Emmy Awards for its fourth season, including Outstanding Drama Series and Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series for Frances Conroy. The series received 9 nominations at the 2006 Primetime Emmy Awards for its fifth and final season. Patricia Clarkson won for the second time for Outstanding Guest Actress in a Drama Series, and Peter Krause, Frances Conroy, and <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Cassidy" title="Joanna Cassidy">Joanna Cassidy</a> received acting nominations. Alan Ball was nominated for writing and directing for the series finale episode "<a href="/wiki/Everyone%27s_Waiting" title="Everyone's Waiting">Everyone's Waiting</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Awards" title="Golden Globe Awards">Golden Globe Awards</a>, the series won for Best Drama Series in 2001, and received nominations in 2002 and 2003. Peter Krause was nominated for Best Actor in a Drama Series in 2001 and 2002. Rachel Griffiths won for Best Supporting Actress in a Series, Miniseries, or TV Film in 2001, and received a nomination in 2002 in the Lead Actress category. Frances Conroy won for Best Actress in a Drama Series in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" title="Screen Actors Guild Awards">Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>, the cast won for Outstanding Ensemble in a Drama Series in 2002 and 2003, and received nominations in 2001, 2004, and 2005. Peter Krause was nominated for Outstanding Male Actor in a Drama Series in 2001 and 2003. Frances Conroy won for Outstanding Female Actor in a Drama Series in 2003.</p>
<p>The series won a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a> for general excellence in 2002 "for its unsettling yet powerfully humane explorations of life and death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
