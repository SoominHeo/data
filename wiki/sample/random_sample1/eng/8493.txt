<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Food chemistry</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">"Food Chemistry" redirects here. For the journal, see <a href="/wiki/Food_Chemistry_(journal)" title="Food Chemistry (journal)">Food Chemistry (journal)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Food chemistry</b> is the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> processes and interactions of all biological and non-biological components of foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The biological substances include such items as <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> as examples. It is similar to <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> in its main components such as <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, but it also includes areas such as water, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral">minerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">colors</a>. This discipline also encompasses how products change under certain <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">food processing</a> techniques and ways either to enhance or to prevent them from happening. An example of enhancing a process would be to encourage fermentation of <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a> products with <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> that convert <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>; an example of preventing a process would be stopping the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Browning_(chemical_process)" title="Browning (chemical process)">browning</a> on the surface of freshly cut <a href="/wiki/Red_Delicious" title="Red Delicious">Red Delicious</a> <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a> using <a href="/wiki/Lemon#Culinary_uses" title="Lemon">lemon</a> juice or other <a href="/wiki/Acidulated_water" title="Acidulated water">acidulated water</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_food_chemistry">History of food chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of food chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scientific approach to food and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a> arose with attention to <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_chemistry" title="Agricultural chemistry">agricultural chemistry</a> in the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._G._Wallerius" title="J. G. Wallerius">J. G. Wallerius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a>, and others. For example, Davy published <i>Elements of Agricultural Chemistry, in a Course of Lectures for the Board of Agriculture</i> (1813) in the United Kingdom which would serve as a foundation for the profession worldwide, going into a fifth edition. Earlier work included that by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> who isolated <a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a> from apples in 1785.</p>
<p>In 1874 the Society of Public Analysts was formed, with the aim of applying analytical methods to the benefit of the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Its early experiments were based on bread, milk and wine.</p>
<p>It was also out of concern for the quality of the food supply, mainly food adulteration and contamination issues that would first stem from intentional contamination to later with chemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_additives" title="Food additives">food additives</a> by the 1950s. The development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colleges" title="Colleges">colleges</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universities" title="Universities">universities</a> worldwide, most notably in the United States, would expand food chemistry as well with research of the dietary substances, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Single-grain_experiment" title="Single-grain experiment">Single-grain experiment</a> during 1907-11. Additional research by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvey_W._Wiley" title="Harvey W. Wiley">Harvey W. Wiley</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> during the late 19th century would play a key factor in the creation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="United States Food and Drug Administration">United States Food and Drug Administration</a> in 1906. The <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> would establish their <a class="external text" href="http://membership.acs.org/a/agfd/" rel="nofollow">Agricultural and Food Chemistry Division</a> in 1908 while the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Food_Technologists" title="Institute of Food Technologists">Institute of Food Technologists</a> would establish their <a class="external text" href="http://www.ift.org/divisions/food_chem/" rel="nofollow">Food Chemistry Division</a> in 1995.</p>
<p>Food chemistry concepts are often drawn from <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">rheology</a>, theories of <a href="/wiki/Transport_phenomena" title="Transport phenomena">transport phenomena</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_thermodynamics" title="Physical thermodynamics">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics" title="Chemical thermodynamics">chemical thermodynamics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_bonds" title="Chemical bonds">chemical bonds</a> and interaction forces, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reaction_kinetics" title="Reaction kinetics">reaction kinetics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biopolymer_science" title="Biopolymer science">biopolymer science</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloidal_interactions" title="Colloidal interactions">colloidal interactions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_transitions" title="Glass transitions">glass transitions</a> and freezing/disordered or noncrystalline solids, and thus has <a href="/wiki/Food_physical_chemistry" title="Food physical chemistry">Food Physical Chemistry</a> as a foundation area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_in_food_systems">Water in food systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Water in food systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a></div>
<p>A major component of food is water, which can encompass anywhere from 50% in <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> products to 95% in <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomato</a> products. It is also an excellent place for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial">bacterial</a> growth and food spoilage if it is not properly processed. One way this is measured in food is by <a href="/wiki/Water_activity" title="Water activity">water activity</a> which is very important in the shelf life of many foods during processing. One of the keys to <a href="/wiki/Food_preservation" title="Food preservation">food preservation</a> in most instances is reduce the amount of water or alter the water's characteristics to enhance shelf-life. Such methods include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_dehydration" title="Food dehydration">dehydration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frozen_food" title="Frozen food">freezing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This field encompasses the <i>"physiochemical principles of the reactions and conversions that occur during the manufacture, handling, and storage of foods"</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> .</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbohydrates">Carbohydrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbohydrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">Carbohydrate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saccharose.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="320" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Saccharose.svg/220px-Saccharose.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Saccharose.svg/330px-Saccharose.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Saccharose.svg/440px-Saccharose.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saccharose.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">Sucrose</a>: ordinary table sugar and probably the most familiar carbohydrate.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Comprising 75% of the biological world and 80% of all food intake for human consumption, the most common known human carbohydrate is <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">Sucrose</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The simplest version of a carbohydrate is a <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharide</a> which possesses the properties of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in a 1:2:1 ratio under a general formula of C<sub>n</sub>H<sub>2n</sub>O<sub>n</sub> where n is a minimum of 3. <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">Glucose</a> is an example of a monosaccharide as is <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>. Combine them in the picture shown to the right and you have <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>, one of the more common <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> products around.</p>
<p>A chain of monosaccharides form to make a <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a>. Such polysaccharides include <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dextran" title="Dextran">dextran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xanthan" title="Xanthan">xanthan</a>.</p>
<p>Sugar content is commonly measured in degrees <a href="/wiki/Brix" title="Brix">brix</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lipids">Lipids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lipids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">Lipid</a></div>
<p>The term lipid comprises a diverse range of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and to some extent is a catchall for relatively water-insoluble or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonpolar" title="Nonpolar">nonpolar</a> compounds of biological origin, including <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">waxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid" title="Essential fatty acid">essential fatty acids</a>), fatty-acid derived phospholipids, sphingolipids, glycolipids and terpenoids, such as retinoids and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steroids" title="Steroids">steroids</a>. Some lipids are linear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> molecules, while others have ring structures. Some are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">aromatic</a>, while others are not. Some are flexible, while others are rigid.</p>
<p>Most lipids have some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_molecule" title="Polar molecule">polar</a> character in addition to being largely nonpolar. Generally, the bulk of their structure is nonpolar or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> ("water-fearing"), meaning that it does not interact well with polar solvents like water. Another part of their structure is polar or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> ("water-loving") and will tend to associate with polar solvents like water. This makes them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiphilic" title="Amphiphilic">amphiphilic</a> molecules (having both hydrophobic and hydrophilic portions). In the case of <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>, the polar group is a mere -OH (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> or alcohol).</p>
<p>Lipids in food include the oils of such grains as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a>, from animal fats, and are parts of many foods such as milk, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, and meat. They also act as vitamin carriers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_proteins">Food proteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Food proteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">Protein (nutrient)</a></div>
<p>Proteins compose over 50% of the dry weight of an average living cell<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and are very complex macromolecules. They also play a fundamental role in the structure and function of cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Consisting mainly of carbon, nitrogen, hydrogen, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, they also may contain <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>.</p>
<p>In food, proteins are essential for growth and survival, and requirements vary depending upon a person's age and <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> (e.g., pregnancy). Protein is commonly obtained from animal sources: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">Nuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> provide vegetable sources of protein, and <a href="/wiki/Protein_combining" title="Protein combining">protein combining</a> of vegetable sources is used to achieve <a href="/wiki/Complete_protein" title="Complete protein">complete protein</a> nutritional quotas from vegetables.</p>
<p>Protein sensitivity as <a href="/wiki/Food_allergy" title="Food allergy">food allergy</a> is detected with the <a href="/wiki/ELISA" title="ELISA">ELISA</a> test.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enzymes">Enzymes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Enzymes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">Enzyme</a></div>
<p>Enzymes are biochemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> used in converting processes from one substance to another. They are also involved in reducing the amount of time and energy required to complete a chemical process. Many aspects of the food industry use catalysts, including <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">fruit juices</a>, to make cheese, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vitamins">Vitamins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vitamins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">Vitamin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Riboflavin.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="188" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Riboflavin.png/120px-Riboflavin.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Riboflavin.png/180px-Riboflavin.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Riboflavin.png 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Riboflavin.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Riboflavin (Vitamin B<sub>2</sub>), water-soluble.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Vitamins are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a> required in small amounts for essential metabolic reactions in the body. These are broken down in nutrition as either water-soluble (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">Vitamin C</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>-soluble (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_E" title="Vitamin E">Vitamin E</a>). An adequate supply of vitamins can prevent diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Beriberi" title="Beriberi">beriberi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a> while an overdose of vitamins can produce <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a> or even death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minerals">Minerals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Minerals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral">Dietary mineral</a></div>
<p>Dietary minerals in foods are large and diverse with many required to function while other trace elements can be hazardous if consumed in excessive amounts. Bulk minerals with a <a href="/wiki/Reference_Daily_Intake" title="Reference Daily Intake">Reference Daily Intake</a> (RDI, formerly Recommended Daily Allowance (RDA)) of more than 200 mg/day are <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> while important trace minerals (RDI less than 200 mg/day) are copper, iron, and zinc. These are found in many foods, but can also be taken in dietary supplements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">Food coloring</a></div>
<p>Food coloring is added to change the color of any food substance. It is mainly for sensory analysis purposes. It can be used to simulate the natural color of a product as perceived by the customer, such as red dye (like FD&amp;C Red No.40 Allura Red AC) to ketchup or to add unnatural colors to a product like <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s" title="Kellogg's">Kellogg's</a> <a href="/wiki/Froot_Loops" title="Froot Loops">Froot Loops</a>. Caramel is a natural food dye; the industrial form, caramel coloring, is the most widely used food coloring and is found in foods from soft drinks to soy sauce, bread, and <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickles</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flavors">Flavors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flavors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">Flavor</a></div>
<p>Flavor in food is important in how food <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smells</a> and tastes to the consumer, especially in sensory analysis. Some of these products occur naturally like <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, but flavor chemists (called a "<a href="/wiki/Flavorist" title="Flavorist">flavorist</a>") develop many of these flavors for food products. Such artificial flavors include <a href="/wiki/Methyl_salicylate" title="Methyl salicylate">methyl salicylate</a> which creates the <a href="/wiki/Wintergreen" title="Wintergreen">wintergreen</a> odor and lactic acid which gives milk a tart taste.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_additives">Food additives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Food additives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">Food additive</a></div>
<p>Food additives are substances added to food for preserving flavors, or improving taste or appearance. The processes are as old as adding <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a> for <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickling</a> or as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsifier" title="Emulsifier">emulsifier</a> for <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a> mixtures like <a href="/wiki/Mayonnaise" title="Mayonnaise">mayonnaise</a>. These are generally listed by "<a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E number</a>" in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRAS" title="GRAS">GRAS</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a>") by the United States <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a>.</p>
