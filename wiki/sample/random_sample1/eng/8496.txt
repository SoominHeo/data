<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Dieu et mon droit</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><i><b>Dieu et mon droit</b></i> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[djø e mɔ̃ dʁwa]</a></span>), meaning <i>God and my right</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royal_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-royal-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup> or literally <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dieu_et_mon_droit" title="fr:Dieu et mon droit">fr:Dieu et mon droit</a> (My divine right) is the motto of the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">Monarch of the United Kingdom</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup> outside Scotland. It appears on a scroll beneath the shield of the version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Coat of arms of the United Kingdom">coat of arms of the United Kingdom</a> used outside Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royal_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-royal-1">[1]</a></sup> The motto is said to have first been used by <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">Richard I</a> (1157-1199) as a battle cry and presumed to be a reference to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine_Right_of_Kings" title="Divine Right of Kings">divine right of the Monarch to govern</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frame_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-frame-3">[3]</a></sup> It was adopted as the royal motto of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">King Henry V</a> (1386-1422)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frame_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-frame-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker2010-4">[4]</a></sup> with the phrase "and my right" referring to his <a href="/wiki/English_claims_to_the_French_throne" title="English claims to the French throne">claim to the French crown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker2010_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barker2010-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieu_et_mon_droit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The motto is French for literally "God and my right",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup> meaning that the king is "Rex Angliae <i>Dei gratia</i>":<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> King of England <i>by the grace of God</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup> It is used to imply that the monarch of a nation has a God-given (divine) right to rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the Royal coat of arms of the Kingdom of England to have a French rather than English motto was not unusual, given that <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Norman French</a> was the primary language of the English <a href="/wiki/Court_(royal)" title="Court (royal)">Royal Court</a> and ruling class following the rule of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> of Normandy and later the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantagenet" title="Plantagenet">Plantagenets</a>. Another Old French phrase also appears in the full achievement of the Royal Arms. The motto of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Order of the Garter</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Honi_soit_qui_mal_y_pense" title="Honi soit qui mal y pense">Honi soit qui mal y pense</a></i> ("Spurned be the one who evil thinks"), appears on a representation of a <a href="/wiki/Garter_(stockings)" title="Garter (stockings)">garter</a> behind the shield. Modern French spelling has changed <i>honi</i> to <i>honni</i>, but the motto has not been updated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_translations">Other translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieu_et_mon_droit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Dieu et mon droit</i> has been translated in several ways, including "God and my right," "God and my right hand,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> "God and my lawful right,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and "God and my right shall me defend."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The literal <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translation</a> of <i>Dieu et mon droit</i> is "God and my right".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup> However, <i>Kearsley's Complete Peerage</i>, published in 1799, translates it to mean "God and my right hand". (In standard French that would be <i>Dieu et la [main] droite</i>, not <i>mon droit.</i>) The Kearsley volume appeared during publication of the 1st edition (1796–1808) of the German <i>Brockhaus Encyclopedia</i>, which emphasised the raising of the "right hand" during installations and coronations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kings_of_Germany" title="Kings of Germany">German Kings</a>.</p>
<p>Diderot's <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a></i> lists the motto as <i>Dieu est mon droit</i>, translated as "God is my right".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_royal_motto">Use as royal motto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieu_et_mon_droit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use as royal motto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Dieu et mon droit</i> has generally been used as the motto of English—and later British (outside Scotland)—monarchs since being adopted by <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">Henry V</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morana_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-morana-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker2010_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barker2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pine-53_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pine-53-15">[15]</a></sup> It was first used as a <a href="/wiki/Battle_cry" title="Battle cry">battle cry</a> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">King Richard I</a> in 1198 at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gisors" title="Battle of Gisors">Battle of Gisors</a>, when he defeated the forces of <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II of France</a> and after he made it his motto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pine-53_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pine-53-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The belief in medieval Europe was not that victory automatically went to the side with the better army but that, as with personal <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_combat" title="Trial by combat">trial by combat</a>, to the side that God viewed with favour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Hence Richard wrote after his victory "It is not us who have done it but God and our right through us".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pine-53_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pine-53-15">[15]</a></sup> So after his victories on the crusades "Richard was speaking what he believed to be the truth when he told the Holy Roman Emperor: 'I am born of a rank which recognises no superior but God'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternatively, the Royal Arms may depict a monarch's personal motto. For example, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a>'s often displayed <i>Semper Eadem</i>; <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "Always the same",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I</a>'s depicted <i>Beati Pacifici</i>, Latin for "Blessed are the peacemakers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_usages">Current usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dieu_et_mon_droit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Dieu et mon droit</i> has been adopted along with the rest of the royal coat of arms by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> as part of its <a href="/wiki/Masthead_(publishing)" title="Masthead (publishing)">masthead</a>. In 1785 when it incorporated the royal coat of arms half the newspapers in London were doing so. Since 1982 the paper abandoned the use of the current royal coat of arms and returned to using the Hanoverian coat of arms of 1785.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Versions of the coat of arms, with the motto, are also used by other newspapers, including Melbourne's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a></i> in Australia, <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Press" title="The Press">The Press</a></i> in New Zealand, the UK's <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> and Canada's <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Standard" title="Toronto Standard">Toronto Standard</a></i>.</p>
<p>It is also found on the official belt buckle of the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Constabulary_Force" title="Jamaica Constabulary Force">Jamaica Constabulary Force</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the front page of a <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passport</a>, the arms of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Victoria" title="Supreme Court of Victoria">Supreme Court of Victoria</a>, and the crests of Hawthorn Rowing Club in Melbourne, Australia, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Law_School" title="Nottingham Law School">Nottingham Law School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sherborne_School" title="Sherborne School">Sherborne School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The coined phrase was as well used by Michael Jackson at his Neverland Ranch's front gates. The crest on the main gates carried the Royal coat of arms of the United Kingdom, along with the phrase at the bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Formerly included in scroll, on the Western Australia Police Force coat of arms before being replaced with "Protect and Serve" when the organisation changed its name from Western Australia Police Force to Western Australia Police Service.</p>

(April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Nilsson" title="Harry Nilsson">Harry Nilsson</a>'s 1975 album <i><a href="/wiki/Duit_on_Mon_Dei" title="Duit on Mon Dei">Duit on Mon Dei</a></i>.</p>
