<p><b>Shingū</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">新宮市</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shingū-shi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Japan" title="Cities of Japan">city</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Wakayama_Prefecture" title="Wakayama Prefecture">Wakayama Prefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The city was founded on October 1, 1933.</p>
<p>As of May 1, 2011, the city has an estimated <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of 32,288, with 16,003 <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">households</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> of 126.41 persons per km². The total area is 255.43 km<sup>2</sup> (98.62 sq mi).</p>
<p>Shingū literally means 'New Shrine' and refers to <a href="/wiki/Kumano_Hayatama_Taisha" title="Kumano Hayatama Taisha">Hayatama Shrine</a>, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kumano_Sanzan" title="Kumano Sanzan">Three Grand Shrines of Kumano</a> (See<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>). The 'old shrine' would be Kamikura Shrine.</p>
<p>Shingū is the central commercial city of the <a href="/wiki/Kumano_Region" title="Kumano Region">Kumano Region</a> in Japan where <a href="/wiki/Shing%C5%AB_Station" title="Shingū Station">Shingū Station</a> is situated, and is one of the largest cities in Wakayama Prefecture.</p>
<p>It is a sister city to <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_California" title="Santa Cruz, California">Santa Cruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each Spring, The City of Santa Cruz sends about 14 middle school/ high school delegates to visit Shingū and to interact with the Japanese society.</p>
<p>On October 1, 2005 the town of <a href="/wiki/Kumanogawa,_Wakayama" title="Kumanogawa, Wakayama">Kumanogawa</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Higashimuro_District,_Wakayama" title="Higashimuro District, Wakayama">Higashimuro District</a>) was merged with Shingū.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sightseeing">Sightseeing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shing%C5%AB,_Wakayama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sightseeing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shingū Castle, a.k.a. Tankaku Castle. Only the foundations remain, for during the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, it was torn down.</p>
<p>For over a thousand years, pilgrims have <a href="/wiki/Kumano_Kod%C5%8D" title="Kumano Kodō">traveled to the Kumano Region</a> to pray at The Three Sacred Kumano Shrines: Hongu, Hayatama, and Nachi. Hayatama Grand Shrine is located in Northwest corner of the original town. It is believed that the city started being referred to as "Shingū" (literally "new shrine"), when this shrine was first established. Hayatama Grand Shrine predates Japan's earliest written records, the <a href="/wiki/Kojiki" title="Kojiki">Kojiki</a>, so there is no way to establish the year it was founded. However, excavations have unearthed remains of religious <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">rituals</a> dating back as far as 300 AD.</p>
<p>Kamikura Shrine commands one of the best views of the city, and is said to be where the 12 <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deities</a> of the Kumano Grand Shrines first descended from the heavens. The object of worship is a great sacred rock called "<span id="Gotobiki-iwa">Gotobiki-iwa</span>".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kumano_River" title="Kumano River">Kumano River</a>- arguably one of the most beautiful rivers in all of Japan</p>
<p>Aikido- One of the "birthplaces" of <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">Aikido</a> (a martial art). Aikido is widely practiced throughout the Kumano region and in many areas throughout the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shing%C5%AB,_Wakayama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A main dish is Nare-zushi, a very traditional type of sushi which, centuries ago, was imported from Southeast Asia to China, and from China to Japan. Narezushi was the birth of sushi, and influenced how the Japanese later made their own styles of sushi that most of us know today.</p>
<p>Another common dish is saury. Saury is a type of fish commonly used as Sashimi in this region. Whale and dolphin meat caught in Taiji-cho (13 mi (21 km) away), is also eaten by a small few.</p>
