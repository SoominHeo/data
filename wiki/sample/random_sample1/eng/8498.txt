<p><i><b>The City of God Against the Pagans</b></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>De ciuitate Dei contra paganos</i></span>), often called <i><b>The City of God</b></i>, is a book of <a href="/wiki/Christian_philosophy" title="Christian philosophy">Christian philosophy</a> written in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a> in the early 5th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">AD</a>. The book was in response to allegations that Christianity brought about the decline of Rome and is considered one of Augustine's most important works, standing alongside <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confessions_(St._Augustine)" title="Confessions (St. Augustine)">The Confessions</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Enchiridion_of_Augustine" title="Enchiridion of Augustine">The Enchiridion</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/De_doctrina_christiana" title="De doctrina christiana">On Christian Doctrine</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Trinity" title="On the Trinity">On the Trinity</a></i>. As a work of one of the most influential <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">Church Fathers</a>, <i>The City of God</i> is a cornerstone of Western thought, expounding on many profound questions of theology, such as the suffering of the righteous, the existence of evil, the conflict between free will and divine omniscience, and the doctrine of original sin.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_City_of_God_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(410)" title="Sack of Rome (410)">sack of Rome</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> in 410 left Romans in a deep state of shock, and many Romans saw it as punishment for abandoning <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">traditional Roman religion</a> for <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. In response to these accusations, and in order to console Christians, Augustine wrote <i>The City of God</i>, arguing for the truth of Christianity over competing religions and philosophies and that Christianity was not responsible for the Sack of Rome, but instead was responsible for its success. He attempted to console Christians, writing that even if the earthly rule of the Empire was imperiled, it was the City of God that would ultimately triumph. Augustine's eyes were fixed on <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a>, a theme of many Christian works of <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>, and despite Christianity's designation as the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Thessalonica" title="Edict of Thessalonica">official religion of the Empire</a>, Augustine declared its message to be spiritual rather than political. Christianity, he argued, should be concerned with the mystical, heavenly city, the <a href="/wiki/New_Jerusalem" title="New Jerusalem">New Jerusalem</a>, rather than with earthly politics.</p>
<p>The book presents human <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> as a conflict between what Augustine calls the Earthly City (often colloquially referred to as the City of Man) and the City of God, a conflict that is destined to end in victory for the latter. The City of God is marked by people who forgot earthly pleasure to dedicate themselves to the eternal truths of God, now revealed fully in the Christian faith. The Earthly City, on the other hand, consists of people who have immersed themselves in the cares and pleasures of the present, passing world.</p>
<p>Augustine’s thesis depicts the history of the world as universal warfare between God and the Devil. This metaphysical war is not limited by time but only by geography on Earth. In this war, God moves (by divine intervention/ <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">Providence</a>) those governments, political /ideological movements and military forces aligned (or aligned the most) with the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> (the City of God) in order to oppose by all means—including military—those governments, political/ideological movements and military forces aligned (or aligned the most) with the Devil (the City of Devil).</p>
<p>Many Catholics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> consider <a href="/wiki/Jacques-B%C3%A9nigne_Bossuet" title="Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet">Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet</a>'s <i>Discours sur l'histoire universelle</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Speech_of_Universal_History" title="Speech of Universal History">Speech of Universal History</a></i> (1681) to be a second edition or continuation of <i>The City of God</i>. This book updates universal history according to Augustine’s thesis of universal war between those humans that follow God and those who follow the Devil.</p>
<p>This concept of world history guided by Divine Providence in a universal war between God and Devil is part of the official doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> as most recently stated in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council's</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaudium_et_Spes" title="Gaudium et Spes">Gaudium et Spes</a></i> document: "The Church ... holds that in her most benign Lord and Master can be found the key, the focal point and the goal of man, as well as of all human history ... all of human life, whether individual or collective, shows itself to be a dramatic struggle between good and evil, between light and darkness ... The Lord is the goal of human history the focal point of the longings of history and of civilization, the center of the human race, the joy of every heart and the answer to all its yearnings."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_City_of_God_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Augustine provides a brief description of the contents of the work:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>However, this great undertaking was at last completed in twenty-two books. Of these, the first five refute those who fancy that the polytheistic worship is necessary in order to secure worldly prosperity, and that all these overwhelming calamities have befallen us in consequence of its prohibition. In the following five books I address myself to those who admit that such calamities have at all times attended, and will at all times attend, the human race, and that they constantly recur in forms more or less disastrous, varying only in the scenes, occasions, and persons on whom they light, but, while admitting this, maintain that the worship of the gods is advantageous for the life to come. But that no one might have occasion to say, that though I had refuted the tenets of other men, I had omitted to establish my own, I devote to this object the second part of this work, which comprises twelve books, although I have not scrupled, as occasion offered, either to advance my own opinions in the first ten books, or to demolish the arguments of my opponents in the last twelve. Of these twelve books, the first four contain an account of the origin of these two cities—the city of God, and the city of the world. The second four treat of their history or progress; the third and last four, of their deserved destinies.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Augustine, <i>Retractions</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>As indicated in the above passage from the <i>Retractions</i>, the <i>City of God</i> can be further subdivided into the following parts:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Part I (Books I–X): a polemical critique of Roman religion and philosophy, corresponding to the Earthly City
<dl>
<dd>Book I–V: A critique of pagan religion
<dl>
<dd>Book I: a criticism of the pagans who attribute the sack of Rome to Christianity despite being saved by taking refuge in Christian churches. The book also explains good and bad things happen to righteous and wicked people alike, and it consoles the women violated in the recent calamity.</dd>
<dd>Book II: a proof that because of the worship of the pagan gods, Rome suffered the greatest calamity of all, that is, moral corruption.</dd>
<dd>Book III: a proof that the pagan gods failed to save Rome numerous times in the past from worldly disasters, such as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Allia" title="Battle of the Allia">sack of Rome by the Gauls</a>.</dd>
<dd>Book IV: a proof that the power and long duration of the Roman empire was due not to the pagan Gods but to the Christian God.</dd>
<dd>Book V: a refutation of the doctrine of fate and an explanation of the Christian doctrine of free will and its consistency with God's omniscience. The book proves that Rome's dominion was due to the virtue of the Romans and explains the true happiness of the Christian emperors.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Book VI–X: A critique of pagan philosophy
<dl>
<dd>Book VI: a refutation of the assertion that the pagan gods are to be worshiped for eternal life (rather than temporal benefits). Augustine claimed that even the esteemed pagan theologist Varro held the gods in contempt.</dd>
<dd>Book VII: a demonstration that eternal life is not granted by Janus, Jupiter, Saturn, and other select gods.</dd>
<dd>Book VIII: an argument against the Platonists and their natural theology, which Augustine views as the closest approximation of Christian truth, and a refutation of <a href="/wiki/Apuleius" title="Apuleius">Apuleius</a>' insistence of the worship of demons as mediators between God and man.</dd>
<dd>Book IX: a proof that all demons are evil and that only Christ can provide man with eternal happiness.</dd>
<dd>Book X: a teaching that the good angels wish that God alone is worshiped and a proof that no sacrifice can lead to purification except that of Christ.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Part II (Books XI-XXII): discussion on the City of God and its relationship to the Earthly City
<dl>
<dd>Books XI-XIV: the origins of the two cities
<dl>
<dd>Book XI: the origins of the two cities from the separation of the good and bad angels, and a detailed analysis of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a> 1.</dd>
<dd>Book XII: answers to why some angels are good and others bad, and a close examination of the creation of man.</dd>
<dd>Book XIII: teaching that death originated as a penalty for Adam's sin.</dd>
<dd>Book XIV: teachings on the original sin as the cause for future lust and shame as a just punishment for lust.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Books XV-XVIII: the history or progress of the two cities, including foundational theological principles about Jews.
<dl>
<dd>Book XV: an analysis of the events in Genesis between the time of <a href="/wiki/Cain_and_Abel" title="Cain and Abel">Cain and Abel</a> to the time of the flood.</dd>
<dd>Book XVI: the progress of the two cities from <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> to <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, and the progress of the heavenly city from Abraham to the kings of Israel.</dd>
<dd>Book XVII: the history of the city of God from <a href="/wiki/Samuel" title="Samuel">Samuel</a> to <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> and to Christ, and Christological interpretations of the prophecies in <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Kings" title="Books of Kings">Kings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a>.</dd>
<dd>Book XVIII: the parallel history of the earthly and heavenly cities from Abraham to the end. Doctrine of Witness, that Jews received prophecy predicting Jesus, and that Jews are dispersed among the nations to provide independent testimony of the Hebrew Scriptures.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Books XIX-XXII: the deserved destinies of the two cities.
<dl>
<dd>Book XIX: the end of the two cities, and the happiness of the people of Christ.</dd>
<dd>Book XX: the prophecies of the Last Judgment in the Old and New Testaments.</dd>
<dd>Book XXI: the eternal punishment for the city of the devil.</dd>
<dd>Book XXII: the eternal happiness for the saints and explanations of the resurrection of the body.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
