<p><i><b>Acer ginnala</b></i> (<b>Amur maple</b>) is a plant species with woody stems native to northeastern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> from easternmost <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and north to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" title="Amur River">Amur River</a> valley. It is a small <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a> with deciduous leaves that is sometimes grown as a garden subject or boulevard tree.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_ginnala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Acer ginnala</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> spreading <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or small <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> growing to 3â10Â m tall, with a short trunk up to 20â40Â cm diameter and slender branches. The <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> is thin, dull gray-brown, and smooth at first but becoming shallowly fissured on old plants. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are opposite and simple, 4â10Â cm long and 3-6 wide, deeply palmately lobed with three or five lobes, of which two small basal lobes (sometimes absent) and three larger apical lobes; the lobes are coarsely and irregularly toothed, and the upper leaf surface glossy. The leaves turn brilliant orange to red in autumn, and are on slender, often pink-tinged, petioles 3â5Â cm long. The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are yellow-green, 5â8Â mm diameter, produced in spreading panicles in spring as the leaves open. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is a paired reddish <a href="/wiki/Samara_(fruit)" title="Samara (fruit)">samara</a>, 8â10Â mm long with a 1.5â2Â cm wing, maturing in late summer to early autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushforth_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_ginnala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amur maple is closely related to <i><a href="/wiki/Acer_tataricum" title="Acer tataricum">Acer tataricum</a></i> (Tatar maple), and some botanists treat it as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>A. tataricum</i> subsp. <i>ginnala</i> (Maxim.) Wesm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-grin-3">[3]</a></sup> The glossy, deeply lobed leaves of <i>A. ginnala</i> distinguish it from <i>A. tataricum</i>, which has matte, unlobed or only shallowly lobed leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushforth_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_ginnala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Acer ginnala</i> is grown as an <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a> in northern regions of Europe and North America. It is the most cold-tolerant maple, hardy to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USDA_hardiness_zone" title="USDA hardiness zone">zone 2</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Naturalisation_(biology)" title="Naturalisation (biology)">naturalised</a> in parts of North America. Planted on exceptional sites facing south west with consistent moisture and light loamy soils, this tree can grow 3 to 4 feet per year making it a fast grower. It is often planted as a shrub along borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-usda-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK it has gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">Royal Horticultural Society</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also valued in Japan and elsewhere as a species suitable for <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">bonsai</a>. It is a nonnative <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in parts of northern America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivars">Cultivars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acer_ginnala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultivars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its vigor and fall colors of yellows and bright reds, the size being a small tree of 20 feet wide by 20 feet tall on average, it suits many for smaller landscapes and for planting under power lines. Cultivars have emerged for those wanting these attributes.</p>
