<p><b>Shin Ramyun</b> (commonly mistaken as Shin Ramen, Shin Ramen Noodle, or Shin Ramien) is a brand of <a href="/wiki/Instant_noodle" title="Instant noodle">instant noodle</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Cup_Ramyeon" title="Cup Ramyeon">cup ramyeon</a>) that is produced by a South Korean food company <a href="/wiki/Nongshim" title="Nongshim">Nongshim</a> since October, 1986. It is now exported to over 100 countries, and is the highest selling instant noodle brand in South Korea.</p>
<p>Shin Ramyun is well known for its spicy flavor. It is produced in two kinds: Shin Ramyun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the original one, and Shin Ramyun Black,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which was introduced in 2011. A standard package of Shin Ramyun consists of noodles, a sachet of flavoring powder (soup base), and a sachet of vegetable flakes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shin Ramyun was introduced in October 1986 by <a href="/wiki/Nongshim" title="Nongshim">Nongshim</a>. The Nongshim R&amp;D team came up with the idea of Sogogijanguk, a Cabbage and Beef Stew, which is one of the most popular traditional South Korean dishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.EC.8B.A0.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.2C_.EB.8D.94_.EC.AB.84.EA.B9.83.ED.95.B4.EC.A7.84_.EB.A9.B4.EB.B0.9C.E2.80.A6.EC.84.B8.EA.B3.84.EC.9D.B8_.EC.9A.B8.EB.A6.AC.EB.8A.94_.27.EA.B5.AD.EA.B0.80.EB.8C.80.ED.91.9C_.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.27_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-.EC.8B.A0.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.2C_.EB.8D.94_.EC.AB.84.EA.B9.83.ED.95.B4.EC.A7.84_.EB.A9.B4.EB.B0.9C.E2.80.A6.EC.84.B8.EA.B3.84.EC.9D.B8_.EC.9A.B8.EB.A6.AC.EB.8A.94_.27.EA.B5.AD.EA.B0.80.EB.8C.80.ED.91.9C_.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.27-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Shin Ramyun was introduced, Nongshim’s market share hit 46.3% in 1987, and exceeded 50% for the first time in 1988 (53.8%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With the market share of over 20% just by itself, Shin Ramyun is a leading brand of the instant noodles in Korea.</p>
<p>In 2015, Shin Ramyun has achieved 28 billion units sold since it was first introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Shin Ramyun is listed on the National Brand Consumption Index (NBCI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as the number 1 brand in South Korea (2012~2016) for its brand awareness and brand power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_Package">Name and Package</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name and Package">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name of Shin Ramyun is from a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese character</a> Shin (<a href="/wiki/Radical_160" title="Radical 160">辛</a>), which means “spicy.” Shin Ramyun uses red and black packaging with the emphasized calligraphic word “辛”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.EC.8B.A0.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.2C_.EB.8D.94_.EC.AB.84.EA.B9.83.ED.95.B4.EC.A7.84_.EB.A9.B4.EB.B0.9C.E2.80.A6.EC.84.B8.EA.B3.84.EC.9D.B8_.EC.9A.B8.EB.A6.AC.EB.8A.94_.27.EA.B5.AD.EA.B0.80.EB.8C.80.ED.91.9C_.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.27_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-.EC.8B.A0.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.2C_.EB.8D.94_.EC.AB.84.EA.B9.83.ED.95.B4.EC.A7.84_.EB.A9.B4.EB.B0.9C.E2.80.A6.EC.84.B8.EA.B3.84.EC.9D.B8_.EC.9A.B8.EB.A6.AC.EB.8A.94_.27.EA.B5.AD.EA.B0.80.EB.8C.80.ED.91.9C_.EB.9D.BC.EB.A9.B4.27-3">[3]</a></sup> The meaning of the Chinese character is shown on the background of the package. Nongshim decided to emphasize the Chinese character Shin (辛) for their brand with a belief that a single Chinese character delivers the brand image better than written in Korean. Additionally, the character is the surname of both the founder of Nongshim and his elder brother, who started <a href="/wiki/Lotte_(conglomerate)" title="Lotte (conglomerate)">Lotte</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shin Ramyun was first introduced in 1986. The main ingredients of Shin Ramyun are beef and seasoned red-pepper sauce. In the U.S, there are two types of Shin Ramyun: a Package type and a cup/bowl type. A package of Shin Ramyun is 120g, and there are 4 sizes of Shin Ramyun cup/bowls: Shin Cup Noodle Soup (68g), Shin Bowl Noodle Soup (86g), Shin Ramyun M-Cup (75g) and Shin Big Bowl Noodle Soup (114g). In Japan, there is Shin Ramyun mini cup.</p>
<p>Shin Ramyun Black was introduced in April 2011, which was 25 years after Shin Ramyun was first introduced to the market. Shin Ramyun Black is a premium version of Shin Ramyun with an additional seolleongtang (Hangul: <a href="/wiki/Seolleongtang" title="Seolleongtang">설렁탕</a>; Meaning: Ox Bone Soup) powder on top of its significant spicy flavor. The vegetable flakes include boiled beef slices, garlic and shitake mushrooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the U.S. there are two types of Shin Ramyun Black: a Package type (130g) and a cup/bowl type (Shin Black Cup Noodle, 101g).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_distribution">International distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shin Ramyun is the most popular instant noodle brand to date in South Korea. It is taking one quarter of the Korean instant noodle market size. Shin Ramyun is now exported to over 100 countries around the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> and is produced in three countries: the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. As of 2015, accumulated sold units of Shin Ramyun in the world reached 28 billion units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_and_Advertising">Marketing and Advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Marketing and Advertising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_in_South_Korea">Marketing in South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marketing in South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As part of the marketing strategies, Nongshim uses “사나이 울리는 신라면 (Romanization: Sanai Ullineun Shin Ramyun; translation: Shin Ramyun can make a man cry<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nongshimusa.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nongshimusa.com-10">[10]</a></sup>)”. The word Sanai (Hangul: 사나이) is a word to describe the man while emphasizing the masculineness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of its commercials include a famous male celebrity, frequently with his family, who is eating Shin Ramyun at home. These commercials emphasize being family friendly, being Korean, and being folksiness. <a href="/wiki/Psy" title="Psy">Psy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> a South Korean singer who is well known for his song "<a href="/wiki/Gangnam_Style" title="Gangnam Style">Gangnam Style</a>," and <a href="/wiki/Park_Ji-sung" title="Park Ji-sung">Park Ji-Sung</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a South Korean footballer, also filmed Shin Ramyun commercials.</p>
<p>Nongshim has many jingles for their products. Adding a simple yet catching jingle at the end of their commercials is one of Nongshim’s important marketing strategies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup> They are easy to remember, and most people in South Korea are aware of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_in_China">Marketing in China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marketing in China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In China, Nongshim uses a slogan: 사나이라면 매운맛을 먹을 줄 알아야지 (Traditional Chinese: 吃不了辣味非好漢; Translation: You better know how to handle the spiciness if you are a true man).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup> This slogan is from a famous phrase in China “不到長城非好漢 (He who has never been to the Great Wall is not a true man)” by <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Being aware of the historical importance of <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">Baduk</a> (Traditional Chinese: <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">圍棋</a>; Japanese: <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">囲碁</a>) in China, Nongshim has been sponsoring an annual Baduk Championship, “<a class="extiw" href="https://ko.wikipedia.org/wiki/%EB%86%8D%EC%8B%AC_%EC%8B%A0%EB%9D%BC%EB%A9%B4%EB%B0%B0_%EC%84%B8%EA%B3%84%EB%B0%94%EB%91%91_%EC%B5%9C%EA%B0%95%EC%A0%84" title="ko:농심 신라면배 세계바둑 최강전">Shin Ramyun Cup Baduk Championship</a>” as part of their marketing strategies since 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_in_Japan">Marketing in Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shin_Ramyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marketing in Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Japan, Nongshim has set April 10 as a Shin Ramyun day since 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[18]</a></sup> The date was chosen for its similarity in pronunciation with “Hot (Japanese: ホット)” when a Japanese pronounces 4 and 10 in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Japanese word “うまからっ<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>” (Romanization: umakarat; Translation: Spicy yet tasty; Etymology: Portmanteau of two Japanese words “うまい (Romanization: umai; Translation: Tasty)" and “からい (Romanization: karai; Translation: Spicy)”) is used to describe the flavor of Shin Ramyun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of the main marketing projects, Nongshim offers “Shin Ramyun Kitchen Car,” a food truck that offers consumers a chance to taste Shin Ramyun, since 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[18]</a></sup> Every year, the truck travels across Japan for seven months, promoting Shin Ramyun to Japanese consumers. As of 2016 April, the truck has hosted a total of 150 tasting sessions, and travelled more than 100,000 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
