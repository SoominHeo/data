<p>A <b>CD single</b> (sometimes abbreviated to <b>CDS</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">music single</a> in the form of a standard size <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a>. It is not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Mini_CD_single" title="Mini CD single">Mini CD single</a>, which uses a smaller form factor and has also been referred to as just a CD Single on some occasions. The format was introduced in the mid-1980s but did not gain its place in the market until the early 1990s. With the rise in digital downloads in the early 2010s, sales of CD singles have decreased.</p>
<p>Commercially released CD singles can vary in length from two songs (an A side and B side, in the tradition of 7" 45rpm records) up to six songs like an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>. Some contain multiple mixes of one or more songs (known as <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remixes</a>), in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" title="12-inch single">12" vinyl singles</a>, and in some cases, they may also contain a <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for the single itself as well as a collectible <a href="/wiki/Poster" title="Poster">poster</a>. Depending on the nation, there may be limits on the number of songs and total length for sales to count in singles charts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms_(song)" title="Brothers in Arms (song)">Brothers in Arms</a>" (1985) is reported to be the world's first CD single,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> issued in the UK in two separate singles as a promotional item, one distinguished with a logo for the tour, Live in '85, and a second to commemorate the Australian leg of the tour marked Live in '86. Containing four tracks, it had a very limited print run. CD singles were first made eligible for the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> in 1987, and the first number 1 available on the format in that country was "<a href="/wiki/I_Wanna_Dance_with_Somebody_(Who_Loves_Me)" title="I Wanna Dance with Somebody (Who Loves Me)">I Wanna Dance with Somebody (Who Loves Me)</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">Whitney Houston</a> in May 1987.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mini_CD_single" title="Mini CD single">Mini CD single</a> format was originally created for use for singles in the late 1980s, but met with limited success, particularly in the US. The smaller CDs were more successful in Japan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSCDNews1989_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSCDNews1989-3">[3]</a></sup> and have recently become more common in Europe, marketed as "Pocket" CD's for being small enough to fit in a shirt pocket. By 1989, the CD3 was in decline in the US (replaced by the 5-inch CD single, called CD-5).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSCDNews1989_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSCDNews1989-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was common in the 1990s for US record companies to release both a two-track CD and a multi-track (usually "remix") maxi CD. In the UK, record companies would also release two CDs but, usually, these consisted of three tracks or more each.</p>
<p>During the 1990s, CD single releases became less common in certain countries and were often released in smaller editions, as the major record labels feared they were cannibalizing the sales of higher-profit-margin CD albums. Pressure from record labels made singles charts in some countries become song charts, allowing album cuts to chart based only on airplay, without a single ever being released. In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> made this change in December 1998, after which very few songs were released in the CD single format in the US, but they remained extremely popular in the UK and other countries, where charts were still based solely on single sales and not radio airplay. At the end of the 1990s, the CD was the biggest-selling single format in the UK, but in the US, the dominant single format was airplay. With the advent of digital music sales, the CD single has largely been replaced as a distribution format in most countries, and most charts now include digital download counts as well as physical single sales.</p>
<p>In Australia, the <i><a href="/wiki/Herald_Sun" title="Herald Sun">Herald Sun</a></i> reported the CD single is "set to become extinct". In early July 2009, leading music store <a href="/wiki/JB_Hi-Fi" title="JB Hi-Fi">JB Hi-Fi</a> ceased stocking CD singles because of declining sales, with copies of the week's No. 1 single often selling as few as only 350 copies across all their stores nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While CD singles no longer maintain their own section of the store, copies are still distributed but placed with the artist's albums. That is predominantly the case for popular Australian artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Mauboy" title="Jessica Mauboy">Jessica Mauboy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kylie_Minogue" title="Kylie Minogue">Kylie Minogue</a> and, most recently, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Goodrem" title="Delta Goodrem">Delta Goodrem</a>, whose recent singles ("<a href="/wiki/What_Happened_to_Us" title="What Happened to Us">What Happened to Us</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Put_Your_Hands_Up_(If_You_Feel_Love)" title="Put Your Hands Up (If You Feel Love)">Put Your Hands Up (If You Feel Love)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Sitting_on_Top_of_the_World_(Delta_Goodrem_song)" title="Sitting on Top of the World (Delta Goodrem song)">Sitting on Top of the World</a>" respectively) were released on CD in limited quantities. The <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">ARIA Singles Chart</a> are now "predominantly compiled from legal downloads", and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">ARIA</a> also stopped compiling their physical singles sales chart. "<a href="/wiki/On_a_Mission_(song)" title="On a Mission (song)">On a Mission</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Gabriella_Cilmi" title="Gabriella Cilmi">Gabriella Cilmi</a> was the last CD single to be stocked in <a href="/wiki/Kmart" title="Kmart">Kmart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Target_Australia" title="Target Australia">Target</a> and <a href="/wiki/Big_W" title="Big W">Big W</a>, who then concluded stocking newly released singles. <a href="/wiki/Sanity_(music_store)" title="Sanity (music store)">Sanity Entertainment</a>, having resisted the decline for longer than the other major outlets, has also ceased selling CD singles.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, CD single releases have been rare ever since the format was introduced, due of the amount of infringement and illegal file sharing over the internet, and most of the time singles have generally been album cuts chart based only on airplay, but with the advent of digital music the charts have also occasionally included digital download counts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, the term "CD single" is used to describe an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a> (EP) in which there may be anywhere from three to six different tracks. These releases charted on the <a href="/wiki/IFPI_Greece" title="IFPI Greece">Greek Singles Chart</a> (before it abandoned tracking altogether) with songs released as <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a>.</p>

<p>In September 2003, there was talk of ringtones for mobile phones outstripping CD singles sales for the year 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Woolworths_Group" title="Woolworths Group">Woolworths Group</a>, which previously accounted for one third of all CD sales in the country, stopped selling CD singles in August 2008, citing the "terminal decline" of the format as customers moved to digital downloads as their preferred method of purchasing single tracks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wallop_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wallop-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (the Woolworths chain itself would collapse the following November).</p>
<p>In July 2009, <i>The Guardian</i> reported that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florence_%2B_The_Machine" title="Florence + The Machine">Florence + The Machine</a>'s single '<a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Heart_(Raise_It_Up)" title="Rabbit Heart (Raise It Up)">Rabbit Heart (Raise It Up)</a>' sold a CD and 7" vinyl combined total of 64 copies, where it reached No. 16 in the Mid-Week Chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2011, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Records" title="Mercury Records">Mercury Records</a> announced that they were to stop manufacturing CD singles for lack of demand and loss of money on the format in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2012, selected <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a> stores sold a small selection of CD singles, mostly charity releases and acts from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_X_Factor_(UK)" title="The X Factor (UK)">The X Factor</a></i>.</p>
