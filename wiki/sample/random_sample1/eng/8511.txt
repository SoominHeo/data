<p>The <b>shoelace formula</b> or <b>shoelace algorithm</b> (also known as <b><a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Gauss</a>'s area formula</b> and the <b><a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">surveyor</a>'s formula</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braden_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braden-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a mathematical <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a> to determine the <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">simple polygon</a> whose vertices are described by their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates" title="Cartesian coordinates">Cartesian coordinates</a> in the plane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathreference_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mathreference-2">[2]</a></sup> The user <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">cross-multiplies</a> corresponding coordinates to find the area encompassing the polygon, and subtracts it from the surrounding polygon to find the area of the polygon within. It is called the shoelace formula because of the constant cross-multiplying for the coordinates making up the polygon, like tying shoelaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathreference_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mathreference-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also sometimes called the <b>shoelace method</b>. It has applications in surveying and forestry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pretzsch_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pretzsch-3">[3]</a></sup> among other areas.</p>
<p>The formula was described by Meister (1724-1788) in 1769<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Gauss</a> in 1795. It can be verified by dividing the polygon into triangles, but it can also be seen as a special case of <a href="/wiki/Green%27s_theorem" title="Green's theorem">Green's theorem</a>.</p>
<p>The area formula is derived by taking each edge <i>AB</i>, and calculating the (signed) area of triangle <i>ABO</i> with a vertex at the origin <i>O</i>, by taking the cross-product (which gives the area of a parallelogram) and dividing by 2. As one wraps around the polygon, these triangles with positive and negative area will overlap, and the areas between the origin and the polygon will be cancelled out and sum to 0, while only the area inside the reference triangle remains. This is why the formula is called the Surveyor's Formula, since the "surveyor" is at the origin; if going counterclockwise, positive area is added when going from left to right and negative area is added when going from right to left, from the perspective of the origin.</p>
<p>The area formula is valid for any non-self-intersecting (<a href="/wiki/Simple_polygon" title="Simple polygon">simple</a>) polygon, which can be convex or concave.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation_of_name">Explanation of name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shoelace_formula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Explanation of name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shoelace3.png"><img alt="Shoelace3.png" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1170" data-file-width="1102" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Shoelace3.png/220px-Shoelace3.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Shoelace3.png/330px-Shoelace3.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Shoelace3.png/440px-Shoelace3.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The reason this formula is called the shoelace formula is because of a common method used to evaluate it. This method uses <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrices</a>. As an example, choose the triangle with vertices (2,4), (3,−8), and (1,2). Then construct the following matrix by “walking around” the triangle and ending with the initial point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</math></span><img alt="{\begin{bmatrix}2&amp;4\\3&amp;-8\\1&amp;2\\2&amp;4\end{bmatrix}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6772190f3b828a737cd21c86521a3df97a2daba8" style="vertical-align: -5.671ex; width:10.36ex; height:12.509ex;"/></span></dd>

<p>First, draw diagonal down and to the right slashes (as shown below),</p>
<dl>
<dd>  <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ShoelaceMatrix2.GIF"><img alt="ShoelaceMatrix2.GIF" data-file-height="139" data-file-width="94" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/ShoelaceMatrix2.GIF" width="94"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>and multiply the two numbers connected by each slash, then add all the products: (2 × −8) + (3 × 2) + (1 × 4) = −6. Do the same thing with slashes diagonal down and to the left (shown below with downwards slashes):</p>
<dl>
<dd>  <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ShoelaceMatrix3.GIF"><img alt="ShoelaceMatrix3.GIF" data-file-height="146" data-file-width="96" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/ShoelaceMatrix3.GIF" width="96"/></a></dd>
</dl>
<p>(4 × 3) + (−8 × 1) + (2 × 2) = 8. Then take the difference of these two numbers: |(−6 )−( 8)| = 14. Halving this gives the area of the triangle: 7. Organizing the numbers like this makes the formula easier to recall and evaluate. With all the slashes drawn, the matrix loosely resembles a shoe with the laces done up, giving rise to the algorithm's name.</p>
