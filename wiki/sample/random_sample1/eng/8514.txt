<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bridesmaid</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>bridesmaids</b> are members of the bride's party in a wedding. A bridesmaid is typically a young woman, and often a close friend or sister. She attends to the <a href="/wiki/Bride" title="Bride">bride</a> on the day of a <a href="/wiki/Wedding" title="Wedding">wedding</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> ceremony. Traditionally, bridesmaids were chosen from unwed young women of <a href="/wiki/Marriageable_age" title="Marriageable age">marriageable age</a>.</p>
<p>The principal bridesmaid, if one is so designated, may be called the <b>chief bridesmaid</b> or <b>maid of honor</b> if she is unmarried, or the <b>matron of honor</b> if she is married. A <b>junior bridesmaid</b> is a girl who is clearly too young to be married, but who is included as an honorary bridesmaid. In the United States, typically only the maid/matron of honor and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_man" title="Best man">best man</a> are the official witnesses for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wedding_license" title="Wedding license">wedding license</a>.</p>
<p>Often there is more than one bridesmaid: in modern times the bride chooses how many to ask. Historically, no person of status went out unattended, and the size of the retinue was closely calculated to be appropriate to the family's social status. A large group of bridesmaids provided an opportunity for showing off the family's social status and <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">wealth</a>. Today, the number of bridesmaids in a wedding party is dependent on many variables, including a bride's preferences, the size of her family, and the number of attendants her partner would like to have as well.</p>
<p>The male equivalent is the <a href="/wiki/Groomsman" title="Groomsman">groomsman</a>, also known in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> as an usher; in the United States, the role of attending to the groom has diverged from that of escorting guests to their seats, and the two positions are no longer synonymous and are often if not usually filled by different persons.</p>
<p>In some cultures, such as in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian Britain</a>, it has been customary for bridesmaids to be small girls rather than grown women. They may carry flowers during the wedding procession and pose with the married couple in bridal photos. In modern English-speaking countries, this role is separate from that of the bridesmaid, and the small child performing it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Flower_girl" title="Flower girl">flower girl</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duties">Duties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridesmaid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Duties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Although many exceed the minimum, the bridesmaids' required duties are very limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-393-05874-3_pg_383_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-393-05874-3_pg_383-1">[1]</a></sup> They are required to attend the wedding ceremony and to assist the bride on the day of the wedding. Bridesmaids in Europe and North America are often asked to assist the bride with planning the wedding and a <a href="/wiki/Wedding_reception" title="Wedding reception">wedding reception</a>. In modern times, a bridesmaid often participates in planning wedding-related events, such as a <a href="/wiki/Bridal_shower" title="Bridal shower">bridal shower</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bachelorette_party" title="Bachelorette party">bachelorette party</a>, if there are any. These, however, are optional activities; according to etiquette expert <a href="/wiki/Judith_Martin" title="Judith Martin">Judith Martin</a>, "Contrary to rumor, bridesmaids are not obliged to entertain in honor of the bride, nor to wear dresses they cannot afford."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-609-60431-7_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-609-60431-7-2">[2]</a></sup> If it is customary in the bride's area to have a bridesmaids luncheon, then it is hosted, and therefore organized and paid for, by the bride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1922_Post_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-1922_Post-3">[3]</a></sup> A junior bridesmaid has no responsibilities beyond attending the wedding.</p>

<p>The duties and costs of being a bridesmaid are parsed out between a bride and her attendants in a variety of ways. Since modern bridesmaids, unlike their historical counterparts, can no longer rely on having their clothes and travel expenses paid for by the bride's family, and are sometimes even told they must pay for parties that the bride wants to have before the wedding, it has become customary for the bride to present the bridesmaids with gifts as a sign of gratitude for the support and financial commitment that comes with their roles. It has become equally customary for women who are invited to serve as bridesmaids to first ask about the amount of time, energy, and money that the bride expects from them before accepting this position, and to decline or resign if this is more than they will be able to give.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinberg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steinberg-4">[4]</a></sup> In some American weddings, each bridesmaid may be asked to spend US $1,700 or more, with travel to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destination_wedding" title="Destination wedding">destination weddings</a> and pre-wedding parties usually being the biggest expense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinberg_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steinberg-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the term "<a href="/wiki/Maid_of_honour" title="Maid of honour">maid of honour</a>" originally referred to the female attendant of a <a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">queen</a>. The term <i>bridesmaid</i> is normally used for all bridal attendants in the UK. However, when the attendant is married, or is a mature woman, the term <i>matron of honour</i> is often used. The influence of American English has led to the chief bridesmaid sometimes being called the maid of honour.</p>
<p>In North America, a wedding party might include several bridesmaids, but the <i>maid of honor</i> is the title and position held by the bride's chief attendant, typically her closest friend or sister. In modern-day weddings some brides opt to choose a long-time male friend or brother as their head attendant, using the title <i>best man</i> or <i>man of honor</i>.</p>
<p>The activities of the principal bridesmaid may be as many or as varied as she allows the bride to impose upon her. Her only required duty is to participate in the wedding ceremony. Typically, however, she is asked for help with the logistics of the wedding as an event, such as addressing <a href="/wiki/Wedding_invitation" title="Wedding invitation">invitations</a>, and for her help as a friend, such as attending the bride as she shops for her wedding dress.</p>
<p>On the day of the wedding, her principal duty is to provide practical and emotional support. She might assist the bride with dressing and, if needed, help the bride manage her veil, a bouquet, a prayer book, or the train of her wedding dress during the day. In a double-ring wedding, the chief bridesmaid is often entrusted with the groom's <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">wedding ring</a> until it is needed during the ceremony. Many brides ask bridesmaids, if they are <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">adults</a>, to be legal witnesses who sign the <a href="/wiki/Marriage_license" title="Marriage license">marriage license</a> after the ceremony. If there is a reception after the wedding, the maid of honor may be asked to offer a toast to the newlywed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_history">Origin and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridesmaid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of the Western bridesmaid tradition likely arose from a combination of many factors. The most-likely factors include, but aren't limited to, socioeconomic class and status, family size, socialization standards of the day, and religion.</p>
<p>Those that religion often cite the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Biblical</a> story of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>, and his two wives <a href="/wiki/Leah" title="Leah">Leah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a>, who both literally came with their own <a href="/wiki/Maid" title="Maid">maids</a> as detailed in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> (29:24, 46:18) as the origin of bridesmaids. These women were <a href="/wiki/Handmaiden" title="Handmaiden">handmaidens</a> (servants or slaves) instead of social peers.</p>
<p>In China during the feudal era, the female womb was seen as a precious resource for the production of an heir. Brides were susceptible to kidnapping at weddings by rival clans and hooligans. Shouldering the responsibility to protect the bride, bridesmaids were dressed up like the bride to lower the risk that she might be identified and robbed. As legal protections for marriage were established, this was no longer necessary, and the role of bridesmaid took a more symbolic turn. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, white was the official color for both male groomsmen and female bridesmaids. This trend originated with <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> when she married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Albert" title="Prince Albert">Prince Albert</a> wearing a long white wedding dress, and her bridesmaids matched the color of her gown.</p>
