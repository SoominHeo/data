<p>The <b>New World Translation of the Holy Scriptures</b> (<b>NWT</b>) is a translation of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> published by the <a href="/wiki/Watch_Tower_Bible_and_Tract_Society_of_Pennsylvania" title="Watch Tower Bible and Tract Society of Pennsylvania">Watch Tower Bible and Tract Society</a>. The New Testament portion was released in 1950, as <i>The New World Translation of the Christian Greek Scriptures</i>, with the complete Bible released in 1961; it is used and distributed by <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Though it is not the first Bible to be published by the group, it is their first original translation of ancient <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Hebrew" title="Biblical Hebrew">Classical Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language#Old_Aramaic" title="Aramaic language">Old Aramaic</a> biblical texts. As of January 2017, the Watch Tower Society has published 217 million copies of the <i>New World Translation</i> in whole or in part in more than 140 languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publishing_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-publishing-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the release of the NWT, Jehovah's Witnesses in English-speaking countries primarily used the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authorized_King_James_Version" title="Authorized King James Version">King James Version</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> According to the publishers, one of the main reasons for producing a new translation was that most Bible versions in common use, including the <i>Authorized Version</i> (King James), employed archaic language. The stated intention was to produce a fresh translation, free of archaisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Additionally, over the centuries since the <i>King James Version</i> was produced, more copies of earlier manuscripts of the original texts in the Hebrew and Greek languages have become available. According to the publishers, better manuscript evidence had made it possible to determine with greater accuracy what the original writers intended, particularly in more obscure passages. They said linguists better understood certain aspects of the original Hebrew and Greek languages than previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 1946, the president of the Watch Tower Society, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nathan_H._Knorr" title="Nathan H. Knorr">Nathan H. Knorr</a>, proposed a fresh translation of the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, which Jehovah's Witnesses usually refer to as the <i>Christian Greek Scriptures</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Work began on December 2, 1947 when the "New World Bible Translation Committee" was formed, composed of Jehovah's Witnesses who professed to be anointed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Watch Tower Society is said to have "become aware" of the committee's existence a year later. The committee agreed to turn over its translation to the Society for publication<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWTrelease_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWTrelease-18">[18]</a></sup> and on September 3, 1949, Knorr convened a joint meeting of the board of directors of both the Watch Tower Society's New York and Pennsylvania corporations where he again announced to the directors the existence of the committee<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and that it was now able to print its new modern English translation of the Christian Greek Scriptures. Several chapters of the translation were read to the directors, who then voted to accept it as a gift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWTrelease_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWTrelease-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>New World Translation of the Christian Greek Scriptures</i> was released at a convention of Jehovah's Witnesses at <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium_(1923)" title="Yankee Stadium (1923)">Yankee Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, on August 2, 1950. The translation of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>, which Jehovah's Witnesses refer to as the <i>Hebrew Scriptures</i>, was released in five volumes in 1953, 1955, 1957, 1958, and 1960. The complete <i>New World Translation of the Holy Scriptures</i> was released as a single volume in 1961, and has since undergone minor revisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cross-reference" title="Cross-reference">Cross references</a> which had appeared in the six separate volumes were updated and included in the complete volume in the 1984 revision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961 the Watch Tower Society began to translate the <i>New World Translation</i> into Dutch, French, German, Italian, Portuguese, and Spanish; the New Testament in these languages was released simultaneously on July 1963 in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. By 1989 the <i>New World Translation</i> was translated into eleven languages, with more than 56,000,000 copies printed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translators">Translators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Translators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>New World Translation</i> was produced by the New World Bible Translation Committee, formed in 1947. This committee is said to have comprised unnamed members of multinational background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The committee requested that the Watch Tower Society not publish the names of its members,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> stating that they did not want to "advertise themselves but let all the glory go to the Author of the Scriptures, God,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> adding that the translation, "should direct the reader... to... Jehovah God".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wt741215p454_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-wt741215p454-27">[27]</a></sup> The publishers believe that "the particulars of [the New World Bible Translation Committee's members] university or other educational training are not the important thing" and that "the translation testifies to their qualification".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wt741215p454_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-wt741215p454-27">[27]</a></sup> Former high ranking Watch Tower staff have claimed knowledge of the translators' identities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evangelical minister <a href="/wiki/Walter_Ralston_Martin" title="Walter Ralston Martin">Walter Ralston Martin</a> identified Nathan H. Knorr, Fredrick W. Franz, Albert D. Schroeder, George Gangas, and Milton Henschel as members of the translation team, writing of them, "The New World Bible translation committee had no known translators with recognized degrees in Greek or Hebrew exegesis or translation... None of these men had any university education except Franz, who left school after two years, never completing even an undergraduate degree." Franz had stated that he was familiar with not only Hebrew, but with Greek, Latin, Spanish, Portuguese, German, and French for the purpose of biblical translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In his critique of the NWT, K. J. Baumgarten wrote, the "NWT must be evaluated on its own merits, the qualifications of the committee members are not as relevant as the quality of their work product. In the end, what matters is whether the NWT is consistent with (a) its stated philosophy of translation, and (b) sound exegesis of the Greek text."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Baumgarten concluded that, "(1) the theological biases of the translators have caused them to violate their own stated philosophy of translation and the rules of Koine Greek grammar and exegesis, (2) resulting in a treatment of the original text that can be objectively determined to be erroneous, and (3) the NWT must therefore be deemed untrustworthy as either an academic or devotional resource."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_Services_Department">Translation Services Department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Translation Services Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989 a Translation Services Department was established at the world headquarters of Jehovah's Witnesses, overseen by the Writing Committee of the Governing Body. The goal of the Translation Services Department was to accelerate Bible translation with the aid of computer technology. Previously, some Bible translation projects lasted twenty years or more. Under the direction of the Translation Services Department, translation of the Old Testament in a particular language may be completed in as little as two years. During the period from 1963 to 1989, the <i>New World Translation</i> became available in ten additional languages. Since the formation of the Translation Services Department in 1989, there has been a significant increase in the number of languages in which the <i>New World Translation</i> has been made available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-translation_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-translation-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YB_12.2C_pg_26_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-YB_12.2C_pg_26-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_revision">2013 revision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2013 revision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the Watch Tower Society's annual meeting on October 5, 2013, a significantly revised translation was released. Many outdated terms were replaced with modern English. The <a href="/wiki/Jesus_and_the_woman_taken_in_adultery" title="Jesus and the woman taken in adultery">Pericope Adulterae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">John</a> 7:53 – 8:11) and the <a href="/wiki/Mark_16#Shorter_ending_of_Mark" title="Mark 16">Short and Long Conclusions of Mark 16</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Mark</a> 16:8–20)—offset from the main text in earlier editions—were removed. The new revision was also released as part of an <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">app</a> called <i>JW Library</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to the Watch Tower Society, the <i>New World Translation</i> attempts to convey the intended sense of original-language words according to the context. The <i>New World Translation</i> employs nearly 16,000 English expressions to translate about 5,500 biblical Greek terms, and over 27,000 English expressions to translate about 8,500 Hebrew terms. The translators state that, where possible in the target language, the <i>New World Translation</i> prefers literal renderings and does not paraphrase the original text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textual_basis">Textual basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Textual basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The master text used for translating the Old Testament into English was <a href="/wiki/Biblia_Hebraica_(Kittel)" title="Biblia Hebraica (Kittel)">Kittel's Biblia Hebraica</a>. The Hebrew texts, <a href="/wiki/Biblia_Hebraica_Stuttgartensia" title="Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia">Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biblia_Hebraica_Quinta" title="Biblia Hebraica Quinta">Biblia Hebraica Quinta</a>, were used for preparing the latest version of this translation. Other works consulted in preparing the translation include Aramaic <a href="/wiki/Targum" title="Targum">Targums</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls" title="Dead Sea Scrolls">Dead Sea Scrolls</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samaritan_Torah" title="Samaritan Torah">Samaritan Torah</a>, the Greek <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>, the Latin <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Masoretic_Text" title="Masoretic Text">Masoretic Text</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Codex_Cairensis" title="Codex Cairensis">Cairo Codex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aleppo_Codex" title="Aleppo Codex">Aleppo Codex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_David_Ginsburg" title="Christian David Ginsburg">Christian David Ginsburg</a>'s Hebrew Text, and the <a href="/wiki/Leningrad_Codex" title="Leningrad Codex">Leningrad Codex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated305_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated305-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Greek master text by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge University</a> scholars <a href="/wiki/Brooke_Westcott" title="Brooke Westcott">B. F. Westcott</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fenton_John_Anthony_Hort" title="Fenton John Anthony Hort">F. J. A. Hort</a> (1881) was used as the basis for translating the New Testament into English. The committee also referred to the <a href="/wiki/Novum_Testamentum_Graece" title="Novum Testamentum Graece">Novum Testamentum Graece</a> (18th edition, 1948) and to works by Catholic Jesuit scholars José M. Bover (1943) and Augustinus Merk (1948). The <a href="/wiki/United_Bible_Societies" title="United Bible Societies">United Bible Societies</a>' text (1975) and the <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Nestle" title="Erwin Nestle">Nestle</a>-<a href="/wiki/Kurt_Aland" title="Kurt Aland">Aland</a> text (1979) were used to update the footnotes in the 1984 version. Additional works consulted in preparing the <i>New World Translation</i> include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenian_Bible_translation" title="Armenian Bible translation">Armenian Version</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coptic_versions_of_the_Bible" title="Coptic versions of the Bible">Coptic Versions</a>, the Latin Vulgate, <a href="/wiki/Sixto-Clementine_Vulgate" title="Sixto-Clementine Vulgate">Sixtine and Clementine Revised Latin Texts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textus_Receptus" title="Textus Receptus">Textus Receptus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jakob_Griesbach" title="Johann Jakob Griesbach">Johann Jakob Griesbach</a>'s Greek text, the <a href="/wiki/Emphatic_Diaglott" title="Emphatic Diaglott">Emphatic Diaglott</a>, and various <a href="/wiki/Papyrus#Collections_of_papyri" title="Papyrus">papyri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated305_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated305-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Translation into other languages is based on the English text, supplemented by comparison with the Hebrew and Greek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The complete <i>New World Translation</i> has been published in more than eighty languages or <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">scripts</a>, with the New Testament available in more than fifty additional languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publishing_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-publishing-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the Writing Committee approves the translation of the Bible into a new language, it appoints a group of baptized Jehovah's Witnesses to serve as a translation team. Translators are given a list of words and expressions commonly used in the English <i>New World Translation</i> with related English words grouped together (e.g. <i>atone</i>, <i>atonement</i> or <i>propitiation</i>). A list of vernacular equivalents is then composed. A database of Greek and Hebrew terms is available where a translator has difficulty rendering a verse. The vernacular terms are then applied to the text in the target language. Further editing and translation is then performed to produce a final version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-translation_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-translation-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The layout resembles the 1901 edition of the <i>American Standard Version</i>. The translators use the terms "Hebrew-Aramaic Scriptures" and "Christian Greek Scriptures" rather than "Old Testament" and "New Testament", stating that the use of "testament" was based on a misunderstanding of 2 Corinthians 3:14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Headings were included at the top of each page to assist in locating texts; these have been replaced in the 2013 revision by an "Outline of Contents" introducing each Bible book. There is also an index listing scriptures by subject.</p>
<p>Square brackets [ ] were added around words that were inserted editorially, but were removed as of the 2006 printing. Double brackets were used to indicate text considered doubtful. The pronoun "you" was printed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_capitals" title="Small capitals">small capitals</a> (i.e., <small>YOU</small>) to indicate plurality, as were some verbs when plurality may be unclear. These features were discontinued in the 2013 release. The <i>New World Translation</i> attempts to indicate progressive rather than completed actions, such as "proceeded to rest" at Genesis 2:2 instead of "rested". The 2013 release indicates progressive verbs only where considered contextually important.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_Jehovah">Use of <i>Jehovah</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use of Jehovah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jehovah" title="Jehovah">Jehovah</a></div>
<p>The name <i><a href="/wiki/Jehovah" title="Jehovah">Jehovah</a></i> is a translation of the <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">Tetragrammaton</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">יהוה</span>‎‎, transliterated as <i>YHWH</i>), although the original pronunciation is unknown. The <i>New World Translation</i> uses the name <i>Jehovah</i> 6,979 times in the Old Testament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Watch Tower Society notes that the Tetragrammaton appears in "the oldest fragments of the Greek Septuagint".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insight_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-insight-41">[41]</a></sup> In reference to the <i><a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a></i>, biblical scholar <a href="/wiki/Paul_E._Kahle" title="Paul E. Kahle">Paul E. Kahle</a> stated, "We now know that the Greek Bible text as far as it was written by Jews for Jews did not translate the Divine name by <a href="/wiki/Kyrios" title="Kyrios">Kyrios</a>, but the Tetragrammaton written with Hebrew or Greek letters was retained in such MSS (manuscripts). It was the Christians who replaced the Tetragrammaton by Kyrios, when the divine name written in Hebrew letters was not understood any more."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>New World Translation</i> also uses the name <i>Jehovah</i> 237 times in the New Testament where the extant texts use only the Greek words <i>kurios</i> (<i>Lord</i>) and <i>theos</i> (<i>God</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Walter_Ralston_Martin" title="Walter Ralston Martin">Walter Martin</a>, an evangelical minister, wrote, "It can be shown from literally thousands of copies of the Greek New Testament that not once does the tetragrammaton appear."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> However, the translators of the <i>New World Translation</i> believed that the name <i>Jehovah</i> was present in the original manuscripts of the New Testament when quoting from the Old Testament, but replaced with the other terms by later copyists. Based on this reasoning, the translators "restored the divine name", though it is not present in any extant manuscripts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of <i>Jehovah</i> in the New Testament is not unique to the NWT; translations in English with similar renderings include <i>A Literal Translation of the New Testament ... From the Text of the Vatican Manuscript</i> (Heinfetter, 1863); <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Emphatic_Diaglott" title="The Emphatic Diaglott">The Emphatic Diaglott</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Wilson_(biblical_scholar)" title="Benjamin Wilson (biblical scholar)">Wilson</a>, 1864); <i>The Epistles of Paul in Modern English</i> (<a href="/wiki/George_Barker_Stevens" title="George Barker Stevens">Stevens</a>, 1898); <i>St. Paul’s Epistle to the Romans</i> (Rutherford, 1900); <i>The Christian’s Bible — New Testament</i> (LeFevre, 1928) and <i>The New Testament Letters</i> (<a href="/wiki/William_Wand" title="William Wand">Wand, Bishop of London</a>, 1946).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Editions">Editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>New World Translation</i> is available as a standard edition, and a <i>Reference</i> edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The regular edition includes several appendices containing arguments for various translation decisions, maps, diagrams and other information; and over 125,000 cross references. The reference edition contains the cross references and adds footnotes about translation decisions and additional appendices that provide further detail relating to certain translation decisions and doctrinal views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingdom_Interlinear"><i>Kingdom Interlinear</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Kingdom Interlinear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The New World Bible Translation Committee included the English text from the NWT in its 1969 and 1985 editions of the <i>Kingdom Interlinear Translation of the Greek Scriptures</i>. It also incorporates the Greek text published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooke_Foss_Westcott" title="Brooke Foss Westcott">Westcott</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fenton_John_Anthony_Hort" title="Fenton John Anthony Hort">Hort</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_Testament_in_the_Original_Greek" title="The New Testament in the Original Greek">The New Testament in the Original Greek</a></i> and a literal word-for-word translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-print_editions">Non-print editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Non-print editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_review">Overall review</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Overall review">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its review of Bible translations released from 1955 to 1985, <i>The HarperCollins Bible Dictionary</i> listed the <i>New World Translation</i> as one of the major modern translations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>New Catholic Encyclopedia</i> says of the NWT reference edition: "[Jehovah's Witnesses]' translation of the Bible [has] an impressive critical apparatus. The work is excellent except when scientific knowledge comes into conflict with the accepted doctrines of the movement." It criticizes the NWT's rendering of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyrios_(Biblical_term)" title="Kyrios (Biblical term)">Kyrios</a></i> as "Jehovah" in 237 instances in the New Testament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Testament">Old Testament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Old Testament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Samuel Haas, in his 1955 review of the 1953 first volume of the <i>New World Translation of the Hebrew Scriptures</i>, in the <i>Journal of Biblical Literature</i>, stated that although "this work indicates a great deal of effort and thought as well as considerable scholarship, it is to be regretted that religious bias was allowed to colour many passages."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Professor Benjamin Kedar, a Professor of History and Director of the Institute for Advanced Studies at the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_University_of_Jerusalem" title="Hebrew University of Jerusalem">Hebrew University of Jerusalem</a>, said in 1989: "In my linguistic research in connection with the Hebrew Bible and translations, I often refer to the English edition of what is known as the <i>New World Translation</i>. In so doing, I find my feeling repeatedly confirmed that this work [the NWT Old Testament] reflects an honest endeavor to achieve an understanding of the text that is as accurate as possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding the NWT's use of English in the 1953 first volume of the NWT (<i>Genesis to Ruth</i>), Baptist biblical scholar <a href="/wiki/H._H._Rowley" title="H. H. Rowley">Harold Henry Rowley</a> was critical of what he called "wooden literalism" and "harsh construction." He characterized these as "an insult to the Word of God", citing various verses of Genesis as examples. Rowley concluded, "From beginning to end this [first] volume is a shining example of how the Bible should not be translated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Testament">New Testament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_Translation_of_the_Holy_Scriptures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: New Testament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2003 study by <a href="/wiki/Jason_BeDuhn" title="Jason BeDuhn">Jason BeDuhn</a>, associate professor of religious studies at <a href="/wiki/Northern_Arizona_University" title="Northern Arizona University">Northern Arizona University</a> in the United States, of nine of "the Bibles most widely in use in the English-speaking world," including the <i><a href="/wiki/New_American_Bible" title="New American Bible">New American Bible</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_King_James_Bible" title="The King James Bible">The King James Bible</a></i> and <i>The <a href="/wiki/New_International_Version" title="New International Version">New International Version</a></i>, examined several New Testament passages in which "bias is most likely to interfere with translation." For each passage, he compared the Greek text with the renderings of each English translation, and looked for biased attempts to change the meaning. BeDuhn reported that the <i>New World Translation</i> was "not bias free", but emerged "as the most accurate of the translations compared", and thus a "remarkably good translation", adding that "most of the differences are due to the greater accuracy of the NW as a literal, conservative translation". BeDuhn said the introduction of the name "Jehovah" into the New Testament 237 times was "not accurate translation by the most basic principle of accuracy", and that it "violate[s] accuracy in favor of denominationally preferred expressions for God", adding that for the NWT to gain wider acceptance and prove its worth its translators might have to abandon the use of "Jehovah" in the New Testament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Theologian and televangelist <a href="/wiki/John_Ankerberg" title="John Ankerberg">John Ankerberg</a> accused the NWT's translators of renderings that conform "to their own preconceived and unbiblical theology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankerberg_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ankerberg-72">[72]</a></sup> John Weldon and Ankerberg cite several examples wherein they consider the NWT to support theological views overriding appropriate translation. Ankerberg and Weldon cite Julius R. Mantey, co-author of <i>A Manual Grammar of the Greek New Testament</i> and <i>A Hellenistic Greek Reader</i>, who also criticized the NWT, calling it "a shocking mistranslation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankerberg_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ankerberg-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Barclay_(theologian)" title="William Barclay (theologian)">William Barclay</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_of_Divinity_and_Biblical_Criticism,_Glasgow" title="Professor of Divinity and Biblical Criticism, Glasgow">Professor of Divinity and Biblical Criticism</a>, concluded that "the deliberate distortion of truth by this sect is seen in the New Testament translation. ... It is abundantly clear that a sect which can translate the New Testament like that is intellectually dishonest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edgar_J._Goodspeed" title="Edgar J. Goodspeed">Edgar J. Goodspeed</a>, translator of the New Testament in <i>An American Translation</i>, wrote in a letter to the Watch Tower Society: "I am interested in the mission work of your people, and its world wide scope, and much pleased with the free, frank and vigorous translation. It exhibits a vast array of sound serious learning, as I can testify."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Former <a href="/wiki/American_Bible_Society" title="American Bible Society">American Bible Society</a> board member <a href="/wiki/Bruce_M._Metzger" title="Bruce M. Metzger">Bruce M. Metzger</a> concluded that "on the whole, one gains a tolerably good impression of the scholarly equipment of the translators,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> but identified instances where the translation has been written to support doctrine, with "several quite erroneous renderings of the Greek."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Metzger noted a number of "indefensible" characteristics of the translation, including its use of <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton_in_the_New_Testament#English_versions_of_the_New_Testament" title="Tetragrammaton in the New Testament">"Jehovah" in the New Testament</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarian</a> theologian <a href="/wiki/Charles_Francis_Potter" title="Charles Francis Potter">Charles Francis Potter</a> stated about the NWT: "Apart from a few semantic peculiarities like translating the Greek word <i><a href="/wiki/Stauros" title="Stauros">stauros</a></i>, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dispute_about_Jesus%27_execution_method#Writers_suggesting_a_simple_stake" title="Dispute about Jesus' execution method">"stake" instead of "cross"</a>, and the often startling use of the colloquial and the vernacular, the anonymous translators have certainly rendered the best manuscript texts, both Greek and Hebrew, with scholarly ability and acumen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Religion writer and editor <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Thomson_(writer)" title="Alexander Thomson (writer)">Alexander Thomson</a> said of the NWT: "The translation is evidently the work of skilled and clever scholars, who have sought to bring out as much of the true sense of the Greek text as the English language is capable of expressing. ... We heartily recommend the <i>New World Translation of the Christian Greek Scriptures</i>, published in 1950 by the Watch Tower Bible and Tract Society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thomas Winter, an instructor of Greek at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska" title="University of Nebraska">University of Nebraska</a> and former president of the Unitarian Church of Lincoln, considered the <i><a href="#Kingdom_Interlinear">Kingdom Interlinear Translation of the Greek Scriptures</a></i> to be a "highly useful aid toward the mastery of <i>koine</i> (and classical) Greek," adding that the translation "is thoroughly up-to-date and consistently accurate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Andover_Newton_Theological_School" title="Andover Newton Theological School">Andover Newton</a> Quarterly</i> reported, "The translation of the New Testament is evidence of the presence in the movement of scholars qualified to deal intelligently with the many problems of Biblical translation. One could question why the translators have not stayed closer to the original meaning, as do most translators ... In not a few instances the <i>New World Translation</i> contains passages which must be considered as 'theological translations.' This fact is particularly evident in those passages which express or imply the deity of Jesus Christ."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
