<p>The <b>Cincinnati Cyclones</b> are a professional hockey team based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>. The team is a member of the <a href="/wiki/ECHL" title="ECHL">ECHL</a>. Originally established in 1990, the team first played their games in the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Gardens" title="Cincinnati Gardens">Cincinnati Gardens</a> and now play at <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bank_Arena" title="U.S. Bank Arena">U.S. Bank Arena</a>.</p>
<p>Cincinnati has fielded Cyclones teams in two different leagues: the <a href="/wiki/International_Hockey_League_(1945%E2%80%932001)" title="International Hockey League (1945–2001)">International Hockey League</a> (1992–2001) and the ECHL (1990–1992, 2001–2004, 2006–present). Together, the franchises have combined to win two Kelly Cups (2008 and 2010), three conference championships (2008, 2010 and 2014), one overall points championship (2008), and four division championships (1996, 2008, 2009 and 2013).</p>
<p>In 2007–08, the team had the most successful season in ECHL history. 55 wins and 115 points earned them the North Division Championship and Brabham Cup trophy, and the team won its first ever conference and league championships, winning the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Cup" title="Kelly Cup">Kelly Cup</a> in a six-game final over the <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Wranglers" title="Las Vegas Wranglers">Las Vegas Wranglers</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009–10, the team, even with the American Conference's #5 seed to begin the playoffs, made history by becoming the first team in ECHL history, and the 6th of seven professional hockey teams overall, to overcome a 3-0 deficit to win a best of seven playoff series, winning the American Conference Championship in the process. Nine days later, Cincinnati finished their improbable run with the 2nd Kelly Cup in team history, both in three years, completing a five-game Kelly Cup Final win over the Idaho Steelheads.</p>
<p>Four years later, Cincinnati won the Eastern Conference, again with the #5 seed, but lost in the 2014 Kelly Cup Final to the Alaska Aces.</p>
<p>Cincinnati moved to the Western Conference for <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_ECHL_season" title="2015–16 ECHL season">2015–16 ECHL season</a> before returning to the Eastern Conference the following season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been three separate <a href="/wiki/Professional_sports_league_organization" title="Professional sports league organization">franchises</a> known as the Cincinnati Cyclones, two in the <a href="/wiki/ECHL" title="ECHL">ECHL</a> and one in the IHL.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_early_ECHL_years:_1990.E2.80.931992">The early ECHL years: 1990–1992</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The early ECHL years: 1990–1992">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first franchise to use the name Cincinnati Cyclones was founded in <a href="/wiki/1990%E2%80%9391_ECHL_season" title="1990–91 ECHL season">1990</a>. The team played their games during this time period at the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Gardens" title="Cincinnati Gardens">Cincinnati Gardens</a>. They lost in the first round of the playoffs in the 1990–91 season. In the <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_ECHL_season" title="1991–92 ECHL season">1991–92 season</a>, the Cyclones lost in the Riley Cup semi-finals. The team's owner at the time, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doug_Kirchhofer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doug Kirchhofer (page does not exist)">Doug Kirchhofer</a>, was granted an <a href="/wiki/International_Hockey_League_(1945%E2%80%932001)" title="International Hockey League (1945–2001)">International Hockey League</a> franchise and chose to move the Cyclones name to that franchise and relocate the ECHL franchise to <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> to form the new <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Bulls_(ECHL)" title="Birmingham Bulls (ECHL)">Birmingham Bulls</a>.</p>
<p>Since moving to Birmingham, this franchise has seen stints in four cities across the United States. The franchise played in Birmingham from the <a href="/wiki/1992%E2%80%9393_ECHL_season" title="1992–93 ECHL season">1992–93 season</a> until the <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_ECHL_season" title="2000–01 ECHL season">2000–01 season</a>. Following the completion of the 2000–01 season, the franchise moved to <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">Atlantic City, New Jersey</a> to become the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City_Boardwalk_Bullies" title="Atlantic City Boardwalk Bullies">Atlantic City Boardwalk Bullies</a>. The Boardwalk Bullies played for four seasons, the 2001–02 season through the 2004–05 season, before being moved to another city. While in New Jersey, the franchise won a <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Cup" title="Kelly Cup">Kelly Cup</a> Championship in the <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_ECHL_season" title="2002–03 ECHL season">2002–03 season</a>, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Inferno" title="Columbia Inferno">Columbia Inferno</a> in the finals. Despite the success on ice, the Boardwalk Bullies were sold to the current ownership group after low attendance in Atlantic City. The franchise was moved to <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">Stockton, California</a> and changed the name to the <a href="/wiki/Stockton_Thunder" title="Stockton Thunder">Stockton Thunder</a>. In <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_ECHL_season" title="2015–16 ECHL season">2015</a>, the franchise moved once again to <a href="/wiki/Glens_Falls,_New_York" title="Glens Falls, New York">Glens Falls, New York</a> to play as the <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_Thunder" title="Adirondack Thunder">Adirondack Thunder</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_IHL_years:_1992.E2.80.932001">The IHL years: 1992–2001</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The IHL years: 1992–2001">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first season in the IHL, Cincinnati failed to qualify for the post-season, which would be the first of only two seasons in which they did not qualify. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lawless" title="Paul Lawless">Paul Lawless</a>, who would eventually be head coach and vice president of player development and have his number 13 retired, began his first of several stints with the team in the <a href="/wiki/1992%E2%80%9393_IHL_season" title="1992–93 IHL season">1992–93 season</a>. On Friday, April 2, 2004, during his number retirement ceremony, he was singled out as a reason for the team's success, both on the ice and in attendance. During the IHL years, the Cyclones set a team record of eight consecutive 90+ point seasons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> ending on June 4, 2001 when the International Hockey League ceased operations. During the Cyclones nine years in the IHL, they won their only Division Championship and Conference Championship, both in the <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_IHL_season" title="1995–96 IHL season">1995–96 season</a>, but lost in a seven-game semi-final series to the <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Solar_Bears_(IHL)" title="Orlando Solar Bears (IHL)">Orlando Solar Bears</a>.</p>
<p>In 1997, the Cyclones' lease agreement with the Cincinnati Gardens came to an end and the two sides were unable to reach an agreement for a new contract. It was at this time that team owner, Doug Kirchhofer purchased what then was known as Riverfront Coliseum in downtown Cincinnati, renamed it The Crown, renovated the arena, and moved the team there. During the seven seasons the Cyclones were in the arena, the venue was renamed the Firstar Center and now is known as <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bank_Arena" title="U.S. Bank Arena">U.S. Bank Arena</a>. The team has since been unsuccessful in reclaiming the higher attendance numbers from the Cincinnati Gardens, until the most recent run at winning the Kelly Cup, where they drew 12,722 fans to the last game of the postseason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Back_in_the_ECHL:_2001.E2.80.932004">Back in the ECHL: 2001–2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Back in the ECHL: 2001–2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The third Cyclones franchise started in the <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_ECHL_season" title="1995–96 ECHL season">1995–96 ECHL season</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_RiverFrogs" title="Louisville RiverFrogs">Louisville RiverFrogs</a>, playing in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>. After three years in Louisville, the team moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a>. After the lone <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_ECHL_season" title="1998–99 ECHL season">1998–99 season</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Matadors" title="Miami Matadors">Miami Matadors</a>, the franchise eventually came into the ownership of a group in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and the franchise went into inactive status while ownership looked into locations for their team. Upon the folding of the IHL, the Cyclones name was sold to this ownership group who moved the franchise to Cincinnati to become the new Cincinnati Cyclones.</p>
<p>The first year back in the ECHL ended the team's streak of 90+ point seasons with the Cyclones finishing just 12 points shy despite a 10-game fewer season length. Former Cyclones player <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lawless" title="Paul Lawless">Paul Lawless</a> became head coach, as a mid-season replacement for Ray Edwards.</p>
<p>Before the start of the <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_ECHL_season" title="2003–04 ECHL season">2003–04 season</a> former Cyclones player and assistant coach <a href="/wiki/Chris_Cichocki" title="Chris Cichocki">Chris Cichocki</a> left the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_RiverBlades" title="Arkansas RiverBlades">Arkansas RiverBlades</a> in order to return as the Cyclones head coach. Despite his success with Arkansas, though, Cichocki led the team to their worst season in points at 54 (previous low being the 61 points in their first IHL season) and failed to make the playoffs for the second time in team history.</p>
<p>Shortly after ending the season without a playoff berth, in April 2004, the Cyclones suspended operations. This suspension in operations was mainly due to lack of revenue brought on by a lack of safe access to the arena as it was limited by construction in Downtown Cincinnati. This dormancy left Cincinnati hockey to the cross-town, Cincinnati Gardens-based <a href="/wiki/American_Hockey_League" title="American Hockey League">AHL</a> rival, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Mighty_Ducks" title="Cincinnati Mighty Ducks">Cincinnati Mighty Ducks</a> and stopped the city's record of two minor league hockey teams in the same city for consecutive years. The Cincinnati Mighty Ducks suspended operations in 2005 after their NHL affiliate, the <a href="/wiki/Anaheim_Ducks" title="Anaheim Ducks">Mighty Ducks of Anaheim</a>, switched their affiliation to the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Pirates" title="Portland Pirates">Portland Pirates</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resumed_play:_2006.E2.80.93present">Resumed play: 2006–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Resumed play: 2006–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With minor league hockey dormant in Cincinnati for a year, and plans for the proposed AHL Cincinnati RailRaiders franchise scrapped for <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_AHL_season" title="2006–07 AHL season">2006–07</a>, on April 21, 2006 the Cyclones announced that they would participate in the upcoming <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_ECHL_season" title="2006–07 ECHL season">2006–07 ECHL season</a>. They returned in the American Conference, North Division of the ECHL and continue to play their home games at U.S. Bank Arena, joining their two ECHL, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> rivals, <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Bombers" title="Dayton Bombers">Dayton Bombers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Storm" title="Toledo Storm">Toledo Storm</a>.</p>
<p>On July 18, 2006, the Cyclones announced their affiliation with the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">NHL</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Bulldogs_(AHL)" title="Hamilton Bulldogs (AHL)">Hamilton Bulldogs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Hockey_League" title="American Hockey League">AHL</a>. The Cyclones won their first game back in the ECHL on October 20 against the <a href="/wiki/Pensacola_Ice_Pilots" title="Pensacola Ice Pilots">Pensacola Ice Pilots</a> at U.S. Bank Arena with a score of 3 to 1.</p>
<p>On May 23, 2007, the Cyclones announced they had re-signed head coach <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Weber_(ice_hockey)" title="Chuck Weber (ice hockey)">Chuck Weber</a> to coach the team for the <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_ECHL_season" title="2007–08 ECHL season">2007–08 season</a>. Weber was also the runner-up for the ECHL's Coach of the Year award for 2006–07.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 2, 2007, In addition to the Montreal/Hamilton affiliation, the Cyclones announced an affiliation with the NHL's <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Predators" title="Nashville Predators">Nashville Predators</a> and the AHL's <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Admirals" title="Milwaukee Admirals">Milwaukee Admirals</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2007.E2.80.9308_season:_The_Kelly_Cup_Championship_season">2007–08 season: The Kelly Cup Championship season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2007–08 season: The Kelly Cup Championship season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Cyclones' 2007–08 season saw the club break numerous franchise and league records. Through 50 games, the Cyclones were 31 games over .500. Attendance at games had increased nearly 40% over 2006–07 and the club had already surpassed their win total from 2006–07 (37). They achieved this mark in 24 fewer games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 22, 2008 the Cyclones tied the ECHL record for a single-season winning streak of 14th consecutive games, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Chiefs" title="Johnstown Chiefs">Johnstown Chiefs</a> 5–2 in Cincinnati.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 23, 2008 <a href="/wiki/David_Desharnais" title="David Desharnais">David Desharnais</a> recorded two assists, extending his streak of games with at least one assist to 18, this broke the existing ECHL record of 17. In the same game, the Cyclones set a new ECHL record for a single-season winning streak of 15 consecutive wins when the Cyclones defeated the <a href="/wiki/Elmira_Jackals" title="Elmira Jackals">Elmira Jackals</a> in a 5–4 shootout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The team finished the regular season with 115 points, earning them the Brabham Cup regular season championship. They also won the Northern Division and American Conference regular season titles. Individually, Chuck Weber was named ECHL coach of the year, earning him the John Brophy trophy. David Desharnais claimed three ECHL awards: Most Valuable Player, Rookie of the Year, and the Leading Scorer award with 29 goals and 77 assists for 106 points. In addition, Chad Starling won the award for the highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plus-minus_(ice_hockey)" title="Plus-minus (ice hockey)">plus/minus</a> rating.</p>
<p>The Cyclones entered the playoffs and defeated the <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Chiefs" title="Johnstown Chiefs">Johnstown Chiefs</a> four games to none. The Cyclones moved on to take on the <a href="/wiki/Reading_Royals" title="Reading Royals">Reading Royals</a>, Cincinnati took the series in seven games to claim their second ever North Division playoff title. Finally, on May 16, 2008, the Cyclones defeated the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Stingrays" title="South Carolina Stingrays">South Carolina Stingrays</a>, 2-1 in overtime, to claim the American Conference Championship. With their victory, Cincinnati also claimed the E.A. "Bud" Gingher Memorial Trophy.</p>
<p>Three weeks later, following a six-game victory over the National Conference Champion Las Vegas Wranglers in the Kelly Cup Finals, the Cyclones clinched the first championship in team history. After splitting the first two games in Cincinnati (Cincinnati 4-3 and Las Vegas 1-0), the series shifted to Las Vegas, where Cincinnati won games three and five to take a 3-2 series lead. In front of a record setting crowd, 12,722 fans, at US Bank Arena in Cincinnati on June 5, the Cyclones defeated Las Vegas in Game Six, 3-1, to take the championship. The clincher was the 71st win of the season. Goalie Cedrick Desjardins was named the Kelly Cup Playoff MVP.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.9309_season">2008–09 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2008–09 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the Cyclones cup win <a href="/wiki/Coach_(ice_hockey)" title="Coach (ice hockey)">coach</a> Chuck Weber and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assistant_coach" title="Assistant coach">assistant coach</a> Dean Stork received multi-year extensions. Weber was also given the title of Vice President. On November 29, Chuck Weber picked up his 100th professional head coaching victory with a 6-3 win at <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Chiefs" title="Johnstown Chiefs">Johnstown</a>.</p>
<p>Cincinnati's season was not as successful as the last one, but they won 41 games for 87 points, repeating as North Division champions. They won a seven-game series against Wheeling, including a double overtime game seven win, and swept the Elmira Jackals to win the North Division playoff title. However, the Cyclones would be swept by the South Carolina Stingrays in the American Conference Finals. These two teams had met in the conference finals the year before, with Cincinnati picking up a five-game win.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2009.E2.80.9310_season:_Champions_again">2009–10 season: Champions again</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2009–10 season: Champions again">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_ECHL_season" title="2009–10 ECHL season">2009–10 season</a> proved to be more successful than the last. Cincinnati ended up with 44 wins and 91 points, the wins total being the most in the American Conference. Despite that, the Cyclones finished 2nd in the North Division to the Kalamazoo Wings and wound up 5th in the conference. This meant a rematch with 4th seeded, and defending champion, South Carolina in the first round, a matchup in which Cincinnati defeated the Stingrays in five games, ending on April 11, when veteran captain Barrett Eghotz scored in overtime for the 3-2 win in Game Five. The game was the third straight overtime game in the series. This set up a matchup with the top seeded Charlotte Checkers, and once again for Cincinnati it went the distance. Game Seven saw Cincinnati post a 2-1 victory, and in the process end Charlotte's tenure in the ECHL, as Charlotte was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Checkers" title="Charlotte Checkers">team</a> in the AHL the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_AHL_season" title="2010–11 AHL season">next season</a>.</p>
<p>Cincinnati drew the <a href="/wiki/Reading_Royals" title="Reading Royals">Reading Royals</a> in the American Conference Final. What was in all other accounts an epic seven game series was marred by several incidents, both on and off the ice, that resulted in suspensions and fines to players and staff of both organizations. The worst of it occurred after game six, when Reading's <a href="/wiki/Scott_Fletcher_(ice_hockey)" title="Scott Fletcher (ice hockey)">Scott Fletcher</a> allegedly struck a Cincinnati fan with a stick, drawing an indefinite suspension by the league.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Nevertheless, the series made ECHL history when, after losing the first two at home, then Game Three at Reading, Cincinnati posted wins of 6-4, 5-0 and 6-3 to force the team's third winner take all match in the 2010 playoffs. The game occurred on May 12 at US Bank Arena. In front of 5,340 fans, the Cyclones posted a 1-0 win to become the first team in ECHL history, and the 6th pro team in history, to come back from a 3-0 deficit to win a best of seven series. The only goal of the contest was scored by <a href="/wiki/Barret_Ehgoetz" title="Barret Ehgoetz">Barret Ehgoetz</a> 13:48 into the game.</p>
<p>The Cincinnati Cyclones defeated the <a href="/wiki/Idaho_Steelheads" title="Idaho Steelheads">Idaho Steelheads</a> in the 2010 Kelly Cup Final, four games to one. Cincinnati used game-winning goals within the last minute in the first two games, a 3-2 win on a goal by Mark Van Guilder with 49.2 seconds remaining on May 14, and a 1-0 win the next night, when Mathieu Aubin netted the only goal in the contest, and only with 20.1 seconds remaining in regulation. The series shifted to US Bank Arena, and witnessed an Idaho victory within the first minute of the 2nd overtime of Game Three, as Evan Barlow received a pass at the bottom of the right circle and fired the puck into a largely vacated goal, as Cyclones goalie Robert Mayer had committed to the left side.</p>
<p>In front of yet another ECHL playoff record setting crowd of 13,483 at US Bank Arena in Cincinnati on May 21, the Cyclones defeated Idaho in Game Five, 2-1, to take the championship. With the victory, the Cyclones clinched their second Kelly Cup title in three years. The game was also the Cyclones 24th Kelly Cup playoff game, surpassing the club record of 22 postseason games played by the 2008 championship team and is one more than South Carolina (23) had in its title run in 2009. Rookie Cyclones goaltenders Robert Mayer and Jeremy Smith were named co-winners of the Kelly Cup playoffs MVP. Cincinnati finished with a total record of 59-32-4.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2010.E2.80.9311_season">2010–11 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010–11 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Cyclones underwent sweeping changes before the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_ECHL_season" title="2010–11 ECHL season">2010–11 season</a> when assistant coach Dean Stork became the head coach of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenville_Road_Warriors" title="Greenville Road Warriors">Greenville Road Warriors</a> in June and head coach Chuck Weber was promoted to the <a href="/wiki/American_Hockey_League" title="American Hockey League">American Hockey League</a> as head coach of the <a href="/wiki/Rochester_Americans" title="Rochester Americans">Rochester Americans</a> on July 27. On August 4, Cincinnati signed a new affiliation agreement with the NHL's <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panthers" title="Florida Panthers">Florida Panthers</a>, who used Rochester as their AHL farm team, creating a working relationship between Chuck Weber and his former Cyclones team. This relationship migrated to San Antonio, along with Weber's coaching title, when Florida moved their AHL affiliation to the <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Rampage" title="San Antonio Rampage">San Antonio Rampage</a> before the 2011–12 season. The Panthers were also the team that caused problems for the Miami Matadors, being located in the same market, causing the Matadors to suspend for two years before becoming the new Cyclones. On August 12, Cincinnati named <a href="/wiki/Jarrod_Skalde" title="Jarrod Skalde">Jarrod Skalde</a> as the new head coach.</p>
<p>After undergoing this major coaching overhaul and losing a large portion of their roster, the Cyclones went on to post their third lowest points total in franchise history. Despite this, they earned a 7th seed in the ECHL playoffs, but lost in the first round to Reading Royals, three games to one.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2011.E2.80.9312_season">2011–12 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2011–12 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_ECHL_season" title="2011–12 ECHL season">2011–12 season</a> would be, in comparison to the lengthy success of the past, disastrous. Despite heavy support by new AHL affiliate San Antonio, the Cyclones could not come out of the gates quick enough, and while they stayed competitive throughout the season, they fell victim to domination by the Atlantic and South divisions, and wound up with 35 wins and 79 points, the third lowest wins total and second lowest points total in team history. To add further infamy, only division winner Kalamazoo made the playoffs from the North division. Cincinnati would finish 10th in the conference, marking only the third time in their 20-season history that they did not make the playoffs.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2012.E2.80.9313_season">2012–13 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2012–13 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Cyclones made a complete turnaround the next season, beginning the season unbeaten in regulation for the first nine games and nearly going wire-to-wire in the season. Their 42 wins and 92 points won them the North Division championship, their third such accolade in six seasons, and placed them second in the Eastern Conference. The season earned head coach Jarrod Skalde Coach of the Year honors. Cincinnati would go on to win a pair of six-game matchups against Toledo and Gwinnett before losing a five-game conference final to the #1 seeded Reading Royals, who would go on to win the Kelly Cup.</p>
<p>On July 9, Coach Skalde accepted an assistant's position with the AHL's <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Admirals_(AHL)" title="Norfolk Admirals (AHL)">Norfolk Admirals</a>, becoming the second consecutive Cyclones coach to be promoted to a higher level. Before the 2014–15 season, Skalde was appointed as Norfolk's head coach.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2013.E2.80.9314_season:_Another_finals_run">2013–14 season: Another finals run</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2013–14 season: Another finals run">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Cincinnati came back strong with new head coach <a href="/wiki/Ben_Simon" title="Ben Simon">Ben Simon</a>. Their 41 wins and 91 points were enough for a 5th-place finish in the Eastern Conference. Cincinnati would win three six-game matchups against Orlando, Fort Wayne and Greenville to win the Eastern Conference championship and book their third trip to the Kelly Cup Finals. In the finals, they battled the Brabham Cup winning Alaska Aces, but would go down to Alaska in six games.</p>
<p>Despite the loss in the final, Cincinnati goaltender <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rob_Madore" title="Rob Madore">Rob Madore</a> was named Most Valuable Player of the 2014 Kelly Cup Playoffs, becoming the first player from the losing team to win the award in the ECHL's 26-year history, and the fourth Cincinnati goaltender to either win the trophy outright or share the trophy. Madore earned the award after leading the ECHL with all 14 of Cincinnati's playoff wins, 1,493 minutes of play, and a Cincinnati record 756 saves while playing every second of Cincinnati's 24 playoff games.</p>
<p>After the season, Coach Simon accepted a role with the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Marlies" title="Toronto Marlies">Toronto Marlies</a>, the top affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Maple_Leafs" title="Toronto Maple Leafs">Toronto Maple Leafs</a>. Simon became the third consecutive Cyclones head coach to accept a role in the AHL. Following Simon's promotion, Matt MacDonald became Cincinnati's head coach.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2014.E2.80.9315_season">2014–15 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2014–15 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After MacDonald became coach, Cincinnati finished fifth in the North Division of the Eastern Conference with a record of 31-30-2-9. The team would miss the playoffs by only three points.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2015.E2.80.9316_season">2015–16 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2015–16 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On February 27, 2016, the Cyclones played in front of their first-ever sellout at US Bank Arena with 16,529 fans were in attendance for <a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles">Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles</a> Night and one dollar pizza slices. While the game was a 3-2 shootout loss to the <a href="/wiki/Indy_Fuel" title="Indy Fuel">Indy Fuel</a>, the Cyclones set the record for the largest crowd for a professional hockey game in the 41-year history of US Bank Arena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniform">Uniform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Uniform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After introducing their current mascot, Twister, in 1995, the Cyclones had always included him in their uniform design. In fact, from 1996–2014 he was the centerpiece of the jerseys they wore. However, prior to the 2014–15 season, the organization did a complete revamp of the uniforms and their colors. Twister was removed from the uniform completely along with the color yellow. The shade of red used was also changed from a darker maroonish style red to a more bright royal red. The logo now is described as being a twister, or cyclone, in the center of a "C" standing for, Cincinnati. The jersey concepts also changed from being a classic hockey style to a more modern era look. The home, away, and alternate jerseys have red shoulders and red stripes that begin at the elbow area and wrap around to the underside of the wrists. The home jerseys are base white with red trim, the away jerseys are base red with black trim, and the alternates are base black with red trim. Other than the change in color, all three jerseys are similar.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_players">Notable players</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Notable players">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>16 - Greg Stewart Played the 06-07 season with the Cyclones and was the first player under Chuck Weber to reach the NHL with Montreal in 2008.</li>
<li>44 - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.T._Wyman" title="J.T. Wyman">J.T. Wyman</a> Played the 08-09 season with Cincinnati in 15 games JT had 8 assist while on loan from Hamilton, became the second player whom Chuck Weber coached to reach the NHL in 2009 with Montreal.</li>
<li>51 - <a href="/wiki/David_Desharnais" title="David Desharnais">David Desharnais</a> Played the 07-08 season with the Cyclones and was the ECHL Most Valuable Player, ECHL Rookie of the Year with 106 points en route to winning the Kelly Cup, became the third Chuck Weber product to make the NHL with Montreal in 2009</li>
<li>20 - <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Kerr_(ice_hockey)" title="Kevin Kerr (ice hockey)">Kevin Kerr</a>: All-time leader in scoring for non-NHL players, played three seasons and 112 games with Cincinnati, tallying 145 points in regular season play and 19 points in 13 playoff games with the Cyclones.</li>
<li>30 - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cedrick_Desjardins_(ice_hockey)" title="Cedrick Desjardins (ice hockey)">Cedrick Desjardins</a>: The 2008 Kelly Cup Playoffs Most Valuable Player... posted a 40-23-3 record in two seasons as Cyclones netminder.</li>
<li>21 - <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Dionne" title="Gilbert Dionne">Gilbert Dionne</a>: scored 134 goals in 313 games with the team, and won the 1999 IHL All Star Game MVP Award in a game played at US Bank Arena.</li>
<li>22 - <a href="/wiki/Don_Biggs" title="Don Biggs">Don Biggs</a>: The centerpiece of the team for many years, Biggs played 458 games for Cincinnati, scoring 147 goals and tallying 444 points total. Both totals are records for any player from any version of the Cyclones.</li>
<li>Coach Ron Smith - Head coach of the Cyclones from 1995-2001. Tallied a franchise record 266 wins during his six years with the team.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_ECHL_history">Additional ECHL history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Additional ECHL history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>Louisville River Frogs</b> were a professional <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> team competing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Coast_Hockey_League" title="East Coast Hockey League">East Coast Hockey League</a>. The team was based in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a> and played from 1995-1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Their home venue was <a href="/wiki/Broadbent_Arena" title="Broadbent Arena">Broadbent Arena</a> (nicknamed "The Swamp" for their duration) at the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Exposition_Center" title="Kentucky Exposition Center">Kentucky Exposition Center</a>. At the conclusion of the 1997-1998 season, the franchise was sold and moved to Florida to become the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Matadors" title="Miami Matadors">Miami Matadors</a> for year before moving to Ohio as the Cincinnati Cyclones in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The team's mascot was Rowdy River Frog. The River Frogs games were locally known for the amount of non-hockey-related entertainment at shows, including a giant frog blimp, hot tubs, and concession booths.</p>
