<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Neocolonialism</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Neocolonialism</b>, <b>neo-colonialism</b> or <b>neo-imperialism</b> is the practice of using <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_imperialism" title="Cultural imperialism">cultural imperialism</a> to influence a developing country in lieu of direct military control (<a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>) or indirect political control (<a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a>). Neo-colonialism is discussed in the works of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonialism_and_neocolonialism" title="Colonialism and neocolonialism"><i>Colonialism and Neo-colonialism</i></a>, 1964)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sartre2001_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sartre2001-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> (<i>The Washington Connection and Third World Fascism</i>, 1979).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChomskyHerman1979_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChomskyHerman1979-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Term">Term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1989_CPA_6101.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="959" data-file-width="681" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/1989_CPA_6101.jpg/185px-1989_CPA_6101.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/1989_CPA_6101.jpg/278px-1989_CPA_6101.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/1989_CPA_6101.jpg/370px-1989_CPA_6101.jpg 2x" width="185"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1989_CPA_6101.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kwame Nkrumah (pictured on a Soviet postage stamp) is a Ghanaian politician who coined the term "Neo-colonialism."</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Neocolonialism labels European countries' continued economic and cultural relationships with their former colonies, African countries that had been liberated in the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Nkrumah" title="Kwame Nkrumah">Kwame Nkrumah</a>, former president of <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> (1960‚Äì66), coined the term, which appeared in the 1963 preamble of the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity" title="Organisation of African Unity">Organization of African Unity</a> Charter, and was the title of his 1965 book <i>Neo-Colonialism, the Last Stage of Imperialism</i> (1965).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold2010-3">[3]</a></sup> Nkrumah theoretically developed and extended to the post‚ÄìWar 20th century the socio-economic and political arguments presented by <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a> in the pamphlet <i><a href="/wiki/Imperialism,_the_Highest_Stage_of_Capitalism" title="Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism">Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism</a></i> (1917). The pamphlet frames 19th-century imperialism as the logical extension of <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">geopolitical</a> power, to meet the financial investment needs of the <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In <i>Neo-Colonialism, the Last Stage of Imperialism</i>, Kwame Nkrumah wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In place of colonialism, as the main instrument of imperialism, we have today neo-colonialism . . . [which] like colonialism, is an attempt to export the social conflicts of the capitalist countries. . . .<br/></p>
<p>The result of neo-colonialism is that foreign capital is used for the <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">exploitation</a> rather than for the development of the less developed parts of the world. Investment, under neo-colonialism, increases, rather than decreases, the gap between the rich and the poor countries of the world. The struggle against neo-colonialism is not aimed at excluding the capital of the developed world from operating in less developed countries. It is aimed at preventing the financial power of the developed countries being used in such a way as to impoverish the less developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_non-aligned_world">The non-aligned world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The non-aligned world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neocolonialism was used to describe a type of foreign intervention in countries belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Pan-Africanism" title="Pan-Africanism">Pan-Africanist</a> movement, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Bandung_Conference" title="Bandung Conference">Bandung Conference</a> (Asian‚ÄìAfrican Conference, 1955), which led to the <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a> (1961). Neocolonialism was formally defined by the All-African Peoples' Conference (AAPC) and published in the <i>Resolution on Neo-colonialism</i>. At both the Tunis conference (1960) and the Cairo conference (1961), AAPC described the actions of the <a href="/wiki/French_Community" title="French Community">French Community</a> of independent states, organized by France, as neocolonial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the decades of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the countries of the <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Solidarity_with_the_People_of_Asia,_Africa_and_Latin_America" title="Organization of Solidarity with the People of Asia, Africa and Latin America">Organization of Solidarity with the People of Asia, Africa and Latin America</a> defined neocolonialism as the primary, collective enemy of the economies and cultures of their respective countries. Moreover, neocolonialism was integrated into the national liberation ideologies of Marxist guerrilla armies. During the 1970s, in the Portuguese African colonies of <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, upon assuming government power, the Liberation Front of Mozambique (<a href="/wiki/FRELIMO" title="FRELIMO">FRELIMO</a>, Frente de Liberta√ß√£o de Mo√ßambique), and the People's Movement for the Liberation of Angola ‚Äî Labour Party (<a href="/wiki/MPLA" title="MPLA">MPLA</a>, Movimento Popular de Liberta√ß√£o de Angola ‚Äî Partido do Trabalho), respectively, established policies to counter neocolonial agreements with the (former) colonist country.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fran.C3.A7afrique">Fran√ßafrique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fran√ßafrique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The representative example of European neocolonialism is <i><a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7afrique" title="Fran√ßafrique">Fran√ßafrique</a></i>, the "French Africa" constituted by the continued close relationships between <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and its former African colonies. In 1955, the initial usage of the "French Africa" term, by President <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Houphou%C3%ABt-Boigny" title="F√©lix Houphou√´t-Boigny">F√©lix Houphou√´t-Boigny</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, denoted positive social, cultural and economic Franco‚ÄìAfrican relations. It was later applied by neocolonialism critics to describe an imbalanced international relation. The politician <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Foccart" title="Jacques Foccart">Jacques Foccart</a>, the principal advisor for African matters to French presidents <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a> (1958‚Äì69) and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Pompidou" title="Georges Pompidou">Georges Pompidou</a> (1969‚Äì1974), was the principal proponent of <i>Fran√ßafrique</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The works of Verschave and Beti reported a forty-year, post-independence relationship with France's former colonial peoples, which featured colonial garrisons <i>in situ</i> and monopolies by French <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporations</a>, usually for the exploitation of mineral resources. It was argued that the African leaders with close ties to France ‚Äî especially during the Soviet‚ÄìAmerican Cold War (1945‚Äì91) ‚Äî acted more as agents of French business and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geopolitical" title="Geopolitical">geopolitical</a> interests, than as the national leaders of sovereign states. Cited examples are <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bongo" title="Omar Bongo">Omar Bongo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>), F√©lix Houphou√´t-Boigny (Ivory Coast), <a href="/wiki/Gnassingb%C3%A9_Eyad%C3%A9ma" title="Gnassingb√© Eyad√©ma">Gnassingb√© Eyad√©ma</a> (<a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denis_Sassou-Nguesso" title="Denis Sassou-Nguesso">Denis Sassou-Nguesso</a> (<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a>), <a href="/wiki/Idriss_D%C3%A9by" title="Idriss D√©by">Idriss D√©by</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Hamani_Diori" title="Hamani Diori">Hamani Diori</a> (<a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>).</p>
<dl>
<dt>Francophonie</dt>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_Community" title="French Community">French Community</a> (1958‚Äì95) and the seventy-five-country <i><a href="/wiki/Francophonie" title="Francophonie">Organisation Internationale de la Francophonie</a></i> (International Francophone Organisation) have been criticized as agents of French neocolonial African influence, especially by promoting the French language; however, in 1966, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algerian</a> intellectual <a href="/wiki/Kateb_Yacine" title="Kateb Yacine">Kateb Yacine</a> said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>La Francophonie</i> might be a neo-colonial political machine, which only perpetuates our <a href="/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation" title="Marx's theory of alienation">alienation</a>, but the usage of the French language does not mean that one is an agent of a foreign power; and I write in French to tell the French that I am not French.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>‚Äî‚ÄâKateb Yacine biography, <i>Arabesques</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgian_Congo">Belgian Congo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Belgian Congo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the decolonization of <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> continued to control, through the <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_G%C3%A9n%C3%A9rale_de_Belgique" title="Soci√©t√© G√©n√©rale de Belgique">Soci√©t√© G√©n√©rale de Belgique</a>, an estimated 70% of the Congolese economy following the decolonization process. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dependency_theory">Dependency theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dependency theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dependency_theory" title="Dependency theory">Dependency theory</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dependency_theory" title="Dependency theory">Dependency theory</a> is the theoretical description of economic neocolonialism. It proposes that the global economic system comprises wealthy countries at the center, and poor countries at the periphery. Economic neocolonialism extracts the human and natural resources of a poor country to flow to the economies of the wealthy countries. It claims that the poverty of the peripheral countries is the result of how they are integrated in the global economic system. Dependency theory derives from the <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> analysis of economic inequalities within the world's system of economies, thus, under-development of the periphery is a direct result of development in the center. It includes the concept of the late 19th century <a href="/wiki/Semi-colony" title="Semi-colony">semi-colony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It contrasts the Marxist perspective of the Theory of Colonial Dependency with capitalist economics. The latter proposes that poverty is a development stage in the poor country's progress towards full integration in the global economic system. Proponents of Dependency Theory, such as Venezuelan historian <a href="/wiki/Federico_Brito_Figueroa" title="Federico Brito Figueroa">Federico Brito Figueroa</a>, who investigated the socioeconomic bases of neocolonial dependency, influenced the thinking of the former President of Venezuela, <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Ch√°vez">Hugo Ch√°vez</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cold_War">The Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a></div>
<p>During the mid-to-late 20th century, in the course of the ideological conflict between the U.S. and the U.S.S.R., each country and its <a href="/wiki/Satellite_state" title="Satellite state">satellite states</a> accused each other of practising neocolonialism in their <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemonic</a> pursuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KanetMiner1992_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-KanetMiner1992-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruether2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruether2008-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi2008_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi2008-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shannon1996_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shannon1996-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard1996_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard1996-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seton-Watson1977_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seton-Watson1977-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The struggle included <a href="/wiki/Proxy_war" title="Proxy war">proxy wars</a>, fought by client states in the decolonised countries. Cuba, the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact bloc</a>, Egypt under <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Gamal Abdel Nasser</a> (1956‚Äì70), <i>et al.</i> accused the U.S. of sponsoring anti-democratic governments whose r√©gimes did not represent the interests of their people and of overthrowing elected governments (African, Asian, Latin American) that did not support U.S. geopolitical interests.</p>
<p>In the 1960s, under the leadership of Chairman <a href="/wiki/Mehdi_Ben_Barka" title="Mehdi Ben Barka">Mehdi Ben Barka</a>, the Cuban <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Solidarity_with_the_People_of_Asia,_Africa_and_Latin_America" title="Organization of Solidarity with the People of Asia, Africa and Latin America">Tricontinental Conference</a> (Organization of Solidarity with the People of Asia, Africa and Latin America) recognised and supported the validity of revolutionary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticolonialism" title="Anticolonialism">anti-colonialism</a> as a means for colonised peoples of the Third World to achieve self-determination, which policy angered the U.S. and France. Moreover, Chairman Barka headed the Commission on Neocolonialism, which dealt with the work to resolve the neocolonial involvement of colonial powers in decolonised counties; and said that the U.S., as the leading capitalist country of the world, was, in practise, the principal neocolonialist political actor.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multinational_corporations">Multinational corporations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Multinational corporations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">Multinational corporation</a></div>
<p>Critics of neocolonialism also argue that investment by <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporations</a> enriches few in underdeveloped countries and causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanitarian" title="Humanitarian">humanitarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> damage to their populations. They argue that this results in <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">unsustainable development</a> and perpetual underdevelopment. These countries remain reservoirs of cheap labor and raw materials, while restricting access to advanced production techniques to develop their own economies. In some countries, privatization of natural resources, while initially leading to an influx of investment, is often followed by increases in unemployment, poverty and a decline in per-capita income.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the West African nations of Guinea-Bissau, Senegal and Mauritania, fishing was historically central to the economy. Beginning in 1979, the European Union began negotiating contracts with governments for fisheries off the coast of West Africa. Commercial, unsustainable, over-fishing by foreign fleets played a significant role in large-scale unemployment and migration of people across the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This violates the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Seas</a>, which recognizes the importance of fishing to local communities and insists that government fishing agreements with foreign companies should target only surplus stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_borrowing">International borrowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: International borrowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_debt" title="Criticism of debt">Criticism of debt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criticism_of_the_International_Monetary_Fund" title="Criticism of the International Monetary Fund">Criticism of the International Monetary Fund</a></div>
<p>To alleviate the effects of neocolonialism, American economist <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Sachs" title="Jeffrey Sachs">Jeffrey Sachs</a> recommended that the entire African debt (ca. 200 billion U.S. dollars) be dismissed, and recommended that African nations not repay the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund (IMF):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The time has come to end this charade. The debts are unaffordable. If they won't cancel the debts, I would suggest obstruction; <i>you</i> do it, yourselves. Africa should say: "Thank you very much, but we need this money to meet the needs of children who are dying, right now, so, we will put the debt-servicing payments into urgent social investment in health, education, drinking water, the control of AIDS, and other needs".</p>
</blockquote>

<p>To ensure a reliable, long-term supply of food, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Korean_government" title="South Korean government">South Korean government</a> and powerful Korean multinationals bought farming rights to millions of <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> of agricultural land in under-developed countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreatimes.co.kr_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-koreatimes.co.kr-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Korea's RG Energy Resources Asset Management CEO Park Yong-soo stressed that "the nation does not produce a single drop of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> and other key industrial minerals. To power economic growth and support people's livelihoods, we cannot emphasize too much that securing natural resources in foreign countries is a must for our future survival."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a> (FAO), <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Diouf" title="Jacques Diouf">Jacques Diouf</a>, stated that the rise in land deals could create a form of " neocolonialism", with poor states producing food for the rich at the expense of their own hungry people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2008, South Korean multinational <a href="/wiki/Daewoo" title="Daewoo">Daewoo</a> Logistics secured 1.3 million hectares of farmland in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> to grow maize and crops for <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>. Roughly half of the country's arable land, as well as rainforests were to be converted into <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm</a> and corn <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monocultures</a>, producing food for export from a country where a third of the population and 50 percent of children under 5 are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malnourished" title="Malnourished">malnourished</a>, using South African workers instead of locals. Local residents were not consulted or informed, despite being dependent on the land for food and income. The controversial deal played a major part in prolonged anti-government protests that resulted in over a hundred deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreatimes.co.kr_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-koreatimes.co.kr-34">[34]</a></sup> This was a source of popular resentment that contributed to the fall of then-President <a href="/wiki/Marc_Ravalomanana" title="Marc Ravalomanana">Marc Ravalomanana</a>. The new president, <a href="/wiki/Andry_Rajoelina" title="Andry Rajoelina">Andry Rajoelina</a>, cancelled the deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> later announced that South Korea was in talks to develop 100,000 hectares for food production and processing for 700 to 800 billion <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_won" title="South Korean won">won</a>. Scheduled to be completed in 2010, it was to be the largest single piece of overseas South Korean agricultural infrastructure ever built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreatimes.co.kr_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-koreatimes.co.kr-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Hyundai_Heavy_Industries" title="Hyundai Heavy Industries">Hyundai Heavy Industries</a> acquired a majority stake in a company cultivating 10,000 hectares of farmland in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> and a South Korean provincial government secured 95,000 hectares of farmland in <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Mindoro" title="Oriental Mindoro">Oriental Mindoro</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, to grow <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Jeolla" title="South Jeolla">South Jeolla</a> province became the first provincial government to benefit from a new central government fund to develop farmland overseas, receiving a loan of $1.9 million. The project was expected to produce 10,000 tonnes of feed in the first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> South Korean multinationals and provincial governments purchased land in <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgan_Province" title="Bulgan Province">Bulgan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. The national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Korean_government" title="South Korean government">South Korean government</a> announced its intention to invest 30 billion <a href="/wiki/South_Korean_won" title="South Korean won">won</a> in land in <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>. As of 2009 discussions with <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> were underway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koreatimes.co.kr_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-koreatimes.co.kr-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_approaches">Other approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the concept of neocolonialism was originally developed within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> theoretical framework and is generally employed by the political <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left</a>, the term " neocolonialism" is found in other theoretical frameworks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coloniality">Coloniality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Coloniality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Coloniality_of_power" title="Coloniality of power">Coloniality</a>" claims that knowledge production is strongly influenced by the context of the person producing the knowledge and that this has further disadvantaged developing countries with limited knowledge production infrastructure. It originated among critics of <a href="/wiki/Subaltern_Studies" title="Subaltern Studies">subaltern theories</a>, which, although strongly <a href="/wiki/Decoloniality" title="Decoloniality">de-colonial</a>, are less concerned with the source of knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_theory">Cultural theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cultural theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One variant of neocolonialism theory critiques <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_colonialism" title="Cultural colonialism">cultural colonialism</a></i>, the desire of wealthy nations to control other nations' values and perceptions through cultural means such as <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a>, language, education and religion, ultimately for economic reasons. One impact of this is "<a href="/wiki/Colonial_mentality" title="Colonial mentality">colonial mentality</a>", feelings of inferiority that lead post-colonial societies to latch onto physical and cultural differences between the foreigners and themselves. Foreign ways become held in higher esteem than indigenous ways. Given that colonists and colonizers were generally of different races, the colonised may over time hold that the colonisers' race was responsible for their superiority. Rejections of the colonizers culture, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negritude" title="Negritude">Negritude</a> movement, have been employed to overcome these associations. Post-colonial importation or continuation of cultural mores or elements may be regarded as a form of neocolonialism.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Postcolonialism">Postcolonialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Postcolonialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Postcolonialism" title="Postcolonialism">Postcolonialism</a></div>
<p>Post-colonialism theories in philosophy, political science, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-colonial_literature" title="Post-colonial literature">literature</a> and film deal with the cultural legacy of colonial rule. Post-colonialism studies examine how once-colonised writers articulate their national identity; how knowledge about the colonised was generated and applied in service to the interests of the colonizer; and how colonialist literature justified colonialism by presenting the colonised people as inferior whose society, culture and economy must be managed for them. Post-colonial studies incorporate subaltern studies of "<a href="/wiki/People%27s_history" title="People's history">history from below</a>"; post-colonial cultural evolution; the <a href="/wiki/Psychopathology" title="Psychopathology">psychopathology</a> of colonization (by <a href="/wiki/Frantz_Fanon" title="Frantz Fanon">Frantz Fanon</a>); and the cinema of film makers such as the Cuban <a href="/wiki/Third_Cinema" title="Third Cinema">Third Cinema</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Guti%C3%A9rrez_Alea" title="Tom√°s Guti√©rrez Alea">Tom√°s Guti√©rrez Alea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kidlat_Tahimik" title="Kidlat Tahimik">Kidlat Tahimik</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_theory">Critical theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Critical theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critiques of postcolonialism/neocolonialism are evident in <a href="/wiki/Literary_theory" title="Literary theory">literary theory</a>. <a href="/wiki/International_relations_theory" title="International relations theory">International relations theory</a> defined "postcolonialism" as a field of study. While the lasting effects of cultural colonialism are of central interest The intellectual antecedents in cultural critiques of neocolonialism are economic. <a href="/wiki/Critical_international_relations_theory" title="Critical international relations theory">Critical international relations theory</a> references neocolonialism from Marxist positions as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postpositivist" title="Postpositivist">postpositivist</a> positions, including <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Postcolonialism" title="Postcolonialism">postcolonial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a> approaches. These differ from both realism and liberalism in their <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontological</a> premises. The neo-liberalist approach tends to depict modern forms of colonialism as a <a href="/wiki/American_imperialism#Benevolent_imperialism" title="American imperialism">Benevolent imperialism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_and_neocolonialism">Conservation and neocolonialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neocolonialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conservation and neocolonialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
