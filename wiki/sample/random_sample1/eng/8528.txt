<p><b>Neognaths</b> (<b>Neognathae</b>) are <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">subclass</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neornithes" title="Neornithes">Neornithes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aves" title="Aves">Aves</a>. The Neognathae include virtually all living birds; exceptions being their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" title="Sister taxon">sister taxon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Palaeognathae" title="Palaeognathae">Palaeognathae</a>), which contains the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tinamous" title="Tinamous">tinamous</a> and the flightless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratites" title="Ratites">ratites</a>.</p>
<p>There are nearly 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of neognaths. Since the late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> period, from which the earliest <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> are known, they have undergone adaptive radiation producing the diversity of form, function, and behavior that we see today. It includes the order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passeriformes" title="Passeriformes">Passeriformes</a> (perching birds), the largest <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of land vertebrates, containing some 60% of living birds and being more than twice as speciose as <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and about five times as speciose as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiroptera" title="Chiroptera">Chiroptera</a> (bats), which are the largest clades of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. There are also some very small orders, usually birds of very unclear relationships like the puzzling <a href="/wiki/Hoatzin" title="Hoatzin">hoatzin</a>.</p>
<p>The neognaths have fused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metacarpal" title="Metacarpal">metacarpals</a>, an elongate third finger, and 13 or fewer <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebrae</a>. They differ from the Palaeognathae in features like the structure of their jawbones. "Neognathae" means "new jaws", but it seems that the supposedly "more ancient" paleognath jaws are among the few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apomorph" title="Apomorph">apomorphic</a> (more derived) features of this group as compared to the neognaths.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Neognathae were long <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">ranked</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superorder" title="Superorder">superorder</a> and not subdivided any further. Attempts to do so, as in the <i>Conspectus</i> of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a>, were never accepted by a significant majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist">ornithologists</a>. Until the 1980s, there was little subdivision of the Aves in general, and even less of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> merit. Since then, the availability of massive amounts of new data from <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Enantiornithes" title="Enantiornithes">Enantiornithes</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> birds) as well as molecular (<a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>) sequences allowed scientists to refine the classification. With new groups of neognath orders being verified, the taxonomic rank of the group needed to shift. Most researchers have now employed the unranked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_nomenclature" title="Phylogenetic nomenclature">phylogenetic nomenclature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol-neognath_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tol-neognath-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Neognathae are universally accepted to subdivide into two lineages, the "fowl" clade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galloanseres" title="Galloanseres">Galloanseres</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Neoaves" title="Neoaves">Neoaves</a> (sometimes called "higher neognaths"). The subdivisions of the latter are still not well resolved, but several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> lineages have been proposed, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mirandornithes" title="Mirandornithes">Mirandornithes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cypselomorphae" title="Cypselomorphae">Cypselomorphae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaves" title="Metaves">Metaves</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronaves" title="Coronaves">Coronaves</a>. Although groups such as the former two (uniting a few closely related orders) are robustly supported, this cannot be said for the Metaves and Coronaves division for which there is no material evidence at present, while the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> record of Neognathae is at present utterly devoid of birds that should have been present if these proposed clades were real.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics">Systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The orders are arranged in a sequence that attempts to follow the modern view on neognath <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogeny</a>. It differs from the widely used <a href="/wiki/The_Clements_Checklist_of_Birds_of_the_World" title="The Clements Checklist of Birds of the World">Clements taxonomy</a> as well as from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibley-Ahlquist_taxonomy" title="Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy">Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy</a>, combining those elements from each that more modern research agrees with while updating those that are refuted. Most of the changes affect those "higher landbirds" that are sometimes united as <a href="/wiki/Near_passerine" title="Near passerine">near passerines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Neognathia">Neognathia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neognathae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neognathia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Feduccia defined the clade <b>Neognathia</b> as birds whose <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">palatal</a> mobility increased due to the following modifications (Feduccia 1980, 1996):</p>
<ul>
<li>Loss of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Basipterygoid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Basipterygoid (page does not exist)">Basipterygoid</a> articulation with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranium" title="Cranium">cranium</a>.</li>
<li>Development of a <a href="/wiki/Pterygoid_bone" title="Pterygoid bone">pterygoid</a>/<a href="/wiki/Palatine_bone" title="Palatine bone">palatine</a> joint.</li>
<li>Reduction of the <a href="/wiki/Vomer" title="Vomer">vomer</a>, such that it does not reach caudally to the <a href="/wiki/Pterygoid_bone" title="Pterygoid bone">pterygoid</a>, or is lost entirely.</li>
</ul>
