<p><b>Renal function</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Nephrology" title="Nephrology">nephrology</a>, is an indication of the state of the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> and its role in <a href="/wiki/Renal_physiology" title="Renal physiology">renal physiology</a>. <b>Glomerular filtration rate</b> (<b>GFR</b>) describes the flow rate of filtered fluid through the kidney. <b>Creatinine clearance rate</b> (<b>C<sub>Cr</sub></b> or <b>CrCl</b>) is the volume of <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a> that is cleared of <a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a> per unit time and is a useful measure for approximating the GFR. Creatinine clearance exceeds GFR due to creatinine secretion,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which can be blocked by <a href="/wiki/Cimetidine" title="Cimetidine">cimetidine</a>. In alternative fashion, overestimation by older serum creatinine methods resulted in an underestimation of creatinine clearance, which provided a less biased estimate of GFR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Both GFR and C<sub>Cr</sub> may be accurately calculated by comparative measurements of substances in the blood and urine, or estimated by formulas using just a blood test result (<b>eGFR</b> and <b>eC<sub>Cr</sub></b>).</p>
<p>The results of these tests are used to assess the excretory function of the kidneys. Staging of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a> is based on categories of GFR as well as <a href="/wiki/Albuminuria" title="Albuminuria">albuminuria</a> and cause of <a href="/wiki/Kidney_disease" title="Kidney disease">kidney disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dosage of drugs that are excreted primarily via urine may need to be modified based on either GFR or creatinine clearance.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_markers">Indirect markers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Indirect markers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most doctors use the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a> concentrations of the waste substances of <a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> (U), as well as <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> (E), to determine renal function. These measures are adequate to determine whether a patient is suffering from <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">kidney disease</a>.</p>
<p>However, <b>blood urea nitrogen</b> (<a href="/wiki/Blood_urea_nitrogen" title="Blood urea nitrogen">BUN</a>) and <b><a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a></b> will not be raised above the normal range until 60% of total kidney function is lost. Hence, the more accurate <b>Glomerular filtration rate</b> or its approximation of the <b>creatinine clearance</b> is measured whenever renal disease is suspected or careful dosing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nephrotoxic" title="Nephrotoxic">nephrotoxic</a> drugs is required.</p>
<p>Elevated protein levels in urine mark some kidney disease. The most sensitive marker of <a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a> is elevated urine albumin. Persistent presence of more than 30 mg albumin per gram creatinine in the urine is diagnostic of chronic kidney disease (<a href="/wiki/Microalbuminuria" title="Microalbuminuria">microalbuminuria</a> is a level of 30 mg/L to 299 mg/L urine or 30–299 mg/24 h; a concentration of <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a> that is not detected by usual urine <a href="/wiki/Dipstick" title="Dipstick">dipstick</a> methods).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glomerular_filtration_rate">Glomerular filtration rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Glomerular filtration rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Glomerular filtration rate</b> (<b>GFR</b>) is the volume of fluid filtered from the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">renal</a> (kidney) <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus_(kidney)" title="Glomerulus (kidney)">glomerular</a> capillaries into the <a href="/wiki/Bowman%27s_capsule" title="Bowman's capsule">Bowman's capsule</a> per unit time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Central to the physiologic maintenance of GFR is the differential basal tone of the afferent and efferent arterioles (see diagram). In other words, the filtration rate is dependent on the difference between the higher blood pressure created by vasoconstriction of the input or afferent arteriole versus the lower blood pressure created lesser vasoconstriction of the output or efferent arteriole.</p>
<p>Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) is equal to the Clearance Rate when any solute is freely filtered and is neither reabsorbed nor secreted by the kidneys. The rate therefore measured is the quantity of the substance in the urine that originated from a calculable volume of blood. Relating this principle to the below equation - for the substance used, the product of urine concentration and urine flow equals the mass of substance excreted during the time that urine has been collected. This mass equals the mass filtered at the glomerulus as nothing is added or removed in the nephron. Dividing this mass by the plasma concentration gives the volume of plasma which the mass must have originally come from, and thus the volume of plasma fluid that has entered Bowman's capsule within the aforementioned period of time. The GFR is typically recorded in units of <i>volume per time</i>, e.g., milliliters per minute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">mL</a>/<a href="/wiki/Minute" title="Minute">min</a>. Compare to <a href="/wiki/Filtration_fraction" title="Filtration fraction">filtration fraction</a>.</p>

<p>There are several different techniques used to calculate or estimate the glomerular filtration rate (GFR or eGFR). The above formula only applies for GFR calculation when it is equal to the Clearance Rate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_using_inulin">Measurement using inulin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Measurement using inulin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GFR can be determined by injecting <a href="/wiki/Inulin" title="Inulin">inulin</a> or the inulin-analog <a href="/wiki/Sinistrin" title="Sinistrin">sinistrin</a> into the plasma. Since both inulin and sinistrin are neither reabsorbed nor secreted by the kidney after glomerular filtration, their rate of excretion is directly proportional to the rate of filtration of water and solutes across the glomerular filter. Compared to the MDRD formula<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, the inulin clearance slightly overestimates the glomerular function. In early stage renal disease, the inulin clearance may remain normal due to <a href="/wiki/Glomerular_hyperfiltration" title="Glomerular hyperfiltration">hyperfiltration</a> in the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nephrons" title="Nephrons">nephrons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Incomplete urine collection is an important source of error in inulin clearance measurement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_with_radioactive_tracers">Measurement with radioactive tracers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measurement with radioactive tracers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GFR can be accurately measured using radioactive substances, in particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromium-51" title="Chromium-51">Chromium-51</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m" title="Technetium-99m">Technetium-99m</a>. These come close to the ideal properties of Inulin (undergoing only glomerular filtration) but can be measured more practically with only a few urine or blood samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Measurement of renal or plasma clearance of 51Cr-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a> is widely used in Europe but not available in the United States, where 99mTc-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DTPA" title="DTPA">DTPA</a> may be used instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Renal and plasma clearance 51Cr-EDTA has been shown to be accurate in comparison with the gold standard, Inulin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Use of 51Cr‑EDTA is considered a reference standard measure in UK guidance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creatinine-based_approximations_of_GFR">Creatinine-based approximations of GFR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Creatinine-based approximations of GFR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In clinical practice, however, <b>creatinine clearance</b> or estimates of creatinine clearance based on the serum creatinine level are used to measure GFR. Creatinine is produced naturally by the body (<a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a> is a breakdown product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creatine_phosphate" title="Creatine phosphate">creatine phosphate</a>, which is found in muscle). It is freely filtered by the glomerulus, but also actively <a href="/wiki/Renal_physiology#Secretion" title="Renal physiology">secreted by the peritubular capillaries</a> in very small amounts such that creatinine clearance overestimates actual GFR by 10% to 20%. This margin of error is acceptable, considering the ease with which creatinine clearance is measured. Unlike precise GFR measurements involving constant infusions of inulin, creatinine is already at a steady-state concentration in the blood, and so measuring creatinine clearance is much less cumbersome. However, creatinine estimates of GFR have their limitations. All of the estimating equations depend on a prediction of the 24-hour creatinine excretion rate, which is a function of muscle mass which is quite variable. One of the equations, the Cockcroft and Gault equation (see below) does not correct for race. With a higher muscle mass, serum creatinine will be higher for any given rate of clearance.</p>
<p>A common mistake made when just looking at serum creatinine is the failure to account for muscle mass. Hence, an older woman with a serum creatinine of 1.4 mg/dL may actually have a moderately severe degree of renal insufficiency, whereas a young muscular male can have a normal level of renal function at this serum creatinine level. Creatinine-based equations should be used with caution in <a href="/wiki/Cachexia" title="Cachexia">cachectic</a> patients and patients with <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>. They often have very low muscle mass and a much lower creatinine excretion rate than predicted by the equations below, such that a cirrhotic patient with a serum creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL may have a moderately severe degree of renal insufficiency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creatinine_Clearance_CCr">Creatinine Clearance C<sub>Cr</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Creatinine Clearance CCr">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One method of determining GFR from creatinine is to collect urine (usually for 24 h) to determine the amount of creatinine that was removed from the blood over a given time interval. If one removes 1440 mg in 24 h, this is equivalent to removing 1 mg/min. If the blood concentration is 0.01 mg/mL (1 mg/dL), then one can say that 100 mL/min of blood is being "cleared" of creatinine, since, to get 1 mg of creatinine, 100 mL of blood containing 0.01 mg/mL would need to have been cleared.</p>
<p>Creatinine clearance (C<sub>Cr</sub>) is calculated from the creatinine concentration in the collected urine sample (U<sub>Cr</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Urine_flow_rate" title="Urine flow rate">urine flow rate</a> (V<i>dt</i>), and the plasma concentration (P<sub>Cr</sub>). Since the product of urine concentration and urine flow rate yields creatinine excretion rate, which is the rate of removal from the blood, creatinine clearance is calculated as removal rate per min (U<sub>Cr</sub>×V<i>dt</i>) divided by the plasma creatinine concentration. <p>Twenty-four-hour urine collection to assess creatinine clearance is no longer widely performed, due to difficulty in assuring complete specimen collection. To assess the adequacy of a complete collection, one always calculates the amount of creatinine excreted over a 24-hour period. This amount varies with muscle mass, and is higher in young people vs. old, in blacks vs. whites, and in men vs. women. An unexpectedly low or high 24-hour creatinine excretion rate voids the test. Nevertheless, in cases where estimates of creatinine clearance from serum creatinine are unreliable, creatinine clearance remains a useful test. These cases include "estimation of GFR in individuals with variation in dietary intake (vegetarian diet, creatine supplements) or muscle mass (amputation, malnutrition, muscle wasting), since these factors are not specifically taken into account in prediction equations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimated_values">Estimated values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Estimated values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of formulae have been devised to estimate GFR or C<sub>cr</sub> values on the basis of serum creatinine levels. Where not otherwise stated serum creatinine is assumed to be stated in mg/dL, not µmol/L—divide by 88.4 to convert from µmol/Lto mg/dL.</p>

<p>One interesting feature of the Cockcroft and Gault equation is that it shows how dependent the estimation of CCr is based on age. The age term is (140 - age). This means that a 20-year-old person (140-20 = 120) will have twice the creatinine clearance as an 80-year-old (140-80 = 60) for the same level of serum creatinine. The C-G equation assumes that a woman will have a 15% lower creatinine clearance than a man at the same level of serum creatinine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimated_GFR_.28eGFR.29_using_Modification_of_Diet_in_Renal_Disease_.28MDRD.29_formula">Estimated GFR (eGFR) using Modification of Diet in Renal Disease (MDRD) formula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Estimated GFR (eGFR) using Modification of Diet in Renal Disease (MDRD) formula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most recently advocated formula for calculating the GFR is the one that was developed by the <i>Modification of Diet in Renal Disease Study Group</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10075613_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10075613-18">[18]</a></sup> Most laboratories in Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17937643_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17937643-19">[19]</a></sup> and The United Kingdom now calculate and report the MDRD estimated GFR along with creatinine measurements and this forms the basis of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease#Stages" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The adoption of the automatic reporting of MDRD-eGFR has been widely criticised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16398632_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16398632-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17041249_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17041249-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18219370_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18219370-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most commonly used formula is the "4-variable MDRD," which estimates GFR using four variables: serum creatinine, age, ethnicity, and gender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11904577_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11904577-24">[24]</a></sup> The original MDRD used six variables with the additional variables being the <a href="/wiki/Blood_urea_nitrogen" title="Blood urea nitrogen">blood urea nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a> levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10075613_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10075613-18">[18]</a></sup> The equations have been validated in patients with chronic kidney disease; however, both versions underestimate the GFR in healthy patients with GFRs over 60 mL/min.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15611490_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15611490-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16908915_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16908915-26">[26]</a></sup> The equations have not been validated in acute renal failure.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimated_GFR_.28eGFR.29_using_the_CKD-EPI_formula">Estimated GFR (eGFR) using the CKD-EPI formula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Estimated GFR (eGFR) using the CKD-EPI formula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CKD-EPI (Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration) formula was published in May 2009. It was developed in an effort to create a formula more accurate than the MDRD formula, especially when actual GFR is greater than 60 mL/min per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup>.</p>
<p>Researchers pooled data from multiple studies to develop and validate this new equation. They used 10 studies that included 8254 participants, randomly using 2/3 of the data sets for development and the other 1/3 for internal validation. Sixteen additional studies, which included 3896 participants, were used for external validation.</p>
<p>The CKD-EPI equation performed better than the MDRD (Modification of Diet in Renal Disease Study) equation, especially at higher GFR, with less bias and greater accuracy. When looking at NHANES (National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey) data, the median estimated GFR was 94.5 mL/min per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> vs. 85.0 mL/min per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup>, and the prevalence of chronic kidney disease was 11.5% versus 13.1%. Despite its overall superiority to the MDRD equation, the CKD-EPI equations performed poorly in certain populations, including black women, the elderly and the obese, and was less popular among clinicians than the MDRD estimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>The method of selection of the constant <i>k</i> has been questioned as being dependent upon the gold-standard of renal function used (i.e., creatinine clearance, inulin clearance, etc.) and also may be dependent upon the urinary flow rate at the time of measurement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10079885_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10079885-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009 the formula was updated to use standardized serum creatinine (recommend <i>k</i>=0.413) and additional formulas that allow improved precision were derived if serum cystatin is measured in addition to serum creatinine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_calibration_of_the_serum_creatinine_level_and_the_IDMS_standardization_effort">Importance of calibration of the serum creatinine level and the IDMS standardization effort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Importance of calibration of the serum creatinine level and the IDMS standardization effort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One problem with any creatinine-based equation for GFR is that the methods used to assay creatinine in the blood differ widely in their susceptibility to non-specific chromogens, which cause the creatinine value to be overestimated. In particular, the MDRD equation was derived using serum creatinine measurements that had this problem. The NKDEP program in the United States has attempted to solve this problem by trying to get all laboratories to calibrate their measures of creatinine to a "gold standard", which in this case is <a href="/wiki/Isotope_dilution" title="Isotope dilution">isotope dilution mass spectrometry</a> (IDMS). In late 2009 not all labs in the U.S. had changed over to the new system. There are two forms of the MDRD equation that are available, depending on whether or not creatinine was measured by an IDMS-calibrated assay. The CKD-EPI equation is designed to be used with IDMS-calibrated serum creatinine values only.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cystatin_C">Cystatin C</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Cystatin C">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Problems with creatinine (varying muscle mass, recent meat ingestion (much less dependent on the diet than urea), etc.) have led to evaluation of alternative agents for estimation of GFR. One of these is <a href="/wiki/Cystatin_C" title="Cystatin C">cystatin C</a>, a ubiquitous protein secreted by most cells in the body (it is an inhibitor of cysteine protease).</p>
<p>Cystatin C is freely filtered at the glomerulus. After filtration, Cystatin C is reabsorbed and catabolized by the tubular epithelial cells, with only small amounts excreted in the urine. Cystatin C levels are therefore measured not in the urine, but in the bloodstream.</p>
<p>Equations have been developed linking estimated GFR to serum cystatin C levels. Most recently, some proposed equations have combined (sex, age and race) adjusted cystatin C and creatinine. The most accurate is (sex, age and race) adjusted cystatin C, followed by (sex, age and race) adjusted creatinine and then cystatine C alone in slightly different with adjusted creatinine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Normal_ranges">Normal ranges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Normal ranges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The normal range of GFR, adjusted for <a href="/wiki/Body_surface_area" title="Body surface area">body surface area</a>, is 100 mL/min/1.73m<sup>2</sup> to 130 mL/min/1.73m<sup>2</sup> in men and 90 mL/min/1.73m<sup>2</sup> to 120 ml/min/1.73m<sup>2</sup> in women younger than the age of 40. In children, GFR measured by inulin clearance is 110 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup> until 2 years of age in both sexes, and then it progressively decreases. After age 40, GFR decreases progressively with age, by about 0.4 mL/min to 1.2 mL/min per year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronic_kidney_disease_stages">Chronic kidney disease stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Chronic kidney disease stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">Chronic kidney disease</a></div>
<p>Risk factors for kidney disease include diabetes, high blood pressure, family history, older age, ethnic group and smoking. For most patients, a GFR over 60 mL/min/1.73m<sup>2</sup> is adequate. But significant decline of the GFR from a previous test result can be an early indicator of kidney disease requiring medical intervention. The sooner kidney dysfunction is diagnosed and treated the greater odds of preserving remaining nephrons, and preventing the need for dialysis.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease#Stages" title="Chronic kidney disease">severity of chronic kidney disease</a> (CKD) is described by six stages; the most severe three are defined by the MDRD-eGFR value, and first three also depend on whether there is other evidence of kidney disease (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a>):</p>
<dl>
<dd>0) Normal kidney function – GFR above 90 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup> and no <a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a></dd>
<dd>1) CKD1 – GFR above 90 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup> with evidence of kidney damage</dd>
<dd>2) CKD2 (mild) – GFR of 60 to 89 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup> with evidence of kidney damage</dd>
<dd>3) CKD3 (moderate) – GFR of 30 to 59 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup></dd>
<dd>4) CKD4 (severe) – GFR of 15 to 29 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup></dd>
<dd>5) CKD5 kidney failure - GFR less than 15 mL/min/1.73 m<sup>2</sup> Some people add CKD5D for those stage 5 patients requiring dialysis; many patients in CKD5 are not yet on dialysis.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Note: others add a "T" to patients who have had a transplant regardless of stage.</p>
<p>Not all clinicians agree with the above classification, suggesting that it may mislabel patients with mildly reduced kidney function, especially the elderly, as having a disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> A conference was held in 2009 regarding these controversies by Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) on CKD: Definition, Classification and Prognosis, gathering data on CKD prognosis to refine the definition and staging of CKD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
