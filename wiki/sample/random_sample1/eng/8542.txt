<p><b>Nephrology</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BD%CE%B5%CF%86%CF%81%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:νεφρός">νεφρός</a> <i>nephros</i> "<a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>", combined with the suffix <i>-logy</i>, "the study of") is a <a href="/wiki/Specialty_(medicine)" title="Specialty (medicine)">specialty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">pediatrics</a> that concerns itself with the study of normal kidney function, kidney problems, the treatment of kidney problems and <a href="/wiki/Renal_replacement_therapy" title="Renal replacement therapy">renal replacement therapy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kidney_transplantation" title="Kidney transplantation">kidney transplantation</a>). Systemic conditions that affect the kidneys (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_disease" title="Autoimmune disease">autoimmune disease</a>) and systemic problems that occur as a result of kidney problems (such as <a href="/wiki/Renal_osteodystrophy" title="Renal osteodystrophy">renal osteodystrophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>) are also studied in nephrology. A physician who has undertaken additional training to become an <a href="/wiki/Expert" title="Expert">expert</a> in nephrology may call themselves a <b>nephrologist</b> or <b>renal physician</b>.</p>
<p>The term "nephrology" was first used in about 1960. Before then, the specialty was usually referred to as "kidney medicine."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nephrology concerns the diagnosis and treatment of kidney diseases, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyte_disturbance" title="Electrolyte disturbance">electrolyte disturbances</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>, and the care of those requiring <a href="/wiki/Renal_replacement_therapy" title="Renal replacement therapy">renal replacement therapy</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_transplant" title="Renal transplant">renal transplant</a> patients. Many diseases affecting the kidney are systemic disorders not limited to the organ itself, and may require special treatment. Examples include acquired conditions such as systemic vasculitides (e.g. ANCA vasculitis) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autoimmune_diseases" title="Autoimmune diseases">autoimmune diseases</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lupus_erythematosus" title="Lupus erythematosus">lupus</a>), as well as congenital or genetic conditions such as polycystic kidney disease.</p>
<p>Patients are referred to nephrology specialists after a urinalysis, for various reasons, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acute_kidney_failure" title="Acute kidney failure">acute kidney failure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hematuria" title="Hematuria">hematuria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_stone" title="Kidney stone">kidney stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>, and disorders of acid/base or <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nephrologist">Nephrologist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nephrologist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A nephrologist is a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> who specializes in the care and treatment of kidney disease. Nephrology requires additional training to become an <a href="/wiki/Expert" title="Expert">expert</a> with advanced skills. Nephrologists may provide care to people without kidney problems and may work in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_medicine" title="General medicine">general/internal medicine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_transplant" title="Organ transplant">transplant medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppression management</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive_care" title="Intensive care">intensive care medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinical_pharmacology" title="Clinical pharmacology">clinical pharmacology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perioperative_medicine" title="Perioperative medicine">perioperative medicine</a>, or pediatric nephrology.</p>
<p>Nephrologists may further sub-specialise in <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_transplant" title="Organ transplant">kidney transplantation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a>, cancer-related kidney diseases (<a href="/wiki/Onconephrology" title="Onconephrology">Onconephrology</a>), procedural nephrology or other non-nephrology areas as described above.</p>
<p>Procedures a nephrologist may perform include native kidney and transplant <a href="/wiki/Renal_biopsy" title="Renal biopsy">kidney biopsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> access insertion (temporary vascular access lines, tunnelled vascular access lines, peritoneal dialysis access lines), <a href="/wiki/Fistula" title="Fistula">fistula</a> management (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiogram" title="Angiogram">angiographic</a> or surgical fistulogram and plasty), and <a class="external text" href="https://www.nlm.nih.gov/medlineplus/ency/article/003923.htm" rel="nofollow">bone biopsy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia_and_New_Zealand">Australia and New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Australia and New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Nephrology training in Australia and New Zealand typically includes completion of a medical degree (<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Medicine,_Bachelor_of_Surgery" title="Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery">Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery</a>: 4–6 years), <a href="/wiki/Internship_(medicine)" title="Internship (medicine)">internship</a> (1 year), Basic Physician Training (3 years minimum), successful completion of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australasian_College_of_Physicians" title="Royal Australasian College of Physicians">Royal Australasian College of Physicians</a> written and clinical examinations, and Advanced Physician Training in Nephrology (2–3 years). The training pathway is overseen and accredited by the Royal Australasian College of Physicians. Increasingly, nephrologists may additionally complete of a post-graduate degree (usually a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhD</a>) in a nephrology research interest (3–4 years). Finally, all Australian and New Zealand nephrologists participate in career-long professional and personal development through the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australasian_College_of_Physicians" title="Royal Australasian College of Physicians">Royal Australasian College of Physicians</a> and other bodies such as the <a class="external text" href="http://www.nephrology.edu.au/" rel="nofollow">Australian and New Zealand Society of Nephrology</a> and the <a class="external text" href="http://www.tsanz.com.au/" rel="nofollow">Transplant Society of Australia and New Zealand</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the United Kingdom, nephrology (or often renal medicine) is a subspecialty of general medicine. A nephrologist has completed medical school, foundation year posts (FY1 and FY2) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_medical_training" title="Core medical training">core medical training</a> (CMT) and passed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Membership_of_the_Royal_College_of_Physicians" title="Membership of the Royal College of Physicians">Membership of the Royal College of Physicians</a> (MRCP) exam before competing for a National Training Number (NTN) in renal medicine. The typical subspecialty training is five years and leads to a <a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_Completion_of_Training" title="Certificate of Completion of Training">Certificate of Completion of Training</a> (CCT) in both renal medicine and general (internal) medicine. Subspecialty trainees often interrupt their clinical training to obtain research degrees (MD/PhD).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Nephrology training can be accomplished through one of two routes. The first being through an internal medicine pathway leading to an Internal Medicine/Nephrology specialty, and sometimes known as "adult nephrology". The second pathway being through <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">Pediatrics</a> leading to a speciality in Pediatric Nephrology. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Medical_school" title="Medical school">medical school</a> adult nephrologists complete a three-year <a href="/wiki/Residency_(medicine)" title="Residency (medicine)">residency</a> in internal medicine followed by a two-year (or longer) <a href="/wiki/Fellowship_(medicine)" title="Fellowship (medicine)">fellowship</a> in nephrology. Complementary to an adult nephrologist, a pediatric nephrologist will complete a three-year pediatric residency after medical school or a four-year Combined Internal Medicine and Pediatrics residency. This is followed by a three-year fellowship in Pediatic Nephrology. Once training is satisfactorily completed, the physician is eligible to take the <a href="/wiki/American_Board_of_Internal_Medicine" title="American Board of Internal Medicine">American Board of Internal Medicine</a> (ABIM) or <a href="/wiki/American_Osteopathic_Board_of_Internal_Medicine" title="American Osteopathic Board of Internal Medicine">American Osteopathic Board of Internal Medicine</a> (AOBIM) nephrology examination. Nephrologists must be approved by one of these boards. To be approved, the physician must fulfill the requirements for education and training in nephrology in order to qualify to take the board's examination. If a physician passes the examination, then he or she can become a nephrology specialist. Typically, nephrologists also need two to three years of training in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACGME" title="ACGME">ACGME</a> or <a href="/wiki/American_Osteopathic_Association" title="American Osteopathic Association">AOA</a> accredited fellowship in nephrology. Nearly all programs train nephrologists in <a href="/wiki/Hemofiltration" title="Hemofiltration">continuous renal replacement therapy</a>; fewer than half in the United States train in the provision of <a href="/wiki/Plasmapheresis" title="Plasmapheresis">plasmapheresis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Only pediatric trained physicians are able to train in pediatric nephrology, and internal medicine (adult) trained physicians may enter general (adult) nephrology fellowships.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/History" title="History">History</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_examination" title="Physical examination">physical examination</a> are central to the diagnostic workup in nephrology. This may include inquires regarding family history, general medical history, diet, medication use, drug use and occupation. Examination typically includes an assessment of volume state, blood pressure, skin, joints, abdomen and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latus_(anatomy)" title="Latus (anatomy)">flank</a>.</p>
<p>Examination of the urine (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urinalysis" title="Urinalysis">urinalysis</a>) allows a direct assessment for possible kidney problems, which may be suggested by appearance of blood in the urine (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haematuria" title="Haematuria">haematuria</a>), protein in the urine (<a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a>), pus cells in the urine (<a href="/wiki/Pyuria" title="Pyuria">pyuria</a>) or cancer cells in the urine. A <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=24-hour_urine_collection&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="24-hour urine collection (page does not exist)">24-hour urine collection</a> can be used to quantify daily protein loss (see <a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a>), urine output, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creatinine_clearance" title="Creatinine clearance">creatinine clearance</a> or electrolyte handling by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_tubules" title="Renal tubules">renal tubules</a>.</p>
<p>Basic blood tests can be used to check the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platelets" title="Platelets">platelets</a>, sodium, potassium, chloride, bicarbonate, <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a>, calcium, magnesium or phosphate in the blood. All of these may be affected by kidney problems. The serum creatinine concentration can be used to estimate the function of the kidney, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creatinine_clearance" title="Creatinine clearance">creatinine clearance</a> or estimated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glomerular_filtration_rate" title="Glomerular filtration rate">glomerular filtration rate</a> (GFR). More specialized tests can be ordered to discover or link certain <a href="/wiki/Systemic_disease" title="Systemic disease">systemic diseases</a> to kidney failure such as infections (<a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B" title="Hepatitis B">hepatitis B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C" title="Hepatitis C">hepatitis C</a>), autoimmune conditions (<a href="/wiki/Lupus_nephritis" title="Lupus nephritis">systemic lupus erythematosus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANCA-associated_vasculitides" title="ANCA-associated vasculitides">ANCA vasculitis</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraproteinemias" title="Paraproteinemias">paraproteinemias</a> (<a href="/wiki/Amyloidosis" title="Amyloidosis">amyloidosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_myeloma" title="Multiple myeloma">multiple myeloma</a>) and metabolic diseases (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cystinosis" title="Cystinosis">cystinosis</a>).</p>
<p>Structural abnormalities of the kidneys are identified with imaging tests. These may include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">Medical ultrasonography/ultrasound</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_axial_tomography" title="Computed axial tomography">computed axial tomography (CT)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scintigraphy" title="Scintigraphy">scintigraphy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a>), <a href="/wiki/Angiography" title="Angiography">angiography</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)</a>.</p>
<p>In certain circumstances, less invasive testing may not provide a certain diagnosis. Where definitive diagnosis is required, a <a href="/wiki/Biopsy" title="Biopsy">biopsy</a> of the kidney (<a href="/wiki/Renal_biopsy" title="Renal biopsy">renal biopsy</a>) may be performed. This typically involves the insertion, under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_anaesthetic" title="Local anaesthetic">local anaesthetic</a> and ultrasound or CT guidance, of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_biopsy" title="Core biopsy">core biopsy</a> needle into the kidney to obtain a small sample of kidney tissue. The kidney tissue is then examined under a microscope, allowing direct visualization of the changes occurring within the kidney. Additionally, the pathology may also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staging_(pathology)" title="Staging (pathology)">stage</a> a problem affecting the kidney, allowing some degree of <a href="/wiki/Prognosis" title="Prognosis">prognostication</a>. In some circumstances, kidney biopsy will also be used to monitor response to treatment and identify early <a href="/wiki/Relapse" title="Relapse">relapse</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Treatments in nephrology can include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medications" title="Medications">medications</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_products" title="Blood products">blood products</a>, surgical interventions (<a href="/wiki/Urology" title="Urology">urology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vascular_surgery" title="Vascular surgery">vascular</a> or <a href="/wiki/General_surgery" title="General surgery">surgical</a> procedures), <a href="/wiki/Renal_replacement_therapy" title="Renal replacement therapy">renal replacement therapy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kidney_transplantation" title="Kidney transplantation">kidney transplantation</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_exchange" title="Plasma exchange">plasma exchange</a>. Kidney problems can have significant impact on quality and length of life, and so psychological support, health education and advanced care planning play key roles in nephrology.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">Chronic kidney disease</a> is typically managed with treatment of causative conditions (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>), avoidance of substances toxic to the kidneys (<a href="/wiki/Nephrotoxicity" title="Nephrotoxicity">nephrotoxins</a> like <a href="/wiki/Contrast_medium" title="Contrast medium">radiologic contrast</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-steroidal_anti-inflammatory_drugs" title="Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs">non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihypertensives" title="Antihypertensives">antihypertensives</a>, diet and weight modification and planning for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End-stage_kidney_failure" title="End-stage kidney failure">end-stage kidney failure</a>. Impaired kidney function has systemic effects on the body. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erythropoetin" title="Erythropoetin">erythropoetin stimulating agent</a> may be required to ensure adequate production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcitriol" title="Calcitriol">activated vitamin D</a> supplements and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphate_binders" title="Phosphate binders">phosphate binders</a> may be required to counteract the effects of kidney failure on <a href="/wiki/Renal_osteodystrophy" title="Renal osteodystrophy">bone metabolism</a>, and blood volume and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyte_disturbance" title="Electrolyte disturbance">electrolyte disturbance</a> may need correction.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto-immune" title="Auto-immune">Auto-immune</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory</a> kidney disease, such as <a href="/wiki/Vasculitis" title="Vasculitis">vasculitis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transplant_rejection" title="Transplant rejection">transplant rejection</a>, may be treated with <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppression</a>. Commonly used agents are <a href="/wiki/Prednisone" title="Prednisone">prednisone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycophenolate" title="Mycophenolate">mycophenolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclophosphamide" title="Cyclophosphamide">cyclophosphamide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ciclosporin" title="Ciclosporin">ciclosporin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tacrolimus" title="Tacrolimus">tacrolimus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Everolimus" title="Everolimus">everolimus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thymoglobulin" title="Thymoglobulin">thymoglobulin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sirolimus" title="Sirolimus">sirolimus</a>. Newer, so-called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biologic_medical_product" title="Biologic medical product">biologic drugs</a>" or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclonal_antibodies" title="Monoclonal antibodies">monoclonal antibodies</a>, are also used in these conditions and include <a href="/wiki/Rituximab" title="Rituximab">rituximab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basiliximab" title="Basiliximab">basiliximab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eculizumab" title="Eculizumab">eculizumab</a>. Blood products including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous_immunoglobulin" title="Intravenous immunoglobulin">intravenous immunoglobulin</a> and a process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_exchange" title="Plasma exchange">plasma exchange</a> can also be employed.</p>
<p>When the kidneys are no longer able to sustain the demands of the body, end-stage kidney failure is said to have occurred. Without <a href="/wiki/Renal_replacement_therapy" title="Renal replacement therapy">renal replacement therapy</a>, death from <a href="/wiki/Kidney_failure" title="Kidney failure">kidney failure</a> will eventually result. <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">Dialysis</a> is an artificial method of replacing some kidney function to prolong life. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_transplantation" title="Renal transplantation">Renal transplantation</a> replaces kidney function by inserting into the body a healthier kidney from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_donor" title="Organ donor">organ donor</a> and inducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunologic_tolerance" title="Immunologic tolerance">immunologic tolerance</a> of that organ with <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppression</a>. At present, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_transplantation" title="Renal transplantation">renal transplantation</a> is the most effective treatment for end-stage kidney failure although its worldwide availability is limited by lack of availability of donor organs.</p>
<p>Most kidney conditions are <a href="/wiki/Chronic_condition" title="Chronic condition">chronic conditions</a> and so long term followup with a nephrologist is usually necessary.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nephrology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The world's first society of nephrology was the French 'Societe de Pathologie Renale'. Its first president was Jean Hamburger, and its first meeting was in Paris in February 1949. In 1959, Hamburger also founded the 'Société de Néphrologie', as a continuation of the older society. The UK's Renal Association was founded in 1950; the second society of nephrologists. Its first president was Arthur Osman. Its first meeting was on 30 March 1950 in London. The Società di Nefrologia Italiana was founded in 1957 and was the first national society to incorporate the phrase nephrologia (or nephrology) into its name.</p>
<p>The word 'nephrology' appeared for the first time in a conference, on 1–4 September 1960 at the "Premier Congrès International de Néphrologie" in Evian and Geneva, the first meeting of the International Society of Nephrology (ISN, <a href="/wiki/International_Society_of_Nephrology" title="International Society of Nephrology">International Society of Nephrology</a>). The first day (1.9.60) was in Geneva and the next three (2-4.9.60) were in Evian, France. The early history of the ISN is described by Robinson and Richet <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.nature.com/ki/journal/v59/n79s/full/4492365a.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> in 2005 and the later history by Barsoum <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.nature.com/ki/journal/v79/n9/abs/ki20117a.html" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> in 2011. The ISN is the largest global society representing medical professionals engaged in advancing kidney care worldwide.</p>
<p>In the USA, founded in 1964, the <a href="/wiki/National_Kidney_Foundation" title="National Kidney Foundation">National Kidney Foundation</a> is a national organization representing patients and professionals who treat kidney diseases. Founded in 1966, the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Nephrology" title="American Society of Nephrology">American Society of Nephrology</a> (ASN) is the world’s largest professional society devoted to the study of kidney disease. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Nephrology_Nurses%27_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Nephrology Nurses' Association (page does not exist)">American Nephrology Nurses' Association</a> (ANNA), founded in 1969, promotes excellence in and appreciation of nephrology nursing to make a positive difference for patients with kidney disease. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Association_of_Kidney_Patients&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Association of Kidney Patients (page does not exist)">American Association of Kidney Patients</a> (AAKP) is a non-profit, patient-centric group focused on improving the health and well-being of CKD and <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> patients. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renal_Administrators_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Renal Administrators Association (page does not exist)">National Renal Administrators Association</a> (NRAA), founded in 1977, is a national organization that represents and supports the independent and community-based dialysis providers. The <a href="/wiki/American_Kidney_Fund" title="American Kidney Fund">American Kidney Fund</a> directly provides financial support to patients in need, as well as participating in health education and prevention efforts. ASDIN (American Society of Diagnostic and Interventional Nephrology) is the main organization of interventional nephrologists. Other organizations include CIDA, VASA etc. which deal with dialysis vascular access. The <a href="/wiki/Renal_Support_Network" title="Renal Support Network">Renal Support Network</a> (RSN) is a nonprofit, patient-focused, patient-run organization that provides non-medical services to those affected by chronic kidney disease (CKD).</p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/UK_National_Kidney_Federation" title="UK National Kidney Federation">UK National Kidney Federation</a> and British Kidney Patient Association (BKPA)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> represents patients, and the <a href="/wiki/Renal_Association" title="Renal Association">Renal Association</a> represents renal physicians and works closely with the <a href="/wiki/National_Service_Framework" title="National Service Framework">National Service Framework</a> for kidney disease.</p>
