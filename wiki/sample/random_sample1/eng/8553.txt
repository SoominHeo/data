<p>The <b>short-tailed albatross</b> or <b>Steller's albatross</b> (<i>Phoebastria albatrus</i>) is a large rare <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a>. Although related to the other North Pacific <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a>, it also exhibits behavioural and morphological links to the albatrosses of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>. It was described by the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> from skins collected by the <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Steller" title="Georg Wilhelm Steller">Georg Wilhelm Steller</a> (after whom its other common name is derived). Once common, it was brought to the edge of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Plume_hunting" title="Plume hunting">trade in feathers</a>, but with protection has recently made a recovery.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-tailed_albatross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Short-tailed albatrosses are a type of albatross that belong to the family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diomedeidae" title="Diomedeidae">Diomedeidae</a>, order <a href="/wiki/Procellariiformes" title="Procellariiformes">Procellariiformes</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fulmar" title="Fulmar">fulmars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diving_petrel" title="Diving petrel">diving petrels</a>. They share certain identifying features. First, they have nasal passages that attach to the upper bill called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naricorns" title="Naricorns">naricorns</a>. Although the nostrils on the albatross are on the sides of the bill. The bills of Procellariiformes are also unique in that they are split into between seven and nine horny plates. Finally, they produce a <a href="/wiki/Stomach_oil" title="Stomach oil">stomach oil</a> made up of <a href="/wiki/Wax_ester" title="Wax ester">wax esters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triglycerides" title="Triglycerides">triglycerides</a> that is stored in the <a href="/wiki/Proventriculus" title="Proventriculus">proventriculus</a>. This is used against predators as well as an energy rich food source for chicks and for the adults during their long flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They also have a salt gland which is situated above the nasal passage helping desalinate their bodies, as an adaptation to the high amount of ocean water they imbibe. It excretes a high saline solution from their nose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Going back in time, <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> of albatrosses from the mid-<a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> are considered to be closest to the short-tailed albatross.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-tailed_albatross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The short-tailed albatross is a medium-sized albatross, with a wingspan of 215 to 230 cm (85–91 in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-6">[6]</a></sup> a length of 84 to 94 cm (33–37 in)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and a body weight that can be 4.3 to 8.5 kg (9.5–18.7 lb). Among standard measurements, the bill is 12.7–15.2 cm (5.0–6.0 in) long, the tail is 14–15.2 cm (5.5–6.0 in) long, the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> around 10 cm (3.9 in) and the wing chord 51 cm (20 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coues1903_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coues1903-9">[9]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> as an adult is overall white with black flight feathers, some coverts, as well as a black terminal bar on its tail. It has a yellow-stained <a href="/wiki/Nape" title="Nape">nape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is large and pink; however, older birds will gain a blue tip. The juveniles are an all-over brown colour, and they will whiten as they mature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLI_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-10">[10]</a></sup> in about 10 to 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sibley_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Floyd_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-12">[12]</a></sup> It can be distinguished from the other two species of albatross in its range, the <a href="/wiki/Laysan_albatross" title="Laysan albatross">Laysan albatross</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black-footed_albatross" title="Black-footed albatross">black-footed albatross</a> by its larger size and its pink bill (with a bluish tip), as well as details of its <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. Contrary to its name its tail is no shorter than that of the Laysan or black-footed, and is actually longer than that of the other member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoebastria" title="Phoebastria">Phoebastria</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Waved_albatross" title="Waved albatross">waved albatross</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_habitat">Range and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-tailed_albatross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Range and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Short-tailed albatrosses now nest on four islands, with the majority of birds nesting on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torishima_(Izu_Islands)" title="Torishima (Izu Islands)">Torishima</a>, and almost all of the rest on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minami-kojima#Nan_Xiaodao.2FMinami_Kojima" title="Minami-kojima">Minami-kojima</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Senkaku_Islands" title="Senkaku Islands">Senkaku Islands</a>. A female-female pair began nesting on <a href="/wiki/Kure_Atoll" title="Kure Atoll">Kure</a> in the late 2000s, but to date they have not produced a viable egg. A chick hatched on 14 January 2011 on <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">Midway</a>. Both Midway and Kure are in the <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Hawaiian_Islands" title="Northwestern Hawaiian Islands">Northwestern Hawaiian Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2012 a pair began incubating an egg on Muko-jima, in the <a href="/wiki/Bonin_Islands" title="Bonin Islands">Bonin Islands</a>, Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> During non-breeding season they range across the North <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, with the males and juveniles gathering in the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>, and the females feeding off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLI_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-10">[10]</a></sup> They can also be found as far east as <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. In fact, the short-tailed albatross is seen on a number of the United States' state endangered species lists including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extirpated" title="Extirpated">extirpated</a> as a breeder from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kita-no-shima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kita-no-shima (page does not exist)">Kita-no-shima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enewetak_Atoll" title="Enewetak Atoll">Enewetak Atoll</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kobishi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kobishi (page does not exist)">Kobishi</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bonin_Islands" title="Bonin Islands">Bonin Islands</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nishino-shima_Island" title="Nishino-shima Island">Nishino Shima</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yomejima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yomejima (page does not exist)">Yomejima</a>, and (until recently) <a href="/wiki/Mukojima" title="Mukojima">Mukojima</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOU_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AOU-3">[3]</a></sup> It also formerly bred on <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, making it the last albatross to inhabit the north Atlantic.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-tailed_albatross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The short-tailed albatross feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, but will follow ships for their discarded <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLI_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-tailed_albatross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This albatross historically preferred to nest on large open areas near stands of the grass, <i><a href="/wiki/Miscanthus_sinensis" title="Miscanthus sinensis">Miscanthus sinensis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLI_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> classifies this species as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> with an occurrence range of 34,800,000 km<sup>2</sup> (13,400,000 sq mi) and a breeding range of 9 km<sup>2</sup> (3.5 sq mi).</p>
<p>The short-tailed albatross came perilously close to <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>. They were <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunted</a> on an almost industrial scale for their feathers in the later half of the 19th century, with some estimates claiming upward of 10 million birds hunted. By the 1930s the only population left was on Torishima, between 1927 and until 1933 hunting continued when the Japanese government declared the ban of hunting to save the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, after which the albatrosses stopped breeding on the island. At this point the species was assumed to be extinct and research became impossible with the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. On 1949 an American researcher arriving on this island declared the species to be extinct, but an estimated 50 individuals, most likely juveniles, survived at sea (all albatross species take a long time to reach sexual maturity and will not return to their natal colony for many years). After the return of the birds they were more carefully protected, and the first egg was laid by the returning birds in 1954. Varieties of albatross decoys were placed around on the island after it was discovered that like other albatross species, this species also were enticed to breed if placed in a group.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Today, <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline</a> fisheries, and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic eruptions</a> on <a href="/wiki/Tori-shima_(Izu_Islands)" title="Tori-shima (Izu Islands)">Tori-shima</a> are the largest threats; however, introduced predators, environmental contaminants, soil instability, and extreme weather are also threats.</p>
<p>There are many measures underway to protect this species. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and the United States list this bird as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_species" title="Protected species">protected species</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tori-shima_(Izu_Islands)" title="Tori-shima (Izu Islands)">Tori-shima</a> is a National Wildlife Protection Area, and native plant species are being transplanted to assist in nesting. Also, most commercial <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline</a> fisheries use <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> mitigation devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLI_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
