<p>The <b>Boeing Vertol CH-46 Sea Knight</b> is a medium-lift <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tandem_rotor" title="Tandem rotor">tandem rotor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_helicopter" title="Transport helicopter">transport helicopter</a> powered by twin <a href="/wiki/Turboshaft" title="Turboshaft">turboshaft</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">aircraft engines</a>. It was used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> (USMC) to provide all-weather, day-or-night assault transport of combat troops, supplies and equipment until it was replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MV-22_Osprey" title="MV-22 Osprey">MV-22 Osprey</a>. Additional tasks included combat support, <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> (SAR), support for forward refueling and rearming points, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CASEVAC" title="CASEVAC">CASEVAC</a> and Tactical Recovery of Aircraft and Personnel (TRAP).</p>
<p>The Sea Knight was also the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s standard medium-lift <a href="/wiki/Utility_helicopter" title="Utility helicopter">utility helicopter</a> until it was phased out in favor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MH-60S_Knighthawk" title="MH-60S Knighthawk">MH-60S Knighthawk</a> in the early 2000s. Canada also operated the Sea Knight, designated as CH-113, and operated them in the SAR role until 2004. Other export customers include Japan, Sweden, and Saudi Arabia. The commercial version is the <b>BV 107-II</b>, commonly referred to simply as the "Vertol".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Piasecki Helicopter was a pioneering developer of tandem-rotor helicopters, with the most famous previous helicopter being the <a href="/wiki/Piasecki_H-21" title="Piasecki H-21">H-21</a> "Flying Banana". Piasecki Helicopter became <a href="/wiki/Piasecki_Helicopter" title="Piasecki Helicopter">Vertol</a> in 1955 and work began on a new tandem rotor helicopter designated the <i>Vertol Model 107</i> or V-107 in 1956. The V-107 prototype had two <a href="/wiki/Lycoming_T53" title="Lycoming T53">Lycoming T53</a> <a href="/wiki/Turboshaft" title="Turboshaft">turboshaft</a> engines, producing 877 shp (640 kW) each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apostolo_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Apostolo-2">[2]</a></sup> The first flight of the V-107 took place on 22 April 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donald_V-107_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donald_V-107-3">[3]</a></sup> The V-107 was then put through a flight demonstration tour in the United States and overseas. In June 1958, the U.S. Army awarded a contract to Vertol for ten production aircraft designated "YHC-1A".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirly_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-whirly-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The order was later decreased to three, so that the Army could divert funds for the V-114, also a turbine powered tandem, but larger than the V-107.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirly_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-whirly-4">[4]</a></sup> The Army's three YHC-1As were powered by <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_T58" title="General Electric T58">GE-T-58</a> engines. The YHC-1As first flew in August 1959, and were followed by an improved commercial/export model, the 107-II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CH-46_hist_USN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CH-46_hist_USN-1">[1]</a></sup> During 1960, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> evolved a requirement for a medium-lift, twin-turbine troop/cargo assault helicopter to replace the piston-engined types then in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert_Tandom_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-vert_Tandom-5">[5]</a></sup> That same year Boeing acquired Vertol and renamed the group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_Helicopters" title="Boeing Helicopters">Boeing Vertol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirly_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-whirly-4">[4]</a></sup> Following a competition, Boeing Vertol was selected to build its model 107M as the HRB-1, early in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CH-46_hist_USN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-CH-46_hist_USN-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1962 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> ordered 12 XCH-46B Sea Knights with the <i>XH-49A</i> designation, but later cancelled the order due to a delivery delay and opted for the <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_S-61R" title="Sikorsky S-61R">Sikorsky S-61R</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the Sea Knight's first flight in August 1962, the designation was changed to CH-46A. In November 1964, introduction of the Marines' CH-46A and the Navy's UH-46As began. The UH-46A variant was modified for the vertical replenishment role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CH-46_hist_USN_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-CH-46_hist_USN-1">[1]</a></sup> The CH-46A was equipped with a pair of T58-GE8-8B turboshaft engines rated at 1,250 shp (930 kW) each and could carry 17 passengers or 4,000 pounds (1,815 kg) of cargo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_developments">Further developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Production of the improved CH-46D followed with deliveries beginning in 1966. Its improvements included modified rotor blades and more powerful T58-GE-10 turboshaft engines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CH-46_hist_USN_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-CH-46_hist_USN-1">[1]</a></sup> rated at 1,400 shp (1,040 kW) each. The increased power allowed the D-model to carry 25 troops or 7,000 pounds (3,180 kg) of cargo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup> The CH-46D was introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam theater</a> in late 1967, supplementing the U.S. Marine Corps' existing unreliable and problematic CH-46A fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Along with the USMC's CH-46Ds, the U.S. Navy received a small number of UH-46Ds for ship resupply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eden-9">[9]</a></sup> Also, approximately 33 CH-46As were upgraded to CH-46Ds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Marines also received CH-46Fs from 1968 to 1971. The F-model retained the D-model's T58-GE-10 engines but revised the avionics and included other modifications. The CH-46F was the final production model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CH-46_hist_USN_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-CH-46_hist_USN-1">[1]</a></sup> The Sea Knight has undergone upgrades and modifications. Most of the U.S. Marine Corps' Sea Knights were upgraded to CH-46E standard. The CH-46E features fiberglass rotor blades, airframe reinforcement, and further uprated T58-GE-16 engines producing 1,870 shp (1,390 kW) each. Some CH-46Es have been given double fuel capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup> The Dynamic Component Upgrade (DCU), incorporated starting in the mid-1990s, provides for increased capability through strengthened drive systems and rotor controls.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The commercial variant, the <i>BV 107-II</i>, was first ordered by <a href="/wiki/New_York_Airways" title="New York Airways">New York Airways</a> in 1960. They took delivery of their first three aircraft, configured for 25 passengers, in July 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert_Tandom_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-vert_Tandom-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1965, Boeing Vertol sold the manufacturing rights of the 107 to <a href="/wiki/Kawasaki_Heavy_Industries" title="Kawasaki Heavy Industries">Kawasaki Heavy Industries</a>. Under this arrangement, all Model 107 civilian and military aircraft <a href="#Japanese_versions">built in Japan</a> are known as <i>KV 107</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert_Tandom_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-vert_Tandom-5">[5]</a></sup> On 15 December 2006, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbia_Helicopters,_Inc" title="Columbia Helicopters, Inc">Columbia Helicopters, Inc</a> acquired the <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">type certificate</a> for the Boeing Vertol 107-II, and was in the process of acquiring a Production Certificate from the FAA. Plans for actual production of the aircraft were not announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert_Tandom_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-vert_Tandom-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="1416" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg/170px-US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg/255px-US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg/340px-US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_020514-M-7364D-002_Exercise_Cobra_Gold_2002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A U.S. Marine watches two CH-46 Sea Knights, 2002</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The CH-46 has tandem counter-rotating rotors powered by two <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_T58" title="General Electric T58">GE T58</a> turboshaft engines. The engines are mounted on each side of the rear rotor pedestal with a driveshaft to the forward rotor. The engines are coupled so either could power both rotors in an emergency. The rotors feature three blades and can be folded for on-ship operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup> The CH-46 has fixed tricycle landing gear, with twin wheels on all three landing gear legs. The gear configuration causes a nose-up stance to facilitate cargo loading and unloading. The main gear are fitted in rear sponsons that also contain fuel tanks with a total capacity of 350 US gallons (1,438 L).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The CH-46 has a cargo bay with a rear loading ramp that could be removed or left open in flight for extended cargo or for parachute drops. An internal winch is mounted in the forward cabin and can be used to pull external cargo on pallets into the aircraft via the ramp and rollers. A belly sling hook (cargo hook) which is usually rated at 10,000 lb (4,500 kg). could be attached for carrying external cargo. Although the hook is rated at 10,000 lb (4,500 kg)., the limited power produced by the engines precludes the lifting of such weight. It usually has a crew of three, but can accommodate a larger crew depending on mission specifics. For example, a Search and Rescue variant will usually carry a crew of five (Pilot, Co-Pilot, Crew Chief, Swimmer, and Medic) to facilitate all aspects of such a mission. A pintle-mounted 0.50 in (12.7 mm) Browning machine gun is mounted on each side of the helicopter for self-defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vect_CH-46_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-vect_CH-46-7">[7]</a></sup> Service in southeast Asia resulted in the addition of armor with the guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CH-46_hist_USN_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-CH-46_hist_USN-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="239" data-file-width="350" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg/220px-CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg/330px-CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3b/CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CH-46_Shot_down_during_Operation_Hastings.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A flaming Marine CH-46 of HMM-265, after being hit by enemy AAA fire in "Helicopter Valley", 15 July 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Known colloquially as the "Phrog", the Sea Knight was used in all U.S. Marine operational environments between its introduction during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> and its frontline retirement in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The type's longevity and reputation for reliability led to mantras such as "phrogs phorever" and "never trust a helicopter under 30".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> CH-46s transported personnel, evacuated wounded, supplied forward arming and refueling points (FARP), performed vertical replenishment, search and rescue, recovered downed aircraft and crews and other tasks.</p>
<p>During the Vietnam War, the CH-46 was one of the prime US troop transport helicopters in the theatre, slotting between the smaller <a href="/wiki/Bell_UH-1_Iroquois" title="Bell UH-1 Iroquois">Bell UH-1 Iroquois</a> and larger <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_CH-53_Sea_Stallion" title="Sikorsky CH-53 Sea Stallion">Sikorsky CH-53 Sea Stallion</a>. During the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Easter_Offensive" title="Easter Offensive">Easter Offensive</a>, Sea Knights saw heavy use to convey US and <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnamese</a> ground forces to and around the front lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> CH-46 operations were plagued by major technical problems; the engines, being prone to <a href="/wiki/Foreign_object_damage" title="Foreign object damage">foreign object damage</a> (FOD) from debris being ingested when hovering close to the ground and subsequently suffering a <a href="/wiki/Compressor_stall" title="Compressor stall">compressor stall</a>, had a lifespan as low as 85 flight hours; on 21 July 1966, all CH-46s were grounded until more efficient filters had been fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> By the end of US military operations in Vietnam, over a hundred Sea Knights had been lost to enemy fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 1968 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps_Training_and_Education_Command" title="United States Marine Corps Training and Education Command">Marine Corps Development and Education Command</a> obtained several CH-46s to perform herbicide dissemination tests using HIDAL (Helicopter, Insecticide Dispersal Apparatus, Liquid) systems; testing indicated the need for redesign and further study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Tandem-rotor helicopters were often used to transport nuclear warheads; the CH-46A was evaluated to deploy Naval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Forces" title="Special Forces">Special Forces</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Special_Atomic_Demolition_Munition" title="Special Atomic Demolition Munition">Special Atomic Demolition Munition</a> (SADM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Nuclear Weapon Accident Exercise 1983 (NUWAX-83), simulating the crash of a Navy CH-46E carrying 3 nuclear warheads, was conducted at the <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Test_Site" title="Nevada Test Site">Nevada Test Site</a> on behalf of several federal agencies; the exercise, which used real radiological agents, was depicted in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Nuclear_Agency" title="Defense Nuclear Agency">Defense Nuclear Agency</a>-produced documentary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>U.S. Marine CH-46s were used to deploy the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8th_Marine_Regiment" title="8th Marine Regiment">8th Marine Regiment</a> into <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Urgent_Fury" title="Operation Urgent Fury">Operation Urgent Fury</a>, evacuated the surviving crew-member of a downed AH-1 Cobra, and then carried infantry from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/75th_Ranger_Regiment" title="75th Ranger Regiment">75th Ranger Regiment</a> to secure an evacuate U.S. students at the Grand Anse campus of <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_University" title="St. George's University">St. George's University</a>, though one crashed after colliding with a palm tree.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ch-46e.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
U.S. Marines load a simulated casualty onto a CH-46E during convoy operations training in May 2004.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>CH-46E Sea Knights were also used by the U.S. Marine Corps during the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>. In one incident on 1 April 2003, Marine CH-46Es and CH-53Es carried U.S. Army Rangers and Special Operations troops on an extraction mission for captured Army Private <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Lynch" title="Jessica Lynch">Jessica Lynch</a> from an Iraqi hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During the subsequent occupation of Iraq and counter-insurgency operations, the CH-46E was heavily used in the CASEVAC role, being required to maintain 24/7 availability regardless of conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> According to authors Williamson Murray and Robert H Scales, the Sea Knight displayed serious reliability and maintenance problems during its deployment to Iraq, as well as "limited lift capabilities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Following the loss of numerous US helicopters in the Iraqi theatre, the Marines opted to equip their CH-46s with more advanced anti-missile countermeasures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Navy retired the type on 24 September 2004, replacing it with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SH-60_Seahawk" title="SH-60 Seahawk">MH-60S Seahawk</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_Swan_song_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Navy_Swan_song-23">[23]</a></sup> the Marine Corps maintained its fleet as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-22_Osprey" title="V-22 Osprey">MV-22 Osprey</a> was fielded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In March 2006 Marine Medium Helicopter Squadron 263 (HMM-263) was deactivated and redesignated <a href="/wiki/VMM-263" title="VMM-263">VMM-263</a> to serve as the first MV-22 squadron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The replacement process continued through the other medium helicopter squadrons into 2014. On 5 October 2014, the Sea Knight performed its final service flight with the U.S. Marine Corps at <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Air_Station_Miramar" title="Marine Corps Air Station Miramar">Marine Corps Air Station Miramar</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMM-364" title="HMM-364">HMM-364</a> was the last squadron to use it outside the United States, landing it aboard the <a href="/wiki/USS_America_(LHA-6)" title="USS America (LHA-6)">USS America (LHA-6)</a> on her maiden transit. On 9 April 2015, the CH-46 was retired by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Medium_Helicopter_Training_Squadron_164" title="Marine Medium Helicopter Training Squadron 164">Marine Medium Helicopter Training Squadron 164</a>, the last Marine Corps squadron to transition to the MV-22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The USMC retired the CH-46 on 1 August 2015 in a ceremony at the <a href="/wiki/Steven_F._Udvar-Hazy_Center" title="Steven F. Udvar-Hazy Center">Udvar-Hazy Center</a> near Washington DC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Force" title="Royal Canadian Air Force">Royal Canadian Air Force</a> procured six CH-113 Labrador helicopters for the SAR role and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">Canadian Army</a> acquired 12 of the similar <i>CH-113A Voyageur</i> for the medium-lift transport role. The RCAF Labradors were delivered first with the first one entering service on 11 October 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milberry_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Milberry-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAM_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAM-30">[30]</a></sup> When the larger CH-147 Chinook was procured by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian Forces</a> in the mid-1970s, the Voyageur fleet was converted to Labrador specifications to undertake SAR missions. The refurbished Voyageurs were re-designated as CH-113A Labradors, thus a total of 15 Labradors were ultimately in service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAM_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-CAM-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="1600" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg/220px-Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg/330px-Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg/440px-Labrador_helicopter_landing_Bell_Island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

CH-113 Labrador landing on "The Clapper", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_stack" title="Sea stack">sea stack</a> off the tip of <a href="/wiki/Bell_Island_(Newfoundland_and_Labrador)" title="Bell Island (Newfoundland and Labrador)">Bell Island</a> in Newfoundland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Labrador was fitted with a watertight hull for marine landings, a 5,000 kilogram cargo hook and an external rescue hoist mounted over the right front door. It featured a 1,110 kilometer flying range, emergency medical equipment and an 18-person passenger capacity. By the 1990s, heavy use and hostile weather conditions had taken their toll on the Labrador fleet, resulting in increasing maintenance costs and the need for prompt replacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAM_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-CAM-30">[30]</a></sup> In 1981, a mid-life upgrade of the fleet was carried out by <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Canada" title="Boeing Canada">Boeing Canada</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnprior,_Ontario" title="Arnprior, Ontario">Arnprior, Ontario</a>. Known as the SAR-CUP (Search and Rescue Capability Upgrade Program), the refit scheme included new instrumentation, a nose-mounted weather radar, a tail-mounted auxiliary power unit, a new high-speed rescue hoist mounted over the side door and front-mounted searchlights. A total of six CH-113s and five CH-113As were upgraded with the last delivered in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAM_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-CAM-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, it was announced that the Labradors were to be replaced by 15 new helicopters, a variant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_EH101" title="AgustaWestland EH101">AgustaWestland EH101</a>, designated <i>CH-149 Chimo</i>. The order was subsequently cancelled by the <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chr%C3%A9tien" title="Jean Chrétien">Jean Chrétien</a> <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a> government in 1993, resulting in cancellation penalties, as well as extending the service life of the Labrador fleet. However, in 1998, a CH-113 from <a href="/wiki/CFB_Greenwood" title="CFB Greenwood">CFB Greenwood</a> crashed on Quebec's <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gaspé Peninsula">Gaspé Peninsula</a> while returning from a SAR mission, resulting in the deaths of all crewmembers on board. The crash placed pressure upon the government to procure a replacement, thus an order was placed with the manufacturers of the EH101 for 15 aircraft to perform the search-and-rescue mission, designated <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CH-149_Cormorant" title="CH-149 Cormorant">CH-149 Cormorant</a></i>. CH-149 deliveries began in 2003, allowing the last CH-113 to be retired in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAM_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-CAM-30">[30]</a></sup> In October 2005 <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Helicopters" title="Columbia Helicopters">Columbia Helicopters</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Oregon" title="Aurora, Oregon">Aurora, Oregon</a> purchased eight of the retired CH-113 Labradors to add to their fleet of 15 Vertol 107-II helicopters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1963, Sweden procured ten UH-46B from the US as a transport and anti-submarine helicopter for the Swedish armed forces, designated Hkp 4A. In 1973, a further eight Kawasaki-built KV-107, which were accordingly designated Hkp 4B, were acquired to replace the older <a href="/wiki/Piasecki_H-21" title="Piasecki H-21">Piasecki H-21</a>. During the Cold War, the fleet's primary missions were anti-submarine warfare and troop transportation, they were also frequently employed in the search and rescue role. In the 1980s, the Hkp 4A was phased out, having been replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Eurocopter_AS332_Super_Puma" title="Eurocopter AS332 Super Puma">Eurocopter AS332 Super Puma</a>; the later Kawasaki-built Sea Knights continued in operational service until 2011, they were replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UH-60_Black_Hawk" title="UH-60 Black Hawk">UH-60 Black Hawk</a> &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NH90" title="NH90">NH90</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civilian">Civilian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Civilian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P2CHE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1413" data-file-width="2266" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/P2CHE.jpg/220px-P2CHE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/P2CHE.jpg/330px-P2CHE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/P2CHE.jpg/440px-P2CHE.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:P2CHE.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Columbia Helicopters BV 107-II</div>

<p>The civilian version, designated as the BV 107-II <i>Vertol</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert_Columbia_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-vert_Columbia-32">[32]</a></sup> was developed prior to the military CH-46. It was operated commercially by <a href="/wiki/New_York_Airways" title="New York Airways">New York Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American World Airways</a> and later on by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Helicopters" title="Columbia Helicopters">Columbia Helicopters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert_Columbia_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-vert_Columbia-32">[32]</a></sup> Among the diversity of tasks was pulling a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hover_barge" title="Hover barge">hover barge</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and constructing <a href="/wiki/Transmission_tower" title="Transmission tower">transmission towers</a> for <a href="/wiki/Overhead_power_line" title="Overhead power line">overhead power lines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, Columbia Helicopters purchased the <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">type certificate</a> of the Model 107 from Boeing, with the aim of eventually producing new-build aircraft themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_versions">American versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: American versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Model 107 </dt>
<dd>Company model number for basic prototype, one built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helis_BV-107_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Helis_BV-107-37">[37]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Model 107-II </dt>
<dd>Commercial airline helicopter. All subsequent commercial aircraft were produced as BV 107-II-2, two built as Boeing Vertol prototypes, five sold to New York Airways, ten supplied to Kawasaki as sub-assemblies or as parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helis_BV-107II_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Helis_BV-107II-38">[38]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Model 107M </dt>
<dd>Company model number for military transport of BV-107/II-2 for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helis_H-46s_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Helis_H-46s-39">[39]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>YHC-1A </dt>
<dd>Vertol Model 107 for test and evaluation by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>. Adopted by the U.S. Marine Corps as the HRB-1. Later redesignated <b>YCH-46C</b>, three built.</dd>
<dt>HRB-1 </dt>
<dd>Original designation before being renamed as CH-46A before delivery under the <a href="/wiki/1962_United_States_Tri-Service_aircraft_designation_system" title="1962 United States Tri-Service aircraft designation system">1962 United States Tri-Service aircraft designation system</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CH-46A </dt>
<dd>Medium-lift assault and cargo transport and SAR helicopter for the USMC, fitted with two 1,250 shp (935 kW) General Electric T58-GE-8 turboshaft engines. Previously designated HRB-1. 160 built for USMC, one static airframe.</dd>
<dt>UH-46A </dt>
<dd>Medium-lift utility transport helicopter for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>. Similar to the CH-46A. 14 built.</dd>
<dt>HH-46A </dt>
<dd>Approximately 50 CH-46As were converted into SAR helicopters for the United States Navy base rescue role.</dd>
<dt>RH-46A </dt>
<dd>Planned conversion of CH-46As into minesweeping helicopters for the US Navy, none converted. Nine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SH-3_Sea_King#US_military" title="SH-3 Sea King">SH-3As</a> were converted to the RH-3A configuration instead.</dd>
<dt>UH-46B </dt>
<dd>Development of the CH-46A to specification HX/H2 for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>; 12 ordered in 1962, cancelled and Sikorsky S-61R / CH-3C ordered instead.</dd>
<dt>YCH-46C </dt>
<dd>YHC-1A redesignated in 1962. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> retained two, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">NASA</a> used one for vertical autonomous landing trials (VALT).</dd>
</dl>

<dt>CH-46D </dt>
<dd>Medium-lift assault and cargo transport helicopter for the USMC, fitted with two 1,400 shp (1,044 kW) General Electric T58-GE-10 turboshaft engines. 266 built.</dd>
<dt>HH-46D </dt>
<dd>Surviving HH-46A were upgraded and a small number of UH-46Ds were converted into SAR helicopters. SAR upgrades included the addition of an external rescue hoist near the front crew door and an 18-inch X 18-inch Doppler RADAR system located behind the nose landing gear, which provided for automatic, day/night, over-water hovering capability for at sea rescue. Additionally a "Loud Hailer" was installed opposite the crew entrance door for communicating with downed aviators on the ground or in the water.</dd>

<dt>CH-46F </dt>
<dd>Improved version of CH-46D, electrical distribution, com/nav update BUNO 154845-157726. Last production model in the United States. 174 built, later reverted to CH-46E.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <dt>CH-46X </dt>
<dd>Replacement helicopter based on the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Model_360" title="Boeing Model 360">Boeing Model 360</a>, this Advance Technology Demonstrator from the 1980s never entered production. The aircraft relied heavily on composites for its construction and had a beefier drive train to handle the twin Avco-Lycoming AL5512 engines (4,200 shp).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_accidents_and_incidents">Notable accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Notable accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On 15 July 1966 during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hastings" title="Operation Hastings">Operation Hastings</a>, two CH-46As BuNo 151930 and BuNo 151936 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMM-164" title="HMM-164">HMM-164</a> collided at LZ Crow while another, BuNo 151961, crashed into a tree avoiding the first two, resulting in 2 Marines killed. Another CH-46 BuNo 152500 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMM-265" title="HMM-265">HMM-265</a> was shot down at the LZ later that day resulting in 13 Marines killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 4 June 1968 CH-46D BuNo 152533 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMM-165" title="HMM-165">HMM-165</a> was hit by anti-aircraft fire at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landing_Zone_Loon" title="Landing Zone Loon">Landing Zone Loon</a> and crashed killing 13 Marines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 14 March 1969 CH-46D BuNo 154841 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMM-161" title="HMM-161">HMM-161</a> was hit by a <a href="/wiki/RPG-2" title="RPG-2">B-40 rocket</a> as it conducted a resupply and medevac mission at <a href="/wiki/Landing_Zone_Sierra" title="Landing Zone Sierra">Landing Zone Sierra</a>, killing 12 Marines and 1 Navy corpsman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 9 December 1999, a CH-46D Sea Knight BuNo 154790 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMM-166" title="HMM-166">HMM-166</a> crashed during a boarding exercise off the coast of San Diego, California, killing seven U.S. Marines. The pilot landed the CH-46 short on the deck of the <a href="/wiki/USNS_Pecos_(T-AO-197)" title="USNS Pecos (T-AO-197)">USNS <i>Pecos</i></a>, causing the left rear tire and strut to become entangled in the safety netting at the back of the ship, which caused it to plunge into the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_.28CH-46E.29">Specifications (CH-46E)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_Vertol_CH-46_Sea_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Specifications (CH-46E)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg" title="Orthographically projected diagram of the CH-46 Sea Knight"><img alt="Orthographically projected diagram of the CH-46 Sea Knight" data-file-height="389" data-file-width="615" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg/350px-CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg/525px-CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg/700px-CH-46_Line_Drawing.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a></div>
<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i> Frawley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Donald<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donald_V-107_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donald_V-107-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> five: two pilots, one crew chief, one aerial gunner/observer, one tail gunner</li>
<li><b>Capacity:</b> ** 24 troops <i>or</i>
<ul>
<li>15 stretchers and two attendants <i>or</i></li>
<li>2270 kg (5,000 lb)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 44 ft 10 in fuselage (13.66 m</li>
<li><b>Fuselage width:</b> 7 ft 3 in (2.2 m)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>)</li>
<li><b>Rotor diameter:</b> 50 ft (15.24 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 16 ft 9 in (5.09 m)</li>
<li><b>Disc area:</b> 3,927 ft² (364.8 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 11,585 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a> (5,255 kg)</li>
<li><b>Loaded weight:</b> 17,396 lb (7,891 kg)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 24,300 lb (11,000 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 2 × <a href="/wiki/GE_Aviation" title="GE Aviation">General Electric</a> <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_T58" title="General Electric T58">T58-GE-16</a> <a href="/wiki/Turboshaft" title="Turboshaft">turboshafts</a>, 1,870 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">shp</a> (1,400 kW) each</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 166 mph (144 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a>, 267 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 633 mi (550 nmi, 1,020 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Ferry range</a>:</b> 690 mi (600 nmi, 1,110 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 17,000 ft (5,180 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 1,715 ft/min (8.71 m/s)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Disk_loading" title="Disk loading">Disc loading</a>:</b> 4.43 lb/ft² (21.6 kg/m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">Power/mass</a>:</b> 0.215 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a>/lb (354 W/kg)</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Guns:</b> Two door-mounted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Browning_Machine_Gun#XM213.2FM213.2C_XM218.2C_GAU-15.2FA.2C_GAU-16.2FA.2C_and_GAU-18.2FA" title="M2 Browning Machine Gun">GAU-15/A</a> <a href="/wiki/.50_BMG" title=".50 BMG">.50 BMG</a> (12.7×99 mm) machine guns (optional), one ramp-mounted <a href="/wiki/M240_machine_gun#M240E1_and_M240D" title="M240 machine gun">M240D</a> <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51 mm</a> machine gun (optional)</li>
</ul>
