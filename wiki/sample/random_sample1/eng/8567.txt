<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Signal integrity</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Signal integrity</b> or <b>SI</b> is a set of measures of the quality of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_signal" title="Electrical signal">electrical signal</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Digital_electronics" title="Digital electronics">digital electronics</a>, a stream of binary values is represented by a voltage (or current) waveform. However, digital signals are fundamentally <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">analog</a> in nature, and all signals are subject to effects such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_noise" title="Electrical noise">noise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distortion" title="Distortion">distortion</a>, and loss. Over short distances and at low bit rates, a simple conductor can transmit this with sufficient fidelity. At high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bit_rates" title="Bit rates">bit rates</a> and over longer distances or through various mediums, various effects can degrade the electrical signal to the point where errors occur and the system or device fails. Signal integrity engineering is the task of analyzing and mitigating these effects. It is an important activity at all levels of electronics packaging and assembly, from internal connections of an <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a> (IC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kariat_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kariat-1">[1]</a></sup> through the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit_packaging" title="Integrated circuit packaging">package</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit board</a> (PCB), the <a href="/wiki/Backplane" title="Backplane">backplane</a>, and inter-system connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While there are some common themes at these various levels, there are also practical considerations, in particular the interconnect flight time versus the bit period, that cause substantial differences in the approach to signal integrity for on-chip connections versus chip-to-chip connections.</p>
<p>Some of the main issues of concern for signal integrity are <a href="/wiki/Ringing_(signal)" title="Ringing (signal)">ringing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crosstalk" title="Crosstalk">crosstalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_bounce" title="Ground bounce">ground bounce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distortion" title="Distortion">distortion</a>, signal loss, and <a href="/wiki/Power_supply" title="Power supply">power supply</a> noise.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Signal integrity primarily involves the electrical performance of the wires and other packaging structures used to move signals about within an electronic product. Such performance is a matter of basic physics and as such has remained relatively unchanged since the inception of electronic signaling. The first <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">transatlantic telegraph cable</a> suffered from <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable#Communication_speeds" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">severe signal integrity problems</a>, and analysis of the problems yielded many of the mathematical tools still used today to analyze signal integrity problems, such as the <a href="/wiki/Telegrapher%27s_equations" title="Telegrapher's equations">telegrapher's equations</a>. Products as old as the Western Electric <a href="/wiki/Crossbar_switch#Electromechanical_.2F_telephony" title="Crossbar switch">crossbar telephone exchange</a> (circa 1940), based on the wire-spring relay, suffered almost all the effects seen today - the ringing, crosstalk, ground bounce, and power supply noise that plague modern digital products.</p>
<p>On printed circuit boards, signal integrity became a serious concern when the transition (rise and fall) times of signals started to become comparable to the propagation time across the board. Very roughly speaking, this typically happens when system speeds exceed a few tens of MHz. At first, only a few of the most important, or highest speed, signals needed detailed analysis or design. As speeds increased, a larger and larger fraction of signals needed SI analysis and design practices. In modern (&gt; 100 MHz) circuit designs, essentially all signals must be designed with SI in mind.</p>
<p>For ICs, SI analysis became necessary as an effect of reduced design rules. In the early days of the modern VLSI era, digital chip circuit design and layout were manual processes. The use of abstraction and the application of <a href="/wiki/Logic_synthesis" title="Logic synthesis">automatic synthesis techniques</a> have since allowed designers to express their designs using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Register_transfer_level" title="Register transfer level">high-level languages</a> and apply an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_flows_(EDA)" title="Design flows (EDA)">automated design process</a> to create very complex designs, ignoring the electrical characteristics of the underlying circuits to a large degree. However, scaling trends (see <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a>) brought electrical effects back to the forefront in recent technology nodes. With scaling of technology below 0.25 µm, the wire delays have become comparable or even greater than the gate delays. As a result, the wire delays needed to be considered to achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timing_Closure" title="Timing Closure">timing closure</a>. In nanometer technologies at 0.13 µm and below, unintended interactions between signals (e.g. crosstalk) became an important consideration for digital design. At these technology nodes, the performance and correctness of a design cannot be assured without considering noise effects.</p>
<p>Most of this article is about SI in relation to modern electronic technology - notably the use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printed_circuit" title="Printed circuit">printed circuit</a> board technology. Nevertheless, the principles of SI are not exclusive to the signalling technology used. SI existed long before the advent of either technology, and will do so as long as electronic communications persist.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="On-chip_signal_integrity">On-chip signal integrity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: On-chip signal integrity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Signal integrity problems in modern integrated circuits (ICs) can have many drastic consequences for digital designs:</p>
<ul>
<li>Products can fail to operate at all, or worse yet, become unreliable in the field.</li>
<li>The design may work, but only at speeds slower than planned</li>
<li>Yield may be lowered, sometimes drastically</li>
</ul>
<p>The cost of these failures is very high, and includes <a href="/wiki/Photomask" title="Photomask">photomask</a> costs, engineering costs and <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a> due to delayed product introduction. Therefore, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_design_automation" title="Electronic design automation">electronic design automation</a> (EDA) tools have been developed to analyze, prevent, and correct these problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kariat_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kariat-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, or ICs, the main cause of signal integrity problems is <a href="/wiki/Crosstalk" title="Crosstalk">crosstalk</a>. In <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> technologies, this is primarily due to coupling <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a>, but in general it may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Inductance#Mutual_inductance" title="Inductance">mutual inductance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Substrate_coupling" title="Substrate coupling">substrate coupling</a>, non-ideal gate operation, and other sources. The fixes normally involve changing the sizes of drivers and/or spacing of wires.</p>
<p>In analog circuits, designers are also concerned with noise that arise from physical sources, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johnson-Nyquist_noise" title="Johnson-Nyquist noise">thermal noise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flicker_noise" title="Flicker noise">flicker noise</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shot_noise" title="Shot noise">shot noise</a>. These noise sources on the one hand present a lower limit to the smallest signal that can be amplified, and on the other, define an upper limit to the useful amplification.</p>
<p>In digital ICs, noise in a signal of interest arises primarily from coupling effects from switching of other signals. Increasing interconnect density has led to each wire having neighbors that are physically closer together, leading to increased crosstalk between neighboring nets. As circuits have continued to shrink in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a>, several effects have conspired to make noise problems worse:</p>
<ul>
<li>To keep resistance tolerable despite decreased width, modern wire geometries are thicker in proportion to their spacing. This increases the sidewall capacitance at the expense of capacitance to ground, hence increasing the induced noise voltage (expressed as a fraction of supply voltage).</li>
<li>Technology scaling has led to lower <a href="/wiki/Threshold_voltage" title="Threshold voltage">threshold voltages</a> for MOS transistors, and has also reduced the difference between threshold and supply voltages, thereby reducing <a href="/wiki/Noise_margin" title="Noise margin">noise margins</a>.</li>
<li>Logic speeds, and clock speeds in particular, have increased significantly, thus leading to faster transition (rise and fall) times. These faster transition times are closely linked to higher capacitive crosstalk. Also, at such high speeds the inductive properties of the wires come into play, especially mutual inductance.</li>
</ul>
<p>These effects have increased the interactions between signals and decreased the noise immunity of digital CMOS circuits. This has led to noise being a significant problem for digital ICs that must be considered by every digital chip designer prior to <a href="/wiki/Tape-out" title="Tape-out">tape-out</a>. There are several concerns that must be mitigated:</p>
<ul>
<li>Noise may cause a signal to assume the wrong value. This is particularly critical when the signal is about to be latched (or sampled), for a wrong value could be loaded into a storage element, causing logic failure.</li>
<li>Noise may delay the settling of the signal to the correct value. This is often called <i>noise-on-delay</i>.</li>
<li>Noise (e.g. ringing) may cause the input voltage of a gate to drop below ground level, or to exceed the supply voltage. This can reduce the lifetime of the device by stressing components, induce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latchup" title="Latchup">latchup</a>, or cause multiple cycling of signals that should only cycle once in a given period.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finding_IC_signal_integrity_problems">Finding IC signal integrity problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Finding IC signal integrity problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Typically, an IC designer would take the following steps for SI verification:</p>
<ul>
<li>Perform a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Layout_extraction" title="Layout extraction">layout extraction</a> to get the <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_element_(electrical_networks)" title="Parasitic element (electrical networks)">parasitics</a> associated with the layout. Usually worst-case parasitics and best-case parasitics are extracted and used in the simulations. For ICs, unlike PCBs, physical measurement of the parasitics is almost never done, since in-situ measurements with external equipment are extremely difficult. Furthermore, any measurement would occur after the chip has been created, which is too late to fix any problems observed.</li>
<li>Create a list of expected noise events, including different types of noise, such as coupling and <a href="/wiki/Charge_sharing" title="Charge sharing">charge sharing</a>.</li>
<li>Create a model for each noise event. It is critical that the model be as accurate as possible.</li>
<li>For each signal event, decide how to excite the circuit so that the noise event will occur.</li>
<li>Create a <a href="/wiki/SPICE" title="SPICE">SPICE</a> (or another circuit simulator) <a href="/wiki/Netlist" title="Netlist">netlist</a> that represents the desired excitation, to include as many effects (such as parasitic <a href="/wiki/Inductance" title="Inductance">inductance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a>, and various distortion effects) as possible.</li>
<li>Run SPICE simulations. Analyze the simulation results and decide whether any re-design is required. It is common to analyze the results with an <a href="/wiki/Eye_pattern" title="Eye pattern">eye pattern</a> and by calculating a timing budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Modern signal integrity tools for IC design perform all these steps automatically, producing reports that give a design a clean bill of health, or a list of problems that must be fixed. However, such tools generally are not applied across an entire IC, but only selected signals of interest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixing_IC_signal_integrity_problems">Fixing IC signal integrity problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fixing IC signal integrity problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once a problem is found, it must be fixed. Typical fixes for IC on-chip problems include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Removing impedance discontinuities. Finding places where significant shifts in the impedance exist and adjusting the geometry of the path to shift the impedance to better match the rest of the path.</li>
<li>Driver optimization. You can have too much drive, and also not enough.</li>
<li>Buffer insertion. In this approach, instead of upsizing the victim driver, a buffer is inserted at an appropriate point in the victim net.</li>
<li>Aggressor downsizing. This works by increasing the transition time of the attacking net by reducing the strength of its driver.</li>
<li>Add shielding. Add shielding of critical nets or clock nets using GND and VDD shields to reduce the effect of crosstalk (this technique may lead to routing overhead).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Routing_(EDA)" title="Routing (EDA)">Routing</a> changes. Routing changes can be very effective in fixing noise problems, mainly by reducing the most troublesome coupling effects via separation.</li>
</ul>
<p>Each of these fixes may possibly cause other problems. This type of issue must be addressed as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_flows_(EDA)" title="Design flows (EDA)">design flows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Design_closure" title="Design closure">design closure</a>. Re-analysis after design changes is a prudent measure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="On-die_termination">On-die termination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: On-die termination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/On-die_termination" title="On-die termination">On-die termination</a> or ODT is the technology where the termination resistor for impedance matching in transmission lines is located within a semiconductor chip, instead of a separate, discrete device mounted on a circuit board.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chip-to-chip_signal_integrity">Chip-to-chip signal integrity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chip-to-chip signal integrity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For wired connections, it is important to compare the interconnect flight time to the bit period to decide whether an impedance matched or unmatched connection is needed.</p>
<p>The channel flight time (delay) of the interconnect is roughly <span class="nowrap">1 ns</span> per <span class="nowrap">15 cm</span> (<span class="nowrap">6 in</span>) of <a href="/wiki/FR-4" title="FR-4">FR-4</a> <a href="/wiki/Stripline" title="Stripline">stripline</a> (the propagation velocity depends on the dielectric and the geometry). Reflections of previous pulses at impedance mismatches die down after a few bounces up and down the line (i.e. on the order of the flight time). At low bit rates, the echoes die down on their own, and by midpulse, they are not a concern. Impedance matching is neither necessary nor desirable. There are many circuit board types other than FR-4, but usually they are more costly to manufacture.</p>
<p>The gentle trend to higher bit rates accelerated dramatically in 2004, with the introduction by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCI-Express" title="PCI-Express">PCI-Express</a> standard. Following this lead, the majority of chip-to-chip connection standards underwent an architectural shift from parallel buses to serializer/deserializer (<a href="/wiki/SerDes" title="SerDes">SERDES</a>) links called "lanes." Such serial links eliminate parallel bus clock skew and reduce the number of traces and resultant coupling effects but these advantages come at the cost of a large increase in bit rate on the lanes, and shorter bit periods.</p>
<p>At multigigabit/s data rates, link designers must consider reflections at impedance changes (e.g. where traces change levels at <a href="/wiki/Via_(electronics)" title="Via (electronics)">vias</a>), noise induced by densely packed neighboring connections (<a href="/wiki/Crosstalk" title="Crosstalk">crosstalk</a>), and high-frequency attenuation caused by the <a href="/wiki/Skin_effect" title="Skin effect">skin effect</a> in the metal trace and dielectric loss tangent. Examples of mitigation techniques for these impairments are a redesign of the via geometry to ensure an impedance match, use of <a href="/wiki/Differential_signaling" title="Differential signaling">differential signaling</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preemphasis" title="Preemphasis">preemphasis</a> filtering, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At these new multigigabit/s bit rates, the bit period is shorter than the flight time; echoes of previous pulses can arrive at the receiver on top of the main pulse and corrupt it. In communication engineering this is called intersymbol interference (ISI). In signal integrity engineering it is usually called eye closure (a reference to the clutter in the center of a type of oscilloscope trace called an eye diagram). When the bit period is shorter than the flight time, elimination of reflections using classic microwave techniques like matching the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_impedance" title="Electrical impedance">electrical impedance</a> of the transmitter to the interconnect, the sections of interconnect to each other, and the interconnect to the receiver, is crucial. <a href="/wiki/Electrical_termination" title="Electrical termination">Termination with a source or load</a> is a synonym for matching at the two ends. The interconnect impedance that can be selected is constrained by the impedance of free space (<span class="nowrap">~377 Ω</span>), a geometric form factor and by the square root of the relative dielectric constant of the stripline filler (typically FR-4, with a relative dielectric constant of ~4). Together, these properties determine the trace's <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_impedance" title="Characteristic impedance">characteristic impedance</a>. <span class="nowrap">50 Ω</span> is a convenient choice for single-end lines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <span class="nowrap">100 ohm</span> for differential.</p>
<p>As a consequence of the low impedance required by matching, PCB signal traces carry much more current than their on-chip counterparts. This larger current induces crosstalk primarily in a magnetic or inductive mode as opposed to a capacitive mode. To combat this crosstalk, digital PCB designers must remain acutely aware of not only the intended signal path for every signal, but also the path of returning signal current for every signal. The signal itself and its returning signal current path are equally capable of generating inductive crosstalk. Differential trace pairs help to reduce these effects.</p>
<p>A third difference between on-chip and chip-to-chip connection involves the cross-sectional size of the signal conductor, namely that PCB conductors are much larger (typically <span class="nowrap">100 µm</span> or more in width). Thus, PCB traces have a small series <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">resistance</a> (typically 0.1 Ω/cm) at DC. The high frequency component of the pulse is however attenuated by additional resistance due to the skin effect and dielectric loss tangent associated with the PCB material.</p>
<p>The main challenge often depends on whether the project is a cost-driven consumer application or a performance-driven infrastructure application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They tend to require extensive post-layout verification (using an <a href="/wiki/Computational_electromagnetics" title="Computational electromagnetics">EM simulator</a>) and pre-layout design optimization (using <a href="/wiki/SPICE" title="SPICE">SPICE</a> and a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Channel_simulator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Channel simulator (page does not exist)">channel simulator</a>), respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finding_signal_integrity_problems">Finding signal integrity problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Finding signal integrity problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Perform a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Layout_extraction" title="Layout extraction">layout extraction</a> to get the parasitics associated with the layout. Usually worst-case parasitics and best-case parasitics are extracted and used in the simulations. Because of the distributed nature of many of the impairments, electromagnetic simulation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is used for extraction.</li>
<li>If the PCB or package already exists, the designer can also measure the impairment presented by the connection using high speed instrumentation such as a <a href="/wiki/Network_analyzer_(electrical)" title="Network analyzer (electrical)">vector network analyzer</a>. For example, IEEE P802.3ap Task Force uses measured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-parameters" title="S-parameters">S-parameters</a> as test cases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> for proposed solutions to the problem of <span class="nowrap">10 Gbit/s</span> <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> over backplanes.</li>
<li>Accurate noise modeling is a must. Create a list of expected noise events, including different types of noise, such as coupling and <a href="/wiki/Charge_sharing" title="Charge sharing">charge sharing</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Input_Output_Buffer_Information_Specification" title="Input Output Buffer Information Specification">Input Output Buffer Information Specification</a> (IBIS) or circuit models may be used to represent drivers and receivers.</li>
<li>For each noise event, decide how to excite the circuit so that the noise event will occur.</li>
<li>Create a <a href="/wiki/SPICE" title="SPICE">SPICE</a> (or another circuit simulator) <a href="/wiki/Netlist" title="Netlist">netlist</a> that represents the desired excitation.</li>
<li>Run SPICE and record the results.</li>
<li>Analyze the simulation results and decide whether any re-design is required. To analyze the results quite often a data eye is generated and a timing budget is calculated. An example video for generating a data eye can be found on YouTube: <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=my7CI84le5g" rel="nofollow">An Eye is Born</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are special purpose <a href="/wiki/Electronic_design_automation" title="Electronic design automation">EDA</a> tools<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> that help the engineer perform all these steps on each signal in a design, pointing out problems or verifying the design is ready for manufacture. In selecting which tool is best for a particular task, one must consider characteristics of each such as capacity (how many nodes or elements), performance (simulation speed), accuracy (how good are the models), convergence (how good is the solver), capability (non-linear versus linear, frequency dependent versus frequency independent etc.), and ease of use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixing_signal_integrity_problems">Fixing signal integrity problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_integrity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fixing signal integrity problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An IC package or PCB designer removes signal integrity problems through these techniques:</p>
<ul>
<li>Placing a solid <a href="/wiki/Ground_plane" title="Ground plane">reference plane</a> adjacent to the signal traces to control crosstalk</li>
<li>Controlling the trace width spacing to the reference plane to create consistent trace <a href="/wiki/Electrical_impedance" title="Electrical impedance">impedance</a></li>
<li>Using <a href="/wiki/Electrical_termination" title="Electrical termination">terminations</a> to control <a href="/wiki/Ringing_(signal)" title="Ringing (signal)">ringing</a></li>
<li>Route traces perpendicular on adjacent layers to reduce crosstalk</li>
<li>Increasing spacing between traces to reduce crosstalk</li>
<li>Providing sufficient ground (and power) connections to limit <a href="/wiki/Ground_bounce" title="Ground bounce">ground bounce</a> (this subdiscipline of signal integrity is sometimes called out separately as <a href="/wiki/Power_integrity" title="Power integrity">power integrity</a>)</li>
<li>Distributing power with solid plane layers to limit power supply noise</li>
<li>Adding a preemphasis <a href="/wiki/Filter_(signal_processing)" title="Filter (signal processing)">filter</a> to the transmitter driving cell<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stratix_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stratix-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Adding an <a href="/wiki/Equalization_(communications)" title="Equalization (communications)">equalizer</a> to the receiving cell<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stratix_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stratix-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Improved <a href="/wiki/Clock_recovery" title="Clock recovery">clock and data recovery</a> (CDR) circuitry with low jitter/phase noise<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Each of these fixes may possibly cause other problems. This type of issue must be addressed as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_flows_(EDA)" title="Design flows (EDA)">design flows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Design_closure" title="Design closure">design closure</a>.</p>
