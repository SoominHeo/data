<p><b>Mythology</b> refers variously to the collected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myths" title="Myths">myths</a> of a group of people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or to the study of such myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk19738_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk19738-2">[2]</a></sup> Myths are the <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">stories</a> people tell to explain nature, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norm_(social)" title="Norm (social)">customs</a>.</p>
<p>Myth is a feature of every <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>. Many sources for myths have been proposed, ranging from personification of nature or <a href="/wiki/Mythopoeic_thought" title="Mythopoeic thought">personification of natural phenomena</a>, to <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">truthful</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperbolic</a> accounts of <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">historical events</a> to <a href="/wiki/Myth_and_ritual" title="Myth and ritual">explanations of existing ritual</a>s. Mythologizing continues, as shown in contemporary <a href="/wiki/Mythopoeia" title="Mythopoeia">mythopoeia</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legends</a> and the expansive <a href="/wiki/Canon_(fiction)" title="Canon (fiction)">fictional mythoi</a> created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fantasy_(genre)" title="Fantasy (genre)">fantasy novels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">comics</a>. A culture's collective mythology helps convey <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-group" title="In-group">belonging</a>, shared and religious experiences, behavioral models and <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">moral and practical lessons</a>.</p>
<p>The study of myth began in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient history</a>. Rival classes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_myths" title="Greek myths">Greek myths</a> by <a href="/wiki/Euhemerus" title="Euhemerus">Euhemerus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sallustius" title="Sallustius">Sallustius</a> were developed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoplatonists" title="Neoplatonists">Neoplatonists</a> and later revived by <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythographers" title="Mythographers">mythographers</a>. The nineteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">comparative mythology</a> reinterpreted myth as a primitive and failed counterpart of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._B._Tylor" title="E. B. Tylor">Tylor</a>), a "disease of language" (<a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max Müller">Müller</a>), or a misinterpretation of <a href="/wiki/Magic_and_religion" title="Magic and religion">magical</a> <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Frazer" title="James Frazer">Frazer</a>).</p>
<p>Recent approaches often view myths as manifestations of psychological, cultural, or societal truths, rather than as inaccurate historical accounts.</p>

<p>The term <i>mythology</i> predates the word <i>myth</i> by centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It first appeared in the fifteenth-century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> term <i>mythologie</i>. The word <i>mythology</i>, ("exposition of myths"), comes from <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> <i>mythologie</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>mythologia</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> μυθολογία <i>mythología</i> ("legendary lore, a telling of mythic legends; a legend, story, tale") from μῦθος <i>mythos</i> ("myth") and -λογία <i>-logia</i> ("study").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-9">[9]</a></sup> Both terms translated the subject of Latin author <a href="/wiki/Fabius_Planciades_Fulgentius" title="Fabius Planciades Fulgentius">Fulgentius</a>' fifth-century <i>Mythologiæ</i>, which was concerned with the explication of Greek and Roman stories about their gods, commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Classical_mythology" title="Classical mythology">classical mythology</a>. Although Fulgentius' conflation with the contemporary African <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabius_Claudius_Gordianus_Fulgentius" title="Fabius Claudius Gordianus Fulgentius">Saint Fulgentius</a> is now questioned,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the <i>Mythologiæ</i> explicitly treated its subject matter as <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegories</a> requiring interpretation and not as true events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word <i>mythología</i> [<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μυθολογία</span>] appears in <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, but was used as a general term for "fiction" or "story-telling" of any kind,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-9">[9]</a></sup> combining <i>mỹthos</i> [<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μῦθος</span>, "narrative, fiction"] and <i>-logía</i> [<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">-λογία</span>, "discourse, able to speak about"].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedlogy_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-oedlogy-12">[12]</a></sup> From <a href="/wiki/John_Lydgate" title="John Lydgate">Lydgate</a> until the seventeenth or eighteenth-century, <i>mythology</i> was similarly used to mean a <a href="/wiki/Moral" title="Moral">moral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Parable" title="Parable">parable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-9">[9]</a></sup> From its earliest use in reference to a collection of traditional stories or beliefs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> mythology implied the falsehood of the stories being described. It came to be applied by analogy with similar bodies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_stories" title="Traditional stories">traditional stories</a> among other <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">polytheistic</a> cultures around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-9">[9]</a></sup> The Greek loanword <i>mythos</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (pl. <i>mythoi</i>) and Latinate <i>mythus</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> (pl. <i>mythi</i>) both appeared in English before the first example of <i>myth</i> in 1830.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>In present use, <i>mythology</i> usually refers to the collected myths of a group of people, but may also mean the study of such myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk19738_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk19738-2">[2]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hittite_mythology" title="Hittite mythology">Hittite mythology</a> all describe the body of myths retold among those cultures. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Dundes" title="Alan Dundes">Dundes</a> defined myth as a <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">sacred</a> <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a> that explains how the world and humanity evolved into their present form. Dundes classified a sacred narrative as "a story that serves to define the fundamental worldview of a culture by explaining aspects of the natural world and delineating the psychological and social practices and ideals of a society".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grassie_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-grassie-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lincoln" title="Bruce Lincoln">Lincoln</a> defined <i>myth</i> as "ideology in narrative form."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Scholars in other fields use the term <i>myth</i> in varied ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDundes1984147_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDundes1984147-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoty200411.E2.80.9312_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoty200411.E2.80.9312-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20155_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20155-26">[26]</a></sup> In a broad sense, the word can refer to any <a href="/wiki/Traditional_story" title="Traditional story">traditional story</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk198457_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk198457-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk197374_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk197374-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApollodorus19763_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApollodorus19763-29">[29]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular_misconception" title="Popular misconception">popular misconception</a> or <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">imaginary</a> entity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Due to this pejorative sense, some scholars opted for the term <i>mythos.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grassie_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-grassie-22">[22]</a></sup> Its use was similarly pejorative and now more commonly refers to its <a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Aristotelian</a> sense as a "plot point" or to a collective mythology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmthos_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-oedmthos-31">[31]</a></sup> as in the <a href="/wiki/Worldbuilding" title="Worldbuilding">world building</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.P._Lovecraft" title="H.P. Lovecraft">H.P. Lovecraft</a>.</p>
<p>The term is often distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Didacticism" title="Didacticism">didactic</a> literature such as fables, but its relationship with other traditional stories, such as <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folktales</a>, is more nebulous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19657_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19657-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Main characters in myths are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demigod" title="Demigod">demigods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> humans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> while legends generally feature humans as their main characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup> However, many exceptions or combinations exist, as in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk197322.2C_32_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk197322.2C_32-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk198455_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk198455-40">[40]</a></sup> Myths are often endorsed by rulers and priests and are closely linked to religion or spirituality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup> In fact, many societies group their myths, legends and history together, considering myths to be true accounts of their remote past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199823_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199823-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni1984102_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni1984102-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">Creation myths</a> particularly, take place in a primordial age when the world had not achieved its later form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDundes19841_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDundes19841-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade19986_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade19986-44">[44]</a></sup> Other myths explain how a society's <a href="/wiki/Norm_(social)" title="Norm (social)">customs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboos</a> were established and sanctified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade19986_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade19986-44">[44]</a></sup> A separate space is created for folktales,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom196517_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom196517-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199810.E2.80.9311_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199810.E2.80.9311-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni198499.E2.80.93101_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni198499.E2.80.93101-47">[47]</a></sup> which are not considered true by anyone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-36">[36]</a></sup> As stories spread to other cultures or as faiths change, myths can come to be considered folktales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoty2004114_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoty2004114-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom196513_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom196513-48">[48]</a></sup> Its divine characters are recast as either as humans or demihumans such as <a href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">giants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elf" title="Elf">elves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faerie" title="Faerie">faeries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euhemerism">Euhemerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Euhemerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">Euhemerism</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a></div>
<p>One theory claims that myths are distorted accounts of historical events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-50">[50]</a></sup> According to this theory, storytellers repeatedly elaborate upon historical accounts until the figures in those accounts gain the status of gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-50">[50]</a></sup> For example the myth of the wind-god <a href="/wiki/Aeolus" title="Aeolus">Aeolus</a> may have evolved from a historical account of a king who taught his people to use sails and interpret the winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> (fifth-century BC) and <a href="/wiki/Prodicus" title="Prodicus">Prodicus</a> made claims of this kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-50">[50]</a></sup> This theory is named <i>euhemerism</i> after mythologist <a href="/wiki/Euhemerus" title="Euhemerus">Euhemerus</a> (c.320 BC), who suggested that Greek gods developed from legends about human beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_50-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegory">Allegory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Allegory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some theories propose that myths began as allegories for natural phenomena: <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> represents the sun, <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> represents water, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_50-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-50">[50]</a></sup> According to another theory, myths began as allegories for philosophical or spiritual concepts: <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> represents wise judgment, <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a> desire, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_50-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max Müller">Müller</a> supported an allegorical theory of myth. He believed myths began as allegorical descriptions of nature and gradually came to be interpreted literally. For example, a poetic description of the sea as "raging" was eventually taken literally and the sea was then thought of as a raging god.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201520_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201520-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personification">Personification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mythopoeic_thought" title="Mythopoeic thought">Mythopoeic thought</a></div>
<p>Some thinkers claimed that myths result from the <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">personification</a> of inanimate objects and forces. According to these thinkers, the ancients worshiped natural phenomena, such as fire and air, gradually deifying them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004195_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004195-53">[53]</a></sup> For example, according to this theory, ancients tended to view things as gods, not as mere objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen20134_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen20134-54">[54]</a></sup> Thus, they described natural events as acts of personal gods, giving rise to myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen201315_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen201315-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth-ritual_theory">Myth-ritual theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Myth-ritual theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Myth_and_ritual" title="Myth and ritual">Myth and ritual</a></div>
<p>According to the myth-ritual theory, myth is tied to ritual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201561_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201561-56">[56]</a></sup> In its most extreme form, this theory claims myths arose to explain rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraf199640_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraf199640-57">[57]</a></sup> This claim was first put forward by <a href="/wiki/William_Robertson_Smith" title="William Robertson Smith">Smith</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeletinsky201419.E2.80.9320_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeletinsky201419.E2.80.9320-58">[58]</a></sup> who claimed that people begin performing rituals for reasons not related to myth. Forgetting the original reason for a ritual, they account for it by inventing a myth and claiming the ritual commemorates the events described in that myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201563_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201563-59">[59]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Frazer" title="James Frazer">Frazer</a> claimed that humans started out with a belief in magical laws. Later, they began to lose faith in magic and invented myths about gods, claiming that their rituals were religious rituals intended to appease the gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg/250px-Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg/375px-Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg/500px-Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Holy_Grail_digital_art.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> digital art part of <a href="/wiki/Christian_mythology" title="Christian mythology">Christian mythology</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mircea_Eliade" title="Mircea Eliade">Eliade</a> argued that one of the foremost functions of myth is to establish models for behavior<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade19988_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade19988-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198451_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198451-62">[62]</a></sup> and that myths may provide a religious experience. By telling or reenacting myths, members of traditional societies detach themselves from the present, returning to the mythical age, thereby coming closer to the divine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199823_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199823-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198451_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198451-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199819_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199819-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lauri_Honko" title="Lauri Honko">Honko</a> asserted that, in some cases, a society reenacts a myth in an attempt to reproduce the conditions of the mythical age. For example, it might reenact the healing performed by a god at the beginning of time in order to heal someone in the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198449_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198449-64">[64]</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Barthes" title="Roland Barthes">Barthes</a> argued that modern culture explores religious experience. Since it is not the job of science to define human morality, a religious experience is an attempt to connect with a perceived moral past, which is in contrast with the technological present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarthes1972_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarthes1972-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Campbell" title="Joseph Campbell">Campbell</a> writes:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"In the long view of the history of mankind, four essential functions of mythology can be discerned. The first and most distinctive – vitalizing all – is that of eliciting and supporting a sense of awe before the mystery of being."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell1991519_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell1991519-66">[66]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>"The second function of mythology is to render a cosmology, an image of the universe that will support and be supported by this sense of awe before the mystery of the presence and the presence of a mystery."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell1991519_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell1991519-66">[66]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>"A third function of mythology is to support the current social order, to integrate the individual organically with his group;"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell1991520_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell1991520-67">[67]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>"The fourth function of mythology is to initiate the individual into the order of realities of his own psyche, guiding him toward his own spiritual enrichment and realization."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell1991521_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell1991521-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a later work Campbell explained the relationship of myth to civilization:</p>
<dl>
<dd>The rise and fall of civilisations in the long, broad course of history can be seen largely to be a function of the integrity and cogency of their supporting canons of myth; for not authority but aspiration is the motivator, builder, and transformer of civilisation. A mythological canon is an organisation of symbols, ineffable in import, by which the energies of aspiration are evoked and gathered toward a focus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell19915_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell19915-69">[69]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Yet the history of civilization is not one of harmony.</p>
<dl>
<dd>There are two pathologies. One is interpreting myth as pseudo-science, as though it had to do with directing nature instead of putting oneself in accord with nature, and the other is the political interpretation of myths to the advantage of one group within a society, or one society within a group of nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Campbell answers the question, "<i>what is the function of myth today</i>?" in the second episode of <a href="/wiki/Bill_Moyers" title="Bill Moyers">Bill Moyers</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Power_of_Myth" title="The Power of Myth">The Power of Myth</a></i> series.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Devdutt_Pattanaik" title="Devdutt Pattanaik">Pattanaik</a> defines mythology as "a subjective truth of people that is communicated through stories, symbols and rituals". He adds, "unlike fantasy that is nobody’s truth, and history that seeks to be everybody’s truth, mythology is somebody’s truth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PD2016_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-PD2016-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_academic_discipline">History of the academic discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History of the academic discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_(1916)_(14801987593).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1240" data-file-width="712" height="383" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_%281916%29_%2814801987593%29.jpg/220px-Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_%281916%29_%2814801987593%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_%281916%29_%2814801987593%29.jpg/330px-Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_%281916%29_%2814801987593%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_%281916%29_%2814801987593%29.jpg/440px-Myths_and_legends_of_Babylonia_and_Assyria_%281916%29_%2814801987593%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Historically, the important approaches to the study of mythology have been those of <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Vico" title="Giambattista Vico">Vico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Joseph_Schelling" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling">Schelling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schiller" title="Schiller">Schiller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Jung</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freud" title="Freud">Freud</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9vy-Bruhl" title="Lévy-Bruhl">Lévy-Bruhl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Lévi-Strauss">Lévi-Strauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Frye" title="Northrop Frye">Frye</a>, the Soviet school, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_and_Ritual_School" title="Myth and Ritual School">Myth and Ritual School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-modern">Pre-modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pre-modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The critical interpretation of myth began with the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">Presocratics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20151_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20151-73">[73]</a></sup> Euhemerus was one of the most important pre-modern mythologists. He interpreted myths as accounts of actual historical events - distorted over many retellings. Sallustius<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> divided myths into five categories – theological, physical (or concerning natural laws), animistic (or concerning soul), material, and mixed. Mixed concerns myths that show the interaction between two or more of the previous categories and are particularly used in initiations.</p>
<p>Plato famously condemned poetic myth when discussing education in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Republic_(Plato)" title="The Republic (Plato)">Republic</a>.</i> His critique was primarily on the grounds that the uneducated might take the stories of gods and heroes literally. Nevertheless, he constantly referred to myths throughout his writings. As <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonism</a> developed in the phases commonly called Middle Platonism and <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">neoplatonism</a>, writers such as <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(philosopher)" title="Porphyry (philosopher)">Porphyry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympiodorus_the_younger" title="Olympiodorus the younger">Olympiodorus,</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damascius" title="Damascius">Damascius</a> wrote explicitly about the symbolic interpretation of traditional and Orphic myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Interest in polytheistic mythology revived during the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, with early works on mythography appearing in the sixteenth-century, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Theologia_mythologica" title="Theologia mythologica">Theologia Mythologica</a></i> (1532). While myths are not the same as fables, legends, folktales, fairy tales, anecdotes, or fiction, the concepts may overlap. Notably, during the nineteenth century period of Romanticism, folktales and fairy tales were perceived as eroded fragments of earlier mythology (famously by the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elias_L%C3%B6nnrot" title="Elias Lönnrot">Elias Lönnrot</a>).</p>
<p>Mythological themes were consciously employed in literature, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>. The resulting work may expressly refer to a mythological background without itself becoming part of a body of myths (<a href="/wiki/Cupid_and_Psyche" title="Cupid and Psyche">Cupid and Psyche</a>). Medieval romance in particular plays with this process of turning myth into literature. <i>Euhemerism</i>, as stated earlier, refers to the rationalization of myths, putting themes formerly imbued with mythological qualities into pragmatic contexts. An example of this would be following a cultural or religious paradigm shift (notably the re-interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> mythology following <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianization</a>).</p>
<p>Conversely, historical and literary material may acquire mythological qualities over time. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a> (the legendary history of Great Britain, especially those focused on <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Round_Table" title="Round Table">the knights of the Round Table</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_France" title="Matter of France">Matter of France</a>, based on historical events of the fifth and eighth-centuries respectively, were first made into <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poetry</a> and became partly mythological over the following centuries. "Conscious generation" of mythology was termed <i>mythopoeia</i> by <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">Tolkien</a> and was notoriously also suggested, separately, by Nazi ideologist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Rosenberg" title="Alfred Rosenberg">Alfred Rosenberg</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nineteenth-century">Nineteenth-century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nineteenth-century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first scholarly theories of myth appeared during the second half of the nineteenth-century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20151_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20151-73">[73]</a></sup> In general, these nineteenth-century theories framed myth as a failed or obsolete mode of thought, often by interpreting myth as the primitive counterpart of modern science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20153.E2.80.934_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20153.E2.80.934-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burnett_Tylor" title="Edward Burnett Tylor">Tylor</a> interpreted myth as an attempt at a literal explanation for natural phenomena. Unable to conceive impersonal natural laws, early humans tried to explain natural phenomena by attributing souls to inanimate objects, giving rise to <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20154_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20154-77">[77]</a></sup> According to Tylor, human thought evolved through stages, starting with mythological ideas and gradually progressing to scientific ideas. Not all scholars, not even all nineteenth-century scholars, accepted this view. <a href="/wiki/Lucien_L%C3%A9vy-Bruhl" title="Lucien Lévy-Bruhl">Lévy-Bruhl</a> claimed "the primitive mentality is a condition of the human mind, and not a stage in its historical development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max Müller">Müller</a> called myth a "disease of language". He speculated that myths arose due to the lack of abstract nouns and neuter gender in ancient languages. Anthropomorphic figures of speech, necessary in such languages, were eventually taken literally, leading to the idea that natural phenomena were in actuality conscious beings or gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201520_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201520-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_George_Frazer" title="James George Frazer">Frazer</a> saw myths as a misinterpretation of magical rituals, which were themselves based on a mistaken idea of natural law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201567.E2.80.9368_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201567.E2.80.9368-79">[79]</a></sup> According to Frazer, humans begin with an unfounded belief in impersonal magical laws. When they realize applications of these laws do not work, they give up their belief in natural law in favor of a belief in personal gods controlling nature, thus giving rise to religious myths. Meanwhile, humans continue practicing formerly magical rituals through force of habit, reinterpreting them as reenactments of mythical events. Finally humans come to realize nature follows natural laws, and they discover their true nature through science. Here again, science makes myth obsolete as humans progress "from magic through religion to science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Segal asserted that by pitting mythical thought against modern scientific thought, such theories imply modern humans must abandon myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20153_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20153-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth-century">Twentieth-century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Twentieth-century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2656" data-file-width="1576" height="371" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg/220px-Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg/330px-Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg/440px-Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gustave_Moreau_Prometheus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a></i> (1868) by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Moreau" title="Gustave Moreau">Gustave Moreau</a>. In the mythos of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesiodus" title="Hesiodus">Hesiodus</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_mythos" title="Greek mythos">Greek</a> trilogy <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Bound" title="Prometheus Bound">Prometheus Bound</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Unbound_(Aeschylus)" title="Prometheus Unbound (Aeschylus)">Prometheus Unbound</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prometheus_Pyrphoros" title="Prometheus Pyrphoros">Prometheus Pyrphoros</a></i>), Prometheus is bound and tortured for giving fire to humanity.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many twentieth-century theories rejected the nineteenth-century theories' opposition of myth and science. In general, "twentieth-century theories have tended to see myth as almost anything but an outdated counterpart to science […]. Consequently, modern individuals are not obliged to abandon myth for science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20153_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20153-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Jung</a> tried to understand the psychology behind world myths. Jung asserted that all humans share certain innate unconscious psychological forces, which he called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archetypes" title="Archetypes">archetypes</a></i>. He believed similarities between the myths of different cultures reveals the existence of these universal archetypes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>Campbell described two orders of mythology: myths that "are metaphorical of spiritual potentiality in the human being," and myths "that have to do with specific societies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell197622_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell197622-82">[82]</a></sup> His major work is <i>The Masks of God I-IV</i>. In the first volume, <i>Primitive Mythology</i>, he clearly outlines his intention:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Without straining beyond the treasuries of evidence already on hand in these widely scattered departments of our subject, therefore, but simply gathering from them the membra disjuncta of a unitary mythological science, I attempt in the following pages the first sketch of a natural history of the gods and heroes, such as in its final form should include in its purview all divine beings—as zoology includes all animals and botany all plants—not regarding any as sacrosanct or beyond its scientific domain. For, as in the visible world of the vegetable and animal kingdoms, so also in the visionary world of the gods: there has been a history, an evolution, a series of mutations, governed by laws; and to show forth such laws is the proper aim of science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell19764_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell19764-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In his fourth volume Campbell coined the phrase, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_mythology" title="Creative mythology">creative mythology</a></i>, which he explains as:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the context of traditional mythology, the symbols are presented in socially maintained rites, through which the individual is required to experience, or will pretend to have experienced, certain insights, sentiments and commitments. In what I'm calling creative mythology, on the other hand, this order is reversed: the individual has had an experience of his own – of order, horror, beauty, or even mere exhilaration-which he seeks to communicate through signs; and if his realization has been of a certain depth and import, his communication will have the force and value of living myth-for those, that is to say, who receive and respond to it of themselves, with recognition, uncoerced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell19914_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell19914-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Lévi-Strauss</a> believed myths reflect patterns in the mind and interpreted those patterns more as fixed mental structures, specifically pairs of opposites (good/evil, compassionate/callous), rather than unconscious feelings or urges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal2015113_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal2015113-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his appendix to <i>Myths, Dreams and Mysteries</i>, and in <i>The Myth of the Eternal Return</i>, <a href="/wiki/Mircea_Eliade" title="Mircea Eliade">Eliade</a> attributed modern humans’ anxieties to their rejection of myths and the sense of the sacred.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the 1950s, Barthes published a series of essays examining modern myths and the process of their creation in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Mythologies_(book)" title="Mythologies (book)">Mythologies</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Following the Structuralist Era (roughly the 1960s to 1980s), the predominant anthropological and sociological approaches to myth increasingly treated myth as a form of narrative that can be studied, interpreted and analyzed like ideology, history and culture. In other words, myth is a form of understanding and telling stories that is connected to power, political structures, and political and economic interests. These approaches contrast with approaches such as those of Campbell and Eliade that hold that myth has some type of essential connection to ultimate sacred meanings that transcend cultural specifics. In particular, myth was studied in relation to history from diverse social sciences. Most of these studies share the assumption that history and myth are not distinct in the sense that history is factual, real, accurate, and truth, while myth is the opposite.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_mythology">Comparative mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Comparative mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">Comparative mythology</a></div>
<p>Comparative mythology is the systematic comparison of myths from different cultures. It seeks to discover underlying themes that are common to the myths of multiple cultures. In some cases, comparative mythologists use the similarities between separate mythologies to argue that those mythologies have a common source. This source may inspire myths or provide a common "protomythology" that diverged into the mythologies of each culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELittleton197332_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELittleton197332-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nineteenth-century interpretations of myth were often comparative, seeking a common origin for all myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeonard2007_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeonard2007-87">[87]</a></sup> Later scholars tend to avoid universal statements about mythology. One exception to this modern trend is Campbell's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hero_With_a_Thousand_Faces" title="The Hero With a Thousand Faces">The Hero with a Thousand Faces</a></i> (1949), which claims that all <a href="/wiki/Hero" title="Hero">hero</a> myths follow the same underlying pattern. This theory of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomyth" title="Monomyth">monomyth</a> later fell out of favor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorthup20068_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorthup20068-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_mythology">Modern mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:10,000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1566" data-file-width="2634" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg/220px-10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg/330px-10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg/440px-10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:10,000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1929 <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknote</a>, depicting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceres_(Roman_mythology)" title="Ceres (Roman mythology)">Ceres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neptune_(mythology)" title="Neptune (mythology)">Neptune</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caduceus" title="Caduceus">caduceus</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In modern society, myth is often regarded as a collection of stories. Scholars in the field of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> research how myth has worked itself into modern discourses. Mythological discourse can reach greater audiences than ever before via digital media. Various mythic elements appear in <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">cinema</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>.</p>
<p>Although myth was traditionally transmitted through the oral tradition on a small scale, the film industry has enabled filmmakers to transmit myths to large audiences via film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Jung</a>ian psychology myths are the expression of a culture or society’s goals, fears, ambitions and dreams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Film is an expression of the society in which it was produced and reflects the culture of its era and location.</p>
<p>The basis of modern visual storytelling is rooted in the mythological tradition. Many contemporary movies rely on ancient myths to construct narratives. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney_Corporation" title="Disney Corporation">Disney Corporation</a> is well-known among cultural study scholars for "reinventing" traditional childhood myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> While many films are not as obvious as Disney fairy tales, the plots of many films are based on the rough structure of myths. Mythological archetypes, such as the cautionary tale regarding the abuse of technology, battles between gods and creation stories, are often the subject of major film productions. These films are often created under the guise of <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_movies" title="Action movies">action movies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">dramas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_literature" title="Apocalyptic literature">apocalyptic</a> tales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorner199947.E2.80.9359_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorner199947.E2.80.9359-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_of_the_Titans_(2010_film)" title="Clash of the Titans (2010 film)">Clash of the Titans</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Immortals_(2011_film)" title="Immortals (2011 film)">Immortals</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Thor_(film)" title="Thor (film)">Thor</a></i> continue the trend of mining traditional mythology to frame modern plots. Authors use mythology as a basis for their books, such as <a href="/wiki/Rick_Riordan" title="Rick Riordan">Rick Riordan</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Percy_Jackson_%26_the_Olympians" title="Percy Jackson &amp; the Olympians">Percy Jackson and the Olympians</a> series is situated in a modern-day world where the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Olympians" title="Twelve Olympians">Greek deities</a> are manifest, as well as his <a href="/wiki/The_Kane_Chronicles" title="The Kane Chronicles">Kane Chronicles</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_deities" title="Ancient Egyptian deities">Egyptian pantheon.</a></p>
<p>Modern myths such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_legends" title="Urban legends">urban legends</a> shows that myth-making continues. Myth-making is not a collection of stories fixed to a remote time and place, but an ongoing social practice within every society.</p>
