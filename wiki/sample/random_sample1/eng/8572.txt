<p><b>Sheila Jeffreys</b> (born 13 May 1948),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Congress_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Congress-2">[2]</a></sup> an English <a href="/wiki/Expatriate" title="Expatriate">expatriate</a> in Australia, is best known as a <a href="/wiki/Lesbian_feminism" title="Lesbian feminism">lesbian feminist</a> scholar and political activist, for her analysis of the history and politics of <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">human sexuality</a> in Britain, and for her controversial views on <a href="/wiki/Transsexual" title="Transsexual">transsexuals</a>. She was a professor in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Melbourne" title="University of Melbourne">University of Melbourne</a> in Australia. Jeffreys's argument that the "<a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual revolution</a>" on men's terms contributed less to women's freedom than to their continued oppression has both commanded respect and attracted intense criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Jeffreys argues that transsexuals reproduce oppressive <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> and mutilate their bodies through <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">sex reassignment surgery</a>, that lesbian culture has been negatively affected by emulating the <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">sexist</a> influence of the gay male subculture of dominant/submissive sexuality, and that women suffering <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a> in pursuit of beauty is a form of submission to <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchal</a> <a href="/wiki/Sadistic_personality_disorder" title="Sadistic personality disorder">sadism</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sheila_Jeffreys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1979, Jeffreys helped write <i>Love Your Enemy? The Debate Between Heterosexual Feminism and Political Lesbianism</i>, along with other members of the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Revolutionary_Feminist_Group" title="Leeds Revolutionary Feminist Group">Leeds Revolutionary Feminist Group</a>. Its authors stated that, "We do think... that all feminists can and should be lesbians. Our definition of a political lesbian is a woman-identified woman who does not fuck men. It does not mean compulsory sexual activity with women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BINDEL2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BINDEL2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jeffreys was one of several contributors to <i>The sexual dynamics of history: men's power, women's resistance</i>, an anthology of feminist writings about gender relations published in 1983 under the name "London Feminist History Group." Jeffreys wrote the chapter on "Sex reform and anti-feminism in the 1920s".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spinster_and_Her_Enemies" title="The Spinster and Her Enemies">The Spinster and Her Enemies</a>: Feminism and Sexuality 1880–1930</i>, published in 1985, Jeffreys examines feminist involvement in the Social Purity movement at the turn of the century. In her 1990 work <i><a href="/wiki/Anticlimax_(book)" title="Anticlimax (book)">Anticlimax</a>: A Feminist Perspective on the Sexual Revolution</i>, Jeffreys offered a critique of the sexual revolution of the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Lesbian Heresy</i> was published in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HERESY_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HERESY-9">[9]</a></sup> In it Jeffreys criticises <a href="/wiki/BDSM" title="BDSM">sadomasochistic</a> practices that involved women. One author involved in sadomasochism cites Jeffreys' views in this book as an example of the "simplistic and dualistic thinking" among anti-sadomasochism campaigners, when she describes sadomasochism as "male supremacist", a re-enactment of heterosexual male dominance and women's oppression, which glorifies violence and uses women's bodies as a sex aid, and as anti-lesbian and <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascistic</a>. The author claims that Jeffreys ignores that some heterosexual women may enjoy sado-masochistic activity, and that '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_(BDSM)" title="Top (BDSM)">tops</a>' may be women who work hard to give their '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottom_(BDSM)" title="Bottom (BDSM)">bottoms</a>' pleasure, rather than the passive recipients of sex in the way she describes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STEIN_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-STEIN-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Industrial_Vagina" title="The Industrial Vagina">The Industrial Vagina</a>: The Political Economy of the Global Sex Trade</i>, was published in 2009. In it, Jeffreys describes the globalisation of the sex industry, and describes marriage as a form of prostitution. Jeffreys states that "the right of men to women's bodies for sexual use has not gone but remains an assumption at the basis of heterosexual relationships", and draws links between marriage and prostitution, such as mail-order brides, which she sees as a form of trafficking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BINDEL1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BINDEL1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_transsexualism">Views on transsexualism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sheila_Jeffreys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Views on transsexualism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The University of Melbourne, Jeffreys' employer until her retirement in May 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> advertised her services as an expert on a number of subjects:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Female-to-male transsexualism; Gay pornography; Feminist critiques of queer theory; Queer political agenda; International sex industry. (Western beauty practices as makeup, high heel shoes, cosmetic surgery, as well as pornochic; Misogyny in fashion and transfemininity.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In an interview, <a href="/wiki/Julie_Bindel" title="Julie Bindel">Julie Bindel</a> explains that Jeffreys believes <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">sex reassignment surgery</a> "is an extension of the beauty industry offering cosmetic solutions to deeper rooted problems" and that in a society without gender this would be unnecessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BINDEL2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BINDEL2-7">[7]</a></sup> Jeffreys has presented these views in various forums. In a 1997 article in the Journal of Lesbian Studies, for example, Jeffreys contended that "transsexualism should be seen as a violation of human rights." Jeffreys also argued that "the vast majority of transsexuals still subscribe to the traditional stereotype of women" and that by <a href="/wiki/Transitioning_(transgender)" title="Transitioning (transgender)">transitioning</a> medically and socially, <a href="/wiki/Trans_woman" title="Trans woman">trans women</a> are "constructing a conservative fantasy of what women should be. They are inventing an <a href="/wiki/Essence" title="Essence">essence</a> of womanhood which is deeply insulting and restrictive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jeffreys' opinions on these topics have been challenged by transgender activists. <a href="/wiki/Roz_Kaveney" title="Roz Kaveney">Roz Kaveney</a>, a trans woman and critic of Jeffreys, wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> that Sheila Jeffreys and <a href="/wiki/Radical_feminism" title="Radical feminism">radical feminists</a> who share her views are "acting like a <a href="/wiki/Cult" title="Cult">cult</a>." Kaveney compared Jeffreys' desire to ban transsexual surgery to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>'s desire to <a href="/wiki/Anti-abortion_movements" title="Anti-abortion movements">ban abortion</a>, arguing that both proposals bear negative "implications for all women." Finally, Kaveney criticised Jeffreys' and her supporters for alleged "<a href="/wiki/Anti-intellectualism" title="Anti-intellectualism">anti-intellectualism</a>, emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Innatism" title="Innatism">innate knowledge</a>, fetishisation of tiny <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideological differences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a> hunting, <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">conspiracy theories</a>, rhetorical use of images of disgust, talk of stabs in the back and romantic apocalypticism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2014 <i>Gender Hurts: A Feminist Analysis of the Politics of Transgenderism</i>, a book cowritten by Jeffreys with Lorene Gottschalk, was published. Timothy Laurie argued that the formalisation of social dynamics between men and women in <i>Gender Hurts</i> in terms of "strategies' and dividends" risks "confusing the continued existence of unequal economic exchanges (well documented by R.W. Connell) with the less predictable, but equally important, struggles over what gets labelled 'masculine' and 'feminine' and for what collective purposes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Judith_Butler" title="Judith Butler">Judith Butler</a> weighed in on Jeffreys' view that sex reassignment surgery is directly political.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In an interview Butler responded to Jeffreys' notion that reassignment surgery is a component of patriarchal control. She claimed that "One problem with that view of social construction is that it suggests that what trans people feel about what their gender is, and should be, is itself “constructed” and, therefore, not real. And then the feminist police comes along…"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jeffreys stated in a 2014 ABC Radio "Sunday Night Safran" program that transsexual women are either "homosexual men who don’t feel they can be homosexual in the bodies of men" or "heterosexual men who have a sexual interest in wearing women’s clothes and having the appearance of women", provoking criticism from members of the Indigenous and trans communities for racism and transphobia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
