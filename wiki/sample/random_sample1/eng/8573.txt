<p><b>Silica gel</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Granularity" title="Granularity">granular</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitreous_lustre" title="Vitreous lustre">vitreous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porous</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a> made synthetically from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_silicate" title="Sodium silicate">sodium silicate</a>. Silica gel contains a nano-porous silica micro-structure, suspended inside a liquid. Most applications of silica gel require it to be dried, in which case it is called silica xerogel. For practical purposes, silica gel is often interchangeable with silica xerogel. Silica xerogel is tough and hard; it is more solid than common household <a href="/wiki/Gel" title="Gel">gels</a> like <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a>. It is a naturally occurring mineral that is purified and processed into either granular or beaded form. As a <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a>, it has an average pore size of 2.4 nanometers and has a strong affinity for water molecules.</p>
<p>Silica gel is most commonly encountered in everyday life as beads in a small (typically <span class="nowrap">2 x 3 cm)</span> paper packet. In this form, it is used as a <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a> to control local <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> to avoid <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">spoilage</a> or degradation of some goods. Because silica gel can have added chemical indicators (<a href="#Humidity_indicator">see below</a>) and absorbs moisture very well, silica gel packets usually bear warnings for the user not to eat the contents.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silica gel was in existence as early as the 1640s as a scientific curiosity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was used in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> of vapors and gases in <a href="/wiki/Gas_mask" title="Gas mask">gas mask</a> canisters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The synthetic route for producing silica gel was patented by chemistry professor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_A._Patrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Walter A. Patrick (page does not exist)">Walter A. Patrick</a> at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a> in 1918.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, silica gel was indispensable in the war effort for keeping <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a> dry, protecting military equipment from moisture damage<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, as a fluid <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> for the production of <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">high octane</a> <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, and as a catalyst support for the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butadiene" title="Butadiene">butadiene</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, feedstock for the <a href="/wiki/Polybutadiene" title="Polybutadiene">synthetic rubber</a> program.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Type A - clear pellets, approximate pore diameter: 2.5 nm, drying and moistureproof properties, can be used as catalyst carriers, adsorbents, separators and variable-pressure adsorbent.</p>
<p>Type B - translucent white pellets, pore diameter: 4.5-7.0 nm, liquid adsorbents, drier and perfume carriers, also may be used as catalyst carriers, cat litter.</p>
<p>Type C - translucent, micro-pored structure, raw material for preparation of silica gel cat litter. Additionally dried and screened, it forms macro-pored silica gel which is used as drier, adsorbent and catalyst carrier.</p>
<p>Silica alumina gel - light yellow, chemically stable, flame-resistant, insoluble except in alkali or hydrofluoric acid. Superficial polarity, thermal stability, performance greater than fine-pored silica gel.</p>
<p>Stabilizing silica gel - non-crystalline micro-porous solid powder, nontoxic, flame-resisting, used in brewery of grains for beer to improve taste, clearness, color and foam, removal of non-micro-organism impurities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silica gel's high <a href="/wiki/Specific_surface_area" title="Specific surface area">specific surface area</a> (around 800 m<sup>2</sup>/g) allows it to adsorb water readily, making it useful as a desiccant (drying agent). Silica gel is often described as "absorbing" moisture, which may be appropriate when the gel's microscopic structure is ignored, as in silica gel packs or other products. However, material silica gel removes moisture by <i><a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a></i> onto the surface of its numerous pores rather than by <i><a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a></i> into the bulk of the gel.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regeneration">Regeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Regeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Once saturated with water, the gel can be regenerated by heating it to 120 °C (250 °F) for 1–2 hours. Some types of silica gel will "pop" when exposed to enough water. This is caused by breakage of the silica spheres when contacting the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_silicate" title="Sodium silicate">sodium silicate</a> is acidified to produce a gelatinous precipitate that is washed, then dehydrated to produce colorless silica gel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-5">[5]</a></sup> When a visible indication of the moisture content of the silica gel is required, ammonium tetrachlorocobaltate(II) (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CoCl<sub>4</sub> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_chloride" title="Cobalt chloride">cobalt chloride</a> CoCl<sub>2</sub> is added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-5">[5]</a></sup> This will cause the gel to be blue when dry and pink when hydrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-5">[5]</a></sup> An alternative indicator is <a href="/wiki/Methyl_violet" title="Methyl violet">methyl violet</a> which is orange when dry and green when hydrated. Due to the connection between cancer and cobalt chloride, it has been forbidden in Europe on silica gel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Desiccant">Desiccant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Desiccant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="864" data-file-width="1152" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg/220px-Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg/330px-Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg/440px-Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silica_gel_bag_open_with_beads.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Silica gel is a commonly used desiccant as beads packed in a permeable bag</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">Desiccant</a></div>
<p>In many items, moisture encourages the growth of mold and spoilage. Condensation may also damage other items like electronics and may speed the decomposition of chemicals, such as those in vitamin pills. Through the inclusion of silica gel packets, these items can be preserved longer.</p>
<p>Silica gel may also be used to keep the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> (RH) inside a high frequency radio or satellite transmission system <a href="/wiki/Waveguide_(electromagnetism)" title="Waveguide (electromagnetism)">waveguide</a> as low as possible (see also <a href="/wiki/Humidity_buffering" title="Humidity buffering">Humidity buffering</a>). Excessive moisture buildup within a waveguide can cause arcing inside the waveguide itself, damaging the power amplifier feeding it. Also, the beads of water that form and condense inside the waveguide change the characteristic impedance and frequency, degrading the signal. It is common for a small compressed air system (similar to a small home aquarium pump) to be employed to circulate the air inside the waveguide over a jar of silica gel.</p>
<p>Silica gel is also used to dry the air in industrial compressed air systems. Air from the compressor discharge flows through a bed of silica gel beads. The silica gel adsorbs moisture from the air, preventing damage at the point of use of the compressed air due to condensation or moisture. The same system is used to dry the compressed air on railway locomotives, where condensation and ice in the brake air pipes can lead to brake failure.</p>
<p>Silica gel is sometimes used as a <a href="/wiki/Preservation_(library_and_archival_science)" title="Preservation (library and archival science)">preservation</a> tool to control relative humidity in museum and library exhibitions and storage.</p>
<p>Other applications include diagnostic test strips, inhalation devices, <a href="/wiki/Syringe" title="Syringe">syringes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drug_test" title="Drug test">drug test kits</a> and hospital sanitation kits.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:62px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P1000495.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="295" height="394" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/P1000495.JPG/60px-P1000495.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/P1000495.JPG/90px-P1000495.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/P1000495.JPG/120px-P1000495.JPG 2x" width="60"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:P1000495.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Chromatography_column" title="Chromatography column">Chromatography column</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In chemistry, silica gel is used in <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationary_phase_(chemistry)" title="Stationary phase (chemistry)">stationary phase</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Column_chromatography" title="Column chromatography">column chromatography</a>, the stationary phase is most often composed of silica gel particles of 40–63 μm. Different particle sizes are used for different kinds of column chromatography as the particle size is related to surface area. The differences in particle size dictate if the silica gel should be used for flash or gravity chromatography. In this application, due to silica gel's polarity, non-polar components tend to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elute" title="Elute">elute</a> before more polar ones, hence the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_phase_chromatography" title="Normal phase chromatography">normal phase chromatography</a>. However, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> groups (such as C<sub>18</sub> groups) are attached to the silica gel then polar components elute first and the method is referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reverse_phase_chromatography" title="Reverse phase chromatography">reverse phase chromatography</a>. Silica gel is also applied to <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, or plastic sheets for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_layer_chromatography" title="Thin layer chromatography">thin layer chromatography</a>.</p>
<p>The hydroxy (OH) groups on the surface of silica can be functionalized to afford specialty silica gels that exhibit unique stationary phase parameters. These so-called functionalized silica gels are also used in organic synthesis and purification as insoluble reagents and <a href="/wiki/Scavenger_(chemistry)" title="Scavenger (chemistry)">scavengers</a>.</p>
<p>Chelating groups have also been covalently bound to silica gel. These materials have the ability to remove metal ions selectively from aqueous media. Chelating groups can be covalently bound to polyamines that have been grafted onto a silica gel surface producing a material of greater mechanical integrity. Silica gel is also combined with <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metals</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/M-SG_reducing_agent" title="M-SG reducing agent">M-SG reducing agent</a>.</p>
<p>Silica gel is not expected to biodegrade in either water or soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hazard_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-hazard-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cat_litter">Cat litter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cat litter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Silica gel is also used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cat_litter#Types_of_litter_box_filler" title="Cat litter">cat litter</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> by itself or in combination with more traditional materials, such as clays including <a href="/wiki/Bentonite" title="Bentonite">bentonite</a>. It is trackless<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and virtually odorless.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_additive">Food additive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Food additive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Silica gel, also referred to as silica aerogel or hydrated silica, is listed by the FDA in the United States as <a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a> (GRAS), meaning it can be added to food products without needing approval. Silica is allowed to be added to food in the US at up to 2% as permitted under 21 CFR 172.480. In the EU it can be in up to 5% concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Listed uses include: anticaking agent, defoaming agent, stabilizer, adsorbent, carrier, conditioning agent, chillproofing agent, filter aid, emulsifying agent, viscosity control agent, and anti-settling agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_filtration">Water filtration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Water filtration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Given the water adsorption properties of silica gel, it is used in domestic water filters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The surface structure of silica gel allows the adsorption of some minerals which are dissolved in the water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> or "Ion-exchange" as it is marketed. Due to the lack of regulations for domestic water filtration products, there are no studies which validate the manufacturer claims regarding the effectiveness of the filtration system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humidity_indicator">Humidity indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Humidity indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Silica gel may be doped with a moisture indicator that gradually changes its color when it transitions from the <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> (dry) state, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrated" title="Hydrated">hydrated</a> (wet) state. Common indicators are <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_chloride" title="Cobalt(II) chloride">cobalt(II) chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methyl_violet" title="Methyl violet">methyl violet</a>. Cobalt (II) chloride is deep blue when dry and pink when wet. It is toxic and carcinogenic, and was reclassified by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in July 2000 as a toxic material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methyl_violet" title="Methyl violet">Methyl violet</a> can be formulated to change from orange to green, or orange to colorless. It is also toxic and potentially carcinogenic<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but is safe enough to have medicinal uses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silica_gel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silica gel is non-toxic, non-flammable, and non-reactive and stable with ordinary usage. It will react with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_difluoride" title="Oxygen difluoride">oxygen difluoride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_trifluoride" title="Chlorine trifluoride">chlorine trifluoride</a>, strong acids, strong bases, and oxidizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hazard_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-hazard-7">[7]</a></sup> Silica gel is irritating to the respiratory tract and may cause irritation of the digestive tract, and dust from the beads may cause irritation to the skin and eyes, so precautions should be taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Crystalline silica dust can cause <a href="/wiki/Silicosis" title="Silicosis">silicosis</a> but synthetic amorphous silica gel is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indurated" title="Indurated">indurated</a>, and so does not cause silicosis.</p>
