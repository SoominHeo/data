<p><b>Sylvester James, Jr.</b> (September 6, 1947 – December 16, 1988), who used the stage name of <b>Sylvester</b>, was an American singer-songwriter. Primarily active in the genres of <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, he was known for his flamboyant and androgynous appearance, <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsetto</a> singing voice, and hit disco singles in the late 1970s.</p>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Watts,_Los_Angeles" title="Watts, Los Angeles">Watts, Los Angeles</a>, to a middle-class <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> family, Sylvester developed a love of singing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospel_choir" title="Gospel choir">gospel choir</a> of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentecostal" title="Pentecostal">Pentecostal</a> church. Leaving the church after the congregation expressed disapproval of his <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>, he found friendship among a group of <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black</a> <a href="/wiki/Cross-dressing" title="Cross-dressing">cross-dressers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trans_woman" title="Trans woman">transgender women</a> who called themselves The Disquotays. Moving to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> in 1970 at the age of 22, Sylvester embraced the <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">counterculture</a> and joined the avant-garde <a href="/wiki/Drag_queen" title="Drag queen">drag</a> troupe <a href="/wiki/The_Cockettes" title="The Cockettes">The Cockettes</a>, producing solo segments of their shows which were heavily influenced by female <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> singers like <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">Billie Holiday</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josephine_Baker" title="Josephine Baker">Josephine Baker</a>. During the Cockettes' critically panned tour of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, Sylvester left them to pursue his career elsewhere. He came to front Sylvester and his Hot Band, a <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock act</a> that released two commercially unsuccessful albums on <a href="/wiki/Blue_Thumb_Records" title="Blue Thumb Records">Blue Thumb Records</a> in 1973 before disbanding.</p>
<p>Focusing on a solo career, Sylvester signed a <a href="/wiki/Recording_contract" title="Recording contract">recording contract</a> with <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Fuqua" title="Harvey Fuqua">Harvey Fuqua</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_Records" title="Fantasy Records">Fantasy Records</a> and obtained three new backing singers in the form of <a href="/wiki/Martha_Wash" title="Martha Wash">Martha Wash</a>, Izora Rhodes – the "<a href="/wiki/The_Weather_Girls" title="The Weather Girls">Two Tons O' Fun</a>" – and <a href="/wiki/Jeanie_Tracy" title="Jeanie Tracy">Jeanie Tracy</a>. His first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sylvester_(album)" title="Sylvester (album)">Sylvester</a></i> (1977), was a moderate success. This was followed with the acclaimed disco album <i>Step II</i> (1978), which spawned the singles "<a href="/wiki/You_Make_Me_Feel_(Mighty_Real)" title="You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)">You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Dance_(Disco_Heat)" title="Dance (Disco Heat)">Dance (Disco Heat)</a>", both of which were hits in the U.S. and Europe. Distancing himself from the disco genre, he recorded four more albums – including a live album – with Fantasy Records. After leaving this label, he signed to <a href="/wiki/Megatone_Records" title="Megatone Records">Megatone Records</a>, the dance-oriented company founded by friend and collaborator <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Cowley" title="Patrick Cowley">Patrick Cowley</a>, where he recorded four more albums, including the Cowley penned hit <a href="/wiki/Hi-NRG" title="Hi-NRG">Hi-NRG</a> track "<a href="/wiki/Do_Ya_Wanna_Funk" title="Do Ya Wanna Funk">Do Ya Wanna Funk</a>." An activist who campaigned against the spread of <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>, Sylvester died from complications arising from the virus in 1988, leaving all future royalties from his work to San Francisco-based HIV/AIDS charities.</p>
<p>During the late 1970s, Sylvester gained the moniker of the "<a href="/wiki/Honorific_nicknames_in_popular_music#S" title="Honorific nicknames in popular music">Queen of Disco</a>" and during his life he attained particular recognition in San Francisco, where he was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Key_to_the_city" title="Key to the city">key to the city</a>. In 2005, he was posthumously inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Dance_Music_Hall_of_Fame" title="Dance Music Hall of Fame">Dance Music Hall of Fame</a>, while his life has been recorded in a biography and made the subject of both a documentary and a musical.</p>

<p>"Oh God, don't ask me how I got started or how old I am or about The Cockettes. Let's just say that I came from an upper-middle class black bourgeois family in Los Angeles, and that I left a boring nine-to-five job to move to San Francisco. </div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Sylvester<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis20151_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis20151-1">[1]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Sylvester James was born on September 6, 1947 in the <a href="/wiki/Watts,_Los_Angeles" title="Watts, Los Angeles">Watts</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200515_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200515-2">[2]</a></sup> into a middle-class family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200576_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200576-3">[3]</a></sup> His mother, Letha Weaver, had been raised near <a href="/wiki/Palestine,_Arkansas" title="Palestine, Arkansas">Palestine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> into a relatively wealthy African-American family who owned their own farmland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200514.E2.80.9315_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200514.E2.80.9315-4">[4]</a></sup> Letha's biological mother, Gertha Weaver, was unmarried and too sickly to care for her child, so Gertha's sister Julia, known to the family as JuJu, became Letha's adoptive mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200515_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200515-2">[2]</a></sup> In the late 1930s, Julia and her husband took part in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a> of African-Americans out of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a>, relocating to Watts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200515_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200515-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was here that Letha was largely raised and where she met and married her first husband, Sylvester "Sweet" James, with the couple moving into a small cottage owned by Letha's parents. Their first child, named Sylvester after his father, was followed by the birth of John Wesley in 1948 and Larry in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200515Davis20151.E2.80.932_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200515Davis20151.E2.80.932-5">[5]</a></sup> Sylvester and his brothers became better known in their predominantly African-American community by their nicknames, with Sylvester's being "Dooni."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200514_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200514-6">[6]</a></sup> Sylvester considered his father to be a "lowlife" because he was an adulterer and left his wife and children when the boys were still young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200514.E2.80.9316Davis20152_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200514.E2.80.9316Davis20152-7">[7]</a></sup> Letha and her three sons moved to a downtown housing project at <a href="/wiki/Aliso_Village" title="Aliso Village">Aliso Village</a> before moving back into her parental home at 114th Street in Watts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200516Davis20152_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200516Davis20152-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Letha was a devout adherent of the <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostal</a> denomination of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, regularly attending the Palm Lane <a href="/wiki/Church_of_God_in_Christ" title="Church of God in Christ">Church of God in Christ</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Los_Angeles" title="South Los Angeles">South Los Angeles</a>. Sylvester and his brothers accompanied her to the church's services, where he developed a particular interest in <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200517.E2.80.9321Brogan201267Davis20152_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200517.E2.80.9321Brogan201267Davis20152-9">[9]</a></sup> Having been an avid singer since the age of three, Sylvester regularly joined in with gospel performances; he sang the song "<a href="/wiki/My_Buddy_(song)" title="My Buddy (song)">My Buddy</a>" at the funeral of one of the other children in the Park Lane congregation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200520_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200520-10">[10]</a></sup> The young Sylvester was often accused of <a href="/wiki/Effeminacy" title="Effeminacy">effeminacy</a> and recognized his own <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a> from an early age. At the age of eight, he engaged in sexual activity with a far older man at the church—at the time rumored to be the church organist—although he would always maintain that this was consensual and not an example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_molestation" title="Sexual molestation">sexual molestation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200517.E2.80.9321Davis20152_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200517.E2.80.9321Davis20152-11">[11]</a></sup> Sylvester was taken to a doctor after receiving injuries during <a href="/wiki/Anal_sex" title="Anal sex">anal sex</a> with this man; it was the doctor who informed Letha that her son was gay, something that she could not accept, viewing homosexual activity as a <a href="/wiki/Perversion" title="Perversion">perversion</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200517.E2.80.9321Davis20152_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200517.E2.80.9321Davis20152-11">[11]</a></sup> News of Sylvester's homosexual activity soon spread through the church congregation, and feeling unwelcome, he ceased his attendance at age 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200524Brogan201267_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200524Brogan201267-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During Sylvester's childhood, his mother gave birth to three more children by different fathers before marrying Robert "Sonny" Hurd in the early 1960s, with whom she adopted three foster children. A supervisor at aerospace manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">North American Rockwell</a>, Hurd's job increased the family income and they were able to move into a more expensive, predominantly Euro-American neighborhood north of Watts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200525Davis20152_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200525Davis20152-13">[13]</a></sup> The relationship between Sylvester and both his mother and stepfather was strained; in the midst of one argument with his mother, Sylvester decided to leave their house permanently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200526.E2.80.9328Davis20153_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200526.E2.80.9328Davis20153-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Disquotays:_1962.E2.80.9370">The Disquotays: 1962–70</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Disquotays: 1962–70">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Now homeless, Sylvester spent much of the next decade staying with friends and relatives, in particular his grandmother Julia, who expressed no disapproval of his homosexuality, having been a friend of a number of gay men in the 1930s. On occasion, he returned to his mother and step-father's house for a few days at a time, particularly to spend time with his younger sisters, Bernadette and Bernadine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200528.E2.80.9329.2C_31_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200528.E2.80.9329.2C_31-15">[15]</a></sup> Aged 15, he began frequenting local gay clubs and built up a group of friends from the local gay black community, eventually forming themselves into a group which they called the Disquotays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200531Davis20154_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200531Davis20154-16">[16]</a></sup> Sylvester's best friend among the Disquotays was a <a href="/wiki/Trans_woman" title="Trans woman">trans woman</a> named Duchess, who earned her money as a prostitute, a job that Sylvester refused to engage in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200534.E2.80.9335_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200534.E2.80.9335-17">[17]</a></sup> The group held lavish house parties, sometimes at the home of their friend, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Etta_James" title="Etta James">Etta James</a>, in which they dressed up in female clothing and wigs, constantly trying to outdo one another in appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson20051.E2.80.934.2C_6_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson20051.E2.80.934.2C_6-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Dooni [Sylvester] and the Disquotays wandered the streets of South Central in the 1960s done up like women, and threw ferocious gay parties in neighborhoods whose strongest institutions were conservative black churches. It's tempting to see them as fearless and heroic, defiant sissies who were forerunners of <a href="/wiki/Stonewall_riots" title="Stonewall riots">Stonewall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">sixties counterculture</a>, part of the dawning of <a href="/wiki/Gay_liberation" title="Gay liberation">gay liberation</a> and African-American civil rights organizing."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Biographer Joshua Gamson, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200537_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200537-19">[19]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Sylvester's boyfriend during the latter part of the 1960s was a young man named Lonnie Prince; well-built and attractive, many of Sylvester's friends described the pair as being "the It couple".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200533_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200533-20">[20]</a></sup> Sylvester often hitchhiked around town while in female dress; such activity carried a risk of arrest and prosecution, for <a href="/wiki/Cross-dressing" title="Cross-dressing">cross-dressing</a> was then illegal in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200533_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200533-20">[20]</a></sup> Although avoiding imprisonment for this crime, he was arrested for shoplifting on several occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200531_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200531-21">[21]</a></sup> He found work in a variety of different professions, including cooking in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McDonalds" title="McDonalds">McDonalds</a>—where he was fired for refusing to wear a <a href="/wiki/Hairnet" title="Hairnet">hairnet</a>—cashier at an airport parking garage, working in a hair salon, at a department store, and as a make-up artist at a mortuary, preparing the corpses for their funerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200531.E2.80.9332_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200531.E2.80.9332-22">[22]</a></sup> In the 1960s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1955%E2%80%931968)" title="African-American Civil Rights Movement (1955–1968)">African-American Civil Rights Movement</a> was at its peak, but Sylvester and his friends did not take an active role within it. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watts_Riots" title="Watts Riots">Watts Riots</a> between members of the black community and the predominantly white police force, they joined in with the widespread rioting and looting, stealing wigs, hairspray, and lipstick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200537_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200537-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although he had little interest in formal education and rarely attended classes, Sylvester was enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Jordan_High_School_(Los_Angeles,_California)" title="Jordan High School (Los Angeles, California)">Jordan High School</a>. He graduated in 1969 at the age of 21; in his graduation photograph, he appeared in drag wearing a blue chiffon prom dress and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beehive_hairstyle" title="Beehive hairstyle">beehive hairstyle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200531Davis20154_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200531Davis20154-16">[16]</a></sup> By the end of the decade, the Disquotays had begun to drift apart, with a number of them abandoning cross-dressing and others recognizing that they were trans women and undergoing <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">sex reassignment surgery</a>. Sylvester always considered himself male and began to tone down the feminine nature of his clothing, aiming for a more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Androgynous" title="Androgynous">androgynous</a> look which combined male and female styles and which was influenced by the fashions of the <a href="/wiki/Hippie" title="Hippie">hippie</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200536.E2.80.9337_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200536.E2.80.9337-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cockettes:_1970.E2.80.9372">The Cockettes: 1970–72</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Cockettes: 1970–72">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Sylvester shared the Cockettes' affinity for outrageous flaming, their celebration of sex and gayness, their love of acid and good hash, and their bent movie-musical fantasies. Like them, he was making himself up, fantasizing a self into existence. But he usually stood a few feet back, <i>among</i> the Cockettes but never quite one <i>of</i> them. His drag and makeup, for one thing, were almost staid in comparison with the Cockettes'; they preferred facial designs that were almost like war paint, and clothing that didn't make sense. Sylvester wore simple period dresses onstage, and created the face of a sane, pretty woman."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Biographer Joshua Gamson, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200557_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200557-24">[24]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>At Los Angeles' <a href="/wiki/Whisky_a_Go_Go" title="Whisky a Go Go">Whisky a Go Go</a> bar, Sylvester met Reggie Dunnigan, who invited him to move to the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a> in order to join the "Chocolate Cockettes"—black members of an <i>avant-garde</i> <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a> drag troupe known as <a href="/wiki/The_Cockettes" title="The Cockettes">The Cockettes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200541_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200541-25">[25]</a></sup> Founded by drag queen <a href="/wiki/Hibiscus_(entertainer)" title="Hibiscus (entertainer)">Hibiscus</a> in 1970, the Cockettes parodied popular culture, were involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_Liberation" title="Gay Liberation">Gay Liberation</a> movement, and were influenced by the ethos of the hippie movement, living communally, embracing <a href="/wiki/Free_love" title="Free love">free love</a>, and consuming mind-altering substances like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marijuana" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">LSD</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200549.E2.80.9354_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200549.E2.80.9354-26">[26]</a></sup> With the Disquotays disbanded, Sylvester had tired of Los Angeles, and was attracted by San Francisco's reputation as a gay and counter-cultural haven. Arriving in the city, he stayed in the Cockettes' communal home for several days. They were impressed with his <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsetto</a> singing voice and his ability to play the piano, asking him to appear in an upcoming show, <i>Radio Rodeo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200543.E2.80.9344_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200543.E2.80.9344-27">[27]</a></sup> Sylvester agreed, and one of his first performances involved singing the theme song of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mickey_Mouse_Club" title="The Mickey Mouse Club">The Mickey Mouse Club</a></i> while dressed in a cowgirl skirt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200547_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200547-28">[28]</a></sup> Moving into the Cockettes' communal residence, he soon found the flat too crowded and had difficulty with the lack of privacy; after a year he moved into a new house on Market Street with two fellow Cockettes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200556.E2.80.9357_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200556.E2.80.9357-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although a significant member of the troupe, Sylvester remained a relatively isolated figure; not only was he one of very few African-American members, he eschewed the group's more <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealist</a> activities for what he saw as classier, more glamorous performances onstage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200558_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200558-30">[30]</a></sup> In the Cockettes' performances, he was usually given an entire scene to himself, often with little relevance to the narrative and theme of the rest of the show, although through doing so, he gained his own following.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200564.E2.80.9365_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200564.E2.80.9365-31">[31]</a></sup> With a piano player named Peter Mintum, Sylvester worked on solo scenes in which he exhibited his interest in <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> by imitating several of his musical idols like <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">Billie Holiday</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josephine_Baker" title="Josephine Baker">Josephine Baker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200561.2C_66.E2.80.9367Brogan201267_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200561.2C_66.E2.80.9367Brogan201267-32">[32]</a></sup> Adding to his image, Sylvester used the pseudonym "Ruby Blue"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200563Davis20154_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200563Davis20154-33">[33]</a></sup> and described himself as "Billie Holiday's cousin once removed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200563_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200563-34">[34]</a></sup> Fascinated by black musical heritage, he read up on the subject and became a collector of "negrobilia"; in some of his Cockette performances, he played up to racial stereotypes of African-Americans in order to ridicule the stereotypes themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200559.E2.80.9362_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200559.E2.80.9362-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Palace_Theater_(Los_Angeles).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Palace_Theater_%28Los_Angeles%29.jpg/220px-Palace_Theater_%28Los_Angeles%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Palace_Theater_%28Los_Angeles%29.jpg/330px-Palace_Theater_%28Los_Angeles%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Palace_Theater_%28Los_Angeles%29.jpg/440px-Palace_Theater_%28Los_Angeles%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Palace_Theater_(Los_Angeles).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Broadway_Theater_District_(Los_Angeles)#Surviving_theaters_on_Broadway" title="Broadway Theater District (Los Angeles)">Palace Theater</a>, where Sylvester performed a one-man show</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1970, Sylvester entered into an <a href="/wiki/Open_relationship" title="Open relationship">open relationship</a> with Michael Lyons, a young Euro-American, and soon proposed marriage to him. Although <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Same-sex marriage in the United States">same-sex marriage</a> was not legally recognised in the U.S., the couple held a wedding in the Shakespeare Garden of <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Park" title="Golden Gate Park">Golden Gate Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200572.E2.80.9374_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200572.E2.80.9374-36">[36]</a></sup> At the invite of the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Theater_District_(Los_Angeles)#Surviving_theaters_on_Broadway" title="Broadway Theater District (Los Angeles)">Palace Theater</a>, Sylvester appeared in a spoof film, <i>Tricia's Wedding</i>, which parodied the marriage of <a href="/wiki/Tricia_Nixon_Cox" title="Tricia Nixon Cox">Tricia Nixon Cox</a>, daughter of President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>. In the film, Sylvester played the role of both <a href="/wiki/Coretta_Scott_King" title="Coretta Scott King">Coretta Scott King</a> and the African ambassador Uma King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenberg2004117Gamson200562.E2.80.9363_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenberg2004117Gamson200562.E2.80.9363-37">[37]</a></sup> In 1971, Sylvester was given a one-man show, <i>Sylvester Sings</i>, at the Palace Theater, for which he was accompanied by Mintum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200568_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200568-38">[38]</a></sup> He nevertheless remained a part of the Cockette troupe during their divisive split, in which Hibiscus and his followers left to form the Angels of Light. Following Hibiscus' departure, the Cockettes began to gain increasing media attention, with celebrities such as <a href="/wiki/Rex_Reed" title="Rex Reed">Rex Reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Vanderbilt" title="Gloria Vanderbilt">Gloria Vanderbilt</a> enthusing about their performances. <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine singled out Sylvester's performances for particular praise, describing him as "a beautiful black androgyne who has a gospel sound with the heat and shimmer of <a href="/wiki/Aretha_Franklin" title="Aretha Franklin">Aretha [Franklin]'s</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200574.E2.80.9377_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200574.E2.80.9377-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The success led the troupe to take their show to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, a city with a long history of <a href="/wiki/Drag_queen" title="Drag queen">drag</a> culture. Arriving in November 1971, they immersed themselves in the city's <i>avant-garde</i>, attending parties held by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Screw_(magazine)" title="Screw (magazine)">Screw</a></i> magazine. Spending so much of their time partying, most of the Cockettes did not rehearse, the exception being Sylvester, who wanted to perfect his act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200577.E2.80.9382Davis20156_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200577.E2.80.9382Davis20156-40">[40]</a></sup> Although the Cockettes' performance at the Anderson Theater was panned by critics, Sylvester's act was widely praised as a highlight of the show. Realizing that he had far better prospects as a solo artist, on the second New York performance he opened his act by telling the audience, "I apologize for this travesty that I'm associated with", while on the seventh he announced that he would be leaving the Cockettes altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200582.E2.80.9386Davis20156_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200582.E2.80.9386Davis20156-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emerging_solo_career">Emerging solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Emerging solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sylvester_and_his_Hot_Band:_1972.E2.80.9374">Sylvester and his Hot Band: 1972–74</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sylvester and his Hot Band: 1972–74">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Returning to San Francisco, Sylvester was offered the opportunity to record a demo album by <i>Rolling Stone</i> editor <a href="/wiki/Jann_Wenner" title="Jann Wenner">Jann Wenner</a>. Financed by <a href="/wiki/A%26M_Records" title="A&amp;M Records">A&amp;M Records</a>, the album featured a cover of <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Bramlett" title="Bonnie Bramlett">Bonnie Bramlett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Russell" title="Leon Russell">Leon Russell</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Superstar_(Delaney_and_Bonnie_song)" title="Superstar (Delaney and Bonnie song)">Superstar</a>," which had been a recent hit single for <a href="/wiki/The_Carpenters" title="The Carpenters">The Carpenters</a>; nevertheless, A&amp;M felt that the work was not commercially viable and declined to release the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200588.E2.80.9389_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200588.E2.80.9389-42">[42]</a></sup> For the album, Sylvester and his manager Dennis Lopez had assembled a group of heterosexual white males—Bobby Blood on trumpet, Chris Mostert on saxophone, James Q. Smith on guitar, Travis Fullerton on drums, and Kerry Hatch on bass—whom he named The Hot Band. After A&amp;M's initial rejection, the band provided two songs for <i>Lights Out San Francisco,</i> an album compiled by San Francisco's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KSAN_(AM)" title="KSAN (AM)">KSAN</a> radio and released on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Thumb" title="Blue Thumb">Blue Thumb</a> label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200589.2C_90.E2.80.9391Davis20156_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200589.2C_90.E2.80.9391Davis20156-43">[43]</a></sup> Gaining a number of local gigs, they were eventually asked to open for English <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rock</a> star <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Winterland_Ballroom" title="Winterland Ballroom">Winterland Ballroom</a>; the gig did not sell particularly well, and Bowie later commented that San Francisco did not need him, because "They've got Sylvester," referring to their shared preference for androgyny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200592_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200592-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1973, Sylvester and The Hot Band were signed by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Krasnow" title="Bob Krasnow">Bob Krasnow</a> to Blue Thumb. On this label, they produced their first album, in which they switched their sound from blues to the more commercially viable <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pointer_Sisters" title="Pointer Sisters">Pointer Sisters</a> were employed as backing singers. Sylvester named this first album <i>Scratch My Flower</i> due to a gardenia-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scratch-and-sniff" title="Scratch-and-sniff">scratch-and-sniff</a> sticker adhered to the cover, although it was instead released under the title of <i>Sylvester and his Hot Band</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200592.E2.80.9394Shapiro200576.E2.80.93.E2.80.9377Brogan201267Davis20157_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200592.E2.80.9394Shapiro200576.E2.80.93.E2.80.9377Brogan201267Davis20157-45">[45]</a></sup> The album consisted primarily of covers of songs by artists such as <a href="/wiki/James_Taylor" title="James Taylor">James Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leiber_and_Stoller" title="Leiber and Stoller">Leiber and Stoller</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200598Shapiro200576.E2.80.9377Davis20157_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200598Shapiro200576.E2.80.9377Davis20157-46">[46]</a></sup> Described by one of Sylvester's biographers as lacking in "the fire and focus of the live shows",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200598_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200598-47">[47]</a></sup> it sold poorly on release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005103_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005103-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvester and his Hot Band toured the United States, receiving threats of violence in several Southern states, where widespread conservative and racist attitudes led to antagonism between the band and locals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200599.E2.80.93100_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200599.E2.80.93100-49">[49]</a></sup> In late 1973, the band recorded their second album, <i>Bazaar</i>, which included both cover songs and original compositions by bassist Kerry Hatch. Hatch later commented that the Hot Band found the album more satisfactory than its predecessor, but nevertheless it again sold poorly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005101.E2.80.93102Davis20157.E2.80.938_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005101.E2.80.93102Davis20157.E2.80.938-50">[50]</a></sup> The music journalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Shapiro_(journalist)" title="Peter Shapiro (journalist)">Peter Shapiro</a> believed that on these Blue Thumb albums, Sylvester's "cottony falsetto was an uncomfortable match with guitars" and that they both had "an unpleasantly astringent quality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200577_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200577-51">[51]</a></sup> Finding Sylvester difficult to work with, and frustrated by his lack of commercial success, the Hot Band left Sylvester in late 1974, after which Krasnow cancelled his recording contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005103_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005103-48">[48]</a></sup> At the same time, Sylvester's relationship with Lyons ended, with Lyons himself moving to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005103.E2.80.93.E2.80.93104_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005103.E2.80.93.E2.80.93104-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_Tons_O.27_Fun_and_Sylvester:_1974.E2.80.9377">Two Tons O' Fun and <i>Sylvester</i>: 1974–77</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Two Tons O' Fun and Sylvester: 1974–77">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Now without the Hot Band or a recording contract, Sylvester set himself up with a new band, The Four As, and a new set of backing singers, two black drag queens named Jerry Kirby and Lady Bianca. With this new entourage, he continued to perform at a number of local venues including <a href="/wiki/Jewel%27s_Catch_One" title="Jewel's Catch One">Jewel's Catch One</a>, a bar in West Hollywood, but reviewers were unimpressed with the new line-up, most of whom abandoned Sylvester in December 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005104_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005104-53">[53]</a></sup> After a brief sojourn in England, Sylvester returned to San Francisco and assembled three young drag queens to be backing singers: Arnold Elzie, Leroy Davis, and Gerry Kirby. Nevertheless, although he performed at such events as the 1975 <a href="/wiki/Castro_Street_Fair" title="Castro Street Fair">Castro Street Fair</a>, success continued to elude him, and he eventually fired Elzie, Davis, and Kirby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005106.E2.80.93111_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005106.E2.80.93111-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Something clicked and sighed into place when Sylvester and the Tons got together – something that wasn't there with the Hot Band white boys, for all that they could cook; something that wasn't there with Peter Mintum, for all the beautiful oddness that he and Sylvester shared; something that wasn't even there with the black drag-queen singers, for all the fierceness they projected. Izora and Martha were whom he came from and who he was ... They were women who got their own. They sounded right with Sylvester, and looked just right, one on either side of him. Plus, next to them, Sylvester, who had grown quite round, looked positively svelte."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Biographer Joshua Gamson, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005118.E2.80.93119_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005118.E2.80.93119-55">[55]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Employing Brent Thomson as his new manager, she suggested that Sylvester rid himself of his androgynous image and wear more masculine clothing in order to gain a recording contract; as she put it, "nobody is giving out recording contracts to drag queens."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005113.E2.80.93114Davis201510.E2.80.9311_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005113.E2.80.93114Davis201510.E2.80.9311-56">[56]</a></sup> Thomson opened auditions for new backing singers, with Sylvester being captivated by one of those auditioning, <a href="/wiki/Martha_Wash" title="Martha Wash">Martha Wash</a>. Sylvester asked her if she had another large black friend who could sing, after which she introduced him to Izora Rhodes. Although he referred to them simply as "the girls", Wash and Rhodes named themselves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_Tons_O%27_Fun" title="Two Tons O' Fun">Two Tons O' Fun</a>, and continued to work with Sylvester intermittently until his death, developing a close friendship with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005115.E2.80.93118Davis201510.E2.80.9311_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005115.E2.80.93118Davis201510.E2.80.9311-57">[57]</a></sup> They were soon joined by bassist John Dunstan and keyboard player Dan Reich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005119_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005119-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Playing gay bars such as The Stud and The Endup, in September 1976 Sylvester and his band gained a regular weekend job at The Palms nightclub on Polk Street, performing two or three sets a night; most of these were covers, but some were original compositions by Sylvester and his then-guitarist Tip Wirrick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005120Davis201512_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005120Davis201512-59">[59]</a></sup> It was through this show that Sylvester came to the attention of <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> producer <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Fuqua" title="Harvey Fuqua">Harvey Fuqua</a>, and Fuqua subsequently signed Sylvester onto a solo deal with <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_Records" title="Fantasy Records">Fantasy Records</a> in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005121.E2.80.93123Brogan201268Davis201512_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005121.E2.80.93123Brogan201268Davis201512-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the middle of that year, he recorded his third album, the self-titled <i><a href="/wiki/Sylvester_(album)" title="Sylvester (album)">Sylvester</a></i>, which featured a cover design depicting Sylvester in male attire. The songs included on the album were influenced by dance music, and included Sylvester's own compositions, such as "Never Too Late," as well as covers of hits like <a href="/wiki/Ashford_%26_Simpson" title="Ashford &amp; Simpson">Ashford &amp; Simpson</a>'s "Over and Over." Many reviewers noted that Sylvester's image had been altered since his early career, moving him away from the glittery androgynous appearance to that of a more conventional rhythm-and-blues singer which would have wider commercial appeal. Released as a single, Sylvester's "Over and Over" proved a minor hit in the U.S., but was more successful in Mexico and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005123.E2.80.93125Davis201515.E2.80.9316_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005123.E2.80.93125Davis201515.E2.80.9316-61">[61]</a></sup> Building on the album's release, Sylvester toured <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005125_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005125-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_II_and_disco_success:_1978"><i>Step II</i> and disco success: 1978</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Step II and disco success: 1978">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castro_street_theatre.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Castro_street_theatre.JPG/220px-Castro_street_theatre.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Castro_street_theatre.JPG/330px-Castro_street_theatre.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Castro_street_theatre.JPG/440px-Castro_street_theatre.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castro_street_theatre.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
San Francisco's Castro neighborhood, where Sylvester regularly performed and socialized among the LGBT community. Biographer Joshua Gamson described him as Castro's "undisputed First Lady".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005169_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005169-63">[63]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sylvester's fame increased following the release of his solo album, and he was employed to perform regularly at The Elephant Walk gay bar in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Castro,_San_Francisco" title="The Castro, San Francisco">the Castro</a>, an area of San Francisco known as a <a href="/wiki/Gay_village" title="Gay village">gay village</a>. He became a friend of <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Milk" title="Harvey Milk">Harvey Milk</a>—known locally as the "Mayor of Castro Street"—who was <a href="/wiki/List_of_lesbian,_gay,_bisexual,_or_transgender_firsts_by_year" title="List of lesbian, gay, bisexual, or transgender firsts by year">the first openly gay man to be elected</a> to public office in California, and performed at Milk's birthday party that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005131.E2.80.93135Davis201517.E2.80.9318_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005131.E2.80.93135Davis201517.E2.80.9318-64">[64]</a></sup> In the spring of 1978, Sylvester successfully auditioned for a cameo appearance in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rose_(film)" title="The Rose (film)">The Rose</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Gay_icon" title="Gay icon">gay icon</a> <a href="/wiki/Bette_Midler" title="Bette Midler">Bette Midler</a>. In the film, he plays one of the drag queens singing along to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Seger" title="Bob Seger">Bob Seger</a>'s "Fire Down Below," in a single scene that was filmed in a run-down bar in downtown Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005135.E2.80.93136Davis201518.E2.80.9319_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005135.E2.80.93136Davis201518.E2.80.9319-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvester released his second solo album, <i>Step II</i>, in September 1978. For this release, he was particularly influenced by the genre of <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a> which was then becoming increasingly popular across the Western world. Disco was closely associated with the gay, black, and Latino communities in the U.S. and dominated by black female artists like <a href="/wiki/Donna_Summer" title="Donna Summer">Donna Summer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Gaynor" title="Gloria Gaynor">Gloria Gaynor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grace_Jones" title="Grace Jones">Grace Jones</a>, with Sylvester initially being unsure that it was a suitable genre for him to work in; he nevertheless recognized its increasing commercial potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005136.E2.80.93141.2C_142Davis201519.E2.80.9321.2C_39_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005136.E2.80.93141.2C_142Davis201519.E2.80.9321.2C_39-66">[66]</a></sup> During production of the album, Sylvester invited the musician <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Cowley" title="Patrick Cowley">Patrick Cowley</a> to join his studio band, being impressed by Cowley's innovative techniques using synthesizers. The album landed Cowley a job as a back-up musician on Sylvester's subsequent world-wide tours, and the two started a close friendship and collaboration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005144.E2.80.93145Davis201521_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005144.E2.80.93145Davis201521-67">[67]</a></sup> Once again co-produced by Harvey Fuqua and released on Fuqua's Fantasy label, <i>Step II</i> contained two disco songs that were subsequently released as singles, "<a href="/wiki/You_Make_Me_Feel_(Mighty_Real)" title="You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)">You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)</a>," written by James Wirrick, and "<a href="/wiki/Dance_(Disco_Heat)" title="Dance (Disco Heat)">Dance (Disco Heat)</a>," written by Eric Robinson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005142.E2.80.93145Shapiro200577.E2.80.9378Brogan201268Davis201523_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005142.E2.80.93145Shapiro200577.E2.80.9378Brogan201268Davis201523-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both singles proved commercial hits both domestically and abroad, topping the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Club_Play" title="Hot Dance Club Play">American dance chart</a> and breaking into the U.S. pop charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005148_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005148-69">[69]</a></sup> The album itself was also a success, being <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">certified gold</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005148.E2.80.93149Davis201540_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005148.E2.80.93149Davis201540-70">[70]</a></sup> and was described by <i>Rolling Stone</i> magazine as being "as good as disco gets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005148.E2.80.93149_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005148.E2.80.93149-71">[71]</a></sup> In his history of disco, Shapiro described "You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)" as Sylvester's "greatest record", "the cornerstone of gay disco", and "an epochal record in disco history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200578_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200578-72">[72]</a></sup> Shapiro noted that Sylvester's work brought together elements from both of the main strands of disco; the "gospel/R&amp;B tradition" and the "mechanical, piston-pumping beats" tradition, but that in doing so he went "way beyond either".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200576_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200576-3">[3]</a></sup> Shapiro expressed the view that "Sylvester propelled his falsetto far above his natural range into the ether and rode machine rhythms that raced toward escape velocity, creating a new sonic lexicon powerful, camp, and otherworldly enough to articulate the exquisite bliss of disco's dance floor utopia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200576_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200576-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In both August and December 1978, Sylvester visited <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> to promote his music; he proved hugely popular in the city, performing at a number of different nightclubs and being mobbed by fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005147Davis201524.E2.80.9327.2C_40.E2.80.9341_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005147Davis201524.E2.80.9327.2C_40.E2.80.9341-73">[73]</a></sup> It was while in the city that he filmed the <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for "You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201533.E2.80.9334_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201533.E2.80.9334-74">[74]</a></sup> Back in the U.S., Sylvester began to appear on television shows to advertise his music, appearing on <i><a href="/wiki/Dinah_Shore" title="Dinah Shore">Dinah Shore</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Bandstand" title="American Bandstand">American Bandstand</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_Concert" title="Rock Concert">Rock Concert</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Merv_Griffin_Show" title="The Merv Griffin Show">The Merv Griffin Show</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005149Davis201536_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005149Davis201536-75">[75]</a></sup> He also undertook a series of tours across the country, opening for both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Commodores" title="The Commodores">The Commodores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chaka_Khan" title="Chaka Khan">Chaka Khan</a>, and performing alongside <a href="/wiki/The_O%27Jays" title="The O'Jays">The O'Jays</a>, <a href="/wiki/War_(band)" title="War (band)">War</a>, and <a href="/wiki/L.T.D._(band)" title="L.T.D. (band)">L.T.D.</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005150.E2.80.93151Davis201536.E2.80.9337_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005150.E2.80.93151Davis201536.E2.80.9337-76">[76]</a></sup> As a result, he earned a number of awards and performed at several award ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005153_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005153-77">[77]</a></sup> Through this developing public presence, Sylvester, alongside other visibly <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a> performers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Village_People" title="The Village People">The Village People</a>, helped to solidify the connection between disco and homosexuality within the public imagination; this however furthered the anti-disco sentiment among rock music fans which would emerge as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disco_Sucks" title="Disco Sucks">Disco Sucks</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrank2007290_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrank2007290-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stars.2C_Sell_My_Soul.2C_and_Too_Hot_To_Sleep:_1979.E2.80.9381"><i>Stars</i>, <i>Sell My Soul</i>, and <i>Too Hot To Sleep</i>: 1979–81</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stars, Sell My Soul, and Too Hot To Sleep: 1979–81">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sylvester followed the success of <i>Step II</i> with an album entitled <i>Stars</i>. Consisting of four <a href="/wiki/Love_song" title="Love song">love songs</a>, the title track – released as a single in January 1979 – had been written by Cowley, and Sylvester would proceed to tell the press that it was his first completely disco album, but that it would also probably be his last.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005168.E2.80.93174_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005168.E2.80.93174-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200578Davis201543.2C_45.2C_46_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200578Davis201543.2C_45.2C_46-80">[80]</a></sup> He premiered the album's four tracks on March 11, 1979, at a sold-out show in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_War_Memorial_Opera_House" title="San Francisco War Memorial Opera House">San Francisco War Memorial Opera House</a>. The performance was attended by a number of senior figures in local government, and halfway through, Mayor <a href="/wiki/Dianne_Feinstein" title="Dianne Feinstein">Dianne Feinstein</a> sent her aide, Harry Britt, to award Sylvester with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Key_to_the_city" title="Key to the city">key to the city</a> and proclaim March 11 to be "Sylvester Day."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005168.E2.80.93174_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005168.E2.80.93174-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201551.E2.80.9352_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201551.E2.80.9352-81">[81]</a></sup> The Opera House gig was recorded, and subsequently released as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_album" title="Live album">live album</a>, <i>Living Proof</i>. Sylvester thought very highly of the album, but it did not sell well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005175Davis201552.E2.80.9353_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005175Davis201552.E2.80.9353-82">[82]</a></sup> A single released from this album, "Can't Stop Dancing", was a hit in the disco clubs but not in the pop music charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201553_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201553-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"I think the media have built this disco thing up out of all proportions in my case and so while people may come to see me on the pretence of seeing a disco show, they soon realise that there is so much more to me."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Sylvester on his move away from disco<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201554_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201554-84">[84]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Despite increasing mainstream success, Sylvester continued to reaffirm his connection to the gay community of San Francisco, performing at the main stage at the 1979 <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Pride" title="San Francisco Pride">Gay Freedom Day parade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005181_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005181-85">[85]</a></sup> Further, during his summer 1979 tour of the UK, he performed at the <a href="/wiki/Pride_London" title="Pride London">London Gay Pride Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201546.E2.80.9350_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201546.E2.80.9350-86">[86]</a></sup> That same year, Sylvester met the singer <a href="/wiki/Jeanie_Tracy" title="Jeanie Tracy">Jeanie Tracy</a> through Harvey Fuqua, and they immediately became friends. A large black woman, Sylvester felt that Tracy would work well with his Two Tons O' Fun, and invited her to join his backing singers, which she proceeded to do. Subsequently befriending the Tons, she would work for Sylvester for the rest of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005177.E2.80.93180Davis201543_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005177.E2.80.93180Davis201543-87">[87]</a></sup> The Tons themselves were convinced by Fuqua to produce their own self-titled album, from which came two dance chart hits, "Earth Can Be Just Like Heaven" and "Just Us"; as a result, they began to work less and less with Sylvester, only joining him on occasion for his live shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005196Davis201543_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005196Davis201543-88">[88]</a></sup> In some interviews he would express bitterness at their departure, while in others he stressed that he had no bad feelings toward them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201561.E2.80.9362_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201561.E2.80.9362-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, Sylvester also reached tabloid headlines after he was arrested on a visit to New York City, accused of being involved in the robbery of several rare coins. After three days of incarceration, he was released on a police bail of $30,000. Sylvester was never charged, and police later admitted their mistake after it was revealed that the real culprit had posed as Sylvester by signing cheques in his name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005188.E2.80.93190Davis201555.E2.80.9356_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005188.E2.80.93190Davis201555.E2.80.9356-90">[90]</a></sup> Returning to San Francisco after this event, it was here that Sylvester produced his next album for Fantasy Records, <i>Sell My Soul</i>. Largely avoiding disco after the genre had become unpopular following the much publicized Disco Sucks movement, <i>Sell My Soul</i> instead represented a selection of <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>-inspired dance tracks. Recorded in two weeks, Sylvester worked largely with backing singers and musicians whom he was unfamiliar with, and regular collaborators Rhodes and Cowley were entirely absent. Reviews were generally poor, describing the album as being average in quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005193Davis201557_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005193Davis201557-91">[91]</a></sup> The only disco song on the album, "I Need You", was released as a single, but fared poorly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201558_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201558-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvester's fifth and final album for Fantasy Records was <i>Too Hot to Sleep</i>, in which he once again eschewed disco for a series of groove soul tunes, ballads, and gospel-style tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005197Davis201562.E2.80.9363_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005197Davis201562.E2.80.9363-93">[93]</a></sup> Missing the Two Tons entirely, Tracy was instead accompanied by a new backing singer, Maurice "Mo" Long, and because the three of them had all grown up in the Church of God in Christ, they decided to refer to themselves as the "C.O.G.I.C. Singers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005197_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005197-94">[94]</a></sup> The album also featured a number of tracks in which Sylvester avoided his usual falsetto tones to sing in a <a href="/wiki/Baritone" title="Baritone">baritone</a> voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005197_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005197-94">[94]</a></sup> The album sold poorly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201563_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201563-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megatone_Records:_1982.E2.80.9386">Megatone Records: 1982–86</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Megatone Records: 1982–86">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Larger companies employ executives who sit behind their office desks, living off past achievements. We are small, we are free, and it's that freedom that makes me creative."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Sylvester on Megatone Records<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201563_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201563-95">[95]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Both the Two Tons and Sylvester came to suspect that Fantasy Records had failed to pay them all of the money that they were owed from the sale of their records. Sylvester left Fantasy and in November 1982 he filed a lawsuit against them; it ultimately proved successful in establishing that the company had been withholding money from him totaling $218,112.50. Nevertheless, Fuqua proved unable to pay anything more than $20,000, meaning that Sylvester never saw the majority of the money that was legally owed to him. Sylvester grew to despise Fuqua, and forbade his friends from ever mentioning his name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005210.E2.80.93212Davis201559_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005210.E2.80.93212Davis201559-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>Closely associated with the now unpopular disco and having had no hit singles in the preceding few years, after leaving Fantasy Sylvester was not a particular draw for major record labels. Recognizing this state of affairs, in 1982 Sylvester commented that "there's nothing worse than a fallen star" who still has "illusions" of their continuing fame. Rather than chasing major chart success, Sylvester wanted to focus on retaining creative control over his music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005213_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005213-97">[97]</a></sup> Hiring his former tour manager and longstanding friend Tim McKenna as his new manager, Sylvester decided to produce his next album with <a href="/wiki/Megatone_Records" title="Megatone Records">Megatone Records</a>, a small San Francisco company that had been founded in 1981 by Patrick Cowley and Marty Blecman and which catered largely to the gay club scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005212.E2.80.93213Davis201563_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005212.E2.80.93213Davis201563-98">[98]</a></sup> The result was <i>All I Need</i> (1982), on which James Wirrick had written most of the songs, which were dance-orientated and influenced by the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> music then in vogue. Sylvester insisted that he include several ballads on the album, which featured cover art by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Amerika" title="Mark Amerika">Mark Amerika</a> depicting Sylvester in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptian</a> garb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005212.E2.80.93214Davis201564_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005212.E2.80.93214Davis201564-99">[99]</a></sup></p>

The <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theatre" title="Castro Theatre">Castro Theatre</a> in San Francisco, where Sylvester performed his "One Night Only" retrospective</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the best known Sylvester songs of this period was "<a href="/wiki/Do_Ya_Wanna_Funk" title="Do Ya Wanna Funk">Do Ya Wanna Funk</a>", a <a href="/wiki/Hi-NRG" title="Hi-NRG">Hi-NRG</a> dance track co-written with Cowley which was released as a single in July 1982, topping the U.S. dance charts and entering the pop charts in a number of countries across the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005209Davis201564_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005209Davis201564-100">[100]</a></sup> Although he had continued working, Cowley was suffering from the recently discovered <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> virus – at the time still referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Gay-related_immune_deficiency" title="Gay-related immune deficiency">gay-related immune deficiency</a>" (GRID) by American doctors – and was in a deteriorating physical condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005208.E2.80.93209Davis201563_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005208.E2.80.93209Davis201563-101">[101]</a></sup> Sylvester continued touring, and it was while in London, preparing to perform at the <a href="/wiki/Heaven_(nightclub)" title="Heaven (nightclub)">Heaven</a> superclub, that he learned of Cowley's death on November 12, 1982. He went onstage, informing the crowd of Cowley's passing and then sang "Do Ya Wanna Funk" in memory of him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005217Davis201563.E2.80.9364_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005217Davis201563.E2.80.9364-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1983, Sylvester became a partner of Megatone Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201563_95-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201563-95">[95]</a></sup> That year he also brought out his second album with the company, <i>Call Me</i>, but it was not a commercial success. Four songs from the album were released as singles, although only "Trouble in Paradise" entered the top 20 of the U.S. dance charts; Sylvester later related that the song was his "AIDS message to San Francisco."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005230Davis201567_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005230Davis201567-103">[103]</a></sup> Sylvester was emotionally moved by the HIV/AIDS epidemic, and began helping out at the Rita Rockett Lounge for patients of the disease at the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_General_Hospital" title="San Francisco General Hospital">San Francisco General Hospital</a> as well as performing at various benefit concerts to raise money and awareness to combat the spread of the disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005231.E2.80.93232_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005231.E2.80.93232-104">[104]</a></sup> In February 1984 he also performed a "One Night Only" retrospective of his work at the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theatre" title="Castro Theatre">Castro Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005232.E2.80.93234Davis201570_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005232.E2.80.93234Davis201570-105">[105]</a></sup> Sylvester still toured both domestically and in Europe, although he found that demand for his performances was decreasing, and that he was now playing to smaller venues and singing to a pre-recorded tape rather than to a live band as he had in the late 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005235_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005235-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>His next album, entitled <i>M-1015</i> (1984), was more frenetic and pumping than his previous releases, having embraced the recently developed genre of Hi-NRG, but it also included elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electro_music" title="Electro music">electro</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rap" title="Rap">rap</a>. The major figures behind the album had been Kessie and Morey Goldstein, and Sylvester himself had not written any of the tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005238.E2.80.93240Davis201571.E2.80.9372_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005238.E2.80.93240Davis201571.E2.80.9372-107">[107]</a></sup> The album also contained increasingly sexually explicit lyrics, in particular in the songs "How Do You Like Your Love" and "Sex".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201573_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201573-108">[108]</a></sup> That year, he also entered into a relationship with an architect named Rick Cramner, and together they moved into a new apartment in the hills, where Sylvester decorated his powder room with posters and memorabilia of <a href="/wiki/Divine_(performer)" title="Divine (performer)">Divine</a>, the drag queen, actor and singer whom he had briefly known when they were in The Cockettes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005241.E2.80.93242_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005241.E2.80.93242-109">[109]</a></sup> In 1985, he fulfilled a lifelong ambition by working with the singer Aretha Franklin; he and Jeanie had been invited to provide backing vocals on Franklin's album <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Zoomin%27_Who%3F" title="Who's Zoomin' Who?">Who's Zoomin' Who?</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201574_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201574-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvester's final album, <i>Mutual Attraction</i> (1986), was produced by Megatone but licensed and released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Bros" title="Warner Bros">Warner Bros</a>. On the album, Sylvester had worked with a wide number of collaborators, and included new tracks alongside covers of songs by <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Gershwin" title="George Gershwin">George Gershwin</a>. Reviews of the album were mixed, with many claiming that it was a poor release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005244.E2.80.93245Davis201575_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005244.E2.80.93245Davis201575-111">[111]</a></sup> One of the album's singles, "Someone Like You", proved more successful, reaching number one on the <i>Billboard</i> dance charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005244.E2.80.93245Davis201575_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005244.E2.80.93245Davis201575-111">[111]</a></sup> Warner Bros booked him to appear on the <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">New Year's Eve</a> edition of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Late_Show_Starring_Joan_Rivers" title="The Late Show Starring Joan Rivers">The Late Show Starring Joan Rivers</a></i>, during which <a href="/wiki/Joan_Rivers" title="Joan Rivers">Joan Rivers</a> described him as a drag queen; visibly annoyed, he corrected her by stating that he was not a drag queen, proclaiming simply "I'm Sylvester!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005245.E2.80.93246Brogan201268_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005245.E2.80.93246Brogan201268-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years_and_death:_1986.E2.80.9388">Final years and death: 1986–88</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Final years and death: 1986–88">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1985, Sylvester's boyfriend, Rick Cranmer, became aware that he had become infected with HIV; with no known medical cure, his health deteriorated rapidly and he died in September 1987. Sylvester was devastated, and although recognizing that he too was probably infected, he refused to have his blood tested, only noticing the virus' first symptoms when he developed a persistent cough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005249.E2.80.93250Davis201577_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005249.E2.80.93250Davis201577-113">[113]</a></sup> Beginning work on an album that would remain unfinished, he moved into a new apartment on Collingwood Street in the Castro, and tried his best to continue performing in the Bay Area, even though he became too sick to undertake a full tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005252.E2.80.93253_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005252.E2.80.93253-114">[114]</a></sup> Eventually diagnosed with AIDS, he was hospitalized for sinus surgery in late 1987, and upon returning to his apartment he began to be cared for by his mother and Tracy, before being hospitalized again in May 1988, this time with <a href="/wiki/Pneumocystis_pneumonia" title="Pneumocystis pneumonia">pneumocystis pneumonia</a>. Returning to his flat, he gave away many of his treasured possessions and wrote his will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005254.E2.80.93255.2C_256Brogan201268.E2.80.9369Davis201577_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005254.E2.80.93255.2C_256Brogan201268.E2.80.9369Davis201577-115">[115]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having lost a lot of weight and unable to walk easily, he attended the Castro's 1988 Gay Freedom Parade in a wheelchair, being pushed along by Mckenna in front of the People with AIDS banner; along Market Street, assembled crowds shouted out his name as he passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005257.E2.80.93258Brogan201268.E2.80.9369Davis201577_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005257.E2.80.93258Brogan201268.E2.80.9369Davis201577-116">[116]</a></sup> The subsequent 1988 Castro Street Fair was named "A Tribute to Sylvester," and although he was too ill to attend, crowds chanted his name to such an extent that he was able to hear them from his bedroom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005260Brogan201268.E2.80.9369Davis201577_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005260Brogan201268.E2.80.9369Davis201577-117">[117]</a></sup> He continued to give interviews to the media, being open about the fact that he was dying of AIDS, and sought in particular to highlight the impact that the disease was having in the African-American community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005259Davis201577_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005259Davis201577-118">[118]</a></sup> In an interview with the <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i>, he stated, "I don't believe that AIDS is the wrath of God. People have a tendency to blame everything on God."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_Rock_.27N.27_Roll_Years_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-NME_Rock_.27N.27_Roll_Years-119">[119]</a></sup></p>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">Thanksgiving</a> 1988, his family spent the holiday with him, although he had developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuropathy" title="Neuropathy">neuropathy</a> and was increasingly bed-ridden and reliant on <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>; he died in his bed on December 16, 1988 at the age of 41.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005264.2C_266.E2.80.93267Davis201577_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005264.2C_266.E2.80.93267Davis201577-120">[120]</a></sup> Sylvester had planned his own funeral, insisting that he be dressed in a red <a href="/wiki/Kimono" title="Kimono">kimono</a> and placed in an open-top coffin for the mourners to see, with his friend <a href="/wiki/Yvette_Flunder" title="Yvette Flunder">Yvette Flunder</a> doing his corpse's makeup. He wanted Tracy to sing at his funeral, accompanied by choirs and many flowers. The whole affair took place in his church, the Love Center, with a sermon being provided by Reverend Walter Hawkins. The event was packed, with standing room only, and the coffin was subsequently taken and buried at his family's plot in <a href="/wiki/Inglewood_Park_Cemetery" title="Inglewood Park Cemetery">Inglewood Park Cemetery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005268.E2.80.93272Davis201579_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005268.E2.80.93272Davis201579-121">[121]</a></sup> An album titled <i>Immortal</i> was posthumously released; it contained Sylvester's final studio recordings, and was compiled by Marty Blecman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201579.E2.80.9380_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201579.E2.80.9380-122">[122]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sylvester has been described as having a "flamboyant and colourful" public persona,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201539_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201539-123">[123]</a></sup> wearing both male and female gendered clothes as part of his attire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201560_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201560-124">[124]</a></sup> with his biographer <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Gamson" title="Joshua Gamson">Joshua Gamson</a> opining that for Sylvester, "gender was an everyday choice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005159_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005159-125">[125]</a></sup> Sylvester described his public persona as "an extension of me, the real me".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201542_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201542-126">[126]</a></sup> Sylvester's friend and publicist Sharon Davis described him as "a quiet, often thoughtful, caring guy, who put others before himself, and was generous to a fault, having little regard for money. His policy was you only live once, so enjoy!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xvi_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xvi-127">[127]</a></sup> She also noted that he could be "unpredictable", being "stubborn as a mule" and "always speak[ing] his mind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xv.2C_xvi_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xv.2C_xvi-128">[128]</a></sup> Sylvester was considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Prima_donna" title="Prima donna">prima donna</a> by members of the Hot Band and could be temperamental and difficult with those whom he worked with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson200598_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson200598-47">[47]</a></sup> He found it difficult saving the money that he earned, instead spending it as soon as he obtained it, both on himself and on his lovers, friends, and family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005234_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005234-129">[129]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Perhaps Sylvester suffered the curse of the control queen and the narcissist: he chose controllable and adoring partners, only to find out that they were not self-possessed enough to meet him where he wanted to be met. But his was also the curse of the generous and gentle-hearted. He used the nurturing talents that Letha and JuJu gave him, teaching people ... that they, too, were loved and fabulous. When he succeeded, they were ready to go out on their own. You ask one person and he'll say that Sylvester just wanted a worshipful housewife; ask another and he'll tell you that Sylvester wanted to give his soul away to someone extraordinary enough to handle it. They are both right, no doubt."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Biographer Joshua Gamson, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005201_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005201-130">[130]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Sylvester was openly gay, with Gamson noting that he tended to enter into relationships with men who were "white, self-doubting and effeminate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005199_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005199-131">[131]</a></sup> In 1978, he entered into a relationship with a young white model named John Maley; Sylvester later devoted the song "Can't Forget the Love" from his <i>Too Hot to Sleep</i> album to his young lover. Maley ended the relationship to move to Los Angeles, later recollecting that Sylvester "was a lovely man, and I owe him a lot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005153.2C_199_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005153.2C_199-132">[132]</a></sup> In 1981, Sylvester entered into a relationship with a slim brunette from <a href="/wiki/Deep_River,_Connecticut" title="Deep River, Connecticut">Deep River, Connecticut</a> named Michael Rayner, but unlike his predecessors, he did not move into Sylvester's house; their partnership ended when Rayner admitted that he had not fallen completely in love with Sylvester.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005199.E2.80.93201_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005199.E2.80.93201-133">[133]</a></sup> Sylvester's next major relationship was with Tom Daniels, a hairdresser whom he met in 1982, but their romance ended after six months when Daniels discovered that Sylvester had been having sex with other men while on tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005219.E2.80.93220_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005219.E2.80.93220-134">[134]</a></sup> The singer's final partner, the architect Rick Cranmer, was a six-foot two blonde, and the duo moved into a house together in the hills. Cranmer died of AIDS-related complications in 1987, the year before Sylvester succumbed to the virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005241.E2.80.93242_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005241.E2.80.93242-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an openly gay man throughout his career, Sylvester came to be seen as a spokesman for the gay community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005221_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005221-135">[135]</a></sup> He informed a journalist that "I realise that gay people have put me on a pedestal and I love it. After all, of all the oppressed minorities, they just have to be the most oppressed. They have all the hassles of finding something or someone to identify with – and they chose me. I like being around gay people and they've proven to be some of my closest friends and most loyal audiences."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201539.E2.80.9340_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201539.E2.80.9340-136">[136]</a></sup> Elsewhere, he nevertheless remarked that he felt his career had "transcended the gay movement. I mean, my sexuality has nothing to do with my music. When I'm fucking I'm not thinking about singing and vice versa."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005221_135-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005221-135">[135]</a></sup> He was openly critical of what he perceived as divisive tendencies within the gay community itself, noting that "I get this conformist shit from queens all the time. They always want to read me. They always want me to do it their way. I am not going to conform to the gay lifestyle as they see it and that's for sure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005221_135-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005221-135">[135]</a></sup> He was particularly critical of "<a href="/wiki/Castro_clone" title="Castro clone">clones</a>" – gay men who dressed alike with boots, boot-cut jeans, checked shirts and handlebar mustaches – stating that all too often they judged those gay people who were flamboyant or extravagant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005222_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005222-137">[137]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis characterized Sylvester as an "absolute perfectionist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xvi_127-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xvi-127">[127]</a></sup> He was very self-conscious about his physical appearance, and when he obtained enough money from the successful <i>Step II</i> album, he spent part of it on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmetic_surgery" title="Cosmetic surgery">cosmetic surgery</a> to remove a bump on his nose, inject <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a> into his cheeks, and have cosmetic work done on his teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005154_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005154-138">[138]</a></sup> He would also insist that all pictures of himself were meticulously <a href="/wiki/Airbrush" title="Airbrush">airbrushed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005191.E2.80.93192_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005191.E2.80.93192-139">[139]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvester was born and raised into the Pentecostal denomination of Christianity, and remained a Christian throughout his life. He often compared the ecstatic feelings that accompanied his onstage performances with the feelings experienced in a gospel choir in a Pentecostal church. When performances reached a certain level of heightened emotion, he would comment that "we had service."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005166.E2.80.93167_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005166.E2.80.93167-140">[140]</a></sup> In later life, he joined the Love Center Church in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Oakland" title="East Oakland">East Oakland</a>, a ministry founded by the preacher and former gospel singer <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hawkins" title="Walter Hawkins">Walter Hawkins</a> in the 1970s. He had been introduced to the church by Jean Tracie in the 1980s and would soon become a regular churchgoer, enjoying the place's welcoming attitude towards societal outcasts. Sylvester requested that his funeral be undertaken by the ministry at the Love Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005225.E2.80.93227_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005225.E2.80.93227-141">[141]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"If there was one artist ... who truly exemplified disco's new language of ecstasy it was Sylvester, whose use of his gospel trained falsetto in the service of gay desire and pleasure is surely the most radical rewrite of pop's lingua franca ever attempted."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">— Peter Shapiro, 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro200575_142-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro200575-142">[142]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>During the late 1970s, Sylvester gained the moniker of the "Queen of Disco",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> a term that continued to be given to the singer into the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrogan201266_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrogan201266-144">[144]</a></sup> The English journalist Stephen Brogan later described him as "a star who shined brightly. He only happened once. He was a radical and a visionary in terms of queerness, music and race."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269_145-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269-145">[145]</a></sup> Reynaldo Anderson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harris-Stowe_State_University" title="Harris-Stowe State University">Harris-Stowe State University</a> described Sylvester's influence upon disco and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a> as "incalculable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson2013_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson2013-146">[146]</a></sup> He added that Sylvester's songs "Dance (Disco Heat)", "You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)", and "Do You Wanna Funk" represented "anthems of disco aficionados for a generation", while also expressing the view that Sylvester himself "personified the excesses of the 1970s and the experimentation that characterized [the decade's] changing social norms" within the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson2013_146-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson2013-146">[146]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shapiro cited Sylvester alongside other artists like <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Carlos" title="Wendy Carlos">Wendy Carlos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Throbbing_Gristle" title="Throbbing Gristle">Throbbing Gristle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terre_Thaemlitz" title="Terre Thaemlitz">Terre Thaemlitz</a> as an individual who used electronic music as "a vehicle to express sexual transgression",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapiro2005111_147-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapiro2005111-147">[147]</a></sup> while in her study of the use of falsetto in disco, Anne-Lise François believed that Sylvester's style of singing "makes the point most obviously about falsetto as a gender-bending device."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFran.C3.A7ois1995446_148-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFran.C3.A7ois1995446-148">[148]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> scholar Tim Lawrence stated that Sylvester embodied "the [disco] movement's gay roots", and in doing could be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">John Travolta</a>, who embodied "its commercialization and suburbanization"; the two figures thus reflected a divide between the gay and straight interpretations and presentations of disco music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELawrence2006144_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELawrence2006144-149">[149]</a></sup> Layli Philips and Marla R. Stewart compared Sylvester to both <a href="/wiki/Willi_Ninja" title="Willi Ninja">Willi Ninja</a> and <a href="/wiki/RuPaul" title="RuPaul">RuPaul</a> as pop icons who exhibited "male femininity" within the "Black male diva (or 'queen') tradition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPhilipsStewart201026_150-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPhilipsStewart201026-150">[150]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his will, Sylvester had declared that royalties from the future sale of this music be devoted to two HIV/AIDS charities, <a href="/wiki/Project_Open_Hand" title="Project Open Hand">Project Open Hand</a> and the AIDS Emergency Fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis201579_151-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis201579-151">[151]</a></sup> Although Sylvester died deeply in debt as a result of taking advances on his royalties, by the early 1990s this debt had been paid off, and a balance had begun to build up. Roger Gross, the attorney to Sylvester's manager and the openly gay lawyer who helped him draw up his will, petitioned the probate court to designate the charities as the beneficiaries of Sylvester's will. The proceeds of $140,000 in accrued royalties were split between the two groups, and they will continue to be paid the royalties in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 19, 2005, Sylvester was one of three artists inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Dance_Music_Hall_of_Fame" title="Dance Music Hall of Fame">Dance Music Hall of Fame</a>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Chic" title="Chic">Chic</a> and Gloria Gaynor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOritz201184_153-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOritz201184-153">[153]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> In December 2016, <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard magazine</a></i> ranked him as the 59th most successful dance artist of all-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biographies.2C_documentaries.2C_and_musicals">Biographies, documentaries, and musicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvester_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Biographies, documentaries, and musicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A biography of Sylvester was authored by Gamson and published in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamson2005_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamson2005-156">[156]</a></sup> Writing for the London-based LGBT magazine <i>Beige: The Provocative Cultural Quarterly</i>, Stephen Brogan expressed his opinion that while Gamson's biography was well researched, it had a fragmented structure and as such was "not a joy to read".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269_145-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269-145">[145]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> called the book "playful and furious" and awarded it a B+ rating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> suggested that it was "as engaging as the times it so energetically resurrects",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup> and <i>The San Francisco Chronicle</i> reported that the author "carefully paints the shifting social tapestry into his subject's life story without ever taking Sylvester out of the foreground".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> <i>The Fabulous Sylvester</i> won the 2006 Stonewall Book Award for nonfiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup> In 2015, Sylvester's publicist Sharon Davis published memoirs of the time that she spent with Sylvester, noting that she planned for it to appear in 2013 to mark the 25th anniversary of Sylvester's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xvi_127-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis2015xvi-127">[127]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Unsung_(TV_series)" title="Unsung (TV series)">Unsung</a></i> aired an episode on Sylvester, that was later made available through <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269_145-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269-145">[145]</a></sup> <i>Sylvester: Mighty Real</i>, an official feature-length documentary on the life and career of Sylvester, entered production; it featured interviews with members of Sylvester's family and other artists and musicians who have been inspired by, but by 2012 the film's progress had halted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269_145-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrogan201269-145">[145]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2014, an <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> musical titled <i>Mighty Real: A Fabulous Sylvester Musical</i> opened at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theatre_At_St._Clement%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theatre At St. Clement's (page does not exist)">Theatre At St. Clement's</a> in New York City; it was co-directed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kendrell_Bowman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kendrell Bowman (page does not exist)">Kendrell Bowman</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_Wayne_(actor)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anthony Wayne (actor) (page does not exist)">Anthony Wayne</a>, the latter of whom also performed as the titular character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Portwood_162-0"><a href="#cite_note-Portwood-162">[162]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_163-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-163">[163]</a></sup> Wayne stated that he discovered Sylvester's story through a television documentary, and was subsequently "inspired by his drive to be who he was regardless of what he went through", performing a concert of Sylvester's songs with friends Anastacia McCleskey and Jacqueline B. Arnold as the Two Tons o' Fun before deciding to begin work on the musical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Portwood_162-1"><a href="#cite_note-Portwood-162">[162]</a></sup> A laudatory review of the musical from <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> noted that Wayne "certainly has the bravado, the androgynous sex appeal and the piercing voice to emulate the original convincingly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_163-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-163">[163]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> review noted that the musical largely avoided dealing with the decline in Sylvester's musical success during the 1980s, and that although " anyone seeking an exhaustively researched play-by-play of the star's life would be better off waiting for a documentary", the musical "succeeds as a collection of infectious performances by a truly gifted cast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup></p>
