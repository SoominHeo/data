<p><b>Real-time strategy</b> (<b>RTS</b>) is a subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Strategy_video_game" title="Strategy video game">strategy video games</a> where the game does not progress incrementally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn-based_game" title="Turn-based game">turns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-c-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an RTS, the participants position and maneuver units and structures under their control to secure areas of the map and/or destroy their opponents' assets. In a typical RTS, it is possible to create additional units and structures during the course of a game. This is generally limited by a requirement to <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">expend accumulated resources</a>. These resources are in turn garnered by controlling special points on the map and/or possessing certain types of units and structures devoted to this purpose. More specifically, the typical game of the RTS genre features <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_gathering" title="Resource gathering">resource gathering</a>, base building, in-game technological development and indirect control of units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bgeryk_gspot_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bgeryk_gspot-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignstate_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ignstate-3">[3]</a></sup> The term "real-time strategy" first appeared in <i>BYTE</i> magazine in 1982,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sartoriangus198212_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sartoriangus198212-4">[4]</a></sup> but American video game designer <a href="/wiki/Brett_Sperry" title="Brett Sperry">Brett Sperry</a> is usually credited with coining the term to market <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_II" title="Dune II">Dune II</a></i> in the early 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-b-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tasks a player must perform to succeed at an RTS can be very demanding, and complex user interfaces have evolved to cope with the challenge. Some features have been borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Desktop_environment" title="Desktop environment">desktop environments</a>; for example, the technique of "clicking and dragging" to select all units under a given area. Though some game genres share conceptual and gameplay similarities with the RTS template, recognized genres are generally not subsumed as RTS games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignstate_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ignstate-3">[3]</a></sup> For instance, <a href="/wiki/City-building_game" title="City-building game">city-building games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction_and_management_simulation" title="Construction and management simulation">construction and management simulations</a>, and games of the <a href="/wiki/Real-time_tactics" title="Real-time tactics">real-time tactics</a> variety are generally not considered to be "real-time strategy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The genre that is recognized today as "real-time strategy" emerged as a result of an extended period of evolution and refinement. Games that are today sometimes perceived as ancestors of the real-time strategy genre were never marketed or designed as such at the original date of publication. As a result, designating "early real-time strategy" titles is problematic because such games are being held up to modern standards. The genre initially evolved separately in the United Kingdom, Japan, and North America, afterward gradually merging into a unified worldwide tradition.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Turn-based_strategy" title="Turn-based strategy">turn-based strategy</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia_(video_game)" title="Utopia (video game)">Utopia</a></i> (1981), which featured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timed_turns" title="Timed turns">timed turns</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dani_Bunten_Berry" title="Dani Bunten Berry">Dani Bunten Berry</a>'s (of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.U.L.E" title="M.U.L.E">M.U.L.E</a></i> fame) <a href="/wiki/Real-time_tactics" title="Real-time tactics">real-time tactics</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Cytron_Masters" title="Cytron Masters">Cytron Masters</a></i> (1982), developed by <a href="/wiki/Ozark_Softscape" title="Ozark Softscape">Ozark Softscape</a> and released by <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Simulations" title="Strategic Simulations">SSI</a>, are considered precursors of the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-matt_barton_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-matt_barton-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dani_memoirs_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-dani_memoirs-9">[9]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BYTE" title="BYTE">BYTE</a></i> in December 1982 published as an <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> <a href="/wiki/Type-in_program" title="Type-in program">type-in program</a> <i>Cosmic Conquest</i>. The winner of the magazine's annual Game Contest, the author described it as a "single-player game of real-time action and strategic decision making". The magazine described it as "a real-time space strategy game". The game has elements of resource management and wargaming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sartoriangus198212_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sartoriangus198212-4">[4]</a></sup> Another 1982 example is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legionnaire_(computer_game)" title="Legionnaire (computer game)">Legionnaire</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_family" title="Atari 8-bit family">Atari 8-bit family</a>, written by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Crawford_(game_designer)" title="Chris Crawford (game designer)">Chris Crawford</a> for <a href="/wiki/Avalon_Hill" title="Avalon Hill">Avalon Hill</a>. This was effectively the opposite of <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia_(video_game)" title="Utopia (video game)">Utopia</a></i> (1981), in that it offered a complete real-time tactical combat system with variable terrain and mutual-help concepts, but lacked any resource collection and economy/production concepts.</p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the genre's roots can be traced to <i><a href="/wiki/Stonkers" title="Stonkers">Stonkers</a></i> by John Gibson, published in 1983 by <a href="/wiki/Imagine_Software" title="Imagine Software">Imagine Software</a> for the <a href="/wiki/ZX_Spectrum" title="ZX Spectrum">ZX Spectrum</a>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nether_Earth_(game)" title="Nether Earth (game)">Nether Earth</a></i> published on ZX Spectrum in 1987. In North America, the oldest game retrospectively classified as real-time strategy by several sources<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignstate_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ignstate-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rakrent_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-rakrent-10">[10]</a></sup> is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ancient_Art_of_War" title="The Ancient Art of War">The Ancient Art of War</a></i> (1984), designed by <a href="/wiki/Evryware" title="Evryware">Evryware</a>'s Dave and Barry Murry, followed by the sequel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ancient_Art_of_War_at_Sea" title="The Ancient Art of War at Sea">The Ancient Art of War at Sea</a></i> in 1987. Scott Sharkey of 1UP, however, considers <i>Cytron Masters</i> and other real-time examples prior to <i>Herzog Zwei</i> to be tactical rather than strategic, due to most lacking the ability to construct units or manage resources, and that the slower processors and modems "made for ticks so long that the games were practically turn based."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP-Duke_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-Duke-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Japan, the genre's roots can be traced to <i><a href="/wiki/Bokosuka_Wars" title="Bokosuka Wars">Bokosuka Wars</a></i> (1983), an early <a href="/wiki/Tactical_role-playing_game" title="Tactical role-playing game">strategy RPG</a> (or "simulation RPG");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nintendo_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nintendo-12">[12]</a></sup> the game revolves around the player leading an army across a battlefield against enemy forces in real-time while recruiting/spawning soldiers along the way, for which it is considered by Ray Barnholt of <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a> to be an early prototype real-time strategy game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP-Druaga_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-Druaga-13">[13]</a></sup> This led to several other games that combine <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing</a> and real-time strategy elements, such as the 1988 <a href="/wiki/Kure_Software_Koubou" title="Kure Software Koubou">Kure Software Koubou</a> computer strategy RPGs, <i><a href="/wiki/First_Queen" title="First Queen">First Queen</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuresoft_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kuresoft-14">[14]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kure_Software_Koubou#Silver_Ghost" title="Kure Software Koubou">Silver Ghost</a></i>, which featured an early example of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point-and-click" title="Point-and-click">point-and-click</a> interface, to control characters using a cursor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Another early title with real-time strategy elements was <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gain_Ground" title="Gain Ground">Gain Ground</a></i> (1988), a strategy-<a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action game</a> that involved directing a set of troops across various enemy-filled levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TechnoSoft" title="TechnoSoft">TechnoSoft</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Herzog_(video_game)" title="Herzog (video game)">Herzog</a></i> (1988) is regarded as a precursor to the real-time strategy genre, being the predecessor to <i>Herzog Zwei</i> and somewhat similar in nature, though primitive in comparison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Herzog_Zwei" title="Herzog Zwei">Herzog Zwei</a></i>, released for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mega_Drive" title="Mega Drive">Sega Mega Drive/Genesis</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_console" title="Home console">home console</a> in 1989, is the earliest example of a game with a feature set that falls under the contemporary definition of modern real-time strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hzweifirstrts_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-hzweifirstrts-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zzap_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-zzap-20">[20]</a></sup> The game combined traditional strategy gameplay with fully real-time, fast-paced, <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action gameplay</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sega-16_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sega-16-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cvg_zwei_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cvg_zwei-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamesmachine_zwei_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamesmachine_zwei-23">[23]</a></sup> featuring a <a href="/wiki/Split_screen_(computer_graphics)" title="Split screen (computer graphics)">split-screen</a> <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_video_game" title="Multiplayer video game">two-player</a> mode where both players are in action simultaneously and there are no pauses while decisions are taken, forcing players to think quickly while on the move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cvg_zwei_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-cvg_zwei-22">[22]</a></sup> In <i>Herzog Zwei</i>, though the player only controls one unit, the manner of control foreshadowed the point-and-click mechanic of later games. It introduced much of the genre conventions, including unit construction and resource management, with the control and destruction of bases being an important aspect of the game, as were the economic/production aspects of those bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP-Duke_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-Duke-11">[11]</a></sup> The game has been credited as a key influence on the creation of <i>Dune II</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_rts_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-origin_rts-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edge_2008_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edge_2008-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable as well are early games like <i><a href="/wiki/Mega_Lo_Mania" title="Mega Lo Mania">Mega Lo Mania</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Sensible_Software" title="Sensible Software">Sensible Software</a> (1991) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overlord_(1990_computer_game)" title="Overlord (1990 computer game)">Supremacy</a></i> (also called <i>Overlord</i> – 1990). Although these two lacked direct control of military units, they both offered considerable control of resource management and economic systems. In addition, <i>Mega Lo Mania</i> has advanced <a href="/wiki/Technology_tree" title="Technology tree">technology trees</a> that determine offensive and defensive prowess. Another early (1988) game, <i><a href="/wiki/Carrier_Command" title="Carrier Command">Carrier Command</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Realtime_Games_Software" title="Realtime Games Software">Realtime Games</a>, involved real-time responses to events in the game, requiring management of resources and control of vehicles. The early game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sim_Ant" title="Sim Ant">Sim Ant</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Maxis" title="Maxis">Maxis</a> (1991) had resource gathering, and controlling an attacking army by having them follow a lead unit. However, it was with the release of <i>Dune II</i> from <a href="/wiki/Westwood_Studios" title="Westwood Studios">Westwood Studios</a> (1992) that real-time strategy became recognized as a distinct genre of video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bgeryk_gspot_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-bgeryk_gspot-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992.E2.80.931998:_seminal_titles">1992–1998: seminal titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1992–1998: seminal titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although real-time strategy games have an extensive history, some titles have served to define the popular perception of the genre and expectations of real-time strategy titles more than others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bgeryk_gspot_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-bgeryk_gspot-2">[2]</a></sup> in particular the games released between 1992 and 1998 by <a href="/wiki/Westwood_Studios" title="Westwood Studios">Westwood Studios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blizzard_Entertainment" title="Blizzard Entertainment">Blizzard Entertainment</a>.</p>
<p>Westwood's <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_II" title="Dune II">Dune II: The Building of a Dynasty</a></i> (1992) featured all the core concepts and mechanics of modern real-time strategy games that are still used today,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspystrategy1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-gspystrategy1-27">[27]</a></sup> such as using the mouse to move units, and gathering resources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignstate_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ignstate-3">[3]</a></sup> and as such served as the prototype for later real-time strategy games. According to its co-designer and lead programmer, Joe Bostic, a "benefit over <i>Herzog Zwei</i> is that we had the advantage of a mouse and keyboard. This greatly facilitated precise player control, which enabled the player to give orders to individual units. The mouse, and the direct control it allowed, was critical in making the RTS genre possible.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edge_2008_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edge_2008-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The success of <i>Dune II</i> encouraged several games which became influential in their own right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignstate_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ignstate-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspystrategy1_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-gspystrategy1-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft:_Orcs_%26_Humans" title="Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans">Warcraft: Orcs &amp; Humans</a></i> (1994) achieved great prominence upon its release, owing in part to its use of a fantasy setting and also to its depiction of a wide variety of buildings (such as farms) which approximated a full fictitious society, not just a military force.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer_(1995_video_game)" title="Command &amp; Conquer (1995 video game)">Command &amp; Conquer</a></i>, as well as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_and_Conquer:_Red_Alert" title="Command and Conquer: Red Alert">Command and Conquer: Red Alert</a></i>, became the most popular early RTS games. These two games contended with <i><a href="/wiki/Warcraft_II:_Tides_of_Darkness" title="Warcraft II: Tides of Darkness">Warcraft II: Tides of Darkness</a></i> after its release in late 1995.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Total_Annihilation" title="Total Annihilation">Total Annihilation</a></i>, released by <a href="/wiki/Cavedog_Entertainment" title="Cavedog Entertainment">Cavedog Entertainment</a> in 1997, introduced 3D units and terrain and focused on huge battles that emphasized macromanagement over micromanagement. It featured a streamlined interface that would influence many RTS games in later years. <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Empires_(video_game)" title="Age of Empires (video game)">Age of Empires</a></i>, released by <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_Studios" title="Ensemble Studios">Ensemble Studios</a> in 1997 tried to put a game in a slower pace, combining elements of <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_(series)" title="Civilization (series)">Civilization</a></i> with the real-time strategy concept by introducing ages of technologies. In 1998, Blizzard released the game <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i>, which became an international phenomenon and is still played in large professional leagues to this day. Collectively, all of these games defined the genre, providing the <i>de facto</i> benchmark against which new real-time strategy games are measured.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refinement_and_transition_to_3D">Refinement and transition to 3D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Refinement and transition to 3D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The real-time strategy genre has been relatively stable since 1995. Additions to the genre's concept in newer games tend to emphasize more of the basic RTS elements (higher unit caps, more unit types, larger maps, etc.). Rather than innovations to the game concept, new games generally focus on refining aspects of successful predecessors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As the paragon example of gameplay refinement, Cavedog's acclaimed <i>Total Annihilation</i> from 1997 distilled the core mechanics of <i>Command &amp; Conquer</i>, and introduced the first 3D units and terrain in real-time strategy games. The Age of Empires idea was refined further by <a href="/wiki/Stainless_Steel_Studios" title="Stainless Steel Studios">Stainless Steel Studios</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_Earth_(video_game)" title="Empire Earth (video game)">Empire Earth</a></i> in 2001. <a href="/wiki/GSC_Game_World" title="GSC Game World">GSC Game World</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Cossacks:_European_Wars" title="Cossacks: European Wars">Cossacks: European Wars</a></i> series took the genre in a different direction, bringing population caps into the tens of thousands.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Keeper" title="Dungeon Keeper">Dungeon Keeper</a></i> (1997), <i><a href="/wiki/Populous:_The_Beginning" title="Populous: The Beginning">Populous: The Beginning</a></i> (1998), <i><a href="/wiki/Jeff_Wayne%27s_The_War_of_the_Worlds_(1998_video_game)" title="Jeff Wayne's The War of the Worlds (1998 video game)">Jeff Wayne's The War of the Worlds</a></i> (1998), <i><a href="/wiki/Warzone_2100" title="Warzone 2100">Warzone 2100</a></i> (1999), <i><a href="/wiki/Machines_(video_game)" title="Machines (video game)">Machines</a></i> (1999), <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld" title="Homeworld">Homeworld</a></i> (1999), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Honour_%26_Freedom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Honour &amp; Freedom (page does not exist)">Honour &amp; Freedom</a></i> (1999), and <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Reign_2" title="Dark Reign 2">Dark Reign 2</a></i> (2000) were among the first completely 3D real-time strategy titles. <i>Homeworld</i> was notable in that it featured a 3D environment in space, therefore allowing movement in every direction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> a feature which its semi-sequel, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeworld_Cataclysm" title="Homeworld Cataclysm">Homeworld Cataclysm</a></i> (2000) continued to build upon adding features such as waypoints. <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld_2" title="Homeworld 2">Homeworld 2</a></i>, released in 2003, streamlined movement in the 360° 3D environment. Furthermore, <i><a href="/wiki/Machines_(video_game)" title="Machines (video game)">Machines</a></i>, which was also released in 1999 and featured a nearly 100% 3D environment, attempted to combine the RTS genre with a first-person shooter (FPS) genre although it was not a particularly successful title. These games were followed by a short period of interest in experimental strategy games such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allegiance_(computer_game)" title="Allegiance (computer game)">Allegiance</a></i> (2000). <i>Jeff Wayne's The War of the Worlds</i> was notable for being one of the few completely non-linear RTS games ever.</p>
<p>It is only in approximately 2002 that 3D real-time strategy became the standard, with both <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warcraft_III" title="Warcraft III">Warcraft III</a></i> (2002) and Ensemble Studio's <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Mythology" title="Age of Mythology">Age of Mythology</a></i> (2002) being built on a full 3D game engine. <i><a href="/wiki/Kohan:_Immortal_Sovereigns" title="Kohan: Immortal Sovereigns">Kohan: Immortal Sovereigns</a></i> introduced classic <a href="/wiki/Wargame_(video_games)" title="Wargame (video games)">wargame</a> elements, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supply_lines" title="Supply lines">supply lines</a> to the genre. <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_Realms" title="Battle Realms">Battle Realms</a></i> (2001) was another full 3D game, but had limited camera views.</p>
<p>The move from 2D to 3D has been criticized in some cases. Issues with controlling the camera and placement of objects have been cited as problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-splanetsacrifice_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-splanetsacrifice-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gameindustry_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-gameindustry-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttgamer_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ttgamer-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Relatively few genres have emerged from or in competition with real-time strategy games, although real-time tactics, a superficially similar genre, emerged around 1995. In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> attempted to combine the real-time strategy and <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> genres in <i><a href="/wiki/Battlezone_(1998_video_game)" title="Battlezone (1998 video game)">Battlezone</a></i>, while in 2002 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rage_Games_Limited" title="Rage Games Limited">Rage Games Limited</a> attempted this with the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hostile_Waters_(game)" title="Hostile Waters (game)">Hostile Waters</a></i> games. Later variants have included <a href="/wiki/Natural_Selection_(video_game)" title="Natural Selection (video game)"><i>Natural Selection</i></a>, a game modification based on the Half-Life engine, and the <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tremulous" title="Tremulous">Tremulous</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Unvanquished_(video_game)" title="Unvanquished (video game)">Unvanquished</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Savage:_The_Battle_for_Newerth" title="Savage: The Battle for Newerth">Savage: The Battle for Newerth</a></i> combined the RPG and RTS elements in an online game.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialization_and_evolution">Specialization and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Specialization and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few games have experimented with diversifying map design, which continues to be largely two-dimensional even in 3D engines. <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_2150" title="Earth 2150">Earth 2150</a></i> allowed units to tunnel underground, effectively creating a dual-layer map; three-layer (orbit-surface-underground) maps were introduced in <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Fatigue_(video_game)" title="Metal Fatigue (video game)">Metal Fatigue</a></i>. In addition, units could even be transported to entirely separate maps, with each map having its own window in the user interface. <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms:_Fate_of_the_Dragon" title="Three Kingdoms: Fate of the Dragon">Three Kingdoms: Fate of the Dragon</a></i> (2001) offered a simpler model: the main map contains locations that expand into their own maps. In these examples, however, gameplay was essentially identical regardless of the map layer in question. <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons:_Dragonshard" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons: Dragonshard">Dragonshard</a></i> (2005) emphasized its dual-layer maps by placing one of the game's two main resources in each map, making exploration and control of both maps fundamentally valuable.</p>
<p>Some games, borrowing from the <a href="/wiki/Real-time_tactics" title="Real-time tactics">real-time tactics</a> (RTT) template, have moved toward an increased focus on tactics while downplaying traditional resource management, in which designated units collect the resources used for producing further units or buildings. Titles like <i><a href="/wiki/Warhammer_40,000:_Dawn_of_War" title="Warhammer 40,000: Dawn of War">Warhammer 40,000: Dawn of War</a></i> (2004), <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Empire_at_War" title="Star Wars: Empire at War">Star Wars: Empire at War</a></i> (2006), and <i><a href="/wiki/Company_of_Heroes" title="Company of Heroes">Company of Heroes</a></i> (2006) replace the traditional resource gathering model with a strategic control-point system, in which control over strategic points yields construction/reinforcement points. <i><a href="/wiki/Ground_Control_(video_game)" title="Ground Control (video game)">Ground Control</a></i> was the first such game to replace individual units with "squads".</p>
<p>Others are moving away from the traditional real-time strategy game model with the addition of other genre elements. One example is <i><a href="/wiki/Sins_of_a_Solar_Empire" title="Sins of a Solar Empire">Sins of a Solar Empire</a></i>, released by <a href="/wiki/Ironclad_Games" title="Ironclad Games">Ironclad Games</a>, which mixes elements of grand-scale stellar empire building games like <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Orion" title="Master of Orion">Master of Orion</a></i> with real-time strategy elements. Another example is indie game <i><a href="/wiki/Achron" title="Achron">Achron</a></i>, which incorporates time travel as a game mechanic, allowing a player to send units forward or backward in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A specific genre of <a href="/wiki/Strategy_video_game" title="Strategy video game">strategy video games</a> referred to as <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_online_battle_arena" title="Multiplayer online battle arena">multiplayer online battle arena</a> (MOBA), that originated as a subgenre of real-time strategy, gained popularity in the 2010s as a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_sports" title="Electronic sports">electronic sports</a>, encompassing games such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Defense_of_the_Ancients" title="Defense of the Ancients">Defense of the Ancients</a></i> mod for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warcraft_III" title="Warcraft III">Warcraft III</a></i>, its <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve</a>-developed sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Dota_2" title="Dota 2">Dota 2</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes_of_the_Storm" title="Heroes of the Storm">Heroes of the Storm</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/League_of_Legends" title="League of Legends">League of Legends</a>.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a typical real-time strategy game, the screen is divided into a map area displaying the game world and terrain, units, and buildings, and an interface overlay containing command and production controls and often a "radar" or "minimap" overview of the entire map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The player is usually given an <a href="/wiki/Isometry" title="Isometry">isometric</a> perspective of the world, or a free-roaming camera from an <a href="/wiki/Bird%27s-eye_view" title="Bird's-eye view">aerial viewpoint</a> for modern 3D games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fundamentals_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-fundamentals-36">[36]</a></sup> Players mainly <a href="/wiki/Scrolling" title="Scrolling">scroll</a> the screen and issue commands with the mouse, and may also use keyboard shortcuts.</p>
<p>In most real-time strategy games, especially the earliest ones, the gameplay is generally fast-paced and requires very quick reflexes. For this reason, the amount of violence in some games makes RTS games close to action games in terms of gameplay.</p>
<p>Gameplay generally consists of the player being positioned somewhere in the map with a few units or a building that is capable of building other units/buildings. Often, but not always, the player must build specific structures to unlock more advanced units in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tech_tree" title="Tech tree">tech tree</a>. Often, but not always, RTS games require the player to build an army (ranging from small squads of no more than 2 units, to literally hundreds of units) and using them to either defend themselves from a virtual form of <a href="/wiki/Human_wave_attack" title="Human wave attack">Human wave attack</a> or to eliminate enemies who possess bases with unit production capacities of their own. Occasionally, RTS games will have a preset number of units for the player to control and do not allow building of additional ones.</p>
<p>Resource gathering is commonly the main focus of the RTS games, but other titles of the genre place higher gameplay significance to the how units are used in combat (<i><a href="/wiki/Z:_Steel_Soldiers" title="Z: Steel Soldiers">Z: Steel Soldiers</a></i> for example, awards credits for territory captured rather than gathered resources), the extreme example of which are games of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real-time_tactical" title="Real-time tactical">real-time tactical</a> genre. Some titles impose a ceiling on the number simultaneous troops, which becomes a key gameplay consideration, a significant example being <i>StarCraft</i>, while other titles have no such unit cap.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Micromanagement_and_macromanagement">Micromanagement and macromanagement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Micromanagement and macromanagement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Micromanagement_(gameplay)" title="Micromanagement (gameplay)">Micromanagement (computer gaming)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macromanagement" title="Macromanagement">Macromanagement (computer gaming)</a></div>
<p>Micromanagement refers to when a player's attention is directed more toward the management and maintenance of his or her own individual units and resources. This creates an atmosphere in which the interaction of the player is constantly needed. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Macromanagement" title="Macromanagement">macromanagement</a> refers to when a player's focus is directed more toward economic development and large-scale strategic maneuvering, allowing time to think and consider possible solutions. Micromanagement frequently involves the use of combat tactics. Macromanagement tends to look to the future of the game, whereas micromanagement tends to the present.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_gameplay">Criticism of gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism of gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of their generally faster-paced nature (and in some cases a smaller learning curve), real-time strategy games have surpassed the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Turn-based_strategy" title="Turn-based strategy">turn-based strategy</a> computer games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspytbsrts_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-gspytbsrts-37">[37]</a></sup> In the past, a common criticism was to regard real-time strategy games as "cheap imitations" of turn-based strategy games, arguing that real-time strategy games had a tendency to devolve into "click-fests"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-armchair_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-armchair-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcp_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcp-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspystrategy2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-gspystrategy2-40">[40]</a></sup> in which the player who was faster with the <a href="/wiki/Computer_mouse" title="Computer mouse">mouse</a> generally won, because they could give orders to their units at a faster rate. The common retort is that success involves not just fast clicking, but also the ability to make sound decisions under time pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcp_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcp-39">[39]</a></sup> The "clickfest" argument is also often voiced alongside a "button babysitting" criticism, which pointed out that a great deal of game time is spent either waiting and watching for the next time a production button could be clicked, or rapidly alternating between different units and buildings, clicking their respective button.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-babysitting_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-babysitting-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>A third common criticism<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2012)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> is that real-time gameplay often degenerates into "<a href="/wiki/Rush_(video_gaming)" title="Rush (video gaming)">rushes</a>" where the players try to gain the advantage and subsequently defeat the opponent as quickly in the game as possible, preferably before the opposition is capable of successfully reacting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_scvsdow_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign_scvsdow-42">[42]</a></sup> For example, the original <i><a href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer" title="Command &amp; Conquer">Command &amp; Conquer</a></i> gave birth to the now-common "tank rush" tactic, where the game outcome is often decided very early on by one player gaining an initial advantage in resources and producing large amounts of a relatively powerful but still quite cheap unit—which is thrown at the opposition before they have had time to establish defenses or production. Although this strategy has been criticized for encouraging overwhelming force over strategy and tactics, defenders of the strategy argue that they're simply taking advantage of the strategies utilized, and some argue that it is a realistic representation of warfare. One of the most infamous versions of a rush is the "Zergling rush" from the real-time strategy game <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i>; in fact, the term "zerging" has become synonymous with rushing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bgeryk_gspot_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-bgeryk_gspot-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A fourth criticism of the RTS genre is the importance of skill over strategy in real-time strategy games. The manual dexterity and ability to multitask and divide one's attention is often considered the most important aspect to succeeding at the RTS genre. According to Troy Dunniway, former Westwood developer who has also worked on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_and_Conquer_3:_Tiberium_Wars" title="Command and Conquer 3: Tiberium Wars">Command and Conquer 3: Tiberium Wars</a></i>, "A player controls hundreds of units, dozens of buildings and many different events that are all happening simultaneously. There is only one player, and he can only pay attention to one thing at a time. Expert players can quickly flip between many different tasks, while casual gamers have more problems with this."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactics_vs._strategy">Tactics vs. strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tactics vs. strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Real-time_tactics#Genre_classification" title="Real-time tactics">Real-time tactics § Genre classification</a></div>
<p>Real-time strategy games have been criticized for an overabundance of tactical considerations when compared to the amount of strategic gameplay found in such games. According to <a href="/wiki/Chris_Taylor_(game_designer)" title="Chris Taylor (game designer)">Chris Taylor</a>, lead designer of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Commander_(game)" title="Supreme Commander (game)">Supreme Commander</a></i>, he said, "[My first attempt at visualizing RTSs in a fresh and interesting new way] was my realizing that although we call this genre 'Real-Time Strategy,' it should have been called 'Real-Time Tactics' with a dash of strategy thrown in."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christaylor_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-christaylor-44">[44]</a></sup> (Taylor then posits his own game as having surpassed this mold by including additional elements of broader strategic scope.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christaylor_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-christaylor-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In general terms, <a href="/wiki/Military_strategy" title="Military strategy">military strategy</a> refers to the use of a broad arsenal of weapons including diplomatic, informational, military, and economic resources, whereas <a href="/wiki/Military_tactics" title="Military tactics">military tactics</a> is more concerned with short-term goals such as winning an individual battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspytbsrts_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-gspytbsrts-37">[37]</a></sup> In the context of strategy video games, however, the difference is often reduced to the more limited criteria of either a presence or absence of base building and unit production.</p>
<p>In an article for <a href="/wiki/Gamasutra" title="Gamasutra">Gamasutra</a>, Nathan Toronto criticizes real-time strategy games for too often having only one valid means of victory — <a href="/wiki/Attrition_warfare" title="Attrition warfare">attrition</a> — comparing them unfavorably to real-time tactics games. Players' awareness that the only way for them to win or lose is militarily makes them unlikely to respond to gestures of diplomacy. The result is that the winner of a real-time strategy game is too often the best tactician rather than the best strategist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gama_toronto_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-gama_toronto-45">[45]</a></sup> Troy Goodfellow counters this by saying that the problem is not that real-time strategy games are lacking in strategy (he says attrition is a form of strategy), rather it is that they too often have the <i>same</i> strategy: produce faster than you consume. He also states that building and managing armies is the conventional definition of real-time strategy, and that it is unfair to make comparisons with other genres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gama_goodfellow_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-gama_goodfellow-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an article for <a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a>, Mark Walker criticizes real-time strategy games for their <i>lack</i> of combat tactics, suggesting real-time tactics games as a more suitable substitute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspytbsrts_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-gspytbsrts-37">[37]</a></sup> He also says that developers need to begin looking outside the genre for new ideas in order for strategy games to continue to be successful in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspytbsrts_pvi_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-gspytbsrts_pvi-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Turn-based_vs._real-time">Turn-based vs. real-time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Turn-based vs. real-time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-keeping_systems_in_games" title="Time-keeping systems in games">Time-keeping systems in games</a></div>
<p>A debate has emerged between fans of real-time strategy and turn-based strategy (and related genres) based on the merits of the real-time and turn-based systems. Some titles attempt to merge the two systems: for example, the role-playing game <i><a href="/wiki/Fallout_(video_game)" title="Fallout (video game)">Fallout</a></i> uses turn-based combat and real-time gameplay, while the real-time strategy games <i>Homeworld</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rise_of_Nations" title="Rise of Nations">Rise of Nations</a></i>, and the games of the <i><a href="/wiki/Total_War_(series)" title="Total War (series)">Total War</a></i> series allow the player to pause the game and issue orders. Additionally, the <i>Total War</i> series has a combination of a turn-based strategy map with a real-time battle map. Another example of a game combining both turn-based game and real-time-strategy is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Battle_for_Middle-Earth_II" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Battle for Middle-Earth II">The Lord of the Rings: The Battle for Middle-Earth II</a></i> which allows players, in a 'War of the Ring' game, to play a turn-based strategy game, but also battle each other in real time.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="On_consoles">On consoles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: On consoles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Despite <i><a href="/wiki/Herzog_Zwei" title="Herzog Zwei">Herzog Zwei</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Console_game" title="Console game">console game</a>, laying the foundations for the real-time strategy genre, RTS games never gained popularity on consoles like they did on the <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP-Duke_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-Duke-11">[11]</a></sup> Real-time strategy games made for <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game consoles</a> have been consistently criticized due to their control schemes, as the PC's keyboard and mouse are generally considered to be superior to a console's <a href="/wiki/Gamepad" title="Gamepad">gamepad</a> for the genre. Thus, RTS games for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_console" title="Home console">home consoles</a> have been met with mixed success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNETLOTR_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNETLOTR-48">[48]</a></sup> Scott Sharkey of <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP</a> notes that <i>Herzog Zwei</i> had already "offered a nearly perfect solution to the problem by giving the player direct control of a single powerful unit and near autonomy for everything else," and is surprised "that more console RTS games aren't designed with this kind of interface in mind from the ground up, rather than imitating" PC control schemes "that just doesn't work very well with a controller".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1UP-Duke_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-1UP-Duke-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, <i><a href="/wiki/Halo_Wars" title="Halo Wars">Halo Wars</a></i>, which was released in 2009 for the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>, generated generally positive reviews, achieved an 82% critic average on aggregate web sites, and sold over 1 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a>, the gameplay lacks the traditional RTS concepts of limited resources and resource gathering and lacks multiple buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphics">Graphics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Graphics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Total Annihilation</i> (1997) was the first real-time strategy game to utilize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/True_3D" title="True 3D">true 3D</a> units, terrain, and physics in both rendering and in gameplay. For instance, the missiles in <i>Total Annihilation</i> travel in real time in simulated 3D space, and they can miss their target by passing over or under it. Similarly, missile-armed units in <i>Earth 2150</i> are at a serious disadvantage when the opponent is on high ground because the missiles often hit the cliffside, even in the case when the attacker is a missile-armed helicopter. <i>Homeworld</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Warzone_2100" title="Warzone 2100">Warzone 2100</a> and <a href="/wiki/Machines_(video_game)" title="Machines (video game)">Machines</a></i> (all released in 1999) advanced the use of fully 3D environments in real-time strategy titles. In the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Homeworld" title="Homeworld">Homeworld</a></i>, the game is set in space, offering a uniquely exploitable 3D environment in which all units can move vertically in addition to the horizontal plane. However, the near-industry-wide switch to full 3D was very gradual and most real-time strategy titles, including the first sequels to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer_(series)" title="Command &amp; Conquer (series)">Command &amp; Conquer</a></i>, initially used isometric 3D graphics made by pre-rendered 3D tiles. Only in later years did these games begin to use true 3D graphics and game-play, making it possible to rotate the view of the battlefield in real-time. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TA_Spring" title="TA Spring">Spring</a></i> is a good example of the transformation from semi-3D to full-3D game simulations. It is an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-source" title="Open-source">open-source</a></i> project which aims to give a <i>Total Annihilation</i> game-play experience in three dimensions. The most ambitious use of full 3D graphics was realized in <i><a href="/wiki/Supreme_Commander_(video_game)" title="Supreme Commander (video game)">Supreme Commander</a></i>, where all projectiles, units and terrain were simulated in real time, taking full advantage of the UI's zoom feature, which allowed cartographic style navigation of the 3D environment. This led to a number of unique gameplay elements, which were mostly obscured by the lack of computing power available in 2007, at the release date.</p>
<p>Japanese game developers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nippon_Ichi" title="Nippon Ichi">Nippon Ichi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanillaware" title="Vanillaware">Vanillaware</a> worked together on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grim_Grimoire" title="Grim Grimoire">Grim Grimoire</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">PlayStation 2</a> title released in 2007, which features hand-drawn animated 2D graphics.</p>
<p>From 2010, real-time strategy games more commonly incorporated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_engines" title="Physics engines">physics engines</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Havok_(software)" title="Havok (software)">Havok</a>, in order to increase realism experienced in gameplay. A modern real-time strategy game that uses a physics engine is Ensemble Studios' <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Empires_III" title="Age of Empires III">Age of Empires III</a></i>, released on October 18, 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstphysics_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-firstphysics-52">[52]</a></sup> which used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Havok_Game_Dynamics_SDK" title="Havok Game Dynamics SDK">Havok Game Dynamics SDK</a> to power its real-time physics. <i>Company of Heroes</i> is another real-time strategy game that uses realistically modeled physics as a part of gameplay, including fully destructible environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tournaments">Tournaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Real-time_strategy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Tournaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_sports" title="Electronic sports">Electronic sports</a></div>
<p>RTS World tournaments have been held for both <a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warcraft_III" title="Warcraft III">Warcraft III</a></i> since its release in 1998, and 2002. The games being that successful, that some players of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warcraft_III" title="Warcraft III">Warcraft III</a></i> playing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warcraft_III_professional_competition" title="Warcraft III professional competition">Warcraft III professional competition</a></i> have earned over $200,000 at the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warcraft_III_World_Championships" title="Warcraft III World Championships">Warcraft III World Championships</a></i>. In addition, hundreds of <a href="/wiki/StarCraft_II:_Wings_of_Liberty" title="StarCraft II: Wings of Liberty">StarCraft II</a> tournaments are held yearly, as it is becoming an increasingly popular branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-sports" title="E-sports">e-sports</a>. Notable tournaments include <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Gaming" title="Major League Gaming">MLG</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOMTV_Global_StarCraft_II_League" title="GOMTV Global StarCraft II League">GSL</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dreamhack" title="Dreamhack">Dreamhack</a>. RTS tournaments are especially popular in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.</p>
