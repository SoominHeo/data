<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Declared death <i>in absentia</i></h1>

<p>A person may be legally declared dead (<b>declared death <i>in absentia</i></b> or <b>legal presumption of death</b>) despite the absence of direct proof of the person's death, such as the finding of remains (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Cadaver" title="Cadaver">corpse</a> or skeleton) attributable to that person. Such a declaration is typically made when a person has been <a href="/wiki/Missing_person" title="Missing person">missing</a> for an extended period of time and in the absence of any evidence that the person is still alive – or after a much shorter period but where the circumstances surrounding a person's disappearance overwhelmingly support the belief that the person has died (e.g., an <a href="/wiki/Aviation_accidents_and_incidents" title="Aviation accidents and incidents">airplane crash</a>).</p>
<p>A declaration that a person is dead resembles other forms of "preventive adjudication", such as the <a href="/wiki/Declaratory_judgment" title="Declaratory judgment">declaratory judgment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Different jurisdictions have different legal standards for obtaining such a declaration and in some jurisdictions a legal presumption of death may arise after a person has been missing under certain circumstances and a certain amount of time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facts.2C_circumstances.2C_and_the_.22balance_of_probabilities.22">Facts, circumstances, and the "balance of probabilities"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title='Edit section: Facts, circumstances, and the "balance of probabilities"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_code" title="Civil code">civil code</a> jurisdictions, it is usually necessary to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Court_order" title="Court order">court order</a> directing the registrar to issue a <a href="/wiki/Death_certificate" title="Death certificate">death certificate</a> in the absence of a physician's certification that an identified individual has died. However, if there is <a href="/wiki/Circumstantial_evidence" title="Circumstantial evidence">circumstantial evidence</a> that would lead a reasonable person to believe that the individual is deceased on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balance_of_probabilities" title="Balance of probabilities">balance of probabilities</a>, jurisdictions may agree to issue death certificates without any such order. For example, <a href="/wiki/Passengers_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Passengers of the RMS Titanic">passengers and crew</a> of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a> who were not rescued by the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Carpathia" title="RMS Carpathia">RMS <i>Carpathia</i></a> were declared legally dead soon after <i>Carpathia</i> arrived at <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. More recently, death certificates for those who perished in the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> were issued by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">State of New York</a> within days of the tragedy. The same is usually true of <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldiers</a> <a href="/wiki/Missing_in_action" title="Missing in action">missing after a major battle</a>, especially if the enemy keeps an accurate record of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>.</p>
<p>If there is not sufficient evidence that death has taken place, it may take somewhat longer, as simple absence does not necessarily prove death. The requirements for declaring an individual legally dead may vary depending on numerous details including the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>The jurisdiction the individual lived in before death</li>
<li>The jurisdiction where they are presumed to have died</li>
<li>How the individual is thought to have died (<a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accident" title="Accident">accident</a>, etc.)</li>
<li>The balance of probabilities that make it more likely than not that the individual is dead</li>
</ul>
<p>Most countries have a set period of time (seven years in many <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions) after which an individual is presumed dead if there is no evidence to the contrary. However, if the missing individual is the owner of a significant estate, the court may delay ordering the issuing of a death certificate if there has been no real effort to locate the missing person. If the death is thought to have taken place in <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">international waters</a> or in a location without a centralized and reliable police force or vital statistics registration system, other laws may apply.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_aspects">Legal aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> generally assumes a person is dead if, after seven years:</p>
<ul>
<li>There has been no evidence that they are still alive.</li>
<li>The people most likely to have heard from them have had no contact.</li>
<li>Inquiries made of that person have had no success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Committee_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Committee-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>This is a rebuttable presumption at common law—if the person subsequently appears, the law no longer considers them dead.</p>
<p>Otherwise, courts may grant leave to applicants to swear that a person is dead (within or after the seven-year period).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Committee_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Committee-2">[2]</a></sup> For example, an executor may make such an application so they can be granted probate for the will. This kind of application would only be made sooner than seven years where death is probable, but not definitive (such as an unrecovered plane crash at sea), following an inquest (see below). Such an application is specific to the court where it is made—thus separate applications must be made at a coroner's inquest, for proceedings under the Matrimonial Causes and Civil Partnership Acts (for remarriage), for <a href="/wiki/Probate" title="Probate">probate</a>, and under the Social Security Act.</p>
<p>These processes were not considered satisfactory, and so in February – March 2013, the <b>Presumption of Death Act 2013</b> was passed to simplify this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The new act, which is based on the Presumption of Death (Scotland) Act 1977,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> allows applying to the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> to declare a person presumed dead. This declaration is conclusive and cannot be appealed. It is recorded on a new Register of Presumed Deaths, and has the same effect as a registration of death. Death is taken to occur on (a) the last day that they could have been alive (if the court is satisfied that they are dead), or (b) the day seven years after the date they were last seen (if death is presumed by the elapse of time).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, if the authorities believe there should be an <a href="/wiki/Inquest" title="Inquest">inquest</a>, the local <a href="/wiki/Coroner" title="Coroner">coroner</a> files a report. This may be done to help a family receive a death certificate that may bring some closure. An inquest strives to bring any suspicious circumstances to light. The coroner then applies to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Justice" title="Secretary of State for Justice">Secretary of State for Justice</a>, under the Coroners Act 1988 section 15, for an inquest with no body. The seven years rule only applies in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> on the settlement of an estate. According to a spokesman for the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Justice (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Justice</a>, the number of requests received each year is fewer than ten, but few of these are refused. Without a body, an inquest relies mostly on evidence provided by the <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a>, and whether senior officers believe the missing person is dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> One notable person presumed dead under the Act is the <a href="/wiki/John_Bingham,_7th_Earl_of_Lucan" title="John Bingham, 7th Earl of Lucan">7th Earl of Lucan</a> (Lord Lucan), who was last seen alive in 1974 (although there have been numerous alleged sightings since that time), and whose death certificate was issued in February 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The incidence of presumed death in England and Wales is considered low - in September 2011, it was estimated that only 1% of the 200,000 missing persons each year remained unaccounted for after 12 months, with a cumulative total of 5,500 missing persons by September 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Committee_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Committee-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Scotland, legal aspects of death <i>in absentia</i> are outlined in the <b>Presumption of Death (Scotland) Act 1977</b>. If a person lived in Scotland on the date they were last known to be alive, authorities can use this act to declare the person legally dead after the standard period of seven years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It takes 20 years in Italy to declare a missing person dead. After ten years from somebody's disappearance, a motion to declare the person legally dead can be filed in a court of law. After that, another ten years must pass before the person can eventually be declared legally dead.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Declaration of death in absentia is derived from articles 29-32 of Polish Civil Code and exercised as a court sentence. In a general case, 10 years is a timespan of disappearance required for the legal declaration, but the following exceptions apply:</p>
<ul>
<li>no one is declared dead before the end of the year in which one becomes 23 years old;</li>
<li>the timespan of disappearance is reduced to 5 years if the person would be 70 years old or more at the time of declaration;</li>
<li>if a person would have been a victim of an air or a sea disaster or in an "other exceptional happening", the disappearance timespan is reduced to 6 months, however if the vessel is presumably lost, the time starts to count 1 year after the unkept scheduled day of arrival or 2 years after the last known whereabouts;</li>
<li>if a person is missing in an other life-threatening event (different than the above), the timespan is 1 year since the life-threatening event ceased.</li>
</ul>
<p>A court's declaration of death in absentia produces legal effects valid from before the date of the declaration, and going back to the assumed day of death (as declared by the court).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to article 45 of <a href="/wiki/Civil_Code_of_Russia" title="Civil Code of Russia">Civil Code of Russia</a>, a person may be declared dead in absentia only by a court decision, on the following grounds:</p>
<ul>
<li>He or she had been missing for 5 years</li>
<li>If the person disappeared under life-threatening circumstances, which made it likely that he or she died from an accident, that person can be assumed dead after 6 months</li>
<li>A military or civil person, who disappeared during a military conflict, can be declared dead no earlier than 2 years after the conflict is over</li>
</ul>
<p>A legal date of death is considered to be the date when the court decision declaring person dead was made. If a person disappeared under life-threatening circumstances, the day of his or her actual disappearance may also be considered the legal date of death.</p>
<p>The declaration of death in absentia by the court has the same legal consequences as if the fact of death was proven:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dependant" title="Dependant">Dependants</a> of the person become eligible for the state pension</li>
<li>Assets can be <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inherited</a></li>
<li>If the person was married, the marriage legally ends</li>
<li>Personal obligations are terminated</li>
</ul>
<p>If such decision was a mistake and the person later returned, the decision is nullified and the person becomes eligible to request most of his assets back. However, if the husband or wife of such person married again, the marriage will not be restored. His funds and securities, taken under bona fide circumstances, also cannot be requested back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The law calls people who disappear <i>missing</i> or <i>absent</i>. Several criteria affect declaring someone dead by assumption:</p>
<ul>
<li>A person's being missing from their home or usual residence for, typically, seven years (the period varies from state to state)</li>
<li>Such absences being continuous and without explanation</li>
<li>Such absences being accompanied by a lack of long-distance communication with those most likely to hear from them</li>
<li>Diligent but unsuccessful search for that person and <a href="/wiki/Inquiry" title="Inquiry">inquiry</a> into their whereabouts.</li>
</ul>
<p>Professor Jeanne Carriere, author of “The Rights of the Living Dead: Absent Persons in <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">Civil Law</a>” (published in the <i>Louisiana Law Review</i>), stated that as of 1990, the number of such cases in the United States was estimated at between 60,000 and 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carriere_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-carriere-9">[9]</a></sup> Often the missing person's bank accounts are checked for activity, and possible sightings investigated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>According to Edgar Sentell, a retired senior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-president" title="Vice-president">vice-president</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_counsel" title="General counsel">general counsel</a> of Southern Farm Bureau Life Insurance Company, almost all states recognize the presumption of death, by <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a> or judicial recognition of the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> rule. Some states have amended their statutes to reduce the seven-year period to five consecutive years missing, and some, such as <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, have reduced the period to four years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sentell_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sentell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>If someone disappears, those interested can file a <a href="/wiki/Petition" title="Petition">petition</a> to have them declared legally dead. They must prove by the criteria above that the person is in fact dead. There are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional" title="Constitutional">constitutional</a> limitations to these procedures: The presumption must arise only after a reasonable amount of time has elapsed. The absent person must be notified. Courts permit notifying claimants by publication. Adequate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safeguards" title="Safeguards">safeguards</a> concerning property provisions must be made in the case that an absent person shows up.</p>
<p>Some states require those who receive the missing person's assets to return them if the person turned out to be alive. If a person is declared dead when only missing, their estate is distributed as if they were dead. In some cases, the presumption of death can be rebutted. According to Sentell, courts will consider evidence that the absent person was a <a href="/wiki/Fugitive" title="Fugitive">fugitive</a> from <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, had money troubles, had a bad relationship, or had no family ties or connection to a <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> as reasons not to presume death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sentell_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sentell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A person can be declared legally dead after they are exposed to "imminent peril" and fail to return—as in a plane crash, as portrayed in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Cast_Away" title="Cast Away">Cast Away</a></i>. In these cases courts generally assume the person was killed, even though the usual waiting time to declare someone dead has not elapsed. Sentell also says, “The element of peril accelerates the presumption of death.” This rule was enacted after the attack on the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a>, so that authorities could release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_certificates" title="Death certificates">death certificates</a>. Although people presumed dead sometimes turn up alive, it is not as common as it used to be. In one case where this occurred, a man named John Burney disappeared in 1976 while having financial problems, and later reappeared in December 1982. His company and wife had already received the death benefits—so, on returning, the <a href="/wiki/Life_insurance" title="Life insurance">life insurance</a> company sued him, his wife, and his company. In the end, the court ruled Burney's actions fraudulent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reappearance">Reappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Declared_death_in_absentia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_premature_obituaries" title="List of premature obituaries">List of premature obituaries</a></div>
<p>Missing persons have on rare occasions been found alive after being declared legally dead. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">Prisoners of war</a>, people with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_illness" title="Mental illness">mental illnesses</a> who become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a>, and in extremely rare circumstances <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">kidnapping</a> victims may be located years after their disappearance. Some people have even <a href="/wiki/Faked_death" title="Faked death">faked their deaths</a> to avoid paying taxes, debts, etc.</p>
