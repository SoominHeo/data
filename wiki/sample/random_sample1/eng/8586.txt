<p><b>Silk Road</b> was an online <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black market</a> and the first modern <a href="/wiki/Darknet_market" title="Darknet market">darknet market</a>, best known as a platform for selling illegal drugs. As part of the <a href="/wiki/Dark_web" title="Dark web">dark web</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> it was operated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tor_hidden_service" title="Tor hidden service">Tor hidden service</a>, such that online users were able to browse it anonymously and securely without potential traffic monitoring. The website was launched in February 2011; development had begun six months prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dbvc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dbvc-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Initially there were a limited number of new seller accounts available; new sellers had to purchase an account in an auction. Later, a fixed fee was charged for each new seller account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> (FBI) shut down the website<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complaint1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-complaint1-12">[12]</a></sup> and arrested <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ross_William_Ulbricht" title="Ross William Ulbricht">Ross William Ulbricht</a> under charges of being the site's pseudonymous founder "Dread Pirate Roberts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ars-3">[3]</a></sup> On 6 November 2013, Silk Road 2.0 came online, run by former administrators of Silk Road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green1113_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-green1113-13">[13]</a></sup> It too was shut down, and the alleged operator was arrested on 6 November 2014 as part of the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Operation_Onymous" title="Operation Onymous">Operation Onymous</a>”.</p>
<p>Ulbricht was convicted of eight charges related to Silk Road in <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">U.S. Federal Court in Manhattan</a> and was sentenced to <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life in prison</a> without possibility of parole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytlife_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytlife-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bweiser_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bweiser-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-conviction_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars-conviction-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Silk Road was founded in February 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-16">[16]</a></sup> The name "Silk Road" comes from a <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">historical network of trade routes</a> started during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> (206 BC – 220 AD) between Europe, India, China, and many other countries on the Afro-Eurasian landmass. Silk Road was operated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudonymous" title="Pseudonymous">pseudonymous</a> "Dread Pirate Roberts" (named after the <a href="/wiki/Dread_Pirate_Roberts" title="Dread Pirate Roberts">fictional character</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Princess_Bride" title="The Princess Bride">The Princess Bride</a></i>), who was known for espousing <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> ideals and criticizing <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ars-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenberg_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenberg-17">[17]</a></sup> Two other individuals were also closely involved in the site's growth and success, known as <a href="/wiki/Variety_Jones_and_Smedley" title="Variety Jones and Smedley">Variety Jones and Smedley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2011, <a href="/wiki/Gawker" title="Gawker">Gawker</a> published an article about the site<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GawkerChen_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-GawkerChen-19">[19]</a></sup> which led to "Internet buzz"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-16">[16]</a></sup> and an increase in website traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dbvc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-dbvc-8">[8]</a></sup> Once the site was known publicly, U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Schumer" title="Chuck Schumer">Charles Schumer</a> asked federal law enforcement authorities to shut it down, including the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> (DEA) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Justice_(United_States)" title="Department of Justice (United States)">Department of Justice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SecNarc_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-SecNarc-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2013, an Australian <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> and <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">MDMA</a> ("ecstasy") dealer became the first person to be convicted of crimes directly related to Silk Road, after authorities intercepted drugs that he was importing through the mail, searched his premises, and discovered his Silk Road alias in an image file on his personal computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Australian police and the DEA have targeted Silk Road users and made arrests, albeit with limited success at reaching convictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GawkerChen_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-GawkerChen-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bthc_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-bthc-23">[23]</a></sup> In December 2013, a New Zealand man was sentenced to two years and four months in jail after being convicted of importing 15 grams of <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">methamphetamine</a> that he had bought on Silk Road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2013, Silk Road was taken down for a short period of time by a sustained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDoS" title="DDoS">DDoS</a> attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On 23 June 2013, it was first reported that the DEA seized 11.02 <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">bitcoins</a>, then worth $814, which the media suspected was a result of a Silk Road <a href="/wiki/Honeypot_(computing)" title="Honeypot (computing)">honeypot</a> sting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The FBI has claimed that the real IP address of the Silk Road server was found via data leaked directly from the site's <a href="/wiki/CAPTCHA" title="CAPTCHA">CAPTCHA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> but security researchers believe that the <a href="/wiki/PHP" title="PHP">PHP</a> login page was manipulated to output its $_SERVER variable and real IP following site maintenance reconfiguration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ross_Ulbricht" title="Ross Ulbricht">Ross Ulbricht</a> was alleged by the FBI to be the founder and owner of Silk Road and the person behind the pseudonym "Dread Pirate Roberts" (DPR). He was arrested on 2 October 2013 in San Francisco<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complaint1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-complaint1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBI_raid_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FBI_raid-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian1-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poststar_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-poststar-33">[33]</a></sup> at 3:15 p.m. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Standard_Time" title="Pacific Standard Time">PST</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes2-34">[34]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Park,_San_Francisco" title="Glen Park, San Francisco">Glen Park</a> Library, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Public_Library" title="San Francisco Public Library">San Francisco Public Library</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes2-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ulbricht was indicted on charges of <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">money laundering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">computer hacking</a>, conspiracy to traffic narcotics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes2_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes2-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBT_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBT-35">[35]</a></sup> and attempting to have six people killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-36">[36]</a></sup> Prosecutors alleged that Ulbricht paid $730,000 to others to commit the murders, although none of the murders actually occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-36">[36]</a></sup> Ulbricht ultimately was not prosecuted for any of the alleged murder attempts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The FBI initially seized 26,000 bitcoins from accounts on Silk Road, worth approximately $3.6 million at the time. An FBI spokesperson said that the agency would hold the bitcoins until Ulbricht's trial finished, after which the bitcoins would be liquidated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In October 2013, the FBI reported that it had seized 144,000 bitcoins, worth $28.5 million, and that the bitcoins belonged to Ulbricht.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On 27 June 2014, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service" title="United States Marshals Service">U.S. Marshals Service</a> sold 29,657 bitcoins in 10 blocks in an online auction, estimated to be worth $18 million at current rates and only about a quarter of the seized bitcoins. Another 144,342 bitcoins were kept which had been found on Ulbricht's computer, roughly $87 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Timothy_C._Draper" title="Timothy C. Draper">Tim Draper</a> bought the bitcoins at the auction with an estimated worth of $17 million, to lend them to a bitcoin start-up called Vaurum which is working in developing economies of emerging markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ulbricht's trial began on 13 January 2015 in Federal Court in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> At the start of the trial, Ulbricht admitted to founding the Silk Road website, but claimed to have transferred control of the site to other people soon after he founded it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Ulbricht's lawyers contended that Dread Pirate Roberts was really <a href="/wiki/Mark_Karpel%C3%A8s" title="Mark Karpelès">Mark Karpelès</a>, and that Karpelès set up Ulbricht as a fall guy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> However, Judge <a href="/wiki/Katherine_B._Forrest" title="Katherine B. Forrest">Katherine B. Forrest</a> ruled that any speculative statements regarding whether Karpelès or anyone else ran Silk Road would not be allowed, and statements already made would be stricken from the record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbush_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-jbush-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the second week of the trial, prosecutors presented documents and chat logs from Ulbricht's computer that, they said, demonstrated how Ulbricht had administered the site for many months, which contradicted the defense's claim that Ulbricht had relinquished control of Silk Road. Ulbricht's attorney suggested that the documents and chat logs were planted there by way of <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent" title="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a>, which was running on Ulbricht's computer at the time of his arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbush_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-jbush-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 4 February 2015, the jury convicted Ulbricht of seven charges,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars-conviction_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ars-conviction-15">[15]</a></sup> including charges of engaging in a continuing criminal enterprise, narcotics trafficking, money laundering, and computer hacking. He faced 30 years to life in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bweiser_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bweiser-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-wsj.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The government also accused Ulbricht of paying for the murders of at least five people, but there is no evidence that the murders were actually carried out, and the accusations never became formal charges against Ulbricht.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhong052915_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhong052915-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the trial, Judge Forrest received death threats. Users of an underground site called <a href="/wiki/The_Hidden_Wiki" title="The Hidden Wiki">The Hidden Wiki</a> posted her personal information there, including her address and Social Security number. Ulbricht's lawyer Joshua Dratel said that he and his client "obviously, and as strongly as possible, condemn" the anonymous postings against the judge. "They do not in any way have anything to do with <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ulbricht" title="Ross Ulbricht">Ross Ulbricht</a> or anyone associated with him or reflect his views or those of anyone associated with him", Dratel said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late March 2015, a criminal complaint issued by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Northern_District_of_California" title="United States District Court for the Northern District of California">United States District Court for the Northern District of California</a> led to the arrest of two former federal agents who had worked undercover in the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> Silk Road investigation of Ulbricht, former Drug Enforcement Administration agent Carl Mark Force IV and Secret Service agent Shaun Bridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-financial_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-financial-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The agents are alleged to have kept funds that Ulbricht transferred to them in exchange for purported information about the investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-financial_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-financial-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The agents were charged with <a href="/wiki/Mail_and_wire_fraud" title="Mail and wire fraud">wire fraud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">money laundering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 15, 2015, director/screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Alex_Winter" title="Alex Winter">Alex Winter</a> debuted at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest_Film_Festival" title="South by Southwest Film Festival">South by Southwest Film Festival</a> a movie based on Silk Road. <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Web_(film)" title="Deep Web (film)">Deep Web</a></i> gives the inside story of the arrest of Ross Ulbricht.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a letter to Judge Forrest before his sentencing, Ulbricht stated that his actions through Silk Road were committed through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libertarian" title="Libertarian">libertarian</a> idealism and that "Silk Road was supposed to be about giving people the freedom to make their own choices" and admitted that he made a "terrible mistake" that "ruined his life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> On May 29, 2015, Ulbricht was given five <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentences</a> to be served concurrently, including two for <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life imprisonment</a> without the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Parole" title="Parole">parole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> He was also ordered to <a href="/wiki/Asset_forfeiture" title="Asset forfeiture">forfeit</a> $183 million. Ulbricht’s lawyer Joshua Dratel said that he would <a href="/wiki/Appellate_procedure_in_the_United_States" title="Appellate procedure in the United States">appeal</a> the sentencing and the original guilty verdict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhong052915_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-nhong052915-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_trials">Other trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dutch drug dealer 23-year-old Cornelis Jan "Maikel" Slomp<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> pled guilty for large scale selling of drugs through the Silk Road website and was sentenced in Chicago to 10 years in prison on 29 May 2015 with his attorney, Paul Petruzzi, present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Dealer Steven Sadler was sentenced to five years in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2013, the site had 10,000 products for sale by vendors, 70% of which were drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GawkerChen_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-GawkerChen-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ball13_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-ball13-62">[62]</a></sup> In October 2014, there were 13,756 listings for drugs, grouped under the headings <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stimulants" title="Stimulants">stimulants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">psychedelics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug" title="Prescription drug">prescription</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">precursors</a>, other, <a href="/wiki/Opioid" title="Opioid">opioids</a>, <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">ecstasy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissociative" title="Dissociative">dissociatives</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performance-enhancing_drugs" title="Performance-enhancing drugs">PEDs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dbvc_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-dbvc-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-one-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acheroin_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-acheroin-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNYCrypto_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-TNYCrypto-64">[64]</a></sup> Fake driver's licenses were also offered for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The site's terms of service prohibited the sale of certain items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-one-16">[16]</a></sup> This included <a href="/wiki/Child_pornography" title="Child pornography">child pornography</a>, stolen credit cards, assassinations, and weapons of any type; other darknet markets such as <a href="/wiki/Black_Market_Reloaded" title="Black Market Reloaded">Black Market Reloaded</a> gained user notoriety because they were not as restrictive on these items as the Silk Road incarnations were.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ball13_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-ball13-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> There were also legal goods and services for sale, such as <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">apparel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">books</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarettes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erotica" title="Erotica">erotica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a>, and writing services. A sister site, called "The Armory", sold weapons (primarily guns) during 2012, but was shut down because of a lack of demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Buyers were able to leave reviews of sellers' products on the site, and in an associated forum where <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourcing</a> provided information about the best sellers and worst scammers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Power2013_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Power2013-69">[69]</a></sup> Most products were delivered through the mail, with the site's seller's guide instructing sellers how to vacuum-seal their products to escape detection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sales">Sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Based on data from 3 February 2012 to 24 July 2012, an estimated $15 million in transactions were made annually on Silk Road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Twelve months later, Nicolas Christin, the study's author, said in an interview that a major increase in volume to "somewhere between $30 million and $45 million" would not surprise him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Buyers and sellers conducted all transactions with bitcoins (BTC), a <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency" title="Cryptocurrency">cryptocurrency</a> that provides a certain degree of <a href="/wiki/Anonymity" title="Anonymity">anonymity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bitcoin_Anonymity_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bitcoin_Anonymity-74">[74]</a></sup> Silk Road held buyers' bitcoins in <a href="/wiki/Escrow" title="Escrow">escrow</a> until the order had been received and a <a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)" title="Hedge (finance)">hedging mechanism</a> allowed sellers to opt for the value of bitcoins held in escrow to be fixed to their value in US$ at the time of the sale to mitigate against Bitcoin's volatility. Any changes in the price of bitcoins during transit were covered by Dread Pirate Roberts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_complaint" title="Criminal complaint">complaint</a> published when Ulbricht was arrested included information the FBI gained from a <a href="/wiki/System_image" title="System image">system image</a> of the Silk Road server collected on 23 July 2013. It noted that, "From February 6, 2011 to July 23, 2013 there were approximately 1,229,465 transactions completed on the site. The total revenue generated from these sales was 9,519,664 Bitcoins, and the total commissions collected by Silk Road from the sales amounted to 614,305 Bitcoins. These figures are equivalent to roughly $1.2 billion in revenue and $79.8 million in commissions, at current Bitcoin exchange rates...", according to the September 2013 complaint, and involved 146,946 buyers and 3,877 vendors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complaint1_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-complaint1-12">[12]</a></sup> This statement was made to emphasize the importance of the operation, because in the years 2011-2013 the value of 9,519,664 Bitcoins was $0.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bitcoin2014_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-bitcoin2014-76">[76]</a></sup> According to information users provided upon registering, 30 percent were from the United States, 27 percent chose to be "undeclared", and beyond that, in descending order of prevalence: the United Kingdom, Australia, Germany, Canada, Sweden, France, Russia, Italy, and the Netherlands. During the 60-day period from 24 May to 23 July, there were 1,217,218 messages sent over Silk Road's private messaging system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complaint1_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-complaint1-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_sites">Similar sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Similar sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Farmer%27s_Market" title="The Farmer's Market">The Farmer's Market</a> was a Tor site similar to Silk Road, but which did not use bitcoins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> It has been considered a 'proto-Silk Road' but the use of payment services such as PayPal and Western Union allowed law enforcement to trace payments and it was subsequently shut down by the FBI in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Power2013_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Power2013-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Other sites already existed when Silk Road was shut down and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> predicted that these would take over the market that Silk Road previously dominated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Sites named '<a href="/wiki/Atlantis_(market)" title="Atlantis (market)">Atlantis</a>', closing in September 2013, and <i>Project Black Flag</i>, closing in October 2013, each stole their users' bitcoins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green1113_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-green1113-13">[13]</a></sup> In October 2013, the site named <i>Black Market Reloaded</i> closed down temporarily after the site's source code was leaked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green1113_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-green1113-13">[13]</a></sup> The market shares of various Silk Road successor sites were described by <i>The Economist</i> in May 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_Club">Book Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Book Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silk Road had a Tor-based <a href="/wiki/Book_discussion_club" title="Book discussion club">book club</a> that continued to operate following the initial site's closure and even following the arrest of one of its members. Reading material included conspiracy theories and computer hacking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_successors">Direct successors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Direct successors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silk_Road_2.0">Silk Road 2.0</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Silk Road 2.0">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png/220px-Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png/330px-Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png/440px-Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silk_Road_Seized_2014.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>On 6 November 2013, administrators from the closed Silk Road relaunched the site, led by a new pseudonymous Dread Pirate Roberts, and dubbed it "Silk Road 2.0". It recreated the original site's setup and promised improved security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green1113_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-green1113-13">[13]</a></sup> The new DPR took the precaution of distributing encrypted copies of the site's source code to allow the site to be quickly recreated in the event of another shutdown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 20 December 2013, it was announced that three alleged Silk Road 2.0 administrators had been arrested;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> two of these suspects, Andrew Michael Jones and Gary Davis, were named as the administrators "Inigo" and "Libertas" who had continued their work on Silk Road 2.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Around this time, the new Dread Pirate Roberts abruptly surrendered control of the site and froze its activity, including its escrow system. A new temporary administrator under the screenname "Defcon" took over and promised to bring the site back to working order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 February 2014, Defcon announced that Silk Road 2.0's escrow accounts had been compromised through a vulnerability in Bitcoin's protocol called "transaction malleability".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandom2014_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-brandom2014-88">[88]</a></sup> While the site remained online, all the bitcoins in its escrow accounts, valued at $2.7 million, were reported stolen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandom2014_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-brandom2014-88">[88]</a></sup> It was later reported that the vulnerability was in the site's "Refresh Deposits" function, and that the Silk Road administrators had used their commissions on sales since 15 February to refund users who lost money, with 50 percent of the hack victims being completely repaid as of 8 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joseph_Cox_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joseph_Cox-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 November 2014, authorities with the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europol" title="Europol">Europol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eurojust" title="Eurojust">Eurojust</a> announced the arrest of Blake Benthall, allegedly the owner and operator of Silk Road 2.0 under the pseudonym "Defcon", the previous day in San Francisco as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Onymous" title="Operation Onymous">Operation Onymous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcnews6nov_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbcnews6nov-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silk_Road_(marketplace)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the closure of Silk Road 2.0 in November 2014, Diabolus Market renamed itself to 'Silk Road 3 Reloaded' in order to capitalise on the brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2015 'Silk Road Reloaded' launched on <a href="/wiki/I2P" title="I2P">I2P</a> with multiple <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency" title="Cryptocurrency">cryptocurrency</a> support and similar listing restrictions to the original Silk Road market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
