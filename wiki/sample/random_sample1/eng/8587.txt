<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Silt</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Silt_(disambiguation)" title="Silt (disambiguation)">Silt (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Silt</b> is <a href="/wiki/Granular_material" title="Granular material">granular material</a> of a size between <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> origin is <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a>. Silt may occur as a <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> (often mixed with sand or clay) or as <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> mixed in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> with water (also known as a suspended load) in a <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">body of water</a> such as a river. It may also exist as soil deposited at the bottom of a water body. Silt has a moderate <a href="/wiki/Specific_surface_area" title="Specific surface area">specific area</a> with a typically non-sticky, plastic feel. Silt usually has a floury feel when dry, and a slippery feel when wet. Silt can be visually observed with a hand lens.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silt is created by a variety of physical processes capable of splitting the generally sand-sized quartz crystals of primary rocks by exploiting deficiencies in their <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">lattice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These involve chemical <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> of rock<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>, and a number of physical weathering processes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frost_shattering" title="Frost shattering">frost shattering</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Haloclasty" title="Haloclasty">haloclasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The main process is <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(geology)" title="Abrasion (geology)">abrasion</a> through transport, including <a href="/wiki/Fluvial" title="Fluvial">fluvial</a> <a href="/wiki/Comminution" title="Comminution">comminution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">aeolian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attrition_(weathering)" title="Attrition (weathering)">attrition</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial" title="Glacial">glacial</a> grinding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is in semi-arid environments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> that substantial quantities of silt are produced. Silt is sometimes known as "rock flour" or "stone dust", especially when produced by glacial action. Mineralogically, silt is composed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">Sedimentary rock</a> composed mainly of silt is known as <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstone</a>. <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction" title="Liquefaction">Liquefaction</a> created by a strong earthquake is silt suspended in water that is hydrodynamically forced up from below ground level.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grain_size_criteria">Grain size criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grain size criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">Udden–Wentworth scale</a> (due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krumbein" title="Krumbein">Krumbein</a>), silt particles range between 0.0039 and 0.0625 mm, larger than <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> but smaller than <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> particles. ISO 14688 grades silts between 0.002 mm and 0.063 mm. In actuality, silt is chemically distinct from clay, and unlike clay, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">grains</a> of silt are approximately the same size in all dimensions; furthermore, their size ranges overlap. Clays are formed from thin plate-shaped particles held together by electrostatic forces, so present a cohesion. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Agriculture" title="U.S. Department of Agriculture">U.S. Department of Agriculture</a> Soil Texture Classification system, the sand-silt distinction is made at the 0.05 mm particle size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The USDA system has been adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO). In the <a href="/wiki/Unified_Soil_Classification_System" title="Unified Soil Classification System">Unified Soil Classification System</a> (USCS) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AASHTO" title="AASHTO">AASHTO</a> Soil Classification system, the sand-silt distinction is made at the 0.075 mm particle size (i.e., material passing the #200 <a href="/wiki/Sieve" title="Sieve">sieve</a>). Silts and clays are distinguished mechanically by their <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plasticity</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silt is easily transported in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> or other liquid and is fine enough to be carried long distances by air in the form of <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>. Thick deposits of silty material resulting from deposition by <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">aeolian processes</a> are often called <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a>. Silt and clay contribute to <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a> in water. Silt is transported by <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a> or by water currents in the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>. When silt appears as a pollutant in water the phenomenon is known as <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a>.</p>
<p>Silt, deposited by annual floods along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>, created the rich, fertile soil that sustained the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptian</a> civilization. Silt deposited by the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> throughout the 20th century has decreased due to a system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levees" title="Levees">levees</a>, contributing to the disappearance of protective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_islands" title="Barrier islands">barrier islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_Delta" title="Mississippi River Delta">delta</a> region surrounding <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In south east Bangladesh, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noakhali_district" title="Noakhali district">Noakhali district</a>, cross dams were built in the 1960s whereby silt gradually started forming new land called "chars". The district of Noakhali has gained more than 28 square miles (73 km<sup>2</sup>) of land in the past 50 years.</p>
<p>With Dutch funding, the Bangladeshi government began to help develop older chars in the late 1970s, and the effort has since become a multi-agency operation building roads, <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">culverts</a>, embankments, cyclone shelters, toilets and ponds, as well as distributing land to settlers. By fall 2010, the program will have allotted some 27,000 acres (100 km<sup>2</sup>) to 21,000 families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A main source of silt in urban rivers is disturbance of soil by <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> activity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A main source in rural rivers is <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> from plowing of farm fields, <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clearcutting</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> treatment of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fertile black silt of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_river" title="Nile river">Nile river</a>'s banks is a symbol of rebirth, associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_pantheon" title="Egyptian pantheon">Egyptian god</a> <a href="/wiki/Anubis" title="Anubis">Anubis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHart198622Freeman199791_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart198622Freeman199791-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
