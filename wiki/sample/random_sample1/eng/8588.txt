<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sylph</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Sylph</b> (also called <b>sylphid</b>) is a mythological spirit of the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term originates in the 16th century works of <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a>, who describes sylphs as invisible beings of the air, his elementals of air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the term sylph itself originates with Paracelsus, there is relatively little pre-Paracelsian legend and mythology that can be confidently associated with it, but a significant number of subsequent literary and occult works have been inspired by the idea. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Alfred_Vaughan" title="Robert Alfred Vaughan">Robert Alfred Vaughan</a> noted that "the wild but poetical fantasies" of Paracelsus had probably exercised a larger influence over his age and the subsequent one than is generally supposed, particularly on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosicrucians" title="Rosicrucians">Rosicrucians</a>, but that through the 18th century they had become reduced to "machinery for the playwright" and "opera figurantes with wings of gauze and spangles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word is possibly a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> from Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/silvestris" title="wikt:silvestris">sylvestris</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nympha" title="wikt:nympha">nympha</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edgecombe_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edgecombe-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>sylvestris</i> being a common synonym for sylph in Paracelsus. Anthon and Trollope note a similar usage in the <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>, where <i>silvestris</i> is taken as an elliptical form of <i>nympha silvestris</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> ("forest nymph"). <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">Jacob Grimm</a> uses this phrase as a gloss for the Anglo-Saxon <i>wudu-mær</i> (roughly equivalent to "woodmare"), which he also takes as a metaphorical name for an <a href="/wiki/Echo" title="Echo">echo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont" title="Jan Baptist van Helmont">Jan Baptist van Helmont</a>, a near contemporary of Paracelsus and coiner of the word "gas," uses <i>sylvestris</i> in the sense of "wild" to describe gaseous emissions, which may be connected to the Paracelsian usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Thorpe's <i>Northern Mythology</i> connects the adjective <i>sylvestres</i> and the related <i>silvaticas</i> to European <a href="/wiki/Wild_man" title="Wild man">wild man</a> legends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A related idea is that "sylph" is from a hyper-urbane respelling of a Latin neologism <i>silves</i>, but in either case this connection to the Latin root <i>silva</i> ("forest") is supported by Paracelsus' use of <i>sylphes</i> as a synonym for <i>schrötlein</i>, a German word for a tree spirit or especially an earth spirit in his <i>Liber de Sanguine ultra Mortem</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternative theory is that it derives from the Ancient Greek σίλφη (silphē), which a number of etymological sources gloss as "moth,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> though the most authoritative reference for Ancient Greek, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Greek%E2%80%93English_Lexicon" title="A Greek–English Lexicon">A Greek–English Lexicon</a></i>, is unaware of this sense and defines it as a "cockroach" or "book worm." French etymological sources often derive it from a Latin word <i>sylphus</i>, glossed as "<a href="/wiki/Genius_(mythology)" title="Genius (mythology)">genius</a>" (in the Latin sense, a type of spirit) and only known from inscriptions rather than literary Latin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsches_W%C3%B6rterbuch" title="Deutsches Wörterbuch">Deutsches Wörterbuch</a></i> however indicates that this idea arises from a 19th-century misreading of the inscription in question. Similarly, the σίλφη etymology can be dismissed from the lack of an objective historical and thus semantic connection. The idea of an intentional portmanteau is also considered doubtful, though extensive evidence can be found that indicates that Paracelsus considered the various sylvan spirits and wild men of legend to be examples of sylphs, which he occasionally took to be earth elementals rather than air elementals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alchemy_and_literature">Alchemy and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alchemy and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger,_bourbon_whiskey_label,_ca._1860.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2954" data-file-width="3630" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger%2C_bourbon_whiskey_label%2C_ca._1860.jpg/220px-La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger%2C_bourbon_whiskey_label%2C_ca._1860.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger%2C_bourbon_whiskey_label%2C_ca._1860.jpg/330px-La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger%2C_bourbon_whiskey_label%2C_ca._1860.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger%2C_bourbon_whiskey_label%2C_ca._1860.jpg/440px-La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger%2C_bourbon_whiskey_label%2C_ca._1860.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Sylphide_by_A.M._Bininger,_bourbon_whiskey_label,_ca._1860.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
<p>The Swiss German physician and alchemist Paracelsus first coined the term sylph in the 16th century to describe an air spirit in his overarching scheme of elemental spirits associated with the four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_elements" title="Classical elements">Classical elements</a>. Paracelsus drew from earlier sources, but his systematic treatment of the idea was definitive, with the names of three of the four types having originated in his works. The other three elemental spirits named were <a href="/wiki/Gnome" title="Gnome">Gnomes</a> (earth), <a href="/wiki/Salamanders_in_folklore_and_legend" title="Salamanders in folklore and legend">Salamanders</a> (fire), and <a href="/wiki/Undine" title="Undine">Undines</a> (water). These ideas were adopted in <a href="/wiki/Rosicrucianism" title="Rosicrucianism">Rosicrucianism</a> and were widely encountered in subsequent <a href="/wiki/Hermeticism" title="Hermeticism">hermetic</a> literature.</p>
<p>In the <i>Liber de Nymphis</i> of the <i>Philosophia Magna</i>, Paracelsus discusses the characteristics of the elementals at length. Sylphs, he says, are rougher, coarser, taller, and stronger<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> than humans. The elementals are said to be able to move through their own elements as human beings move through air. Because of this, sylphs are the closest to humans in his conception because they move through air like we do, while in fire they burn, in water they drown, and in earth, they get stuck.</p>
<p>The French pseudo-novel <i><a href="/wiki/Comte_de_Gabalis" title="Comte de Gabalis">Comte de Gabalis</a></i> (1670) was important in passing sylphs into the literary sphere. It appears to have originated the derivative term "sylphid" (French <i>sylphide</i>), which it uses as the feminine counterpart to "sylph". While modern scholars consider <i>Comte de Gabalis</i> to have been intended as a satire of occult philosophy, many of its contemporaries considered it to be an earnest exposition of occult lore. Its author, Abbé de Montfaucon de Villars, was assassinated on the road in 1673 and one rumor had it that he had been killed by a gang of sylphs for disclosing their secrets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the best-known discussions of sylphs comes with <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>. In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rape_of_the_Lock" title="Rape of the Lock">Rape of the Lock</a></i> (final ed. 1717), Pope <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirizes</a> French Rosicrucian and alchemical writings when he invents a theory to explain the sylph. In a parody of heroic poetry and the "dark" and "mysterious" alchemical literature, and in particular the sometimes esoterically Classical heroic poetry of the 18th century in England and France, Pope pretends to have a new alchemy, in which the sylph is the mystically, chemically condensed <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">humors</a> of peevish women. In Pope's poem, women who are full of <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> and vanity turn into sylphs when they die because their spirits are too full of dark vapors to ascend to the skies. Belinda, the heroine of Pope's poem, is attended by a small army of sylphs, who foster her vanity and guard her beauty.</p>
<p>The poem is a parody of Paracelsian ideas, inasmuch as Pope imitates the pseudo-science of alchemy to explain the seriousness with which vain women approach the dressing room. In a slight parody of the divine battle in Pope's <i>Rape of the Lock</i>, when the Baron of the poem attempts to cut a lock of Belinda's hair, the sylphs interpose their airy bodies between the blades of the scissors (to no effect whatsoever).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ariel, the chief sylph in <i>the Rape of the Lock</i>, has the same name as <a href="/wiki/Prospero" title="Prospero">Prospero</a>'s servant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ariel_(Shakespeare)" title="Ariel (Shakespeare)">Ariel</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">The Tempest</a></i> (ca. 1611), and Shakespeare's character is described literally as an "airy spirit" in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dramatis_personae" title="Dramatis personae">dramatis personae</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This name is generally thought to have been original with Shakespeare, though the exact inspiration for the character is unclear. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-17">[17]</a></sup> Pope explicitly cited <i>Comte de Gabalis</i> as a source for elemental lore in the dedication.</p>
<p>In the 1778 British novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sylph" title="The Sylph">The Sylph</a></i>, a sylph appears as a guardian spirit for the female protagonist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1765, the French author <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Marmontel" title="Jean-François Marmontel">Jean-François Marmontel</a> had found the sylph legend notable enough that he included among his <i>Moral Tales</i> the story of "the Sylph-Husband," in which a young woman obsessed with the idea of marrying a sylph is deluded into falling in love with her arranged-husband after he impersonates one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Willow, a character in <a href="/wiki/Terry_Brooks" title="Terry Brooks">Terry Brooks</a>' <a href="/wiki/Magic_Kingdom_of_Landover" title="Magic Kingdom of Landover">Magic Kingdom of Landover</a> series of novels (1986), is a sylph and the wife of protagonist <a href="/wiki/Ben_Holiday" title="Ben Holiday">Ben Holiday</a>. She is the daughter of the River Master and a wood elemental, giving her pale green skin and emerald hair. Her dual nature is reflected in the fact that she must transform into a willow tree once every 21 days to maintain her life force. She has a tense and distant relationship with her father, as her existence serves as a permanent reminder to him of the brief relationship he desires to reclaim, but never can. And so it is to her mother that she turns for guidance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theater">Theater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The famous ballet <i><a href="/wiki/La_Sylphide" title="La Sylphide">La Sylphide</a></i> ("The (Female) Sylph", Paris, 1832) is a prominent example of sylph lore in theater in the 19th century. It appeared in a second version in Denmark in 1836. A similarly themed opera <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mountain_Sylph" title="The Mountain Sylph">The Mountain Sylph</a></i> appeared in England in 1834.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edgecombe_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edgecombe-4">[4]</a></sup> Sylphs again took to the stage in the 1909 ballet <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Sylphides" title="Les Sylphides">Les Sylphides</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fairy_link">Fairy link</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fairy link">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of their association with the ballet <i>La Sylphide</i>, where sylphs are identified with <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairies</a> and the medieval legends of fairyland, as well as a confusion with other "airy spirits" (e.g., in William Shakespeare's <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i>), a slender girl may be referred to as a sylph.</p>
<p><i>Sylph</i> has passed into general language as a term for minor spirits, elementals, or faeries of the air. <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">Fantasy</a> authors will sometimes employ sylphs in their fiction, for example creating giant artistic clouds in the skies with airy wings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
