<p><b>Psychological projection</b> is a theory in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> in which humans defend themselves against their own unconscious impulses or qualities (both positive and negative) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denying" title="Denying">denying</a> their existence in themselves while <a href="/wiki/Attribution_(psychology)" title="Attribution (psychology)">attributing</a> them to others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, a person who is habitually <a href="/wiki/Rudeness" title="Rudeness">rude</a> may constantly accuse other people of being rude. It incorporates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blame_shifting" title="Blame shifting">blame shifting</a>.</p>
<p>According to some research, the projection of one's unconscious qualities onto others is a common process in everyday life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_precursors">Historical precursors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical precursors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A prominent precursor in the formulation of the projection principle was <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Vico" title="Giambattista Vico">Giambattista Vico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1841, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Feuerbach" title="Ludwig Feuerbach">Ludwig Feuerbach</a> was the first <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">enlightenment</a> thinker to employ this concept as the basis for a systematic critique of religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Babylon Talmud (500 CE) notes the human tendency toward projection and warns against it: "Do not taunt your neighbour with the blemish you yourself have."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychoanalytic_developments">Psychoanalytic developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Psychoanalytic developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Projection (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Projektion</i></span>) was conceptualised by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freud</a> in his letters to <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Fliess" title="Wilhelm Fliess">Wilhelm Fliess</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and further refined by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Abraham" title="Karl Abraham">Karl Abraham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Freud" title="Anna Freud">Anna Freud</a>. Freud considered that, in projection, thoughts, motivations, desires, and feelings that cannot be accepted as one's own are dealt with by being placed in the outside world and attributed to someone else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> What the ego repudiates is <a href="/wiki/Splitting_(psychology)" title="Splitting (psychology)">split off</a> and placed in another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Freud would later come to believe that projection did not take place arbitrarily, but rather seized on and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exaggerated" title="Exaggerated">exaggerated</a> an element that already existed on a small scale in the other person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (The related defence of <a href="/wiki/Projective_identification" title="Projective identification">projective identification</a> differs from projection in that there the other person is expected to become identified with the <a href="/wiki/Impulse_(psychology)" title="Impulse (psychology)">impulse</a> or desire projected outside,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> so that the self maintains a connection with what is projected, in contrast to the total repudiation of projection proper.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Melanie_Klein" title="Melanie Klein">Melanie Klein</a> saw the projection of good parts of the self as leading potentially to over-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idealisation_and_devaluation" title="Idealisation and devaluation">idealisation</a> of the object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Equally, it may be one's conscience that is projected, in an attempt to escape its control: a more benign version of this allows one to come to terms with outside authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_examples">Theoretical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Projection tends to come to the fore in normal people at times of personal or political crisis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but is more commonly found in the neurotic or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychotic" title="Psychotic">psychotic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in personalities functioning at a primitive level as in <a href="/wiki/Narcissistic_personality_disorder" title="Narcissistic personality disorder">narcissistic personality disorder</a> or <a href="/wiki/Borderline_personality_disorder" title="Borderline personality disorder">borderline personality disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabbard2010_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gabbard2010-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a> considered that the unacceptable parts of the personality represented by the <a href="/wiki/Shadow_(psychology)" title="Shadow (psychology)">Shadow</a> archetype were particularly likely to give rise to projection, both small-scale and on a national/international basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_and_macro_projection_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-micro_and_macro_projection-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie-Louise_Von_Franz" title="Marie-Louise Von Franz">Marie-Louise Von Franz</a> extended her view of projection, stating that "wherever known reality stops, where we touch the unknown, there we project an <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetypal</a> image".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Psychological projection is one of the <a href="/wiki/Medical_explanations_of_bewitchment" title="Medical explanations of bewitchment">medical explanations of bewitchment</a> used to explain the behavior of the afflicted children at <a href="/wiki/Salem_witch_trials" title="Salem witch trials">Salem</a> in 1692. The historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Demos" title="John Demos">John Demos</a> asserts that the symptoms of bewitchment experienced by the afflicted girls were due to the girls undergoing psychological projection of repressed aggression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_examples">Practical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Practical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Victim_blaming" title="Victim blaming">Victim blaming</a>:</b> The victim of someone else's accident or bad luck may be offered criticism, the theory being that the victim may be at fault for having attracted the other person's hostility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Projection of marital guilt:</b> Thoughts of <a href="/wiki/Infidelity" title="Infidelity">infidelity</a> to a partner may be <a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">unconsciously</a> projected in self-defence on to the partner in question, so that the <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(emotion)" title="Guilt (emotion)">guilt</a> attached to the thoughts can be repudiated or turned to <a href="/wiki/Blame" title="Blame">blame</a> instead, in a process linked to <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Bullying:</b> A <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">bully</a> may project his/her own feelings of <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability" title="Vulnerability">vulnerability</a> onto the target(s) of the bullying activity. Despite the fact that a bully's typically denigrating activities are aimed at the bully's targets, the true source of such negativity is ultimately almost always found in the bully's own sense of personal <a href="/wiki/Emotional_security" title="Emotional security">insecurity</a> and/or vulnerability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Such aggressive projections of displaced negative emotions can occur anywhere from the micro-level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationships" title="Interpersonal relationships">interpersonal relationships</a>, all the way up through to the macro-level of international politics, or even international armed conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_and_macro_projection_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-micro_and_macro_projection-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Projection of general guilt:</b> Projection of a severe conscience<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> is another form of defense, one which may be linked to the making of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_accusations" title="False accusations">false accusations</a>, personal or political.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_and_macro_projection_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-micro_and_macro_projection-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Projection of hope:</b> Also, in a more positive light, a patient may sometimes project his or her feelings of <a href="/wiki/Hope" title="Hope">hope</a> onto the therapist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-projection">Counter-projection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Counter-projection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jung wrote, "All projections provoke counter-projection when the object is unconscious of the quality projected upon it by the subject."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Thus, what is unconscious in the recipient will be projected back onto the projector, precipitating a form of mutual <a href="/wiki/Acting_out" title="Acting out">acting out</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a rather different usage, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Stack_Sullivan" title="Harry Stack Sullivan">Harry Stack Sullivan</a> saw counter-projection in the therapeutic context as a way of warding off the <a href="/wiki/Compulsive_behavior" title="Compulsive behavior">compulsive</a> re-enactment of a <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">psychological trauma</a>, by emphasising the difference between the current situation and the projected <a href="/wiki/Fixation_(psychology)" title="Fixation (psychology)">obsession</a> with the perceived perpetrator of the original trauma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_approaches">Clinical approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clinical approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Drawing on <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Allport" title="Gordon Allport">Gordon Allport</a>'s idea of the expression of self onto activities and objects, projective techniques have been devised to aid personality assessment, including the <a href="/wiki/Rorschach_test" title="Rorschach test">Rorschach</a> ink-blots and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thematic_Apperception_Test" title="Thematic Apperception Test">Thematic Apperception Test</a> (TAT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Projection may help a fragile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Id,_ego,_and_super-ego" title="Id, ego, and super-ego">ego</a> reduce <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>, but at the cost of a certain <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(psychology)" title="Dissociation (psychology)">dissociation</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Dissociative_identity_disorder" title="Dissociative identity disorder">dissociative identity disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In extreme cases, an individual's personality may end up becoming critically depleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In such cases, therapy may be required which would include the slow rebuilding of the personality through the "taking back" of such projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Psychological_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some studies were critical of Freud's theory. Research supports the existence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False-consensus_effect" title="False-consensus effect">false-consensus effect</a> whereby humans have a broad tendency to believe that others are similar to themselves, and thus "project" their personal traits onto others. This applies to good traits as well as bad traits and is not a defense mechanism for denying the existence of the trait within the self.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Empirical_Findings_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Empirical_Findings-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instead, Newman, Duff, and Baumeister (1997) proposed a new model of defensive projection. In this view, <a href="/wiki/Psychological_repression" title="Psychological repression">repressors</a> try to <a href="/wiki/Thought_suppression" title="Thought suppression">suppress thoughts</a> of their undesirable traits, and these efforts make those trait categories highly accessible—so that they are then used all the more often when forming impressions of others. The projection is then only a by-product of the real defensive mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
