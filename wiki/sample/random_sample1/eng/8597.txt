<p><b>Ventricular fibrillation</b> (<b>V-fib</b> or <b>VF</b>) is when the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> quivers instead of pumping due to disorganized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_activity" title="Electrical activity">electrical activity</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2011-1">[1]</a></sup> It results in <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loss_of_consciousness" title="Loss of consciousness">loss of consciousness</a> and no <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bal2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bal2016-2">[2]</a></sup> This is followed by irreversible <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> in the absence of treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2011_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2011-1">[1]</a></sup> Ventricular fibrillation is found initially in about 10% of people in cardiac arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bal2016_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bal2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ventricular fibrillation can occur due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_heart_disease" title="Coronary heart disease">coronary heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valvular_heart_disease" title="Valvular heart disease">valvular heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiomyopathy" title="Cardiomyopathy">cardiomyopathy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brugada_syndrome" title="Brugada syndrome">Brugada syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_QT_syndrome" title="Long QT syndrome">long QT syndrome</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Intracranial_hemorrhage" title="Intracranial hemorrhage">intracranial hemorrhage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2011_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bal2016_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bal2016-2">[2]</a></sup> Diagnosis is by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" title="Electrocardiogram">electrocardiogram</a> (ECG) showing irregular unformed <a href="/wiki/QRS_complex" title="QRS complex">QRS complexes</a> without any clear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P_waves" title="P waves">P waves</a>. An important <a href="/wiki/Differential_diagnosis" title="Differential diagnosis">differential diagnosis</a> is <a href="/wiki/Torsades_de_pointes" title="Torsades de pointes">torsades de pointes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bal2016_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bal2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Treatment is with <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">cardiopulmonary resuscitation</a> (CPR) and <a href="/wiki/Defibrillation" title="Defibrillation">defibrillation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Defibrillation#Change_to_a_biphasic_waveform" title="Defibrillation">Biphasic defibrillation</a> may be better than monophasic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHA2015Part1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHA2015Part1-3">[3]</a></sup> The medication <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">epinephrine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Amiodarone" title="Amiodarone">amiodarone</a> may be given if initial treatments are not effective. Rates of survival are about 46%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bal2016_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bal2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ventricular fibrillation is a cause of <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudden_cardiac_death" title="Sudden cardiac death">sudden cardiac death</a>. The ventricular muscle twitches randomly rather than contracting in a co-ordinated fashion (from the apex of the heart to the outflow of the ventricles), and so the <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricles</a> fail to pump blood around the body - because of this, it is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> rhythm, and patients in V-fib should be treated with <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">cardiopulmonary resuscitation</a> and prompt <a href="/wiki/Defibrillation" title="Defibrillation">defibrillation</a>. Left untreated, ventricular fibrillation is rapidly fatal as the vital organs of the body, including the heart, are starved of oxygen, and as a result patients in this rhythm will not be conscious or responsive to stimuli. Prior to cardiac arrest, patients may complain of varying symptoms depending on the underlying <a href="#Causes">cause</a>. Patients may exhibit signs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agonal_breathing" title="Agonal breathing">agonal breathing</a>, which to the layperson can look like normal spontaneous breathing, but it is in fact a sign of <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">hypoperfusion</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a>.</p>
<p>It has an appearance on <a href="/wiki/Electrocardiography" title="Electrocardiography">electrocardiography</a> of irregular electrical activity with no discernable pattern. It may be described as 'coarse' or 'fine' depending on its amplitude, or as progressing from coarse to fine V-fib. Coarse V-fib may be more responsive to defibrillation, while fine V-fib can mimic the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Asystole" title="Asystole">asystole</a> on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defibrillator" title="Defibrillator">defibrillator</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_monitor" title="Cardiac monitor">cardiac monitor</a> set to a low <a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a>. Some clinicians may attempt to defibrillate fine V-fib in the hope that it can be reverted to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_rhythm" title="Cardiac rhythm">cardiac rhythm</a> compatible with life, whereas others will deliver <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">CPR</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drugs</a> as described in the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_cardiac_life_support" title="Advanced cardiac life support">advanced cardiac life support</a> protocols in an attempt to increase its amplitude and the odds of successful defibrillation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ventricular fibrillation has been described as "chaotic asynchronous fractionated activity of the heart" (Moe et al. 1964). A more complete definition is that ventricular fibrillation is a "turbulent, disorganized electrical activity of the heart in such a way that the recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" title="Electrocardiogram">electrocardiographic</a> deflections continuously change in shape, magnitude and direction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ventricular fibrillation most commonly occurs within <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseased</a> hearts, and, in the vast majority of cases, is a manifestation of underlying ischemic heart disease. Ventricular fibrillation is also seen in those with <a href="/wiki/Cardiomyopathy" title="Cardiomyopathy">cardiomyopathy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myocarditis" title="Myocarditis">myocarditis</a>, and other heart pathologies. In addition, it is seen with electrolyte disturbances and overdoses of cardiotoxic drugs. It is also notable that ventricular fibrillation occurs where there is no discernible heart pathology or other evident cause, the so-called idiopathic ventricular fibrillation.</p>
<p>Idiopathic ventricular fibrillation occurs with a reputed incidence of approximately 1% of all cases of out-of-hospital arrest, as well as 3%-9% of the cases of ventricular fibrillation unrelated to <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a>, and 14% of all ventricular fibrillation resuscitations in patients under the age of 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It follows then that, on the basis of the fact that ventricular fibrillation itself is common, idiopathic ventricular fibrillation accounts for an appreciable mortality. Recently described syndromes such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brugada_Syndrome" title="Brugada Syndrome">Brugada Syndrome</a> may give clues to the underlying mechanism of ventricular arrhythmias. In the Brugada syndrome, changes may be found in the resting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECG" title="ECG">ECG</a> with evidence of <a href="/wiki/Right_bundle_branch_block" title="Right bundle branch block">right bundle branch block</a> (RBBB) and ST elevation in the chest leads V1-V3, with an underlying propensity to sudden cardiac death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The relevance of this is that theories of the underlying pathophysiology and electrophysiology must account for the occurrence of fibrillation in the apparent "healthy" heart. It is evident that there are mechanisms at work that we do not fully appreciate and understand. Investigators are exploring new techniques of detecting and understanding the underlying mechanisms of sudden cardiac death in these patients without pathological evidence of underlying heart disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Familial conditions that predispose individuals to developing ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death are often the result of gene mutations that affect cellular transmembrane ion channels. For example, in Brugada Syndrome, sodium channels are affected. In certain forms of long QT syndrome, the potassium inward rectifier channel is affected.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abnormal_automaticity">Abnormal automaticity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Abnormal automaticity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Automaticity is a measure of the propensity of a fiber to initiate an impulse spontaneously. The product of a <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxic</a> myocardium can be hyperirritable myocardial cells. These may then act as pacemakers. The ventricles are then being stimulated by more than one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacemaker" title="Pacemaker">pacemaker</a>. Scar and dying tissue is inexcitable, but around these areas usually lies a penumbra of hypoxic tissue that is excitable. Ventricular excitability may generate <a href="/wiki/Re-entry_ventricular_arrhythmia" title="Re-entry ventricular arrhythmia">re-entry ventricular arrhythmia</a>.</p>
<p>It is interesting to note that most cardiac myocardial cells with an associated increased propensity to arrhythmia development have an associated loss of <a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">membrane potential</a>. That is, the maximum diastolic potential is less negative and therefore exists closer to the <a href="/wiki/Threshold_potential" title="Threshold potential">threshold potential</a>. Cellular depolarisation can be due to a raised external concentration of <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> ions K<sup>+</sup>, a decreased intracellular concentration of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> ions Na<sup>+</sup>, increased permeability to Na<sup>+</sup>, or a decreased permeability to K<sup>+</sup>. The ionic basic automaticity is the net gain of an intracellular positive charge during diastole in the presence of a voltage-dependent channel activated by potentials negative to –50 to –60 mV.</p>
<p>Myocardial cells are exposed to different environments. Normal cells may be exposed to hyperkalaemia; abnormal cells may be perfused by normal environment. For example, with a healed myocardial infarction, abnormal cells can be exposed to an abnormal environment such as with a myocardial infarction with myocardial ischaemia. In conditions such as myocardial ischaemia, possible mechanism of arrhythmia generation include the resulting decreased internal K<sup>+</sup> concentration, the increased external K<sup>+</sup> concentration, norepinephrine release and acidosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When myocardial cell are exposed to hyperkaliemia, the maximum diastolic potential is depolarized as a result of the alteration of Ik1 potassium current, whose intensity and direction is strictly dependent on intracellular and extracellular potassium concentrations. With Ik1 suppressed, an hyperpolarizing effect is lost and therefore there can be activation of <a href="/wiki/Funny_current" title="Funny current">funny current</a> even in myocardial cells (which is normally suppressed by the hyperpolarizing effect of coexisting potassium currents). This can lead to the instauration of automaticity in ischemic tissue.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-entry">Re-entry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Re-entry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The role of re-entry or <b>circus motion</b> was demonstrated separately by Mines and Garrey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Mines created a ring of excitable tissue by cutting the atria out of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray_fish" title="Ray fish">ray fish</a>. Garrey cut out a similar ring from the <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtle</a> ventricle. They were both able to show that, if a ring of excitable tissue was stimulated at a single point, the subsequent waves of depolarisation would pass around the ring. The waves eventually meet and cancel each other out, but, if an area of transient block occurred with a <a href="/wiki/Refractory_period_(physiology)" title="Refractory period (physiology)">refractory period</a> that blocked one wavefront and subsequently allowed the other to proceed retrogradely over the other path, then a self-sustaining circus movement phenomenon would result. For this to happen, however, it is necessary that there be some form of non-uniformity. In practice, this may be an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ischemic" title="Ischemic">ischemic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infarct" title="Infarct">infarcted</a> myocardium, or underlying <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_scarring" title="Myocardial scarring">scar tissue</a>.</p>
<p>It is possible to think of the advancing wave of depolarisation as a dipole with a head and a tail. The length of the refractory period and the time taken for the dipole to travel a certain distance—the propagation velocity—will determine whether such a circumstance will arise for re-entry to occur. Factors that promote re-entry would include a slow-propagation velocity, a short refractory period with a sufficient size of ring of conduction tissue. These would enable a dipole to reach an area that had been refractory and is now able to be depolarised with continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Wavefront" title="Wavefront">wavefront</a>.</p>
<p>In clinical practice, therefore, factors that would lead to the right conditions to favour such re-entry mechanisms include increased heart size through <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophy" title="Hypertrophy">hypertrophy</a> or dilatation, drugs which alter the length of the refractory period and areas of cardiac disease. Therefore, the substrate of ventricular fibrillation is transient or permanent conduction block. Block due either to areas of damaged or refractory tissue leads to areas of myocardium for initiation and perpetuation of fibrillation through the phenomenon of re-entry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Triggered_activity">Triggered activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Triggered activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Triggered activity can occur due to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Afterdepolarization" title="Afterdepolarization">afterdepolarisations</a>. These are depolarising oscillations in the membrane voltage induced by preceding action potentials. These can occur before or after full repolarisation of the fiber and as such are termed either early (EADs) or delayed afterdepolarisations (DADs). All afterdepolarisations may not reach threshold potential, but, if they do, they can trigger another afterdepolarisation, and thus self-perpetuate.</p>

<p>Myofibre break-up, abbreviated MFB, is associated with ventricular fibrillation leading to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15837088_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15837088-13">[13]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histomorphology" title="Histomorphology">Histomorphologically</a>, MFB is characterized by fractures of the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_muscle" title="Cardiac muscle">cardiac myofibres</a> perpendicular to their long axis, with squaring of the myofibre <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nuclei</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Defibrillation" title="Defibrillation">Defibrillation</a> is the definitive treatment of ventricular fibrillation, whereby an electrical current is applied to the ventricular mass either directly or externally through pads or paddles, with the aim of depolarising enough of the myocardium for co-ordinated contractions to occur again. The use of this is often dictated around the world by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Cardiac_Life_Support" title="Advanced Cardiac Life Support">Advanced Cardiac Life Support</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Life_Support" title="Advanced Life Support">Advanced Life Support</a> algorithms, which is taught to medical practitioners including <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">doctors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse" title="Nurse">nurses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paramedic" title="Paramedic">paramedics</a> and also advocates the use of <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drugs</a>, predominantly <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">epinephrine</a>, after every second unsuccessful attempt at defibrillation, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">cardiopulmonary resuscitation</a> (CPR) in between defibrillation attempts. Though ALS/ACLS algorithms encourage the use of drugs, they state first and foremost that defibrillation should not be delayed for any other intervention and that adequate cardiopulmonary resuscitation be delivered with minimal interruption.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Precordial_thump" title="Precordial thump">precordial thump</a> is a manoeuver promoted as a mechanical alternative to defibrillation. Some advanced life support algorithms advocate its use once and only in the case of witnessed and monitored V-fib arrests as the likelihood of it successfully <a href="/wiki/Cardioversion" title="Cardioversion">cardioverting</a> a patient are small and this diminishes quickly in the first minute of onset.</p>
<p>Patients who survive a 'V-fib arrest' and who make a good recovery from this are often considered for implantation of an <a href="/wiki/Implantable_cardioverter-defibrillator" title="Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator">implantable cardioverter-defibrillator</a>, which can quickly deliver this same life-saving defibrillation should another episode of ventricular fibrillation occur outside a hospital environment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sudden <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> is the leading cause of death in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialised" title="Industrialised">industrialised</a> <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a>. It exacts a significant mortality with approximately 70,000 to 90,000 sudden cardiac deaths each year in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and survival rates are only 2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The majority of these deaths are due to ventricular fibrillation secondary to <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a>, or "heart attack".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> During ventricular fibrillation, <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a> drops to zero, and, unless remedied promptly, death usually ensues within minutes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventricular_fibrillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lyman Brewer suggests that the first recorded account of ventricular fibrillation dates as far back as 1500 BC, and can be found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebers_papyrus" title="Ebers papyrus">Ebers papyrus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>. The extract recorded 3500 years ago may even date from as far back as 3500 BC. It states: "When the heart is diseased, its work is imperfectly performed: the vessels proceeding from the heart become inactive, so that you cannot feel them … if the heart trembles, has little power and sinks, the disease is advanced and death is near." A book authored by Jo Miles suggests that it may even go back farther. Tests done on frozen remains found in the Himalayas seemed fairly conclusive that the first known case of ventricular fibrillation dates back to at least 2500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whether this is a description of ventricular fibrillation is debatable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The next recorded description occurs 3000 years later and is recorded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesalius" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a>, who described the appearance of "worm-like" movements of the heart in animals prior to death.</p>
<p>The significance and clinical importance of these observations and descriptions possibly of ventricular fibrillation were not recognised until John Erichsen in 1842 described ventricular fibrillation following the <a href="/wiki/Ligature_(medicine)" title="Ligature (medicine)">ligation</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_artery" title="Coronary artery">coronary artery</a> (Erichsen JE 1842). Subsequent to this in 1850, fibrillation was described by Ludwig and Hoffa when they demonstrated the provocation of ventricular fibrillation in an animal by applying a "<a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Faradic</a>" (electrical) current to the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1874, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Vulpian" title="Alfred Vulpian">Edmé Félix Alfred Vulpian</a> coined the term <i>mouvement fibrillaire</i>, a term that he seems to have used to describe both atrial and ventricular fibrillation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> John A. MacWilliam, a physiologist who had trained under Ludwig and who subsequently became Professor of Physiology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">University of Aberdeen</a>, gave an accurate description of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhythmia" title="Arrhythmia">arrhythmia</a> in 1887. This definition still holds today, and is interesting in the fact that his studies and description predate the use of <a href="/wiki/Electrocardiography" title="Electrocardiography">electrocardiography</a>. His description is as follows: "The ventricular muscle is thrown into a state of irregular arrhythmic contraction, whilst there is a great fall in the arterial blood pressure, the ventricles become dilated with blood as the rapid quivering movement of their walls is insufficient to expel their contents; the muscular action partakes of the nature of a rapid incoordinate twitching of the muscular tissue … The cardiac pump is thrown out of gear, and the last of its vital energy is dissipated in the violent and the prolonged turmoil of fruitless activity in the ventricular walls." MacWilliam spent many years working on ventricular fibrillation and was one of the first to show that ventricular fibrillation could be terminated by a series of induction shocks through the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" title="Electrocardiogram">electrocardiogram</a> recording of ventricular fibrillation was by August Hoffman in a paper published in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> At this time, two other researchers, Mines and Garrey, working separately, produced work demonstrating the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_fibrillation#Re-entry" title="Ventricular fibrillation">circus movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_fibrillation#Re-entry" title="Ventricular fibrillation">re-entry</a> as possible substrates for the generation of arrhythmias. This work was also accompanied by Lewis, who performed further outstanding work into the concept of "circus movement."</p>
<p>Later milestones include the work by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=W._J._Kerr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="W. J. Kerr (page does not exist)">W. J. Kerr</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=W._L._Bender&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="W. L. Bender (page does not exist)">W. L. Bender</a> in 1922, who produced an electrocardiogram showing <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_tachycardia" title="Ventricular tachycardia">ventricular tachycardia</a> evolving into ventricular fibrillation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The re-entry mechanism was also advocated by DeBoer, who showed that ventricular fibrillation could be induced in late systole with a single shock to a frog heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The concept of "R on T ectopics" was further brought out by Katz in 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This was called the “vulnerable period” by Wiggers and Wegria in 1940, who brought to attention the concept of the danger of <a href="/wiki/Premature_ventricular_contraction" title="Premature ventricular contraction">premature ventricular beats</a> occurring on a <a href="/wiki/T_wave" title="T wave">T wave</a>.</p>
<p>Another definition of VF was produced by Wiggers in 1940. He described ventricular fibrillation as "an incoordinate type of contraction which, despite a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolic_rate" title="Metabolic rate">metabolic rate</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myocardium" title="Myocardium">myocardium</a>, produces no useful beats. As a result, the arterial pressure falls abruptly to very low levels, and death results within six to eight minutes from anemia [ischemia] of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spontaneous conversion of ventricular fibrillation to a more benign rhythm is rare in all but small animals. <a href="/wiki/Defibrillation" title="Defibrillation">Defibrillation</a> is the process that converts ventricular fibrillation to a more benign rhythm. This is usually by application of an <a href="/wiki/Electric_shock" title="Electric shock">electric shock</a> to the myocardium and is discussed in detail in the relevant article.</p>
