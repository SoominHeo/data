<p><b>Deep Space Climate Observatory</b> (<b>DSCOVR</b>; formerly known as <b>Triana</b>, unofficially known as <b>GoreSat</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> observation and <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> launched by <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9_v1.1" title="Falcon 9 v1.1">Falcon 9</a> launch vehicle on February 11, 2015 from Cape Canaveral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was originally developed as a <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> satellite proposed in 1998 by then-<a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> for the purpose of Earth observation. It is in a <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_orbit" title="Lissajous orbit">Lissajous orbit</a> at the Sun-Earth <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian point</a>, 1,500,000 km (930,000 mi) from Earth, to monitor variable <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> condition, provide early warning of approaching <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejections</a> and observe phenomena on Earth including changes in ozone, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, dust and volcanic ash, cloud height, vegetation cover and climate. At this location it has a continuous view of the Sun and the sunlit side of the Earth. The satellite is orbiting the Sun-Earth L<sub>1</sub> point in a six-month period, with a spacecraft-Earth-Sun angle varying from 4 to 15 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It takes full-Earth pictures about every two hours and is able to process them faster than other <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>DSCOVR started orbiting around L<sub>1</sub> by June 8, 2015, just over 100 days after launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa20150608_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa20150608-8">[8]</a></sup> After the spacecraft arrived on site and entered its operational phase, NASA began releasing near-real time images of Earth through the EPIC instrument's website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epicsite_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-epicsite-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dscovr.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="360" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Dscovr.jpg/170px-Dscovr.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Dscovr.jpg/255px-Dscovr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Dscovr.jpg/340px-Dscovr.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dscovr.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Deep Space Climate Observatory</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Originally known as Triana, named after <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_de_Triana" title="Rodrigo de Triana">Rodrigo de Triana</a>, the first of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Columbus</a>'s crew to sight land in the Americas, the satellite's original purpose was to provide a near-continuous view of the entire Earth and make that live image available via the Internet. Gore hoped not only to advance science with these images, but also to raise awareness of the Earth itself, updating the influential <a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Marble" title="The Blue Marble"><i>Blue Marble</i></a> photograph taken by <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition to an imaging camera, a radiometer would take the first direct measurements of how much sunlight is reflected and emitted from the whole Earth (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>). This data could constitute a barometer for the process of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. The scientific goals expanded to measure the amount of solar energy reaching Earth, cloud patterns, weather systems, monitor the health of Earth's vegetation, and track the amount of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a> light reaching the surface through the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>.</p>
<p>In 1999, NASA's <a href="/wiki/NASA_Office_of_Inspector_General" title="NASA Office of Inspector General">Inspector General</a> reported that "the basic concept of the Triana mission was not peer reviewed", and "Triana's added science may not represent the best expenditure of NASA's limited science funding."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_George_W._Bush" title="Presidency of George W. Bush">Bush Administration</a> put the project on hold shortly after George W. Bush's inauguration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Members of the U.S. Congress asked the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> whether the project was worthwhile. The resulting report, released March 2000, stated that the mission was "strong and scientifically vital."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-Triana_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-Triana-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Triana was removed from its original launch opportunity on <a href="/wiki/STS-107" title="STS-107">STS-107</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">ill-fated <i>Columbia</i> mission</a> in 2003). The $100 million satellite remained in storage for the duration of the Bush administration. In November 2008 the satellite was removed from storage and began recertification for a possible launch on board a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> used part of his book <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Choice" title="Our Choice">Our Choice</a></i> (2009) as an attempt to revive debate on the DSCOVR payload. The book mentions legislative efforts by Senators <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Mikulski" title="Barbara Mikulski">Barbara Mikulski</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nelson" title="Bill Nelson">Bill Nelson</a> to get the satellite launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> NASA renamed the satellite Deep Space Climate Observatory (DSCOVR), in an attempt to regain support for the project. In February 2011, the Obama administration attempted to secure funding to re-purpose the DSCOVR spacecraft as a solar observatory to replace the aging <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">Advanced Composition Explorer</a> (ACE) spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2013 NASA cleared <i>DSCOVR</i> to proceed to the implementation phase targeting an early 2015 launch,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> which had been announced in December 2012 as launching on a <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> NASA <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> is providing management and systems engineering to the mission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="1091" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg/220px-Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg/330px-Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg/440px-Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory_spacecraft_diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram of DSCOVR</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>DSCOVR is built on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMEX-Lite" title="SMEX-Lite">SMEX-Lite</a> spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Satellite_bus" title="Satellite bus">bus</a>, and has a launch mass of approximately 570 kg (1,257 lb). The main science instrument sets are the Sun-observing Plasma Magnetometer (PlasMag) and the Earth-observing NIST Advanced Radiometer (NISTAR) and Earth Polychromatic Imaging Camera (EPIC). DSCOVR has two deployable solar arrays, a propulsion module, boom, and antenna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_spacecraft_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_spacecraft-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PlasMag">PlasMag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: PlasMag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Plasma-<a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">Magnetometer</a> (PlasMag) measures <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> for <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a> predictions. It has three instruments:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_plasmag_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_plasmag-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Magnetometer measures magnetic field.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Faraday_cup" title="Faraday cup">Faraday cup</a> measures positively charged particles.</li>
<li>Electrostatic analyzer measures electrons.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="EPIC">EPIC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: EPIC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1756" data-file-width="1788" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg/220px-Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg/330px-Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg/440px-Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth-DSCOVR-20150706-IFV.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The first DSCOVR EPIC image released by NASA, showing the full sunlit Earth from one million miles away, showing the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20150720_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa20150720-22">[22]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Earth Polychromatic Imaging Camera (EPIC) takes images of the sunlit side of Earth for various Earth sciences purposes, in 10 different channels from <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> to near <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">Ozone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> levels will be monitored, as well as cloud dynamics, properties of the land and vegetations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_epic_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_epic-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>EPIC has an aperture diameter of 30.5 cm (12.0 in), <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">f</a> 9.38, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FOV" title="FOV">FOV</a> of 0.61° and an angular sampling resolution of 1.07 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc#Symbols.2C_abbreviations_and_subdivisions" title="Minute of arc">arcsec</a>. Earth apparent diameter will vary from 0.45° to 0.53° full width. <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">Exposure</a> time for each of the 10 narrowband channels (317, 325, 340, 388, 443, 552, 680, 688, 764 and 779 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>) is about 40 ms, and the camera will produce 2048×2048 pixel images, but to increase number of downloadable images to 10 per hour the resolution will be averaged to 1024×1024 on board. The final resolution will be 25 km/pixel (16 mi/pixel).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_epic_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_epic-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="NISTAR">NISTAR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: NISTAR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>National Institute of Standards and Technology Advanced <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">Radiometer</a> (NISTAR) measures irradiance of the sunlit face of the Earth. This data is to be used to study changes in Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_budget" title="Radiation budget">radiation budget</a> caused by natural and human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_nistar_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_nistar-24">[24]</a></sup> The radiometer measures in four channels:</p>
<ul>
<li>For total radiation in ultraviolet, visible and infrared in range of 0.2-100 µm.</li>
<li>For reflected solar radiation in ultraviolet, visible and near infrared in range of 0.2-4 µm.</li>
<li>For reflected solar radiation in infrared in range of 0.7-4 µm.</li>
<li>For calibration purposes in range of 0.3-1 µm.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-launch_flight_test">Post-launch flight test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-launch flight test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_reusable_launch_system_development_program" title="SpaceX reusable launch system development program">SpaceX reusable launch system development program</a></div>
<p>SpaceX planned to conduct a <a href="/wiki/Flight_test" title="Flight test">test flight</a> where they would <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falcon_9_ocean_booster_landing_tests" title="Falcon 9 ocean booster landing tests">attempt to bring the first stage back through the atmosphere</a> and land the expended <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">first stage</a> on a 90-by-50-meter (300 ft × 160 ft) <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_spaceport_drone_ship" title="Autonomous spaceport drone ship">floating landing platform</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20141217_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf20141217-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20150208_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf20150208-27">[27]</a></sup> Relative to earlier tests, the first stage return on DSCOVR was much more challenging, especially in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">atmospheric reentry</a> due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_solar_system" title="Inner solar system">deep space</a> nature of the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Earth-Sun L1</a> launch trajectory for DSCOVR. SpaceX expected deceleration-force loads to be twice as high and rocket heating to quadruple over the reentry conditions on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falcon_9_Flight_14" title="Falcon 9 Flight 14">Falcon 9 Flight 14</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sx20150208_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-sx20150208-28">[28]</a></sup> Before the launch, the drone ship was in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_swell" title="Ocean swell">ocean surface conditions</a> that made the barge landing infeasible. Therefore, the landing platform attempt was called off and the first stage made an over-water soft landing instead. This continued the collection of returnable first stage test data on all the earlier phases of the flight test, and added data on stage survival following a high-speed, high-load atmospheric entry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20150211_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20150211-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On July 6, 2015, DSCOVR returned its first view of the entire sunlit side of Earth from approximately one million miles (1,600,000 km) away, taken by the EPIC instrument. EPIC will provide a daily series of Earth images enabling the first time study of daily variations over the entire globe. The images, available 12 to 36 hours after they are made, will be posted to a dedicated web page by September 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20150720_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa20150720-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>DSCOVR was placed in operation at the L<sub>1</sub> <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian point</a> to monitor the sun, because the constant stream of particles from the sun (the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>) reaches L<sub>1</sub> about 60 minutes before reaching Earth. DSCOVR will usually be able to provide a 15 to 60 minute warning before a surge of particles and magnetic field, from a <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejection</a> (CME) reaches Earth, and creates a <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storm</a>. DSCOVR data will also be used to improve predictions of the impact locations of a geomagnetic storm to be able to take preventative action. Electronic technologies such as satellites in geosynchronous orbit are at risk of unplanned disruptions without warnings from DSCOVR and other monitoring satellites at L1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 16–17, 2015, DSCOVR took a series of images showing the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> engaged in a <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">transit</a> of Earth. The images were taken between 19:50 and 00:45 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>. The animation was composed of monochrome images taken in different color filters at 30 second intervals for each frame, resulting in a slight color fringing for the Moon in each finished frame. Due to its position at Sun-Earth L<sub>1</sub>, DSCOVR will always see the Moon illuminated, and will always see its far side when it passes in front of Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 19, 2015, NASA opened a new website to host near-live '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Marble" title="Blue Marble">Blue Marble</a>' images taken by EPIC of Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epicsite_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-epicsite-9">[9]</a></sup> At least twelve images are released each day at regular intervals, showcasing the Earth as it rotates on its axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epicimages_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-epicimages-32">[32]</a></sup> The resolution of the images will range from 6.2 to 9.4 miles per pixel (10 to 15 km/pixel), and the short exposure times will render points of starlight invisible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epicimages_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-epicimages-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
