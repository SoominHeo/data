<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Simcheongga</h1>

<p><i><b>Simcheongga</b></i> is one of the five surviving stories of the Korean <i><a href="/wiki/Pansori" title="Pansori">pansori</a></i> storytelling tradition. The other stories are <i><a href="/wiki/Chunhyangga" title="Chunhyangga">Chunhyangga</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Heungbuga" title="Heungbuga">Heungbuga</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jeokbyeokga" title="Jeokbyeokga">Jeokbyeokga</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sugungga" title="Sugungga">Sugungga</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simcheongga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2498" data-file-width="3905" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg/250px-The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg/375px-The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg/500px-The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_National_Theater_of_Korea_Simcheongga.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Simcheongga</i> performance by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_National_Theatre_of_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The National Theatre of Korea (page does not exist)">The National Theatre of Korea</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The exact date of when <i>Simcheong</i> story was adapted into a <i>panosori</i> is unknown. According to records, <i>Simcheongga</i> can be found in the <i>Gwanuhui</i> written by Manjae Song in the time of <a href="/wiki/Sunjo_of_Joseon" title="Sunjo of Joseon">Sunjo of Joseon</a>, and also in <i>The History of Joseon Traditional Opera</i> written by Nosik Hung in the 1900s.</p>
<p><i>Simcheongga</i> is considered to have been made a great piece of art through the contribution of <i>pansori</i> singers' <i><a href="/wiki/Deoneum" title="Deoneum">deoneum</a></i>.</p>
<p><i>Simcheongga</i> is the most tragic of the five <i>pansori</i>s. The story is almost wholly one of grief and sadness. Therefore, the melody sounds low and gloomy as well. In <i>pansori</i>, the feeling of the voice is more important than its purity or beauty: a beautiful voice can't express the joy and grief of living. To express deep sorrow, the <i>pansori</i> requires "a voice like <i>geuneul</i> (shadows)." In <i>Simcheongga</i>, the mystic <i>geneul</i> is important, so only a master singer of <i>pansori</i> is considered to possess the ability to perform <i>Simcheongga</i> well.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Story">Story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simcheongga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are five parts in <i>Simcheongga.</i></p>
<p><i>Simcheongga</i> is about Simcheong and her father, Sim Hak-Gyu, whom people call <i>Sim-Bongsa</i> (<i>Sim the Blind</i>). She has to take care of her blind father. The sad story is occasionally lightened with humor.</p>
<p>The highlight of this <i>pansori</i> is when Sim-Bongsa regains his eyesight. A realistic expression of this requires a master singer of <i>pansori</i>. After Sim-Bongsa regains his eyesight, other blind people also recover. Finally, <i>Simcheongga</i> ends in a festive mood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
