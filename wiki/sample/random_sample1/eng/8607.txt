<p><i><b>Shinkai</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">深海</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is the 5th studio album recorded by Japanese rock band <a href="/wiki/Mr._Children" title="Mr. Children">Mr. Children</a>, released in June 1996 under the record label Toy's Factory.</p>
<p>After their breakthrough in 1994, Mr. Children yielded eight consecutive chart-topping singles. Three of those hits were appeared on the album; "Na mo Naki Uta", "Hana (Mémento-Mori)", and "Machine Gun o Buppanase" — while others were reserved for their subsequent release <i>Bolero</i> in 1997.</p>
<p>Kazutoshi Sakurai, a chief singer-songwriter of the band named the album's working title as <i><b>Coelacanth</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">シーラカンス</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shīrakansu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which was misheard by his band-mate as "Shinkai" (lit. "deep sea"). Sakurai, who favored the sound of the phrase, decided to change the title of the album.</p>
<p><i>Shinkai</i> was a sort of <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a>, featuring more insightful compositions and harder-edged sound compared to the band's previous materials. The whole album is played gaplessly, though each tracks are separated on CD. They had not anticipated that such an adventurous effort would be a big hit.</p>
<p>Contrary to the group's prediction, <i>Shinkai</i> met with commercial success, although it underperformed <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Heart" title="Atomic Heart">its predecessor</a> in cumulative sales. It debuted at number one on the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Oricon" title="Oricon">Oricon</a> chart with first-week sales of over 1.5 million copies, becoming the country's fastest selling album at the time. The album spent 46 weeks on the top 100, selling in excess of 2.7 million units during its chart run. Upon its release, <i>Shinkai</i> was certified double-million by the Recording Industry Association of Japan, for shipments of over 2 million copies.</p>
