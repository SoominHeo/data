<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regular_decagon">Regular decagon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regular decagon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Regular_polygon" title="Regular polygon">regular</a> decagon</i> has all sides of equal length and each internal angle will always be equal to 144°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sidebotham_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sidebotham-1">[1]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol" title="Schläfli symbol">Schläfli symbol</a> is {10} <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and can also be constructed as a <a href="/wiki/Truncation_(geometry)" title="Truncation (geometry)">truncated</a> <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagon</a>, t{5}, a quasiregular decagon alternating two types of edges.</p>

<p>An alternative (but similar) method is as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>Construct a pentagon in a circle by one of the methods shown in <a href="/wiki/Pentagon#Construction_of_a_regular_pentagon" title="Pentagon">constructing a pentagon</a>.</li>
<li>Extend a line from each vertex of the pentagon through the center of the <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a> to the opposite side of that same circle. Where each line cuts the circle is a vertex of the decagon.</li>
<li>The five corners of the pentagon constitute alternate corners of the decagon. Join these points to the adjacent new points to form the decagon.</li>
</ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symmetry">Symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Symmetries_of_decagon.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1604" data-file-width="2076" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Symmetries_of_decagon.png/320px-Symmetries_of_decagon.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Symmetries_of_decagon.png/480px-Symmetries_of_decagon.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Symmetries_of_decagon.png/640px-Symmetries_of_decagon.png 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Symmetries_of_decagon.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Symmetries of a regular decagon. Vertices are colored by their symmetry positions. Blue mirrors are drawn through vertices, and purple mirrors are drawn through edge. Gyration orders are given in the center.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>regular decagon</i> has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihedral_symmetry" title="Dihedral symmetry">Dih<sub>10</sub> symmetry</a>, order 20. There are 3 subgroup dihedral symmetries: Dih<sub>5</sub>, Dih<sub>2</sub>, and Dih<sub>1</sub>, and 4 <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_group" title="Cyclic group">cyclic group</a> symmetries: Z<sub>10</sub>, Z<sub>5</sub>, Z<sub>2</sub>, and Z<sub>1</sub>.</p>
<p>These 8 symmetries can be seen in 10 distinct symmetries on the decagon, a larger number because the lines of reflections can either pass through vertices or edges. <a href="/wiki/John_Horton_Conway" title="John Horton Conway">John Conway</a> labels these by a letter and group order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Full symmetry of the regular form is <b>r20</b> and no symmetry is labeled <b>a1</b>. The dihedral symmetries are divided depending on whether they pass through vertices (<b>d</b> for diagonal) or edges (<b>p</b> for perpendiculars), and <b>i</b> when reflection lines path through both edges and vertices. Cyclic symmetries in the middle column are labeled as <b>g</b> for their central gyration orders.</p>
<p>Each subgroup symmetry allows one or more degrees of freedom for irregular forms. Only the <b>g10</b> subgroup has no degrees of freedom but can seen as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directed_edge" title="Directed edge">directed edges</a>.</p>
<p>The highest symmetry irregular decagons are <b>d10</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Isogonal_figure" title="Isogonal figure">isogonal</a> decagon constructed by five mirrors which can alternate long and short edges, and <b>p10</b>, an <a href="/wiki/Isotoxal_figure" title="Isotoxal figure">isotoxal</a> decagon, constructed with equal edge lengths, but vertices alternating two different internal angles. These two forms are <a href="/wiki/Dual_polygon" title="Dual polygon">duals</a> of each other and have half the symmetry order of the regular decagon.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissection_of_regular_decagon">Dissection of regular decagon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dissection of regular decagon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coxeter" title="Coxeter">Coxeter</a> states that every parallel-sided 2<i>m</i>-gon can be divided into m(m-1)/2 rhombs. For the decagon, <i>m</i>=5, and it can be divided into 10 rhombs, with one example shown below. This decomposition can be seen as 10 of 80 faces in a <a href="/wiki/Petrie_polygon" title="Petrie polygon">Petrie polygon</a> projection plane of the <a href="/wiki/5-cube" title="5-cube">5-cube</a>. A second dissection is based on 10 of 30 faces of the <a href="/wiki/Rhombic_triacontahedron" title="Rhombic triacontahedron">rhombic triacontahedron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<table class="wikitable">

<p>A <b>skew decagon</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Skew_polygon" title="Skew polygon">skew polygon</a> with 10 vertices and edges but not existing on the same plane. The interior of such an decagon is not generally defined. A <i>skew zig-zag decagon</i> has vertices alternating between two parallel planes.</p>
<p>A <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_skew_decagon" title="Regular skew decagon">regular skew decagon</a></b> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertex-transitive" title="Vertex-transitive">vertex-transitive</a> with equal edge lengths. In 3-dimensions it will be a zig-zag skew decagon and can be seen in the vertices and side edges of a <a href="/wiki/Pentagonal_antiprism" title="Pentagonal antiprism">pentagonal antiprism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentagrammic_antiprism" title="Pentagrammic antiprism">pentagrammic antiprism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pentagrammic_crossed-antiprism" title="Pentagrammic crossed-antiprism">pentagrammic crossed-antiprism</a> with the same D<sub>5d</sub>, [2<sup>+</sup>,10] symmetry, order 20.</p>
<p>These can also be seen in these 4 convex polyhedra with <a href="/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry" title="Icosahedral symmetry">icosahedral symmetry</a>. The polygons on the perimeter of these projections are regular skew decagons.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petrie_polygons">Petrie polygons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Petrie polygons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>regular skew decagon</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Petrie_polygon" title="Petrie polygon">Petrie polygon</a> for many higher-dimensional polytopes, shown in these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_projection" title="Orthogonal projection">orthogonal projections</a> in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coxeter_plane" title="Coxeter plane">Coxeter planes</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The number of sides in the Petrie polygon is equal to the <a href="/wiki/Coxeter_element#Definitions" title="Coxeter element">Coxeter number</a>, <i>h</i>, for each symmetry family.</p>
