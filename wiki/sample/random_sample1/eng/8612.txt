<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cross-flow filtration</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_engineering" title="Biochemical engineering">biochemical engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein_purification" title="Protein purification">protein purification</a>, <b>crossflow filtration</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAC-1">[1]</a></sup> (also known as <b>tangential flow filtration</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millipore_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Millipore-2">[2]</a></sup>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a> (a particular <a href="/wiki/Unit_operation" title="Unit operation">unit operation</a>). Crossflow filtration is different from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead-end_filtration" title="Dead-end filtration">dead-end filtration</a> in which the feed is passed through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Membrane_(selective_barrier)" title="Membrane (selective barrier)">membrane</a> or bed, the solids being trapped in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(chemistry)" title="Filter (chemistry)">filter</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filtrate" title="Filtrate">filtrate</a> being released at the other end. Cross-flow filtration gets its name because the majority of the feed flow travels tangentially <i>across</i> the surface of the filter, rather than into the filter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAC-1">[1]</a></sup> The principal advantage of this is that the <a href="/wiki/Filter_cake" title="Filter cake">filter cake</a> (which can blind the filter) is substantially washed away during the filtration process, increasing the length of time that a filter unit can be operational. It can be a continuous process, unlike batch-wise dead-end filtration.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crossflow-filtration.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1052" data-file-width="744" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Crossflow-filtration.svg/150px-Crossflow-filtration.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Crossflow-filtration.svg/225px-Crossflow-filtration.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Crossflow-filtration.svg/300px-Crossflow-filtration.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crossflow-filtration.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram of cross-flow filtration</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This type of <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a> is typically selected for feeds containing a high proportion of small particle size solids (where the permeate is of most value) because solid material can quickly block (blind) the filter surface with dead-end filtration. Industrial examples of this include the extraction of soluble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> liquors.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceramic_membrane_(crossflow_filtration).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1505" data-file-width="2002" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Ceramic_membrane_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg/200px-Ceramic_membrane_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Ceramic_membrane_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg/300px-Ceramic_membrane_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Ceramic_membrane_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg/400px-Ceramic_membrane_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceramic_membrane_(crossflow_filtration).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ceramic membrane for industrial cross-flow filtration</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In crossflow filtration, the feed is passed across the filter membrane (tangentially) at positive <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> relative to the permeate side. A proportion of the material which is smaller than the membrane pore size passes through the membrane as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permeate" title="Permeate">permeate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filtrate" title="Filtrate">filtrate</a>; everything else is retained on the feed side of the membrane as retentate.</p>
<p>With crossflow filtration the <a href="/wiki/Tangent" title="Tangent">tangential</a> motion of the bulk of the fluid across the membrane causes trapped <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Particle" title="wiktionary:Particle">particles</a> on the filter surface to be rubbed off. This means that a crossflow filter can operate continuously at relatively high solids loads without blinding.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_over_conventional_filtration">Benefits over conventional filtration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits over conventional filtration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>A higher overall liquid removal rate is achieved by the prevention of filter cake formation</li>
<li>Process feed remains in the form of a mobile slurry, suitable for further processing</li>
<li>Solids content of the product slurry may be varied over a wide range</li>
<li>It is possible to fractionate particles by size <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tubular_pinch_effect" title="Tubular pinch effect">Tubular pinch effect</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_applications">Industrial applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industrial applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Filtration_unit_(crossflow_filtration).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Filtration_unit_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg/200px-Filtration_unit_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Filtration_unit_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg/300px-Filtration_unit_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Filtration_unit_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg/400px-Filtration_unit_%28crossflow_filtration%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Filtration_unit_(crossflow_filtration).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Filtration unit for industrial cross-flow filtration</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cross flow membrane filtration technology has been used widely in industry globally. Filtration membranes can be polymeric or ceramic, depending upon the application. The principles of cross-flow filtration are used in <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanofiltration" title="Nanofiltration">nanofiltration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultrafiltration" title="Ultrafiltration">ultrafiltration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microfiltration" title="Microfiltration">microfiltration</a>. When purifying water, it can be very cost effective in comparison to the traditional evaporation methods.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Protein_purification" title="Protein purification">protein purification</a>, the term Tangential Flow Filtration (TFF) is used to describe cross-flow filtration with membranes. The process can be used at different stages during purification, depending on the type of membrane selected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millipore_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Millipore-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the photograph of an industrial filtration unit (right), it is possible to see that the recycle pipework is considerably larger than either the feed pipework (vertical pipe on the right hand side) or the permeate pipework (small manifolds near to the rows of white clamps). These pipe sizes are directly related to the proportion of liquid flows through the unit. A dedicated pump is used to recycle the feed several times around the unit before the solids-rich retentate is transferred to the next part of the process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques_to_improve_performance_of_cross_flow_filtration">Techniques to improve performance of cross flow filtration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Techniques to improve performance of cross flow filtration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Backwashing">Backwashing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Backwashing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In backwashing, the transmembrane pressure is periodically inverted by the use of a secondary pump, so that permeate flows back into the feed, lifting the fouling layer from the surface of the membrane. Backwashing is not applicable to spirally wound membranes and is not a general practice in most applications. (See CIP) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulson_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coulson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean-in-place_.28CIP.29">Clean-in-place (CIP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clean-in-place (CIP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clean-in-place" title="Clean-in-place">Clean-in-place</a> systems are typically used to remove fouling from membranes after extensive use. The CIP process may use detergents, reactive agents such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">sodium hypochlorite</a> and acids and alkalis such as <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">Sodium hypochlorite</a> (bleach) must be removed from the feed in some membrane plants. Bleach oxidizes thin-film membranes. Oxidation will degrade the membranes to a point where they will no longer perform at rated rejection levels and have to be replaced. Bleach can be added to a NaOH <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> CIP during an initial system start-up before spirally-wound membranes are loaded into the plant to help disinfect the system. Bleach is also used to CIP perforated stainless steel (Graver) membranes, as their tolerance for sodium hypochlorite is much higher than a spirally-wound membrane. Caustics and acids are most often used as primary CIP chemicals. Caustic removes organic fouling and acid removes minerals. Enzyme solutions are also used in some systems for helping remove organic fouling material from the membrane plant. The pH and temperature are important to a CIP program. If pH and temperature are too high the membrane will degrade and flux performance will suffer. If pH and temperature are too low, the system simply will not be cleaned properly. Every application has different CIP requirements. e.g. a dairy RO <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a> plant most likely will require a more rigorous CIP program than a water purification RO plant. Each membrane manufacturer has their own guidelines for CIP procedures for their product.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concentration">Concentration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Concentration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The volume of the fluid is reduced by allowing permeate flow to occur. Solvent, solutes, and particles smaller than the membrane pore size pass through the membrane, while particles larger than the pore size are retained, and thereby concentrated. In bioprocessing applications, concentration may be followed by diafiltration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diafiltration">Diafiltration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diafiltration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In order to effectively remove permeate components from the slurry, fresh solvent may be added to the feed to replace the permeate volume, at the same rate as the permeate flow rate, such that the volume in the system remains constant. This is analogous to the washing of filter cake to remove soluble components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coulson_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coulson-4">[4]</a></sup> Dilution and re-concentration is sometimes also referred to as "diafiltration."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_flow_disruption_.28PFD.29">Process flow disruption (PFD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cross-flow_filtration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Process flow disruption (PFD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A technically simpler approach than backwashing is to set the transmembrane pressure to zero by temporarily closing off the permeate outlet, which increases the attrition of the fouling layer without the need for a second pump. PFD is not as effective as backwashing in removing fouling, but can be advantageous.</p>
