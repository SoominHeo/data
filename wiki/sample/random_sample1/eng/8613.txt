<p>The <i><b>Sib Pal Gi Association</b></i> (십팔기협회 <i>Dae Han Sibpalki Hyeop Hwe</i>; also <i>The Korea Sibpalki Association</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Korean_martial_arts" title="Korean martial arts">Korean martial arts</a> association established in 1981 under the leadership of Kim Kwang-Seok (Kim Gwang-suk 김광석; 金光錫, b. 1936, style name <i>Haebeom</i>). <i>Sib Pal Ki</i> (literally "eighteen skills") is a Korean term for "martial arts", either <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Korean_martial_arts" title="Korean martial arts">Korean martial arts</a> (as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_martial_arts" title="Japanese martial arts">Japanese martial arts</a> introduced during the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese rule in Korea</a>).</p>
<p>Kim Kwang-Seok had opened a martial arts school in 1969, having studied martial arts during the late 1950s to middle 1960s under Choi sang-chul, Korean Kungfu master. In 1986 Korean folklorist Sim U-seong worked together with Kim Kwang-Seok to compare his style with the historical <i><a href="/wiki/Muyedobotongji" title="Muyedobotongji">Muyedobotongji</a></i> or "Comprehensive Illustrated Manual of Martial Arts" of 1790. In the same year, the first public performance of Sib Pal Gi took place at the Batanggol Small Theater in Seoul.</p>
<p>In 2001, students of Kim Kwang Seok founded the <i>Sib Pal Gi Preservation Society</i> (십팔기 보존회), dedicated to staging public performances and disseminating public awareness of Korean martial arts in the South Korean public.</p>
<p>There is an amount of controversy in the Korean martial arts community<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2014)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> as to which extent the <i>Sib Pal Gi</i> system taught by Kim Kwang-Seok corresponds to the historical 18th-century (as suggested by Kim Kwang-Seok 1987) and to which extent the system reflects more modern influence of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a>; but the emphasis of <i>Sib Pal Gi</i> in Korea is that of being a "native" system in the sense of avoiding the modern <a href="/wiki/Japanese_martial_arts" title="Japanese martial arts">Japanese martial arts</a> which had become mainstream under the Japanese occupation of Korea during the first half of the 20th century.</p>
<p>The term <i>Sip Pal Gi</i> (십팔기 "Eighteen Skills") in modern <a href="/wiki/Korean_martial_arts" title="Korean martial arts">Korean martial arts</a> has come to identify four separate activities.</p>
<ul>
<li>there are practitioners in South Korea who follow the practices of an eclectic Chinese system of armed and unarmed Martial Arts termed <i>Sip Pal Gi</i> owing to the number of systems, methods and practices in its curriculum. Individual schools will vary in the weapons used and the manner of practice.</li>
<li>A second and more general application of the term is as a label to identify <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">Chinese martial arts</a> generically, much like "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kung_fu" title="Kung fu">kung fu</a>" has become an umbrella term for them in the West.</li>
<li>Also, there are small groups of practitioners who use the term <i>Sip Pal Gi</i> historically, for the attempted reconstruction of 18th-century Korean martial arts based on the historical manuals, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Muyesinbo" title="Muyesinbo">Muyesinbo</a>, much in the same way as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_European_martial_arts_revival" title="Historical European martial arts revival">martial arts reconstruction in the West</a>.</li>
<li>Lastly, the style usually spelled Sipalki, of dubious relation with the Chosun period martial arts, taught by Yoo Soo Nam.</li>
</ul>
