<p>The <b>Thing</b> (<b>Benjamin</b> "<b>Ben</b>" <b>Grimm</b>) is a fictional <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> appearing in <a href="/wiki/American_comic_book" title="American comic book">American comic books</a> published by <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>. The character is a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a>. The character is known for his trademark rocky orange appearance, sense of humor, blue eyes, and famous battle cry, "It's clobberin' time!". The Thing's speech patterns are loosely based on those of <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Durante" title="Jimmy Durante">Jimmy Durante</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Actor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bailey_Smith" title="Michael Bailey Smith">Michael Bailey Smith</a> played Ben Grimm in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fantastic_Four_(unreleased_film)" title="The Fantastic Four (unreleased film)">The Fantastic Four</a></i> film from 1994, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Chiklis" title="Michael Chiklis">Michael Chiklis</a> portrayed the Thing in the <a href="/wiki/2005_in_film" title="2005 in film">2005</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four_(2005_film)" title="Fantastic Four (2005 film)">Fantastic Four</a></i> and its <a href="/wiki/2007_in_film" title="2007 in film">2007</a> sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four:_Rise_of_the_Silver_Surfer" title="Fantastic Four: Rise of the Silver Surfer">Fantastic Four: Rise of the Silver Surfer</a></i>, while <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Bell" title="Jamie Bell">Jamie Bell</a> acted the part in <i><a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four_(2015_film)" title="Fantastic Four (2015 film)">Fantastic Four</a></i> (2015).</p>
<p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> ranked the Thing 18th in the "Top 100 Comic Book Heroes",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and 23rd in their list of "The Top 50 Avengers" in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Thing was named <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine's tenth of "The 50 Greatest Comic Book Characters" in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">Publication history</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four#Publication_history" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four Â§Â Publication history</a></div>
<p>Created by writer-editor <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a> and artist/co-plotter <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>, the character first appeared in <i>The Fantastic Four</i> #1 (Nov. 1961).</p>
<p>In addition to appearing in the Fantastic Four, the Thing has been the star of <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Two-in-One" title="Marvel Two-in-One">Marvel Two-in-One</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Tales" title="Strange Tales">Strange Tales</a></i> (with his fellow Fantastic Four member the <a href="/wiki/Human_Torch" title="Human Torch">Human Torch</a>), and two incarnations of his own eponymous series, as well as numerous <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">miniseries</a> and <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">one-shots</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strange_Tales"><i>Strange Tales</i></span></h3>
<p>The Thing joined his Fantastic Four partner and frequent rival the <a href="/wiki/Human_Torch" title="Human Torch">Human Torch</a> in #124 (<a href="/wiki/1964_in_comics" title="1964 in comics">1964</a>) of <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Tales#Monsters_and_magician" title="Strange Tales">Strange Tales</a></i>, which previously featured solo adventures of the Human Torch and backup <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a> stories. The change was intended to liven the comic through the always humorous chemistry between the Torch and the Thing. They were replaced in #135 (1965) with the "modern-day" version of <a href="/wiki/Nick_Fury" title="Nick Fury">Nick Fury</a>, Agent of <a href="/wiki/S.H.I.E.L.D." title="S.H.I.E.L.D.">S.H.I.E.L.D.</a>, who had already been appearing in <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Fury_and_his_Howling_Commandos" title="Sgt. Fury and his Howling Commandos">Sgt. Fury and his Howling Commandos</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Two-in-One_.281974.E2.80.931983.29"><i>Marvel Two-in-One</i> (1974â1983)</span></h3>
<p>After a 1973 try-out in two issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Feature" title="Marvel Feature">Marvel Feature</a></i>, the Thing starred in the long-running series <i><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Two-in-One" title="Marvel Two-in-One">Marvel Two-in-One</a></i>. In each issue, Ben Grimm would team up with another character from the <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a>, often an obscure or colorful character. The series helped to introduce characters from Marvel's lineup, by way of teaming up with the more recognizable Thing. In 1992, Marvel reprinted four <i>Two-in-One stories</i> (#50, 51, 77 and 80) as a miniseries under the title <i>The Adventures of the Thing</i>. The series was cancelled after 100 issues to make way for a solo series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Thing_.281983.E2.80.931986.29"><i>The Thing</i> (1983â1986)</span></h3>
<p>The cancellation of <i>Marvel Two-in-One</i> led to the Thing's first completely solo series, which ran for 36 issues. It was originally written by <a href="/wiki/John_Byrne_(comics)" title="John Byrne (comics)">John Byrne</a> and later, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Carlin" title="Mike Carlin">Mike Carlin</a>. The series also featured art by <a href="/wiki/Ron_Wilson_(comics)" title="Ron Wilson (comics)">Ron Wilson</a> and later by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Neary" title="Paul Neary">Paul Neary</a>. It was notable for elaborating on Ben Grimm's poor childhood on <a href="/wiki/Yancy_Street_Gang" title="Yancy Street Gang">Yancy Street</a> in its early issue, as well as chronicling the Thing's later foray into the world of <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling" title="Professional wrestling">professional wrestling</a>. It also featured a major storyline offshoot from Marvel's <a href="/wiki/Secret_Wars" title="Secret Wars">Secret Wars</a> event, in which the Thing elects to remain on the <a href="/wiki/Beyonder" title="Beyonder">Beyonder</a>'s Battleworld after discovering that the planet enables him to return to human form at will. A full third of the series' stories take place on Battleworld.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002.E2.80.93present">2002âpresent</span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Marvel released <i>The Thing: Freakshow</i>, a four-issue miniseries starring the Thing, in which he takes time away from the <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a> to ride the rails across America, inadvertently stumbling on a deformed gypsy boy he once ridiculed as a teenager â now the super-strong main attraction of a troupe of traveling <a href="/wiki/Freak_show" title="Freak show">circus freaks</a> â and a town full of <a href="/wiki/Kree" title="Kree">Kree</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skrull" title="Skrull">Skrull</a> warriors fighting over a <a href="/wiki/Watcher_(comics)" title="Watcher (comics)">Watcher</a> infant. The series was written by <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Johns" title="Geoff Johns">Geoff Johns</a> and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Kolins" title="Scott Kolins">Scott Kolins</a>.</p>
<p>In 2003, Marvel released another four-issue miniseries, <i>The Thing: Night Falls on Yancy Street</i>. The story was of a less action-oriented and more character-driven and analytical type than is usual for the Thing. Some reviewers considered the story a nostalgic homage to <a href="/wiki/Silver_Age_of_Comic_Books" title="Silver Age of Comic Books">Silver Age</a> comics, while others found its noir-ish atmosphere "depressing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It was written by <a href="/wiki/Evan_Dorkin" title="Evan Dorkin">Evan Dorkin</a> and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Dean_Haspiel" title="Dean Haspiel">Dean Haspiel</a>.</p>
<p>After the success of the <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Four_(2005_film)" title="Fantastic Four (2005 film)"><i>Fantastic Four</i> feature film</a> and events in <i>Fantastic Four</i> that resulted in Grimm becoming a millionaire, the Thing was once again given his own series in 2005, <i>The Thing</i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Dan_Slott" title="Dan Slott">Dan Slott</a> and penciled by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Di_Vito" title="Andrea Di Vito">Andrea Di Vito</a> and, later, <a href="/wiki/Kieron_Dwyer" title="Kieron Dwyer">Kieron Dwyer</a>. It was canceled with #8 in 2006.</p>
<p>The Thing was a member of <a href="/wiki/The_New_Avengers_(comics)" title="The New Avengers (comics)">The New Avengers</a>, when that team debuted in their self-titled series in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He appeared as a regular character throughout the 2010â2013 <i>New Avengers</i> series, from issue #1 (August 2010) through its final issue #34 (January 2013).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_character_biography">Fictional character biography</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span></h3>
<p>Born on Yancy Street in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a>, to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish-8">[8]</a></sup> family, <b>Benjamin Jacob "Ben" Grimm</b> had an early life of poverty and hardship, shaping him into a tough, streetwise scrapper. His older brother Daniel, whom Ben idolized, was killed in a street gang fight when Ben was eight years old. This portion of his own life is modeled on that of <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>, who grew up on tough <a href="/wiki/Delancey_Street" title="Delancey Street">Delancey Street</a>, whose brother died when he was young, whose father was named Benjamin, and who was named Jacob at birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.D.2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.D.2009-9">[9]</a></sup> Following the death of his parents, Ben was raised by his Uncle Jake (who had married a much younger wife, Petunia, who would become a frequent reference used by the character until her death).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He comes to lead the Yancy Street gang at one point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Excelling in football as a high school student, Ben received a full scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_University" title="Empire State University">Empire State University</a>, where he first met his eventual lifelong friend in a teenaged genius named <a href="/wiki/Mister_Fantastic" title="Mister Fantastic">Reed Richards</a>, as well as future enemy <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Doom" title="Doctor Doom">Victor von Doom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Despite their being from radically different backgrounds, science student Richards described to Grimm his dream of building a space rocket to explore the regions of space around Mars; Grimm jokingly agreed to fly that rocket when the day came.</p>
<p>After finishing college, having earned multiple advanced degrees in engineering,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Grimm joined the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a>, where he was trained as a <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>. (His exploits as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">military aviator</a> are chronicled to a limited extent in issue #7 of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leatherneck_Raiders" title="Leatherneck Raiders">Captain Savage and his Leatherneck Raiders</a></i> comic, in a story entitled "Objective: Ben Grimm!") While in the Air Force, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Fury" title="Nick Fury">Nick Fury</a> orders him to serve as a pilot during a top secret surveillance mission into <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, along with Logan (the future <a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Man</a> <a href="/wiki/Wolverine_(character)" title="Wolverine (character)">Wolverine</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Carol_Danvers" title="Carol Danvers">Carol Danvers</a> (the future <a href="/wiki/Avengers_(comics)" title="Avengers (comics)">Avenger</a> <a href="/wiki/Ms._Marvel" title="Ms. Marvel">Ms. Marvel</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Following this, he becomes an astronaut for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific volume in which the source appears. (April 2009)">volumeÂ &amp;Â issueÂ needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The details of his life story have been modified over the years to keep the character current.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the earlier stories, up until the one published in the 1970s, Grimm had served in the air force during World War II and the space flight in which he was transformed into the Thing was an attempt to reach the Moon, occurring at a time before any manned space ship had escaped Earth's orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Captain Savage story mentioned above was set during the Second World War.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span></h4>
<p>In keeping with an early taboo in the comic superhero world against revealing a character's religion, the fact that Grimm was Jewish was not explicitly revealed until four decades after his creation, in the story, "Remembrance of Things Past" (in <i>Fantastic Four</i>, vol. 3, #56, August 2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish-8">[8]</a></sup> In this story, Grimm returns to his old neighborhood to find Mr. Sheckerberg, a pawn shop owner he had known as a child. Flashbacks during this story reveal Grimm's Jewish heritage. He even recites the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shema" title="Shema">Shema</a> (an important Jewish prayer often recited) over the dying Sheckerberg, who eventually recovers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish-8">[8]</a></sup> In a later story, Grimm agrees to celebrate his <a href="/wiki/Bar_and_Bat_Mitzvah" title="Bar and Bat Mitzvah">Bar Mitzvah</a>, since it has been 13 years (the age a Jewish boy celebrates his Bar Mitzvah) since he began his "second life" as the Thing. To celebrate the ceremony, Grimm organizes a <a href="/wiki/Poker" title="Poker">poker</a> tournament for every available superhero in the <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The story of the Thing was unusual for another religious reason, as well: the fact that the Thing was actually brought back to life in one story not by science, magic, or alien power or technology, but by the hand of God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some years later, Reed Richards, now a successful scientist, once again makes contact with Grimm. Richards has built his spaceship, and reminds Grimm of his promise to fly the ship. After the government denies him permission to fly the spaceship himself, Richards plots a clandestine flight piloted by Grimm and accompanied by his future wife <a href="/wiki/Invisible_Woman" title="Invisible Woman">Susan Storm</a>, who had helped provide funding for the rocket, and her younger brother <a href="/wiki/Human_Torch" title="Human Torch">Johnny Storm</a>, who helped the group gain access to the launch system. Although reluctant to fly the rocket, Ben is persuaded to do so by Sue, for whom he has a soft spot. During this unauthorized ride into the upper atmosphere of Earth and the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen Belts</a>, they are pelted by a <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> storm and exposed to radiation against which the ship's shields are no protection. Upon crashing down to Earth, each of the four learn that they have developed fantastic superhuman abilities. Grimm's skin is transformed into a thick, lumpy orange hide, which gradually evolves into his now-familiar craggy covering of large rocky plates. Richards proposes the quartet band together to use their new abilities for the betterment of humanity, and Grimm, in a moment of self-pity, adopts the super-heroic sobriquet, <i>The Thing</i>. The team clashes with the <a href="/wiki/Mole_Man" title="Mole Man">Mole Man</a> in their first appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Trapped in his monstrous form, Grimm is an unhappy yet reliable member of the team. He trusts in his friend Reed Richards to one day develop a cure for his condition. However, when he encounters blind sculptress <a href="/wiki/Alicia_Masters" title="Alicia Masters">Alicia Masters</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Grimm develops an unconscious resistance to being transformed back to his human form. Subconsciously fearing that Masters prefers him to remain in the monstrous form of the Thing, Grimm's body rejects various attempts by Richards to restore his human form, lest he lose Masters' love. Grimm has remained a stalwart member of the Fantastic Four for years. The Thing first fought the <a href="/wiki/Hulk_(comics)" title="Hulk (comics)">Hulk</a> early in his career,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> with many such further clashes over the years. Not long after that, he is first reverted to his human form, but is then restored to his Thing form to battle <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Doom" title="Doctor Doom">Doctor Doom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Despite his unquestioned loyalty to the Fantastic Four, Grimm has been temporarily replaced on the team twice. First, after Grimm temporarily lost his powers and reverted to human form,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Reed Richards hired <a href="/wiki/Luke_Cage" title="Luke Cage">Luke Cage</a> (then using the code name "Power Man") to take his place until Richards had completed a Thing-suit for Ben (until Ben unexpectedly reverted into the actual Thing again later on).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Years later, after Grimm chose to remain on Battleworld in the aftermath of the "Secret Wars" due to his apparent control over his transformation between his human and Thing states, he asked the <a href="/wiki/She-Hulk" title="She-Hulk">She-Hulk</a> to fill in for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On returning to Earth, he learns that Alicia had become romantically involved with his teammate Johnny Storm during his absence <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> (it is eventually revealed that this Alicia was actually the <a href="/wiki/Skrull" title="Skrull">Skrull</a> impostor <a href="/wiki/Lyja" title="Lyja">Lyja</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>). An angry Grimm wallows in self-pity for a time, later on accompanying the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Avengers" title="West Coast Avengers">West Coast Avengers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and actually joining the team for a moment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Eventually, he returns to his surrogate family as leader of the Fantastic Four when Mr. Fantastic and the Invisible Woman leave the team to raise their son Franklin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Ben invites <a href="/wiki/Crystal_(comics)" title="Crystal (comics)">Crystal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Ventura" title="Sharon Ventura">Ms. Marvel II (Sharon Ventura)</a> to fill their slots.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific volume in which the source appears. (May 2009)">volumeÂ &amp;Â issueÂ needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Soon after Sharon and Ben are irradiated with cosmic rays, Sharon becomes a lumpy Thing much like Ben was in his first few appearances, while Ben mutates into a new rocky form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After being further mutated into the more monstrous rocky form, Ben was briefly changed back to his human form, and returned leadership of the Fantastic Four to Reed Richards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Grimm once more returned to his traditional orange rocky form, out of love for Ms. Marvel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He remains a steadfast member of the Fantastic Four.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_21st_century">In the 21st century</span></h3>
<p>In a <i>Fantastic Four</i> comic published in 2005, Ben learns that he is entitled to a large sum of money, his share of the Fantastic Four fortune, which Reed Richards had never touched, as he had the shares of the other teammates (who were family members) in order to pay off various debts of the group.</p>
<p>The Thing uses his newfound wealth to build a community center in his old neighborhood on Yancy Street, the "Grimm Youth Center". Thinking the center is named after the Thing himself, the <a href="/wiki/Yancy_Street_Gang" title="Yancy Street Gang">Yancy Street Gang</a> plans to graffiti the building exterior, but discovers the building was actually named after Daniel Grimm, Ben's deceased older brother and former leader of the gang. The relationship between the Yancy Streeters and the Thing is then effectively reconciled, or at least changed to a more good-natured, playful rivalry (as exemplified by the comic ending, with Yancy Streeters spray-painting the sleeping Thing).</p>
<p>Some personality traits of the cantankerously lovable, occasionally cigar-smoking, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> native of the Lower East Side are popularly recognized as having been inspired by those of co-creator Jack Kirby, who in interviews has said he intended Grimm to be an alter ego of himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_War.2FThe_Initiative">Civil War/The Initiative</span></h3>
<p>Initially in the superhero <a href="/wiki/Civil_War_(comics)" title="Civil War (comics)">Civil War</a>, Ben is a reluctant member at Iron Man's side, until he witnesses a battle on Yancy Street in which <a href="/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a>'s forces try to rescue captured allies held by <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">Iron Man</a>'s forces. Old Fantastic Four foes the <a href="/wiki/Mad_Thinker" title="Mad Thinker">Mad Thinker</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Puppet_Master_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Puppet Master (Marvel Comics)">Puppet Master</a> try to escalate the battle, using a mind-controlled Yancy Streeter to deliver a bomb. The young man dies and the Thing verbally blasts both sides for not caring about the civilians caught in the conflict. He announces that, while he thinks the registration is wrong, he is also not going to fight the government and is thus leaving the country for <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. While in France he meets <i>Les HÃ©ros de Paris</i> (The Heroes of Paris).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ben returns to New York as both sides of the SHRA battle in the city. Oblivious to whichever side gets in his way, Ben makes it his job to protect civilians from harm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Fantastic Four</i> #543 (March 2007), Ben celebrates the Fantastic Four's 11th anniversary along with the Human Torch, and late-comers Reed and Sue. The aftermath of the Civil War is still being felt in this issue, as Ben and Johnny (and even <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Richards" title="Franklin Richards">Franklin</a>) consider the future of the team and Reed and Sue's marriage. When Reed and Sue arrive near issue's end, they announce that they are taking a break from the team and have found two replacement members: <a href="/wiki/Black_Panther_(comics)" title="Black Panther (comics)">Black Panther</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Storm_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Storm (Marvel Comics)">Storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a>. The title of the story in this issue is a quote from Ben, "Come on, Suzie, don't leave us hangin'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ben Grimm served as one of the pallbearers at the memorial service for Captain America, along with Tony Stark, Ms. Marvel, Rick Jones, T'Challa and Sam Wilson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ben has been identified as Number 53 of the 142 registered superheroes who appear on the cover of the comic book <i><a href="/wiki/Avengers:_The_Initiative" title="Avengers: The Initiative">Avengers: The Initiative</a></i> #1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_Hulk">World War Hulk</span></h3>
<p>Ben once again tries to take on the Hulk within the events of <a href="/wiki/World_War_Hulk" title="World War Hulk">World War Hulk</a> in order to buy Reed Richards the time he needs to complete his plans for the Hulk. Ben gives his best shots, but the Hulk takes his punches without slowing down. The Hulk proceeds to knock out Ben by punching both sides of his head simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> He is later seen captive in Madison Square Garden, which the Hulk has turned into a gladiatorial arena, with an obedience disk fitted on him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Released from his imprisonment, Ben, <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Luke_Cage" title="Luke Cage">Luke Cage</a> attack the <a href="/wiki/Warbound" title="Warbound">Warbound</a>, with Ben fighting <a href="/wiki/Korg_(comics)" title="Korg (comics)">Korg</a>. Their battle is brought to an abrupt end when Hiroim repairs the damage to <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> Island, drawing the energy to do so from Ben and Korg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secret_Invasion">Secret Invasion</span></h3>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Invasion" title="Secret Invasion">Secret Invasion</a>: Fantastic Four</i> miniseries, the Skrull Lyja, posing as Sue, sends the <a href="/wiki/Baxter_Building" title="Baxter Building">Baxter Building</a>, with Ben, Johnny, Franklin, and <a href="/wiki/Valeria_Richards" title="Valeria Richards">Valeria</a> inside, into the <a href="/wiki/Negative_Zone" title="Negative Zone">Negative Zone</a>. Not long after their arrival, Ben has to protect Franklin and Valeria from an impending onslaught of giant insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> With the aid of the <a href="/wiki/Tinkerer" title="Tinkerer">Tinkerer</a>, who Ben broke out of the Negative Zone Prison, they, with the exception of Lyja who stayed behind,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> were able to return to the regular Marvel Universe just after the invasion was over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heroic_Age">Heroic Age</span></h3>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Siege_(comics)" title="Siege (comics)">Siege</a> of <a href="/wiki/Asgard_(comics)" title="Asgard (comics)">Asgard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luke_Cage" title="Luke Cage">Luke Cage</a> asks Ben to serve on his <a href="/wiki/The_New_Avengers_(comics)" title="The New Avengers (comics)">Avengers</a> team. Although Ben states that his loyalty will always be to the Fantastic Four, Cage confirms that he is not asking Ben to resign from his original team, merely suggesting that Ben split his time between the two teams, as <a href="/wiki/Wolverine_(character)" title="Wolverine (character)">Wolverine</a> divides his time between the <a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a> and the Avengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fear_Itself">Fear Itself</span></h3>
<p>During the <i><a href="/wiki/Fear_Itself_(comics)" title="Fear Itself (comics)">Fear Itself</a></i> storyline, Ben lifts one of the seven fallen hammers of the <a href="/wiki/Serpent_(comics)" title="Serpent (comics)">Serpent</a> and becomes Angrir: Breaker of Souls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In this form, he then destroys Yancy Street and Avengers Tower,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and battles Spider-Man,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Mister Fantastic and Invisible Woman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> before confronting Thor, who seriously wounds him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Franklin then uses his powers to restore Ben to his normal self, free from the Serpent's possession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span></h2>
<p>The Thing is generally well liked among other heroes within the Marvel universe. Grimm's relationship with his teammates has been a close but occasionally edgy one given his temper. He and the Torch (aka Johnny Storm) are always arguing and have often clashed, causing no end of mayhem in the Fantastic Four headquarters.</p>
<p>Grimm's first love-interest was the blind Alicia Masters, and he was intensely jealous of her. When Johnny started a relationship of his own with Alicia Masters and they became engaged, Grimm was upset. However, he had to concede that, unlike himself and his stone-covered body, Johnny could "be a man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> He even agreed to act as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wedding_ceremony_participants#Best_man" title="Wedding ceremony participants">best man</a> at their wedding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The relationship between Alicia and Johnny was vehemently disliked by many fans,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and was later <a href="/wiki/Retroactive_continuity" title="Retroactive continuity">retconned</a> and explained that the Alicia that Johnny fell in love with was actually <a href="/wiki/Lyja" title="Lyja">Lyja</a>, a member of the shape-changing <a href="/wiki/Skrull" title="Skrull">Skrull</a> race. The real Alicia, who was kept in <a href="/wiki/Suspended_animation" title="Suspended animation">suspended animation</a>, was soon rescued by the Fantastic Four and reunited with the Thing.</p>
<p>Ben began dating a teacher named Debbie Green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Their relationship went so well that, after only six weeks, Ben asked Debbie to marry him, which she accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> He later left her at the altar when he realizes the dangers of the <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Refrigerators" title="Women in Refrigerators">wives of superheroes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

However, Grimm also holds Reed responsible for his condition since he had dismissed the potential danger of the cosmic rays that gave them their powers, although Grimm had taken them very seriously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> At times of real frustration towards Reed, Grimm refers to him simply as "Richards".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>

<p>As a result of a <a href="/wiki/Mutagen" title="Mutagen">mutagenic</a> effect due to exposure to cosmic radiation, the Thing possesses high levels of superhuman strength, stamina, and resistance to physical injury. His strength has continued to increase over the years due to a combination of further mutation and special exercise equipment designed for him by Reed Richards.</p>
<p>He is capable of surviving impacts of great strength and force without sustaining injury, as his body is covered with an orange, flexible, rock-like hide. He is also able to withstand gunfire from high-caliber weapons as well as armor-piercing rounds. It is possible to breach his exterior, however, and he does bleed as a result. One such instance involved <a href="/wiki/Wolverine_(character)" title="Wolverine (character)">Wolverine</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Adamantium" title="Adamantium">adamantium</a> claws scarring The Thing's face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Due to the resulting scars, the Thing wore a helmet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> until he was healed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperstorm" title="Hyperstorm">Hyperstorm</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific volume in which the source appears. (August 2014)">volumeÂ &amp;Â issueÂ needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Thing's highly advanced musculature generates fewer fatigue toxins during physical activity, granting him superhuman levels of stamina. When in his Thing form, he has only three fingers and a thumb on each hand. The loss of one digit on each hand, and the increase in volume of the remainder, does not affect his manual dexterity. However, he has been shown doing things like holding a pencil and using it to dial a phone (from stories during the era when rotary-dial phones were common), or to push buttons on a keypad, to demonstrate how he adapts to using devices that would ordinarily be too small for him.</p>
<p>Aside from his physical attributes, the Thing's senses can withstand higher levels of sensory stimulation than an ordinary human, with the exception of his sense of touch. His lungs are possessed of greater efficiency and volume than those of an ordinary human. As a result, the Thing is capable of holding his breath for much longer periods of time.</p>
<p>Despite his brutish, even monstrous form, the Thing suffers no change in his personality nor his level of intelligence. Despite his greatly increased size, the Thing's agility and reflexes remain at the same level they had been prior to his transformation.</p>
<p>The Thing is an exceptionally skilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilot</a>, due to his time spent as a test pilot in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and as a founding member of the Fantastic Four. He is also a formidable and relentless hand-to-hand combatant. His fighting style incorporates elements of <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jujitsu" title="Jujitsu">jujitsu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Street_fighting" title="Street fighting">street-fighting</a> techniques, as well as <a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">hand-to-hand combat</a> training from the military.</p>
<p>After an encounter with the <a href="/wiki/Grey_Gargoyle" title="Grey Gargoyle">Grey Gargoyle</a>, the Thing seemed to have gained the ability to shift between his human and rock forms at will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> That ability has since been lost when it was discovered that he created versions of himself in other universes whenever he transformed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific volume in which the source appears. (April 2009)">volumeÂ &amp;Â issueÂ needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On occasion, when Ben Grimm regained his human form and lost his Thing powers, he used a suit of powered battle armor designed by Reed Richards that simulated the strength and durability of his mutated body, albeit to a weaker degree. Wearing the suit, which was designed to physically resemble his rocky form, Ben continued to participate in the Fantastic Four's adventures. The first exo-skeletal Thing suit was destroyed after <a href="/wiki/Galactus" title="Galactus">Galactus</a> restored Ben's natural powers and form. A second suit was built (presumably by Richards) and used sporadically when Ben had been returned to his human form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been revealed â after Franklin and Valeria created a formula that allows Ben to become human for one week each year â that Ben is almost immortal when in his Thing form, as he only ages when he is human. Reed and Nathaniel traveled over two thousand years into the future to see Ben still alive after all that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In the March 17, 1979 episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>, Thing appeared in the skit "Superhero Party", where he was a guest at a party hosted by Superman (<a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">Bill Murray</a>) and Lois Lane (<a href="/wiki/Margot_Kidder" title="Margot Kidder">Margot Kidder</a>).</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/I_Am_Furious_(Yellow)" title="I Am Furious (Yellow)">I Am Furious (Yellow)</a>", a senile/insane version of Stan Lee (<a href="/wiki/Comic_Book_Guy" title="Comic Book Guy">Comic Book Guy</a> says that Lee's brain is no longer in "near-mint" condition) tries to cram a Thing <a href="/wiki/Action_figure" title="Action figure">action figure</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Batmobile" title="Batmobile">Batmobile</a> toy. Also in the "<a href="/wiki/Treehouse_of_Horror_XIV" title="Treehouse of Horror XIV">Treehouse of Horror XIV</a>" story "Stop the World, I Want to Goof Off", there is a quick moment where the Simpson family members are turned into members of the Fantastic Four. Homer is the Thing. In "<a href="/wiki/Sex,_Pies_and_Idiot_Scrapes" title="Sex, Pies and Idiot Scrapes">Sex, Pies and Idiot Scrapes</a>" the Thing is shown fighting the Hulk in the middle of an Irish riot (between Orangemen Loyalists and Green Irish Nationalists - the Hulk and Thing taking this conflict to its ultimate illogical conclusion). They are both later shown on the front page of the Springfield newspaper beating up on Homer.</li>
<li>The Thing and <a href="/wiki/Invisible_Woman" title="Invisible Woman">Invisible Woman</a> are referenced in particular along with the rest of the Fantastic Four throughout the fourth season of <i><a href="/wiki/Arrested_Development_(TV_series)" title="Arrested Development (TV series)">Arrested Development</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
