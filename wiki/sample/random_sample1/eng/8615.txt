<p><i><b>Sing Street</b></i> is a 2016 <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">musical</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">comedy-drama</a> film written, co-produced and directed by <a href="/wiki/John_Carney_(director)" title="John Carney (director)">John Carney</a>. Starring <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Boynton" title="Lucy Boynton">Lucy Boynton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Doyle_Kennedy" title="Maria Doyle Kennedy">Maria Doyle Kennedy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Reynor" title="Jack Reynor">Jack Reynor</a>, Kelly Thornton and <a href="/wiki/Ferdia_Walsh-Peelo" title="Ferdia Walsh-Peelo">Ferdia Walsh-Peelo</a>, the story revolves around a boy starting a band to impress a girl.</p>
<p>The film had its world premiere at the <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a> on 24 January 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sing_Street_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sing_Street-4">[4]</a></sup> It was released in Ireland on 17 March 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmdates.co.uk_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-filmdates.co.uk-5">[5]</a></sup> in the United States on 15 April and in the United Kingdom on 20 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Alessandro_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Alessandro-6">[6]</a></sup> The film received positive reviews from critics and grossed $13 million worldwide, and was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Motion_Picture_%E2%80%93_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Musical or Comedy">Best Motion Picture – Musical or Comedy</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globes" title="Golden Globes">Golden Globes</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In south inner-city Dublin in 1985, Robert Lawlor (<a href="/wiki/Aidan_Gillen" title="Aidan Gillen">Aidan Gillen</a>) is struggling with his architecture practice and his marriage, and drinks and smokes to excess. At a family meeting, he announces that in order to save money he is taking his youngest son Conor (<a href="/wiki/Ferdia_Walsh-Peelo" title="Ferdia Walsh-Peelo">Ferdia Walsh-Peelo</a>) out of his expensive fee-paying school and moving him to a free state-school, <a href="/wiki/Synge_Street_CBS" title="Synge Street CBS">Synge Street CBS</a>, which Robert asserts is of equally high repute. Conor's elder brother Brendan (<a href="/wiki/Jack_Reynor" title="Jack Reynor">Jack Reynor</a>) ribs him about the change as well as about the state of the family unit.</p>
<p>Conor appears in school on the first day in his new uniform, but without the regulation black shoes. The school principal Br. Baxter (<a href="/wiki/Don_Wycherley" title="Don Wycherley">Don Wycherley</a>), takes him to task for this despite Conor’s pleas of being unable to afford new black shoes, eventually forcing him to complete the day shoeless. The shoe problem is resolved by painting the shoes black using paint from the art room. An encounter with the school bully, Barry (Ian Kenny) introduces Conor to Darren (Ben Carolan), an ally and budding entrepreneur. When Conor tells the beautiful aspiring model, Raphina (<a href="/wiki/Lucy_Boynton" title="Lucy Boynton">Lucy Boynton</a>), in order to impress her, that he needs a model for a music video his band is making, Darren agrees to manage the outfit and introduces him to multi-instrumentalist Eamon (Mark McKenna )</p>
<p>The band practise in Eamon's living room, playing a bunch of 1980s covers. Conor however has a sibling-induced epiphany when Brendan encourages him to drop the covers and develop the band's own style. He begins writing original songs with Eamon. The band film their first music video for their song "The Riddle of the Model", wearing a motley assortment of often comical costumes, with Raphina acting as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ingenue_(stock_character)" title="Ingenue (stock character)">ingenue</a> and makeup artist. Raphina gives Conor the name "Cosmo", which she says is more in keeping with his new band's image and the band continues to write and record new songs. After spending the day filming a music video for a new song, Conor and Raphina kiss and exchange confidences. Conor also manages to stand up to Barry by pointing out the insignificance of his life as well as his own, which greatly upsets Barry.</p>
<p>At home Robert and Penny (<a href="/wiki/Maria_Doyle_Kennedy" title="Maria Doyle Kennedy">Maria Doyle Kennedy</a>)'s marriage is falling apart, while Raphina and Cosmo's relationship blossoms. Cosmo takes Raphina out to <a href="/wiki/Dalkey_Island" title="Dalkey Island">Dalkey Island</a> in his granddad's motor cruiser. Here they view the <a href="/wiki/Roll-on/roll-off" title="Roll-on/roll-off">car ferry</a> leaving <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Laoghaire" title="Dún Laoghaire">Dún Laoghaire</a> for Britain and discuss Raphina's dream of leaving Ireland for <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.</p>
<p>Cosmo, Raphina, and the band then prepare to film a "Back to the Future" inspired music video for their new song, "Drive It Like You Stole It," but Cosmo is disheartened when Raphina fails to show up. Later, she reveals that she was set to leave for London with her boyfriend that day, but he abandoned her. Deeply hurt by her lack of disclosure and becoming even more offended by her nonchalance, Cosmo breaks up with Raphina. Because of the break-up, along with his family's problems, Cosmo finds difficulty in writing or playing music, but Brendan urges Cosmo to carry on so that at least he can escape his small-town doldrums and make a better future for himself. An opportunity arises for the band to play live at an end of year party at school. Cosmo manages to strike a friendship with Barry, offering him the chance to be the band's roadie and escape the physically and emotionally abusive relationship he has with his alcoholic father, which he accepts.</p>
<p>Cosmo prepares a new song, "Brown Shoes," mocking Baxter, which the band perform as an encore while distributing homemade masks of Baxter’s face. Raphina arrives before the band finishes their song and reconciles with Cosmo, and both of them leave the party and the school in uproar. After secretly saying his goodbyes to his family later that night, Cosmo and Raphina persuade Brendan to drive them to Dalkey so they can escape in the motor cruiser and head to London. Brendan agrees and drives them to the harbour, where Brendan and Cosmo exchange an emotional embrace. Cosmo and Raphina sail out to sea, following the ferry across the rough <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> to a new life in London. Brendan watches them disappear into the distance and cheers, overjoyed that his younger brother has left to go on to greater things.</p>

<p>The film's closing titles include the dedication "For brothers everywhere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relish-1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-relish-1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2014, It was announced that <a href="/wiki/John_Carney_(director)" title="John Carney (director)">John Carney</a> would be directing the film, from a screenplay he wrote about a boy starting a band in order to impress a girl, with Carney producing through his Distressed Films banner, along with Anthony Bregman through his Likely Story Banner, Kevin Frakes for PalmStar Media, and Raj Brinder Singh for Merced Media Partners, with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Trijbits" title="Paul Trijbits">Paul Trijbits</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Grass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Christian Grass (page does not exist)">Christian Grass</a> for FilmWave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNary_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNary-9">[9]</a></sup> The film is a semi-autobiographical depiction of Carney's upbringing in Dublin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.hitfix.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.hitfix.com-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a July 2014 interview, Carney announced he would be casting unknown actors in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The unknown actors turned out to be <a href="/wiki/Ferdia_Walsh-Peelo" title="Ferdia Walsh-Peelo">Ferdia Walsh-Peelo</a>, Ben Carolan, Mark McKenna, Percy Chamburuka, Conor Hamilton, Karl Rice, and Ian Kenny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In September 2014, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Aidan_Gillen" title="Aidan Gillen">Aidan Gillen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Doyle_Kennedy" title="Maria Doyle Kennedy">Maria Doyle Kennedy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Reynor" title="Jack Reynor">Jack Reynor</a> had joined the cast of the film, portraying the role of a father, wife and son respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> on the film began in September 2014 in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, and concluded on 25 October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Among the locations where the film was shot was at its namesake school <a href="/wiki/Synge_Street_CBS" title="Synge Street CBS">Synge Street CBS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rte-1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-rte-1-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of the original music by the band "Sing Street" was composed by 80s veteran composer <a href="/wiki/Gary_Clark_(musician)" title="Gary Clark (musician)">Gary Clark</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> with Carney, Ken and Carl Papenfus of the band <a href="/wiki/Relish_(Northern_Irish_band)" title="Relish (Northern Irish band)">Relish</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Graham Henderson and Zamo Riffman also receiving writing credits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic-2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-2-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adam_Levine" title="Adam Levine">Adam Levine</a> co-wrote (with Carney and <a href="/wiki/Glen_Hansard" title="Glen Hansard">Glen Hansard</a>) and sings on the track "Go Now".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew-1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ew-1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film also features music of the period from <a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a>, <a href="/wiki/A-ha" title="A-ha">A-ha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">Duran Duran</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hall_%26_Oates" title="Hall &amp; Oates">Hall &amp; Oates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spandau_Ballet" title="Spandau Ballet">Spandau Ballet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Jam" title="The Jam">The Jam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ut-weinstein-trailer_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ut-weinstein-trailer-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2014, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/FilmNation_Entertainment" title="FilmNation Entertainment">FilmNation Entertainment</a> had been selected to sell international rights to the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In May 2014, it was announced <a href="/wiki/The_Weinstein_Company" title="The Weinstein Company">The Weinstein Company</a> had acquired U.S distribution rights to the film, for $3 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film had its world premiere at the <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a> on 24 January 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sing_Street_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sing_Street-4">[4]</a></sup> The film screened at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dublin_Film_Festival" title="Dublin Film Festival">Dublin Film Festival</a> on 18 February 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-screendaily-1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-screendaily-1-28">[28]</a></sup> and at <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> on 11 March 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sxsw-1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-sxsw-1-29">[29]</a></sup> The film was released in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> on 17 March,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> on 20 May 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmdates.co.uk_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-filmdates.co.uk-5">[5]</a></sup> It was released in the United States on 15 April 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27Alessandro_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-D.27Alessandro-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Sing Street</i> grossed $3.2 million in the United States and Canada and $10.4 million in other countries for a worldwide total of $13.6 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sing_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a> website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film has an approval rating of 96% based on 167 reviews and an average rating is 8.1/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "<i>Sing Street</i> is a feel-good musical with huge heart and irresistible optimism, and its charming cast and hummable tunes help to elevate its familiar plotting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has a score of 79 out of 100 based on 37 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guy Lodge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variety.com" title="Variety.com">Variety.com</a> gave the film a positive review, writing "Perched on a tricky precipice between chippy kitchen-sink realism and lush wish-fulfilment fantasy, this mini-<i><a href="/wiki/The_Commitments_(film)" title="The Commitments (film)">Commitments</a></i> gets away with even its cutesiest indulgences thanks to a wholly lovable ensemble of young Irish talent and the tightest pop tunes—riffing on Duran Duran and the Cure with equal abandon and affection—any gaggle of Catholic schoolboys could hope to write themselves. Given the right marketing and word of mouth, this Weinstein Co. release could <i>Sing</i> a song of far more than sixpence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Kermode" title="Mark Kermode">Mark Kermode</a> gave the film four out of five stars, writing: "When it comes to capturing the strange, romantic magic of making music, few modern film-makers are more on the money than John Carney." He added, "The bittersweet, 'happy sad' drama that follows has drawn inevitable, if misguided, comparisons with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Commitments_(film)" title="The Commitments (film)">The Commitments</a></i>, yet tonally this is closer to the teen spirit of Todd Graff's 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/Bandslam" title="Bandslam">Bandslam</a></i> [...] or even Richard Linklater’s sublime <i><a href="/wiki/School_of_Rock" title="School of Rock">School of Rock</a></i>. As Carney has proved previously, he knows how to straddle the line between the sound in the room and the sound in your head – a sequence that segues from bedroom composition to living room rehearsal (with tea and biscuits) to full studio production perfectly negotiates the space between kitchen-sink realism and musical fantasy in which this lovely, lyrical movie casts its spell". Kermode concluded by saying, "Happy sad indeed. I laughed, I cried, I bought the soundtrack album."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observer_22_May_2016_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Observer_22_May_2016-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
