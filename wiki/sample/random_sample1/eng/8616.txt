<p><b>Singapore English</b> is the English language spoken in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, of which there are two main forms, Standard Singapore English and Singapore Colloquial English (better known as <a href="/wiki/Singlish" title="Singlish">Singlish</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bunkyo.ac.jp_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bunkyo.ac.jp-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Singapore is a cosmopolitan city with 42% of its population born outside the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (June 2013)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Singaporeans, even those of the same ethnic group, have many different first languages and cultures. For example, in 2005, among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Singaporean" title="Chinese Singaporean">Chinese Singaporeans</a>, nearly a third speak English as their main language at home while almost half speak Mandarin, and the rest speak various mutually unintelligible Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topolect" title="Topolect">topolects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singstat_General_Household_Survey_2005_Statistical_Release_1:_Socio-Demographic_and_Economic_Characteristics_Chapter_2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singstat_General_Household_Survey_2005_Statistical_Release_1:_Socio-Demographic_and_Economic_Characteristics_Chapter_2-4">[4]</a></sup> In the Indian community, most Singaporeans of Indian descent speak either English or Tamil at home. The English language is now the most popular medium of communication among students from primary school to university. Many families use two or three languages on a regular basis, and English is often one of them. In the past,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> some children received fewer years of English education than others. As such, the level of fluency in English among residents in Singapore varies greatly from person to person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerlin2015_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerlin2015-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CITESHORT" title="Wikipedia:CITESHORT"><span title="More information is required to link this short citation to its long citation. (December 2016)">incomplete short citation</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_Singapore_English">Classification of Singapore English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification of Singapore English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Singapore English can be classified into Singapore Standard English (SSE) and Singapore Colloquial English (Singlish).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The language consists of three sociolects; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrolect" title="Acrolect">Acrolect</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesolect" title="Mesolect">Mesolect</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilect" title="Basilect">Basilect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Both Acrolect and Mesolect are regarded as Standard Singapore English, while Basilect is considered as Singlish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Acrolect; there is no significant and consistent difference from the features of Standard British English (SBE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mesolect; it has some features distinct from SBE <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup>
<ol>
<li>Question tenses in an indirect form; e.g. "May I ask where is the toilet?"</li>
<li>Indefinite article deletion (copula absence); e.g. "May I apply for car licence?" (Instead of saying "a" car licence)</li>
<li>Lack of marking in verb forms (Regularization); e.g. "He always go to the shopping centre."</li>
</ol>
</li>
<li>Basilect (Singlish);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup>
<ol>
<li>Generalised "is it" question tag; e.g. "You are coming today, Is it?"</li>
<li>Consistent copula deletion; e.g. "My handwriting not good, lah."</li>
<li>Use of particles like ah; lah, e.g. "Wait ah; Hurry lah, I need to go now!"</li>
</ol>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Singaporeans vary their language according to social situations (Pakir 1991) and attitudes that they want to convey (Poedjosoedarmo 1993).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-videoweb.nie.edu.sg_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-videoweb.nie.edu.sg-9">[9]</a></sup> The better educated Singaporeans who have a "higher" standard of English , tend to speak "Standard" Singapore English (the acrolect). On the other hand, and typically the less-educated or Singaporeans whose first language isn't English, speak Singlish (the basilect).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-videoweb.nie.edu.sg_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-videoweb.nie.edu.sg-9">[9]</a></sup> Gupta (1994) said that most Singaporean speakers systematically alternate between colloquial and formal language depending on the formality of the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-videoweb.nie.edu.sg_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-videoweb.nie.edu.sg-9">[9]</a></sup> The constant use of both SSE and Singlish has resulted in the gradual emergence of a mesolect, an intermediate form of Singapore English, half-way between formal and informal Singapore English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-videoweb.nie.edu.sg_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-videoweb.nie.edu.sg-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Singapore_English">Standard Singapore English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standard Singapore English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Standard Singapore English is the standard form of English used in Singapore. It generally resembles British English and is often used in more formal settings such as the workplace or when communicating with people of higher authority such as teachers, bosses and government officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Singapore English acts as the "bridge" among different Singapore's ethnic groups<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leimgruber_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leimgruber-11">[11]</a></sup> Standard Singapore English retains British spelling<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and grammar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, the spelling "tyre" is used instead of "tire", and shopping malls are referred to as "shopping centres".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British established a trading post on the island of Singapore in 1819, and the population grew rapidly thereafter, attracting many immigrants from Chinese provinces and from India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The roots of Standard Singapore English derive from nearly a century and a half of British control. Its local character seems to have developed early in the English-medium schools of the 19th and early-20th centuries, where the teachers often came from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceylon" title="Ceylon">Ceylon</a>, as well as from various parts of Europe and from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a>. By 1900 <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_(mixed_ancestry)" title="Eurasian (mixed ancestry)">Eurasians</a> and other locals were employed as teachers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Apart from a period of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Singapore" title="Japanese occupation of Singapore">Japanese occupation</a> (1942-1945), Singapore remained a British colony until 1963, when it joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malaysian_Federation" title="Malaysian Federation">Malaysian Federation</a>, but this proved a short-lived alliance, largely due to ethnic rivalries. Since <a href="/wiki/Singapore_in_Malaysia#Expulsion" title="Singapore in Malaysia">its expulsion from the Federation in 1965</a>, Singapore has operated as an independent <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a>. English served as the administrative language of the British colonial government, and when Singapore <a href="/wiki/Self-governance_of_Singapore" title="Self-governance of Singapore">gained self-government</a> in 1959 and independence in 1965, the Singaporean government decided to keep English as the main language to maximise economic prosperity. The use of English as the nation's first language serves to bridge the gap between the diverse ethnic groups in Singapore; English operates as the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> of the nation. The use of English - as the global language for commerce, technology and science - also helped to expedite Singapore's development and integration into the global economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Schools generally use English as the language of instruction, although children also learn the traditional Singapore language<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2016)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> to remain strong with their culture and traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The standard Singaporean accent used to be officially <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">RP</a>. However, in recent decades,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> a standard Singaporean accent, quite independent of any external standard, including RP, started to emerge. A 2003 study by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Education" title="National Institute of Education">National Institute of Education</a> in Singapore suggests that a standard Singaporean pronunciation is emerging and is on the cusp of being standardised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Singaporean accents can be said<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">non-rhotic</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore.E2.80.99s_Speak_Good_English_Movement">Singapore’s Speak Good English Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singapore’s Speak Good English Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The wide use of Singlish led the government to launch the Speak Good English movement in Singapore in 2000 in an attempt to replace Singlish with Standard English. This movement was made to show the need for Singaporeans to speak Standard English. Nowadays, all children in schools are being taught Standard English with one of the other official languages (Chinese, Malay, Tamil) being taught as a second language. In Singapore, English is a “working language” that serves the economy and development and is associated with the broader global community. Meanwhile, the rest are “mother tongues” that are associated with the country’s culture. Speaking Standard English also helps Singaporeans communicate and express themselves in their everyday life. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Singaporean government recently made an announcement named “Speak Good English Movement brings fun back to Grammar and good English” where the strategies used in order to promote their program are explained. Specifically, it would release a series of videos that demystify the difficulty and dullness of the grammatical rules of the English language. These videos provide a more humorous approach in learning basic grammar rules. The Singaporeans will now be able to practise the grammatical rules in both written and spoken English thanks to a more interactive approach. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Singaporean_accent">Standard Singaporean accent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Standard Singaporean accent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like in most Commonwealth countries outside of Canada and Australia, the accents of most reasonably educated Singaporeans who speak English as their native language are more similar to British <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> (RP) than <a href="/wiki/General_American" title="General American">General American</a>, although immediately noticeable differences exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This is the same for people who speak English as their second language.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malay.2C_Indian.2C_and_Chinese_Influences">Malay, Indian, and Chinese Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Malay, Indian, and Chinese Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although Standard Singapore English (SSE) is mainly influenced by British English and, recently, American English, there are other languages that also contribute to its use on a regular basis. The majority of Singaporeans speak more than one language, with many speaking three to four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singapore_Colloquial_English_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singapore_Colloquial_English-21">[21]</a></sup> Most Singaporean kids are brought up bilingual. They are introduced to Malay, Chinese, Tamil, or Singapore Colloquial English (Singlish) as their native languages, depending on their families' ethnic backgrounds and/or socioeconomic status. They also acquire those languages from interacting with friends in school and other places. Naturally, the presence of other languages in Singapore has influenced Singapore English, something particularly apparent in Singlish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singapore_Colloquial_English_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singapore_Colloquial_English-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Singapore English and Singapore colloquial English are used with multiple accents. Words from Malay, Chinese, and Tamil are also borrowed, if not code-switched, into Singapore English. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> words “makan” (to eat), “habis” (finished), and the <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a> word "<a href="/wiki/Kiasu" title="Kiasu">kiasu</a>" are constantly used and adopted to SE vocabularies, to the point that Singaporeans are not necessarily aware of which language those words are from. Furthermore, the word “kiasu” has been used in the Singapore press since 2000 without being <a href="/wiki/Italic_type" title="Italic type">italicized</a>; Kiasu means "always wanting the best for oneself and willing to try hard to get it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singapore_Colloquial_English_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Singapore_Colloquial_English-21">[21]</a></sup> In another journal, "Kiasu" is also defined as 'characterized by a grasping or selfish attitude arising from a fear of missing out on something' (usu. adj., definition from OED (Simpson and Weiner 2000); Hokkien kia(n)su).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leimgruber_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leimgruber-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_dialects_of_English_in_Singapore">Foreign dialects of English in Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreign dialects of English in Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Indian_English" title="Indian English">Indian English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasians_in_Singapore" title="Eurasians in Singapore">Eurasians in Singapore</a></div>
<p>A wide range of foreign English dialects can be heard in Singapore. American and British accents are often heard on local television and radio due to the frequent airing of foreign television programmes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Indian accent, spoken by Indian expatriates, can also be heard daily on the streets of Singapore. In addition, accents originating from Hong Kong, China, Indonesia, Australia and Malaysia, with some possibly from Latin American countries, can also be heard among the population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore_Colloquial_English_.2F_Singlish">Singapore Colloquial English / Singlish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Singapore Colloquial English / Singlish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Singlish" title="Singlish">Singlish</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Singlish" title="Singlish">Singlish</a> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English-based_creole_language" title="English-based creole language">English-based creole language</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latrobe.edu.au_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-latrobe.edu.au-23">[23]</a></sup> spoken in Singapore. Unlike SSE, Singlish includes many discourse particles and loan words from <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singaporean_Mandarin" title="Singaporean Mandarin">Mandarin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singaporean_Hokkien" title="Singaporean Hokkien">Hokkien</a>. It is commonly regarded with low prestige in the country and is hence not used in formal communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bunkyo.ac.jp_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bunkyo.ac.jp-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, Singlish has been used in several locally produced films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Army_Daze" title="Army Daze">Army Daze</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mee_Pok_Man" title="Mee Pok Man">Mee Pok Man</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Talking_Cock_the_Movie" title="Talking Cock the Movie">Talking Cock the Movie</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> among others. Some local sitcoms, in particular <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phua_Chu_Kang" title="Phua Chu Kang">Phua Chu Kang Pte Ltd</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> also feature extensive use of Singlish.</p>
<p>The proliferation of Singlish has been controversial and the use of Singlish is not endorsed by the country government. Singapore's first two prime ministers, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew" title="Lee Kuan Yew">Lee Kuan Yew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goh_Chok_Tong" title="Goh Chok Tong">Goh Chok Tong</a>, have publicly declared<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> that Singlish is a substandard variety that handicaps Singaporeans, presents an obstacle to learning standard English, and renders the speaker incomprehensible to everyone except another Singlish speaker. The country's third and current prime minister, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Hsien_Loong" title="Lee Hsien Loong">Lee Hsien Loong</a>, has also said that Singlish should not be part of Singapore's identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In addition, the government launched the <a href="/wiki/Speak_Good_English_Movement" title="Speak Good English Movement">Speak Good English Movement</a> in 2000 to encourage Singaporeans to speak proper English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite strong criticisms of Singlish, linguist David Yoong has put forward the argument that "Singaporeans who subscribe to Singlish and have a positive attitude towards the code see Singlish as a language that transcends social barriers" and that the language can be used to "forge rapport and, perhaps more importantly, the Singaporean identity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latrobe.edu.au_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-latrobe.edu.au-23">[23]</a></sup> Sociolinguist Anthea Fraser Gupta also argues that Singlish and standard English can and do co-exist, saying that "there is no evidence that the presence of Singlish causes damage to standard English".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_language_trends_in_Singapore">English language trends in Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: English language trends in Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2010, speakers of English in Singapore were classified into five different groups: 1. Those who have no knowledge of English (very few people, most of whom were born before the 1950s); 2. Those who regard English as a foreign language, have limited command of, and seldom speak the language (mostly the older age groups, people, the less educated young);</p>
<p>3. Those who learnt English at school and can use it but have a dominant other language (many people, of all ages); 4. Those who learnt English at school and use it as their dominant language (many people, of all ages); 5. Those who learnt English as a native language (sometimes as a sole native language, but usually alongside other languages) and use it as their dominant language (many people, mostly children born after 1965 to highly educated parents).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, English is the most commonly spoken language in all Singaporean homes. One effect of mass immigration into Singapore since 2000, especially from China, has been to increase the proportion of the population to whom English is a foreign language. The trend favours an increasing use of English and stability in Mandarin use at the expense of Chinese dialects (apparently as the Chinese population switches from Chinese dialects to Mandarin, and then to English), whilst Malay use slowly erodes whereas Tamil use continues.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_official_languages_in_Singapore">Other official languages in Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other official languages in Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>English is one of Singapore's four official languages, along with <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, Chinese and <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constitution_of_Singapore_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Constitution_of_Singapore-36">[36]</a></sup> The national language is Malay<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constitution_of_Singapore_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Constitution_of_Singapore-36">[36]</a></sup> for historical reasons, as Singapore was part of the <a href="/wiki/Johor_Sultanate" title="Johor Sultanate">Johor Sultanate</a> until the 19th century and was briefly <a href="/wiki/Singapore_in_Malaysia" title="Singapore in Malaysia">in union with Malaysia</a> between 1963 and 1965. All official signs, legislation and documents are required to be in English, although translations in the other official languages are sometimes included. Under the <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Singapore" title="Education in Singapore">education system</a>, English is the language of instruction for nearly all subjects except the official Mother Tongue languages (the other three official languages) and the literatures of those languages.</p>
