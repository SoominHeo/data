<p><b>37 Geminorum</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> located at the northwest part of the northern <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a> <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_(constellation)" title="Gemini (constellation)">Gemini</a>. Its distance from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is about 56.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a>. It is a solitary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-type_main_sequence_star" title="G-type main sequence star">G-type main sequence star</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classification</a> of G0 V. No extrasolar planets have yet been discovered around it.</p>
<p>37 Geminorum has many physical properties in common with the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and is one of the nearest <a href="/wiki/Solar_analog" title="Solar analog">solar analogs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Due to this fact some scientists believe that the prospects for life in its vicinity are good. In September 2003, 37 Geminorum was identified by astrobiologist <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Turnbull" title="Margaret Turnbull">Margaret Turnbull</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tucson" title="Tucson">Tucson</a> as one of the most promising nearby candidates for hosting life, based on her analysis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HabCat" title="HabCat">HabCat</a> list of stars.</p>
<p>There was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Message_to_Extra-Terrestrial_Intelligence" title="Message to Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence">METI</a> message sent to 37 Geminorum. It was transmitted from <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>'s largest <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, 70-meter <a href="/wiki/Yevpatoria" title="Yevpatoria">Yevpatoria</a> Planetary Radar. The message was named the <a href="/wiki/Teen_Age_Message" title="Teen Age Message">Teen Age Message</a>, it was sent on September 3, 2001, and it will arrive at 37 Geminorum in December 2057.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>To date no solar twin with an exact match as that of the Sun has been found, however, there are some stars that come very close to being identical to that of the Sun, and are such considered solar twins by the majority of the public. An exact solar twin would be a G2V star with a 5,778K temperature, be 4.6 billion years old, with the correct metallicity and a 0.1% <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosity</a> variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Stars with an age of 4.6 billion years are at the most stable state. Proper metallicity and size are also very important to low luminosity variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
