<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pair-instability supernova</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3870" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg/400px-Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg/600px-Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg/800px-Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sn2006gy_collapse_ill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This illustration explains the pair-instability supernova process that astronomers think triggered the explosion in <a href="/wiki/SN_2006gy" title="SN 2006gy">SN 2006gy</a>. When a star is very massive, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">gamma-rays</a> produced in its core can become so energetic that some of their energy is drained away into production of particle and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-particle" title="Anti-particle">anti-particle</a> pairs. The resulting drop in pressure causes the star to partially collapse under its own huge gravity. After this violent collapse, runaway thermonuclear reactions (not shown here) ensue and the star explodes, spewing the remains into space.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>pair-instability supernova</b> occurs when <a href="/wiki/Pair_production" title="Pair production">pair production</a>, the production of free <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> in the collision between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_nuclei" title="Atomic nuclei">atomic nuclei</a> and energetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">gamma rays</a>, reduces thermal pressure inside a <a href="/wiki/Hypergiant" title="Hypergiant">supermassive star</a>'s core. This pressure drop leads to a partial collapse, then greatly accelerated burning in a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">runaway</a> thermonuclear explosion which blows the star completely apart without leaving a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> remnant behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Pair-instability <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> can only happen in stars with a mass range from around 130 to 250 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a> and low to moderate <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a> (low abundance of elements other than hydrogen and helium, a situation common in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_III_stars" title="Population III stars">Population III stars</a>). The recently observed objects <a href="/wiki/SN_2006gy" title="SN 2006gy">SN 2006gy</a>, <a href="/wiki/SN_2007bi" title="SN 2007bi">SN 2007bi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN2007bi_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SN2007bi-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SN_2213-1745" title="SN 2213-1745">SN 2213-1745</a> and <a href="/wiki/SN_1000%2B0216" title="SN 1000+0216">SN 1000+0216</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooke_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cooke-3">[3]</a></sup> are hypothesized to have been pair-instability supernovae.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">Physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photon_pressure">Photon pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Photon pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Light in thermal equilibrium has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_body_spectrum" title="Black body spectrum">black body spectrum</a> with an energy density proportional to the fourth power of the temperature (hence the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann law">Stefan-Boltzmann law</a>). The wavelength of maximum emission from a blackbody is inversely proportional to its temperature. That is, the frequency, and the energy, of the greatest population of photons of black body radiation is directly proportional to the temperature, and reaches the gamma ray energy range at temperatures above <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7008300000000000000♠</span>3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>8</sup></span> K.</p>
<p>In very large hot stars, pressure from gamma rays in the stellar core keeps the upper layers of the star supported against gravitational pull from the core. If the energy density of gamma rays is suddenly reduced, then the outer layers of the star will collapse inwards. The sudden heating and compression of the core generates gamma rays energetic enough to be converted into an avalanche of electron-positron pairs, further reducing the pressure. When the collapse stops, the positrons find electrons and the pressure from gamma rays is driven up, again. The population of positrons provides a brief reservoir of new gamma rays as the expanding supernova's core pressure drops.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pair_creation_and_annihilation">Pair creation and annihilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pair creation and annihilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sufficiently energetic gamma rays can interact with nuclei, electrons, or one another to produce electron-positron pairs, and electron-positron pairs can annihilate, producing gamma rays. From Einstein's equation <span class="nowrap"><i>E</i> = <i>mc</i><sup>2</sup></span>, gamma rays must have more energy than the mass of the electron–positron pairs to produce these pairs.</p>
<p>At the high densities of a stellar core, pair production and annihilation occur rapidly, thereby keeping gamma rays, electrons, and positrons in thermal equilibrium. The higher the temperature, the higher the gamma ray energies, and the larger the amount of energy transferred.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pair-instability">Pair-instability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pair-instability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As temperatures and gamma ray energies increase, more and more gamma ray energy is absorbed in creating electron-positron pairs. This reduction in gamma ray energy density reduces the radiation pressure that supports the outer layers of the star. The star contracts, compressing and heating the core, thereby increasing the proportion of energy absorbed by pair creation. Pressure nonetheless increases, but in a pair-instability collapse, the increase in pressure is not enough to resist the increase in gravitational forces as the star becomes denser.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_susceptibility">Stellar susceptibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stellar susceptibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For a star to undergo pair-instability supernova, the increased creation of positron/electron pairs by gamma ray collisions must reduce outward pressure enough for inward gravitational pressure to overwhelm it. High rotational speed and/or metallicity can prevent this. Stars with these characteristics still contract as their outward pressure drops, but unlike their slower or less metal-rich cousins, these stars continue to exert enough outward pressure to prevent gravitational collapse.</p>
<p>Stars formed by collision mergers having a metallicity Z between 0.02 and 0.001 may end their lives as pair-instability supernovae if their mass is in the appropriate range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Very large high metallicity stars are probably unstable due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddington_limit" title="Eddington limit">Eddington limit</a>, and would tend to shed mass during the formation process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_behavior">Stellar behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stellar behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="512" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg/250px-Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg/375px-Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg/500px-Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Supernovae_as_initial_mass-metallicity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Supernovae as initial mass-metallicity</div>

<p>Several sources describe the stellar behavior for large stars in pair-instability conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fryer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fryer-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Below_100_solar_masses">Below 100 solar masses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Below 100 solar masses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gamma rays produced by stars of fewer than 100 or so solar masses are not energetic enough to produce electron-positron pairs. Some of these stars will undergo supernovae at the end of their lives, but the causative mechanisms are unrelated to pair-instability.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100_to_130_solar_masses">100 to 130 solar masses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 100 to 130 solar masses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These stars are large enough to produce gamma rays with enough energy to create electron-positron pairs, but the resulting net reduction in counter-gravitational pressure is insufficient to cause the core-overpressure required for supernova. Instead, the contraction caused by pair-creation provokes increased thermonuclear activity within the star that repulses the inward pressure and returns the star to equilibrium. It is thought that stars of this size undergo a series of these pulses until they shed sufficient mass to drop below 100 solar masses, at which point they are no longer hot enough to support pair-creation. Pulsing of this nature may have been responsible for the variations in brightness experienced by <a href="/wiki/Eta_Carinae#Brightness_variations" title="Eta Carinae">Eta Carinae in 1843</a>, though this explanation is not universally accepted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="130_to_250_solar_masses">130 to 250 solar masses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 130 to 250 solar masses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For very high mass stars, with mass at least 130 and up to perhaps roughly 250 solar masses, a true pair-instability supernova can occur. In these stars, the first time that conditions support pair creation instability, the situation runs out of control. The collapse proceeds to efficiently compress the star's core; the overpressure is sufficient to allow runaway nuclear fusion to burn it in several seconds, creating a thermonuclear explosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fryer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fryer-6">[6]</a></sup> With more thermal energy released than the star's <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_binding_energy" title="Gravitational binding energy">gravitational binding energy</a>, it is completely disrupted; no <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> or other remnant is left behind.</p>
<p>In addition to the immediate energy release, a large fraction of the star's core is transformed to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_nickel" title="Isotopes of nickel">nickel-56</a>, a radioactive <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> which decays with a half-life of 6.1 days into <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cobalt" title="Isotopes of cobalt">cobalt-56</a>. <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cobalt" title="Isotopes of cobalt">Cobalt-56</a> has a half-life of 77 days and then further decays to the stable isotope <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_iron" title="Isotopes of iron">iron-56</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis" title="Supernova nucleosynthesis">Supernova nucleosynthesis</a>). For the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypernova" title="Hypernova">hypernova</a> <a href="/wiki/SN_2006gy" title="SN 2006gy">SN 2006gy</a>, studies indicate that perhaps 40 solar masses of the original star were released as Ni-56, almost the entire mass of the star's core regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-5">[5]</a></sup> Collision between the exploding star core and gas it ejected earlier, and radioactive decay, release most of the visible light.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="250_solar_masses_or_more">250 solar masses or more</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 250 solar masses or more">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A different reaction mechanism, <a href="/wiki/Photodisintegration" title="Photodisintegration">photodisintegration</a>, results after collapse starts in stars of at least 250 solar masses. This endothermic (energy-absorbing) reaction causes the star to continue collapse into a black hole rather than exploding due to thermonuclear reactions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Luminosity">Luminosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Luminosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pair instability supernovae are popularly thought to be highly luminous. This is only the case for the most massive progenitors since the luminosity depends strongly on the ejected mass of radioactive Ni<sub>56</sub>. They can have peak luminosities of over 10<sup>37</sup> W, brighter than type Ia supernovae, but at lower masses peak luminosities are less than 10<sup>35</sup> W, comparable to or less than typical type II supernovae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appearance_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-appearance-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectrum">Spectrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Spectrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The spectra of pair instability supernovae depend on the nature of the progenitor star. Thus they can appear as type II or type Ib/c supernova spectra. Progenitors with a significant remaining hydrogen envelope will produce a type II supernova, those with no hydrogen but significant helium will produce a type Ib, and those with no hydrogen and virtually no helium will produce a type Ic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appearance_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-appearance-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_curves">Light curves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Light curves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to the spectra, the <a href="/wiki/Supernova#Light_curves" title="Supernova">light curves</a> are quite different from the common types of supernova. The light curves are highly extended, with peak luminosity occurring months after onset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appearance_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-appearance-7">[7]</a></sup> This is due to the extreme amounts of <sup>56</sup>Ni expelled, and the optically dense ejecta, as the star is entirely disrupted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remnant">Remnant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pair-instability_supernova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Remnant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="315" data-file-width="512" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg/250px-Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg/375px-Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg/500px-Remnants_of_single_massive_stars.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>Pair instability supernovae completely destroy the progenitor star and do not leave behind a neutron star or black hole. The entire mass of the star is ejected, so a nebular remnant is produced and many solar masses of heavy elements are returned to interstellar space.</p>
