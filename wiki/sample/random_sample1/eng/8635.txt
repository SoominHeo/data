<p>A <b>circumbinary planet</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> that orbits two <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> instead of one. Because of the short orbits of some <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary stars</a>, the only way for planets to form is by forming outside the orbit of the two stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations_and_discoveries">Observations and discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observations and discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confirmed_planets">Confirmed planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Confirmed planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="PSR_B1620-26">PSR B1620-26</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: PSR B1620-26">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first confirmed circumbinary extrasolar planet was found orbiting the system <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1620-26" title="PSR B1620-26">PSR B1620-26</a>, which contains a <a href="/wiki/Millisecond_pulsar" title="Millisecond pulsar">millisecond pulsar</a> and a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a> and is located in the <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a> <a href="/wiki/Messier_4" title="Messier 4">M4</a>. The existence of the third body was first reported in 1993,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and was suggested to be a planet based on 5 years of observational data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2003 the planet was characterised as being 2.5 times the mass of Jupiter in a low eccentricity orbit with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">semimajor axis</a> of 23 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="HD_202206">HD 202206</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: HD 202206">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first circumbinary extrasolar planet around a main sequence star was found in 2005 in the system <a href="/wiki/HD_202206" title="HD 202206">HD 202206</a>: a Jupiter-size planet orbiting a system composed of a Sun-like star and a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Correia_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Correia-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="HW_Virginis">HW Virginis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: HW Virginis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Announced in 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eclipsing_binary" title="Eclipsing binary">eclipsing binary</a> system <a href="/wiki/HW_Virginis" title="HW Virginis">HW Virginis</a>, comprising a <a href="/wiki/Subdwarf_B_star" title="Subdwarf B star">subdwarf B star</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a>, was claimed to also host a planetary system. The claimed planets have masses at least 8.47 and 19.23 times that of Jupiter respectively, and were proposed to have orbital periods of 9 and 16 years. The proposed outer planet is sufficiently massive that it may be considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a> under some definitions of the term,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but the discoverers claimed that the orbital configuration implies it would have formed like a planet from a circumbinary disc. Both planets may have accreted additional mass when the primary star lost material during its <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further work on the system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> showed that the orbits proposed for the candidate planets were catastrophically unstable on timescales far shorter than the age of the system. Indeed, the authors found that the system was so unstable that it simply cannot exist, with mean lifetimes of less than a thousand years across the whole range of plausible orbital solutions. Like other planetary systems proposed around similar evolved binary star systems, it seems likely that some mechanism other than claimed planets is responsible for the observed behaviour of the binary stars - and that the claimed planets simply do not exist.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kepler-16">Kepler-16</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kepler-16">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 15 September 2011, astronomers announced the first partial-eclipse-based discovery of a circumbinary planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The planet, called Kepler-16b, is about 200 light years from Earth, in the constellation Cygnus, and is believed to be a frozen world of rock and gas, about the mass of Saturn. It orbits two stars that are also circling each other, one about two-thirds the size of our sun, the other about a fifth the size of our sun. Each orbit of the stars by the planet takes 229 days, while the planet orbits the system's center of mass every 225 days; the stars eclipse each other every three weeks or so. Scientists made the finding through NASA's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_spacecraft" title="Kepler spacecraft">Kepler spacecraft</a>, which launched in 2009 and has been a driving force in the recent explosion in the discovery of distant planets.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kepler-1647">Kepler-1647</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kepler-1647">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A new planet, called <a href="/wiki/Kepler-1647b" title="Kepler-1647b">Kepler-1647b</a>, was announced on June 13, 2016. It was discovered using the Kepler telescope. The planet is a gas giant, similar in size to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, which makes it the largest circumbinary planet ever discovered. It is located in the stars' habitable zone, and it orbits the star system in 1107 days, which makes it the longest period of any confirmed transiting exoplanet so far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-Kepler-1647b_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-Kepler-1647b-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_observations">Other observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HD_98800.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/HD_98800.jpg/220px-HD_98800.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/HD_98800.jpg/330px-HD_98800.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/HD_98800.jpg/440px-HD_98800.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HD_98800.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An artist's impression of the binary star system <a href="/wiki/HD_98800" title="HD 98800">HD 98800 B</a>, which is surrounded by a disc that may be in the process of forming planets. HD 98800 B is itself a member of a quadruple star system.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Claims of a planet discovered via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microlensing" title="Microlensing">microlensing</a>, orbiting the close binary pair <a href="/wiki/MACHO-1997-BLG-41" title="MACHO-1997-BLG-41">MACHO-1997-BLG-41</a>, were announced in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The planet was said to be in a wide orbit around the two <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a> companions, but the claims were later retracted, as it turned out the detection could be better explained by the orbital motion of the binary stars themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several attempts have been made to detect planets around the eclipsing binary system <a href="/wiki/CM_Draconis" title="CM Draconis">CM Draconis</a>, itself part of the triple system GJ 630.1. The eclipsing binary has been surveyed for transiting planets, but no conclusive detections were made and eventually the existence of all the candidate planets was ruled out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> More recently, efforts have been made to detect variations in the timing of the eclipses of the stars caused by the reflex motion associated with an orbiting planet, but at present no discovery has been confirmed. The orbit of the binary stars is eccentric, which is unexpected for such a close binary as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">tidal forces</a> ought to have circularised the orbit. This may indicate the presence of a massive planet or <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a> in orbit around the pair whose gravitational effects maintain the eccentricity of the binary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Circumbinary discs that may indicate processes of planet formation have been found around several stars, and are in fact common around binaries with separations less than 3 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> One notable example is in the HD 98800 system, which comprises two pairs of binary stars separated by around 34 AU. The binary subsystem HD 98800 B, which consists of two stars of 0.70 and 0.58 solar masses in a highly eccentric orbit with semimajor axis 0.983 AU, is surrounded by a complex dust disc that is being warped by the gravitational effects of the mutually-inclined and eccentric stellar orbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The other binary subsystem, HD 98800 A, is not associated with significant amounts of dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="System_characteristics">System characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: System characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">Kepler</a> results indicate circumbinary planetary systems are relatively common (as of October 2013 the spacecraft had found seven planets out of roughly 1000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eclipsing_binaries" title="Eclipsing binaries">eclipsing binaries</a> searched).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_configuration">Stellar configuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stellar configuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a wide range of stellar configurations for which circumbinary planets can exist. Primary star masses range from 0.69 to 1.53 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kepler-16" title="Kepler-16">Kepler-16</a> A &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PH1" title="PH1">PH1</a> Aa), star mass ratios from 1.03 to 3.76 (<a href="/wiki/Kepler-34" title="Kepler-34">Kepler-34</a> &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PH1" title="PH1">PH1</a>), and binary eccentricity from 0.023 to 0.521 (<a href="/wiki/Kepler-47" title="Kepler-47">Kepler-47</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Kepler-34" title="Kepler-34">Kepler-34</a>). The distribution of planet eccentricities, range from nearly circular e=0.007 to a significant e=0.182 (<a href="/wiki/Kepler-16" title="Kepler-16">Kepler-16</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Kepler-34" title="Kepler-34">Kepler-34</a>). No <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonances</a> with the binary have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_dynamics">Orbital dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Orbital dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The binary stars Kepler-34 A and B have a highly eccentric orbit (e=0.521) around each other and their interaction with the planet is strong enough that a deviation from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws" title="Kepler's laws">Kepler's laws</a> is noticeable after just one orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-planarity">Co-planarity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Co-planarity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All Kepler circumbinary planets that were known as of August 2013 orbit their stars very close to the plane of the binary (in a prograde direction) which suggests a single-<a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">disk</a> formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup>However, not all circumbinary planets are co-planar with the binary: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kepler-413&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kepler-413 (page does not exist)">Kepler-413b</a> is <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">tilted</a> 2.5 degrees which may be due to the gravitational influence of other planets or a third star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kepler413wobble_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-kepler413wobble-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-413arxiv_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-413arxiv-24">[24]</a></sup> Taking into account the selection biases, the average mutual inclination between the planetary orbits and the stellar binaries is within ~3 degrees, consistent with the mutual inclinations of planets in multi-planetary systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Axial_tilt_precession">Axial tilt precession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Axial tilt precession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kepler-413&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kepler-413 (page does not exist)">Kepler-413b</a>'s spin axis might vary by as much as 30 degrees over 11 years, leading to rapid and erratic changes in seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-413arxiv_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-413arxiv-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simulations show that it is likely that all of the circumbinary planets known prior to a 2014 study <a href="/wiki/Planetary_migration" title="Planetary migration">migrated</a> significantly from their formation location with the possible exception of <a href="/wiki/Kepler-47" title="Kepler-47">Kepler-47</a>(AB)c.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-major_axes_close_to_critical_radius">Semi-major axes close to critical radius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Semi-major axes close to critical radius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The minimum stable star to circumbinary planet separation is about 2-4 times the binary star separation, or <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> about 3-8 times the binary period. The innermost planets in all the Kepler circumbinary systems have been found orbiting close to this radius. The planets have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axes</a> that lie between 1.09 and 1.46 times this critical radius. The reason could be that <a href="/wiki/Planetary_migration" title="Planetary migration">migration</a> might become inefficient near the critical radius, leaving planets just outside this radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, it has been found that the distribution of the innermost planetary semi-major axes is consistent with a log-uniform distribution, taking into account the selection biases, where closer-in planets can be detected more easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-25">[25]</a></sup> This questions the pile-up of planets near the stability limit as well as the dominance of planet migration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absence_of_planets_around_shorter_period_binaries">Absence of planets around shorter period binaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Absence of planets around shorter period binaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most Kepler eclipsing binaries have periods less than 1 day but the shortest period of a Kepler eclipsing binary hosting a planet is 7.4 days (<a href="/wiki/Kepler-47" title="Kepler-47">Kepler-47</a>). The short-period binaries are unlikely to have formed in such a tight orbit and their lack of planets may be related to the mechanism that removed <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> allowing the stars to orbit so closely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planet_size_limit">Planet size limit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Planet size limit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of June 2016, all but one of the confirmed Kepler circumbinary planets are smaller than Jupiter. This cannot be a selection effect because larger planets are easier to detect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup> Simulations had predicted this would be the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitability">Habitability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Habitability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Habitability_of_binary_star_systems" title="Habitability of binary star systems">Habitability of binary star systems</a></div>
<p>All the Kepler circumbinary planets are either close to or actually in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a>. None of them are <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a>, but large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moons" title="Moons">moons</a> of such planets could be habitable. Because of the stellar binarity, the insolation received by the planet will likely be time-varying in a way quite unlike the regular sunlight Earth receives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky_22-6"><a href="#cite_note-WelshOroszCarterFabrycky-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><big>Transit Probability</big></b></p>
<p>Circumbinary planets are generally more likely to transit than planets around a single star. The probability when the planetary orbit overlaps with the stellar binary orbit has been obtained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> For planets orbiting eclipsing stellar binaries (such as the detected systems), the analytical expression of the transit probability in a finite observation time has been obtained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-:8-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_pair_of_planets_around_HD_202206_or_a_circumbinary_planet.3F">A pair of planets around HD 202206 or a circumbinary planet?</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: A pair of planets around HD 202206 or a circumbinary planet?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/HD_202206" title="HD 202206">HD 202206</a> is a Sun-like star orbited by two objects, one of 17 <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass">Mj</a> and one of 2.4 <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_mass" title="Jupiter mass">Mj</a>. The classification of HD 202206 b as a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a> or "superplanet" is currently unclear. The two objects could have both formed in a protoplanetary disk with the inner one becoming a superplanet, or the outer planet could have formed in a circumbinary disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Correia_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Correia-5">[5]</a></sup> A dynamical analysis of the system further shows a 5:1 mean motion resonance between the planet and the brown dwarf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> These observations raise the question of how this system was formed, but numerical simulations show that a planet formed in a circumbinary disk can migrate inward until it is captured in resonance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumbinary_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Circumbinary planets are common in many <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> stories:</p>
<ul>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Trigun" title="Trigun">Trigun</a></i> series, the planet orbits a binary star system.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars"><i>Star Wars</i> series</a>, planet <a href="/wiki/Tatooine" title="Tatooine">Tatooine</a> orbits in a close binary system.</li>
<li>In the series <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a></i>, planet <a href="/wiki/Gallifrey" title="Gallifrey">Gallifrey</a> once orbited a binary star system.</li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Fox_series" title="Star Fox series"><i>Star Fox</i> series</a>, the planets orbit Lylat and Solar (an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_stellar_classes#Class_M" title="List of stellar classes">M-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a>)</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy"><i>Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy</i> series</a>, the circumbinary planet Magrathea is described as the "impossible planet".</li>
</ul>
