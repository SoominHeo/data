<p><b>Su Rong</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">苏荣</span>; born October 1948) is a former senior regional official and politician in China. He began his career in his native <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a>, and successively served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Party_chief_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Party chief of the Communist Party of China">Communist Party Secretary</a> of <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a> provinces. In March 2013, he became one of the vice-chairmen of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_People%27s_Political_Consultative_Conference" title="Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference">Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference</a> (CPPCC).</p>
<p>In June 2014, he was probed by the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party</a>'s anti-graft watchdog for "disciplinary violations", which typically indicate corruption. He is one of the highest-ranking officials to come under investigation for graft since <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a> became <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary of the Communist Party</a> in 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career_in_Jilin">Life and career in Jilin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Su_Rong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career in Jilin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Su Rong was born 1948 in <a href="/wiki/Taonan" title="Taonan">Taonan</a> (formerly Tao'an County), <a href="/wiki/Baicheng" title="Baicheng">Baicheng</a> prefecture, in <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> province. In 1968 he began working as an accountant in Najin Commune of Tao'an. He joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> in January 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, Su became the deputy Communist Party chief of Najin Commune, and later party chief. Starting in 1980 he served as deputy party chief of Tao'an County, party chief of <a href="/wiki/Fuyu,_Jilin" title="Fuyu, Jilin">Fuyu County</a>, deputy party chief and then party chief of Baicheng prefecture. In 1989 he became the party chief of <a href="/wiki/Siping,_Jilin" title="Siping, Jilin">Siping</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefecture-level_city" title="Prefecture-level city">prefecture-level city</a>, and from 1995 until 1998 he was the party chief of <a href="/wiki/Yanbian_Korean_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture">Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture</a>. Starting in 1996 he concurrently served as deputy party chief of Jilin province, a position he held until 2001. From 1994 to 1997 he studied at <a href="/wiki/Jilin_University" title="Jilin University">Jilin University</a> on a part-time basis, receiving a master's degree in economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_in_Qinghai.2C_Gansu.2C_and_Jiangxi">Career in Qinghai, Gansu, and Jiangxi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Su_Rong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career in Qinghai, Gansu, and Jiangxi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001, Su Rong was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a> province in <a href="/wiki/Northwest_China" title="Northwest China">Northwest China</a>, serving as its Communist Party Chief, the top official of the province. He also became the chairman of Qinghai Provincial People's Congress in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, he became the Communist Party Chief of the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> province, and concurrently served as chairman of Gansu Provincial People's Congress in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2006 to 2007, Su was the vice president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Party_School" title="Central Party School">Central Party School</a> in Beijing, which is a minister-level position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, he became the Communist Party Chief of <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a> province in <a href="/wiki/East_China" title="East China">East China</a>, succeeding <a href="/wiki/Meng_Jianzhu" title="Meng Jianzhu">Meng Jianzhu</a>. The next year he also became chairman of Jiangxi Provincial People's Congress. He held both positions until 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhua_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-xinhua-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was an alternate member of the <a href="/wiki/14th_Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="14th Central Committee of the Communist Party of China">14th</a> and the <a href="/wiki/15th_Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="15th Central Committee of the Communist Party of China">15th</a> <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of China">Central Committee of the Communist Party of China</a>, and a full member of the <a href="/wiki/16th_Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="16th Central Committee of the Communist Party of China">16th</a> and the <a href="/wiki/17th_Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="17th Central Committee of the Communist Party of China">17th</a> Central Committees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinavitae_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-chinavitae-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CPPCC_and_downfall">CPPCC and downfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Su_Rong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CPPCC and downfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2013, Su became one of the 23 vice-chairmen of the 12th <a href="/wiki/Chinese_People%27s_Political_Consultative_Conference" title="Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference">Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference</a> (CPPCC). The position enjoyed the official ranking as a "national leader".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp-4">[4]</a></sup> However, in June 2014, the Communist Party's anti-graft watchdog announced that he was being probed for "disciplinary violations", which typically indicate corruption. At the time of the announcement, he was the highest-ranking official, and only "national leader"-class figure, to come under investigation for graft since <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a> became <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary of the Communist Party</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp-4">[4]</a></sup> Several other senior officials of Jiangxi province had already been under investigation, including vice governors <a href="/wiki/Yao_Mugen" title="Yao Mugen">Yao Mugen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Zhiyong" title="Zhao Zhiyong">Zhao Zhiyong</a>, and vice-chairman of the provincial congress, <a href="/wiki/Chen_Anzhong" title="Chen Anzhong">Chen Anzhong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-2">[2]</a></sup> His CPPCC colleague and former Hu Jintao aide, CPPCC Vice-Chairman <a href="/wiki/Ling_Jihua" title="Ling Jihua">Ling Jihua</a>, was also detained for corruption in December 2014.</p>
<p>The results of the CCDI investigation into Su Rong was announced February 16, 2015. It concluded that Su Rong "violated organizational discipline, unilaterally upstaged decisions made by consensus [...] used his position of power to seek gain for others during the promotion process of officials and the operations of businesses, took a massive amount of bribes." It also said that he was responsible for wasting government resources and had "leading responsibility" for problems with corruption in Jiangxi province which festered under his watch. In the past, the CCDI's investigation announcements into officials have largely followed a bland and oft-repeated formula. In Su's case, however, the CCDI used many new phrases to describe the details of his alleged wrongdoing. He was said to have "shown blatant disregard about party political rules," "liberally sold offices for cash," "severely poisoned the local political environment," "encouraged and condoned his relatives to use their relationship to him to influence political affairs." His offenses were "of an especially egregious nature and caused extraordinarily bad influence." Su was expelled from the Communist Party of China, and indicted on charges of bribery and abuse of power, and his case moved to judicial authorities for prosecution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 23, 2017, Su Rong was sentenced to life in prison, who took bribes cost 116 million yuan (S$19 million) between 2002 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
