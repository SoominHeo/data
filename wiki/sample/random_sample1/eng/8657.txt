<p><b>Tsushima Province</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">対馬国</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tsushima-no kuni</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_provinces_of_Japan" title="Old provinces of Japan">old province of Japan</a> on <a href="/wiki/Tsushima_Island" title="Tsushima Island">Tsushima Island</a> which occupied the area corresponding to modern-day <a href="/wiki/Tsushima,_Nagasaki" title="Tsushima, Nagasaki">Tsushima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nagasaki_Prefecture" title="Nagasaki Prefecture">Nagasaki</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was sometimes called <i>Taishū</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">対州</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> .</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_history">Political history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tsushima_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of Tsushima Province is unclear. It is possible that Tsushima was recognized as a province of the Yamato Court in the 5th century. Under the <a href="/wiki/Ritsury%C5%8D" title="Ritsuryō">Ritsuryō</a> system, Tsushima formally became a province.</p>
<p>Tsushima Province has been a strategic area that took a major role in the national defense against possible invasions from the continent and in trade with Korea. After Japan was defeated by <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Baekgang" title="Battle of Baekgang">Battle of Baekgang</a> in 663, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kaneda_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kaneda Castle (page does not exist)">Kaneda Castle</a> was constructed on this island.</p>
<p>Tsushima Province had been controlled by the Tsushima no <a href="/wiki/Kuni_no_miyatsuko" title="Kuni no miyatsuko">Kuni no miyatsuko</a> until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">Heian Period</a>. This clan was later replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Abiru_clan" title="Abiru clan">Ahiru clan</a>. The <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8D_clan" title="Sō clan">Sō clan</a> rose to power around the middle 13th century and seized control of the entire island in the late 15th century. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edo_Period" title="Edo Period">Edo Period</a>, Tsushima Province was dominated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsushima_Fuchu_domain" title="Tsushima Fuchu domain">Tsushima Fuchu domain</a> (Izuhara domain) of the So clan. It was put in charge of diplomacy and monopolized trade with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> of Korea.</p>
<p>As a result of the <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_the_han_system" title="Abolition of the han system">abolition of the han system</a>, the Tsushima Fuchu domain became Izuhara Prefecture in 1871. In the same year, Izuhara Prefecture was merged into Imari Prefecture, which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Saga_Prefecture" title="Saga Prefecture">Saga Prefecture</a> in 1872. Tsushima was transferred to Nagasaki Prefecture in 1872. At the same time, the province continued to exist for some purposes. For example, Tsushima is explicitly recognized in treaties in 1894 (a) between Japan and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and (b) between Japan and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_districts">Historical districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tsushima_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout history, Tsushima Province consisted of two districts:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nagasaki_Prefecture" title="Nagasaki Prefecture">Nagasaki Prefecture</a>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamiagata_District,_Nagasaki" title="Kamiagata District, Nagasaki">Kamiagata District</a> (上県郡)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shimoagata_District,_Nagasaki" title="Shimoagata District, Nagasaki">Shimoagata District</a> (下県郡)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The capital of Tsushima Province was located at Izuhara. In the modern local municipality system, they were divided into Kamiagata and Shimoagata Districts respectively, and were subsequently merged into the city of <a href="/wiki/Tsushima,_Nagasaki" title="Tsushima, Nagasaki">Tsushima</a> today.</p>
