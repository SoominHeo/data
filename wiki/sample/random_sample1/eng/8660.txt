<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sichuan Basin</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Sichuan Basin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">四川盆地</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Sìchuān Péndì</span></i>), or <b>Szechwan Basin</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">lowland region</a> in southwestern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It comprises the central and eastern portions of <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan province</a>, and the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Chongqing" title="Chongqing">Chongqing Municipality</a> (which itself was part of Sichuan until 1997). Due to its relative flatness and fertile grounds, it is heavily populated with a population of more than 100 million. In addition to being a dominant geographical feature of the region, the Sichuan basin also constitutes a cultural sphere that is distinguished by its own unique customs, cuisine, and dialects. It is also called the "Red Basin." It is famous for rice cultivation. It is also the principal gas-producing region of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_ranges">Mountain ranges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sichuan_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mountain ranges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mountains frame the Sichuan Basin on all sides:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Qionglai_Mountains" title="Qionglai Mountains">Qionglai Range</a> in the west</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Longmen_Mountains" title="Longmen Mountains">Longmen Mountains</a> in the northwest</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Daba_Mountains" title="Daba Mountains">Daba</a> in the northeast.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> River, flowing from the Sichuan Basin into the eastern China, passes through the <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges" title="Three Gorges">Three Gorges</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wu_Mountains" title="Wu Mountains">Wu Mountains</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hills.2C_plains_and_rivers">Hills, plains and rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sichuan_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hills, plains and rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sichuan Basin consists of low hills and alluvial plains, and several major rivers flow into the Chang Jiang (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze River</a>), which passes through the southern part of the basin. The seismically active <a href="/wiki/Longmenshan_Fault" title="Longmenshan Fault">Longmenshan Fault</a> - the source of the catastrophic <a href="/wiki/2008_Sichuan_earthquake" title="2008 Sichuan earthquake">2008 Sichuan earthquake</a> - runs along the western boundary of the basin, separating it from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetan_plateau" title="Tibetan plateau">Tibetan plateau</a> to the west.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sichuan_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A moist, often overcast, four-season climate dominates here, with cool to mild winters, with occasional frost, and hot, very humid summers; the intensity of summer varies rather widely, depending on location. See the articles on <a href="/wiki/Chongqing#Climate" title="Chongqing">Chongqing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chengdu#Climate" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a> for further details.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flatland">Flatland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sichuan_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flatland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg/220px-Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg/330px-Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg/440px-Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zitong-sichuan-china.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Zitong_County" title="Zitong County">Zitong County</a>, in the northwest of the Sichuan Basin</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These days the Sichuan Basin flatlands are mostly covered by farmers' fields and cities, while the original <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sichuan_Basin_evergreen_broadleaf_forests&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sichuan Basin evergreen broadleaf forests (page does not exist)">Sichuan Basin evergreen broadleaf forests</a> landscapes have been reduced to small patches on occasional steep hill slopes, on a few sacred mountains such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Emei" title="Mount Emei">Mount Emei</a>, or around temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A greater variety of natural landscapes and wildlife has been at least partially preserved in the mountains surrounding the basin. The natural ecosystems of these mountains have been classified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Qionglai-Minshan_conifer_forests&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Qionglai-Minshan conifer forests (page does not exist)">Qionglai-Minshan conifer forests</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daba_Mountains_evergreen_forests&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daba Mountains evergreen forests (page does not exist)">Daba Mountains evergreen forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwf_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wwf-4">[4]</a></sup> It was in the hilly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lichuan_City" title="Lichuan City">Lichuan County</a>, on the eastern mountain fringe of the basin, where the <a href="/wiki/Metasequoia" title="Metasequoia">Dawn Redwood</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Metasequoia_glyptostroboides" title="Metasequoia glyptostroboides">Metasequoia glyptostroboides</a></i>, previously only known in fossils and thought to be extinct) was discovered in 1944. The Dawn Redwood is distinctive because it is a deciduous conifer.</p>
