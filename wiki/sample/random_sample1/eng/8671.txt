<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Agustín Barrios</h1>

<p><b>Agustín Pío Barrios</b> (also known as <b>Agustín Barrios Mangoré</b> and <b>Nitsuga</b> --Agustin spelled backwards-- <b>Mangoré</b>; May 5, 1885 – August 7, 1944) was a <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguayan</a> <a href="/wiki/Virtuoso" title="Virtuoso">virtuoso</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_guitarist" title="Classical guitarist">classical guitarist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a>, largely regarded as one of the greatest performers and most prolific composers for the guitar. His music remained undiscovered for over three decades after his death.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birthplace">Birthplace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birthplace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been generally accepted that Barrios was born in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_San_Juan_Bautista_de_las_Misiones" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of San Juan Bautista de las Misiones">San Juan Bautista de las Misiones, Paraguay</a>. However, there is no definitive proof of this as his baptismal document found in the book of registries in the cathedral in San Juan Bautista does not state his precise place of birth. Also, several biographers and authorities present convincing documented evidence that Barrios was born, instead, in the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Villa_Florida" title="Villa Florida">Villa Florida</a>, Misiones, situated on the Tebicuary River some 30 km north of San Juan Bautista.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When he was a child, Barrios began to develop a love for <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, two areas that were very important to his family. Barrios would eventually speak two languages (Spanish and <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">Guarani</a>), and read three more (English, French and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Language" title="German Language">German</a>).</p>
<p>Barrios began to show an interest in musical instruments, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>, before he reached his teens. He went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n,_Paraguay" title="Asunción, Paraguay">Asunción</a> in 1901, at the age of fifteen, to attend a <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> (<a href="/wiki/Universidad_Nacional_de_Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Universidad Nacional de Asunción">Universidad Nacional de Asunción</a>) with a scholarship in <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, thus becoming one of the youngest university students in Paraguayan history. Apart from his studies in the college's music department, Barrios was also well appreciated by members of the college's <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a> and literature departments.</p>
<p>After leaving college, Barrios dedicated his life to music and writing poems. He composed more than 300 songs for which he would first write the lyrics and then the guitar accompaniment<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Barrios made several friends during his multiple trips across <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. He was known for giving his friends and fans <a href="/wiki/Autograph" title="Autograph">signed</a> copies of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">poems</a>. Because of that, there are several different versions of his poetical works which have surfaced across South America, other areas of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Many current collectors warn potential buyers to be careful when they come across a poem reportedly autographed by Barrios.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barrios was famed for his phenomenal performances, both live and on gramophone recordings. Barrios has been credited as the first classical guitarist to make recordings, in 1909/10, but the earliest known recording were by guitarists <a href="/wiki/Early_classical_guitar_recordings" title="Early classical guitar recordings">Luis and Simon Ramirez</a>, onto <a href="/wiki/Phonograph_cylinder" title="Phonograph cylinder">cylinders</a>, for the "Viuda de Aramburo" label, in Madrid, between 1897 and 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Barrios at times performed in concert in traditional Paraguayan dress (he was partly of <a href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_people" title="Guaraní people">Guaraní</a> origin), using the pseudonym of Nitsuga Mangoré ('Nitsuga' being Agustín spelled backwards, and 'Mangoré' being the name of a <a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">cacique</a> of the South American indigenous group Timbú).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>His works were largely late-<a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic</a> in character, despite his having lived well into the twentieth century. Many of them are also adaptations of, or are influenced by, <a href="/wiki/List_of_South_American_folk_music_traditions" title="List of South American folk music traditions">South American</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Central_American_folk_music_traditions" title="List of Central American folk music traditions">Central American folk music</a>. Very many of them are of a virtuosic nature.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios_1922.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Barrios in 1922</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>-inspired <i>La Catedral</i>, from 1921, is widely considered to be Barrios' <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a>, even winning the approval of <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Segovia" title="Andrés Segovia">Andrés Segovia</a>, who said "In 1921 in Buenos Aires, I played at the hall La Argentina noted for its good acoustics for guitar, where Barrios had concertized just weeks before me. He was presented to me by his secretary Elbio Trapani. At my invitation Barrios visited me at the hotel and played for me upon my very own guitar several of his compositions among which the one that really impressed me was a magnificent concert piece <i>The Cathedral</i> whose first movement is an andante, like an introduction and prelude, and a second very virtuosic piece which is ideal for the repertory of any concert guitarist. Barrios had promised to send me immediately a copy of the work (I had ten days remaining before continuing my journey) but I never received a copy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, it equally possible that Segovia did receive the score and chose not to play it, either out of distaste for Barrios' folk-based music or professional jealousy (because Barrios was more of a composer than he was).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barrios returned touring <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in <a href="/wiki/1936" title="1936">1936</a> and performed in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>. He would not fulfill his dream to reach the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, as his partner Gloria's <a href="/wiki/Travel_visa" title="Travel visa">travel visa</a> was denied.</p>
<p>While in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, he suffered a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> and was strongly advised to limit touring to reduce stress. He accepted the invitation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximiliano_Hernandez_Martinez" title="Maximiliano Hernandez Martinez">Maximiliano Hernandez Martinez</a>, then <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">President</a> of <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, to move to <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>.</p>
<p>From 1939 onwards Barrios lived in <a href="/wiki/San_Salvador" title="San Salvador">San Salvador</a>; after recovering, he accepted Hernandez Martinez appointment as Head of Classical Guitar at the National Conservatory.</p>
<p>He suffered a second <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> on August 7, 1944, causing his death. Barrios was buried at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cementerio_de_Los_Ilustres&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cementerio de Los Ilustres (page does not exist)">Cementerio de Los Ilustres</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Twelve_.22Mangoreans.22">The Twelve "Mangoreans"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: The Twelve "Mangoreans"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barrios refused to teach during his career as active performer, only providing guitar instruction after arriving in <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> to twelve selected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maestros" title="Maestros">maestros</a>, all of Salvadoran nationality.</p>
<p>They were known as the Twelve Mangoreans:</p>
<p>Luis Mario Samayoa ( -1969), Benjamín Cisneros ( -1987), Rubén Urquilla ( -1993), René Cortés-Andrino ( -1995), Mario Cardona Lazo ( -1999), Jesús Quiroa ( -2001), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jose_C%C3%A1ndido_Morales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jose Cándido Morales (page does not exist)">Jose Cándido Morales</a> ( -2002), Julio Cortés-Andrino ( -2006), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cecilio_Orellana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cecilio Orellana (page does not exist)">Cecilio Orellana</a> ( -2007), Roberto Bracamontes ( -2007), Víctor Urrutia ( -2010) and Elena Valdivieso.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jose_C%C3%A1ndido_Morales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jose Cándido Morales (page does not exist)">Jose Cándido Morales</a> was the only to learn from Barrios as live-in student. After Barrios' death, Morales remained the gatekeeper of Barrios' legacy, technique and late works.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_music">Folk music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Folk music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> of Paraguay (including the <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_polka" title="Paraguayan polka">polca paraguaya</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vals_(Peruvian)" title="Vals (Peruvian)">vals</a>) provided the young Barrios with his first introduction to music. In 1898, Barrios was formally introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Classical_guitar" title="Classical guitar">classical guitar</a> repertoire by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavo_Sosa_Escalada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gustavo Sosa Escalada (page does not exist)">Gustavo Sosa Escalada</a>. At that time, Barrios had already composed works for the guitar, and also performed pieces written by his former teacher Alias, such as <i>La Chinita</i> and <i>La Perezosa</i>. Under the influence of his new teacher, Barrios went on to perform and study the works of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_T%C3%A1rrega" title="Francisco Tárrega">Tárrega</a>, Vinas, Sor and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dionisio_Aguado" title="Dionisio Aguado">Aguado</a>. Sosa Escalada was so impressed with his new pupil that he convinced Barrios's parents to let him move to <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">Asunción</a> to continue his education. Having already surpassed the technical and performing abilities of most guitarists, Barrios began seriously to compose around 1905.</p>
<p>Among the folkloric influences, Barrios is known to have played such popular Paraguayan works as "Campamento Cerro León", "Londón Carapé", <i>"Guyrá campana"</i>, "Mamá Cumandá".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As an example, "Guyrá campana" is very interesting, since some of the material can be heard in parts of Barrios' recording of "Caazapá — Aire Popular Paraguayo"... Though "Guyrá campana" is traditional music, many maintain that it is very closely related to guitarist <a class="external text" href="http://www.luisszaran.org/DiccionarioDetalle.php?lang=en&amp;DiccID=684" rel="nofollow">Carlos Talavera</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.ciudadhechizo.com.py/personajes.html#ct" rel="nofollow">1</a> (from Caazapá), whom Barrios knew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-felix_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-felix-9">[9]</a></sup> There are various versions of "Guyrá campana" (it is also known as "Pájaro campana") e.g. for <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_harp" title="Paraguayan harp">Paraguayan harp</a> (Félix Pérez Cardozo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-felix_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-felix-9">[9]</a></sup>), etc.; and in some versions, the birdsong imitations can be very clearly heard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composer">Composer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Composer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barrios's compositions can be divided into three basic categories: folkloric, imitative and religious. Barrios paid tribute to the music and people of his native land by composing pieces modeled after folk songs from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. Imitating the compositional style and techniques of the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> and Romantic periods was another side to his craftsmanship. "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_Catedral_(suite)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La Catedral (suite) (page does not exist)">La Catedral</a>" may be viewed as Barrios' tribute to Bach. It is believed that "La Catedral" was inspired by a religious experience for Barrios; therefore, the piece may also be categorized under religion. Religious beliefs and experiences also played an important role in Barrios' compositional process. <i>Una Limosna por el Amor de Dios</i> (<i>Alms for the Love of God</i>) is another example of a religiously-inspired work. Breaking down the works of Barrios into these three categories helps the guitar enthusiast to understand Barrios's musical intent.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over 300 of Barrios' compositions survive. He is still revered in <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> to this day, where he is seen as one of the greatest musicians of all time by many. <a href="/wiki/John_Williams_(guitarist)" title="John Williams (guitarist)">John Williams</a>, former student of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andres_Segovia" title="Andres Segovia">Andres Segovia</a>, said of Barrios: <i>"As a guitarist/composer, Barrios is the best of the lot, regardless of era. His music is better formed, it's more poetic, it's more everything! And it's more of all those things in a timeless way."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agust%C3%ADn_Barrios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While in Paraguay, Barrios had access only to instruments of limited quality. However, soon after his arrival in Buenos Aires in 1910, he was exposed to, and played, the finest instruments of his time for the remainder of his career. Normally Barrios traveled with <a class="external text" href="http://www.mangore.com/mangore_guitar.html" rel="nofollow">2 guitars</a>, and had several modified with the addition of a 20th fret. He is documented by photograph to have played the instruments of Spanish makers José Ramírez I, Enrique García, Francisco Simplicio, Domingo Esteso, Enrique Sanfeliu and Ricardo Sanchis Nacher, Brazilian maker Di Giorgio, and in print by Uruguayan maker Rodolfo Camacho.</p>
