<p><i><b>Aguirre, the Wrath of God</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Aguirre, der Zorn Gottes</i></span>), known in the UK as <i><b>Aguirre, Wrath of God</b></i>, is a 1972 <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West German</a> <a href="/wiki/Epic_film" title="Epic film">epic film</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Werner_Herzog" title="Werner Herzog">Werner Herzog</a>. <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Kinski" title="Klaus Kinski">Klaus Kinski</a> stars in the title role. The <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a> was composed and performed by West German <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive</a>/<a href="/wiki/Krautrock" title="Krautrock">Krautrock</a> band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh_(German_band)" title="Popol Vuh (German band)">Popol Vuh</a>. The story follows the travels of Spanish soldier <a href="/wiki/Lope_de_Aguirre" title="Lope de Aguirre">Lope de Aguirre</a>, who leads a group of <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadores</a> down the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> in South America in search of the legendary city of gold, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Dorado_(legend)" title="El Dorado (legend)">El Dorado</a>. Using a <a href="/wiki/Minimalism" title="Minimalism">minimalist</a> story and dialogue, the film creates a vision of madness and folly, counterpointed by the lush but unforgiving Amazonian jungle. Although based loosely on what is known of the historical figure of Aguirre, the film's storyline is, as Herzog acknowledged years after the film's release, a work of imagination. Some of the people and situations may have been inspired by <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_de_Carvajal" title="Gaspar de Carvajal">Gaspar de Carvajal</a>'s account of an earlier Amazonian expedition, although Carvajal was not on the historical voyage represented in the film. Other accounts state that the expedition went into the jungles but never returned to civilization.</p>
<p><i>Aguirre</i> was the first of five collaborations between Herzog and the volatile Kinski. The director and the actor had differing views as to how the role should be played, and they clashed throughout filming; Kinski's tantrums terrorized both the crew and the local natives who were assisting the production. Shooting was entirely on location, and was fraught with difficulties. Filming took place in the <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peruvian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> on the Amazon River during an arduous five-week period, shooting on <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributaries</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ucayali_region" title="Ucayali region">Ucayali region</a>. The cast and crew climbed mountains, cut through heavy vines to open routes to the various jungle locations, and rode treacherous river rapids on rafts built by natives.</p>
<p><i>Aguirre</i> opened to widespread critical acclaim, and quickly developed a large international <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">cult film</a> following. It was given an extensive <a href="/wiki/Art_film" title="Art film">arthouse</a> theatrical release in the United States in 1977, and remains one of the <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">director's</a> best-known films. Several critics have declared the film a masterpiece, and it has appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's list of "All Time 100 Best Films". <i>Aguirre</i>’s visual style and narrative elements had a strong influence on <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a>'s 1979 film <i><a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Now" title="Apocalypse Now">Apocalypse Now</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1560, several score of Spanish conquistadors, and a hundred <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indian</a> slaves, march down from the newly conquered <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> mountains into the jungles to the east, in search of the fabled country of El Dorado. Under the command of <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Pizarro" title="Gonzalo Pizarro">Gonzalo Pizarro</a> (Alejandro Repullés), the men, clad in half <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_armor" title="Personal armor">armor</a>, pull cannons down narrow mountainous paths and through dense, muddy jungle. On <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">New Year's Eve</a>, reaching the end of his supplies and unable to go on without more information, Pizarro orders a group of forty men to scout ahead by raft down river. If they do not return to the main party within one week with news of what lies beyond, they will be considered lost. Pizarro chooses Don <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Urs%C3%BAa" title="Pedro de Ursúa">Pedro de Ursúa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ruy_Guerra" title="Ruy Guerra">Ruy Guerra</a>) as the commander of the expedition, Don Lope de Aguirre (Klaus Kinski) as his <a href="/wiki/Second-in-command" title="Second-in-command">second-in-command</a>, fat nobleman Don Fernando de Guzmán (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Berling" title="Peter Berling">Peter Berling</a>) representing <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Royal_Family" title="Spanish Royal Family">The Royal House of Spain</a> and Brother Gaspar de Carvajal (<a href="/wiki/Del_Negro_(actor)" title="Del Negro (actor)">Del Negro</a>) to bring the word of God. Also accompanying the expedition, against Pizarro's better judgment, are Ursúa's mistress, Doña Inés (<a href="/wiki/Helena_Rojo" title="Helena Rojo">Helena Rojo</a>) and Aguirre's teenage daughter, Florés (Cecilia Rivera, in her only film role).</p>
<p>Traveling through rapids, one of the four rafts gets caught in an <a href="/wiki/Whitewater#Eddies" title="Whitewater">eddy</a>, and the others are unable to help free it. That night, gunfire erupts on the trapped raft; in the morning the men on board are found dead, with two missing. Ursúa wants the bodies to be brought back to camp for proper burial. Knowing this would slow down the expedition, Aguirre hints to Perucho (Daniel Ades) to "keep the rust off of the cannon". Perucho proceeds to fire the cannon at the raft, destroying it and throwing the bodies into the river.</p>
<p>During the night, the remaining rafts are swept away by the rising river. Time has run out for the scouting mission, and Ursúa decides to return to Pizarro's group. Aguirre leads a <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutiny</a> against Ursúa, telling the men that untold riches await them ahead, and reminding them that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hernan_Cortez" title="Hernan Cortez">Hernan Cortez</a> won an empire in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> by disobeying orders. Ursúa attempts to put Aguirre in chains, but he and a soldier loyal to him are shot. Inés cares for Ursúa. Aguirre coerces the soldiers to elect the fat, lazy Don de Guzmán as the new leader of the expedition. Aguirre proclaims de Guzman Emperor of the new country, and declares <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> dethroned. A farcical trial of Ursúa results in his being sentenced to death, but de Guzmán surprises Aguirre by granting Ursúa clemency.</p>
<p>Aguirre proves to be an oppressive leader, so terrifying that few protest his leadership. Only Inés has the courage to speak out against him. Knowing that some of the soldiers are still loyal to Ursúa, Aguirre simply ignores her.</p>
<p>The expedition continues on a single, newly built, large raft. An Indian couple approaching peacefully by canoe is captured by the explorers, and when the man expresses confusion when presented with a Bible, Brother Carvajal kills them for <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy" title="Blasphemy">blasphemy</a>. de Guzmán dines on the low food supplies while the men starve, and has the expedition's only remaining horse pushed off the raft because it annoys him. Soon afterwards he is found strangled near the raft's <a href="/wiki/Outhouse" title="Outhouse">outhouse</a>. After de Guzmán's death, Aguirre proclaims himself leader. Ursúa is then taken ashore and hanged in the jungle. The group attacks an Indian village, where several soldiers are killed by spears and arrows. The distraught Inés walks into the jungle and disappears.</p>
<p>On the raft again, the group of slowly starving, feverish men begin disbelieving everything they see, even when shot with arrows. The group stares in disbelief at a <a href="/wiki/Sailing_ship" title="Sailing ship">wooden ship</a> perched in the highest branches of a tall tree, which Aguirre orders be brought down and refurbished, but Brother Carvajal refuses. In a series of final attacks by unseen assailants, the remaining survivors including Aguirre’s daughter are killed by arrows. Aguirre alone remains alive on the slowly drifting raft. The raft becomes overrun by monkeys. The crazed Aguirre tells them: "I, the Wrath of God, will marry my own daughter and with her I will found the purest dynasty the world has ever seen. Together, we shall rule this entire continent. We shall endure. I am the Wrath of God! Who else is with me?"</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea for the film began when Herzog borrowed a book on historical adventurers from a friend. After reading a half-page devoted to Lope de Aguirre, the filmmaker became inspired and immediately devised the story. He fabricated most of the plot details and characters, although he did use some historical figures in purely fictitious ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Screenplay">Screenplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Screenplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Herzog wrote the screenplay "in a frenzy", and completed it in two-and-a-half days. Much of the script was written during a 200-mile (320 km) bus trip with Herzog's football team. His teammates got drunk after winning a game and one of them vomited on several pages of Herzog's manuscript, which he immediately tossed out of the window. Herzog claims he can't remember all of the things that he wrote on these pages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The screenplay was shot as written, with some minor differences. In an early scene in which Pizarro instructs Ursúa to lead the scouting team down the river, in the script Pizarro mentions that in the course of the expedition Ursúa could possibly discover what happened to <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Orellana" title="Francisco de Orellana">Francisco de Orellana</a>'s expedition, which had vanished without a trace years before (see "Historical Accuracy" section). Later in the screenplay, Aguirre and his men find a boat and the long-dead remains of Orellana's soldiers. Further down the river, they discover another ship lodged in some tree tops. In the screenplay, Aguirre and others explore the boat but find no sign of Orellana or his men. Herzog ultimately eliminated any such references to Orellana's expedition from the film. The sequence with the boat caught in the upper branches of a tree remains, but as filmed it seems to be simply a hallucinatory vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fritze_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fritze-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The finale is significantly different from Herzog's original script. The director recalled, "I only remember that the end of the film was totally different. The end was actually the raft going out into the open ocean and being swept back inland, because for many miles you have a counter-current, the Amazon actually goes backwards. And it was tossed to and fro. And a parrot would scream: "El Dorado, El Dorado"..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Herzog_and_Kinski">Herzog and Kinski</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Herzog and Kinski">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Herzog's first choice for the role of Aguirre was actor Klaus Kinski. The two had met many years earlier when the then-struggling young actor rented a room in Herzog’s family apartment, and Kinski’s often terrifying and deranged antics during the three months he lived there left a lasting impression on the director. Years later, Herzog remembered the volatile actor and knew that he was the only possible man who could play the mad Aguirre, and he sent Kinski a copy of the screenplay. "Between three and four in the morning, the phone rang," Herzog recalled. "It took me at least a couple of minutes before I realized that it was Kinski who was the source of this inarticulate screaming. And after an hour of this, it dawned on me that he found it the most fascinating screenplay and wanted to be Aguirre."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the beginning of the production, Herzog and Kinski argued about the proper manner to portray Aguirre. Kinski wanted to play a "wild, ranting madman", but Herzog wanted something "quieter, more menacing". In order to get the performance he desired, before each shot Herzog would deliberately infuriate Kinski. After waiting for the hot-tempered actor's inevitable tantrum to "burn itself out", Herzog would then roll the camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knipfel_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knipfel-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On one occasion, irritated by the noise from a hut where cast and crew were playing cards, the explosive Kinski fired three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunshots" title="Gunshots">gunshots</a> at it, blowing the tip off one extra's finger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog-4">[4]</a></sup> Subsequently, Kinski started leaving the jungle location (over Herzog's refusal to fire a sound assistant), only changing his mind after Herzog threatened to shoot first Kinski and then himself. The latter incident has given rise to the legend that Herzog made Kinski act for him at gunpoint. However Herzog has repeatedly denied the claim during interviews, explaining he only verbally threatened Kinski in the heat of the moment, in a desperate attempt to keep him from leaving the set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The famous incident is parodied in <i><a href="/wiki/Incident_at_Loch_Ness" title="Incident at Loch Ness">Incident at Loch Ness</a></i>, which Herzog co-wrote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was made for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>370,000, with one-third of the budget paying for Kinski's salary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog1-11">[11]</a></sup> It was filmed on location in the <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peruvian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machu_Picchu" title="Machu Picchu">Machu Picchu</a> (the stone steps of <a href="/wiki/Huayna_Picchu" title="Huayna Picchu">Huayna Picchu</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog1-11">[11]</a></sup> and on the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributaries</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ucayali_region" title="Ucayali region">Ucayali region</a>. <i>Aguirre</i> was shot in five weeks, following nine months' worth of pre-production planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog-4">[4]</a></sup> The film was shot in chronological order, because Herzog believed the film crew's progress on the river directly mirrored that of the explorers' journey in the story. The director and his cast and crew floated in rafts down the <a href="/wiki/Huallaga_River" title="Huallaga River">Huallaga</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanay" title="Nanay">Nanay</a> rivers through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urubamba_Valley" title="Urubamba Valley">Urubamba Valley</a> in Peru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog1_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>All of the actors spoke their dialogue in English. The members of the cast and crew came from sixteen different countries, and English was the only common language among them. In addition, Herzog felt that shooting <i>Aguirre</i> in English would improve the film's chances for international distribution. However, the small amount of money that had been set aside for post-synchronization "left Peru with the man in charge of the process; both absconded <i>en route</i>." The English-language track was ultimately replaced by a higher-quality <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German language</a> version, which was <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">post-synched</a> after production was completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overbey_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Overbey-1">[1]</a></sup> Herzog claims that Kinski requested too much money for the dubbing session, and so his lines were performed by another actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_budget" title="Low budget">low budget</a> precluded the use of stunt men or elaborate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">special effects</a>. The cast and crew climbed up mountains, hacked through thick jungle, and rode ferocious Amazonian river rapids on rafts built by natives. At one point, a storm caused a river to flood, covering the film sets in several feet of water and destroying all the rafts built for the film. This flooding was immediately incorporated into the story, as a sequence including a flood and subsequent rebuilding of rafts was shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The camera used to shoot the film was stolen by Herzog from the Munich Film School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Years later, Herzog recalled:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"It was a very simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/35mm" title="35mm">35mm</a> camera, one I used on many other films, so I do not consider it a theft. For me, it was truly a necessity. I wanted to make films and needed a camera. I had some sort of natural right to this tool. If you need air to breathe, and you are locked in a room, you have to take a chisel and hammer and break down a wall. It is your absolute right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog1_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog1-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>To obtain the monkeys used in the climactic sequence, Herzog paid several locals to trap 400 monkeys; he paid them half in advance and was to pay the other half upon receipt. The trappers sold the monkeys to someone in Los Angeles or Miami, and Herzog came to the airport just as the monkeys were being loaded to be shipped out of the country. He pretended to be a veterinarian and claimed that the monkeys needed vaccinations before leaving the country. Abashed, the handlers unloaded the monkeys, and Herzog loaded them into his jeep and drove away, used them in the shot they were required for, and released them afterwards into the jungle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aguirre_(soundtrack)" title="Aguirre (soundtrack)">Aguirre (soundtrack)</a></div>
<p><i>Aguirre'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">musical score</a> was performed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh_(German_band)" title="Popol Vuh (German band)">Popol Vuh</a>, a West German <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive</a>/<a href="/wiki/Krautrock" title="Krautrock">Krautrock</a> band. The band was formed in 1970 by keyboardist Florian Fricke, who had known Herzog for several years prior to the formation of the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He had appeared as an actor in the director’s first full-length film, <i><a href="/wiki/Signs_of_Life_(1968_film)" title="Signs of Life (1968 film)">Signs of Life</a></i> (1968), playing a pianist. <i>Aguirre</i> was only the first of many collaborations between the band and the director.</p>
<p>Popol Vuh’s “hypnotic music”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> for <i>Aguirre</i> met with considerable acclaim. Roger Ebert wrote, “The music sets the tone. It is haunting, ecclesiastical, human and yet something else... [T]he music is crucial to <i>Aguirre, the Wrath of God</i>..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> noted, “The film's central <a href="/wiki/Motif_(narrative)" title="Motif (narrative)">motif</a> blends pulsing <a href="/wiki/Moog_synthesizer" title="Moog synthesizer">Moog</a> and spectral voices conjured from Florian Fricke's Mellotron-related "choir organ" to achieve something sublime, in the truest sense of the word: it's hard not to find the music's awe-inspiring, overwhelming beauty simultaneously unsettling. The power of the legendary opening sequence of Herzog's film...owes as much to Popol Vuh's music as it does to the director's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mise_en_sc%C3%A8ne" title="Mise en scène">mise-en-scène</a>.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Herzog explained how the choir-like sound was created, "We used a strange instrument, which we called a '<a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">choir-organ</a>.' It has inside it three dozen different tapes running parallel to each other in loops. ... All these tapes are running at the same time, and there is a keyboard on which you can play them like an organ so that [it will] sound just like a human choir but yet, at the same time, very artificial and really quite eerie."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1975 Popol Vuh released an album entitled <a href="/wiki/Aguirre_(soundtrack)" title="Aguirre (soundtrack)"><i>Aguirre</i></a>. Although ostensibly a <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack_album" title="Soundtrack album">soundtrack album</a> to Herzog's film, the six-track <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album">LP</a> included only two songs ("Aguirre I (L'Acrime Di Rei)" and "Aguirre II") taken from <i>Aguirre, the Wrath of God</i>. The four remaining tracks were derived from various recordings made by the group between about 1972 and 1974. At the time of <i>Aguirre</i> the band members were Fricke (<a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">Mellotron</a>), Fichelscher (<a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel-string_acoustic_guitar" title="Steel-string acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drum_kit" title="Drum kit">drums</a>), Djong Yun (<a href="/wiki/Singing" title="Singing">vocals</a>), and Robert Eliscu (<a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pan_flute" title="Pan flute">pan pipe</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was produced in part by West German television station <i><a href="/wiki/Hessischer_Rundfunk" title="Hessischer Rundfunk">Hessischer Rundfunk</a></i>, which televised the film on the same day it opened in theatres. Herzog has blamed this for the relatively poor commercial reception of the film in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herzog1_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Herzog1-11">[11]</a></sup> However, outside Germany the film became an "enormous <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">cult</a> favorite" in "such places as Mexico, Venezuela, and Algiers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peary_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peary-18">[18]</a></sup> The film had a theatrical run of fifteen months in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>Aguirre</i> received a theatrical release in the United States in 1977 by <a href="/wiki/New_Yorker_Films" title="New Yorker Films">New Yorker Films</a>. It immediately became a cult film, and New Yorker Films reported four years after its initial release that it was the only film in its catalog that never went out of circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peary_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peary-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Germany, the <i><a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCddeutsche_Zeitung" title="Süddeutsche Zeitung">Süddeutsche Zeitung</a></i> described the film as "a colour-drenched, violently physical moving painting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgardt_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgardt-20">[20]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung">Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung</a></i> described Kinski's acting as "too theatrical" to embody God's wrath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgardt_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgardt-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the US and the UK the film received mostly positive critical notices upon release. <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Canby" title="Vincent Canby">Vincent Canby</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, called it "[A]bsolutely stunning ... Mr. Herzog views all the proceedings with fixed detachment. He remains cool. He takes no sides. He may even be slightly amused. Mainly he is a poet who constantly surprises us with unexpected juxtapositions ... This is a splendid and haunting work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Schickel" title="Richard Schickel">Richard Schickel</a> opined that "[Herzog] does the audience the honor of allowing it to discover the blindnesses and obsessions, the sober lunacies he quietly lays out on the screen. Well acted, most notably by Klaus Kinski in the title role, gloriously photographed by Thomas Mauch, <i>Aguirre</i> is, not to put too fine a point on it, a movie that makes a convincing claim to greatness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Out_(company)" title="Time Out (company)"><i>Time Out's</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Tony_Rayns" title="Tony Rayns">Tony Rayns</a> noted, "...each scene and each detail is honed down to its salient features. On this level, the film effectively pre-empts analysis by analysing itself as it proceeds, admitting no ambiguity. Yet at the same time, Herzog's flair for charged explosive imagery has never had freer rein, and the film is rich in <a href="/wiki/Oneiric_(film_theory)" title="Oneiric (film theory)">oneiric moments</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film's reputation through the years has continued to grow. <a href="/wiki/J._Hoberman" title="J. Hoberman">J. Hoberman</a> has written that <i>Aguirre</i> "is not just a great movie but an essential one ... Herzog's third feature ... is both a landmark film and a magnificent social metaphor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoberman_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoberman-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Danny_Peary" title="Danny Peary">Danny Peary</a> wrote, "To see <i>Aguirre</i> for the first time is to discover a genuine masterpiece. It is overwhelming, spellbinding; at first dreamlike, and then hallucinatory."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peary_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Peary-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> has added it to his list of <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Movies" title="The Great Movies">The Great Movies</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and in a 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> poll of critics and filmmakers on the best films ever made, Ebert listed it in his top ten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In the same poll, critic Nigel Andrews and director <a href="/wiki/Santosh_Sivan" title="Santosh Sivan">Santosh Sivan</a> also placed it in their top ten list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1999, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> included the film on the magazine's "100 Maverick Movies of the Last 100 Years" list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <i>Aguirre</i> was included in <i>Time Magazine'</i>s "All Time 100 Best Films", compiled by Richard Schickel and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Corliss" title="Richard Corliss">Richard Corliss</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a> named it the 46th greatest <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">cult film</a> ever made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The film was ranked #19 in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine's "The 100 Best Films Of World Cinema" in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Aguirre</i> has won several prestigious film awards. In 1973 it won the <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Filmpreis" title="Deutscher Filmpreis">Deutscher Filmpreis</a> (German Film Award) for "Outstanding Individual Achievement: Cinematography".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1976 it was voted the "Best Foreign Film" by the French Syndicate of Film Critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1977 the <a href="/wiki/National_Society_of_Film_Critics" title="National Society of Film Critics">National Society of Film Critics</a> US gave it their "Best Cinematography" Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> It won the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(Belgian_Film_Critics_Association)" title="Grand Prix (Belgian Film Critics Association)">Grand Prix</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Film_Critics_Association" title="Belgian Film Critics Association">Belgian Film Critics Association</a> in 1976 and was nominated for a "Best Film" <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award" title="César Award">César Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a>'s 1979 film <i><a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Now" title="Apocalypse Now">Apocalypse Now</a></i>, a film based on <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>'s 1902 <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Darkness" title="Heart of Darkness">Heart of Darkness</a></i>, was influenced also by <i>Aguirre</i>, as it contains seemingly deliberate visual "quotations" of Herzog's film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Coppola himself has noted, "<i>Aguirre</i>, with its incredible imagery, was a very strong influence. I'd be remiss if I didn't mention it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several critics have noted that <i>Aguirre</i> appears to have had a direct influence on several other films. Martin Rubin has written that "[a]mong the films strongly influenced by <i>Aguirre</i> are Coppola’s <i>Apocalypse Now</i> and <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Malick" title="Terrence Malick">Terrence Malick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_World_(2005_film)" title="The New World (2005 film)">The New World</a></i> (2005)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-37">[37]</a></sup> J. Hoberman agreed, noting that Herzog’s "<a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a> Amazon fever dream” was “the influence Malick's over-inflated <i>New World</i> can't shake."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoberman_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoberman-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> opined "This is an astonishing, deceptively simple, pocket-sized epic whose influence, in terms of both style and narrative, is seen in films as diverse as <i>Apocalypse Now</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mission_(1986_film)" title="The Mission (1986 film)">The Mission</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Predator_(film)" title="Predator (film)">Predator</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blair_Witch_Project" title="The Blair Witch Project">The Blair Witch Project</a></i> (1999)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_accuracy">Historical accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aguirre,_the_Wrath_of_God&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Historical accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although plot details and many of the characters in <i>Aguirre</i> come directly from Herzog’s own imagination, historians have pointed out that the film fairly accurately incorporates some 16th-century events and historical personages into a fictional narrative. The film’s major characters, Aguirre, Ursúa, Don Fernando, Inez and Florés, were indeed involved in a 1560 expedition that left Peru to find the city of El Dorado. Commissioned by Peru’s governor, Ursúa organized an expeditionary group of 300 men to travel by way of the Amazon River. He was accompanied by his <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">mixed-race</a> mistress, Doña Inez. At one point during the journey, Aguirre, a professional soldier, decided that he could use the 300 men to overthrow the Spanish rule of Peru. Aguirre had Ursúa murdered and proclaimed Fernando as “The Prince of Peru”. Fernando himself was eventually murdered when he questioned Aguirre’s scheme of sailing to the Atlantic, conquering Panama, crossing the isthmus and invading Peru. Many others who attempted to rebel against Aguirre were also killed. The surviving soldiers conquered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_Margarita" title="Isla Margarita">Isla Margarita</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> and made preparations to attack the mainland. However, by that time Spanish authorities had learned of Aguirre’s plans, and when the rebels arrived in Venezuela, government agents offered full pardons to Aguirre’s men. All of them accepted the deal. Immediately prior to his arrest, Aguirre murdered his daughter Florés, who had remained by his side during the entire journey. He was then captured and dismembered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waller_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waller-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Herzog’s screenplay merged the 1560 expedition with the events of an earlier Amazonian journey in 1541 and 1542. Like Ursúa, <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Pizarro" title="Gonzalo Pizarro">Gonzalo Pizarro</a> and his men entered the Amazon basin in search of El Dorado. Various troubles afflicted the expedition and, sure that El Dorado was very close, Pizarro set up a smaller group led by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Orellana" title="Francisco de Orellana">Francisco de Orellana</a> to break off from the main force and forge ahead, then return with news of what they had found. This group utilized a <a href="/wiki/Brigantine" title="Brigantine">brigantine</a> to journey down the river. Accompanying Orellana was <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_de_Carvajal" title="Gaspar de Carvajal">Gaspar de Carvajal</a>, who kept a journal of the group’s experiences. The historic Gaspar de Carvajal (1500–1584) was a Spanish Dominican friar who had settled in Peru and dedicated himself to the conversion of the Indians. His general attitude towards the Indians was consistent with the benevolence of his better-known brother Dominican friar, Bartolome de las Casas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This personality is at odds with the description in the film where Carvajal is portrayed as a cowardly priest who claimed that "the church was always on the side of the strong."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After failing to find the legendary city, Orellana was unable to return because of the current, and he and his men continued to follow the river until he reached the <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> of the Amazon in 1542.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fritze_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fritze-5">[5]</a></sup> Other Spanish expeditions outside the Amazon influenced the story; the conversation in which the Indians refuse a Bible comes from events before the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cajamarca" title="Battle of Cajamarca">Battle of Cajamarca</a>, in which Inca emperor <a href="/wiki/Atahualpa" title="Atahualpa">Atahualpa</a> allegedly rejected the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Requerimiento" title="Requerimiento">Requerimiento</a>. The chronicle of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvar_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca" title="Álvar Núñez Cabeza de Vaca">Álvar Núñez Cabeza de Vaca</a>, <i>La Relación</i> ("<i>The Account</i>"), mentions the appearance of a boat in a treetop after a fierce tropical storm in <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Monday morning we went down to the port and did not find the ships. We saw their buoys in the water, from which we realized that they had been lost, and we went along the coast to see if we could find signs of them. Since we found nothing, we went into the woods, and a quarter of a league into them we found one of the ship's boats in some trees.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Alvar Nuñez Cabeza de Vaca,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Kinski’s crazed performance bore similarities to the real Aguirre, a “true homicidal megalomaniac”. Many of his fellow soldiers considered his actions to be that of a madman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fritze_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fritze-5">[5]</a></sup> Kinski’s use of a limp reflected one that Aguirre actually had, the result of a battle injury. Aguirre’s frequent short but impassioned speeches to his men in the film were accurately based on the man’s noted “simple but effective rhetorical ability.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waller_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Waller-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
