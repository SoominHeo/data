<p><b>Reeves's muntjac</b> (<i>Muntiacus reevesi</i>; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">山羌</span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Muntjac" title="Muntjac">muntjac</a> species found widely in southeastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> to <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a>), <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C5%8Ds%C5%8D_Peninsula" title="Bōsō Peninsula">Bōsō Peninsula</a> on <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Izu_%C5%8Cshima" title="Izu Ōshima">Izu Ōshima</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-2">[2]</a></sup> It has also been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (south <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HYSTDD_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HYSTDD-4">[4]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Midlands" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HYSTDD_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-HYSTDD-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/East_Wales" title="East Wales">east Wales</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HYSTDD_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-HYSTDD-4">[4]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> by 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blossom" title="Blossom">blossoms</a>, succulent shoots, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">grasses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, and was also reported to eat trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HYSTDD_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-HYSTDD-4">[4]</a></sup> It takes its name from John Reeves, who was appointed Assistant Inspector of Tea for the British East India Company in 1812.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reeves%27s_muntjac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This muntjac grows to 0.5 metres (1 ft 8 in) high at the shoulder,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DEFRA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-DEFRA-6">[6]</a></sup> 0.95 m (3 ft 1 in) in length, and weighs between 10 and 18 kilograms (22 and 40 lb) when fully grown. It is dog-like in appearance but has striped markings on its face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DEFRA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-DEFRA-6">[6]</a></sup> The male has short <a href="/wiki/Antler" title="Antler">antlers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DEFRA_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-DEFRA-6">[6]</a></sup> usually four inches or less, and uses them to push enemies off balance so he can wound them with his upper two inch canine teeth. The Taiwanese <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> (<i>M. r. micrurus</i>), commonly known as the Formosan Reeves' Muntjac, is relatively dark compared to the other subspecies.</p>
<p>Reeves's muntjac is also called the barking deer, known for its distinctive bark,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HYSTDD_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-HYSTDD-4">[4]</a></sup> though this name is also used for the other species of <a href="/wiki/Muntjac" title="Muntjac">muntjacs</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg/220px-Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg/330px-Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg/440px-Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muntiacus_reevesi_Toru%C5%84_.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Muntiacus reevesi</i> in the Zoo-Botanical Garden in Toruń (Poland)</div>

<p>The <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> preferred habitats are forest and <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a>. It is a solitary and <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a> animal, feeding on tree leaves, grass and fruits.</p>
<p>The tanned skin of <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> is notable for its softness and is occasionally used for specialist application like beauty care product, musical instruments, lenses and antique items packaging. Low-fat muntjack meat is also noted for its culinary qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reeves%27s_muntjac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Female muntjacs (known as "does"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BDS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BDS-8">[8]</a></sup>) become sexually mature within the first year of life. Mating occurs throughout the year. Gestation period lasts from 209 to 220 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Females limit the number of mating bouts, though time between successive bouts is determined by males<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (known as "bucks"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BDS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BDS-8">[8]</a></sup>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_into_UK_and_Japan">Introduction into UK and Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reeves%27s_muntjac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Introduction into UK and Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An unspecified species of <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> was introduced to the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Woburn_Abbey" title="Woburn Abbey">Woburn Abbey</a> in Bedfordshire in the nineteenth century by the then <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bedford" title="Duke of Bedford">Duke of Bedford</a>. While a small number are reported as escaping, it is extremely unlikely that they are the source of the current UK population. Larger numbers of <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> escaped from <a href="/wiki/Whipsnade_Zoo" title="Whipsnade Zoo">Whipsnade Zoo</a>, and they are the more likely ancestors, in addition to other releases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981</a>, it has been illegal to release the species except where already established. Reeves's muntjac colonies exist throughout <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, and the population continues to grow. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the first sightings of muntjac in 2008 caused the Government, concerned at the risk of the species becoming established, to quickly introduce an all year round hunting season.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> was introduced in Japanese <a href="/wiki/B%C5%8Ds%C5%8D_Peninsula" title="Bōsō Peninsula">Bōsō Peninsula</a> after several specimens escaped the exhibition zoo in 1960-1970 period. As in 2017, the <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> numbers on <a href="/wiki/B%C5%8Ds%C5%8D_Peninsula" title="Bōsō Peninsula">Bōsō Peninsula</a> reached at least 60000 and it is considered a harmful invasive species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In particular, the muntjac has inflicted severe damage to the <a href="/wiki/Ashitaba" title="Ashitaba">Ashitaba</a> plantations. Also, the <b>Reeves's muntjac</b> group has escaped zoo on <a href="/wiki/Izu_%C5%8Cshima" title="Izu Ōshima">Izu Ōshima</a> in 1970 when the fence fell due typhoon. The muntjac eradication company on <a href="/wiki/Izu_%C5%8Cshima" title="Izu Ōshima">Izu Ōshima</a> was performed in 2007-2014 but completely failed, with at least 11000 specimens and yearly population growth rate of 15% as in 2014. This failure exposed the inadequate survey methods strongly under-estimating the population of these nocturnal species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reeves%27s_muntjac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, it is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_protected_species_in_Hong_Kong" title="List of protected species in Hong Kong">protected species</a> under Wild Animals Protection Ordinance Cap 170. Overall though it generally remains common and widespread, resulting in it being listed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
