<p><b>Anatoly Aleksandrovich Sobchak</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Анато́лий Алекса́ндрович Собча́к</span>; <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Russian" title="Help:IPA for Russian">[ɐnɐˈtolʲɪj ɐlʲɪˈksandrəvʲɪtɕ sɐpˈtɕak]</a></span>, 10 August 1937 – 20 February 2000) was a <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> politician, a co-author of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Constitution of the Russian Federation">Constitution of the Russian Federation</a>, the first democratically elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, and a mentor and teacher of both <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">Dmitry Medvedev</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anatoly_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_legal_scholar">Soviet legal scholar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anatoly_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soviet legal scholar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anatoly Sobchak was born in <a href="/wiki/Chita,_Zabaykalsky_Krai" title="Chita, Zabaykalsky Krai">Chita</a>, Siberia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a>, on 10 August 1937. His father, Aleksander Antonovich, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroad</a> <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>, and his mother, Nadezhda Andreyevna Litvinova, was an accountant. Anatoly was one of four brothers. In 1939, the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Uzbek_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic">Uzbekistan</a>, where Anatoly lived until 1953 before entering <a href="/wiki/Stavropol" title="Stavropol">Stavropol</a> Law College. In 1954, he transferred to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_State_University" title="Saint Petersburg State University">Leningrad State University</a>. In 1958, he married Nonna Gandzyuk, a student of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Herzen" title="Alexander Herzen">Hertzen</a> Teacher's College. They had a daughter called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maria Sobchak (page does not exist)">Maria Sobchak</a> born in 1965, who is currently a St Petersburg lawyer while her son <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gleb_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gleb Sobchak (page does not exist)">Gleb Sobchak</a> born in 1983, graduated from the Law Faculty of St. Petersburg State University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating from Leningrad State University, he worked for three years as a lawyer in <a href="/wiki/Stavropol" title="Stavropol">Stavropol</a>, then returned to Leningrad State University for graduate studies (1962–1965). After obtaining his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a>, he taught law at the Leningrad Police School and the Leningrad Institute for Cellulose and Paper Industries' Technology (1965–1973) and between 1973 and 1990 he taught at Leningrad State University. In 1980 he married <a href="/wiki/Lyudmila_Narusova" title="Lyudmila Narusova">Lyudmila Narusova</a>, at that time a history student at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leningrad_Academy_of_Soviet_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leningrad Academy of Soviet Culture (page does not exist)">Leningrad Academy of Soviet Culture</a> and later a prominent MP. They had a daughter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kseniya_Sobchak" title="Kseniya Sobchak">Ksenia Sobchak</a>.</p>
<p>After obtaining his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D.Sc." title="D.Sc.">D.Sc.</a> in 1982 he was appointed Professor and Head of the Department of Common Law in Socialist Economics. He was very popular among law students, especially for his mildly anti-government comments. During his work at Leningrad State University he established close relations with its then administrator of international affairs, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>, which he maintained for the rest of his life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislator">Legislator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anatoly_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legislator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989 after election laws changed during <a href="/wiki/Perestroika" title="Perestroika">Perestroika</a>, he <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union_legislative_election,_1989" title="Soviet Union legislative election, 1989">was elected</a> as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" title="Independent (politician)">independent candidate</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_People%27s_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union">Congress of People's Deputies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. He was one of only a few deputies who had a legal background, so he contributed enormously to most of the laws created during 1989–1991. He became one of the founders and a co-chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inter-Regional_Deputies_Group" title="Inter-Regional Deputies Group">Inter-Regional Deputies Group</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov" title="Andrei Sakharov">Andrei Sakharov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a>. He also was a chairman of the Parliamentary Commission on the Investigation of the <a href="/wiki/April_9_tragedy" title="April 9 tragedy">events of 9 April 1989</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>. The Commission condemned the military, which was blamed for many deaths when dispersing demonstrators. The Commission's report made it more difficult to use military force against civil demonstrations in the Soviet Union and Russia.</p>
<p>He was a member of the President's Consultative Council during <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>'s tenure and contributed to legislation that originated from the presidential administration.</p>
<p>After the Soviet Union was dissolved in 1991, Sobchak was not a member of the central Parliament but was a member of Yeltsin's Presidential Council and the chairman of the Constitutional Assembly that prepared the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Constitution of the Russian Federation">Constitution of the Russian Federation</a> in 1993. The constitution is often informally called <i>Sobchak's constitution</i>, although its real authors have been somewhat less known.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayor_of_Saint_Petersburg">Mayor of Saint Petersburg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anatoly_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mayor of Saint Petersburg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 1990, Sobchak was elected a deputy of the Leningrad City Council, and in May he became the chairman of the Council. From the beginning his leadership was marked by a strongly authoritarian bent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Council decided to change the structure of the city governance so as to have a Mayor elected by direct elections. The first of such elections in June 1991 were combined with the <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> on the city name. Sobchak won the elections, and the city voted to return to its historical name of Saint Petersburg. The name change was established in one of the last sessions of the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_People%27s_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union">Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union</a>, held on 12 September 1991. The change needed an amendment of the Constitution of the Soviet Union and its passage required much effort by Sobchak.</p>
<p>Sobchak was Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> in 1991–1996. During his tenure the city became a place of glamorous cultural and sporting events. Most of the everyday control of the city structure was handled by two Mayor's deputies - <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Anatolyevich_Yakovlev" title="Vladimir Anatolyevich Yakovlev">Vladimir Yakovlev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>; critics alleged deterioration of city infrastructure, growing corruption, and crime during this time. In the 1996 mayoral election Sobchak was opposed by his former first deputy Vladimir Yakovlev and lost by a margin of 1.2%. The major pitch of Yakovlev's campaign was that Sobchak's patronage of the arts (with city money) and involvement in federal politics prevented him from solving the real problems of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emigration_and_Return">Emigration and Return</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anatoly_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Emigration and Return">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1997 a criminal investigation started against Sobchak. He was accused of irregularities in the privatization of his own apartment, his elder daughter's apartment, and his wife's art studio. By the standards of the 1990s in Russia the allegations were relatively minor (although the alleged losses for city finances were still in the tens of thousands of dollars). Thus, Sobchak's supporters saw the criminal proceedings as a political repression. According to <a href="/wiki/Ksenia_Sobchak" title="Ksenia Sobchak">Ksenia Sobchak</a>, this campaign was started in 1995 from Moscow to prevent her father from running in future presidential elections <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.aif.ru/politics/world/16345" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>.</p>
<p>On 7 November 1997, Sobchak flew to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> on a private plane without passport processing on the Russian side. The formal reason for his departure was medical treatment in a Paris hospital for his heart condition, but Sobchak never checked in at the hospital. Between 1997 and 1999 he lived the typical life of a political émigré in Paris.</p>
<p>In June 1999, his friend <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> became much stronger politically (in a few weeks he became the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Russia" title="Prime Minister of Russia">Prime Minister of Russia</a>), and he was able to make the prosecutors drop the charges against Sobchak. On 12 June 1999, Sobchak returned to Russia. After his return Sobchak became a very active supporter of Putin in his quest for the Russian presidency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anatoly_Sobchak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="500" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg/220px-Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg/330px-Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg/440px-Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vladimir_Putin_24_February_2000-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Funerals of Sobchak. Vladimir Putin with <a href="/wiki/Lyudmila_Narusova" title="Lyudmila Narusova">the widow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ksenia_Sobchak" title="Ksenia Sobchak">daughter</a> of the late mayor of Saint Petersburg</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On February 17, 2000, Putin met with Sobchak and asked him to urgently travel to <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a> to support his election campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessen_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gessen-6">[6]</a></sup> Sobchak traveled there, accompanied by two assistants who also served as his bodyguards. On 20 February 2000, Sobchak died suddenly in the town of <a href="/wiki/Svetlogorsk,_Kaliningrad_Oblast" title="Svetlogorsk, Kaliningrad Oblast">Svetlogorsk</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad_Oblast" title="Kaliningrad Oblast">Kaliningrad Oblast</a>. The initial suspected cause of death was a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>, but the findings of two medical experts were contradictory<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessen2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gessen2-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sobchak260_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sobchak260-8">[8]</a></sup> A criminal investigation of Sobchak's death as a possible "premeditated murder with aggravating circumstances" was opened only on 6 May 2000, more than two months later. After three months, the investigation was closed without a finding <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessen_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gessen-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Union_(Russia)" title="Democratic Union (Russia)">Democratic Union</a> party led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valeria_Novodvorskaya" title="Valeria Novodvorskaya">Valeria Novodvorskaya</a> made an official statement that not only Sobchak, but also two of his aides had heart attacks simultaneously, which indicated poisoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Two other men were present with Sobchak during his death, but their names were not publicly disclosed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sobchak260_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-sobchak260-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to an independent investigation by <a href="/wiki/Arkady_Vaksberg" title="Arkady Vaksberg">Arkady Vaksberg</a>, both bodyguards of Sobchak were treated for symptoms of poisoning after Sobchak's death, indicating at a probable <a href="/wiki/Contract_killing" title="Contract killing">contract killing</a> by poisoning<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessen_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gessen-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was interred in Nikola Cemetery at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Nevsky_Monastery" title="Alexander Nevsky Monastery">Alexander Nevsky Monastery</a> in St. Petersburg, near the grave of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galina_Starovoitova" title="Galina Starovoitova">Galina Starovoitova</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
