<p><b>Anaximander</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀναξίμανδρος</span> <i>Anaximandros</i>; c. 610 – c. 546 BC) was a <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">pre-Socratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy" title="Ancient Greek philosophy">Greek philosopher</a> who lived in <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-3">[3]</a></sup> a city of <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionia</a> (in modern-day Turkey). He belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Milesian_school" title="Milesian school">Milesian school</a> and learned the teachings of his master <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thales" title="Thales">Thales</a>. He succeeded Thales and became the second master of that school where he counted <a href="/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus" title="Anaximenes of Miletus">Anaximenes</a> and, arguably, <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> amongst his pupils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Little of his life and work is known today. According to available historical documents, he is the first philosopher known to have written down his studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> although only one fragment of his work remains. Fragmentary testimonies found in documents after his death provide a portrait of the man.</p>
<p>He was an early proponent of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and tried to observe and explain different aspects of the universe, with a particular <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">interest in its origins</a>, claiming that nature is ruled by laws, just like human societies, and anything that disturbs the balance of nature does not last long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Like many thinkers of his time, Anaximander's <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> included contributions to many disciplines. In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, he attempted to describe the mechanics of celestial bodies in relation to the Earth. In physics, his postulation that the indefinite (or <a href="/wiki/Apeiron_(cosmology)" title="Apeiron (cosmology)">apeiron</a>) was the source of all things led Greek philosophy to a new level of conceptual abstraction. His knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> allowed him to introduce the <a href="/wiki/Gnomon" title="Gnomon">gnomon</a> in Greece. He created a map of the world that contributed greatly to the advancement of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>. He was also involved in the <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> of Miletus and was sent as a leader to one of its colonies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anaximander.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="282" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Anaximander.jpg/140px-Anaximander.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Anaximander.jpg/210px-Anaximander.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Anaximander.jpg/280px-Anaximander.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anaximander.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail of <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>'s painting <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">The School of Athens</a></i>, 1510–1511. This could be a representation of Anaximander leaning towards <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a> on his left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Anaximander, son of Praxiades, was born in the third year of the 42nd <a href="/wiki/Olympiad" title="Olympiad">Olympiad</a> (610 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Refutation_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Refutation-8">[8]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Apollodorus_of_Athens" title="Apollodorus of Athens">Apollodorus of Athens</a>, Greek grammarian of the 2nd century BC, he was sixty-four years old during the second year of the 58th Olympiad (547–546 BC), and died shortly afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Establishing a timeline of his work is now impossible, since no document provides chronological references. <a href="/wiki/Themistius" title="Themistius">Themistius</a>, a 4th-century <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetorician</a>, mentions that he was the "first of the known Greeks to publish a written document on nature." Therefore, his texts would be amongst the earliest written in <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">prose</a>, at least in the Western world. By the time of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, his philosophy was almost forgotten, and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, his successor <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> and a few <a href="/wiki/Doxography" title="Doxography">doxographers</a> provide us with the little information that remains. However, we know from Aristotle that Thales, also from Miletus, precedes Anaximander. It is debatable whether Thales actually was the teacher of Anaximander, but there is no doubt that Anaximander was influenced by Thales' theory that everything is derived from water. One thing that is not debatable is that even the ancient Greeks considered Anaximander to be from the Monist school which began in Miletus with Thales followed by Anaximander and finished with <a href="/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus" title="Anaximenes of Miletus">Anaximenes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> 3rd-century <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> rhetorician <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus" title="Claudius Aelianus">Aelian</a> depicts him as leader of the Milesian colony to <a href="/wiki/Sozopol" title="Sozopol">Apollonia</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coast, and hence some have inferred that he was a prominent citizen. Indeed, <i>Various History</i> (III, 17) explains that philosophers sometimes also dealt with political matters. It is very likely that leaders of Miletus sent him there as a legislator to create a constitution or simply to maintain the colony’s allegiance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anaximander's theories were influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythical</a> tradition, and by some ideas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thales" title="Thales">Thales</a> – the father of philosophy – as well as by observations made by older civilizations in the East<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (October 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Anaximander#Dubious" title="Talk:Anaximander">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> (especially by the Babylonian astrologers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> All these were elaborated rationally. In his desire to find some universal principle, he assumed, like traditional religion, the existence of a cosmic order; and in elaborating his ideas on this he used the old mythical language which ascribed divine control to various spheres of reality. This was a common practice for the Greek philosophers in a society which saw gods everywhere, therefore they could fit their ideas into a tolerably elastic system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some scholars saw a gap between the existing mythical and the new <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rational</a> way of thought which is the main characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">archaic period</a> (8th to 6th century BC) in the Greek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_states" title="City states">city states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Because of this, they did not hesitate to speak for a "Greek miracle". But if we follow carefully the course of Anaximander's ideas, we will notice that there was not such an abrupt break as initially appears. The basic elements of nature (<a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">fire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">earth</a>) which the first Greek philosophers believed that constituted the universe represent in fact the primordial forces of previous thought. Their collision produced what the mythical tradition had called cosmic harmony. In the old cosmogonies – <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> (8th – 7th century BC) and <a href="/wiki/Pherecydes_of_Syros" title="Pherecydes of Syros">Pherecydes</a> (6th century BC) – <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> establishes his order in the world by destroying the powers which were threatening this harmony, (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titans_(mythology)" title="Titans (mythology)">Titans</a>). Anaximander claimed that the cosmic order is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarchic" title="Monarchic">monarchic</a> but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric" title="Geometric">geometric</a> and this causes the equilibrium of the earth which is lying in the centre of the universe. This is the projection on nature of a new political order and a new space organized around a centre which is the static point of the system in the society as in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In this space there is <i>isonomy</i> (equal rights) and all the forces are symmetrical and transferrable. The decisions are now taken by the assembly of <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_rhetorical_terms#Demos" title="Glossary of rhetorical terms">demos</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Agora" title="Agora">agora</a> which is lying in the middle of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The same <i>rational</i> way of thought led him to introduce the abstract <a href="/wiki/Apeiron_(cosmology)" title="Apeiron (cosmology)">apeiron</a> (indefinite, infinite, boundless, unlimited<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>) as an origin of the universe, a concept that is probably influenced by the original <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(mythology)" title="Chaos (mythology)">Chaos</a> (gaping void, abyss, formless state) of the mythical <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">cosmogony</a> from which everything else appeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It also takes notice of the mutual changes between the four elements. Origin, then, must be something else unlimited in its source, that could create without experiencing decay, so that genesis would never stop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apeiron">Apeiron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apeiron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Apeiron_(cosmology)" title="Apeiron (cosmology)">Apeiron (cosmology)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matter#Antiquity_.28c._610_BC.E2.80.93c._322_BC.29" title="Matter">Matter § Antiquity (c. 610 BC–c. 322 BC)</a></div>
<p>The <i>Refutation</i> attributed to <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">Hippolytus of Rome</a> (I, 5), and the later 6th century Byzantine philosopher <a href="/wiki/Simplicius_of_Cilicia" title="Simplicius of Cilicia">Simplicius of Cilicia</a>, attribute to Anaximander the earliest use of the word <i>apeíron</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἄπειρον</span> "infinite" or "limitless") to designate the original principle. He was the first philosopher to employ, in a philosophical context, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Arche" title="Arche">archế</a></i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀρχή</span>), which until then had meant beginning or origin. For him, it became no longer a mere point in time, but a source that could perpetually give birth to whatever will be. The indefiniteness is spatial in early usages as in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> (indefinite sea) and as in <a href="/wiki/Xenophanes" title="Xenophanes">Xenophanes</a> (6th century BC) who said that the earth went down indefinitely (to apeiron) i.e. beyond the imagination or concept of men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aristotle writes (<i><a href="/wiki/Metaphysics_(Aristotle)" title="Metaphysics (Aristotle)">Metaphysics</a></i>, I III 3–4) that the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">Pre-Socratics</a> were searching for the element that constitutes all things. While each pre-Socratic philosopher gave a different answer as to the identity of this element (<a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">water</a> for Thales and <a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">air</a> for Anaximenes), Anaximander understood the beginning or first principle to be an endless, unlimited primordial mass (<i>apeiron</i>), subject to neither old age nor decay, that perpetually yielded fresh materials from which everything we perceive is derived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He proposed the theory of the apeiron in direct response to the earlier theory of his teacher, Thales, who had claimed that the primary substance was water. The notion of temporal infinity was familiar to the Greek mind from remote antiquity in the religious concept of immortality and Anaximander's description was in terms appropriate to this conception. This arche is called "eternal and ageless". (Hippolytus (?), <i>Refutation</i>, I,6,I;DK B2)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>For Anaximander, the <a href="/wiki/Principle_(chemistry)" title="Principle (chemistry)">principle</a> of things, the constituent of all substances, is nothing determined and not an element such as water in Thales' view. Neither is it something halfway between air and water, or between air and fire, thicker than air and fire, or more subtle than water and earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Anaximander argues that water cannot embrace all of the opposites found in nature — for example, water can only be wet, never dry — and therefore cannot be the one primary substance; nor could any of the other candidates. He postulated the <i>apeiron</i> as a substance that, although not directly perceptible to us, could explain the opposites he saw around him.</p>
<p>Anaximander explains how the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">four elements</a> of ancient physics (<a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">fire</a>) are formed, and how Earth and terrestrial beings are formed through their interactions. Unlike other Pre-Socratics, he never defines this principle precisely, and it has generally been understood (e.g., by Aristotle and by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Saint Augustine</a>) as a sort of primal <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">chaos</a>. According to him, the Universe originates in the separation of opposites in the primordial matter. It embraces the opposites of hot and cold, wet and dry, and directs the movement of things; an entire host of shapes and differences then grow that are found in "all the worlds" (for he believed there were many).</p>
<p>Anaximander maintains that all dying things are returning to the element from which they came (<i>apeiron</i>). The one surviving fragment of Anaximander's writing deals with this matter. Simplicius transmitted it as a quotation, which describes the balanced and mutual changes of the elements:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Whence things have their origin,<br/>
Thence also their destruction happens,<br/>
According to necessity;<br/>
For they give to each other justice and recompense<br/>
For their injustice<br/>
In conformity with the ordinance of Time.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Simplicius mentions that Anaximander said all these "in poetic terms", meaning that he used the old mythical language. The goddess Justice (<a href="/wiki/Dike_(mythology)" title="Dike (mythology)">Dike</a>) keeps the cosmic order. This concept of returning to the element of origin was often revisited afterwards, notably by Aristotle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and by the Greek <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedian</a> <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a>: "what comes from earth must return to earth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_in_the_Tragic_Age_of_the_Greeks" title="Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks">Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks</a></i>, stated that Anaximander viewed "... all coming-to-be as though it were an illegitimate emancipation from eternal being, a wrong for which destruction is the only penance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Physicist <a href="/wiki/Max_Born" title="Max Born">Max Born</a>, in commenting upon <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>'s arriving at the idea that the elementary particles of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> are to be seen as different manifestations, different quantum states, of one and the same “primordial substance,”' proposed that this primordial substance be called <i>apeiron</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmology">Cosmology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cosmology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Anaximander's bold use of non-<a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">mythological</a> explanatory hypotheses considerably distinguishes him from previous cosmology writers such as <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>. It confirms that pre-Socratic philosophers were making an early effort to demystify physical processes. His major contribution to history was writing the oldest prose document about the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a> and the origins of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>; for this he is often called the "Father of <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">Cosmology</a>" and founder of astronomy. However, <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Plutarch" title="Pseudo-Plutarch">pseudo-Plutarch</a> states that he still viewed celestial bodies as deities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anaximander was the first to conceive a <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanical</a> model of the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a>. In his model, the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> floats very still in the centre of the infinite, not supported by anything. It remains "in the same place because of its indifference", a point of view that Aristotle considered ingenious, but false, in <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Heavens" title="On the Heavens">On the Heavens</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Its curious shape is that of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)" title="Cylinder (geometry)">cylinder</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> with a height one-third of its diameter. The flat top forms the inhabited world, which is surrounded by a circular oceanic mass.</p>
<p>Anaximander's realization that the Earth floats free without falling and does not need to be resting on something has been indicated by many as the first cosmological revolution and the starting point of scientific thinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Karl Popper calls this idea "one of the boldest, most revolutionary, and most portentous ideas in the whole history of human thinking."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Such a model allowed the concept that <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">celestial bodies</a> could pass under the Earth, opening the way to Greek astronomy.</p>

<p>At the origin, after the separation of <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">hot and cold</a>, a ball of flame appeared that surrounded Earth like bark on a tree. This ball broke apart to form the rest of the Universe. It resembled a system of hollow concentric wheels, filled with fire, with the rims pierced by holes like those of a flute. Consequently, the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> was the fire that one could see through a hole the same size as the Earth on the farthest wheel, and an eclipse corresponded with the <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occlusion</a> of that hole. The diameter of the solar wheel was twenty-seven times that of the Earth (or twenty-eight, depending on the sources)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> wheel, whose fire was less intense, eighteen (or nineteen) times. Its hole could change shape, thus explaining <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phases</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, located closer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> followed the same model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anaximander was the first astronomer to consider the Sun as a huge mass, and consequently, to realize how far from Earth it might be, and the first to present a system where the celestial bodies turned at different distances. Furthermore, according to Diogenes Laertius (II, 2), he built a <a href="/wiki/Celestial_spheres" title="Celestial spheres">celestial sphere</a>. This invention undoubtedly made him the first to realize the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">obliquity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">Zodiac</a> as the Roman philosopher <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> reports in <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i> (II, 8). It is a little early to use the term <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>, but his knowledge and work on astronomy confirm that he must have observed the inclination of the celestial sphere in relation to the plane of the Earth to explain the seasons. The <a href="/wiki/Doxography" title="Doxography">doxographer</a> and theologian Aetius attributes to Pythagoras the exact measurement of the obliquity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_worlds">Multiple worlds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Multiple worlds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Simplicius, Anaximander already speculated on the plurality of <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">worlds</a>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Atomism" title="Atomism">atomists</a> <a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">Leucippus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a>, and later philosopher <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a>. These thinkers supposed that worlds appeared and disappeared for a while, and that some were born when others perished. They claimed that this movement was eternal, "for without movement, there can be no generation, no destruction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to Simplicius, Hippolytus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> reports Anaximander's claim that from the infinite comes the principle of beings, which themselves come from the heavens and the worlds (several doxographers use the plural when this philosopher is referring to the worlds within,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> which are often infinite in quantity). <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> writes that he attributes different gods to the countless worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>This theory places Anaximander close to the Atomists and the <a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicureans</a> who, more than a century later, also claimed that an infinity of worlds appeared and disappeared. In the <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Western_philosophers#Classical_philosophers" title="Timeline of Western philosophers">timeline of the Greek history of thought</a>, some thinkers conceptualized a single world (Plato, Aristotle, <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archelaus_(philosopher)" title="Archelaus (philosopher)">Archelaus</a>), while others instead speculated on the existence of a series of worlds, continuous or non-continuous (Anaximenes, Heraclitus, <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">Empedocles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diogenes_Apolloniates" title="Diogenes Apolloniates">Diogenes</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_phenomena">Meteorological phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Meteorological phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anaximander attributed some phenomena, such as <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, to the intervention of elements, rather than to divine causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In his system, thunder results from the shock of clouds hitting each other; the loudness of the sound is proportionate with that of the shock. Thunder without lightning is the result of the wind being too weak to emit any flame, but strong enough to produce a sound. A flash of lightning without thunder is a jolt of the air that disperses and falls, allowing a less active fire to break free. Thunderbolts are the result of a thicker and more violent air flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>He saw the sea as a remnant of the mass of humidity that once surrounded Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> A part of that mass evaporated under the sun's action, thus causing the winds and even the rotation of the celestial bodies, which he believed were attracted to places where water is more abundant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> He explained rain as a product of the humidity pumped up from Earth by the sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Refutation_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Refutation-8">[8]</a></sup> For him, the Earth was slowly drying up and water only remained in the deepest regions, which someday would go dry as well. According to Aristotle's <i><a href="/wiki/Meteorology_(Aristotle)" title="Meteorology (Aristotle)">Meteorology</a></i> (II, 3), Democritus also shared this opinion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_humankind">Origin of humankind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origin of humankind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anaximander speculated about the beginnings and <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">origin</a> of animal life. Taking into account the existence of fossils<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Anaximander#Dubious" title="Talk:Anaximander">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>, he claimed that animals sprang out of the sea long ago. The first animals were born trapped in a spiny bark, but as they got older, the bark would dry up and break.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> As the early humidity evaporated, dry land emerged and, in time, humankind had to adapt. The 3rd century Roman writer <a href="/wiki/Censorinus" title="Censorinus">Censorinus</a> reports:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Anaximander of Miletus considered that from warmed up water and earth emerged either fish or entirely fishlike animals. Inside these animals, men took form and embryos were held prisoners until puberty; only then, after these animals burst open, could men and women come out, now able to feed themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Anaximander put forward the idea that humans had to spend part of this transition inside the mouths of big fish to protect themselves from the Earth's climate until they could come out in open air and lose their scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> He thought that, considering humans' extended infancy, we could not have survived in the primeval world in the same manner we do presently.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_accomplishments">Other accomplishments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other accomplishments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartography">Cartography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cartography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anaximander_world_map-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1063" data-file-width="1063" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Anaximander_world_map-en.svg/280px-Anaximander_world_map-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Anaximander_world_map-en.svg/420px-Anaximander_world_map-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Anaximander_world_map-en.svg/560px-Anaximander_world_map-en.svg.png 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anaximander_world_map-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Possible rendering of Anaximander's world map<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agathemerus" title="Agathemerus">Agathemerus</a> (later Greek geographers) claim that, according to the geographer <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a>, Anaximander was the first to publish a map of the world. The map probably inspired the Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus" title="Hecataeus of Miletus">Hecataeus of Miletus</a> to draw a more accurate version. Strabo viewed both as the first geographers after <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>.</p>
<p>Maps were produced in ancient times, also notably in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>. Only some small examples survived until today. The unique example of a world map comes from late Babylonian tablet <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_Map_of_the_World" title="Babylonian Map of the World">BM 92687</a> later than 9th century BC but is based probably on a much older map. These maps indicated directions, roads, towns, borders, and geological features. Anaximander's innovation was to represent the entire inhabited land known to the ancient Greeks.</p>
<p>Such an accomplishment is more significant than it at first appears. Anaximander most likely drew this map for three reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> First, it could be used to improve navigation and trade between <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a>'s colonies and other colonies around the Mediterranean Sea and Black Sea. Second, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thales" title="Thales">Thales</a> would probably have found it easier to convince the Ionian <a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">city-states</a> to join in a federation in order to push the <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Median</a> threat away if he possessed such a tool. Finally, the philosophical idea of a global representation of the world simply for the sake of knowledge was reason enough to design one.</p>
<p>Surely aware of the sea's convexity, he may have designed his map on a slightly rounded metal surface. The centre or “navel” of the world (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ὀμφαλός γῆς</span> <i>omphalós gẽs</i>) could have been <a href="/wiki/Delphi" title="Delphi">Delphi</a>, but is more likely in Anaximander's time to have been located near Miletus. The <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> was near the map's centre and enclosed by three continents, themselves located in the middle of the ocean and isolated like islands by sea and rivers. <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> was bordered on the south by the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and was separated from <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> by the Black Sea, the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">Lake Maeotis</a>, and, further east, either by the <a href="/wiki/Rioni_River" title="Rioni River">Phasis River</a> (now called the Rioni) or the <a href="/wiki/Tanais" title="Tanais">Tanais</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> flowed south into the ocean, separating <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a> (which was the name for the part of the then-known <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> continent) from Asia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gnomon">Gnomon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gnomon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Suda" title="Suda">Suda</a></i> relates that Anaximander explained some basic notions of geometry. It also mentions his interest in the measurement of time and associates him with the introduction in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> of the gnomon. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacedaemon" title="Lacedaemon">Lacedaemon</a>, he participated in the construction, or at least in the adjustment, of <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundials</a> to indicate <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Indeed, a gnomon required adjustments from a place to another because of the difference in latitude.</p>
<p>In his time, the gnomon was simply a vertical pillar or rod mounted on a horizontal plane. The position of its shadow on the plane indicated the time of day. As it moves through its apparent course, the sun draws a curve with the tip of the projected shadow, which is shortest at noon, when pointing due south. The variation in the tip’s position at noon indicates the solar time and the seasons; the shadow is longest on the winter solstice and shortest on the summer solstice.</p>
<p>The invention of the gnomon itself cannot be attributed to Anaximander because its use, as well as the division of days into twelve parts, came from the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonians</a>. It is they, according to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>' <a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)" title="Histories (Herodotus)">Histories</a> (II, 109), who gave the Greeks the art of time measurement. It is likely that he was not the first to determine the solstices, because no calculation is necessary. On the other hand, equinoxes do not correspond to the middle point between the positions during solstices, as the Babylonians thought. As the <i>Suda</i> seems to suggest, it is very likely that with his knowledge of geometry, he became the first Greek to accurately determine the equinoxes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prediction_of_an_earthquake">Prediction of an earthquake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Prediction of an earthquake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his philosophical work <a href="/wiki/De_Divinatione" title="De Divinatione">De Divinatione</a> (I, 50, 112), Cicero states that Anaximander convinced the inhabitants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacedaemon" title="Lacedaemon">Lacedaemon</a> to abandon their city and spend the night in the country with their weapons because an earthquake was near.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The city collapsed when the top of the <a href="/wiki/Taygetus" title="Taygetus">Taygetus</a> split like the stern of a ship. Pliny the Elder also mentions this anecdote (II, 81), suggesting that it came from an "admirable inspiration", as opposed to Cicero, who did not associate the prediction with divination.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaximander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a> in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Western_Philosophy_(Russell)" title="History of Western Philosophy (Russell)">History of Western Philosophy</a></i> interprets Anaximander's theories as an assertion of the necessity of an appropriate balance between earth, fire, and water, all of which may be independently seeking to aggrandize their proportions relative to the others. Anaximander seems to express his belief that a natural order ensures balance between these elements, that where there was fire, ashes (earth) now exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> His Greek peers echoed this sentiment with their belief in natural boundaries beyond which not even the gods could operate.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_in_the_Tragic_Age_of_the_Greeks" title="Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks">Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks</a></i>, claimed that Anaximander was a pessimist who asserted that the primal being of the world was a state of indefiniteness. In accordance with this, anything definite has to eventually pass back into indefiniteness. In other words, Anaximander viewed "...all coming-to-be as though it were an illegitimate emancipation from eternal being, a wrong for which destruction is the only penance". (<i>Ibid.</i>, § 4) The world of individual objects, in this way of thinking, has no worth and should perish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Martin Heidegger</a> lectured extensively on Anaximander, and delivered a lecture entitled "Anaximander's Saying" which was subsequently included in <i>Off the Beaten Track</i>. The lecture examines the ontological difference and the oblivion of Being or <i><a href="/wiki/Dasein" title="Dasein">Dasein</a></i> in the context of the Anaximander fragment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Heidegger's lecture is, in turn, an important influence on the French philosopher <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
