<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Subantarctic</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Subantarctic</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Physiographic_regions_of_the_world" title="Physiographic regions of the world">region</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a>, located immediately north of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> region. This translates roughly to a <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> of between <a href="/wiki/46th_parallel_south" title="46th parallel south">46°</a> – <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_south" title="60th parallel south">60°</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>. The subantarctic region includes many <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> in the southern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, especially those situated north of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Convergence" title="Antarctic Convergence">Antarctic Convergence</a>. <b>Subantarctic glaciers</b> are, by definition, located on islands within the subantarctic region. All <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> are by definition considered to be <a href="/wiki/List_of_glaciers_in_the_Antarctic" title="List of glaciers in the Antarctic">Antarctic glaciers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>subantarctic region</b> comprises two geographic zones and three distinct <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">fronts</a>. The northernmost boundary of the subantarctic region is the rather ill-defined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_Front" title="Subtropical Front">Subtropical Front</a> (<b>STF</b>), also referred to as the Subtropical Convergence. To the south of the STF is a geographic zone, the Subantarctic Zone (<b>SAZ</b>). South of the SAZ is the Subantarctic Front (<b>SAF</b>). South of the SAF is another marine zone, called the Polar Frontal Zone (<b>PFZ</b>). The SAZ and the PFZ together form the subantarctic region. The southernmost boundary of the PFZ (and hence, the southern border of the subantarctic region) is the Antarctic Convergence, located approximately 200 kilometers south of the <a href="/wiki/Polar_front" title="Polar front">Antarctic Polar Front</a> (<b>APF</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2008-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_the_Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current_and_thermohaline_circulation">Influence of the Antarctic Circumpolar Current and thermohaline circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influence of the Antarctic Circumpolar Current and thermohaline circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

In addition to the global <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, the ACC strongly influences regional and global <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.</div>

<p>The Subantarctic Front, found between <a href="/wiki/48th_parallel_south" title="48th parallel south">48°S</a> and <a href="/wiki/58th_parallel_south" title="58th parallel south">58°S</a> in the Indian and Pacific Ocean and between <a href="/wiki/42nd_parallel_south" title="42nd parallel south">42°S</a> and 48°S in the Atlantic Ocean, defines the northern boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a> (or <b>ACC</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The ACC is the most important <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean current</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>, and the only current that flows completely around the Earth. Flowing eastward through the southern portions of the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans, the ACC links these three otherwise separate <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_basin" title="Oceanic basin">oceanic basins</a>. Extending from the sea surface to depths of 2000–4000 meters, and with a width of as great as 2000 kilometers, the ACC transports more water than any other ocean current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The ACC carries up to 150 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">Sverdrups</a> (150 million cubic meters per second), equivalent to 150 times the volume of water flowing in all the world's rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The ACC and the global <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> strongly influence regional and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">global climate</a> as well as underwater <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another factor that contributes to the climate of the subantarctic region, though to a much lesser extent than the thermohaline circulation, is the formation of <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Bottom_Water" title="Antarctic Bottom Water">Antarctic Bottom Water</a> (<b>ABW</b>) by <a href="/wiki/Halothermal_circulation" title="Halothermal circulation">halothermal dynamics</a>. The halothermal circulation is that portion of the global ocean circulation that is driven by global <a href="/wiki/Density_gradient" title="Density gradient">density gradients</a> created by surface heat and <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_subantarctic:_political_versus_scientific">Definition of subantarctic: political versus scientific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definition of subantarctic: political versus scientific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_bottom_water.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="470" data-file-width="745" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Antarctic_bottom_water.svg/220px-Antarctic_bottom_water.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Antarctic_bottom_water.svg/330px-Antarctic_bottom_water.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Antarctic_bottom_water.svg/440px-Antarctic_bottom_water.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_bottom_water.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram showing different <a href="/wiki/Water_mass" title="Water mass">water masses</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Several distinct <a href="/wiki/Water_mass" title="Water mass">water masses</a> converge in the immediate vicinity of the APF or Antarctic Convergence (in particular the <a href="/wiki/Subantarctic_Mode_Water" title="Subantarctic Mode Water">Subantarctic Surface Water</a> (Subantarctic Mode Water or <b>SAMW</b>), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antarctic_Surface_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antarctic Surface Water (page does not exist)">Antarctic Surface Water</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Intermediate_Water" title="Antarctic Intermediate Water">Antarctic Intermediate Water</a>). This convergence creates a unique environment, noted for its very high <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">marine productivity</a>, especially for <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_krill" title="Antarctic krill">Antarctic krill</a>. Because of this, all lands and waters situated south of the Antarctic Convergence are considered to belong to the Antarctic from a <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrological</a> standpoint.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the text of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty_System" title="Antarctic Treaty System">Antarctic Treaty</a>, article VI ("Area covered by Treaty") states: "The provisions of the present Treaty shall apply to the area south of <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_south" title="60th parallel south">60° South latitude</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treaty_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-treaty-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, Antarctica is defined from a <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> standpoint as all land and <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelves</a> south of 60°S latitude.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Tristan da Cunha">Tristan da Cunha group</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_Amsterdam" title="Île Amsterdam">Île Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_Saint-Paul" title="Île Saint-Paul">Île Saint-Paul</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gough_Island" title="Gough Island">Gough Island</a> are all isolated <a href="/wiki/High_island" title="High island">volcanic islands</a> situated at between <a href="/wiki/37th_parallel_south" title="37th parallel south">37°</a> - <a href="/wiki/40th_parallel_south" title="40th parallel south">40°</a> south of the equator, just south of the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">southern Horse latitudes</a>. Because they are located far to the north of the Antarctic Convergence and have a relatively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> climate, they are not typically considered to be subantarctic islands.</p>
<p>At between about <a href="/wiki/46th_parallel_south" title="46th parallel south">46°</a> – <a href="/wiki/50th_parallel_south" title="50th parallel south">50°</a> south of the equator, in the region often referred to as the <i><a href="/wiki/Roaring_Forties" title="Roaring Forties">Roaring Forties</a></i>, are the <a href="/wiki/Crozet_Islands" title="Crozet Islands">Crozet Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Islands" title="Prince Edward Islands">Prince Edward Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bounty_Islands" title="Bounty Islands">Bounty Islands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snares_Islands" title="Snares Islands">Snares Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antipodes_Islands" title="Antipodes Islands">Antipodes Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Islands" title="Auckland Islands">Auckland Islands</a>. These islands, all located approximately on the Antarctic Convergence (roughly the northern boundary of the subantarctic region), are properly considered to be subantarctic islands.</p>
<p>At between <a href="/wiki/51st_parallel_south" title="51st parallel south">51°</a> – <a href="/wiki/56th_parallel_south" title="56th parallel south">56°</a> south of the equator, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_los_Estados" title="Isla de los Estados">Isla de los Estados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ildefonso_Islands" title="Ildefonso Islands">Ildefonso Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diego_Ram%C3%ADrez_Islands" title="Diego Ramírez Islands">Diego Ramírez Islands</a>, and other islands associated with <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a>, lie north of the Antarctic Convergence in the region often referred to as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furious_Fifties" title="Furious Fifties">Furious Fifties</a></i>. Unlike other subantarctic islands, these islands have <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_and_Saint-Paul_Islands_temperate_grasslands" title="Amsterdam and Saint-Paul Islands temperate grasslands">temperate grasslands</a> (mostly <i><a href="/wiki/Poa_flabellata" title="Poa flabellata">tussac grass</a></i>), and even <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a>. They also lack <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow_line" title="Snow line">permanent snow</a> and ice at their lowest elevations. Despite their more southerly location, it is debatable whether these islands should be considered as such because their climate and geography differs significantly from other subantarctic islands.</p>
<p>At between 52° – 57° south of the equator, <a href="/wiki/Campbell_Island_group" title="Campbell Island group">Campbell Island group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heard_Island_and_McDonald_Islands" title="Heard Island and McDonald Islands">Heard Island and McDonald Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bouvet_Island" title="Bouvet Island">Bouvet Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_and_the_South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Georgia and the South Sandwich Islands">South Georgia Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Island" title="Macquarie Island">Macquarie Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_and_the_South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Georgia and the South Sandwich Islands">South Sandwich Islands</a> are also located in the Furious Fifties. The geography of these islands is characterized by tundra, <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>. These islands are situated south of the Antarctic Convergence, but north of <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_south" title="60th parallel south">60°S latitude</a> (the continental limit according to the Antarctic Treaty).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treaty_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-treaty-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, despite their being located significantly south of the Antarctic Convergence, they should still be considered to be subantarctic islands by virtue of their location north of the 60° latitude.</p>
<p>At between 60° – 69° south of the equator, the <a href="/wiki/South_Orkney_Islands" title="South Orkney Islands">South Orkney Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balleny_Islands" title="Balleny Islands">Balleny Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Island" title="Scott Island">Scott Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_Island" title="Peter I Island">Peter I Island</a> are all properly considered to be Antarctic islands for the following three reasons:</p>
<ol>
<li>they are all located south of the Antarctic Convergence</li>
<li>they are all located within the Southern (or Antarctic) Ocean</li>
<li>they are all located south of the 60° latitude (in the region often referred to as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrieking_Sixties" title="Shrieking Sixties">Shrieking Sixties</a></i>)</li>
</ol>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subantarctic_glaciers">Subantarctic glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subantarctic glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_climate_change_on_SAMW">Impact of climate change on SAMW</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Impact of climate change on SAMW">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="505" data-file-width="720" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/300px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/450px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/600px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Air-sea exchange of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Together, the Subantarctic Mode Water (SAMW) and Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW) act as a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a>, absorbing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">atmospheric carbon dioxide</a> and storing it in solution. If the SAMW temperature increases as a result of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, the SAMW will have less capacity to store dissolved carbon dioxide. Research using a computerized climate system model suggests that if atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration were to increase to 860 ppm by the year 2100 (roughly double today's concentration), the SAMW will decrease in density and salinity. The resulting reductions in the subduction and transport capacity of SAMW and AAIW water masses could potentially decrease the absorption and storage of CO<sub>2</sub> in the Southern Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-down2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-down2009-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i>Main: <a href="/wiki/Category:Flora_of_subantarctic_islands" title="Category:Flora of subantarctic islands">Category: Flora of subantarctic islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Category:Fauna_of_subantarctic_islands" title="Category:Fauna of subantarctic islands">Category: Fauna of subantarctic islands</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_ecozone" title="Antarctic ecozone">Antarctic ecozone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Floristic_Kingdom" title="Antarctic Floristic Kingdom">Antarctic Floristic Kingdom</a> include most of the subantarctic islands native <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>, with many <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subantarctic_island_example">Subantarctic island example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Subantarctic island example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Flora_of_Heard_Island_and_McDonald_Islands" title="Category:Flora of Heard Island and McDonald Islands">Flora of Heard Island and McDonald Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Category:Fauna_of_Heard_Island_and_McDonald_Islands" title="Category:Fauna of Heard Island and McDonald Islands">Fauna of Heard Island and McDonald Islands</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The physical landscape and biota communities of <a href="/wiki/Heard_Island_and_McDonald_Islands" title="Heard Island and McDonald Islands">Heard Island and McDonald Islands</a> are constantly changing due to <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, strong winds and waves, and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Volcanic activity has been observed in this area since the mid-1980s, with fresh <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> flows on the southwest flanks of Heard Island. <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">Satellite imagery</a> shows that McDonald Island increased in size from about 1 to 2.5 square kilometers between 1994 and 2004, as a result of volcanic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-aad2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to new land being produced by volcanism, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> of the climate is causing the <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">retreat of glaciers</a> on the islands (<i>see section below</i> ). These combined processes produce new ice-free <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion#Terrestrial" title="Ecoregion">terrestrial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion#Freshwater" title="Ecoregion">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, which are now available for colonization by plants and animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad2008_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-aad2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heard Island has vast colonies of <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a>, and large harems of land-based marine predators such as <a href="/wiki/Elephant_seal" title="Elephant seal">elephant seals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fur_seal" title="Fur seal">fur seals</a>. Due to the very high numbers of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammals</a> on Heard Island, the area is considered a "biological hot spot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad2008_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-aad2008-10">[10]</a></sup> The marine environment surrounding the islands features diverse and distinctive <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic habitats</a> that support a range of species including <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">sponges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>. This marine environment also serves as a nursery area for a range of fishes, including some species of commercial interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad2008_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-aad2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retreat_of_subantarctic_glaciers">Retreat of subantarctic glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Retreat of subantarctic glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Retreat of glaciers since 1850</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">Glacier mass balance</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="992" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif/300px-San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif/450px-San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif/600px-San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Retreat of <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael_Glacier" title="San Rafael Glacier">San Rafael Glacier</a> from 1990 to 2000. <a href="/wiki/San_Quint%C3%ADn_Glacier" title="San Quintín Glacier">San Quintín Glacier</a> is shown in the background</div>

<p>Glaciers are currently <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">retreating</a> at significant rates throughout the southern hemisphere. With respect to glaciers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andes_mountains" title="Andes mountains">Andes mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, abundant evidence has been collected from ongoing research at <a href="/wiki/Nevado_del_Ruiz" title="Nevado del Ruiz">Nevado del Ruiz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Major1989_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Major1989-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hug2007_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hug2007-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quelccaya_Ice_Cap" title="Quelccaya Ice Cap">Quelccaya Ice Cap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qori_Kalis_Glacier" title="Qori Kalis Glacier">Qori Kalis Glacier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peru_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-peru-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quel_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-quel-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zongo_Glacier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zongo Glacier (page does not exist)">Zongo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chacaltaya" title="Chacaltaya">Chacaltaya</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charquini_Glacier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charquini Glacier (page does not exist)">Charquini</a> glaciers in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glac2001_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-glac2001-15">[15]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Aconcagua_River" title="Aconcagua River">Aconcagua River</a> Basin in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountains_in_Chile" title="List of mountains in Chile">central Chilean Andes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Patagonian_Ice_Field" title="Northern Patagonian Ice Field">Northern Patagonian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Patagonian_Ice_Field" title="Southern Patagonian Ice Field">Southern Patagonian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_field" title="Ice field">ice fields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amos2004_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-amos2004-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masio2009_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Masio2009-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EORC_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-EORC-19">[19]</a></sup> Retreat of glaciers in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and Antarctica is also well documented.</p>
<p>Bearing this in mind, it should come as no surprise that many subantarctic glaciers are also in retreat. <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">Mass balance</a> is significantly negative on many glaciers on Kergeulen Island, Heard Island, South Georgia and Bouvet Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddell_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddell-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciers_of_Heard_Island">Glaciers of Heard Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subantarctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Glaciers of Heard Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Heard Island is a heavily glacierized, subantarctic <a href="/wiki/High_island" title="High island">volcanic island</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>, roughly 4000 kilometers southwest of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. 80% of the island is covered in ice, with glaciers descending from 2400 meters to <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup> Due to the steep <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> of Heard Island, most of its glaciers are relatively thin (averaging only about 55 meters in depth).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddell_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddell-22">[22]</a></sup> The presence of glaciers on Heard Island provides an excellent opportunity to measure the rate of glacial retreat as an indicator of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad2008_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-aad2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Available records show no apparent change in <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">glacier mass balance</a> between 1874 and 1929. Between 1949 and 1954, marked changes were observed to have occurred in the ice formations above 5000 feet on the southwestern slopes of <a href="/wiki/Big_Ben_(Heard_Island)" title="Big Ben (Heard Island)">Big Ben</a>, possibly as a result of <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanic activity</a>. By 1963, major recession was obvious below 2000 feet on almost all glaciers, and minor recession was evident as high as 5000 feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budd1970_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Budd1970-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Retreat of glacier fronts across Heard Island is evident when comparing <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photographs</a> taken in December 1947 with those taken on a return visit in early 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thost2008_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thost2008-24">[24]</a></sup> Retreat of Heard Island glaciers is most dramatic on the eastern section of the island, where the <a href="/wiki/Glacier_terminus" title="Glacier terminus">termini</a> of former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidewater_glacier" title="Tidewater glacier">tidewater glaciers</a> are now located inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup> Glaciers on the northern and western coasts have narrowed significantly, while the area of glaciers and <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a> on <a href="/wiki/Laurens_Peninsula" title="Laurens Peninsula">Laurens Peninsula</a> have shrunk by 30% - 65%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddell_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddell-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the time period between 1947 and 1988, the total area of Heard Island's glaciers decreased by 11%, from 288 km² (roughly 79% of the total area of Heard Island) to only 257 km².<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddell_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddell-22">[22]</a></sup> A visit to the island in the spring of 2000 found that the <a href="/wiki/Stephenson_Glacier" title="Stephenson Glacier">Stephenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown_Glacier" title="Brown Glacier">Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baudissin_Glacier" title="Baudissin Glacier">Baudissin</a> glaciers, among others, had retreated even further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddell_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddell-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thost2008_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thost2008-24">[24]</a></sup> The terminus of Brown Glacier has retreated approximately 1.1 kilometres since 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad2008_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-aad2008-10">[10]</a></sup> The total ice-covered area of Brown Glacier is estimated to have decreased by roughly 29% between 1947 and 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thost2008_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thost2008-24">[24]</a></sup> This degree of loss of glacier mass is consistent with the measured increase in temperature of +0.9 °C over that time span.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thost2008_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thost2008-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The coastal ice cliffs of Brown Glacier and Stephenson Glacier, which in 1954 were over 50 feet high, had disappeared by 1963 when the glaciers terminated as much as 100 yards inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budd1970_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Budd1970-23">[23]</a></sup> Baudissin Glacier on the north coast, and <a href="/wiki/Vahsel_Glacier" title="Vahsel Glacier">Vahsel Glacier</a> on the west coast have lost at least 100 and 200 vertical feet of ice, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budd1970_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Budd1970-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Winston_Glacier" title="Winston Glacier">Winston Glacier</a>, which retreated approximately one mile between 1947 and 1963, appears to be a very sensitive indicator of glacier change on the island. The young moraines flanking <a href="/wiki/Winston_Lagoon" title="Winston Lagoon">Winston Lagoon</a> show that Winston Glacier has lost at least 300 vertical feet of ice within a recent time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budd1970_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Budd1970-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The glaciers of Laurens Peninsula, whose maximum elevation is only 500 m above sea level, are smaller and shorter than most of the other Heard Island glaciers, and therefore much more sensitive to temperature effects. Accordingly, their total area has decreased by over 30 percent. <a href="/wiki/Jacka_Glacier" title="Jacka Glacier">Jacka Glacier</a> on the east coast of Laurens Peninsula has also demonstrated marked recession since 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budd1970_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Budd1970-23">[23]</a></sup> In the early 1950s, Jacka Glacier had receded only slightly from its position in the late 1920s, but by 1997 it had receded about 700 m back from the coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddell_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddell-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quil2000_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quil2000-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budd2000_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Budd2000-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Possible causes of glacier recession on Heard Island include:</p>
<ol>
<li>Volcanic activity</li>
<li>Southward movement of the Antarctic Convergence: such a movement conceivably might cause glacier retreat through a rise in sea and air temperatures</li>
<li>Climatic change</li>
</ol>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Antarctic_Division" title="Australian Antarctic Division">Australian Antarctic Division</a> conducted an expedition to Heard Island during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austral_summer" title="Austral summer">austral summer</a> of 2003-04. A small team of scientists spent two months on the island, conducting studies on <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">avian</a> and terrestrial biology and glaciology. Glaciologists conducted further research on the Brown Glacier, in an effort to determine whether glacial retreat is rapid or punctuated. Using a portable <a href="/wiki/Echo_sounding" title="Echo sounding">echo sounder</a>, the team took measurements of the volume of the glacier. Monitoring of climatic conditions continued, with an emphasis on the impact of <a href="/wiki/Foehn_wind" title="Foehn wind">Foehn winds</a> on glacier mass balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heard_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-heard-27">[27]</a></sup> Based on the findings of that expedition, the rate of loss of glacier ice on Heard Island appears to be accelerating. Between 2000 and 2003, repeat <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">surface surveys</a> revealed that the rate of loss of ice in both the <a href="/wiki/Ablation_zone" title="Ablation zone">ablation zone</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Accumulation_zone" title="Accumulation zone">accumulation zone</a> of Brown Glacier was more than double average rate measured from 1947 to 2003. The increase in the rate of ice loss suggests that the glaciers of Heard Island are reacting to ongoing climate change, rather than approaching <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">dynamic equilibrium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thost2008_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Thost2008-24">[24]</a></sup> The retreat of Heard Island's glaciers is expected to continue for the foreseeable future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allis1986_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-Allis1986-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
