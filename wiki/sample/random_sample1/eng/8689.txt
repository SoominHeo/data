<p><b>Anuket</b> was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">personification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_mythology" title="Egyptian mythology">Egyptian mythology</a> in <a href="/wiki/Elephantine" title="Elephantine">Elephantine</a>, at the start of the Nile's journey through <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, and in nearby regions of <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anuket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Ancient Egyptian</a>, she was known as Anuket, <b>Anaka</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>Anqet</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Her name meant the "Clasper" or "Embracer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-1">[1]</a></sup> In Greek, this became <b>Anoukis</b> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ανουκις</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-1">[1]</a></sup> sometimes also spelled <b>Anukis</b>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Interpretatio_graeca" title="Interpretatio graeca">interpretatio graeca</a></i>, she was considered equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Hestia" title="Hestia">Hestia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vesta_(mythology)" title="Vesta (mythology)">Vesta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depictions">Depictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anuket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Depictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anuket was usually depicted as a woman with a headdress of either reed or ostrich feathers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinch_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pinch-3">[3]</a></sup> (thought by most egyptologists to be a detail deriving from Nubia).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> She was usually depicted as holding a <a href="/wiki/Sceptre" title="Sceptre">Sceptre</a> topped with an <a href="/wiki/Ankh" title="Ankh">ankh</a>, and her sacred animal was the gazelle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> She was also shown <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">suckling</a> the Pharoah through the <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">New Kingdom</a> and a goddess of lust in later years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> In later periods, she was assoiciated with the <a href="/wiki/Cowry" title="Cowry">Cowry</a>, especially the shell, which resembled the vagina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_.26_Roles">History &amp; Roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anuket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History &amp; Roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was originally the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>, but was always related to <a href="/wiki/Satet" title="Satet">Satet</a> in some way. For example, both goddesses were called the "<a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Ra" title="Eye of Ra">Eye of Ra</a>", along with <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hathor" title="Hathor">Hathor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sekhmet" title="Sekhmet">Sekhmet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> Also, they were both related in some way to the <a href="/wiki/Uraeus" title="Uraeus">Uraeus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worship">Worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anuket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anuket was part of a triad with the god <a href="/wiki/Khnum" title="Khnum">Khnum</a>, and the goddess <a href="/wiki/Satet" title="Satet">Satis</a>. She may have been the sister of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Satet" title="Satet">Satis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinch_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pinch-3">[3]</a></sup> or she may have been a junior consort to Khnum instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinch_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pinch-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A temple dedicated to Anuket was erected on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seheil" title="Seheil">Island of Seheil</a>. Inscriptions show that a shrine or altar was dedicated to her at this site by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Thirteenth dynasty of Egypt">13th dynasty</a> <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">Pharaoh</a> <a href="/wiki/Sobekhotep_III" title="Sobekhotep III">Sobekhotep III</a>. Much later, during the 18th dynasty, <a href="/wiki/Amenhotep_II" title="Amenhotep II">Amenhotep II</a> dedicated a chapel to the goddess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the New Kingdom, Anuket’s cult at <a href="/wiki/Elephantine" title="Elephantine">Elephantine</a> included a river procession of the goddess during the first month of Shemu. Inscriptions mention the processional festival of Khnum and Anuket during this time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ceremonially, when the Nile started its annual flood, the <i><b>Festival of Anuket</b></i> began. People threw coins, gold, jewelry, and precious gifts into the river, in thanks for the life-giving water and returning benefits derived from the wealth provided by her fertility to the goddess. The <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a> held in several parts of Egypt, against eating certain <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> which were considered sacred, was lifted during this time, suggesting that a fish species of the Nile was a <a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">totem</a> for Anuket and that they were consumed as part of the <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> of her major religious festival.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
