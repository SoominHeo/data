<p>The <b>CN Tower</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>Tour CN</i></span>) is a 553.3 m-high (1,815.3 ft) <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> communications and observation <a href="/wiki/Tower" title="Tower">tower</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Toronto" title="Downtown Toronto">downtown</a> <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skyscraper_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-skyscraper-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup> Built on the former <a href="/wiki/Railway_Lands" title="Railway Lands">Railway Lands</a>, it was completed in 1976, becoming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_freestanding_structures_in_the_world" title="List of tallest freestanding structures in the world">world's tallest free-standing structure</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_towers_in_the_world" title="List of tallest towers in the world">world's tallest tower</a> at the time. It held both records for 34 years until the completion of <a href="/wiki/Burj_Khalifa" title="Burj Khalifa">Burj Khalifa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canton_Tower" title="Canton Tower">Canton Tower</a> in 2010. It is now the third tallest tower in the world and remains the tallest free-standing structure in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>, a signature icon of Toronto's skyline, and a symbol of Canada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> attracting more than two million international visitors annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-great_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-great-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FACTS_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FACTS-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its name "CN" originally referred to <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National</a>, the railway company that built the tower. Following the railway's decision to <a href="/wiki/Divestment" title="Divestment">divest</a> non-core freight railway assets, prior to the company's <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> in 1995, it transferred the tower to the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Lands_Company" title="Canada Lands Company">Canada Lands Company</a>, a federal <a href="/wiki/Crown_corporations_of_Canada" title="Crown corporations of Canada">Crown corporation</a> responsible for real estate development. Since the name <i>CN Tower</i> became common in daily usage, the abbreviation was eventually expanded to <b>Canadian National Tower</b> or <b>Canada's National Tower</b>. However, neither of these names is commonly used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CN_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CN-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, the CN Tower was declared one of the modern Seven <a href="/wiki/Wonders_of_the_World" title="Wonders of the World">Wonders of the World</a> by the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a>. It also belongs to the World Federation of Great Towers, where it holds second-place ranking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-info_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-info-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-great_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-great-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The idea of the CN Tower originated in 1968 when the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National Railway</a> wanted to build a large TV and radio communication platform to serve the Toronto area, as well as demonstrate the strength of Canadian industry and CN in particular. These plans evolved over the next few years, and the project became official in 1972. The tower would have been part of Metro Centre (see <a href="/wiki/CityPlace,_Toronto" title="CityPlace, Toronto">CityPlace</a>), a large development south of <a href="/wiki/Front_Street_(Toronto)" title="Front Street (Toronto)">Front Street</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Railway_Lands" title="Railway Lands">Railway Lands</a>, a large railway switching yard that was being made redundant by newer yards outside the city. Key project team members were NCK Engineering as structural engineer; <a href="/wiki/John_Andrews_(architect)" title="John Andrews (architect)">John Andrews Architects</a>; Webb, Zerafa, Menkes, Housden Architects; Foundation Building Construction; and Canron (Eastern Structural Division).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skyscraper_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-skyscraper-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emporis_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-emporis-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>As Toronto grew rapidly during the late 1960s and early 1970s, multiple skyscrapers were constructed in the downtown core, most notably <a href="/wiki/First_Canadian_Place" title="First Canadian Place">First Canadian Place</a>. The reflective nature of the new buildings compromised the quality of broadcast signals necessitating new, higher antennas that were at least 300 m (980 ft) tall. At the time, most data communications took place over <a href="/wiki/Microwave#Communication" title="Microwave">point-to-point microwave</a> links, whose dish antennae covered the roofs of large buildings. As each new skyscraper was added to the downtown, former line-of-sight links were no longer possible. CN intended to rent "hub" space for microwave links, visible from almost any building in the Toronto area. The CN Tower can be seen from at least as far away as Kennedy Street in <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Ontario" title="Aurora, Ontario">Aurora, Ontario</a>, approximately 40 km (25 mi) to the north, 60 km (37 mi) east of Toronto, in <a href="/wiki/Oshawa" title="Oshawa">Oshawa</a>, and from several points on the south shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>, 48 km (30 mi) to the south in the U.S. state of New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-great_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-great-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original plan for the tower envisioned a tripod consisting of three independent cylindrical "pillars" linked at various heights by structural bridges. Had it been built, this design would have been considerably shorter, with the metal antenna located roughly where the concrete section between the main level and the SkyPod lies today. As the design effort continued, it evolved into the current design with a single continuous hexagonal core to the SkyPod, with three support legs blended into the hexagon below the main level, forming a large Y-shape structure at the ground level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The idea for the main level in its current form evolved around this time, but the Space Deck (now named SkyPod) was not part of the plans until some time later. One engineer in particular felt that visitors would feel the higher observation deck would be worth paying extra for, and the costs in terms of construction were not prohibitive. It was also some time around this point that it was realized that the tower could become the world's tallest structure, and plans were changed to incorporate subtle modifications throughout the structure to this end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CNTowerLong.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/CNTowerLong.jpg/220px-CNTowerLong.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/CNTowerLong.jpg/330px-CNTowerLong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/CNTowerLong.jpg/440px-CNTowerLong.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CNTowerLong.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The CN Tower as seen from its base</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Construction on the CN Tower began on February 6, 1973, with massive excavations at the tower base for the foundation. By the time the foundation was complete, 56,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">t</a> (61,729 <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short tons</a>; 55,116 <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> were removed to a depth of 15 m (49.2 ft) in the centre, and a base incorporating 7,000 m<sup>3</sup> (9,156 cu yd) of concrete with 450 t (496 short tons; 443 long tons) of <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a> and 36 t (40 short tons; 35 long tons) of <a href="/wiki/Wire_rope" title="Wire rope">steel cable</a> had been built to a thickness of 6.7 m (22.0 ft). This portion of the construction was fairly rapid, with only four months needed between the start and the foundation being ready for construction on top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>To build the main support pillar, a hydraulically raised <a href="/wiki/Slip_forming" title="Slip forming">slipform</a> was built at the base. This was a fairly impressive engineering feat on its own, consisting of a large metal platform that raised itself on jacks at about 6 m (19.7 ft) per day as the concrete below set. Concrete was poured continuously by a team of 1,532 people until February 22, 1974, during which it had already become the tallest structure in Canada, surpassing the recently built <a href="/wiki/Inco_Superstack" title="Inco Superstack">Inco Superstack</a> in Sudbury, which was built using similar methods. In total, the tower contains 40,500 m<sup>3</sup> (52,972 cu yd) of concrete, all of which was mixed on-site in order to ensure batch consistency. Through the pour, the vertical accuracy of the tower was maintained by comparing the slip form's location to massive <a href="/wiki/Plumb_bob" title="Plumb bob">plumb bobs</a> hanging from it, observed by small telescopes from the ground. Over the height of the tower, it varies from true vertical accuracy by only 29 mm (1.1 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1974, construction of the main level commenced. Using 45 hydraulic jacks attached to cables strung from a temporary steel crown anchored to the top of the tower, twelve giant steel and wooden bracket forms were slowly raised, ultimately taking about a week to crawl up to their final position. These forms were used to create the brackets that support the main level, as well as a base for the construction of the main level itself. The Space Deck (currently named SkyPod) was built of concrete poured into a wooden frame attached to rebar at the lower level deck, and then reinforced with a large steel compression band around the outside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The antenna was originally to be raised by crane as well, but during construction the <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_S-64_Skycrane" title="Sikorsky S-64 Skycrane">Sikorsky S-64 Skycrane</a> helicopter became available when the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> sold off theirs to civilian operators. The helicopter, named "Olga", was first used to remove the crane, and then flew the antenna up in 36 sections. The flights of the antenna pieces were a minor tourist attraction of their own, and the schedule was printed in the local newspapers. Use of the helicopter saved months of construction time, with this phase taking only three and a half weeks instead of the planned six months. The tower was topped off on April 2, 1975, after 26 months of construction, officially capturing the height record from <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ostankino_Tower" title="Ostankino Tower">Ostankino Tower</a>, and bringing the total mass to 118,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">t</a> (130,073 <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short tons</a>; 116,136 <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a>).</p>
<p>Two years into the construction, plans for Metro Centre were scrapped, leaving the tower isolated on the Railway Lands in what was then a largely abandoned light-industrial space. This caused serious problems for tourists to access the tower. Ned Baldwin, project architect with John Andrews, wrote at the time that "All of the logic which dictated the design of the lower accommodation has been upset," and that "Under such ludicrous circumstances Canadian National would hardly have chosen this location to build."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening">Opening</span></h3>
<p>The CN Tower opened to the public on June 26, 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The construction costs of approximately <span style="white-space: nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CA$</a>63 million</span> ($257 million in 2017 dollars)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-CA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-CA-16">[16]</a></sup> were repaid in fifteen years. <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National Railway</a> sold the tower prior to taking the company private in 1995, when they decided to divest themselves of all operations not directly related to their core freight shipping businesses.</p>
<p>From the mid-1970s to the mid-1980s, the CN Tower was practically the only development along Front Street West; it was still possible to see Lake Ontario from the foot of the CN Tower due to the expansive parking lots and lack of development in the area at the time. As the area around the tower was developed, particularly with the completion of the <a href="/wiki/Metro_Toronto_Convention_Centre" title="Metro Toronto Convention Centre">Metro Toronto Convention Centre</a> in 1984 and the SkyDome in 1989 (renamed <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Centre" title="Rogers Centre">Rogers Centre</a> in 2005), the former <a href="/wiki/Railway_Lands" title="Railway Lands">Railway Lands</a> were redeveloped and the tower became the centre of a newly developing entertainment area. Access was greatly improved with the construction of the <a href="/wiki/SkyWalk" title="SkyWalk">SkyWalk</a> in 1989, which connected the tower and SkyDome to the nearby <a href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Toronto)" title="Union Station (Toronto)">Union Station</a> railway and subway station, and, in turn, to the city's <a href="/wiki/PATH_(Toronto)" title="PATH (Toronto)">PATH</a> underground pedestrian system. By the mid-1990s, it was the centre of a thriving tourist district. The entire area continues to be an area of intense building, notably a boom in <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominium</a> construction in the first two decades of the 21st century, as well as the 2013 opening of the <a href="/wiki/Ripley%27s_Aquarium_of_Canada" title="Ripley's Aquarium of Canada">Ripley's Aquarium</a> by the base of the tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-great_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-great-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="720" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg/220px-2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg/330px-2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg/440px-2015_Toronto_Pan_American_Games-Laslovarga.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Fireworks display on the CN Tower during <a href="/wiki/2015_Pan_American_Games_opening_ceremony" title="2015 Pan American Games opening ceremony">the opening ceremony</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2015_Pan_American_Games" title="2015 Pan American Games">2015 Pan American Games</a>.</div>
</div>

<ul>
<li>When the CN Tower opened in 1976, there were three public observation points: the SkyPod (then known as the Space Deck) that stands at 447 m (1,467 ft), the Indoor Observation Level (now named Indoor Lookout Level) at 346 m (1,135 ft), and the Outdoor Observation Terrace (at the same level as the Glass Floor) at 342 m (1,122 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-great_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-great-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup> One floor above the Indoor Observation Level was the Top of Toronto Restaurant, which completed a revolution once every 90 minutes.</li>
<li>Stuntman <a href="/wiki/Dar_Robinson" title="Dar Robinson">Dar Robinson</a> jumped off of the CN Tower on two occasions in 1979 and 1980. The first was for a scene from the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Highpoint_(film)" title="Highpoint (film)">Highpoint</a></i>, in which Robinson received $250,000 for the stunt. The second was for a personal documentary. Both stunts used a wire decelerator attached to his back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robinson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-robinson-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>On June 26, 1986, the ten-year anniversary of the tower's opening, <a href="/wiki/Tower_block" title="Tower block">high-rise</a> firefighting and rescue advocate <a href="/wiki/Dan_Goodwin" title="Dan Goodwin">Dan Goodwin</a>, in a sponsored publicity event, used his hands and feet to climb the outside of the tower, a feat he performed twice on the same day. Following both ascents, he used multiple rappels to descend to the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Glass Floor, at an elevation of 342 m (1,122 ft), opened to the public on June 26, 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>From 1997 to January 2004, <a href="/wiki/The_Hahn_Company" title="The Hahn Company">TrizecHahn Corporation</a> managed the tower and instituted several expansion projects including a <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">C$</a>26 million entertainment expansion and revitalization that included the 1997 addition of two new elevators (to a total of six) and the consequential relocation of the staircase from the north side leg to inside the core of the building, a conversion that also added nine stairs to the climb. TrizecHahn also owned the <a href="/wiki/Willis_Tower" title="Willis Tower">Willis Tower</a> (Sears Tower at the time) in Chicago approximately at the same time.</li>
<li>In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diode</a> (LED) lights replaced the incandescent lights that lit the CN Tower at night, the reason cited that LED lights are more cost and energy efficient than the incandescent lights. The colour of the LED lights can change, compared to the constant white colour of the incandescent lights.</li>
<li>On September 12, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Burj_Khalifa" title="Burj Khalifa">Burj Khalifa</a>, then under construction, surpassed the CN Tower as the world's tallest free-standing structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBCSurpassed_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBCSurpassed-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2008, glass panels were installed in one of the CN Tower elevators, which established a world record (346 m) for highest glass floor panelled elevator in the world.</li>
<li>The CN Tower was closed during the <a href="/wiki/2010_G20_Toronto_summit" title="2010 G20 Toronto summit">G20 summit</a> on June 26–27, 2010, for security reasons, given its proximity to the Metro Toronto Convention Centre.</li>
<li>In 2011, EdgeWalk (where at an elevation of 356 metres (1,168 ft), visitors tethered to an overhead rail system can walk on the roof of the main pod) opened to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>On July 10, 2015, the CN Tower was incorporated into <a href="/wiki/2015_Pan_American_Games_opening_ceremony" title="2015 Pan American Games opening ceremony">the opening ceremony</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2015_Pan_American_Games" title="2015 Pan American Games">2015 Pan American Games</a>. A pre-recorded segment featured <a href="/wiki/Bruny_Surin" title="Bruny Surin">Bruny Surin</a> passing the flame to <a href="/wiki/Donovan_Bailey" title="Donovan Bailey">Donovan Bailey</a> on the EdgeWalk and parachuting into Rogers Centre. A fireworks display off the tower served as the ceremony's finale.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Falling_ice_danger">Falling ice danger</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg/170px-CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg/255px-CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg/340px-CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CN_Tower_seen_from_its_base.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
CN Tower as seen from its base.</div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a> storm on March 2, 2007, resulted in a layer of ice several centimetres thick forming on the side of the tower and other downtown buildings. The sun thawed the ice, and winds of up to 90 km/h (56 mph) blew some of it away from the structure. There were fears that cars and windows of nearby buildings would be smashed by large chunks of ice. In response, police closed some streets surrounding the tower. During morning rush hour on March 5 of the same year, police expanded the area of closed streets to include the <a href="/wiki/Gardiner_Expressway" title="Gardiner Expressway">Gardiner Expressway</a> 310 metres (1,017 ft) away from the tower, as increased winds blew the ice farther away, as far north as <a href="/wiki/King_Street_(Toronto)" title="King Street (Toronto)">King Street</a>, 490 m (1,608 ft) away, where a <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxicab</a> window was shattered.</p>
<p>Subsequently, on March 6, 2007, the Gardiner Expressway was reopened after winds died down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The CN Tower consists of several substructures. The main portion of the tower is a hollow concrete hexagonal pillar containing the <a href="/wiki/Stairs" title="Stairs">stairwells</a> and power and plumbing connections. The Tower's six <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevators</a> are located in the three inverted angles created by the Tower's hexagonal shape (two elevators per angle). Each of the three elevator shafts is lined with glass, allowing for views of the city as the glass-windowed elevators make their way up the Tower. The stairwell was originally located in one of these angles (the one facing north), but was moved into the central hollow of the Tower; the Tower's new fifth and sixth elevators were placed in the hexagonal angle that once contained the stairwell. On top of the main concrete portion of the Tower is a 102-metre (334.6 ft) tall metal broadcast antenna, carrying TV and radio signals. There are three visitor areas: the Glass Floor and Outdoor Observation Terrace which are both located at an elevation of 342 metres (1,122 ft), the Indoor Lookout Level (formerly known as "Indoor Observation Level") located at 346 metres (1,135 ft), and the higher SkyPod (formerly known as "Space Deck") at 446.5 metres (1,465 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> just below the metal <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antenna</a>. The hexagonal shape can be seen between the two areas; however, below the main deck, three large supporting legs give the tower the appearance of a large <a href="/wiki/Tripod" title="Tripod">tripod</a>.</p>
<p>The main deck level is seven storeys, some of which are open to the public. Below the public areas — at 338 m (1,108.9 ft) — is a large white donut-shaped <a href="/wiki/Radome" title="Radome">radome</a> containing the structure's microwave receivers. The <a href="/wiki/Glass_floor" title="Glass floor">glass floor</a> and outdoor observation deck are at 342 metres (1,122.0 ft). The glass floor has an area of 24 m<sup>2</sup> (258 sq ft) and can withstand a pressure of 4,100 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kilopascals</a> (595 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>). The floor's thermal glass units are 64 mm (2.5 in) thick, consisting of a pane of 25-millimetre (1.0 in) laminated glass, 25 mm (1.0 in) airspace and a pane of 13 mm (0.5 in) laminated glass. Some people experience <a href="/wiki/Acrophobia" title="Acrophobia">acrophobia</a> when standing on the glass floor and looking down at the ground 342 m (1,122.0 ft) below. In 2008, one elevator was upgraded to add a glass floor panel, believed to have the highest vertical rise of any elevator equipped with this feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Horizons Cafe and the lookout level are at 346 metres (1,135.2 ft). The 360 Restaurant, a <a href="/wiki/Revolving_restaurant" title="Revolving restaurant">revolving restaurant</a> that completes a full rotation once every 72 minutes, is at 351 m (1,151.6 ft). When the tower first opened, it also featured a disco named Sparkles (at the Indoor Observation Level), billed as the highest disco and dance floor in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SkyPod was once the highest public <a href="/wiki/Observation_deck" title="Observation deck">observation deck</a> in the world until it was surpassed by the <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_World_Financial_Center" title="Shanghai World Financial Center">Shanghai World Financial Center</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swfc-e_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-swfc-e-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A metal staircase reaches the main deck level after 1,776 steps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-great_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-great-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSTEPS_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNSTEPS-25">[25]</a></sup> and the SkyPod 100 m (328 ft) above after 2,579 steps; it is the tallest metal staircase on Earth. These stairs are intended for emergency use only and are not open to the public, except for two times per year for charity stair-climb events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFClimb_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWFClimb-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFClimb_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWFClimb-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorontoStarSteps_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-TorontoStarSteps-27">[27]</a></sup> The average climber takes approximately 30 minutes to climb to the base of the radome, but the fastest climb on record is 7 minutes and 52 seconds in 1989 by Brendan Keenoy, an <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Provincial_Police" title="Ontario Provincial Police">Ontario Provincial Police</a> officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorontoStarSteps_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-TorontoStarSteps-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2002, Canadian Olympian and <a href="/wiki/Paralympic_Games" title="Paralympic Games">Paralympic</a> champion <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Adams" title="Jeff Adams">Jeff Adams</a> climbed the stairs of the tower in a specially designed <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair" title="Wheelchair">wheelchair</a>. The stairs were originally on one of the three sides of the tower (facing north), with a glass view, but these were later replaced with the third elevator pair and the stairs were moved to the inside of the core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSTEPS_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNSTEPS-25">[25]</a></sup> Top climbs on the new, windowless stairwell used since around 2003 have generally been over ten minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

</div>

<p>On August 1, 2011, the CN Tower opened the EdgeWalk, an amusement in which thrill-seekers can walk on and around the roof of the main pod of the tower at 356 m (1,168.0 ft), which is directly above the 360 Restaurant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It is the world's highest full-circle, hands-free walk. Visitors are tethered to an overhead rail system and walk around the edge of the CN Tower's main pod above the 360 Restaurant on a 1.5 m (4.9 ft) metal floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The attraction is closed throughout the winter season and during periods of electrical storms and high winds.</p>
<p>One of the notable guests who visited EdgeWalk includes comedian <a href="/wiki/Rick_Mercer" title="Rick Mercer">Rick Mercer</a>, as featured on his <a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">CBC Television</a> show, <i><a href="/wiki/Rick_Mercer_Report" title="Rick Mercer Report">Rick Mercer Report</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_features">Safety features</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Insidecntower360.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Insidecntower360.JPG/220px-Insidecntower360.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Insidecntower360.JPG/330px-Insidecntower360.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Insidecntower360.JPG/440px-Insidecntower360.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Insidecntower360.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Inside the 360 Restaurant in the CN Tower</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In August 2000, a fire broke out at the <a href="/wiki/Ostankino_Tower" title="Ostankino Tower">Ostankino Tower</a> in Moscow. It killed three people and caused extensive damage. The fire was blamed on poor maintenance and outdated equipment. The failure of the fire-suppression systems and the lack of proper equipment for firefighters allowed the fire to destroy most of the interior and spark fears the tower might even collapse.</p>
<p>The Ostankino Tower was completed nine years before the CN Tower, and is only 13 m (43 ft) shorter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doom_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-doom-32">[32]</a></sup> The parallels between the towers led to some concern that the CN Tower could be at risk of a similar tragedy. However, Canadian officials subsequently stated that it is "highly unlikely" that a similar disaster could occur at the CN Tower, as it has important safeguards that were not present in the Ostankino Tower. Specifically, officials cited:</p>
<ul>
<li>the fireproof building materials used in the tower's construction,</li>
<li>frequent and stringent safety inspections,</li>
<li>an extensive sprinkler system,</li>
<li>a 24-hour emergency monitoring operation,</li>
<li>two 68,160-litre (15,000-imperial gallon; 18,006-US gallon) water reservoirs at the top, which are automatically replenished,</li>
<li>a fire hose at the base of the structure capable of sending 2725 litres (600 imperial gallons; 720 US Gallon) a minute to any location in the tower,</li>
<li>a ban on natural gas appliances anywhere in the tower (including the restaurant in the main pod),</li>
<li>an elevator that can be used during a fire as it runs up the outside of the building and can be powered by three emergency generators at the base of the structure (unlike the elevator at the Ostankino Tower, which malfunctioned).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-safety_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-safety-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Officials also noted that the CN Tower has an excellent safety record and that there has never been an accidental fire in the tower since it was opened in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-safety_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-safety-33">[33]</a></sup> Moreover, other supertall structures built between 1967 and 1976 — such as the <a href="/wiki/Willis_Tower" title="Willis Tower">Willis Tower</a> (formerly the Sears Tower), the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a> (until its <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">destruction on September 11, 2001</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Fernsehturm_Berlin" title="Fernsehturm Berlin">Fernsehturm Berlin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aon_Center_(Chicago)" title="Aon Center (Chicago)">Aon Center</a>, the <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock_Center" title="John Hancock Center">John Hancock Center</a>, and <a href="/wiki/First_Canadian_Place" title="First Canadian Place">First Canadian Place</a> — also have excellent safety records, which suggests that the Ostankino Tower accident was a rare safety failure, and that the likelihood of similar events occurring at other supertall structures is extremely low.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting">Lighting</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4288" data-file-width="2848" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg/170px-Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg/255px-Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg/340px-Toronto_-_ON_-_CN_Tower_bei_Nacht2.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>The CN Tower was originally lit at night with <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent</a> lights, but they were removed in 1997, because they were inefficient and expensive to repair. In June 2007, the tower was outfitted with 1,330 super-bright <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> lights inside the elevator shafts, shooting up over the main pod and upward to the top of the tower's mast to light the tower from dusk until 2 a.m. The official opening ceremony took place on June 28 before the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Day" title="Canada Day">Canada Day</a> holiday weekend.</p>
<p>The tower changes its lighting scheme on holidays and to commemorate major events. After the <a href="/wiki/95th_Grey_Cup" title="95th Grey Cup">95th Grey Cup</a> in Toronto, the tower was lit up in green and white to represent the colours of the Grey Cup champion <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Roughriders" title="Saskatchewan Roughriders">Saskatchewan Roughriders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LED_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-LED-34">[34]</a></sup> From sundown on August 27, 2011, to sunrise the following day, the tower was lit in orange, the official colour of the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party" title="New Democratic Party">New Democratic Party</a> (NDP), to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/Death_and_state_funeral_of_Jack_Layton" title="Death and state funeral of Jack Layton">death</a> of federal NDP leader and leader of the official opposition <a href="/wiki/Jack_Layton" title="Jack Layton">Jack Layton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-layton_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-layton-35">[35]</a></sup> When former <a href="/wiki/President_of_South_Africa" title="President of South Africa">South African president</a> <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Nelson_Mandela" title="Death of Nelson Mandela">died</a>, the tower was lit up in the colours of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_South_Africa" title="Flag of South Africa">South African flag</a>. When former <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(Canada)" title="Minister of Finance (Canada)">federal finance minister</a> under <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservatives</a> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Flaherty" title="Jim Flaherty">Jim Flaherty</a> died, the tower was lit in green to reflect his <a href="/wiki/Irish_Canadians" title="Irish Canadians">Irish Canadian</a> heritage. On the night of the <a href="/wiki/November_2015_Paris_attacks" title="November 2015 Paris attacks">attacks on Paris on November 13, 2015</a>, the CN Tower was lit up in the colours of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_France" title="Flag of France">French flag</a>.</p>
<p>Programmed from a <a href="/wiki/Desktop_computer" title="Desktop computer">desktop computer</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_network_interface_controller" title="Wireless network interface controller">wireless network interface controller</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Ontario" title="Burlington, Ontario">Burlington, Ontario</a>, the LEDs use less energy to light than the previously used incandescent lights (10% less energy than the dimly lit version and 60% less than the brightly lit version).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The estimated cost to use the LEDs is $1,000 per month.</p>
<p>During the spring and autumn <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">bird migration</a> seasons, the lights would be turned off to comply with the voluntary Fatal Light Awareness Program, which "encourages buildings to dim unnecessary exterior lighting to mitigate bird mortality during spring and summer migration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Height_comparisons">Height comparisons</span></h2>

<p>The CN Tower is the tallest freestanding structure in the Western Hemisphere. As of 2013, there are only two other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_freestanding_structures_in_the_world" title="List of tallest freestanding structures in the world">freestanding structures in the Western Hemisphere</a> which exceed 500 m (1,640.4 ft) in height; the <a href="/wiki/Willis_Tower" title="Willis Tower">Willis Tower</a> in Chicago, which stands at 527 m (1,729.0 ft) when measured to its pinnacle; and the topped-out <a href="/wiki/One_World_Trade_Center" title="One World Trade Center">One World Trade Center</a> in New York City, which has a pinnacle height of 541.33 m (1,776.0 ft), or approximately 12 m (39.4 ft) shorter than the CN Tower. Due to the symbolism of the number 1776 (the year of the signing of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">United States Declaration of Independence</a>), the height of One World Trade Center is unlikely to be increased. The proposed <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Spire" title="Chicago Spire">Chicago Spire</a> was expected to exceed the height of the CN Tower, but its construction was halted early due to financial difficulties amid the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a>, and was eventually cancelled in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Height_distinction_debate">Height distinction debate</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".22World.27s_Tallest_Tower.22_title">"World's Tallest Tower" title</span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> has called the CN Tower "the world's tallest self-supporting tower" and "the world's tallest free-standing tower".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_1982_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness_1982-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_2005_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness_2005-40">[40]</a></sup> Although Guinness did list this description of the CN Tower under the heading "tallest building" at least once,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_2005_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness_2005-40">[40]</a></sup> it has also listed it under "tallest tower", omitting it from its list of "tallest buildings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guinness_1982_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness_1982-39">[39]</a></sup> In 1996, Guinness changed the tower's classification to "World's Tallest Building and Freestanding Structure". <a href="/wiki/Emporis" title="Emporis">Emporis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Tall_Buildings_and_Urban_Habitat" title="Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat">Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat</a> both listed the CN Tower as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_buildings_and_structures" title="List of tallest buildings and structures">world's tallest free-standing structure on land</a>, and specifically state that the CN Tower is not a true building, thereby awarding the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_buildings_in_the_world" title="List of tallest buildings in the world">world's tallest building</a> to <a href="/wiki/Taipei_101" title="Taipei 101">Taipei 101</a>, which is 44 metres (144 ft) shorter than the CN Tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-info_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-info-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CTBUH_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-CTBUH-41">[41]</a></sup> The issue of what was tallest became moot when Burj Khalifa, then under construction, exceeded the height of the CN Tower in 2007 (see below).</p>
<p>Although the CN Tower contains a restaurant, a gift shop and multiple observation levels, it does not have floors continuously from the ground, and therefore <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_buildings_and_structures#Tallest_buildings" title="List of tallest buildings and structures">it is not considered a building</a> by the Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat (CTBUH) or Emporis. CTBUH defines a building as "a structure that is designed for residential, business, or manufacturing purposes. An essential characteristic of a building is that it has floors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CTBUH_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-CTBUH-41">[41]</a></sup> The CN Tower and other similar structures — such as the Ostankino Tower in Moscow, Russia; the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Pearl_Tower" title="Oriental Pearl Tower">Oriental Pearl Tower</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, China; the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere_Las_Vegas" title="Stratosphere Las Vegas">Stratosphere Tower</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>, Nevada, USA; and the <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, France — are categorized as "towers", which are free-standing structures that may have observation decks and a few other habitable levels, but do not have floors from the ground up. The CN Tower was the tallest tower by this definition, until 2010 (see below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-info_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-info-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Taller than the CN Tower are numerous <a href="/wiki/Radio_masts_and_towers" title="Radio masts and towers">radio masts and towers</a>, which are held in place by <a href="/wiki/Guy-wire" title="Guy-wire">guy-wires</a>, the tallest being the <a href="/wiki/KVLY-TV_mast" title="KVLY-TV mast">KVLY-TV mast</a> in <a href="/wiki/Blanchard,_North_Dakota" title="Blanchard, North Dakota">Blanchard, North Dakota</a>, USA at 628 metres (2,060 ft) tall, leading to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_structures_in_the_world" title="List of tallest structures in the world">a distinction</a> between these and "free-standing" structures. Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Petronius_(oil_platform)" title="Petronius (oil platform)">Petronius Platform</a> stands 610 metres (2,001 ft) above its base on the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, but only the top 75 metres (246 ft) of this <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil and natural gas platform</a> are above water, and the structure is thus partially supported by its <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>. Like the CN Tower, none of these taller structures are commonly considered buildings.</p>
<p>On September 12, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Burj_Khalifa" title="Burj Khalifa">Burj Khalifa</a>, which is a hotel, residential and commercial building in <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, United Arab Emirates, and was formerly known as Burj Dubai before opening, passed the CN Tower's 553.33-metre<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNTower_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-CNTower-6">[6]</a></sup> height. The CN Tower held the record of tallest free-standing structure on land for over 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBCSurpassed_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBCSurpassed-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Burj Khalifa had been formally recognized by the Guinness World Records as the world's tallest freestanding structure, Guinness re-certified CN Tower as the world's tallest freestanding tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The tower definition used by Guinness was defined by the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Tall_Buildings_and_Urban_Habitat" title="Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat">Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat</a> as 'a building in which less than 50% of the construction is usable floor space'. <i>Guinness World Records</i> editor-in-chief Craig Glenday announced that Burj Khalifa was not classified as a tower because it has too much usable floor space to be considered to be a tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> CN Tower still held world records for highest above ground wine cellar (in 360 Restaurant) at 351 metres, highest above ground restaurant at 346 metres (Horizons Restaurant),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and tallest free-standing concrete tower during Guinness's recertification. The CN Tower was surpassed by the <a href="/wiki/Canton_Tower" title="Canton Tower">Canton Tower</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, China, which stands at 600 metres (2,000 ft) tall, as the world's tallest tower, in 2010; which in turn was surpassed by the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Skytree" title="Tokyo Skytree">Tokyo Skytree</a>, completed in February 2012, which currently is the tallest tower at 634.0 metres (2,080.1 ft) in height. The CN Tower, as of 2014, stands as the fifth-tallest free-standing structure on land in the world and the third-tallest free-standing tower.</p>
