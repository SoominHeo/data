<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anouk Aimée</h1>

<p><b>Anouk Aimée</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[anuk ɛme]</a></span>; born 27 April 1932) is a French film actress, who has appeared in 70 films since 1947, having begun her film career at age 14. In her early years she studied acting and dance besides her regular education. Although the majority of her films were French, she also made a number of films in Spain, Great Britain, Italy and Germany, along with some American productions.</p>
<p>Among her films are <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/La_Dolce_Vita" title="La Dolce Vita">La Dolce Vita</a></i> (1960), after which she was considered a "rising star who exploded" onto the film world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-1">[1]</a></sup> She subsequently acted in Fellini's <i><a href="/wiki/8%C2%BD" title="8½">8½</a></i> (1963), <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Demy" title="Jacques Demy">Jacques Demy</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Lola_(1961_film)" title="Lola (1961 film)">Lola</a></i> (1961), <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">George Cukor</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Justine_(1969_film)" title="Justine (1969 film)">Justine</a></i> (1969), <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bertolucci" title="Bernardo Bertolucci">Bernardo Bertolucci</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_a_Ridiculous_Man" title="Tragedy of a Ridiculous Man">Tragedy of a Ridiculous Man</a></i> (1981) and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Altman" title="Robert Altman">Robert Altman</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%AAt-%C3%A0-Porter_(film)" title="Prêt-à-Porter (film)">Prêt à Porter</a></i> (1994). She won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actress_-_Motion_Picture_Drama" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actress - Motion Picture Drama">Golden Globe Award for Best Actress - Motion Picture Drama</a> and the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Actress_in_a_Leading_Role" title="BAFTA Award for Best Actress in a Leading Role">BAFTA Award for Best Actress</a> and was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a> for her acting in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Man_and_a_Woman" title="A Man and a Woman">A Man and a Woman</a></i> (1966). The film "virtually reignited the lush on-screen romance in an era of skeptical modernism," and brought her international fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prix_d%27interpr%C3%A9tation_f%C3%A9minine" title="Prix d'interprétation féminine">Award for Best Actress</a> at the 1980 <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>. In 2002 she received an honorary <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award" title="César Award">César Award</a>, France's national film award.</p>
<p>She was noted for her "striking features" and beauty, and considered "one of the hundred sexiest stars in film history," according to a 1995 poll conducted by <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire Magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup> Her acting style often portrays a <a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">femme fatale</a>, with a melancholy aura. In the 1960s, <i>Life</i> magazine wrote that "after each picture her enigmatic beauty lingered" in the memories of her audience, and called her "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_Bank" title="Left Bank">Left Bank</a>'s most beautiful resident."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Life-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anouk_Aim%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aimée was born <b>Nicole Françoise Florence Dreyfus</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in Paris, France, the daughter of actor Henri Murray (born Henry Dreyfus)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup> and actress <a href="/wiki/Genevi%C3%A8ve_Sorya" title="Geneviève Sorya">Geneviève Sorya</a> (born Durand).</p>
<p>According to one historian, although some have speculated that her background may be related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Alfred_Dreyfus" title="Captain Alfred Dreyfus">Captain Alfred Dreyfus</a>, this has never been confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup> Her father was Jewish and her mother was Roman Catholic. She was raised Catholic but later converted to Judaism as an adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold-5">[5]</a></sup> Her early education took place at l'École de la rue Milton, in Paris; École de Barbezieux; Pensionnat de Bandol; and Institution de Megève. She studied dance at Marseille Opera; studied theater in England, after which she studied dramatic art and dance with Andrée Bauer-Thérond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unterburger_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unterburger-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anouk_Aim%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="806" data-file-width="576" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg/220px-Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg/330px-Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg/440px-Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anouk_Aimee_-_1963.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anouk Aimée in <i><a href="/wiki/8%C2%BD" title="8½">8½</a></i>, 1963</div>

<p>Aimée (then still Françoise Dreyfus) made her film debut in 1946, at the age of fourteen, in the role of "Anouk" in <i>La Maison sous la mer</i>, and she kept the name afterwards. <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Pr%C3%A9vert" title="Jacques Prévert">Jacques Prévert</a>, while writing <i><a href="/wiki/Les_amants_de_V%C3%A9rone" title="Les amants de Vérone">Les amants de Vérone</a></i> (The Lovers of Verona, 1949) specifically for her, suggested she take the symbolic last name Aimée, "that would forever associate her with the affective power of her screen roles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup> In French, it means "beloved."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Life-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among her notable films were <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Astruc" title="Alexandre Astruc">Alexandre Astruc</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crimson_Curtain_(1953_film)" title="The Crimson Curtain (1953 film)">Le Rideau Cramoisi</a></i> (The Crimson Curtain, 1952), <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/La_Dolce_Vita" title="La Dolce Vita">La Dolce Vita</a></i> (1960), Fellini's <i><a href="/wiki/8%C2%BD" title="8½">8½</a></i> (1963), <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Demy" title="Jacques Demy">Jacques Demy</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Lola_(1961_film)" title="Lola (1961 film)">Lola</a></i> (1961), <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Delvaux" title="André Delvaux">André Delvaux</a>’s <i>Un Soir, un Train</i> (One Evening, One Train, 1968), <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">George Cukor</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Justine_(1969_film)" title="Justine (1969 film)">Justine</a></i> (1969), <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bertolucci" title="Bernardo Bertolucci">Bernardo Bertolucci</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_a_Ridiculous_Man" title="Tragedy of a Ridiculous Man">Tragedy of a Ridiculous Man</a></i> (1981), <a href="/wiki/Robert_Altman" title="Robert Altman">Robert Altman</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%AAt-%C3%A0-Porter_(film)" title="Prêt-à-Porter (film)">Prêt à Porter</a></i> (<i>Ready to Wear</i>, 1994) and, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lelouch" title="Claude Lelouch">Claude Lelouch</a>’s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Man_and_a_Woman" title="A Man and a Woman">Un Homme et une femme</a></i> (<i>A Man and a Woman</i>, 1966) — described as a "film that virtually reignited the lush on-screen romance in an era of skeptical modernism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup> Words like "regal," "intelligent" and "enigmatic" are frequently associated with her, notes one author, giving Aimée "an aura of disturbing and mysterious beauty" that has earned her the status of "one of the hundred sexiest stars in film history," according to a 1995 poll conducted by <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire Magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of her "striking features" and her beauty, she has been compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy" title="Jacqueline Kennedy">Jacqueline Kennedy</a>. Film historian <a href="/wiki/Ginette_Vincendeau" title="Ginette Vincendeau">Ginette Vincendeau</a> notes that Aimée’s films "established her as an ethereal, sensitive and fragile beauty with a tendency to tragic destinies or restrained suffering."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-2">[2]</a></sup> Her abilities as an actress and the photogenic qualities of her face, its "fine lines, expression of elation and a suggestive gaze," helped her achieve success in her early films. Among those were <i><a href="/wiki/Pot-Bouille" title="Pot-Bouille">Pot-Bouille</a></i> (1957), a story by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Zola" title="Émile Zola">Émile Zola</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lovers_of_Montparnasse" title="The Lovers of Montparnasse">Les Amants de Montparnasse (Montparnasse 19)</a></i> (The Lovers of Montparnasse, (1958) and <i><a href="/wiki/Head_Against_the_Wall" title="Head Against the Wall">La tête contre les murs</a></i> (Head Against the Wall, 1958).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unterburger_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Unterburger-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CL09.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1344" data-file-width="1918" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CL09.jpg/260px-CL09.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CL09.jpg/390px-CL09.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CL09.jpg/520px-CL09.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>

<p>Besides the French cinema, Aimée's career include a number of films made in Spain, Great Britain, Italy and Germany. She achieved worldwide attention in Fellini's <i><a href="/wiki/La_Dolce_Vita" title="La Dolce Vita">La Dolce Vita</a></i> (the Good Life, 1960) and <i>Lola</i> (1961). She appeared again in Fellini's <i>8½</i>, and would remain in Italy during the first half of the 1960s, making films for a number of Italian directors. Because of her role in <i>La Dolce Vita</i>, biographer Dave Thompson describes Aimée as a "rising star who exploded" onto the film world. He adds that singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Patti_Smith" title="Patti Smith">Patti Smith</a>, who in her teens saw the film, began to idolize her, and "dreamed of being an actress like Aimée."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aimée's greatest success came in 1966 with the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Un_homme_et_une_femme" title="Un homme et une femme">Un homme et une femme</a></i> (<i>A Man and a Woman,</i>) directed by little known <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lelouch" title="Claude Lelouch">Claude Lelouch</a>. Primarily due to the excellent acting by its stars, Aimée and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Trintignant" title="Jean-Louis Trintignant">Jean-Louis Trintignant</a>, the film became an international success, winning both the Grand Prize at the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> in 1966 and an Oscar. Tabery states that with her "subtle portrayal of the heroine—self-protective, then succumbing to a new love—Aimée seemed to create a new kind of <a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">femme fatale</a>. . ."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unterburger_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Unterburger-6">[6]</a></sup> Film historian Jurgen Muller adds, "whether one like the film or not, it's still hard for anyone to resist the melancholy aura of Anouk Aimée."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In many of her subsequent films, she would continue to play that type of role, "a woman of sensitivity whose emotions are often kept secret."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unterburger_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Unterburger-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1969 she starred in the American film production of <a href="/wiki/Justine_(1969_film)" title="Justine (1969 film)"><i>Justine</i></a>, costarring <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Bogarde" title="Dirk Bogarde">Dirk Bogarde</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">George Cukor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Strick" title="Joseph Strick">Joseph Strick</a>. The film contained some nudity, with one writer observing, "Anouk is always impeccable, oozing the sexy, detached air of the elite . . . when she drops these trappings, along with her couture clothing, Anouk's naked perfection will annihilate you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Photojournalist <a href="/wiki/Eve_Arnold" title="Eve Arnold">Eve Arnold</a>, assigned to photograph and write a story about Aimée and her role, spoke to Dirk Bogarde, who had known her since she was fifteen. He said that "She is never so happy as when she is miserable between love affairs," referencing her recent love affair with <a href="/wiki/Omar_Sharif" title="Omar Sharif">Omar Sharif</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arnold photographed Aimée, who talked about her role as the character Justine. Justine was also Jewish. Arnold recalls one of their talks:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I am still haunted by two things she quoted. They seemed to say more about her than anything else I experienced with her during the three weeks I knew her on the film:<br/></p>
<p>Quote from <a href="/wiki/Treblinka_extermination_camp" title="Treblinka extermination camp">Treblinka</a>: "The Jews are prone to anguish but seldom given to despair."<br/>
And a quote by an anonymous Jewish poet to his wife when the Nazis came to get them: "Till now we have lived with fear, now we can know hope."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another American film, <i>La Brava</i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Hoffman" title="Dustin Hoffman">Dustin Hoffman</a>, was set to be made in 1984 but never completed. Hoffman at first decided it would play better if he were in love with a younger girl rather than the original story's older woman. "Where are you going to get a good-looking older woman?" he asked. He rejected <a href="/wiki/Faye_Dunaway" title="Faye Dunaway">Faye Dunaway</a>, feeling she was "too obvious." A month later, after a chance meeting with Aimée in Paris, he changed his mind, telling his producer, "I can fall in love with the older woman. I met Anouk Aimée over the weekend. She looks great." He begged his producer to at least talk to her: "Come on, get on the phone, say hello to her. . . Just listen to her voice, it's great."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Altman" title="Robert Altman">Robert Altman</a>, at another time, wanted to use Aimée in a film to be called, <i>Lake Lugano,</i> about a woman who was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> survivor returning long after the war. She "loved the script," according to Altman. However, she backed out after discussing the part with him more thoroughly:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I do remember he was like a bomb. He had a strong personality. He was tall, and he had a big voice. "I want this," and "I want that." I remember thinking it would be very difficult to work with him, and we didn't make the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In late 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Cinemania_(film_festival)" title="Cinemania (film festival)">Cinemania film festival</a> in Montreal, Canada, paid tribute to Aimée's career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2002, she received an honorary <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award" title="César Award">César Award</a>, France's national film award, and in 2003 received an honorary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Berlin_Bear" title="Golden Berlin Bear">Golden Berlin Bear</a> at the Berlin International Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscherwitz_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscherwitz-14">[14]</a></sup> In the 1960s, <i>Life</i> magazine called her "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_Bank" title="Left Bank">Left Bank</a>'s most beautiful resident," adding that "after each picture her enigmatic beauty lingered" in the memories of her audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Life-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anouk_Aim%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aimée has been married and divorced four times. First to Edouard Zimmermann (married February 1949, divorced October 1950), secondly to film director <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nico_Papatakis" title="Nico Papatakis">Nico Papatakis</a> (married August 1951, divorced October 1955), thirdly to actor and musical producer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Barouh" title="Pierre Barouh">Pierre Barouh</a> (married April 1966, divorced March 1969) and lastly to actor <a href="/wiki/Albert_Finney" title="Albert Finney">Albert Finney</a> (married August 1970, divorced June 1978). She had one child, Manuela Papatakis (born 1951), from her second marriage.</p>
