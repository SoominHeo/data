<p><b>Sir Anish Kapoor</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBE" title="CBE">CBE</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" title="Royal Academy">RA</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi">अनीश कपूर</span>, <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi</a>: <span lang="pa">ਅਨੀਸ਼ ਕਪੂਰ</span>), (born 12 March 1954) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British-Indian" title="British-Indian">British-Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptor</a>. Born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombay" title="Bombay">Bombay</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Kapoor has lived and worked in London since the early 1970s when he moved to study art, first at the <a href="/wiki/Hornsey_College_of_Art" title="Hornsey College of Art">Hornsey College of Art</a> and later at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelsea_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="Chelsea School of Art and Design">Chelsea School of Art and Design</a>.</p>
<p>He represented Britain in the XLIV <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a> in 1990, when he was awarded the Premio Duemila Prize. In 1991 he received the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> and in 2002 received the Unilever Commission for the Turbine Hall at <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>. Notable public sculptures include <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Gate" title="Cloud Gate">Cloud Gate</a></i> (colloquially known as "the Bean") in Chicago's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennium_Park_(Chicago)" title="Millennium Park (Chicago)">Millennium Park</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Mirror" title="Sky Mirror">Sky Mirror</a></i>, exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Center" title="Rockefeller Center">Rockefeller Center</a> in New York City in 2006 and <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a> in London in 2010;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serpentinegalleries.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-serpentinegalleries.org-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Giants" title="Tees Valley Giants">Temenos</a></i>, at Middlehaven, <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough" title="Middlesbrough">Middlesbrough</a>; <i>Leviathan</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anishkapoor.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-anishkapoor.com-4">[4]</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palais" title="Grand Palais">Grand Palais</a> in Paris in 2011; and <i><a href="/wiki/ArcelorMittal_Orbit" title="ArcelorMittal Orbit">ArcelorMittal Orbit</a></i>, commissioned as a permanent artwork for London's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_Park,_London" title="Olympic Park, London">Olympic Park</a> and completed in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kapoor received a <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighthood</a> in the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Birthday_Honours" title="Birthday Honours">Birthday Honours</a> for services to visual arts. He was awarded an honorary doctorate degree from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2012 he was awarded <a href="/wiki/Padma_Bhushan" title="Padma Bhushan">Padma Bhushan</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_government" title="Indian government">Indian government</a> which is India's third highest civilian award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> An image of Kapoor features in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_culture" title="British culture">British cultural icons</a> section of the newly designed <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passport</a> in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2016 he was announced as a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/LennonOno_Grant_for_Peace" title="LennonOno Grant for Peace">LennonOno Grant for Peace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He owns exclusive rights to use <a href="/wiki/Vantablack" title="Vantablack">Vantablack</a>, the blackest substance known, for artistic purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anish Kapoor was born in Bombay, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>), to a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> father and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> mother whose family immigrated from <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> when she was a few months old. According to Kapoor, his mother had an Indian-Jewish upbringing, her father being the <a href="/wiki/Hazzan" title="Hazzan">cantor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>. At this time, <a href="/wiki/Baghdadi_Jews" title="Baghdadi Jews">Baghdadi Jews</a> constituted the majority of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Mumbai" title="History of the Jews in Mumbai">Jewish community in Mumbai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeinerJulia_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-WeinerJulia-12">[12]</a></sup> His father, from a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjabi_people" title="Punjabi people">Punjabi</a> family, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrographer" title="Hydrographer">hydrographer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higgins_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins-13">[13]</a></sup> Kapoor is the brother of Canada-based academic <a href="/wiki/Ilan_Kapoor" title="Ilan Kapoor">Ilan Kapoor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kapoor attended the all-boys <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding school</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Doon_School" title="The Doon School">The Doon School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dehradun" title="Dehradun">Dehradun</a>, Uttarakhand, India. He is said to have "hated" his time at Doon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1971-1973, he traveled to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> with one of his two brothers, initially living on a <a href="/wiki/Kibbutz" title="Kibbutz">kibbutz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He began to study <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeinerJulia_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-WeinerJulia-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but had trouble with mathematics and quit after six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In Israel, he decided to become an artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1973, he left for Britain to attend <a href="/wiki/Hornsey_College_of_Art" title="Hornsey College of Art">Hornsey College of Art</a> and Chelsea School of Art and Design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higgins_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins-13">[13]</a></sup> There he found a role model in <a href="/wiki/Paul_Neagu" title="Paul Neagu">Paul Neagu</a>, an artist who provided a meaning to what he was doing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Kapoor went on to teach at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolverhampton_Polytechnic" title="Wolverhampton Polytechnic">Wolverhampton Polytechnic</a> in 1979 and in 1982 was Artist in Residence at the <a href="/wiki/Walker_Art_Gallery" title="Walker Art Gallery">Walker Art Gallery</a>, Liverpool. He has lived and worked in London since the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalacademy_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-royalacademy-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anish Kapoor became known in the 1980s for his geometric or biomorphic sculptures made using simple materials such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, pigment, and <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publicartfund.org_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-publicartfund.org-22">[22]</a></sup> These early sculptures are frequently simple, curved forms, usually monochromatic and brightly coloured, using powder <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> to define and permeate the form. "While making the pigment pieces, it occurred to me that they all form themselves out of each other. So I decided to give them a generic title, <i>A Thousand Names</i>, implying <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinity</a>, a thousand being a symbolic number. The powder works sat on the floor or projected from the wall. The powder on the floor defines the surface of the floor and the objects appear to be partially submerged, like icebergs. That seems to fit inside the idea of something being partially there."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bombsite1990_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-bombsite1990-23">[23]</a></sup> Such use of pigment characterised his first high-profile exhibit as part of the <i>New Sculpture</i> exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Hayward_Gallery" title="Hayward Gallery">Hayward Gallery</a> London in 1978.</p>
<p>In the late 1980s and 1990s, he was acclaimed for his explorations of matter and non-matter, specifically evoking the void in both free-standing sculptural works and ambitious installations. Many of his sculptures seem to recede into the distance, disappear into the ground or distort the space around them. In 1987, he began working in stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> His later stone works are made of solid, quarried stone, many of which have carved apertures and cavities, often alluding to, and playing with dualities (earth-sky, matter-spirit, lightness-darkness, visible-invisible, conscious-unconscious, male-female, and body-mind). "In the end, I’m talking about myself. And thinking about making nothing, which I see as a void. But then that’s something, even though it really is nothing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bombsite1990_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-bombsite1990-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1995, he has worked with the highly reflective surface of polished <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>. These works are <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirror-like</a>, reflecting or distorting the viewer and surroundings. Over the course of the following decade Kapoor's sculptures ventured into more ambitious manipulations of form and space. He produced a number of large works, including <i>Taratantara</i> (1999),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-25">[25]</a></sup> a 35-metre-high piece installed in the Baltic Flour Mills in <a href="/wiki/Gateshead" title="Gateshead">Gateshead</a>, England, before renovation began there; and <i><a href="/wiki/Marsyas_(sculpture)" title="Marsyas (sculpture)">Marsyas</a></i> (2002), a large work consisting of three steel rings joined by a single span of <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> membrane that reached end to end of the 3,400-square-foot (320 m<sup>2</sup>) Turbine Hall of <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>. Kapoor's <i>Eye in Stone</i> (Norwegian: <i>Øye i stein</i>) is permanently placed at the shore of the <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a> in <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B8dingen" title="Lødingen">Lødingen</a> in northern Norway as part of <a href="/wiki/Artscape_Nordland" title="Artscape Nordland">Artscape Nordland</a>. In 2000, one of Kapoor's works, <i>Parabolic Waters</i>, consisting of rapidly rotating coloured water, was shown outside the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Dome" title="Millennium Dome">Millennium Dome</a> in London.</p>
<p>The use of red <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a> is also part of his repertoire, evocative of flesh, blood, and transfiguration. In 2007, he showed <i>Svayambh</i> (which translated from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> means "self-generated"), a 1.5-metre block of red wax that moved on rails through the <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Beaux-Arts_de_Nantes" title="Musée des Beaux-Arts de Nantes">Musée des Beaux-Arts</a> as part of the Biennale estuaire; this piece was shown again in a major show at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haus_Der_Kunst" title="Haus Der Kunst">Haus Der Kunst</a> in Munich and in 2009 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Some his work blurs the boundaries between architecture and art. In 2008, Kapoor created <i>Memory</i> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> and New York for the <a href="/wiki/John_Simon_Guggenheim_Memorial_Foundation" title="John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation">Guggenheim Foundation</a>, his first piece in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cor-Ten" title="Cor-Ten">Cor-Ten</a>, which is formulated to produce a protective coating of rust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Weighing 24 tons and made up of 156 parts, it calls to mind <a href="/wiki/Richard_Serra" title="Richard Serra">Richard Serra</a>’s huge, rusty steel works, which also invite viewers into perceptually confounding interiors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, Kapoor became the first Guest Artistic Director of <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Festival" title="Brighton Festival">Brighton Festival</a>. Kapoor installed four sculptures during the festival: <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Mirror" title="Sky Mirror">Sky Mirror</a></i> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brighton_Pavilion" title="Brighton Pavilion">Brighton Pavilion</a> gardens; <i>C-Curve</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chattri_(Brighton)" title="Chattri (Brighton)">The Chattri</a>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_Relations_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blood Relations (book) (page does not exist)">Blood Relations</a></i> (a collaboration with author <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>); and <i>1000 Names</i>, both at Fabrica. He also created a large site-specific work titled <i>The Dismemberment of Jeanne d’Arc</i> and a performance-based installation: <i>Imagined Monochrome</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The public response was so overwhelming that police had to re-divert traffic around <i>C Curve</i> at the Chattri and exercise crowd control.</p>
<p>In September 2009, Kapoor was the first living artist to have a solo exhibition at the Royal Academy of Arts. As well as surveying his career to date, the show also included new works. On display were <i>Non-Object</i> mirror works, cement sculptures previously unseen, and <i>Shooting into the Corner</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> a cannon that fires pellets of wax into the corner of the gallery. Previously shown at MAK, Vienna, in January 2009, it is a work with dramatic presence and associations and also continues Kapoor's interest in the self-made object, as the wax builds up on the walls and floor of the gallery the work slowly oozes out its form.</p>
<p>In spring 2011, Kapoor's work, <i>Leviathan</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anishkapoor.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-anishkapoor.com-4">[4]</a></sup> was the annual <i>Monumenta</i> installation for the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palais" title="Grand Palais">Grand Palais</a> in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Kapoor described the work as: "A single object, a single form, a single colour...My ambition is to create a space with in a space that responds to the height and luminosity of the Nave at the Grand Palais. Visitors will be invited to walk inside the work, to immerse themselves in colour, and it will, I hope, be a contemplative and poetic experience."</p>
<p>In 2011, Kapoor exhibited <i>Dirty Corner</i> at the Fabbrica del Vapore in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Fully occupying the site's "cathedral" space, the work consists of a huge steel volume, 60 metres long and 8 metres high, that visitors enter. Inside, they gradually lose their perception of space, as it gets progressively darker and darker until there is no light, forcing people to use their other senses to guide them through the space. The entrance of the tunnel is goblet-shaped, featuring an interior and exterior surface that is circular, making minimal contact with the ground. Over the course of the exhibition, the work was progressively covered by some 160 cubic metres of earth by a large mechanical device, forming a sharp mountain of dirt which the tunnel appears to be running through.</p>
<p>In 2016, his art exposition in <a href="/wiki/Museo_Universitario_Arte_Contempor%C3%A1neo" title="Museo Universitario Arte Contemporáneo">MUAC</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>) was a success, with literary contributions from Catherine Lampert, Cecilia Delgado, and Mexican writer <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Soler_Frost" title="Pablo Soler Frost">Pablo Soler Frost</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>Kapoor's earliest public commissions include the <i>Cast Iron Mountain</i> at the Tachikawa Art Project in Japan, as well as an untitled 1995 piece installed at Toronto's <a href="/wiki/Simcoe_Place" title="Simcoe Place">Simcoe Place</a> resembling mountain peaks. In 2001, <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Mirror" title="Sky Mirror">Sky Mirror</a></i>, a large mirror piece that reflects the sky and surroundings, was commissioned for a site outside the <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> Playhouse. Since 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Gate" title="Cloud Gate">Cloud Gate</a></i>, a 110-<a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">ton</a> stainless steel sculpture with a mirror finish, has been permanently installed in <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Park" title="Millennium Park">Millennium Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. Viewers are able to walk beneath the sculpture and look up into an "<a href="/wiki/Omphalos" title="Omphalos">omphalos</a>" or navel above them.</p>
<p>In the autumn of 2006, a second 10-metre <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Mirror" title="Sky Mirror">Sky Mirror</a></i>, was installed at <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Center" title="Rockefeller Center">Rockefeller Center</a>, New York City. This work was later exhibited in Kensington Gardens in 2010 as part of the show <i>Turning the World Upside Down</i>, along with three other major mirror works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serpentinegalleries.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-serpentinegalleries.org-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2009, Kapoor created the permanent, site-specific work <i>Earth Cinema</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/Pollino_National_Park" title="Pollino National Park">Pollino National Park</a>, the largest national park in Italy, as part of the project <i>ArtePollino – Another South</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-uni-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artkey_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-artkey-38">[38]</a></sup> Kapoor's work, <i>Cinema di Terra</i> (<i>Earth Cinema</i>), is a 45m long, 3m wide and 7m deep cut into the landscape made from concrete and earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-uni-37">[37]</a></sup> People can enter from both sides and walk along it, viewing the earth void within.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artkey_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-artkey-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pisani_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pisani-39">[39]</a></sup> <i>Cinema di Terra</i> officially opened to public in September 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-uni-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kapoor was also commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Regeneration" title="Tees Valley Regeneration">Tees Valley Regeneration</a> (TVR) to produce five pieces of public art, collectively known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Giants" title="Tees Valley Giants">Tees Valley Giants</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The first of these sculptures, <i>Tememos</i>, was unveiled to the public in June 2010. <i>Temenos</i> stands 50 metres high and is 110 metres in length. A steel wire mesh pulled taut between two enormous steel hoops, it remains an ethereal and an uncertain form despite its colossal scale.</p>
<p>In 2010, <i>Turning the World Upside Down, Jerusalem</i> was commissioned and installed at the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Museum" title="Israel Museum">Israel Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The sculpture is described as a "16-foot tall polished-steel hourglass" and it "reflects and reverses the Jerusalem sky and the museum's landscape, a likely reference to the city's duality of celestial and earthly, holy and profane".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronner_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bronner-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in June, Kapoor's <i>Orbit</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> was announced as the winning proposal for an artwork for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012_Olympic_Games" title="2012 Olympic Games">2012 Olympic Games</a>. The Greater London Authority selected Kapoor's sculpture from a shortlist of five artists as the permanent artwork for the Olympic Park. At 115 metres tall, <i>Orbit</i> is the tallest sculpture in the UK.</p>
<p>A granite monument, a memorial garden, is to commemorate the British victims of 9/11 in New York’s Hanover Square.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_projects">Architectural projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Architectural projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout his career, Kapoor has worked extensively with architects and engineers. Kapoor says this body of work is neither pure sculpture nor pure architecture. Notable architectural projects include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Ark Nova</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> an inflatable concert Hall that will travel around the earthquake struck regions of Japan. In collaboration with architect <a href="/wiki/Arata_Isozaki" title="Arata Isozaki">Arata Isozaki</a></li>
<li><i>Orbit</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> the permanent artwork for the Olympic Park, in collaboration with engineer <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Balmond" title="Cecil Balmond">Cecil Balmond</a>.</li>
<li><i>Temenos</i> the first work of the recently announced <i><a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Giants" title="Tees Valley Giants">Tees Valley Giants</a></i>, the world's five largest sculptures, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Balmond" title="Cecil Balmond">Cecil Balmond</a>. Temenos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> is situated in Middlehaven Dock, Middlesbrough.</li>
<li><i>Dismemberment Site 1</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> installed in New Zealand at the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_Farm" title="Gibbs Farm">Gibbs Farm</a> sculpture park, owned by New Zealand businessman and art patron <a href="/wiki/Alan_Gibbs" title="Alan Gibbs">Alan Gibbs</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/56_Leonard_Street" title="56 Leonard Street">56 Leonard Street</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> New York, Collaboration with architects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herzog_and_de_Meuron" title="Herzog and de Meuron">Herzog and de Meuron</a>.</li>
<li>Two subway stations in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> at Monte San Angelo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and Triano<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Future_Systems" title="Future Systems">Future Systems</a>.</li>
<li>A proposal for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Diana" title="Princess Diana">Princess Diana</a> Memorial Fountain.</li>
<li><i>Taratantara</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-25">[25]</a></sup> (1999–2000) was installed at the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Centre_for_Contemporary_Art" title="Baltic Centre for Contemporary Art">Baltic Centre for Contemporary Art</a>, Gateshead and later at Piazza Plebiscito, Naples.</li>
<li>An unrealised project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> for the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Dome" title="Millennium Dome">Millennium Dome</a>, London, (1995) in collaboration with Philip Gumuchdjian.</li>
<li>Building for a Void,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> created for Expo '92, Seville, in collaboration with David Connor.</li>
</ul>
<p>Of his vision for the Cumana station in Monte Sant'Angelo, Naples, Italy under construction (as of June 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>), Kapoor has said:</p>

<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">It’s very vulva-like. The tradition of the Paris or Moscow metro is of palaces of light, underground. I wanted to do exactly the opposite – to acknowledge that we are going underground. So it’s dark, and what I’ve done is bring the tunnel up and roll it over as a form like a sock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></td>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_with_text">Working with text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Working with text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A collaboration with author <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>, Kapoor conceived a sculpture consisting of two bronze boxes conjoined with red wax and inscribed around the outside with the first two paragraphs of Rushdie's text; "Blood Relations"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> or an "Interrogation of the Arabian Nights" in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_projects">Other projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kapoor has designed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stage_set" title="Stage set">stage sets</a> that include: the opera <i><a href="/wiki/Idomeneo" title="Idomeneo">Idomeneo</a></i> at Glyndebourne in 2003; <i><a href="/wiki/Pell%C3%A9as_et_M%C3%A9lisande_(opera)" title="Pelléas et Mélisande (opera)">Pelléas et Mélisande</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/La_Monnaie" title="La Monnaie">La Monnaie</a> in Brussels, and a dance-theatre piece called <i>in-i</i> for <a href="/wiki/Akram_Khan_(dancer)" title="Akram Khan (dancer)">Akram Khan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juliette_Binoche" title="Juliette Binoche">Juliette Binoche</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">National Theatre</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kapoor initially began exhibiting as part of <a href="/wiki/New_British_Sculpture" title="New British Sculpture">New British Sculpture</a> art scene, along with fellow British sculptors <a href="/wiki/Tony_Cragg" title="Tony Cragg">Tony Cragg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Deacon_(sculptor)" title="Richard Deacon (sculptor)">Richard Deacon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-publicartfund.org_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-publicartfund.org-22">[22]</a></sup> His first solo exhibition took place at Patrice Alexandra, Paris, in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> He achieved widespread recognition when he represented Britain at the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In 1992 Kapoor contributed to <a href="/wiki/Documenta" title="Documenta">documenta IX</a> with the <i>Building Decent into Limbo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In 2004 he participated in The 5th <a href="/wiki/Gwangju_Biennale" title="Gwangju Biennale">Gwangju Biennale</a> in Gwangju, Korea. Solo exhibitions of his work have since been held in the <a href="/wiki/Tate" title="Tate">Tate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hayward_Gallery" title="Hayward Gallery">Hayward Gallery</a> in London, <a href="/wiki/Kunsthalle_Basel" title="Kunsthalle Basel">Kunsthalle Basel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Museo_Nacional_Centro_de_Arte_Reina_Sof%C3%ADa" title="Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofía">Reina Sofia</a> in Madrid, the National Gallery in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, Musee des arts contemporains (Grand-Hornu) in Belgium, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAPC_Museum_of_Contemporary_Art" title="CAPC Museum of Contemporary Art">CAPC Museum of Contemporary Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Centro_Cultural_Banco_do_Brasil" title="Centro Cultural Banco do Brasil">Centro Cultural Banco do Brasil</a> in Brazil, and the <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Museum_Bilbao" title="Guggenheim Museum Bilbao">Guggenheim</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Contemporary_Art,_Boston" title="Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston">Institute of Contemporary Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> exhibited Kapoor's first US mid-career survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> That same year, Kapoor’s <i>Islamic Mirror</i> (2008), a circular concave mirror, was installed in a 13th-century Arab palace now being used as by the Convent of Santa Clara in Murcia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kapoor was the first living British artist to take over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a>, London, in 2009;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> the show attracted 275,000 visitors, making it the most successful exhibition ever by a living artist in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> This show subsequently traveled to the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao. In 2010, Kapoor retrospective exhibitions were held at the National Gallery of Modern Art (NGMA) in New Delhi and Mumbai’s <a href="/wiki/Mehboob_Studio" title="Mehboob Studio">Mehboob Studio</a>, the first showcase of his work in the country of his birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2011 Kapoor has had a solo touring exhibition with the Arts Council, part of their "Flashback " series of shows. In May he exhibited <i>Leviathan</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palais" title="Grand Palais">Grand Palais</a>, and two concurrent shows in Milan at the <a href="/wiki/Rotonda_della_Besana" title="Rotonda della Besana">Rotonda della Besana</a> and Fabbrica del Vapore. He had a major exhibition at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_Contemporary_Art,_Sydney" title="Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney">Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney</a> (MCA) from December 2012 to April 2013 as part of the Sydney International Art Series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Dirty Corner</i>, exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a> in 2015, was a topic of controversy due to its "blatantly sexual" nature. Kapoor himself reportedly described the work as "the vagina of a queen who is taking power".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections">Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kapoor's work is collected worldwide, notably by the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> in New York City; <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a> in London; Fondazione Prada in Milan; the <a href="/wiki/Art_Gallery_of_New_South_Wales" title="Art Gallery of New South Wales">Art Gallery of New South Wales</a>, Sydney; the <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Museum_Bilbao" title="Guggenheim Museum Bilbao">Guggenheim</a> in Bilbao; <a href="/wiki/De_Pont_Museum_of_Contemporary_Art" title="De Pont Museum of Contemporary Art">De Pont Museum of Contemporary Art</a> in the Netherlands; the <a href="/wiki/Moderna_Museet" title="Moderna Museet">Moderna Museet</a>, Stockholm; the <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Museum_of_Contemporary_Art,_Kanazawa" title="21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art, Kanazawa">21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanazawa,_Ishikawa" title="Kanazawa, Ishikawa">Kanazawa</a>, Japan; and the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Museum" title="Israel Museum">Israel Museum</a> in Jerusalem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalacademy_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-royalacademy-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1995, Kapoor married the German-born art historian Susanne Spicale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> They have a daughter and a son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Until 2013, they lived in a house in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea, London</a>, designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Tony_Fretton" title="Tony Fretton">Tony Fretton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
