<p><b>Adad-nirari III</b> (also <b>Adad-narari</b>) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Assyria" title="King of Assyria">King of Assyria</a> from 811 to 783 BC.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adad-nirari_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adad-nirari was a son and successor of king <a href="/wiki/Shamshi-Adad_V" title="Shamshi-Adad V">Shamshi-Adad V</a>, and was apparently quite young at the time of his accession, because for the first five years of his reign, his mother <a href="/wiki/Shammuramat" title="Shammuramat">Shammuramat</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> was highly influential, which may have given rise to the legend of <a href="/wiki/Semiramis" title="Semiramis">Semiramis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is widely rejected that his mother acted as regent, but she was surprisingly influential for the time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was the father of kings <a href="/wiki/Ashur-nirari_V" title="Ashur-nirari V">Ashur-nirari V</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shalmaneser_IV" title="Shalmaneser IV">Shalmaneser IV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ashur-dan_III" title="Ashur-dan III">Ashur-dan III</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tiglath-Pileser_III" title="Tiglath-Pileser III">Tiglath-Pileser III</a> described himself as a son of Adad-nirari in his inscriptions, but it is uncertain if this is truthful.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adad-nirari_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Adad-nirari's youth, and the struggles his father had faced early in his reign, caused a serious weakening for the Assyrian rulership over <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, and gave way to the ambitions of the most officers, governors, and the local rulers.</p>

<p>According to Adad-nirari's inscriptions, he led several military campaigns with the purpose of regaining the strength Assyria enjoyed in the times of his grandfather <a href="/wiki/Shalmaneser_III" title="Shalmaneser III">Shalmaneser III</a>.</p>
<p>According to the eponym canon, he campaigned in all directions until the last of his 18 years of reign (783 BC), and he was the builder of the temple of <a href="/wiki/Nabu" title="Nabu">Nabu</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nineveh" title="Nineveh">Nineveh</a>. Among his actions was a siege of <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a> in the time of <a href="/wiki/Ben-Hadad_III" title="Ben-Hadad III">Ben-Hadad III</a> in 796 BCE, which led to the eclipse of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaean_Kingdom_of_Damascus" title="Aramaean Kingdom of Damascus">Aramaean Kingdom of Damascus</a> and allowed the recovery of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Israel_(Samaria)" title="Kingdom of Israel (Samaria)">Israel</a> under <a href="/wiki/Jehoash_of_Israel" title="Jehoash of Israel">Jehoash</a> (who paid the Assyrian king tribute at this time) and <a href="/wiki/Jeroboam_II" title="Jeroboam II">Jeroboam II</a>.</p>
<p>Despite Adad-nirari's vigor, Assyria entered a several decades long period of weakness following his death.</p>
