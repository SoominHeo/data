<p><b>Adama</b> (<a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">Oromo</a>: <i><span lang="om" xml:lang="om">Adaamaa</span></i> or <i><span lang="om" xml:lang="om">Hadaamaa</span></i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a>: <span lang="am" xml:lang="am">አዳማ</span>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="am EAE transliteration">Ädama</span></i>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="am BGN/PCGN transliteration">Ādama</span></i>), also known<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> as <b>Nazret</b> or <b>Nazreth</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a>: <span lang="am" xml:lang="am">ናዝሬት</span><a href="/wiki/Help:Multilingual_support_(Ethiopic)" title="Help:Multilingual support (Ethiopic)"><sup><span class="t nihongo icon" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;text-decoration:none;padding:0 .1em;">?</span></sup></a>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="am EAE transliteration">Nazret</span></i>), is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and the previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_(political)" title="Capital (political)">capital</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Oromia_Region" title="Oromia Region">Oromia Region</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009map_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-2009map-3">[3]</a></sup> Adama forms a Special Zone of Oromia and is surrounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misraq_Shewa_Zone" title="Misraq Shewa Zone">Misraq Shewa Zone</a>. It is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Adama&amp;params=8.54_N_39.27_E_type:city"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">8°32′N</span> <span class="longitude">39°16′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">8.54°N 39.27°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">8.54; 39.27</span></span></span></a></span> at an elevation of 1712 meters, 99 km southeast of <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a>. The city sits between the base of an <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a> to the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Rift_Valley,_Ethiopia" title="Great Rift Valley, Ethiopia">Great Rift Valley</a> to the east.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adama is a busy transportation center. The city is situated along the road that connects <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a> with <a href="/wiki/Dire_Dawa" title="Dire Dawa">Dire Dawa</a>. A large number of trucks use this same route to travel to and from the <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">seaports</a> of <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asseb" title="Asseb">Asseb</a> (though the latter is not currently used by Ethiopia, following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eritrean-Ethiopian_War" title="Eritrean-Ethiopian War">Eritrean-Ethiopian War</a>). Additionally, the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa-Djibouti_Railway" title="Addis Ababa-Djibouti Railway">Addis Ababa-Djibouti Railway</a> runs through Adama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009map_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-2009map-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bingmap_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bingmap-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortna05_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ortna05-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adama_University" title="Adama University">Adama University</a> (formerly Adama Technical Teachers College) is located in Adama. <a href="/wiki/Adama_Stadium" title="Adama Stadium">Adama Stadium</a> is the home of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adama_City_FC" title="Adama City FC">Adama City FC</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Football_Federation" title="Ethiopian Football Federation">Ethiopian Football Federation</a> league.</p>
<p>The city name Adama may have been derived from the <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">Oromo</a> word <i><span lang="om" xml:lang="om">adaamii</span></i>, which means a cactus or a cactus-like tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> More specifically, <i><span lang="om" xml:lang="om">adaamii</span></i> means <i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbia_candelabrum" title="Euphorbia candelabrum">Euphorbia candelabrum</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> a tree of the spurge family, while <i><span lang="om" xml:lang="om">hadaamii</span></i> would mean <a href="/wiki/Opuntia_ficus-indica" title="Opuntia ficus-indica">Indian fig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haile_Selassie_of_Ethiopia" title="Haile Selassie of Ethiopia">Haile Selassie</a> renamed the town after <a href="/wiki/Nazareth" title="Nazareth">Biblical Nazareth</a>, and this name was used for the remainder of the twentieth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortna05_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ortna05-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2000, the city officially reverted to its original <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">Oromo language</a> name, Adama,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortna05_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ortna05-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortaa_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ortaa-10">[10]</a></sup> though "Nazareth" is still widely used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walta2000-07-13_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-walta2000-07-13-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, the government moved the regional capital of Oromia from <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a> to Adama,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortna05_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ortna05-5">[5]</a></sup> sparking considerable controversy. Critics of the move believed that the Ethiopian government wished to deemphasize Addis Ababa's location within Oromia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On the other hand, the government maintained that Addis Ababa "has been found inconvenient from the point of view of developing the language, culture and history of the Oromo people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walta2000-07-13_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-walta2000-07-13-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 10, 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Oromo_Peoples%27_Democratic_Organization" title="Oromo Peoples' Democratic Organization">Oromo Peoples' Democratic Organization</a> (OPDO), part of the ruling <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_People%27s_Revolutionary_Democratic_Front" title="Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front">EPRDF</a> coalition, officially announced plans to move the regional capital back to Finfinne (the Oromo name for Addis Ababa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on the 2007 Census conducted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Statistical_Agency_(Ethiopia)" title="Central Statistical Agency (Ethiopia)">Central Statistical Agency</a> of Ethiopia (CSA), this city has a total population of 220,212, an increase of 72.25% over the population recorded in the 1994 census, of whom 108,872 are men and 111,340 women. With an area of 29.86 square kilometers, Adama has a population density of 7,374.82; all are urban inhabitants. A total of 60,174 households were counted in this city, which results in an average of 3.66 persons to a household, and 59,431 housing units. The four largest ethnic groups reported in Adama were the <a href="/wiki/Oromo_people" title="Oromo people">Oromo</a> (39.02%), the <a href="/wiki/Amhara_people" title="Amhara people">Amhara</a> (34.53%), the <a href="/wiki/Gurage_people" title="Gurage people">Gurage</a> (11.98%) and the <a href="/wiki/Silt%27e_people" title="Silt'e people">Silte</a> (5.02%); all other ethnic groups made up 9.45% of the population. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a> was spoken as a first language by 59.25%, 26.25% spoke <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">Oromiffa</a> and 6.28% spoke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gurage_language" title="Gurage language">Guragiegna</a>; the remaining 8.22% spoke all other primary languages reported. The majority of the inhabitants said they practiced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Christianity" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity">Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity</a>, with 63.62% of the population reporting they observed this belief, while 24.7% of the population were <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Ethiopia" title="Islam in Ethiopia">Muslim</a>, and 10.57% were <a href="/wiki/P%27ent%27ay" title="P'ent'ay">Protestant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
