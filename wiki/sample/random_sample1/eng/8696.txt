<p><b>Mitsuru Adachi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">あだち 充 or 安達 充</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Adachi Mitsuru</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born February 9, 1951 in <a href="/wiki/Isesaki,_Gunma" title="Isesaki, Gunma">Isesaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunma_Prefecture" title="Gunma Prefecture">Gunma Prefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>)</span> is a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Mangaka" title="Mangaka">manga artist</a>. After graduating from Gunma Prefectural Maebashi Commercial High School in 1969, Adachi worked as an assistant for Isami Ishii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He made his <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> debut in 1970 with <i>Kieta Bakuon</i>, based on a manga originally created by Satoru Ozawa. <i>Kieta</i> was published in <i>Deluxe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dnen" title="Shōnen">Shōnen</a> Sunday</i> (a <a href="/wiki/List_of_manga_magazines" title="List of manga magazines">manga magazine</a> published by <a href="/wiki/Shogakukan" title="Shogakukan">Shogakukan</a>) .</p>
<p>Adachi is well known for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">romantic comedy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports" title="Sports">sports</a> <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>) such as <i><a href="/wiki/Touch_(manga)" title="Touch (manga)">Touch</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/H2_(manga)" title="H2 (manga)">H2</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Slow_Step" title="Slow Step">Slow Step</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Miyuki_(manga)" title="Miyuki (manga)">Miyuki</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cross_Game" title="Cross Game">Cross Game</a></i>. He has been described as a writer of "delightful dialogue", a genius at portraying everyday life,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ann-carl_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ann-carl-2">[2]</a></sup> "the greatest pure storyteller",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carlo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-carlo-3">[3]</a></sup> and "a master <a href="/wiki/Mangaka" title="Mangaka">manga artist</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-casey-4">[4]</a></sup> He is one of the few manga artists to write for <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dnen_manga" title="Shōnen manga">shōnen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Djo_manga" title="Shōjo manga">shōjo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seinen_manga" title="Seinen manga">seinen manga</a> magazines, and be popular in all three.</p>
<p>His works have been carried in <a href="/wiki/List_of_manga_magazines" title="List of manga magazines">manga magazines</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Sunday" title="Weekly Shōnen Sunday">Weekly Shōnen Sunday</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ciao_(magazine)" title="Ciao (magazine)">Ciao</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Djo_Comic" title="Shōjo Comic">Shōjo Comic</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Comic" title="Big Comic">Big Comic</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Petit_Comic" title="Petit Comic">Petit Comic</a></i>, and most of his works are published through Shogakukan and <a href="/wiki/Gakken" title="Gakken">Gakken</a>. He was one of the flagship authors in the new <i><a href="/wiki/Monthly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Sunday" title="Monthly Shōnen Sunday">Monthly Shōnen Sunday</a></i> magazine which began publication in June 2009. Two short story collections, <i><a href="/wiki/Short_Program_(manga)" title="Short Program (manga)">Short Program</a></i> and <i>Short Program 2</i> (both through <a href="/wiki/Viz_Media" title="Viz Media">Viz Media</a>), have been released in North America, and <a href="/wiki/Viz_Media" title="Viz Media">Viz Media</a> scheduled to begin publishing <i>Cross Game</i> in October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The first volume was released on October 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He modeled the spelling of あだち (rather than 安達) for his family name after the example of his older brother, manga artist <a href="/wiki/Tsutomu_Adachi" title="Tsutomu Adachi">Tsutomu Adachi</a>. In addition, it has been suggested that the accurate portrayal of sibling rivalry in <i>Touch</i> may come from Adachi's experiences while growing up with his older brother. Adachi did the character designs for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OVA" title="OVA">OVA</a> anime series <i><a href="/wiki/Nozomi_Witches" title="Nozomi Witches">Nozomi Witches</a></i>, so he is sometimes incorrectly given credit for creating the original series.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brief_history">Brief history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mitsuru_Adachi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Brief history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to 1969, Adachi began submitting works to the manga magazine <i><a href="/wiki/COM_(manga_magazine)" title="COM (manga magazine)">COM</a></i>. In 1969, he followed his older brother's lead and moved to <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> to begin work as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assistant_manga_artist" title="Assistant manga artist">assistant</a> to manga artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isami_Ishii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isami Ishii (page does not exist)">Isami Ishii</a> The following year, he made his professional debut with <i>Kieta Bakuon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maicomi-20110420_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-maicomi-20110420-7">[7]</a></sup> He continued publishing various short stories and shorter series throughout the 1970s based on works created by others, the most well known being his adaptation of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbowman" title="Rainbowman">Rainbowman</a></i> from 1972 to 1973. In 1978, he published his first original series, <i><a href="/wiki/Nine_(manga)" title="Nine (manga)">Nine</a></i>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Sunday" title="Weekly Shōnen Sunday">Weekly Shōnen Sunday</a></i>. He published two other original series, <i><a href="/wiki/Hiatari_Ry%C5%8Dk%C5%8D!" title="Hiatari Ryōkō!">Hiatari Ryōkō!</a></i> from 1979 to 1981 in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Djo_Comic" title="Weekly Shōjo Comic">Weekly Shōjo Comic</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Miyuki_(manga)" title="Miyuki (manga)">Miyuki</a></i> from 1980-1984 in <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dnen_Big_Comic" title="Shōnen Big Comic">Shōnen Big Comic</a></i>.</p>
<p>Adachi became a household name with the publication of his series <i><a href="/wiki/Touch_(manga)" title="Touch (manga)">Touch</a></i> from 1981 to 1986 in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i>. In 1982, <i>Hiatari Ryōkō!</i> was adapted into a <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> TV <a href="/wiki/Japanese_television_drama" title="Japanese television drama">drama</a> series. The following year, 1983, was a big year for Adachi. He received the 28th Annual <a href="/wiki/Shogakukan_Manga_Award" title="Shogakukan Manga Award">Shogakukan Manga Award</a> for shōnen/shōjo manga his two series <i>Touch</i> and <i>Miyuki</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maicomi-20110420_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-maicomi-20110420-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShogakukanAward_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShogakukanAward-8">[8]</a></sup> His <i>Miyuki</i> series was adapted into both an anime television series and a live-action film, and <i>Nine</i> was adapted into three films, with another following in 1984.</p>
<p><i>Touch</i> was adapted into an anime television series in 1985, and the series ran for two years on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuji_TV" title="Fuji TV">Fuji TV</a>. Adachi's romantic <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Djo_manga" title="Shōjo manga">shōjo manga</a> series, <i><a href="/wiki/Slow_Step" title="Slow Step">Slow Step</a></i> was serialized in <i><a href="/wiki/Ciao_(magazine)" title="Ciao (magazine)">Ciao</a></i> from 1986 to 1991, and another romantic comedy series, <i><a href="/wiki/Rough_(manga)" title="Rough (manga)">Rough</a></i>, appeared in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i> from 1987 to 1989. Adachi then released <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niji_Iro_T%C5%8Dgarashi" title="Niji Iro Tōgarashi">Niji Iro Tōgarashi</a></i>, a fantasy medieval romantic comedy manga series, from 1990 to 1992 in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Jinb%C4%93" title="Jinbē">Jinbē</a></i>, a romantic comedy about the relationship between a stepfather and stepdaughter, was serialized in <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Comic_Original" title="Big Comic Original">Big Comic Original</a></i> from 1992 to 1997. Adachi's longest manga series, <i><a href="/wiki/H2_(manga)" title="H2 (manga)">H2</a></i> was serialized in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i> from 1992 to 1999 and compiled in 34 <a href="/wiki/Tank%C5%8Dbon" title="Tankōbon">volumes</a>. This manga was adapted into an anime television series which aired on <a href="/wiki/TV_Asahi" title="TV Asahi">TV Asahi</a> from 1995 to 1996.</p>
<p>From 2000 to 2001, Adachi published a fantasy romantic comedy series in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i> titled <i><a href="/wiki/Itsumo_Misora" title="Itsumo Misora">Itsumo Misora</a></i>. His next longer series was the boxing romantic comedy, <i><a href="/wiki/Katsu!" title="Katsu!">Katsu!</a></i>, published from 2001 to 2005 in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i>. In 2005, <i>H2</i> was adapted into a live action drama series aired on <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Broadcasting_System" title="Tokyo Broadcasting System">TBS</a> in Japan, and <i>Touch</i> was adapted into a live action movie released by <a href="/wiki/Toho" title="Toho">Toho</a>. He also began his manga series <i><a href="/wiki/Cross_Game" title="Cross Game">Cross Game</a></i>, serialized in <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i>. The following year, <i>Rough</i> was adapted into a live action movie, also released by Toho.</p>
<p>Due to achieving total manga sales numbering over 200 million volumes, <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i> devoted issue 26 in 2008 to Adachi and his works. In 2009, Adachi won the 54th Annual Shogakukan Manga Award for shōnen manga for <i>Cross Game</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which was adapted into an anime television series which began airing on <a href="/wiki/TV_Tokyo" title="TV Tokyo">TV Tokyo</a> in April 2009.</p>
<p>Adachi began <i><a href="/wiki/Q_and_A_(manga)" title="Q and A (manga)">Q and A</a></i> in the inaugural issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Monthly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Sunday" title="Monthly Shōnen Sunday">Monthly Shōnen Sunday</a></i> in June 2009. <i><a href="/wiki/Asaoka_High_School_Baseball_Club_Diary:_Over_Fence" title="Asaoka High School Baseball Club Diary: Over Fence">Asaoka High School Baseball Club Diary: Over Fence</a></i> began in the April 27, 2011 issue of <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maicomi-20110420_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-maicomi-20110420-7">[7]</a></sup> In May 2012 he finished <i>Q and A</i> and began his new series, <a href="/wiki/Mix_(manga)" title="Mix (manga)"><i>Mix</i></a>, a semi-sequel to <i>Touch</i> set 30 years later, in the June 2012 issue of <i>Monthly Shōnen Sunday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Adachi's works have sold over 200 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maicomi-20110420_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-maicomi-20110420-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_people">Related people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mitsuru_Adachi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Related people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Tsutomu_Adachi" title="Tsutomu Adachi">Tsutomu Adachi</a></dt>
<dd>Mitsuru's older brother was a <a href="/wiki/Mangaka" title="Mangaka">manga artist</a> and an assistant to <a href="/wiki/Fujio_Akatsuka" title="Fujio Akatsuka">Fujio Akatsuka</a>. He died of <a href="/wiki/Stomach_cancer" title="Stomach cancer">stomach cancer</a> in 2004.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Shinji_Nagashima" title="Shinji Nagashima">Shinji Nagashima</a></dt>
<dd>Adachi became a fan of Nagashima around the age of 10, and began tracing his works. He became an assistant to Nagashima for a short time after graduating from high school. However, Nagashima suddenly moved overseas, so he then became an assistant to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isami_Ishii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isami Ishii (page does not exist)">Isami Ishii</a>. In the 16 October 2005 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Comic" title="Big Comic">Big Comic</a></i>, Adachi published a short work titled <i>The Runaway God</i> which was meant as a memorialization of Nagashima.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quick-japan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-quick-japan-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Rumiko_Takahashi" title="Rumiko Takahashi">Rumiko Takahashi</a></dt>
<dd>From the early 1980s, both Adachi and Takahashi were popular authors in <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Sunday" title="Weekly Shōnen Sunday">Weekly Shōnen Sunday</a></i> and they formed a friendly rivalry. He even commented about how he had a lot to live up to with how popular Takahashi was, especially with it being a <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dnen_manga" title="Shōnen manga">shōnen magazine</a>. Several times a year they would meet, to share their thoughts and ideas with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quick-japan2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-quick-japan2-15">[15]</a></sup> At the end of <i>Weekly Shōnen Sunday</i> issue 43 in 2006, the authors were asked, "If you could pick one penname to use which was different than your own, which one would you pick?", and Takahashi replied, "Adachi Mitsuru."</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Kazuhiko_Shimamoto" title="Kazuhiko Shimamoto">Kazuhiko Shimamoto</a></dt>
<dd>Shimamoto and Adachi are mutual fans of each others' works. The main character in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blazing_Transfer_Student" title="Blazing Transfer Student">Blazing Transfer Student</a></i> manga series, Noboru Takizawa, made a guest appearance in <i><a href="/wiki/Touch_(manga)" title="Touch (manga)">Touch</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quick-japan_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-quick-japan-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Mr._Pogo" title="Mr. Pogo">Mr. Pogo</a></dt>
<dd>Pogo and Adachi graduated in the same class and have spoken about this in various magazine interviews. The character Kōtarō Matsudaira from <i>Touch</i> is modeled after Pogo.</dd>
</dl>
