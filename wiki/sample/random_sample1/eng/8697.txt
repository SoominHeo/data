<p><i><b>The Creation of Adam</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> painting by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, which forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_ceiling" title="Sistine Chapel ceiling">Sistine Chapel's ceiling</a>, painted c. 1508–1512. It illustrates the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">creation narrative</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> in which <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father#Christianity" title="God the Father">God</a> breathes life into <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">man</a>. The fresco is part of a complex iconographic scheme and is chronologically the fourth in the series of panels depicting episodes from Genesis.</p>
<p>The image of the near-touching hands of God and Adam has become iconic of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humanity.</a> The painting has been reproduced in countless imitations and parodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pop_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pop-4">[4]</a></sup> Leonardo da Vinci's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Supper_(Leonardo_da_Vinci)" title="The Last Supper (Leonardo da Vinci)">The Last Supper</a></i> and Michelangelo's <i>Creation of Adam</i> are the most replicated religious paintings of all time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Creation_of_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_ceiling" title="Sistine Chapel ceiling">Sistine Chapel ceiling</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg/330px-Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg/495px-Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg/660px-Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vatican-ChapelleSixtine-Plafond.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Michelangelo painted the ceiling of the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a>; the work took approximately four years to complete (1508–12)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1505, Michelangelo was invited back to Rome by the newly elected <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a>. He was commissioned to build the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Pope_Julius_II" title="Tomb of Pope Julius II">Pope's tomb</a>, which was to include forty statues and be finished in five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldcheider14_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldcheider14-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the patronage of the Pope, Michelangelo experienced constant interruptions to his work on the tomb in order to accomplish numerous other tasks. Although Michelangelo worked on the tomb for 40 years, it was never finished to his satisfaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldcheider14_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goldcheider14-5">[5]</a></sup> It is located in the <a href="/wiki/San_Pietro_in_Vincoli" title="San Pietro in Vincoli">Church of S. Pietro in Vincoli</a> in Rome and is most famous for the central figure of <a href="/wiki/Moses_(Michelangelo)" title="Moses (Michelangelo)">Moses</a>, completed in 1516.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartz134_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bartz134-6">[6]</a></sup> Of the other statues intended for the tomb, two known as <i>the Heroic Captive</i> and <i>the Dying Captive</i>, are now in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louvre_Museum" title="Louvre Museum">Louvre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldcheider14_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goldcheider14-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the same period, Michelangelo painted the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel, which took approximately four years to complete (1508–1512).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartz134_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bartz134-6">[6]</a></sup> According to Condivi's account, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bramante" title="Bramante">Bramante</a>, who was working on the building of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St Peter's Basilica">St Peter's Basilica</a>, resented Michelangelo's commission for the Pope's tomb and convinced the Pope to commission him in a medium with which he was unfamiliar, in order that he might fail at the task.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Michelangelo was originally commissioned to paint the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" title="Twelve Apostles">Twelve Apostles</a> on the triangular pendentives that supported the ceiling, and cover the central part of the ceiling with ornament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldscheider12_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldscheider12-8">[8]</a></sup> Michelangelo persuaded Pope Julius to give him a free hand and proposed a different and more complex scheme, representing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_story" title="Genesis creation story">Creation</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_Man" title="Fall of Man">Fall of Man</a>, the Promise of Salvation through the prophets, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genealogy_of_Christ" title="Genealogy of Christ">genealogy of Christ</a>. The work is part of a larger scheme of decoration within the chapel which represents much of the doctrine of the Catholic Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldscheider12_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goldscheider12-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The composition stretches over 500 square metres of ceiling,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and contains over 300 figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldscheider12_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goldscheider12-8">[8]</a></sup> At its centre are nine episodes from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>, divided into three groups: God's Creation of the Earth; God's Creation of Humankind and their fall from God's grace; and lastly, the state of Humanity as represented by <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> and his family. On the pendentives supporting the ceiling are painted twelve men and women who prophesied the coming of Jesus; seven <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophets</a> of Israel and five <a href="/wiki/Sibyl" title="Sibyl">Sibyls</a>, prophetic women of the Classical world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldscheider12_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Goldscheider12-8">[8]</a></sup> Among the most famous paintings on the ceiling are <i>The Creation of Adam</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a></i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deluge_myth" title="Deluge myth"><i>Deluge</i></a>, the <i>Prophet <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Cumaean_Sibyl" title="Cumaean Sibyl">Cumaean Sibyl</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Creation_of_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg"><img alt="'Adam's Creation Sistine Chapel ceiling' by Michelangelo JBU33cut.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="4492" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg/600px-%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg/900px-%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg/1200px-%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg 2x" width="600"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%27Adam%27s_Creation_Sistine_Chapel_ceiling%27_by_Michelangelo_JBU33cut.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>God is depicted as an elderly white-bearded man wrapped in a swirling cloak while Adam, on the lower left, is completely nude. God's right arm is outstretched to impart the spark of life from his own finger into that of Adam, whose left arm is extended in a pose mirroring God's, a reminder that man is created in the image and likeness of God (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Gen+1%3A26&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Gen 1:26</a></span>). Another point is that Adam's finger and God's finger are not touching. It gives the impression that God, the giver of life, is reaching out to Adam who has yet to receive it; they are not on "the same level" as would be two humans shaking hands, for instance.</p>
<p>Many hypotheses have been formulated regarding the identity and meaning of the figures around God. The person protected by God's left arm might be <a href="/wiki/Eve" title="Eve">Eve</a> due to the figure's feminine appearance and gaze towards Adam, but was also suggested to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sophia_(wisdom)" title="Sophia (wisdom)">Sophia</a> goddess of wisdom, the personified human soul, or an angel of feminine build.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Creation of Adam</i> is generally thought to depict the excerpt "God created man in his own image, in the image of God He created him" (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Gen+1%3A27&amp;version=NTSV" rel="nofollow">Gen 1:27</a></span>). The inspiration for Michelangelo's treatment of the subject may come from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymn</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Veni_Creator_Spiritus" title="Veni Creator Spiritus">Veni Creator Spiritus</a>", which asks the 'finger of the paternal right hand' (<i>digitus paternae dexterae</i>) to give the faithful speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Creation_of_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Michelangelo's main source of inspiration for his Adam in his <i>Creation of Adam</i> may have been a <a href="/wiki/Cameo_(carving)" title="Cameo (carving)">cameo</a> showing a nude <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus Caesar</a> riding sidesaddle on a <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_(astrology)" title="Capricorn (astrology)">Capricorn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This cameo is now at <a href="/wiki/Alnwick_Castle" title="Alnwick Castle">Alnwick Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The cameo used to belong to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a> <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Grimani" title="Domenico Grimani">Domenico Grimani</a> who lived in Rome while Michelangelo painted the ceiling. Evidence suggests that Michelangelo and Grimani were friends. Some scholars have been dissatisfied with the theory Michelangelo being mainly inspired by <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ghiberti" title="Lorenzo Ghiberti">Lorenzo Ghiberti</a>'s Adam in his <i>Creation of Adam</i>. This cameo offers an alternative theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Creation_of_Adam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several hypotheses have been put forward about the meaning of <i>The Creation of Adam'</i>s highly original composition, many of them taking Michelangelo's well-documented expertise in human anatomy as their starting point. In 1990, an <a href="/wiki/Anderson,_Indiana" title="Anderson, Indiana">Anderson, Indiana</a> <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a>, Frank Meshberger, noted in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">Journal of the American Medical Association</a></i> that the background figures and shapes portrayed behind the figure of God appeared to be an anatomically accurate picture of the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meshberger_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meshberger-15">[15]</a></sup> On close examination, borders in the painting correlate with major <a href="/wiki/Sulcus_(neuroanatomy)" title="Sulcus (neuroanatomy)">sulci</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telencephalon" title="Telencephalon">cerebrum</a> in the inner and outer surface of the brain, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_stem" title="Brain stem">brain stem</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe" title="Frontal lobe">frontal lobe</a>, the basilar artery, the <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Optic_chiasm" title="Optic chiasm">optic chiasm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meshberger_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meshberger-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternatively, it has been observed that the red cloth around God has the shape of a human <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> (one art historian has called it a "uterine mantle"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>) and that the scarf hanging out, coloured green, could be a newly cut <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cord" title="Umbilical cord">umbilical cord</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Recently a group of Italian researchers published on <i><a href="/wiki/Mayo_Clinic_Proceedings" title="Mayo Clinic Proceedings">Mayo Clinic Proceedings</a></i> an article where the images of the mantle and the postpartum uterus were overlapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> According to Enrico Bruschini (2004), "This is an interesting hypothesis that presents the Creation scene as an idealised representation of the physical birth of man ("The Creation"). It explains the <a href="/wiki/Navel" title="Navel">navel</a> that appears on <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>, which is at first perplexing because he was created, not born of a woman."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
