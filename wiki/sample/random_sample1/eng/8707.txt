<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Adidas Telstar</h1>

<p><b>Telstar</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Ball_(association_football)" title="Ball (association football)">football</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a>. The 32-panel design of the ball, based on the work of <a href="/wiki/Eigil_Nielsen_(footballer,_born_1918)" title="Eigil Nielsen (footballer, born 1918)">Eigil Nielsen</a>, has become iconic and is the standard design now used to portray a football in different media.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ball">Ball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adidas_Telstar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ball was first introduced as the <b>Telstar Elast</b> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1968_European_Football_Championship" title="1968 European Football Championship">1968 European Football Championship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blizzard6_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-blizzard6-1">[1]</a></sup> It was also the official match <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_football_(ball)" title="Association football (ball)">ball</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1970_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1970 FIFA World Cup">1970 FIFA World Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifalist_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fifalist-2">[2]</a></sup> The ball was then used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1972_European_Football_Championship" title="1972 European Football Championship">1972</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1976_European_Football_Championship" title="1976 European Football Championship">1976</a> European Championships. The similar <b>Telstar Durlast</b> was one of two official balls, along with the <i>Chile Durlast</i>, of the <a href="/wiki/1974_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1974 FIFA World Cup">1974 FIFA World Cup</a> held in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifalist_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-fifalist-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soccerballworld_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-soccerballworld-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Telstar was the first World Cup ball to use the now-familiar <a href="/wiki/Truncated_icosahedron" title="Truncated icosahedron">truncated icosahedron</a> for its design, consisting of 12 black <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagonal</a> and 20 white <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagonal</a> panels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifalist_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-fifalist-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soccerballworld_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-soccerballworld-3">[3]</a></sup> The 32-panel configuration had been introduced in 1962 by <a href="/wiki/Select_Sport" title="Select Sport">Select Sport</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernsen_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernsen-5">[5]</a></sup> and was also used in the official logo for the 1970 World Cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The black-and-white pattern, to aid visibility on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_and_white_television" title="Black and white television">black and white television</a> broadcasts, was also well established before the Telstar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernsen_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernsen-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The name came from the <a href="/wiki/Telstar" title="Telstar">Telstar communications satellite</a>, which was roughly spherical and dotted with solar panels, somewhat similar in appearance to the football.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifalist_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-fifalist-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ball was made of <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifalist_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-fifalist-2">[2]</a></sup> The 1974 model's "Durlast" <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> coating provided <a href="/wiki/Waterproofing" title="Waterproofing">waterproofing</a> as well as protection from damage such as scuffs and tears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only 20 Telstars were provided for the World Cup; an estimated 600,000 replicas were sold subsequently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifalist_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-fifalist-2">[2]</a></sup> Some 1970 matches were played with a brown ball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The 1974 Chile Durlast was all white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soccerballworld_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-soccerballworld-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
