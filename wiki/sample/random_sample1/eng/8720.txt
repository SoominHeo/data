<p><b>Hirohiko Araki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">荒木 飛呂彦</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>Araki Hirohiko</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born June 7, 1960 in <a href="/wiki/Sendai" title="Sendai">Sendai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miyagi_Prefecture" title="Miyagi Prefecture">Miyagi</a>)</span> is a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Mangaka" title="Mangaka">manga artist</a>. He made his debut under the name <b>Toshiyuki Araki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">荒木 利之</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Araki Toshiyuki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in 1980 with his one-shot <i>Poker Under Arms</i>, and began his professional career with the short series <i>Cool Shock B.T.</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Baoh" title="Baoh">Baoh</a></i>, and <i>The Gorgeous Irene</i>. Araki is best known for his long-running series <i><a href="/wiki/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure" title="JoJo's Bizarre Adventure">JoJo's Bizarre Adventure</a></i>, first published in <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Jump" title="Weekly Shōnen Jump">Weekly Shōnen Jump</a></i> in 1986 and which to date has sold over 100 million copies in Japan alone. The story frequently references <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, both of which Araki is reportedly very fond of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lecture3-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hirohiko_Araki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hirohiko_Araki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Araki grew up in Sendai, Japan with his parents and younger identical twin sisters. He cites his sisters' annoyances as the reason he spent time alone in his room reading manga, naming <i><a href="/wiki/Ai_to_Makoto" title="Ai to Makoto">Ai to Makoto</a></i> as the most important one to him,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lecture3-1">[1]</a></sup> and his father's art books, he supposes this was his motive for drawing manga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-2">[2]</a></sup> He was particularly influenced by the work of French artist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After a school friend praised his manga, he began secretly drawing manga behind his parent's backs. He submitted his first work to a magazine in his first year of high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-2">[2]</a></sup> All his submissions were rejected while other artists his age or younger were making successful debuts. He decided to go to the publishers' offices in Tokyo to find out why in person, taking a manga he stayed up all-night to finish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Shueisha" title="Shueisha">Shueisha</a> editor he met highly criticized the work, but said it had potential and to clean it up for the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Tezuka_Award" title="Tezuka Award">Tezuka Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-2">[2]</a></sup> The manga was <i>Poker Under Arms</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Debut_and_JoJo.27s_Bizarre_Adventure">Debut and <i>JoJo's Bizarre Adventure</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hirohiko_Araki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Debut and JoJo's Bizarre Adventure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Araki left <a href="/wiki/Miyagi_University_of_Education" title="Miyagi University of Education">Miyagi University of Education</a> before graduating, and made his debut in 1980 with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_west" title="Wild west">wild west</a> one-shot <i>Poker Under Arms</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup> which was a "Selected Work" at that year's Tezuka Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lecture_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-lecture-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup> His first serialization was <i>Cool Shock B.T.</i> in 1983, about a young magician who solves mysteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup> But the first series to display his signature amount of <a href="/wiki/Graphic_violence" title="Graphic violence">gore</a> was 1984's <i><a href="/wiki/Baoh" title="Baoh">Baoh</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup> It tells the story of a man who is implanted with a parasite by an evil organization, giving him superhuman powers, and follows as he fights against them. It was adapted into an <a href="/wiki/Original_video_animation" title="Original video animation">OVA</a> in 1989, the manga was released in the US by <a href="/wiki/Viz_Media" title="Viz Media">Viz Media</a> in 1990 (in <i><a href="/wiki/Tank%C5%8Dbon" title="Tankōbon">tankōbon</a></i> form in 1995), but the OVA didn't get a stateside release until 2002. It wasn't until <i>The Gorgeous Irene</i> in 1985, that he really developed his signature art style of buff, muscular characters (it would later become more flamboyant).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>His next series would become his <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a>, 1986's <i><a href="/wiki/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure" title="JoJo's Bizarre Adventure">JoJo's Bizarre Adventure</a></i>. The series takes place in 1880's <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and follows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jonathan_Joestar" title="Jonathan Joestar">Jonathan Joestar</a> (JoJo) and his adopted brother <a href="/wiki/Dio_Brando" title="Dio Brando">Dio Brando</a>, the latter eventually trying to kill their father to get the inheritance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup> When confronted, Dio puts on an ancient mask that turns him into a <a href="/wiki/Vampire" title="Vampire">vampire</a>. JoJo then learns a martial arts technique called <i>hamon</i> (or ripple), and travels to Dio's castle to kill him. Subsequent arcs of <i>JoJo</i> follow different descendants of the Joestar family, and many are set in different parts of the world. Part 3, which would become the most popular, downplays the vampire story and ripple technique and instead introduces the power of <a href="/wiki/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure#Stand_powers" title="JoJo's Bizarre Adventure">Stands</a>, which continues in the series today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000manga_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-1000manga-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_work">Recent work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hirohiko_Araki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The September 2007 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Cell_(journal)" title="Cell (journal)">Cell</a></i> had a cover drawn by Araki with a <a href="/wiki/Ligase" title="Ligase">ligase</a> represented as one of his Stands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2008, Araki drew the cover art for a collection featuring <a href="/wiki/Yasunari_Kawabata" title="Yasunari Kawabata">Yasunari Kawabata</a>'s short story "<a href="/wiki/The_Dancing_Girl_of_Izu" title="The Dancing Girl of Izu">The Dancing Girl of Izu</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He drew the cover for the limited edition of <a href="/wiki/Base_Ball_Bear" title="Base Ball Bear">Base Ball Bear</a>'s "Breeeeze Girl" single, which actually takes an image from the <i>JoJo</i> manga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 2009, Hirohiko Araki was one of five artists selected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre" title="Musée du Louvre">Musée du Louvre</a> to create original works set at the famous museum. His piece <i>Rohan at the Louvre</i> starred JoJo's <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Is_Unbreakable#Characters" title="Diamond Is Unbreakable">Rohan Kishibe</a> and was shown at the exhibit titled Le Louvre invite la bande dessinée ("The Louvre Invites Comic-Strip Art"), which was created to show the diversity of comics, from January 19 to April 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louvre_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Louvre-9">[9]</a></sup> The following year, <i>Rohan at the Louvre</i> was published in France and ran in Japan's <i>Ultra Jump</i>. It was released in the US by <a href="/wiki/NBM_Publishing" title="NBM Publishing">NBM Publishing</a> in February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>From September 17 to October 6, 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Gucci" title="Gucci">Gucci</a> store in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shinjuku,_Tokyo" title="Shinjuku, Tokyo">Shinjuku</a> hosted the Gucci x Hirohiko Araki x Spur "Rohan Kishibe Goes to Gucci" Exhibition, a collaboration between the luxury Italian clothing brand, JoJo's creator and the fashion magazine <i>Spur</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gucci_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gucci-11">[11]</a></sup> The exhibit celebrated the 90th anniversary of Gucci and featured a life-size figure of Rohan Kishibe, as well as numerous illustrations by Araki; including actual pieces of the brand's own 2011-2012 fall/winter collection and his own original fashion designs. For <i>Spur</i>, Araki drew <i>Kishibe Rohan meets Gucci.</i>, a full-color one-shot featuring Rohan Kishibe that ran in its October 2011 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Spur</i> once again ran a <i>JoJo</i> spinoff by Araki, <i>Jolyne, Fly High with Gucci</i> starring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jolyne_Cujoh" title="Jolyne Cujoh">Jolyne Cujoh</a> from Part 6, in their February 2013 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jolyne_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jolyne-13">[13]</a></sup> A free English translation is available on Gucci's <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> page.</p>
<p>To raise awareness of the ongoing reconstruction efforts of the <a href="/wiki/Historic_Monuments_and_Sites_of_Hiraizumi" title="Historic Monuments and Sites of Hiraizumi">Hiraizumi ruins</a>, that were damaged by the March <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a> and declared a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in June, Araki drew artwork depicting the ruins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A "Hirohiko Araki JoJo Exhibition" opened in Araki's native Sendai at the end of July 2012 to celebrate the 25th anniversary of <i>JoJo's Bizarre Adventure</i>, it then moved to Tokyo in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>He drew the album cover for <a href="/wiki/Sayuri_Ishikawa" title="Sayuri Ishikawa">Sayuri Ishikawa</a>'s 2012 album <i><a href="/wiki/X_-Cross-" title="X -Cross-">X-Cross</a></i>, where she performs one of the series' iconic poses and is drawn wearing jewelry from the manga, the cover of the 2012 reprint of <a href="/wiki/Tamaki_Sait%C5%8D" title="Tamaki Saitō">Tamaki Saitō</a>'s <i>Lacan for Surviving</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the cover of the 2015 compilation album for composer <a href="/wiki/Akira_Senju" title="Akira Senju">Akira Senju</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>A book explaining Araki's methodology on creating manga, titled <i>Araki Hirohiko no Manga Jutsu</i>, was released on April 17, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A stage adaptation of Araki's 1994 one-shot <i>Under Execution, Under Jailbreak</i> will run from November 20 to November 29, 2015, at the Galaxy Theatre before touring the country in December. The play will also include elements of his 1996 one-shot <i>Dolce, and His Master.</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
