<p><b>Arenal Volcano</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><b>Volcán Arenal</b></span>, is an active <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a> in north-western <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> around 90 km northwest of <a href="/wiki/San_Jos%C3%A9,_Costa_Rica" title="San José, Costa Rica">San José</a>, in the province of <a href="/wiki/Alajuela_Province" title="Alajuela Province">Alajuela</a>, canton of <a href="/wiki/San_Carlos_(canton)" title="San Carlos (canton)">San Carlos</a>, and district of <a href="/wiki/La_Fortuna,_Costa_Rica" title="La Fortuna, Costa Rica">La Fortuna</a>. The Arenal volcano measures at least 1,633 metres (5,358 ft) high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRVSO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRVSO-2">[2]</a></sup> It is conically shaped with a crater 140 metres (460 ft) in diameter. Geologically, Arenal is considered a young volcano and it is estimated to be less than 7,500 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Arenal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Arenal-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also known as "Pan de Azúcar", "Canaste", "Volcan Costa Rica", "Volcan Río Frío" or "Guatusos Peak".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Arenal_Subsym_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Arenal_Subsym-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The volcano was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dormant_volcano" title="Dormant volcano">dormant</a> for hundreds of years and exhibited a single crater at its summit, with minor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumaroles" title="Fumaroles">fumaroles</a> activity, covered by dense vegetation. In 1968 it erupted unexpectedly, destroying the small town of Tabacón. Due to the eruption three more craters were created on the western flanks but only one of them still exists today. Since 2010, Arenal has been dormant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_setting_and_description">Geographic setting and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographic setting and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arenal is one of seven historically active Costa Rican volcanoes along with <a href="/wiki/Po%C3%A1s_Volcano" title="Poás Volcano">Poás</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraz%C3%BA_Volcano" title="Irazú Volcano">Irazú</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miravalles_Volcano" title="Miravalles Volcano">Miravalles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oros%C3%AD_Volcano" title="Orosí Volcano">Orosí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rinc%C3%B3n_de_la_Vieja_Volcano" title="Rincón de la Vieja Volcano">Rincón de la Vieja</a> complex, and <a href="/wiki/Turrialba_Volcano" title="Turrialba Volcano">Turrialba</a>. It was Costa Rica's most active volcano until 2010, and one of the ten most active volcanoes in the world. It has been studied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismologists" title="Seismologists">seismologists</a> for many years.</p>
<p>The volcano is located at the center of <a href="/wiki/Arenal_Volcano_National_Park" title="Arenal Volcano National Park">Arenal Volcano National Park</a> in the northern zone of the country, 15 kilometres (9.3 mi) southwest of the <a href="/wiki/La_Fortuna,_Costa_Rica" title="La Fortuna, Costa Rica">La Fortuna</a> district in <a href="/wiki/San_Carlos_(canton)" title="San Carlos (canton)">San Carlos (canton)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The temperature varies from 9 °C (48 °F) in the high areas to 89 °C (192 °F) in the low areas and the annual rainfall is around 349 centimetres (137 in).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Arenal Volcano area is an important <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">watershed</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Arenal" title="Lake Arenal">Arenal Lake Reservoir</a>. The reservoir's water is used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a>. It is also connected to the national system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arenal has several eruptive vents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chato_Volcano" title="Chato Volcano">Chato</a> is a dormant <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcanic cone</a>. It is believed Chato first erupted 38,000 years ago during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> period and last erupted about 3,500 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Arenal_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Arenal-3">[3]</a></sup> Chatito <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Arenal_Volcano&amp;params=10.438_N_84.687_W_type:mountain_region:CR&amp;title=Chatito+volcano"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">10°26′17″N</span> <span class="longitude">84°41′13″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">10.438°N 84.687°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">10.438; -84.687</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">Chatito volcano</span>)</span></span></span></a></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Lava_dome" title="Lava dome">lava dome</a> with an elevation of 1,100 metres (3,609 ft). Espina is another lava dome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Arenal_Subsym_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Arenal_Subsym-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geologic_history">Geologic history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geologic history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arenal is the youngest and most active of all the volcanoes in Costa Rica. Scientists have been able to date its activity back to more than 7000 years ago. The area remained largely unexplored until 1937, when a documented expedition took place to reach the summit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It has been considered eruptive since 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Arenal_-_Eruptive_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Arenal_-_Eruptive-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="July_29.2C_1968">July 29, 1968</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: July 29, 1968">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Monday, July 29, 1968 at 7:30 AM, the Arenal Volcano suddenly and violently erupted. The eruptions continued unabated for several days, burying over 15 square kilometers under rocks, lava and ash. When it was finally over, the eruptions had killed 87 people and buried 3 small villages – Tabacón, Pueblo Nuevo and San Luís – and affected more than 232 square kilometers of land. Crops were spoiled, property was ruined, and large amounts of livestock were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the height of its ferocious activity, the volcano flung giant rocks – some weighing several tons – more than a kilometer away at a rate of 600 meters per second. These explosions would go on to form three new active craters.</p>
<p>As the three towns were destroyed on the western side of the volcano, a town by the name of El Borio on the east side was untouched and unharmed . It is a popular myth that after the volcano ceased to erupt, El Borio was renamed La Fortuna, which means "the fortunate", referring to its luckiness that the volcano erupted to the west and not the east. However, the town was renamed La Fortuna by its residents before the 1968 eruption. La Fortuna means "The Fortune", and refers to the flat, fertile lands in the area, which are unlike the rough, mountainous terrain which surrounds most of the Arenal volcano. If the town had been renamed "The Fortunate", it would have been called "El Afortunado."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="June_1975">June 1975</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: June 1975">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between the 17th and the 21st of June 1975 several avalanches went down from one of the craters. The vegetation along Tabacon River was destroyed and a great amount of material was deposited on the riverbed. Four strong explosions also blew large amounts of ash into the sky. The ash was spread within a distance of 26 kilometers (16 mi).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="June_1984">June 1984</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: June 1984">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After a period of high effusive activity of intermittent lava flows, a new explosive phase began with 3 to 20 explosions per day of low to moderate magnitude. These explosions of steam, water, gases and ash reached altitudes of up to 5 kilometers where winds again carried the material across the Arenal reservoir (Lake Arenal) and over the town of <a href="/wiki/Tilar%C3%A1n" title="Tilarán">Tilarán</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="August_1993">August 1993</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: August 1993">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A northwest wall of one of the craters collapsed and generated several pyroclastic flows. The collapsed crater wall was shaped as a V, about 60 meters deep and 100 meters wide. In this V-shaped crater the lava started to flow again.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="March_1994">March 1994</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: March 1994">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flows started to fill the U-shaped crater and deposited materials around the crater.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="March_1996">March 1996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: March 1996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At this time the volcano started to produce regular lava flows, accompanied by intermittent explosions of gases. This was the regular activity of the volcano until May 5, 1998.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_5.2C_1998">May 5, 1998</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: May 5, 1998">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Arenal Volcano experienced a series of large eruptions on Tuesday afternoon, May 5, 1998. The first ratchet eruption was recorded at 1:05 p.m. when part of the northwest wall of the crater fell apart. Large amounts of lava, rocks and ash flew out of the volcano during this explosion. Another eruption took place at 2:20 p.m. with material emerging from the same part of the volcano.</p>
<p>A specialist from the Costa Rica Volcanic and Seismic Observatory explained that the eruptions are nothing unusual for the volcano. Nevertheless, during this occasion the amount of lava within the crater was significantly greater than normal and therefore more material was poured out. This time a landslide (avalanche) took place, too, as a part of the crater wall fell apart on the northwest side. The phenomenon occurs sporadically, although this time the consequences were greater than usual.</p>
<p>As a normal precaution, authorities declared a red alert, closed the road between La Fortuna and Tilarán, which runs around the north side of the volcano, and evacuated approximately 450 people (mostly tourists) from the immediate area,including several hotels and tourism oriented businesses. There were no reports of injuries caused by the volcanic activity.</p>
<p>At 5:20 p.m. on Tuesday the volcano was still discharging material, but activity had decreased significantly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_7.2C_1998">May 7, 1998</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenal_Volcano&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: May 7, 1998">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eruptions of May 7, 1998 damaged two square kilometers and destroyed a 400 by 100 meter area of green forest in the vicinity of Arenal Volcano. A fissure, 500 meters long and 10 meters deep, was opened up in the wall of the crater and all the material slid down the side of the volcano.</p>
<p>During this day 23 eruptions were reported, between 1:05 p.m. and 7:00 p.m., and thereafter the volcano returned to its normal state. Authorities reported no unusual behavior and the national park was reopened the same week. Local seismologists investigate the activity of the volcano and park rangers continue to vigorously enforce the safety perimeter.</p>
