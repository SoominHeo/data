<p>The <b>Arecibo Observatory</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a> in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Arecibo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Arecibo, Puerto Rico">Arecibo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a> is operated by <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universities_Space_Research_Association" title="Universities Space Research Association">USRA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_University_(Puerto_Rico)" title="Metropolitan University (Puerto Rico)">UMET</a>, under cooperative agreement with the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation (NSF)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sri_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sri-4">[4]</a></sup> The observatory is the sole facility of the National Astronomy and Ionosphere Center (NAIC), which refers to the observatory, and the staff that operates it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010RFP_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010RFP-5">[5]</a></sup> From its construction in the 1960s until 2011, the observatory was managed by <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>.</p>
<p>The observatory's 1,000-foot (305-metre) <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a> was the largest single-aperture telescope from its completion in 1963 until July 2016 when the <a href="/wiki/Five_hundred_meter_Aperture_Spherical_Telescope" title="Five hundred meter Aperture Spherical Telescope">Five hundred meter Aperture Spherical Telescope</a> was completed. It is used in three major areas of research: <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_science" title="Atmospheric science">atmospheric science</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">radar astronomy</a>. Scientists who want to use the observatory submit proposals that are evaluated by an independent scientific board.</p>
<p>The observatory has appeared in film and television productions, gaining more recognition in 1999 when it began to collect data for the <a href="/wiki/SETI@home" title="SETI@home">SETI@home</a> project. It has been listed on the American <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> starting in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newlistings20081003_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-newlistings20081003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrhpreg_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpreg-6">[6]</a></sup> It was the featured listing in the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>'s weekly list of October 3, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-featured_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-featured-7">[7]</a></sup> The center was named an <a href="/wiki/List_of_IEEE_milestones" title="List of IEEE milestones">IEEE Milestone</a> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It has a visitor center that is open part-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_information">General information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main collecting dish is 305 m (1,000 ft) in diameter, constructed inside the depression left by a <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkhole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The dish surface is made of 38,778 perforated aluminum panels, each about 3 by 6 feet (1 by 2 m), supported by a mesh of steel cables. The ground beneath is accessible and supports shade-tolerant vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The observatory has four <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> transmitters, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EIRP" title="EIRP">effective isotropic radiated powers</a> of 20 TW (continuous) at 2380 MHz, 2.5 TW (pulse peak) at 430 MHz, 300 MW at 47 MHz, and 6 MW at 8 MHz.</p>
<p>The reflector is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical_reflector" title="Spherical reflector">spherical reflector</a>, not a <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflector</a>. To aim the device, the receiver is moved to intercept signals reflected from different directions by the spherical dish surface of 270 m (870 ft) radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A parabolic mirror would have varying <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism_(optical_systems)" title="Astigmatism (optical systems)">astigmatism</a> when the receiver is off the focal point, but the <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">error of a spherical mirror</a> is uniform in every direction.</p>
<p>The receiver is on a 900-ton platform suspended 150 m (492 ft) above the dish by 18 cables running from three <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> towers, one 111 m (365 ft) high and the other two 81 m (265 ft) high, placing their tops at the same elevation. The platform has a rotating, bow-shaped track 93 m (305 ft) long, called the <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a> arm, carrying the receiving antennas and secondary and tertiary reflectors. This allows the telescope to observe any region of the sky in a forty-degree cone of visibility about the local <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a> (between −1 and 38 degrees of <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a>). <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>'s location near the Northern <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic</a> allows Arecibo to view the planets in the Solar System over the Northern half of their orbit. The round trip light time to objects beyond <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> is longer than the 2.6 hour time that the telescope can track a celestial position, preventing <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> observations of more distant objects.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_architecture">Design and architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design and architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg/220px-Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg/330px-Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg/440px-Arecibo_Observatory_Aerial.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The origins of the observatory trace to late 1950s efforts to develop <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">anti-ballistic missile</a> (ABM) defences as part of the newly formed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">ARPA's</a> ABM umbrella-effort, Project Defender. Even at this early stage it was clear that the use of <a href="/wiki/Chaff_(countermeasure)" title="Chaff (countermeasure)">radar decoys</a> would be a serious problem at the long ranges needed to successfully attack a warhead, ranges on the order of 1,000 miles (1,600 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darpa_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-darpa-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-press-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the many Defender projects were several studies based on the concept that a re-entering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_warhead" title="Nuclear warhead">nuclear warhead</a> would cause unique physical signatures while still in the upper atmosphere. It was known that hot, high-speed objects caused ionization of the atmosphere that reflects <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> waves, and it appeared that a warhead's signature would be different enough from decoys that a detector could pick out the warhead directly, or alternately, provide added information that would allow operators to focus a conventional tracking radar on the single return from the warhead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darpa_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-darpa-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-press-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the concept appeared to offer a solution to the tracking problem, there was almost no information on either the physics of re-entry or a strong understanding of the normal composition of the upper layers of the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. ARPA began to address both simultaneously. To better understand the radar returns from a warhead, several radars were built on <a href="/wiki/Kwajalein_Atoll" title="Kwajalein Atoll">Kwajalein Atoll</a>, while Arecibo started with the dual purpose of understanding the ionosphere's F-layer while also producing a general-purpose scientific radio observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darpa_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-darpa-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-press-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The observatory was built between mid-1960 and November 1963 and designed by <a href="/wiki/William_E._Gordon" title="William E. Gordon">William E. Gordon</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, who intended to use it to study Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. He was attracted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinkholes" title="Sinkholes">sinkholes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> regions of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> that offered perfect cavities for a very large dish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Originally, a fixed parabolic reflector was envisioned, pointing in a fixed direction with a 150 m (492 ft) tower to hold equipment at the focus. This design would have limited its use in other research areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">radar astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> and atmospheric science, which require the ability to point at different positions in the sky and track those positions for an extended time as Earth rotates. Ward Low of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Advanced Research Projects Agency">Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (ARPA) pointed out this flaw and put Gordon in touch with the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Cambridge_Research_Laboratories" title="Air Force Cambridge Research Laboratories">Air Force Cambridge Research Laboratory</a> (AFCRL) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, where one group headed by Phil Blacksmith was working on spherical reflectors and another group was studying the propagation of <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> in and through the upper atmosphere. Cornell University proposed the project to ARPA in mid-1958 and a contract was signed between the AFCRL and the University in November 1959. Cornell University and Zachary Sears published a request for proposals (RFP) asking for a design to support a feed moving along a spherical surface 435 feet (133 m) above the stationary reflector. The RFP suggested a tripod or a tower in the center to support the feed. On the day the project for the design and construction of the antenna was announced at Cornell University, Gordon had also envisioned a 435 ft (133 m) tower centered in the 1,000 ft (305 m) reflector to support the feed.</p>
<p>George Doundoulakis, who directed research at General Bronze Corporation in Garden City, New York, along with Zachary Sears, who directed Internal Design at Digital B &amp; E Corporation, New York, received the RFP from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> for the antenna design and studied the idea of suspending the feed with his brother, <a href="/wiki/Helias_Doundoulakis" title="Helias Doundoulakis">Helias Doundoulakis</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">civil engineer</a>. George Doundoulakis identified the problem that a tower or tripod would have presented around the center, (the most important area of the reflector), and devised a better design by suspending the feed. He presented his proposal to Cornell for a doughnut <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">truss</a> suspended by four cables from four towers above the reflector, having along its edge a rail track for the <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a> <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">truss</a> positioning. This second <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">truss</a>, in the form of an arc, or <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arch</a>, was to be suspended below, which would rotate on the rails through 360 degrees. The arc also had rails on which the unit supporting the feed would move for the feed's elevational positioning. A <a href="/wiki/Counterweight" title="Counterweight">counterweight</a> would move symmetrically opposite to the feed for stability and, if a hurricane struck, the whole feed could be raised and lowered. <a href="/wiki/Helias_Doundoulakis" title="Helias Doundoulakis">Helias Doundoulakis</a> designed the <a href="/wiki/Cable" title="Cable">cable</a> suspension system which was finally adopted. Although the present configuration is substantially the same as the original drawings by George and Helias, (though with three towers instead of the original four), the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">U.S. Patent office</a> granted Helias a patent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> for the brothers' innovative idea. Another assignee on the patent, <a href="/wiki/William_J._Casey" title="William J. Casey">William J. Casey</a>, later became director of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> under President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>.</p>
<p>The idea of a spherical reflecting mirror with a steerable secondary has since been used in optical telescopes, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Hobby%E2%80%93Eberly_Telescope" title="Hobby–Eberly Telescope">Hobby–Eberly Telescope</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_African_Large_Telescope" title="Southern African Large Telescope">Southern African Large Telescope</a>.</p>
<p>Construction began in mid-1960, with the official opening on November 1, 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As the primary dish is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical" title="Spherical">spherical</a>, its focus is along a line rather than at one point, as would be the case for a <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflector</a>. As a result, complex line feeds were implemented to carry out observations. Each line feed covered a narrow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_range" title="Frequency range">frequency band</a>: 2–5% of the center frequency of the band. A limited number of line feeds could be used at any one time, limiting the telescope's flexibility.</p>
<p>Since then, the telescope has been upgraded several times. Initially, when the maximum expected operating frequency was about 500 MHz, the surface consisted of half-inch galvanized wire mesh laid directly on the support cables. In 1974, a high-precision surface consisting of 40,000 individually adjustable aluminum panels replaced the old wire mesh, and the highest usable frequency rose to about 5000 MHz. A <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_telescope" title="Gregorian telescope">Gregorian reflector system</a> was installed in 1997, incorporating secondary and tertiary reflectors to focus radio waves at one point. This allowed installing a suite of receivers, covering the full 1–10 GHz range, that could be easily moved to the <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focal point</a>, giving Arecibo more flexibility. A metal mesh screen was also installed around the perimeter to block the ground's thermal radiation from reaching the feed antennas. Finally, a more powerful 2400 MHz transmitter was added.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_discoveries">Research and discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research and discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Many scientific discoveries have been made with the observatory. On April 7, 1964, soon after it began operating, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Pettengill" title="Gordon Pettengill">Gordon Pettengill</a>'s team used it to determine that the <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a> period of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> was not 88 days, as formerly thought, but only 59 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1968, the discovery of the periodicity of the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Pulsar" title="Crab Pulsar">Crab Pulsar</a> (33 milliseconds) by Lovelace and others provided the first solid evidence that <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron stars</a> exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Russell_Alan_Hulse" title="Russell Alan Hulse">Hulse</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Hooton_Taylor,_Jr." title="Joseph Hooton Taylor, Jr.">Taylor</a> discovered the first binary pulsar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PSR_B1913%2B16" title="PSR B1913+16">PSR B1913+16</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> an accomplishment for which they later received the Nobel Prize in Physics. In 1982, the first <a href="/wiki/Millisecond_pulsar" title="Millisecond pulsar">millisecond pulsar</a>, <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1937%2B21" title="PSR B1937+21">PSR B1937+21</a>, was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Donald_C._Backer" title="Donald C. Backer">Donald C. Backer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrinivas_Kulkarni" title="Shrinivas Kulkarni">Shrinivas Kulkarni</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Heiles" title="Carl Heiles">Carl Heiles</a>, Michael Davis, and Miller Goss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This object spins 642 times per second and, until the discovery of <a href="/wiki/PSR_J1748-2446ad" title="PSR J1748-2446ad">PSR J1748-2446ad</a> in 2005, was identified as the fastest-spinning pulsar.</p>
<p>In August 1989, the observatory directly imaged an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> for the first time in history: <a href="/wiki/4769_Castalia" title="4769 Castalia">4769 Castalia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The following year, Polish astronomer <a href="/wiki/Aleksander_Wolszczan" title="Aleksander Wolszczan">Aleksander Wolszczan</a> made the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a> <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1257%2B12" title="PSR B1257+12">PSR B1257+12</a>, which later led him to discover its three orbiting planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> These were the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planets</a> discovered. In 1994, John Harmon used the Arecibo Radio Telescope to map the distribution of ice in the polar regions of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2008, detection of prebiotic molecules <a href="/wiki/Imine" title="Imine">methanimine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a> were reported from the observatory's radio spectroscopy measurements of the distant starburst galaxy <a href="/wiki/Arp_220" title="Arp 220">Arp 220</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily2008_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily2008-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>From January 2010 to February 2011, American astronomers Matthew Route and <a href="/wiki/Aleksander_Wolszczan" title="Aleksander Wolszczan">Aleksander Wolszczan</a> detected bursts of radio emission from the T6.5 brown dwarf 2MASS J10475385+2124234. This was the first time that radio emission had been detected from a T dwarf, which has methane absorption lines in its atmosphere. It is also the coolest brown dwarf (at a temperature of ~900K) from which radio emission has been observed. The highly polarized and highly energetic radio bursts indicated that the object has a &gt;1.7 kG-strength magnetic field and magnetic activity similar to both the planet <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="SETI.2C_METI">SETI, METI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: SETI, METI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Arecibo_Message">The Arecibo Message</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Arecibo Message">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arecibo_Message" title="Arecibo Message">Arecibo Message</a></div>
<p>In 1974, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arecibo_Message" title="Arecibo Message">Arecibo Message</a>, an attempt to communicate with potential <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a>, was transmitted from the radio telescope toward the globular cluster <a href="/wiki/Messier_13" title="Messier 13">Messier 13</a>, about 25,000 light-years away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The 1,679 <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> pattern of 1s and 0s defined a 23 by 73 pixel <a href="/wiki/Bitmap" title="Bitmap">bitmap</a> image that included numbers, stick figures, chemical formulas and a crude image of the telescope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_RuBisCo_Stars">The RuBisCo Stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The RuBisCo Stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/RuBisCo_Stars" title="RuBisCo Stars">RuBisCo Stars</a></div>
<p>On November 7, 2009, as part of the 35th anniversary of the Drake/Sagan transmission to M13, the <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a> gene sequence was transmitted to three nearby stars: GJ 83.1, Teagarden's star SO 025300.5+165258 and Kappa Ceti (G5B). The project was by artist <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis_(artist)" title="Joe Davis (artist)">Joe Davis</a> with support from Paul Gilster, the Arecibo Observatory, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> and others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SETI_and_METI_projects">SETI and METI projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: SETI and METI projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SETI" title="SETI">SETI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_SETI" title="Active SETI">Active SETI</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_for_Extra-Terrestrial_Intelligence" title="Search for Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence">Search for Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence</a> (SETI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> is a program to search for extraterrestrial life or advanced technologies. SETI aims to answer the question "Are we alone in the Universe?" by scanning the skies for transmissions from intelligent civilizations elsewhere in our galaxy. In comparison, METI (Messaging to Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence) refers to the <a href="/wiki/Active_SETI" title="Active SETI">active search</a> by transmitting messages.</p>
<p>Arecibo is the source of data for the <a href="/wiki/SETI@home" title="SETI@home">SETI@home</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astropulse" title="Astropulse">Astropulse</a> <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a> projects put forward by the Space Sciences Laboratory at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> and was used for the <a href="/wiki/SETI_Institute" title="SETI Institute">SETI Institute</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Project_Phoenix_(SETI)" title="Project Phoenix (SETI)">Project Phoenix</a> observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Einstein@Home" title="Einstein@Home">Einstein@Home</a> distributed computing project has found more than 20 <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a> in Arecibo data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Terrestrial aeronomy experiments at Arecibo have included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Coqui" title="NASA Coqui">Coqui 2</a> experiment, supported by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>. The telescope also has <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">military intelligence</a> uses, some of which include locating <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> installations by detecting their signals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EME_(communications)" title="EME (communications)">bouncing</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Limited amateur radio operations have occurred, using <i>moon bounce</i> or <a href="/wiki/Earth%E2%80%93Moon%E2%80%93Earth_communication" title="Earth–Moon–Earth communication">Earth–Moon–Earth communication</a>, in which radio signals aimed at the Moon are reflected back to Earth. The first of these operations was on June 13–14, 1964, using the call KP4BPZ. A dozen or so two-way contacts were made on 144 and 432 MHz. On July 3 and July 24, 1965, KP4BPZ was again activated on 432 MHz, making approximately 30 contacts on 432 MHz during the limited time slots available. For these tests, a very wide-band instrumentation recorder captured a large segment of the receiving bandwidth, enabling later verification of other amateur station callsigns. These were not two-way contacts. From April 16–18, 2010, again, the Arecibo Amateur Radio Club KP4AO conducted moon-bounce activity using the antenna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> On November 10, 2013, the KP4AO Arecibo Amateur Radio Club conducted a Fifty-Year Commemoration Activation, lasting 7 hours on 14.250 MHz SSB, without using the main dish antenna.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_concerns">Funding concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Funding concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the early 1970s, the Arecibo Observatory has been supported by the NSF (National Science Foundation divisions of Astronomical Sciences and of Atmospheric Sciences) with incremental support by NASA, for operating the planetary radar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SP4218_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-SP4218-36">[36]</a></sup> Between 2001 and 2006, NASA decreased, then eliminated, its support of the planetary radar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> but restored and increased the funding in FY-2010.</p>
<p>A report by the NSF division of Astronomical Sciences, made public on November 3, 2006, recommended substantially decreased astronomy funding for the Arecibo Observatory, from $10.5 million in 2007 to $4.0 million in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> If other sources of money could not be obtained, the observatory would be forced to close. The report also advised that 80 percent of observing time be allocated to the surveys already in progress, reducing available time for smaller programs.</p>
<p>Academics and researchers responded by organizing to protect and advocate for the observatory. They established the Arecibo Science Advocacy Partnership (ASAP), to advance the scientific excellence of Arecibo Observatory research and to publicize its accomplishments in astronomy, aeronomy and planetary radar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-areciboscience.org_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-areciboscience.org-40">[40]</a></sup> ASAP's goals included mobilizing the existing broad base of support for Arecibo science within the fields it serves directly, the broad scientific community; provide a forum for the Arecibo research community and enhance communication within it; promote the potential of Arecibo for groundbreaking science; suggest paths that will maximize it into the foreseeable future, and showcase the broad impact and far-reaching implications of the science currently carried out with this unique instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-areciboscience.org_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-areciboscience.org-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contributions by the government of Puerto Rico may be one way to help fill the funding gap, but remain controversial and uncertain. At <a href="/wiki/Town_hall_meeting" title="Town hall meeting">town hall meetings</a> about the potential closure, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican</a> Senate President <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_McClintock" title="Kenneth McClintock">Kenneth McClintock</a> announced an initial local appropriation of $3.0 million during fiscal year 2008 to fund a major maintenance project to restore the three pillars that support the antenna platform to their original condition, pending inclusion in the next bond issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The bond authorization, with a $3.0 million appropriation, was approved by the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Senate of Puerto Rico">Senate of Puerto Rico</a> on November 14, 2007, on the first day of a special session called by <a href="/wiki/An%C3%ADbal_Acevedo_Vil%C3%A1" title="Aníbal Acevedo Vilá">Aníbal Acevedo Vilá</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_House_of_Representatives" title="Puerto Rico House of Representatives">Puerto Rico House of Representatives</a> repeated this action on June 30, 2008. Puerto Rico's governor signed the measure into law in August 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> These funds were made available during the second half of 2009.</p>
<p>In a letter published on September 19, 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Enrique_Serrano" title="José Enrique Serrano">José Enrique Serrano</a>, a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._House_of_Representatives" title="U.S. House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a> Appropriations Committee, asked the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> to keep Arecibo operating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Language similar to that of the letter of September 19 was included in the FY-2008 omnibus spending bill. In October 2007, Puerto Rico's then-<a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner" title="Resident Commissioner">Resident Commissioner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Fortu%C3%B1o" title="Luis Fortuño">Luis Fortuño</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Dana_Rohrabacher" title="Dana Rohrabacher">Dana Rohrabacher</a>, filed legislation to assure the continued operation of the famed observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> A similar bill was filed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> in April 2008 by the Junior Senator from New York, <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the Arecibo observatory is owned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_States" title="Government of the United States">Government of the United States</a>, donations by private or corporate donors cannot be made. However, as non-profit (<a href="/wiki/501(c)_organization" title="501(c) organization">501(c)(3)</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_charity" title="Public charity">"public charities"</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_States" title="Government of the United States">US</a> law, the operators, <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_University_(Puerto_Rico)" title="Metropolitan University (Puerto Rico)">Universidad Metropolitana</a>, can accept contributions on behalf of the Arecibo Observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2007, in an open letter to researchers, the NSF clarified the status of the budget for NAIC, stating the present plan could hit the targeted budgetary revision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> No mention of private funding was made. However, in the event that its budget target is not reached, it must be noted that the NSF is undertaking studies to mothball or demolish the observatory to return it to its natural setting.</p>
<p>In November 2007, <a href="/wiki/The_Planetary_Society" title="The Planetary Society">The Planetary Society</a> urged the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> to prevent the Arecibo Observatory from closing because of insufficient funding, since its radar contributes greatly to the accuracy of predictions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_impact" title="Asteroid impact">asteroid impacts</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Earth" title="The Earth">the Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The Planetary Society believes that continued operation of the observatory will reduce the cost of mitigation (that is, deflection of a <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">near-Earth asteroid</a> on collision to Earth), should that be necessary.</p>
<p>Also in November of that year <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> described the consequences of the budget cuts at the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In July 2008, the British newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> reported that the funding crisis, due to federal budget cuts, was still very much alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/SETI@home" title="SETI@home">SETI@home</a> program is using the telescope as a primary source for ET research. The program urges people to send a letter to their political representatives in support of full federal funding of the observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The NAIC received $3.1 million from the <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a>. This was used for basic maintenance and for a second, much smaller, antenna to be used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_long_baseline_interferometry" title="Very long baseline interferometry">very long baseline interferometry</a>, new <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">Klystron</a> amplifiers for the <a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">planetary radar</a> system and student training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> This allotment was an increase of about 30 percent over the FY-2009 budget. However, the FY-2010 funding request by NSF was cut by $1.2 million (−12.5%) over the FY-2009 budget, in light of their continued plans to reduce funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2011 NSF budget was reduced by a further $1.6 million, −15% compared to 2010, with a further $1.0 million reduction projected by FY-2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011budget_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-2011budget-55">[55]</a></sup> Starting in FY-2010, NASA restored its historical support by contributing $2.0 million per year for <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>, particularly the study of <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">near-Earth objects</a>, at Arecibo. NASA implemented this funding through its Near Earth Object Observations program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> NASA increased its support to $3.5 million per year in 2012.</p>
<p>Furthermore, in 2010 the NSF issued a call for new proposals for the management of NAIC starting in FY-2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010RFP_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-2010RFP-5">[5]</a></sup> On May 12, 2011, the agency informed <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> that, as of October 1, 2011, it would no longer be the operator of the NAIC and the Arecibo Observatory. At that time, Cornell transferred its operations to <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>, along with two other managing partners, <a href="/wiki/Universities_Space_Research_Association" title="Universities Space Research Association">Universities Space Research Association</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_University_(Puerto_Rico)" title="Metropolitan University (Puerto Rico)">Universidad Metropolitana de Puerto Rico</a>, with a number of other collaborators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sri_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sri-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Upon the award of the new cooperative agreement for NAIC management and operation, NSF also decertified NAIC as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federally_Funded_Research_and_Development_Center" title="Federally Funded Research and Development Center">Federally Funded Research and Development Center (FFRDC)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011budget_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-2011budget-55">[55]</a></sup> with the stated goal of providing the NAIC with greater freedom to establish broader scientific partnerships and pursue funding opportunities for activities beyond the scope of those supported by NSF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2015, the NSF released a "Dear Colleague Letter" reiterating its desire for a "substantially reduced funding commitment from NSF".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCL2015_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCL2015-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 2016 September 30, the NSF released a followup to the October 2015 "Dear Colleague Letter" announcing a solicitation for future operation of the Observatory stating "The subject Solicitation will request the submission of formal proposals involving the continued operation of Arecibo Observatory under conditions of a substantially reduced funding commitment from NSF."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCL2016_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCL2016-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".C3.81ngel_Ramos_Foundation_Visitor_Center">Ángel Ramos Foundation Visitor Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ángel Ramos Foundation Visitor Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Opened in 1997, the Ángel Ramos Foundation Visitor Center features interactive exhibits and displays about the operations of the radio telescope, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">atmospheric sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The center is named after the financial foundation that honors <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_Ramos_(industrialist)" title="Ángel Ramos (industrialist)">Ángel Ramos</a>, owner of the <i><a href="/wiki/El_Mundo_(Puerto_Rico)" title="El Mundo (Puerto Rico)">El Mundo</a></i> newspaper and founder of <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a>. The Foundation provided half of the funds to build the Visitor Center, with the remainder received from private donations and Cornell University.</p>
<p>The center, in collaboration with the Caribbean Astronomical Society,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> host a series of Astronomical Nights throughout the year, which feature diverse discussions regarding <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanets</a>, and astronomical phenomena and discoveries (such as <a href="/wiki/Comet_ISON" title="Comet ISON">Comet ISON</a>). The main purpose of the center is to increase public interest in astronomy, the observatory's research successes, and space endeavors.</p>
<p>The center is open Wednesday - Sunday, on many holidays and during school breaks. The admission fee is $12.00 for adults, and $8.00 for seniors and children under 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<li>A radio telescope based on the observatory is featured in the videogame <i><a href="/wiki/Just_Cause_2" title="Just Cause 2">Just Cause 2</a></i>.</li>
<li>Although never specifically named, the first lines of the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/2010:_Odyssey_Two" title="2010: Odyssey Two">2010: Odyssey Two</a></i> strongly imply that the opening scene takes place at the observatory. However, for the 1984 <a href="/wiki/2010_(film)" title="2010 (film)">film adaptation</a>, this scene was filmed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Large_Array" title="Very Large Array">Very Large Array</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Battlefield_4" title="Battlefield 4">Battlefield 4</a></i> multiplayer map, Rogue Transmission, is inspired by the observatory.</li>
</ul>
