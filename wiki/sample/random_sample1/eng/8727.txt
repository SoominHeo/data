<p>The <b>Areopagus</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a prominent rock outcropping located northwest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acropolis,_Athens" title="Acropolis, Athens">Acropolis</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athens,_Greece" title="Athens, Greece">Athens, Greece</a>. Its English name is the composite form of the Greek name <b>Areios Pagos</b>, translated "<a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a> Rock" (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἄρειος Πάγος</span>). In classical times, it functioned as the court for trying deliberate homicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ath_Pol_57.3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ath_Pol_57.3-1">[1]</a></sup> Ares was supposed to have been tried here by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_gods" title="Greek gods">gods</a> for the murder of <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a>'s son <a href="/wiki/Halirrhothius" title="Halirrhothius">Halirrhothius</a> (a typical example of an <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">aetiological</a> myth).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Areopagus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2286" data-file-width="7000" height="72" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg/220px-20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg/330px-20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg/440px-20101024_Acropolis_panoramic_view_from_Areopagus_hill_Athens_Greece.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The origin of its name is not clear. In Ancient Greek, πάγος <i>pagos</i> means "big piece of rock". Areios could have come from <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a> or from the <a href="/wiki/Erinyes" title="Erinyes">Erinyes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as on its foot was erected a temple dedicated to the Erinyes where murderers used to find shelter so as not to face the consequences of their actions. Later, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> referred to the rocky hill as "Mars Hill", after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_(god)" title="Mars (god)">Mars</a>, the Roman God of War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Near the Areopagus was also constructed the basilica of <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_the_Areopagite" title="Dionysius the Areopagite">Dionysius Areopagites</a>.</p>
<p>In pre-classical times (before the 5th century BC), the Areopagus was the council of elders of the city, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman Senate</a>. Like the Senate, its membership was restricted to those who had held high public office, in this case that of <a href="/wiki/Archon" title="Archon">Archon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 594 BC, the Areopagus agreed to hand over its functions to <a href="/wiki/Solon" title="Solon">Solon</a> for reform. He instituted democratic reforms, reconstituted its membership and returned control to the organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 462 BC, <a href="/wiki/Ephialtes" title="Ephialtes">Ephialtes</a> put through reforms which deprived the Areopagus of almost all its functions except that of a murder tribunal in favour of <a href="/wiki/Heliaia" title="Heliaia">Heliaia</a>.</p>
<p>In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Eumenides" title="The Eumenides">The Eumenides</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> (458 BC), the Areopagus is the site of the trial of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orestes_(mythology)" title="Orestes (mythology)">Orestes</a> for killing his mother (<a href="/wiki/Clytemnestra" title="Clytemnestra">Clytemnestra</a>) and her lover (<a href="/wiki/Aegisthus" title="Aegisthus">Aegisthus</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phryne" title="Phryne">Phryne</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hetaera" title="Hetaera">hetaera</a> from 4th century BC Greece and famed for her beauty, appeared before the Areopagus accused of profaning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleusinian_mysteries" title="Eleusinian mysteries">Eleusinian mysteries</a>. One story has her letting her cloak drop, so impressing the judges with her almost divine form that she was summarily acquitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an unusual development, the Areopagus acquired a new function in the 4th century BC, investigating corruption, although conviction powers remained with the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesia_(ancient_Athens)" title="Ecclesia (ancient Athens)">Ecclesia</a>.</p>
<p>The Areopagus, like most city-state institutions, continued to function in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times, and it was from this location, drawing from the potential significance of the Athenian altar to the <a href="/wiki/Unknown_God" title="Unknown God">Unknown God</a>, that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_Paul" title="Apostle Paul">Apostle Paul</a> is said to have delivered the famous speech, "Now what you worship as something unknown I am going to proclaim to you. The God who made the world and everything in it is the Lord of heaven and earth and does not live in temples built by hands." (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#17:24" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">Acts 17:24</a>)</p>
<p>The term "Areopagus" also refers to the judicial body of aristocratic origin that subsequently formed the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Cassation_(Greece)" title="Court of Cassation (Greece)">higher court</a> of modern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_references">Modern references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Areopagus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The English poet <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a> titled his defence of freedom of the press "<a href="/wiki/Areopagitica" title="Areopagitica">Areopagitica</a>," arguing that the censors of ancient Athens, based at the Areopagus, had not practiced the kind of prior restraint of publication being called for in the English Parliament of Milton's time.</li>
</ul>

<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://wsu.edu/~dee/GREECE/ATHENS.HTM" rel="nofollow">Ancient Greece:Athens</a> <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20101203213131/http://wsu.edu/~dee/GREECE/ATHENS.HTM" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> December 3, 2010, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>.</span></li>
