<p><b>Arun Manilal Gandhi</b> (born 1934) is an Indian-American socio-political activist, and the fifth grandson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohandas_Gandhi" title="Mohandas Gandhi">Mohandas Gandhi</a> through his second son <a href="/wiki/Manilal_Gandhi" title="Manilal Gandhi">Manilal</a>. Although he has followed in the footsteps of his grandfather as an activist, he eschews the <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">ascetic</a> lifestyle of his grandfather.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arun_Manilal_Gandhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arun Gandhi grew up on his grandfather's <a href="/wiki/Ashram" title="Ashram">ashram</a>, a small, remote settlement in South Africa. Growing up, Gandhi had the advantage of education over the illiterate farm families who worked the surrounding fields, for which his grandfather urged him to play after school with the neighbor children in order to "learn what it was like to live in poverty", as well as to teach those children what he learned in class each day, which Gandhi saw as "the most creative and enlightening experience for me." Eventually, crowds of children and their parents started showing up for his lessons, which is when Gandhi learned about compassion and the need to share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbian_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbian-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arun_Manilal_Gandhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arun Manilal Gandhi considers himself to be a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> but expresses <a href="/wiki/Universalism" title="Universalism">universalist</a> views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Gandhi has worked closely with Christian priests and his philosophies are strongly influenced by Buddhist, Hindu, Muslim and Christian concepts. Like his grandfather, he also believes in the concept of '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-violence" title="Non-violence">non-violence</a>' (<a href="/wiki/Ahimsa" title="Ahimsa">Ahimsa</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1982, when <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> released the feature film, <i><a href="/wiki/Gandhi_(film)" title="Gandhi (film)">Gandhi</a></i>, based on his grandfather's life, Gandhi wrote an article criticizing the Indian government for subsidizing the film with $25 million, arguing that there were more important things to spend such money on. Though his article was widely reprinted and celebrated, after attending a special screening of the film, Gandhi concluded that it accurately conveyed his grandfather's philosophy and legacy (despite its historical inaccuracies), and was so moved by it that he wrote another article retracting the first one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbian_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Columbian-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987, Arun Gandhi moved to the United States along with his wife, Sunanda, to work on a study at the University of <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. This study examined and contrasted the sorts of prejudices that existed in India, the U.S., and South Africa. Afterward they moved to <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a> and founded the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=M._K._Gandhi_Institute_for_Nonviolence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="M. K. Gandhi Institute for Nonviolence (page does not exist)">M. K. Gandhi Institute for Nonviolence</a> hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Brothers_University" title="Christian Brothers University">Christian Brothers University</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> academic institution. This institute was dedicated to applying the principles of <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a> at both local and global scales. For his work at the Institute, Gandhi was presented with the Peace Abbey Courage of Conscience Award at the John F. Kennedy Library in Boston. In 1996, he cofounded the <a href="/wiki/Season_for_Nonviolence" title="Season for Nonviolence">Season for Nonviolence</a> as a yearly celebration of the philosophies and lives of Mohandas Gandhi and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rev._Dr._Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." title="Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr.">Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003 Gandhi was one of the signatories to <i><a href="/wiki/Humanism_and_Its_Aspirations" title="Humanism and Its Aspirations">Humanism and Its Aspirations</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Humanist_Manifesto" title="Humanist Manifesto">Humanist Manifesto</a> III).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2007, Gandhi co-taught a course entitled "Gandhi on Personal Leadership and Nonviolence" at <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_University" title="Salisbury University">Salisbury University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_Maryland" title="Salisbury, Maryland">Salisbury, Maryland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On November 12, 2007, Gandhi gave a lecture for the Salisbury University Center for Conflict Resolution’s “One Person Can Make a Difference” Lecture Series, entitled “Nonviolence in the Age of Terrorism”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In late 2008, Gandhi returned to Salisbury University to co-teach a course entitled "The Global Impact of Gandhi".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, after the passing of his wife, the institute moved to <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a>, and is currently located on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester" title="University of Rochester">University of Rochester</a> River Campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After a January 2008 op-ed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a>'s "On Faith" section where Gandhi said that Israelis talked too much about the Holocaust and were losing world sympathy and that Israel and the U.S. were the biggest contributors to the world-threatening "culture of violence", his ties to Rochester were imperiled. Gandhi apologized by saying he had only meant to say right-wing <a href="/wiki/Likud" title="Likud">Likud</a> supporters were part of the problem, but the University did not accept his explanation and informed him that the institute would be closed unless he resigned from it. Gandhi quit soon after and has not returned to the institute in any capacity since then.</p>
<p>Gandhi has given many speeches about nonviolence in many countries. During his tour to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, he urged the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palestinian_people" title="Palestinian people">Palestinians</a> to resist Israeli occupation peacefully to assure their freedom. In August 2004, Gandhi proposed to the Palestinian Parliament a peaceful march of 50,000 refugees across the Jordan River to return to their homeland, and said MPs should lead the way. Gandhi also claimed that the fate of Palestinians is ten times worse than that of blacks in South African Apartheid. He asked: "What would happen? Maybe the Israeli army would shoot and kill several. They may kill 100. They may kill 200 men, women and children. And that would shock the world. The world will get up and say, 'What is going on?'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 12, 2009 Gandhi visited the <a href="/wiki/Brunton_Theatre" title="Brunton Theatre">Brunton Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Musselburgh" title="Musselburgh">Musselburgh</a> to talk to P7's from all over Eastlothian in Scotland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On November 11, 2009 Gandhi visited Chattanooga State Technical Community College in Chattanooga, Tennessee to speak and spread his message of peace.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On November 13, 2009 Gandhi visited Cleveland State Community College in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Tennessee" title="Cleveland, Tennessee">Cleveland, Tennessee</a> to speak and spread his message of peace. On November 16, 2010 Gandhi visited The University of Wyoming in <a href="/wiki/Laramie,_Wyoming" title="Laramie, Wyoming">Laramie, Wyoming</a> to speak and spread his message of peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 2, 2011, Arun Gandhi spoke at the East West Center on the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii_at_Manoa" title="University of Hawaii at Manoa">University of Hawaii at Manoa</a>, Honolulu, Hawaii. He spoke about Nonviolence: A Means for Social Change. On the same day he also spoke at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iolani_School" title="Iolani School">Iolani School</a> in Honolulu, on the subject of The Wisdom of Choosing Peace. On March 3, 2011, Gandhi spoke at the University of Hawaii Architecture Building, in an event sponsored by the Spark Matsunaga Institute for Peace and Conflict Resolution in Honolulu, Hawaii. On March 4, 2011 spoke at the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Buddhist_Academy" title="Pacific Buddhist Academy">Pacific Buddhist Academy</a> in Honolulu, Hawaii. He also spoke at the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_State_Capitol" title="Hawaii State Capitol">Hawaii State Capitol</a> (public auditorium) on the subject of "The Power of Peace to Create a Culture of Human Rights in Hawaii and the World." This was part of the Human Rights Week, sponsored by the State of Hawaii. He also spoke at the Pioneer Plaza Club in downtown Honolulu on the subject of "Gandhian Peace (Nonviolence) A Pathway for Resolving Modern Day Conflict." On March 5, 2011 Gandhi visited <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Krishna_Consciousness" title="International Society for Krishna Consciousness">The International Society for Krishna Consciousness Temple</a> in Honolulu, Hawaii to speak and spread his message of peace. He also spoke at To Ho No Hikari Church in Honolulu, in an event sponsored by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._Terry_Shintani" title="Dr. Terry Shintani">Dr. Terry Shintani</a>, on the subject of "The Way of Nonviolence Towards All Living Beings", and at the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Convention_Center" title="Hawaii Convention Center">Hawaii Convention Center</a> as part of the PAAAC Youth Conference. On March 6, 2011 Gandhi spoke at Unity Church, Diamond Head, Honolulu on the subject of "Lessons I Learned With My Grandfather".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Gandhi's 2011 tour of Honolulu was sponsored by Barbara Altemus of the We Are One Foundation and by the Gandhian International Institute for Peace. Gandhi is featured in "THE CALLING: Heal Ourselves Heal our Planet" a Documentary Film in Production created by Barbara Altemus, directed by Oscar-nominated <a href="/wiki/William_Gazecki" title="William Gazecki">William Gazecki</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On March 23, 2012, Gandhi was the keynote speaker at the first annual Engaging Peace Conference at <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_University" title="Arcadia University">Arcadia University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Glenside,_Pennsylvania" title="Glenside, Pennsylvania">Glenside, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In March 2014, Atheneum Books for Young Readers published <i>Grandfather Gandhi</i>, a children's book that Arun Gandhi co-authored with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bethany_Hegedus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bethany Hegedus (page does not exist)">Bethany Hegedus</a>, and illustrated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Evan_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Evan Turk (page does not exist)">Evan Turk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Picture_book" title="Picture book">picture book</a> memoir, which carries a pro-peace message, tells the story of how Arun's grandfather, likening anger to lightning that could either destroy or illuminate, taught Arun to respond to injustice using peaceful methods, in order to "turn darkness into light". The book also focuses on how Arun, jealous of the other people who commanded his grandfather's attention, frustrated with his schoolwork, and embarrassed at his inability to control his anger, struggled strove to make his grandfather proud. The book was met with positive reviews for its use of a child's point of view in order to make a complex historical issue understandable to child readers, and for Turk's use of cut-paper abstract images to create illustrations with emotional resonance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
