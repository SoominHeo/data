<p><b>Ardennes</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[aʁ.dɛn]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Est" title="Grand Est">Grand Est</a> region of northeastern France named after the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a> area. Its prefecture is the town <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a>. The inhabitants of the department are known as <i>Ardennais</i> or <i>Ardennaises</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inhabitants_of_Ardennes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Inhabitants_of_Ardennes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The department is surrounded by the French departments of <a href="/wiki/Aisne" title="Aisne">Aisne</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Marne" title="Marne">Marne</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Meuse_(department)" title="Meuse (department)">Meuse</a> to the east and by the <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Namur_(province)" title="Namur (province)">province of Namur</a> to the north.</p>
<p>It is traversed in its northern part by the winding valley of the <a href="/wiki/Meuse" title="Meuse">Meuse</a> and it is in this part of the department that the majority of people and activities are focused. <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedan,_Ardennes" title="Sedan, Ardennes">Sedan</a> are the main urban centres.</p>
<p>The department is part of the Academy of Reims and under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_(France)" title="Court of Appeal (France)">Court of Appeal</a> of Reims. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INSEE" title="INSEE">INSEE</a> and Post Code is 08.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_geographic_data">General geographic data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General geographic data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With an area of 5,229 square kilometres (2,019 square miles), the Ardennes was the smallest of the four departments that made up the region <a href="/wiki/Champagne-Ardenne" title="Champagne-Ardenne">Champagne-Ardenne</a>. It nevertheless presents a degree of geographical diversity.</p>
<p>Ardennes owes its name to a vast natural area, the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a>, a plateau deeply cut by the Meuse and its many tributaries which extend above the <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Walloon</a> part of southern Belgium as well as <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, Germany (<a href="/wiki/Eifel" title="Eifel">Eifel</a>), and the north of the neighbouring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Meuse" title="Department of Meuse">department of Meuse</a>.</p>
<p>The highest point of the department is 504m and is situated on the southern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Croix_Scaille" title="Croix Scaille">Croix Scaille</a> (located between the French commune of <a href="/wiki/Les_Hautes-Rivi%C3%A8res" title="Les Hautes-Rivières">Les Hautes-Rivières</a> and the Belgian commune of <a href="/wiki/Gedinne" title="Gedinne">Gedinne</a>).</p>
<p>It is in this part of the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Ardennes</a> mountains that the Meuse winds through a picturesque valley, known locally as "the valley". Flowing into the northern part of the Ardennes department it waters both upstream and downstream the main cities of <a href="/wiki/Sedan,_Ardennes" title="Sedan, Ardennes">Sedan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nouzonville" title="Nouzonville">Nouzonville</a>. It has numerous tributaries – the main ones in the department being the <a href="/wiki/Semois" title="Semois">Semois</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chiers" title="Chiers">Chiers</a>.</p>
<p>In the south of the department where the <a href="/wiki/Aisne_(river)" title="Aisne (river)">Aisne</a> flows lies the vast treeless plain of Champagne chalk (formerly called flea-ridden Champagne) extended to the south-west by the small grain-growing region of Porcien, while <a href="/wiki/Thi%C3%A9rache" title="Thiérache">Thiérache</a> in the west and <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Argonne" title="Forest of Argonne">Argonne</a> in the east are fringe grasslands with very highly individualized soils.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ardennes department does not have a uniform climate throughout its territory especially not during the winter period.</p>
<p>In the north of the department near the department of Aisne and the border with Belgium to the centre of the department near the Canton of Omont and in the south of the valley of the Meuse, the climate is considered "degraded continental" (heavy rainfall in autumn and frequent frosts in winter), while the rest of the department has a "degraded oceanic" or "temperate continental" climate (relative to an oceanic climate, the winters are colder and the summers are hotter, rainfall in the lowlands is lower and winds are of lower strength). All this stems from the location of the department, which is midway between the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and the interior of Europe.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rocroi_neige.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a> in the snow: a common situation in winter in the northern Ardennes department.</div>
</div>

<p>This difference can be easily observed in practice. Winter is more rigorous and there is a higher risk of snow at <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sedan,_Ardennes" title="Sedan, Ardennes">Sedan</a> – all cities in the north of the department where the common characteristics of the degraded continental climate prevail.</p>
<p>This nuance of climate is also evident by the temperature difference with the adjoining regions. The <a href="/wiki/Nord-Pas-de-Calais" title="Nord-Pas-de-Calais">Nord-Pas-de-Calais</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parisian_Basin" title="Parisian Basin">Parisian Basin</a> benefit from the maritime influences of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pas_de_Calais" title="Pas de Calais">Pas de Calais</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> as well as the geophysical conditions in the presence of relatively flat terrain.</p>
<p>This climatic difference is particularly pronounced in the presence of frost especially in the valleys of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meuse_(river)" title="Meuse (river)">Meuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semois" title="Semois">Semoy</a>, the plateau of <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a>, and around the Croix-Scaille where it can be very marked and has the disadvantage of persisting longer in the year with a significant influence on the vegetation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_evolution_and_distribution_of_the_population">The evolution and distribution of the population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The evolution and distribution of the population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite a high birth rate (the highest rate in France in 1968), the department continues to lose population: 300,000 in 2000 due to high unemployment (hence "migration" continues). The two world wars have each time resulted in a loss of population (such as the "exodus" of 1940). There were 330,000 people at the end of the 19th century.</p>
<p>That the major urban areas of the department are the most affected is characterized by a stagnation of the population – a population decline of up to 2% compared to 1999 in the city centres and suburbs (<a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revin" title="Revin">Revin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fumay" title="Fumay">Fumay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rethel" title="Rethel">Rethel</a>).</p>
<p>The communes, however, are gaining inhabitants (the phenomenon of urban sprawl). This is explained by the search for better living in the countryside which matches the desire of many people to build a small land-holding, typically a house with land to the detriment of their proximity to their workplace. This highly contemporary concept favours commuting between Home and Work. This is the phenomenon of suburbanization which has become common in the whole of France from which Ardennes does not escape.</p>
<p>On 1 January 2006, the Ardennes population stood at 295,653 inhabitants. The population is declining in urban areas but five times less than in rural areas. The limited decline in the urban space where two thirds of the Ardennes people live is the result of two opposite dynamics. Semi-urban communes have gained 0.5% of inhabitants per year over the period 1999–2006 at the expense of urban centres (downtown and suburbs) which lost 0.6% per year. For thirty years the population has lagged in the main cities of Ardennes. Between 1999 and 2006, the annual decline was 0.2% for <a href="/wiki/Sedan,_Ardennes" title="Sedan, Ardennes">Sedan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rethel" title="Rethel">Rethel</a>, 1.8% for <a href="/wiki/Revin" title="Revin">Revin</a>, and 1% for <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a>. The most unfavourable rural population change came from degradation of rural employment centres, such as <a href="/wiki/Fumay" title="Fumay">Fumay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vouziers" title="Vouziers">Vouziers</a> and to a lesser extent that of their periphery. This was slightly mitigated by a small increase in population in other rural communes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name of the department is related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toponym" title="Toponym">toponym</a> <i>Ardenne</i> which could derive from the Gallic <i>ardu</i> meaning "high". It would have been transformed into <i>Arduenna</i> by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> to designate the ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ardennes_forest" title="Ardennes forest">Ardennes forest</a> and the mountains mentioned by Julius Caesar in the work attributed to him: <i><a href="/wiki/Commentarii_de_Bello_Gallico" title="Commentarii de Bello Gallico">Commentaries on the Gallic War</a></i>. <i>Arduenna Sylva</i> was used for the pine forest on the plateau of <a href="/wiki/Bastogne" title="Bastogne">Bastogne</a>. It was then transformed into <i>Ardenna</i> in the 6th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>This toponym is absent from the names of communes in the department of Ardennes while those of Argonne (<a href="/wiki/Beaumont-en-Argonne" title="Beaumont-en-Argonne">Beaumont-en-Argonne</a>), Porcien (<a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau-Porcien" title="Château-Porcien">Château-Porcien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novion-Porcien" title="Novion-Porcien">Novion-Porcien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaumont-Porcien" title="Chaumont-Porcien">Chaumont-Porcien</a>, and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champagne_(historical_province)" title="Champagne (historical province)">Champagne</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vaux-Champagne" title="Vaux-Champagne">Vaux-Champagne</a>) are sometimes fused and all have a connotation of regional belonging. This is why the name of <i>Ardenne</i> is not specific to the department as it is found in many other parts of France – for example in western and central-western France,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[N 1]</a></sup> Belgium, and Luxembourg where it has its usual sense – from Celtic origin – of "high", "high woods" or "forest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_Ardennes_department">History of the Ardennes department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History of the Ardennes department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The department is one of the original 83 departments created during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> on 4 March 1790 under the Act of 22 December 1789. It includes part of the former provinces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champagne_(historical_province)" title="Champagne (historical province)">Champagne</a> and Argonne, several principalities including those of Arches and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_of_Sedan" title="Prince of Sedan">Sedan</a>, countships (such as <a href="/wiki/Rethel" title="Rethel">Rethel</a>), and different areas returned to France (from the former <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a>) in the 18th century.</p>
<p>On 12 May 1793 the department expanded itself with the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Prince-Bishopric of Liège">Bailiwick of Liège</a>, <a href="/wiki/Couvin" title="Couvin">Couvin</a>, and the countships from the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> of Fagnolle and on 26 October 1795 a part of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bouillon" title="Duchy of Bouillon">Duchy of Bouillon</a>.</p>
<p>After the victory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventh_coalition" title="Seventh coalition">allies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a> on 18 June 1815, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1815)" title="Treaty of Paris (1815)">second Treaty of Paris</a> subtracted territory from Ardennes to attach to the Netherlands: <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bouillon" title="Duchy of Bouillon">Duchy of Bouillon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Couvin" title="Couvin">Couvin</a>, Mariembourg, Fagnolle, and <a href="/wiki/Philippeville" title="Philippeville">Philippeville</a>. In addition, the department was occupied by Prussian troops from June 1815 to November 1818.</p>
<p>On 2 September 1870 Sedan was the place of surrender for <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sedan" title="Battle of Sedan">Battle of Sedan</a> against the troops of the Prussian states, a coalition commanded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helmut_von_Moltke" title="Helmut von Moltke">Helmut von Moltke</a>. The King of Prussia <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_I" title="Wilhelm I">Wilhelm I</a>, the future emperor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Reich" title="Second Reich">Second Reich</a> under the same name and <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Bismarck</a> viewed the battle from the hills overlooking Sedan. The defeat marked the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_Empire" title="Second French Empire">Second Empire</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> and at the same time the birth of the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">French Third Republic</a> on 4 September 1870.</p>

<p>During the two world wars for strategic reasons the region each time became the passage for the invading armies because of the narrow width of the Meuse and its deep valley. The French military believed that the region was defended by its terrain and thick forests present in the northern of the department and so neglected the defence of the territory. During the First World War the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Ardennes" title="Battle of the Ardennes">Battle of the Ardennes</a> was fought in the department and <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a> became the headquarters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William,_Crown_Prince_of_Germany_and_Prussia" title="William, Crown Prince of Germany and Prussia">German Crown Prince</a>. It was at Vouziers and other places that the <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Legion" title="Czechoslovak Legion">Czechoslovak legions</a> fought with the man who would become the first president of the Czechoslovak republic, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Masaryk" title="Jan Masaryk">Jan Masaryk</a>, and it was also near the same city that the aeroplane of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roland_Garros_(aviator)" title="Roland Garros (aviator)">Roland Garros</a> was shot down.</p>
<p>It was the only French department have been fully occupied during that conflict except for northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" title="Lorraine (region)">Lorraine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Moselle" title="Moselle">Moselle</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> which had been under German administration since 1871.</p>
<p>During the Second World War the main effort of the German army was again focused on this area, especially on the right bank of the River Meuse, symbolized by the breakthrough at Sedan which would lead the French troops into the strategic trap the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_Plan" title="Yellow Plan">Yellow Plan</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Erich_von_Manstein" title="Erich von Manstein">General von Manstein</a> and approved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitler" title="Hitler">Hitler</a>.</p>
<p>It is in this department that the <a href="/wiki/Maginot_Line" title="Maginot Line">Maginot Line</a> ended: the last fort of the line (<a href="/wiki/Ouvrage_La_Fert%C3%A9" title="Ouvrage La Ferté">Fort Villy la Ferte</a>) was located about five kilometres from <a href="/wiki/Carignan,_Ardennes" title="Carignan, Ardennes">Carignan</a>. The French General staff did not want to continue the line of defence along the border with Belgium, a neutral and friendly country. Furthermore, they hoped that the unique geography and the forest would stop the German army.</p>
<p>After the armistice of 1940 Ardennes was declared a "forbidden zone" (actually a German settlement area) throughout the occupation by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> army.</p>
<p>In 1944 the "<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bulge" title="Battle of the Bulge">Battle of the Bulge</a>" was fought partly in this department.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_organization">Administrative organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Administrative organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The seat of the prefecture of the department is at <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a> and the three sub-prefectures are <a href="/wiki/Rethel" title="Rethel">Rethel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sedan,_Ardennes" title="Sedan, Ardennes">Sedan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vouziers" title="Vouziers">Vouziers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a> was also a sub-prefecture until 1926. In addition, the departmental seat of the <i>General Council of Ardennes</i> is located at Charleville-Mézières.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_organization">Territorial organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Territorial organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="490" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png/220px-Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png/330px-Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png/440px-Carte_des_communes_des_Ardennes.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Ardennes is composed of 457 communes which are grouped into 19 cantons and four arrondissements of varying sizes.</p>
<p>The largest arrondissement of the department is that of <a href="/wiki/Arrondissement_of_Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Arrondissement of Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a> while the smallest is <a href="/wiki/Arrondissement_of_Sedan" title="Arrondissement of Sedan">Sedan</a> which is twice as wide. The two arrondissements that occupy the northern part of the Ardennes, however, have four fifths of the departmental population.</p>
<p>The other two arrondissements, <a href="/wiki/Arrondissement_of_Rethel" title="Arrondissement of Rethel">Rethel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arrondissement_of_Vouziers" title="Arrondissement of Vouziers">Vouziers</a>, occupy the southern part of the department with roughly comparable areas but are very sparsely populated.</p>

<p>Before the Poincaré decree of 10 September 1926 which removed many sub-prefectures in France, the department had five arrondissements. In addition to the four mentioned above, the fifth was that of <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a> – a small historic city in the north-west of the department close to Belgium – which has been since annexed in its entirety to the district of Charleville-Mézières. The former arrondissement of Rocroi then consisted of six cantons – including four on the border with Belgium – which were <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fumay" title="Fumay">Fumay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revin" title="Revin">Revin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rumigny,_Ardennes" title="Rumigny, Ardennes">Rumigny</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Signy-le-Petit" title="Signy-le-Petit">Signy-le-Petit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Under the intercommunality framework, Ardennes consists of an urban community organized around the city préfecture, called <i>Heart of Ardennes</i> and has fifteen communities of communes including in Sedan the <i>Communauté de communes du Pays sedanais</i> which is the most important.</p>
<p>In addition, the Ardennes has 33 communes that do not adhere to any Intercommunal cooperative organisation (EPCI).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The economy of the department, after previously resting on agriculture (forestry and livestock – crops are poor), has been based for over a century now on industry (now in a difficult position) and the services sector, although the proportion of the Ardennes labour force working in this sector is lower than the national average. At the beginning of the 19th century, the region was the largest in France for metal working using <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>. There was no <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> found in the department (only <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slate</a> from <a href="/wiki/Fumay" title="Fumay">Fumay</a> was usable) but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallurgical_industry" title="Metallurgical industry">metallurgical industry</a> developed there (bolts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screws" title="Screws">screws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nail_(fastener)" title="Nail (fastener)">nails</a>). The railway with many branches (<i>Compagnie des chemins de fer des Ardennes</i> from the Sellière family which merged with the <a href="/wiki/Chemins_de_fer_de_l%27Est" title="Chemins de fer de l'Est">Compagnie de l'Est</a>) accelerated the industrialization at the end of the 19th century in Charleville, Sedan (which had its <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">trams</a>), and Revin. The newspaper <i>L'Usine ardennaise</i> (The Ardennes Machine) became <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Usine_nouvelle" title="L'Usine nouvelle">L'Usine nouvelle</a></i> (The New Machine). The crisis in the 1970s precipitated the decline of metallurgical activity in the department (the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> and small workshops closed one after the other: <a href="/wiki/Blagny" title="Blagny">Blagny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vireux-Molhain" title="Vireux-Molhain">Vireux-Molhain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murtin-et-Bogny" title="Murtin-et-Bogny">Murtin-et-Bogny</a>, etc.). Today there are still many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subcontractors" title="Subcontractors">subcontractors</a> for the railway industry (<a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> for example) and the car industry (<a href="/wiki/GMC_(automobile)" title="GMC (automobile)">GMC</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PSA_Peugeot_Citro%C3%ABn" title="PSA Peugeot Citroën">PSA Peugeot Citroën</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a>, among others), <a href="/wiki/Herm%C3%A8s" title="Hermès">Hermès</a> has recently installed themselves at <a href="/wiki/Murtin-et-Bogny" title="Murtin-et-Bogny">Murtin-et-Bogny</a>, PSA Peugeot Citroën is installed at <a href="/wiki/Les_Ayvelles" title="Les Ayvelles">Les Ayvelles</a>. There is also a nuclear site – the <a href="/wiki/Chooz_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Chooz Nuclear Power Plant">Chooz Nuclear Power Plant</a> with several reactors including the first <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactor</a> (REP) in France. Agriculture has grown considerably using industrial techniques (<a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a>).</p>
<p>There are direct TGV trains from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gare_de_l%27Est" title="Gare de l'Est">Gare de l'Est</a> in Paris to <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a> (1h 35m), Sedan, and several TGV trains to Reims with a change for Charleville-Mézières.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transport_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-transport-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A "Y" road has also been set up with the A4 Reims-Paris, the A34 (freeway), and the links to <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> which need to take the N5 to join the motorway ring of <a href="/wiki/Charleroi" title="Charleroi">Charleroi</a>. Similarly, the junction with the highway leading to <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> needs to use a portion of highway in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transport_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-transport-8">[7]</a></sup> Improved means of land communication (TGV and A34 motorway) provides the benefit of relative proximity to Paris, <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> and Belgian cities such as <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Liège">Liège</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charleroi" title="Charleroi">Charleroi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.</p>
<p>Every year in September, the Sedan fair is a big shopping event (2nd in the region) which attracts about 250,000 people each year.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Information">General Information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: General Information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the department there are iconic landmarks such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Sedan" title="Château de Sedan">Château de Sedan</a></i> (the busiest Ardennes pay site with an average of 60,000 admissions per year), the fortified site of Charlemont at <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a>, the fort at <a href="/wiki/Les_Ayvelles" title="Les Ayvelles">Les Ayvelles</a>, and the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a> attract many visitors each year. There are also many fortified churches and medieval sites in the department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[N 2]</a></sup> There is the Ardennes forest, the "Green Way" (a bicycle path connecting Montcy-Notre Dame near <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a> to <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a> along the Meuse valley) promoting weekend tourism and tourist routes (green tourism).</p>
<p>Cultural tourism is booming with many music festivals (Le Cabaret Vert, the Douzy'k festival, the Aymon Folk Festival) not to mention museums (Museum of the Ardennes etc.) and castles (<a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Sedan" title="Château de Sedan">Castle of Sedan</a>) and the growing interest in industrial heritage which is of more and more value.</p>
<p>Finally, the creation of the Natural Regional Park of Ardennes (Regional Natural Park of Ardennes on 21 December 2011, should continue to promote this type of tourism which is increasingly sought.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_homes">Second homes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Second homes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Home purchases by Belgians and Dutch people are common in the region because the prices are much lower than in their country of residence. However, according to the census of the general population of 1 January 2008, 3.7% of available housing in the department are second homes which is rather low.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For more than 40 years (the first time in 1961 at the initiative of Jacques Félix) there has been in Charleville-Mézières the <i>World Festival of Puppet Theatre</i> (now every three years). At the last festival in 2009 more than 200,000 people were present. In the same city there is also a school: the International Institute of Puppetry. The <i>Rock Festival and Territory Le Cabaret Vert</i> had more than 50,000 festival-goers at the seventh festival in 2011 and this takes place every year in Charleville-Mézières making it the 7th largest festival in France by attendance. The festival <i>The Poetic Otherworld</i> organizes events in October in some Ardennes communes.</p>

<p>At Sedan the medieval festival is held every year around the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Sedan" title="Château de Sedan">Castle of Sedan</a>, the largest castle in Europe. This is an event that brings together more than 30,000 spectators.</p>

<p>At <a href="/wiki/Rethel" title="Rethel">Rethel</a> there are the famous festivals of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anne" title="Saint Anne">Saint Anne</a> which have been held for over 200 years.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Bogny-sur-Meuse" title="Bogny-sur-Meuse">Bogny-sur-Meuse</a> there is the <i>Aymon Folk Festival</i> which brings together nearly 10,000 people.</p>
<p>The Ardennes also has other well-known festivals such as the <i>Festimeuse</i> which attracts 10,000 people, the festival of <i>Cassine</i> with 7,000 people at the 3rd festival in 2010.</p>
<p>The metal festival of Vouziers attractes about 2000 people. The Rock festival in El Mont at Aiglemont hosted more than 2,000 people in 2010. Lastly there is at <a href="/wiki/Douzy" title="Douzy">Douzy</a> every July and every second year the Douzy'k Festival which brings between 5,000 and 7,000 people each time.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Argonne" title="Forest of Argonne">Argonne</a> does not have only two musical events. For 14 years, the village of Louvergny has staged a lyrical festival called <i>Encounters of Louvergny</i> in early August as the heart of the Argonne campaign, with singers from different countries. More recently <i>Notes of Argonne</i> propose to cross the Argonne mountains with copncerts of classical music with regional and national performers. The May 2008 festival included Patrice Fontanarosa and Marielle Nordmann. Both events had an immediate success in the region and the public has not stopped coming since.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ardennes_in_tales_and_legends">The Ardennes in tales and legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: The Ardennes in tales and legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For a long time the region was a land of legends with its rocks, rivers, lakes, and thick dark forests: for example <a href="/wiki/The_Four_Sons_of_Aymon" title="The Four Sons of Aymon">The Four Sons of Aymon</a>, knights on their horse called <a href="/wiki/Bayard_(legend)" title="Bayard (legend)">Bayard</a> helped by the enchanter Maugis who gave his name to village of <a href="/wiki/Noyers-Pont-Maugis" title="Noyers-Pont-Maugis">Noyers-Pont-Maugis</a> at the time of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ardennes_in_literature">The Ardennes in literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: The Ardennes in literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The novel by Yves Gibeau, <i>Les gros sous</i> (The big money) (1953) takes place in the south-west of the department.</p>
<p>The Ardennes serves as the backdrop to the novel by <a href="/wiki/Julien_Gracq" title="Julien Gracq">Julien Gracq</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Balcony_in_the_Forest" title="A Balcony in the Forest">A Balcony in the Forest</a></i> (<i>Un balcon en forêt</i>), published in 1958 and for which <a href="/wiki/Michel_Mitrani" title="Michel Mitrani">Michel Mitrani</a> made a film in 1979 with <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Villeret" title="Jacques Villeret">Jacques Villeret</a>. This novel/story is based on the experience as a soldier by the author at the beginning of the Second World War.</p>
<p>The region serves as a backdrop for the Ardennes writer <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Dh%C3%B4tel" title="André Dhôtel">André Dhôtel</a> (1900-1991), especially in <i>Le Pays où l'on n’arrive jamais</i> (The country where one never arrives).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ardennes_in_cinema">The Ardennes in cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: The Ardennes in cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The department has a varied natural environment (the Meuse valley, the border between Belgium and France, the Ardennes plateau, forests, etc.) which attracts filmmakers and television which first began in the late 1960s and in the 1970s.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Train_(1973_film)" title="The Train (1973 film)">Le Train</a></i> (The Train) (1973) with <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Trintignant" title="Jean-Louis Trintignant">Jean-Louis Trintignant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romy_Schneider" title="Romy Schneider">Romy Schneider</a> was partly filmed in the valley of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meuse_(river)" title="Meuse (river)">Meuse</a></li>
<li><i>Maigret chez les Flamands</i> (Maigret and the Flemes) <i>(1976), a novel by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Simenon" title="Georges Simenon">Georges Simenon</a> who knew the area nearby (<a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Liège">Liège</a>). Since then he travelled a lot and located the action at <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a> (the TV movie with <a href="/wiki/Jean_Richard" title="Jean Richard">Jean Richard</a> was shot here).</i></li>
<li>The film by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Autant-Lara" title="Claude Autant-Lara">Claude Autant-Lara</a>, <i>Les Patates</i> (The Potatoes), produced in 1969 with <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Perret" title="Pierre Perret">Pierre Perret</a>, was shot entirely in <a href="/wiki/Bourg-Fid%C3%A8le" title="Bourg-Fidèle">Bourg-Fidèle</a>, a village on the plateau of <a href="/wiki/Rocroi" title="Rocroi">Rocroi</a>.</li>
<li>In 2006, <i>Les Enfants du Pays</i> (The children of the country) by Pierre Javaux with <a href="/wiki/Michel_Serrault" title="Michel Serrault">Michel Serrault</a> was located in a small village lost in the Ardennes Forest in May 1940 when five <a href="/wiki/Senegalese_Tirailleurs" title="Senegalese Tirailleurs">Senegalese Tirailleurs</a> are isolated from their regiment and lost, meet an old man and his two small children left alone after the exodus of the population.</li>
<li>In 2007, Marcel Trillat made <i>Silence dans la vallée</i> (Silence in the valley), a documentary about the liquidation of the ironworks at <a href="/wiki/Nouzonville" title="Nouzonville">Nouzonville</a>, the <i>Ateliers Thomé-Génot</i> (workshops Thomé-Génot) by American buyers who empty the cash for themselves. Cellatex suffered the same fate at <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">Givet</a> in 2000.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_local_press_in_Ardennes">The local press in Ardennes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardennes_(department)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: The local press in Ardennes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Regional newspapers are: <i>L'Ardennais</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Charleville-M%C3%A9zi%C3%A8res" title="Charleville-Mézières">Charleville-Mézières</a>) and <i>L'Union</i>. They now have shared writing – only the first page differs. Since 2009, a weekly newspaper has been published in the Ardennes: <i>La Semaine des Ardennes</i>. Printed in Charleville-Mézières, over 2000 copies are printed.</p>
