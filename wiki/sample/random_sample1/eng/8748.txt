<p><b>Arsine</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">As</a><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><sub>3</sub>. This flammable, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a>, and highly toxic <a href="/wiki/Pnictogen_hydride" title="Pnictogen hydride">pnictogen hydride</a> gas is one of the simplest compounds of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-4">[4]</a></sup> Despite its lethality, it finds some applications in the semiconductor industry and for the synthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoarsenic_compound" title="Organoarsenic compound">organoarsenic compounds</a>. The term <i>arsine</i> is commonly used to describe a class of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoarsenic" title="Organoarsenic">organoarsenic</a> compounds of the formula AsH<sub>3âx</sub>R<sub>x</sub>, where R = <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a>. For example, As(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, called <a href="/wiki/Triphenylarsine" title="Triphenylarsine">triphenylarsine</a>, is referred to as "an arsine."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_properties">General properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At its standard state, arsine is a colorless, denser-than-air gas that is slightly soluble in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> (20% at 20 C) and in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvent" title="Organic solvent">organic solvents</a> as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Whereas arsine itself is odorless<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, owing to its oxidation by air it is possible to smell a slight <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a> or fish-like scent when the compound is present above 0.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This compound is generally regarded as stable, since at room temperature it decomposes only slowly. At temperatures of ca. 230Â Â°C decomposition to arsenic and hydrogen is rapid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Several factors, such as <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, presence of light and certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> (namely <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>) facilitate the rate of decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INRS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-INRS-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>AsH<sub>3</sub> is a pyramidal molecule with HâAsâH angles of 91.8Â° and three equivalent AsâH bonds, each of 1.519 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="ÃngstrÃ¶m">Ã</a> length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_synthesis">Discovery and synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery and synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The understanding of the chemical properties of AsH<sub>3</sub> is well developed and can be anticipated based on an average of the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Phosphine" title="Phosphine">PH<sub>3</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Stibine" title="Stibine">SbH<sub>3</sub></a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_decomposition">Thermal decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermal decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Typical for a heavy hydride (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Stibine" title="Stibine">SbH<sub>3</sub></a>, H<sub>2</sub>Te, SnH<sub>4</sub>), AsH<sub>3</sub> is unstable with respect to its elements. In other words, AsH<sub>3</sub> is stable kinetically but not thermodynamically.</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>2 AsH<sub>3</sub> â 3 H<sub>2</sub> + 2 As</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>This decomposition reaction is the basis of the Marsh Test described below, which detects the metallic As.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Continuing the analogy to SbH<sub>3</sub>, AsH<sub>3</sub> is readily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidized</a> by concentrated O<sub>2</sub> or the dilute O<sub>2</sub> concentration in air:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>2 AsH<sub>3</sub> + 3 O<sub>2</sub> â As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Arsine will react violently in presence of strong oxidizing agents, such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">potassium permanganate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">sodium hypochlorite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INRS_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-INRS-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursor_to_metallic_derivatives">Precursor to metallic derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Precursor to metallic derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>AsH<sub>3</sub> is used as a precursor to metal complexes of "naked" (or "nearly naked") As. Illustrative is the dimanganese species [(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)Mn(CO)<sub>2</sub>]<sub>2</sub>AsH, wherein the Mn<sub>2</sub>AsH core is planar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herrmann_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herrmann-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gutzeit_test">Gutzeit test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gutzeit test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A characteristic test for arsenic involves the reaction of AsH<sub>3</sub> with Ag<sup>+</sup>, called the Gutzeit test for arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-King-12">[12]</a></sup> Although this test has become obsolete in <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">analytical chemistry</a>, the underlying reactions further illustrate the affinity of AsH<sub>3</sub> for "soft" metal cations. In the Gutzeit test, AsH<sub>3</sub> is generated by reduction of aqueous arsenic compounds, typically <a href="/wiki/Arsenite" title="Arsenite">arsenites</a>, with Zn in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>. The evolved gaseous AsH<sub>3</sub> is then exposed to AgNO<sub>3</sub> either as powder or as a solution. With solid AgNO<sub>3</sub>, AsH<sub>3</sub> reacts to produce yellow Ag<sub>4</sub>AsNO<sub>3</sub>, whereas AsH<sub>3</sub> reacts with a solution of AgNO<sub>3</sub> to give black Ag<sub>3</sub>As.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid-base_reactions">Acid-base reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Acid-base reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The acidic properties of the AsâH bond are often exploited. Thus, AsH<sub>3</sub> can be deprotonated:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>AsH<sub>3</sub> + NaNH<sub>2</sub> â NaAsH<sub>2</sub> + NH<sub>3</sub></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Upon reaction with the aluminium trialkyls, AsH<sub>3</sub> gives the trimeric [R<sub>2</sub>AlAsH<sub>2</sub>]<sub>3</sub>, where R = (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atwood_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atwood-13">[13]</a></sup> This reaction is relevant to the mechanism by which GaAs forms from AsH<sub>3</sub> (see below).</p>
<p>AsH<sub>3</sub> is generally considered non-basic, but it can be protonated by <a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacids</a> to give isolable salts of the tetrahedral species [AsH<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minkwitz_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Minkwitz-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_halogen_compounds">Reaction with halogen compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reaction with halogen compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reactions of arsine with the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>) or some of their compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_trichloride" title="Nitrogen trichloride">nitrogen trichloride</a>, are extremely dangerous and can result in explosions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INRS_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-INRS-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catenation">Catenation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Catenation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to the behavior of PH<sub>3</sub>, AsH<sub>3</sub> does not form stable chains, although H<sub>2</sub>AsâAsH<sub>2</sub> and even H<sub>2</sub>AsâAs(H)âAsH<sub>2</sub> have been detected. The diarsine is unstable above â100Â Â°C.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microelectronics_applications">Microelectronics applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Microelectronics applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>AsH<sub>3</sub> is used in the synthesis of semiconducting materials related to <a href="/wiki/Microelectronics" title="Microelectronics">microelectronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state lasers</a>. Related to <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">Phosphorus</a>, Arsenic is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductors)" title="Doping (semiconductors)">n-dopant</a> for silicon and germanium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INRS_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-INRS-6">[6]</a></sup> More importantly, AsH<sub>3</sub> is used to make the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GaAs" title="GaAs">GaAs</a> by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition (CVD)</a> at 700â900Â Â°C:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Ga(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + AsH<sub>3</sub> â GaAs + 3 CH<sub>4</sub></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>For microelectronic applications, arsine can be provided via a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-atmospheric&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sub-atmospheric (page does not exist)">sub-atmospheric</a> gas source. In this type of gas package, the arsine is adsorbed on a solid microporous adsorbent inside a gas cylinder. This method allows the gas to be stored without pressure, significantly reducing the risk of an arsine gas leak from the cylinder. With this apparatus, arsine is obtained by applying vacuum to the gas cylinder valve outlet. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductor_manufacturing" title="Semiconductor manufacturing">semiconductor manufacturing</a>, this method is practical as these processes usually operate under high vacuum.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_warfare">Chemical warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chemical warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">WWII</a> AsH<sub>3</sub> was proposed as a possible <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical warfare</a> weapon. The gas is colorless, almost odorless, and 2.5 times denser than air, as required for a blanketing effect sought in chemical warfare. It is also lethal in concentrations far lower than those required to smell its <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>-like scent. In spite of these characteristics, arsine was never officially used as a weapon, because of its high flammability and its lower efficacy when compared to the non-flammable alternative <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>. On the other hand, several <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> based on arsine, such as <a href="/wiki/Lewisite" title="Lewisite">lewisite</a> (Î²-chlorovinyldichloroarsine), <a href="/wiki/Adamsite" title="Adamsite">adamsite</a> (diphenylaminechloroarsine), <a href="/wiki/Diphenylchlorarsine" title="Diphenylchlorarsine">Clark I</a> (diphenylchloroarsine) and <a href="/wiki/Diphenylcyanoarsine" title="Diphenylcyanoarsine">Clark II</a> (diphenylcyanoarsine) have been effectively developed for use in chemical warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suchard_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suchard-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forensic_science_and_the_Marsh_test">Forensic science and the Marsh test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Forensic science and the Marsh test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AsH<sub>3</sub> is also well known in <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">forensic science</a> because it is a chemical intermediate in the detection of arsenic poisoning. The old (but extremely sensitive) <a href="/wiki/Marsh_test" title="Marsh test">Marsh test</a> generates AsH<sub>3</sub> in the presence of arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-4">[4]</a></sup> This procedure, published in 1836 by <a href="/wiki/James_Marsh_(chemist)" title="James Marsh (chemist)">James Marsh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> is based upon treating an As-containing sample of a victim's body (typically the stomach contents) with As-free <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>: if the sample contains arsenic, gaseous arsine will form. The gas is swept into a glass tube and decomposed by means of heating around 250â300Â Â°C. The presence of As is indicated by formation of a deposit in the heated part of the equipment. On the other hand, the appearance of a black mirror deposit in the <i>cool</i> part of the equipment indicates the presence of antimony (the highly unstable <a href="/wiki/Stibine" title="Stibine">SbH<sub>3</sub></a> decomposes even at low temperatures).</p>
<p>The Marsh test was widely used by the end of the 19th century and the start of the 20th; nowadays more sophisticated techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy" title="Atomic spectroscopy">atomic spectroscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inductively_coupled_plasma" title="Inductively coupled plasma">inductively coupled plasma</a> and <a href="/wiki/X-ray_fluorescence" title="X-ray fluorescence">x-ray fluorescence</a> analysis are employed in the forensic field. Though <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">neutron activation</a> analysis was used to detect trace levels of arsenic in the mid 20th century, it has since fallen out of use in modern forensics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicology">Toxicology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Toxicology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>For the toxicology of other arsenic compounds, see <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">Arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">Arsenic trioxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning#Arsenicosis:_chronic_arsenic_poisoning_from_drinking_water" title="Arsenic poisoning">Arsenicosis</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minkwitz_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Minkwitz-14">[14]</a></sup> The toxicity of arsine is distinct from that of other arsenic compounds. The main route of exposure is by inhalation, although poisoning after skin contact has also been described. Arsine attacks <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemoglobin" title="Haemoglobin">haemoglobin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a>, causing them to be destroyed by the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first signs of exposure, which can take several hours to become apparent, are <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertigo_(medical)" title="Vertigo (medical)">vertigo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, followed by the symptoms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemolytic_anaemia" title="Haemolytic anaemia">haemolytic anaemia</a> (high levels of unconjugated <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">bilirubin</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemoglobinuria" title="Haemoglobinuria">haemoglobinuria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nephropathy" title="Nephropathy">nephropathy</a>. In severe cases, the damage to the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a> can be long-lasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PGCH_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-PGCH-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Exposure to arsine concentrations of 250Â ppm is rapidly fatal: concentrations of 25â30Â ppm are fatal for 30Â min exposure, and concentrations of 10Â ppm can be fatal at longer exposure times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDLH_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-IDLH-3">[3]</a></sup> Symptoms of poisoning appear after exposure to concentrations of 0.5Â ppm. There is little information on the chronic toxicity of arsine, although it is reasonable to assume that, in common with other arsenic compounds, a long-term exposure could lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenicosis" title="Arsenicosis">arsenicosis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is classified as an <a href="/wiki/List_of_extremely_hazardous_substances" title="List of extremely hazardous substances">extremely hazardous substance</a> in the United States as defined in Section 302 of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Planning_and_Community_Right-to-Know_Act" title="Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act">Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act</a> (42 U.S.C. 11002), and is subject to strict reporting requirements by facilities which produce, store, or use it in significant quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov-right-know_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov-right-know-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
