<p><i><b>Et in Arcadia ego</b></i> (also known as <i><b>Les bergers d'Arcadie</b></i> or <i><b>The Arcadian Shepherds</b></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a 1637–38 painting by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Poussin" title="Nicolas Poussin">Nicolas Poussin</a> (1594–1665). It depicts a <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a> scene with idealized <a href="/wiki/Shepherd" title="Shepherd">shepherds</a> from <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> clustering around an austere <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tomb</a>. It is held in the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p>
<p>Poussin painted two versions of the subject under the same title; his earlier version, painted in 1627, is held at <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth_House" title="Chatsworth House">Chatsworth House</a>. An earlier treatment of the theme was painted by <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a> circa 1618–22, also titled <i><a href="/wiki/Et_in_Arcadia_ego_(Guercino)" title="Et in Arcadia ego (Guercino)">Et in Arcadia ego</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Et_in_Arcadia_ego&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The translation of the phrase is "Even in Arcadia, there am I". The usual interpretation is that "I" refers to death, and "Arcadia" means a utopian land. It would thus be a <i><a href="/wiki/Memento_mori" title="Memento mori">memento mori</a></i>. During Antiquity, many Greeks lived in cities close to the sea, and led an urban life. Only <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(ancient_region)" title="Arcadia (ancient region)">Arcadians</a>, in the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>, lacked cities, were far from the sea, and led a shepherd life. Thus for urban Greeks, especially during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_era" title="Hellenistic era">Hellenistic era</a>, Arcadia symbolized pure, rural, idyllic life, far from the city.</p>
<p>However, Poussin's biographer, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_F%C3%A9libien" title="André Félibien">André Félibien</a>, interpreted the phrase to mean that "the person buried in this tomb lived in Arcadia"; in other words, that the person too once enjoyed the pleasures of life on earth. This reading was common in the 18th and 19th centuries. For example, <a href="/wiki/William_Hazlitt" title="William Hazlitt">William Hazlitt</a> wrote that Poussin "describes some shepherds wandering out in a morning of the spring, and coming to a tomb with this inscription, 'I also was an Arcadian'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The former interpretation ("ego" referring to death) is now generally considered more likely; the ambiguity of the phrase is the subject of a famous essay by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_historian" title="Art historian">art historian</a> <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Panofsky" title="Erwin Panofsky">Erwin Panofsky</a> (see References). Either way, the sentiment was meant to set up an <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">ironic</a> contrast between the shadow of death and the usual idle merriment that the <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymphs</a> and swains of ancient Arcadia were thought to embody.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inspiration">Inspiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Et_in_Arcadia_ego&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inspiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="867" data-file-width="1031" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg/220px-Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg/330px-Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg/440px-Et-in-Arcadia-ego.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Et_in_Arcadia_ego_(Guercino)" title="Et in Arcadia ego (Guercino)">Guercino's version</a> of the subject.</div>

<p>The first appearance of a tomb with a memorial inscription (to <a href="/wiki/Daphnis" title="Daphnis">Daphnis</a>) amid the idyllic settings of Arcadia appears in <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Eclogues" title="Eclogues">Eclogues</a></i> V 42 ff. Virgil took the idealized Sicilian rustics that had first appeared in the <i>Idylls</i> of <a href="/wiki/Theocritus" title="Theocritus">Theocritus</a> and set them in the primitive Greek district of Arcadia (see Eclogues VII and X). The idea was taken up anew in the circle of <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo de' Medici</a> in the 1460s and 1470s, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florentine_Renaissance" title="Florentine Renaissance">Florentine Renaissance</a>.</p>
<p>In his pastoral work <i>Arcadia</i> (1504), <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Sannazaro" title="Jacopo Sannazaro">Jacopo Sannazaro</a> fixed the Early Modern perception of Arcadia as a lost world of <a href="/wiki/Idyll" title="Idyll">idyllic</a> bliss, remembered in regretful dirges. The first pictorial representation of the familiar <i>memento mori</i> theme that was popularized in 16th-century Venice, now made more concrete and vivid by the inscription <small>ET IN ARCADIA EGO</small>, is <a href="/wiki/Et_in_Arcadia_ego_(Guercino)" title="Et in Arcadia ego (Guercino)">Guercino</a>'s version, painted between 1618 and 1622 (in the <a href="/wiki/Galleria_Nazionale_d%27Arte_Antica" title="Galleria Nazionale d'Arte Antica">Galleria Nazionale d'Arte Antica</a>, Rome), in which the inscription gains force from the prominent presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skull_(mythology)" title="Skull (mythology)">skull</a> in the foreground, beneath which the words are carved.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1627_version">1627 version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Et_in_Arcadia_ego&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1627 version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Poussin1627.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="559" data-file-width="437" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Poussin1627.jpg/220px-Poussin1627.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Poussin1627.jpg/330px-Poussin1627.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Poussin1627.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Poussin1627.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Poussin" title="Nicolas Poussin">Poussin's</a> 1627 version of the <i>Arcadian Shepherds</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth_House" title="Chatsworth House">Chatsworth House</a>, depicting a different tomb with the same inscription.</div>
</div>

<p>Poussin's own first version of the painting (now in <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth_House" title="Chatsworth House">Chatsworth House</a>) was probably commissioned as a reworking of Guercino's version. It is in a far more <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> style than the later version, characteristic of Poussin's early work. In the Chatsworth painting the shepherds are actively discovering the half-hidden and overgrown tomb, and are reading the inscription with curious expressions. The shepherdess, standing at the left, is posed in sexually suggestive fashion, very different from her austere counterpart in the later version. The later version has a far more geometric composition and the figures are much more contemplative. The mask-like face of the shepherdess conforms to the conventions of the Classical "Greek profile".</p>

<p>The most important difference between the two versions is that in the latter version, one of the two shepherds recognizes the shadow of his companion on the tomb and circumscribes the silhouette with his finger. According to an ancient tradition (see Pliny the Elder, nat. Hist. XXXV 5, 15), this is the moment in which the art of painting is first discovered. Thus, the shepherd's shadow is the first image in art history. But the shadow on the tomb is also a symbol of death (in the first version symbolized by a skull on the top of the tomb). The meaning of this highly intricate composition seems to be that, from prehistory onward, the discovery of art has been the creative response of humankind to the shocking fact of mortality. Thus, death’s claim to rule even Arcadia is challenged by art (symbolized by the beautifully dressed maiden), who must insist that she was discovered in Arcadia too, and that she is the legitimate ruler everywhere, whilst death only usurps its power. In the face of death, art's duty—indeed, her <i>raison d’être</i>—is to recall absent loved ones, console anxieties, evoke and reconcile conflicting emotions, surmount isolation, and facilitate the expression of the unutterable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The undated mid-eighteenth-century marble bas-relief is part of the Shepherds Monument, a garden feature at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shugborough_House" title="Shugborough House">Shugborough House</a>, Staffordshire, England. Beneath it is the cryptic <a href="/wiki/Shugborough_inscription" title="Shugborough inscription">Shugborough inscription</a>, as yet undeciphered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The reversed composition suggests that it was copied from an <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a>, the compositions of which are commonly reversed because direct copies to the plate produce mirror images on printing.</p>
<p>In 1832 another relief was sculpted as part of the monument marking Poussin's tomb in Rome, on which it appears beneath a bust of the artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the words of the art historian Richard Verdi, it appears as if the shepherds are contemplating "their own author's death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><i>Et in Arcadia ego</i> (the German <i>Auch ich in Arkadien!</i> is used) is the motto of <i><a href="/wiki/Italian_Journey" title="Italian Journey">Italian Journey</a></i> (1816–17) by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Et in Arcadia ego</i> is the title of section 295, Volume II, Part Two ("The Wanderer and his Shadow"), of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Human,_All_Too_Human" title="Human, All Too Human">Human, All Too Human</a></i> (1880). The passage describes a pastoral evening scene in the high mountains, finishing with the lines: "Unconsciously, as if nothing could be more natural, you peopled this pure, clear world of light (which had no trace of yearning, of expectancy, of looking forward or backward) with Greek heroes. You felt it all as Poussin and his school felt it, at once heroic and idyllic. So individual men too have lived, constantly feeling themselves in the world and the world in themselves, and among them one of the greatest men, the inventor of a heroico-idyllic form of philosophy, Epicurus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>I Too Lived in Arcadia</i> is the title of the 1942 memoirs of <a href="/wiki/Marie_Adelaide_Belloc_Lowndes" title="Marie Adelaide Belloc Lowndes">Marie Adelaide Belloc Lowndes</a>, describing the life of her mother, the English feminist <a href="/wiki/Bessie_Rayner_Parkes" title="Bessie Rayner Parkes">Bessie Rayner Parkes</a></li>
<li><i>Et in Arcadia Ego</i> is the title of Book One of <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a></i> (1945) in which a skull bearing the phrase acts as a metaphor for death and for a yearning for a lost paradise resulting from the transformation of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevenson1993_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson1993-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Et in Arcadia Ego</i> is the title of a dystopian poem published by <a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a> in 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auden1965_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Auden1965-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>In conjunction with John Andrew, the artist <a href="/wiki/Ian_Hamilton_Finlay" title="Ian Hamilton Finlay">Ian Hamilton Finlay</a> created a marble carving entitled <i>Et in Arcadia ego</i> in 1976. The main part of the carving shows a military tank in a pastoral landscape, below which are carved the title and the words "After Nicholas Poussin". Several other works by Finlay directly or indirectly reference Poussin's artwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abrioux1985_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abrioux1985-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>The authors of the pseudohistory <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Blood_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail">The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</a></i> (1982), under the false impression that "et in arcadia ego" was not a proper Latin sentence, proposed that it is an <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a> for <i>I! Tego arcana dei</i>, which translates to "Begone! I keep God's secrets", suggesting that the tomb contains the remains of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> or another important <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> figure.</li>
<li>The phrase appears in <a href="/wiki/Cormac_McCarthy" title="Cormac McCarthy">Cormac McCarthy</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_Meridian" title="Blood Meridian">Blood Meridian</a></i> (1985) when the character Tobin informs The Kid that the phrase is the name <a href="/wiki/Judge_Holden" title="Judge Holden">Judge Holden</a> has ascribed to his rifle, noting "A reference to the lethal in it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(band)" title="Arcadia (band)">Arcadia</a> was the name of <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">Duran Duran</a>'s spin off band formed in 1985 by Simon Le Bon, Nick Rhodes and Roger Taylor, reportedly because of the inscription on this painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Et in Arcadia ego</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(play)#Title" title="Arcadia (play)">originally planned title</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Stoppard" title="Tom Stoppard">Tom Stoppard</a>'s 1993 play, <i><a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(play)" title="Arcadia (play)">Arcadia</a></i>, in which the phrase is used erroneously by one character (whose misuse is acknowledged by two other characters). The phrase also reflects the themes of the play itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Earthquake_Weather_(novel)" title="Earthquake Weather (novel)">Earthquake Weather</a></i> (1997) by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Powers" title="Tim Powers">Tim Powers</a>, the phrase, interpreted as an unfinished sentence meaning "Even in Arcadia, I...", plays a recurring role, tied into the book's themes of the <a href="/wiki/Fisher_King" title="Fisher King">Fisher King</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a> in his role as a <a href="/wiki/Psychopomp" title="Psychopomp">psychopomp</a>.</li>
<li><i>Et in Arcadia Ego</i> is the title of a closing chapter in <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Bullington" title="Jesse Bullington">Jesse Bullington</a>'s historical fantasy novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Enterprise_of_Death" title="The Enterprise of Death">The Enterprise of Death</a></i> (2001).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ben_Okri" title="Ben Okri">Ben Okri</a>'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=In_Arcadia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="In Arcadia (page does not exist)">In Arcadia</a></i> (2002) follows a filmcrew shooting a documentary about Poussin's painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
