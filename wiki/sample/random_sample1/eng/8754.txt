<p><b>Arcesilaus</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɑːr/ 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀρκεσίλαος</span>; 316/5–241/0 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDorandi199948_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorandi199948-1">[1]</a></sup>) was a <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> and founder of the Second or Middle <a href="/wiki/Platonic_Academy" title="Platonic Academy">Academy</a>—the phase of <a href="/wiki/Academic_skepticism" title="Academic skepticism">Academic skepticism</a>. Arcesilaus succeeded <a href="/wiki/Crates_of_Athens" title="Crates of Athens">Crates</a> as the sixth head (<a href="/wiki/Scholarch" title="Scholarch">scholarch</a>) of the Academy c. 264 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He did not preserve his thoughts in writing, so his opinions can only be gleaned second-hand from what is preserved by later writers. He was the first Academic to adopt a position of <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_skepticism" title="Philosophical skepticism">philosophical skepticism</a>, that is, he doubted the ability of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senses" title="Senses">senses</a> to discover <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a> about the world, although he may have continued to believe in the existence of truth itself. This brought in the skeptical phase of the Academy. His chief opponents were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoics" title="Stoics">Stoics</a> and their belief that reality could be comprehended with certainty.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcesilaus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arcesilaus was born in <a href="/wiki/Pitane_(Aeolis)" title="Pitane (Aeolis)">Pitane</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aeolis" title="Aeolis">Aeolis</a>. His early education was provided by <a href="/wiki/Autolycus_of_Pitane" title="Autolycus of Pitane">Autolycus</a> the <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, with whom he migrated to <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a>. Afterwards, he studied <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a> in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>; but adopted philosophy and became a disciple first of <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> and afterwards of <a href="/wiki/Crantor" title="Crantor">Crantor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He subsequently became intimate with <a href="/wiki/Polemon_(scholarch)" title="Polemon (scholarch)">Polemo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crates_of_Athens" title="Crates of Athens">Crates</a>, and eventually became head of the school (σχολάρχης). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diogenes_La%C3%ABrtius" title="Diogenes Laërtius">Diogenes Laërtius</a> says that, like his successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacydes" title="Lacydes">Lacydes</a>, he died of excessive drinking, but the testimony of others (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cleanthes" title="Cleanthes">Cleanthes</a>) and his own precepts discredit the story, and he is known to have been much respected by the Athenians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcesilaus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arcesilaus committed nothing to writing, his opinions were imperfectly known to his contemporaries, and can now only be gathered from the confused statements of his opponents. This makes his philosophy difficult to evaluate and partly inconsistent. This led scholars to see his skepticism in several ways. Some see his philosophy as completely negative or destructive of all philosophical views. Others regard him as taking the position that nothing can be known on the basis of his philosophical arguments. Others claimed he held no positive views on any philosophical topic, including the possibility of knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the one hand, he is said to have restored the doctrines of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> in an incorrupted form; while, on the other hand, according to <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> he summed up his opinions in the formula, "that he knew nothing, not even his own ignorance." There are two ways of reconciling the difficulty: either we may suppose him to have thrown out such aphorisms as an exercise for his pupils, as <a href="/wiki/Sextus_Empiricus" title="Sextus Empiricus">Sextus Empiricus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> who calls him a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sceptic" title="Sceptic">Sceptic</a>, would have us believe; or he may have really doubted the esoteric meaning of Plato, and have supposed himself to have been stripping his works of the figments of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogmatists" title="Dogmatists">Dogmatists</a>, while he was in fact taking from them all certain principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoics" title="Stoics">Stoics</a> were the chief opponents of Arcesilaus; he attacked their doctrine of a convincing conception (<i><a href="/wiki/Katalepsis" title="Katalepsis">katalêptikê phantasia</a></i>) as understood to be a mean between science and opinion - a mean which he asserted could not exist, and was merely the interpolation of a name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It involved a contradiction in terms, as the very idea of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phantasia_(philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phantasia (philosophy) (page does not exist)">phantasia</a></i> implied the possibility of false as well as true conceptions of the same object.</p>
<p>It is a question of some importance as to how the <a href="/wiki/Academic_skepticism" title="Academic skepticism">Academic skepticism</a> of the Middle and New Academy was distinguished from that of <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhonism" title="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonism</a>. Admitting the formula of Arcesilaus, "that he knew nothing, not even his own ignorance," to be an exposition of his real sentiments, it was impossible in one sense that skepticism could proceed further: but the Academic skeptics do not seem to have doubted the existence of <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a> in itself, only our capacities for obtaining it. It differed also from the principles of the pure skeptic in the practical tendency of its doctrines: while the object of the one was the attainment of perfect equanimity, the other seems rather to have retired from the barren field of speculation to practical life, and to have acknowledged some vestiges of a moral law within, at best but a probable guide, the possession of which, however, formed the real distinction between the sage and the fool. Slight as the difference may appear between the speculative statements of the two schools, a comparison of the lives of their founders and their respective successors leads to the conclusion, that a practical moderation was the characteristic of the Academic skeptics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commentary_on_Arcesilaus">Commentary on Arcesilaus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcesilaus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commentary on Arcesilaus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a> wrote of Arcesilaus in his <i><a href="/wiki/Pens%C3%A9es" title="Pensées">Pensées</a></i> (1669, para. 375):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I have seen changes in all nations and men, and thus after many changes of judgement regarding true justice, I have recognized that our nature was but in continual change, and I have not changed since; and if I changed, I would confirm my opinion. The sceptic Arcesilaus, who became a dogmatist.</p>
</blockquote>
