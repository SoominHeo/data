<p><b>Arta</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Άρτα</span>) is a city in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, capital of the <a href="/wiki/Arta_(regional_unit)" title="Arta (regional unit)">regional unit of Arta</a>, which is part of <a href="/wiki/Epirus_(region)" title="Epirus (region)">Epirus</a> region. The city was known in ancient times as <a href="/wiki/Ambracia" title="Ambracia">Ambracia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀμβρακία</span>). Arta is known for the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_of_Arta" title="Bridge of Arta">medieval bridge</a> over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachthos_River" title="Arachthos River">Arachthos River</a>. Arta is also known for its ancient sites from the era of <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhus_of_Epirus" title="Pyrrhus of Epirus">Pyrrhus of Epirus</a> and its well-preserved 13th-century castle. Arta's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> history is reflected in its many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> churches; perhaps the best known is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panagia_Paregoretissa" title="Panagia Paregoretissa">Panagia Paregoretissa</a> (Mother of God the Consoling), built about 1290 by <a href="/wiki/Despot_(court_title)" title="Despot (court title)">Despot</a> <a href="/wiki/Nikephoros_I_Komnenos_Doukas" title="Nikephoros I Komnenos Doukas">Nikephoros I Komnenos Doukas</a>. The city is the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Technological_Educational_Institute_of_Epirus" title="Technological Educational Institute of Epirus">Technological Educational Institute of Epirus</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arta,_Greece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arta,_Greece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ambracia" title="Ambracia">Ambracia</a></div>
<p>The first settlement in the area of the modern city dates to the 9th century B.C. Ambracia was founded as a <a href="/wiki/Corinth" title="Corinth">Corinthian</a> <a href="/wiki/Colonies_in_antiquity" title="Colonies in antiquity">colony</a> in the 7th century B.C.</p>
<p>In 295 BC <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhus_of_Epirus" title="Pyrrhus of Epirus">Pyrrhus of Epirus</a>, king of the <a href="/wiki/Molossians" title="Molossians">Molossians</a>, transferred the capital of his kingdom to Ambracia, which he used as a base from which he attacked the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Romans</a>. Pyrrhus managed to achieve great but costly victories against the Romans, hence the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Pyrrhic_victory" title="Pyrrhic victory">Pyrrhic victory</a>" which refers to an exchange at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Asculum" title="Battle of Asculum">Battle of Asculum</a>. In 146 BC, Ambracia became part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>.</p>

<p>Despite the existence several churches from the 9th and 10th centuries, Arta is first attested only in the late 11th century. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Komnenian_period" title="Komnenian period">Komnenian period</a>, the city flourished as a commercial centre, with links to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, and quickly rose to become an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishopric" title="Archbishopric">archbishopric</a> (by 1157). By the end of the 12th century, Arta probably formed a distinct fiscal district (<i><a href="/wiki/Episkepsis" title="Episkepsis">episkepsis</a></i>) within the wider <a href="/wiki/Theme_(Byzantine_district)" title="Theme (Byzantine district)">theme</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nicopolis_(theme)" title="Nicopolis (theme)">Nicopolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1205, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(1204)" title="Siege of Constantinople (1204)">fall</a> of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a>, Arta became the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Despotate_of_Epirus" title="Despotate of Epirus">Despotate of Epirus</a>. It continued to prosper under its new rulers, despite repeated attempts by another <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Greek</a> successor state, the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Nicaea" title="Empire of Nicaea">Empire of Nicaea</a> (and after 1261, the restored Byzantine Empire), to subdue Epirus. As late as the 15th century, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_the_Tocco" title="Chronicle of the Tocco">Chronicle of the Tocco</a></i> attests to the prosperity of Arta and its fertile region, "with many water buffaloes, cows, and horses", and a lively commercial activity in dried meat, lard, ham, furs, and <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a> drawing merchants from Venice and <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a>. Archaeological finds also attest to a local ceramic industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-2">[2]</a></sup> Fortified in 1227, Arta was briefly occupied in 1259, following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pelagonia" title="Battle of Pelagonia">Battle of Pelagonia</a>, by the Nicaeans. The restored Byzantine emperors continued their assaults, but it was not until 1338 that <a href="/wiki/Andronikos_III_Palaiologos" title="Andronikos III Palaiologos">Andronikos III Palaiologos</a> finally secured control of the city, only for it to be wrested from the Byzantines a few years later by Serbian emperor <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Du%C5%A1an" title="Stefan Dušan">Stefan Dušan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-2">[2]</a></sup> Serbian rule was followed by <a href="/wiki/Despotate_of_Arta" title="Despotate of Arta">Albanian</a> rule (1358–1416), until it was taken by <a href="/wiki/Carlo_I_Tocco" title="Carlo I Tocco">Carlo I Tocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_palatine_of_Cephalonia_and_Zakynthos" title="County palatine of Cephalonia and Zakynthos">Count of Cephalonia and Zakynthos</a>. The city remained in Tocco hands until 1449, when the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> captured it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_period_and_Independence">Ottoman period and Independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arta,_Greece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ottoman period and Independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Greece" title="Ottoman Greece">Ottoman Greece</a></div>
<p>Under Ottoman rule, the town was called in Turkish <i>Narda</i>. It was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetians</a> in 1717 and the French in 1797, but the Ottomans retook it in 1799.</p>
<p>Several battles took place near the city during the <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek War of Independence</a>. The city was finally annexed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Kingdom" title="Greek Kingdom">Greek Kingdom</a> in 1881 by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Berlin_(1878)" title="Treaty of Berlin (1878)">Treaty of Berlin</a>.</p>

<p>The modern city is on the site of ancient <a href="/wiki/Ambracia" title="Ambracia">Ambracia</a>. Remains of the classic era include the ancient walls, the ruins of an ancient temple of <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, a small theatre, and remnants of the southwest cemetery.</p>

<p>The town's fortifications were built by <a href="/wiki/Michael_I_Komnenos_Doukas" title="Michael I Komnenos Doukas">Michael I Komnenos Doukas</a> in the early 13th century, but their present form is largely post-Byzantine. Secular architecture from the Byzantine period, including the palace of the Despots of Epirus, has vanished completely, but the city preserves numerous churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most important Byzantine church is the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Paregoretissa" title="Church of the Paregoretissa">Church of the Paregoretissa</a>, built ca. 1290 by <a href="/wiki/Nikephoros_I_Komnenos_Doukas" title="Nikephoros I Komnenos Doukas">Nikephoros I Komnenos Doukas</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Anna_Palaiologina_Kantakouzene" title="Anna Palaiologina Kantakouzene">Anna Palaiologina Kantakouzene</a>. Other important churches of the late Byzantine period are the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Church_of_the_Kato_Panagia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Church of the Kato Panagia (page does not exist)">Church of the Kato Panagia</a>, built by Nikephoros I's father <a href="/wiki/Michael_II_Komnenos_Doukas" title="Michael II Komnenos Doukas">Michael II Komnenos Doukas</a>, and the Monastery of Saint Theodora, housing the tomb of the city's patron, <a href="/wiki/Theodora_of_Arta" title="Theodora of Arta">Theodora of Arta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODB_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-ODB-2">[2]</a></sup> Several other churches dating to the 9th and 10th centuries also survive in and around the city: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Basil_of_the_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saint Basil of the Bridge (page does not exist)">Saint Basil of the Bridge</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Demetrios_of_Katsoures&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saint Demetrios of Katsoures (page does not exist)">Saint Demetrios of Katsoures</a> in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plisioi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plisioi (page does not exist)">Plisioi</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Panagia_Blacherna_monastery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Panagia Blacherna monastery (page does not exist)">Panagia Blacherna monastery</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Panagia_Vryoni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Panagia Vryoni (page does not exist)">Panagia Vryoni</a> in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neochoraki,_Arta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neochoraki, Arta (page does not exist)">Neochoraki</a>, the Red Church in <a href="/wiki/Vourgareli" title="Vourgareli">Vourgareli</a>, the Panagia of Koronisia in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koronisia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koronisia (page does not exist)">Koronisia</a> and the Church of the Pantanassa in <a href="/wiki/Filippiada" title="Filippiada">Filippiada</a>.</p>

<p>The Technological Educational Institute of Epirus (T.E.I.) <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.teiep.gr" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> has a main campus and its administration seat in Arta and has branch campuses in the three other main cities of Epirus, <a href="/wiki/Ioannina" title="Ioannina">Ioannina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Igoumenitsa" title="Igoumenitsa">Igoumenitsa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Preveza" title="Preveza">Preveza</a>. It has four (4) Faculties and thirteen (13) Departments.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arta,_Greece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arta is located NNW of <a href="/wiki/Antirrio" title="Antirrio">Antirrio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messolongi" title="Messolongi">Messolongi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agrinio" title="Agrinio">Agrinio</a>, NE of <a href="/wiki/Preveza" title="Preveza">Preveza</a>, SSE of <a href="/wiki/Ioannina" title="Ioannina">Ioannina</a> and nearly SW of <a href="/wiki/Trikala" title="Trikala">Trikala</a>.</p>
<p>Regular bus lines connect Arta with all bigger Greek cities. (bus to Athens departs several times a day and trip takes about 5 hours)</p>
<p>The city is linked with the <a href="/wiki/Greek_National_Road_5" title="Greek National Road 5">GR-5</a> (Antirrio - Ioannina) and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_National_Road_30" title="Greek National Road 30">GR-30</a> which links with <a href="/wiki/Peta,_Greece" title="Peta, Greece">Peta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trikala" title="Trikala">Trikala</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachthos_River" title="Arachthos River">Arachthos River</a> flows to the west with its reservoir lying directly to the north.</p>

<p>The present municipality Arta was formed at the 2011 local government reform by the merger of the following 5 former municipalities, that became municipal units (constituent communities in brackets):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kallikratis_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kallikratis-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amvrakikos" title="Amvrakikos">Amvrakikos</a> (Aneza, Vigla, Gavria, Kalogeriko, Koronisia, Polydroso, Rachi, Strongyli, Psathotopi)</li>
<li><b>Arta</b> (Arta, Keramates, Kostakioi, <a href="/wiki/Limini" title="Limini">Limini</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Filothei,_Arta" title="Filothei, Arta">Filothei</a> (Agios Spyridon, Kalamia, Kalovatos, Kirkizates, Rokka, Chalkiades)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vlacherna" title="Vlacherna">Vlacherna</a> (Vlacherna, Grammenitsa, Grimpovo, Korfovouni)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Xirovouni" title="Xirovouni">Xirovouni</a> (Ammotopos, Dafnoti, Kampi, Pantanassa, Pistiana, Rodavgi, Skoupa, Faneromeni)</li>
</ul>
<p>The municipality has an area of 457.248 km<sup>2</sup>, the municipal unit 47.493 km<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stat01_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-stat01-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
