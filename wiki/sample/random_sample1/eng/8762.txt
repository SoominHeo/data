<p><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Seyss-Inquart.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/Arthur_Seyss-Inquart.ogg" title="Arthur Seyss-Inquart.ogg"><b>Arthur Seyss-Inquart</b></a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Seyss-Inquart.ogg" title="File:Arthur Seyss-Inquart.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> (in German: <b>Seyß-Inquart</b>; 22 July 1892 – 16 October 1946) was an <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Austrian_National_Socialism" title="Austrian National Socialism">Nazi</a> politician who served as <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Austria" title="Chancellor of Austria">Chancellor of Austria</a> for two days – from 11 to 13 March 1938 – before the <i><a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a></i> annexation of Austria by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, signing the constitutional law as acting <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> upon the resignation of President <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Miklas" title="Wilhelm Miklas">Wilhelm Miklas</a>.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, he served the Third Reich in the <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">General Government</a> of <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and as <i><a href="/wiki/Reichskommissar" title="Reichskommissar">Reichskommissar</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg trials</a>, he was found guilty of <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a>, was sentenced to death, and was subsequently executed for his crimes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_before_the_Anschluss">Life before the Anschluss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Seyss-Inquart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life before the Anschluss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seyss-Inquart was born in 1892 in <a href="/wiki/Stona%C5%99ov" title="Stonařov">Stonařov</a>, at that time Stannern, a <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>-speaking village in the neighbourhood of the predominantly German-speaking town of <a href="/wiki/Jihlava" title="Jihlava">Jihlava</a> (Iglau). This area constituted a German linguistic island in the midst of a <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>-speaking environment and this situation may have contributed to the outspoken national consciousness of the family and the young Arthur in particular. Iglau was an important town in <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, one of the Czech provinces of the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>, in which an increasing competition between Germans and Czechs was going on. His parents were the school principal Emíl Seyss-Inquart and Augusta Hirenbach.</p>
<p>The family moved to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1907. Seyss-Inquart later went to study <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">law</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">University of Vienna</a>. At the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in August 1914 Seyss-Inquart enlisted with the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Army" title="Austro-Hungarian Army">Austrian Army</a> and was given a commission with the <a href="/wiki/German_Tyrol" title="German Tyrol">Tyrolean</a> <i>Kaiserjäger</i>, subsequently serving in <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania_during_World_War_I" title="Romania during World War I">Romania</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Italy</a>. He was decorated for bravery on a number of occasions and while recovering from wounds in 1917 he completed his final examinations for his degree. Seyss-Inquart had five older siblings: Hedwig (born 1881), Richard (born 3 April 1883, became a Catholic priest, but left the Church and ministry, married in civil ceremony and became <i>Oberregierungsrat</i> and prison superior by 1940 in the <a href="/wiki/Ostmark_(Austria)" title="Ostmark (Austria)">Ostmark</a>), Irene (born 1885), Henriette (born 1887) and Robert (born 1891).</p>
<p>In 1911, Seyss-Inquart met Gertrud Maschka. The couple married in December 1916 and had three children: Ingeborg Carolina Augusta Seyss-Inquart (born 18 September 1917), Richard Seyss-Inquart (born 22 August 1921) and Dorothea Seyss-Inquart (born 7 May 1928).</p>
<p>He went into law after the war and in 1921 set up his own practice. During the early years of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_First_Republic" title="Austrian First Republic">Austrian First Republic</a>, he was close to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaterl%C3%A4ndische_Front" title="Vaterländische Front">Vaterländische Front</a>. A successful lawyer, he was invited to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_(politics)" title="Cabinet (politics)">cabinet</a> of Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Engelbert_Dollfuss" title="Engelbert Dollfuss">Engelbert Dollfuss</a> in 1933. Following Dollfuss' murder in 1934, he became a State Councillor from 1937 under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurt_von_Schuschnigg" title="Kurt von Schuschnigg">Kurt von Schuschnigg</a>. A keen mountaineer, Seyss-Inquart became the head of the German-Austrian Alpine Club. He later became a devotee of <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a>'s concepts of racial purity and sponsored various expeditions to Tibet and other parts of Asia in hopes of proving Aryan racial concepts and theories. He was not initially a member of the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Austrian_National_Socialism" title="Austrian National Socialism">National Socialist</a> party, though he was sympathetic to many of their views and actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> By 1938, however, Seyss-Inquart knew which way the political wind was blowing and became a respectable frontman for the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_National_Socialism" title="Austrian National Socialism">Austrian National Socialists</a>.</p>

<p>In February 1938, Seyss-Inquart was appointed Minister of the Interior by Schuschnigg, after <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> had threatened Schuschnigg with military actions against Austria in the event of non-compliance. On 11 March 1938, faced with a German invasion aimed at preventing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plebiscite" title="Plebiscite">plebiscite</a> of independence, Schuschnigg resigned as Austrian Chancellor. Under growing pressure from Berlin, President <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Miklas" title="Wilhelm Miklas">Wilhelm Miklas</a> reluctantly appointed Seyss-Inquart his successor. On the next day German troops crossed the border of Austria at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraphed</a> invitation of Seyss-Inquart. This telegram had actually been drafted beforehand and was released after the troops had begun to march, so as to justify the action in the eyes of the international community. Before his triumphant entry into <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, Hitler had planned to leave Austria as a puppet state, with an independent but pro-Nazi government headed by Seyss-Inquart. He was carried away, however, by the wild reception given to the German army by the majority of the Austrian population and shortly decreed that Austria would be incorporated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> as the province of <a href="/wiki/Ostmark_(Austria)" title="Ostmark (Austria)">Ostmark</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a>). Only then, on 13 March 1938, did Seyss-Inquart join the Nazi Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judseyss_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Judseyss-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_of_Ostmark_and_Southern_Poland">Head of Ostmark and Southern Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Seyss-Inquart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Head of Ostmark and Southern Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seyss-Inquart drafted the legislative act reducing Austria to a province of Germany and signed it into law on 13 March. With Hitler's approval he became Governor (<i>Reichsstatthalter</i>) of the newly named Ostmark, with <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Kaltenbrunner" title="Ernst Kaltenbrunner">Ernst Kaltenbrunner</a> his chief minister and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josef_Burckel" title="Josef Burckel">Josef Burckel</a> as Commissioner for the Reunion of Austria (concerned with the "Jewish Question"). Seyss-Inquart also received an honorary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">SS</a> rank of <a href="/wiki/Gruppenf%C3%BChrer" title="Gruppenführer">Gruppenführer</a> and in May 1939 he was made a <a href="/wiki/Minister_without_portfolio" title="Minister without portfolio">Minister without portfolio</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_Hitler" title="Cabinet Hitler">Hitler's cabinet</a>. Almost as soon as he took office, he ordered the confiscation of Jewish property and sent Jews to concentration camps. Late in his regime, he collaborated in the deportation of Jews from Austria.</p>
<p>Following the invasion of <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, Seyss-Inquart became administrative chief for Southern Poland, but did not take up that post before the <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">General Government</a> was created, in which he became a deputy to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_General" title="Governor General">Governor General</a> <a href="/wiki/Hans_Frank" title="Hans Frank">Hans Frank</a>. He fully supported the heavy-handed policies put into effect by Frank, including persecution of Jews. He was also aware of the <a href="/wiki/Abwehr" title="Abwehr">Abwehr</a>'s murder of dozens of Polish intellectuals.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reichskommissar_in_the_Netherlands">Reichskommissar in the Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Seyss-Inquart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reichskommissar in the Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976,_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="797" data-file-width="536" height="327" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976%2C_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976%2C_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976%2C_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976%2C_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976%2C_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976%2C_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_121-1976,_Arthur_Sey%C3%9F-Inquart.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Following the capitulation of the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> Seyss-Inquart was appointed <a href="/wiki/Reichskommissar" title="Reichskommissar">Reichskommissar</a> for the Occupied <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in May 1940, charged with directing the civil administration, with creating close economic collaboration with Germany and with defending the interests of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Reich</a>. Among the Dutch people he was mockingly referred to as "Zes en een kwart" (six and a quarter), a play on his name, and the fact that Seyss-Inquart was limping. He supported the Dutch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationaal-Socialistische_Beweging" title="Nationaal-Socialistische Beweging">NSB</a> and allowed them to create a paramilitary <i>Landwacht</i>, which acted as an auxiliary police force. Other political parties were banned in late 1941 and many former government officials were imprisoned at <a href="/wiki/Sint-Michielsgestel" title="Sint-Michielsgestel">Sint-Michielsgestel</a>. The administration of the country was controlled by Seyss-Inquart himself and he answered directly to Hitler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He oversaw the politicization of cultural groups from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nederlandsche_Kultuurkamer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nederlandsche Kultuurkamer (page does not exist)">Nederlandsche Kultuurkamer</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nederlandsche_Kultuurkamer" title="nl:Nederlandsche Kultuurkamer">nl</a>)</span> "right down to the chessplayers' club", and set up a number of other politicised associations.</p>
<p>He introduced measures to combat resistance, and when a widespread strike took place in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnhem" title="Arnhem">Arnhem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hilversum" title="Hilversum">Hilversum</a> in May 1943, special summary <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">court-martial</a> procedures were brought in, and a collective fine of 18 million <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">guilders</a> was imposed. Up until the liberation, Seyss-Inquart authorized the execution of around 800 people, although some reports put this total at over 1,500, including the executions of people under the so-called "Hostage Law", the death of political prisoners who were close to being liberated, the <a href="/wiki/Putten_raid" title="Putten raid">Putten raid</a>, and the reprisal executions of 117 Dutchmen for the attack on SS and Police Leader <a href="/wiki/Hanns_Albin_Rauter" title="Hanns Albin Rauter">Hanns Albin Rauter</a>. Although the majority of Seyss-Inquart's powers were transferred to the military commander in the Netherlands and the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> in July 1944, he remained a force to be reckoned with.</p>
<p>There were two small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" title="Concentration camp">concentration camps</a> in the Netherlands – <a href="/wiki/Herzogenbusch_concentration_camp" title="Herzogenbusch concentration camp">KZ Herzogenbusch</a> near <a href="/wiki/Vught" title="Vught">Vught</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amersfoort_concentration_camp" title="Amersfoort concentration camp">Kamp Amersfoort</a> near <a href="/wiki/Amersfoort" title="Amersfoort">Amersfoort</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Westerbork_transit_camp" title="Westerbork transit camp">Westerbork transit camp</a> (a "Jewish assembly camp"); there were a number of other camps variously controlled by the military, the police, the SS or Seyss-lnquart's administration. These included a "voluntary labour recruitment" camp at <a href="/wiki/Ommen" title="Ommen">Ommen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kamp_Erika" title="Kamp Erika">Camp Erika</a>). In total around 530,000 Dutch civilians forcibly worked for the Germans, of whom 250,000 were sent to factories in Germany. There was an unsuccessful attempt by Seyss-Inquart to send only workers aged 21 to 23 to Germany, and he refused demands in 1944 for a further 250,000 Dutch workers and in that year sent only 12,000 people.</p>

<p>Seyss-Inquart was an unwavering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Semite" title="Anti-Semite">anti-Semite</a>: within a few months of his arrival in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, he took measures to remove Jews from the government, the press and leading positions in industry. Anti-Jewish measures intensified after 1941: approximately 140,000 Jews were registered, a 'ghetto' was created in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> and a transit camp was set up at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westerbork_concentration_camp" title="Westerbork concentration camp">Westerbork</a>. Subsequently, in February 1941, 600 Jews were sent to <a href="/wiki/Buchenwald_concentration_camp" title="Buchenwald concentration camp">Buchenwald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mauthausen-Gusen_concentration_camp" title="Mauthausen-Gusen concentration camp">Mauthausen</a> concentration camps. Later, the Dutch Jews were sent to <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz</a>. As Allied forces approached in September 1944, the remaining Jews at Westerbork were removed to <a href="/wiki/Theresienstadt_concentration_camp" title="Theresienstadt concentration camp">Theresienstadt</a>. Of 140,000 registered, only 30,000 Dutch Jews survived the war.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> advanced into the Netherlands in late 1944, the Nazi regime had attempted to enact a <a href="/wiki/Scorched_earth" title="Scorched earth">scorched earth</a> policy, and some docks and harbours were destroyed. Seyss-Inquart, however, was in agreement with Armaments Minister <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a> over the futility of such actions, and with the open connivance of many military commanders, they greatly limited the implementation of the scorched earth orders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judseyss_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Judseyss-2">[2]</a></sup> At the very end of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_famine_of_1944" title="Dutch famine of 1944">hunger winter</a>" in April 1945, Seyss-Inquart was with difficulty persuaded by the Allies to allow airplanes to <a href="/wiki/Operations_Manna_and_Chowhound" title="Operations Manna and Chowhound">drop food for the hungry people of the occupied northwest of the country</a>. Although he knew the war was lost, Seyss-Inquart did not want to surrender. This led General <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bedell_Smith" title="Walter Bedell Smith">Walter Bedell Smith</a> to snap: "<i>Well, in any case, you are going to be shot</i>". "<i>That leaves me cold</i>", Seyss-Inquart replied, to which Smith then retorted: "<i>It will</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before Hitler committed suicide in April 1945, he named a new government headed by Grand Admiral <a href="/wiki/Karl_D%C3%B6nitz" title="Karl Dönitz">Karl Dönitz</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Last_will_and_testament_of_Adolf_Hitler" title="Last will and testament of Adolf Hitler">last will and testament</a>, in which Seyss-Inquart replaced <a href="/wiki/Joachim_von_Ribbentrop" title="Joachim von Ribbentrop">Joachim von Ribbentrop</a>, who had long since fallen out of favour, as Foreign Minister. It was a tribute to the high regard Hitler felt for his Austrian comrade, at a time when he was rapidly disowning or being abandoned by so many of the other key lieutenants of the Third Reich. Unsurprisingly, at such a late stage in the war, Seyss-Inquart failed to achieve anything in his new office.</p>
<p>He remained in his posts until 7 May 1945, when, after a meeting with Dönitz to confirm his blocking of the scorched earth orders, he was arrested on the Elbe Bridge at Hamburg by two members of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Welch_Fusiliers" title="Royal Welch Fusiliers">Royal Welch Fusiliers</a>, one of whom was Norman Miller (birth name: Norbert Mueller), a German Jew from Nuremberg who had escaped to Britain at the age of 15 on a <a href="/wiki/Kindertransport" title="Kindertransport">kindertransport</a> just before the war and then returned to Germany as part of the British occupation forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Miller's entire family had been killed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jungfernhof_Camp" title="Jungfernhof Camp">Jungfernhof Camp</a> in <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, Latvia in March 1942.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuremberg_trials">Nuremberg trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Seyss-Inquart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nuremberg trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg trials</a>, Seyss-Inquart was defended by Gustav Steinbauer and faced four charges: conspiracy to commit crimes against peace; planning, initiating and waging wars of aggression; war crimes; and crimes against humanity. During the trial, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Gilbert" title="Gustave Gilbert">Gustave Gilbert</a>, an American army psychologist, was allowed to examine the Nazi leaders who were tried at Nuremberg for war crimes. Among other tests, a German version of the <a href="/wiki/Wechsler_Adult_Intelligence_Scale" title="Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale">Wechsler-Bellevue</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IQ_test" title="IQ test">IQ test</a> was administered. Arthur Seyss-Inquart scored 141, the second highest among the defendants, behind <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Schacht" title="Hjalmar Schacht">Hjalmar Schacht</a>.</p>
<p>Seyss-Inquart was acquitted of conspiracy, but convicted on all other counts and sentenced to death by hanging. The final judgment against him cited his involvement in harsh suppression of Nazi opponents and atrocities against the Jews during all his billets, but particularly stressed his reign of terror in the Netherlands. It was these atrocities that sent him to the gallows.</p>
<p>Upon hearing of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_sentence" title="Death sentence">death sentence</a>, Seyss-Inquart was fatalistic: "Death by hanging... well, in view of the whole situation, I never expected anything different. It's all right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was hanged on 16 October 1946, at the age of 54, together with nine other Nuremberg defendants. He was the last to mount the scaffold, and his last words were the following: "I hope that this execution is the last act of the tragedy of the Second World War and that the lesson taken from this world war will be that peace and understanding should exist between peoples. I believe in Germany."</p>
<p>Before his execution, Seyss-Inquart returned to the Roman Catholic church,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> receiving absolution in the sacrament of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrament_of_Penance_(Catholic_Church)" title="Sacrament of Penance (Catholic Church)">confession</a> from prison chaplain Father Bruno Spitzl. His body, with those of the other nine executed men and that of Hermann Göring, was cremated at the <a href="/wiki/Ostfriedhof_(Munich)" title="Ostfriedhof (Munich)">Ostfriedhof</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, and their ashes were scattered into the river <a href="/wiki/Isar" title="Isar">Isar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManvell2011393_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManvell2011393-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOvery2001205_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOvery2001205-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
