<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Artin reciprocity law</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Artin reciprocity law</b>, which was established by <a href="/wiki/Emil_Artin" title="Emil Artin">Emil Artin</a> in a series of papers (1924; 1927; 1930), is a general theorem in <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> that forms a central part of global <a href="/wiki/Class_field_theory" title="Class field theory">class field theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reciprocity_law_(mathematics)" title="Reciprocity law (mathematics)">reciprocity law</a>" refers to a long line of more concrete number theoretic statements which it generalized, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadratic_reciprocity_law" title="Quadratic reciprocity law">quadratic reciprocity law</a> and the reciprocity laws of <a href="/wiki/Gotthold_Eisenstein" title="Gotthold Eisenstein">Eisenstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Kummer" title="Ernst Kummer">Kummer</a> to <a href="/wiki/David_Hilbert" title="David Hilbert">Hilbert's</a> product formula for the <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_symbol" title="Hilbert symbol">norm symbol</a>. Artin's result provided a partial solution to <a href="/wiki/Hilbert%27s_ninth_problem" title="Hilbert's ninth problem">Hilbert's ninth problem</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artin_reciprocity_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Artin's reciprocity law implies a description of the <a href="/wiki/Commutator_subgroup" title="Commutator subgroup">abelianization</a> of the absolute <a href="/wiki/Galois_group" title="Galois group">Galois group</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Global_field" title="Global field">global field</a> <i>K</i> which is based on the <a href="/wiki/Hasse_principle" title="Hasse principle">Hasse local–global principle</a> and the use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frobenius_element" title="Frobenius element">Frobenius elements</a>. Together with the <a href="/wiki/Takagi_existence_theorem" title="Takagi existence theorem">Takagi existence theorem</a>, it is used to describe the <a href="/wiki/Abelian_extension" title="Abelian extension">abelian extensions</a> of <i>K</i> in terms of the arithmetic of <i>K</i> and to understand the behavior of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Place_(mathematics)" title="Place (mathematics)">nonarchimedean places</a> in them. Therefore, the Artin reciprocity law can be interpreted as one of the main theorems of global class field theory. It can be used to prove that <a href="/wiki/Artin_L-function" title="Artin L-function">Artin L-functions</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meromorphic" title="Meromorphic">meromorphic</a> and for the proof of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chebotarev_density_theorem" title="Chebotarev density theorem">Chebotarev density theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two years after the publication of his general reciprocity law in 1927, Artin rediscovered the <a href="/wiki/Artin_transfer_(group_theory)" title="Artin transfer (group theory)">transfer homomorphism</a> of I. Schur and used the reciprocity law to translate the <a href="/wiki/Principalization_(algebra)" title="Principalization (algebra)">principalization problem</a> for ideal classes of algebraic number fields into the group theoretic task of determining the kernels of transfers of finite non-abelian groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statement_in_terms_of_L-functions">Statement in terms of <i>L</i>-functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artin_reciprocity_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Statement in terms of L-functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternative version of the reciprocity law, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Langlands_program" title="Langlands program">Langlands program</a>, connects <a href="/wiki/Artin_L-function" title="Artin L-function">Artin L-functions</a> associated to abelian extensions of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Number_field" title="Number field">number field</a> with Hecke L-functions associated to characters of the idèle class group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milne_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-milne-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hecke_character" title="Hecke character">Hecke character</a> (or Größencharakter) of a number field <i>K</i> is defined to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasicharacter" title="Quasicharacter">quasicharacter</a> of the idèle class group of <i>K</i>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langlands" title="Robert Langlands">Robert Langlands</a> interpreted Hecke characters as <a href="/wiki/Automorphic_form" title="Automorphic form">automorphic forms</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reductive_algebraic_group" title="Reductive algebraic group">reductive algebraic group</a> <i>GL</i>(1) over the <a href="/wiki/Adele_ring" title="Adele ring">ring of adeles</a> of <i>K</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gelbart_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gelbart-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Let <i>E</i>⁄<i>K</i> be an abelian Galois extension with <a href="/wiki/Galois_group" title="Galois group">Galois group</a> <i>G</i>. Then for any <a href="/wiki/Character_(mathematics)" title="Character (mathematics)">character</a> <i>σ</i>: <i>G</i>  → <b>C</b><sup>×</sup> (i.e. one-dimensional complex <a href="/wiki/Group_representation" title="Group representation">representation</a> of the group <i>G</i>), there exists a Hecke character <i>χ</i> of <i>K</i> such that</p>
