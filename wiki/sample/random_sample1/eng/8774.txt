<p>The <b>history of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a></b> is divided by historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> into four main parts: the pre-Columbian time or early history (up to the sixteenth century), the colonial period (1530–1810), the period of nation-building (1810-1880), and the history of modern Argentina (from around 1880).</p>
<p>Prehistory in the present territory of Argentina began with the first human settlements on the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> around 13,000 years ago. Written history began with the arrival of Spanish chroniclers in the expedition of <a href="/wiki/Juan_D%C3%ADaz_de_Sol%C3%ADs" title="Juan Díaz de Solís">Juan Díaz de Solís</a> in 1516 to the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">Río de la Plata</a>, which marks the beginning of Spanish domination in this region.</p>
<p>In 1776 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Crown" title="Spanish Crown">Spanish Crown</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata">Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata</a>, an umbrella of territories from which, with the <a href="/wiki/May_Revolution" title="May Revolution">Revolution of May 1810</a>, began a process of gradual formation of several independent states, including one called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Provinces_of_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="United Provinces of Río de la Plata">United Provinces of Río de la Plata</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Argentine Declaration of Independence">declaration of independence</a> on July 9, 1816 and the military defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> in 1824, a federal state was formed in 1853-1861, known today as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Argentina" title="Republic of Argentina">Republic of Argentina</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Columbian_era">Pre-Columbian era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-Columbian era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG"><img alt="small stone structure, with cacti and mountains in the background" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG/250px-Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG/375px-Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG/500px-Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara_01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The fortification of <i><a href="/wiki/Pucar%C3%A1_de_Tilcara" title="Pucará de Tilcara">Pucará de Tilcara</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Jujuy_Province" title="Jujuy Province">Jujuy Province</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The area now known as Argentina was relatively sparsely populated until the period of European colonization. The earliest traces of human life are dated from the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> period, and there are further signs in the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, large areas of the interior and Piedmont were apparently depopulated during an extensive dry period between 4000 and 2000 B.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Uruguayan archaeologist Raúl Campá Soler divided the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Argentina" title="Indigenous peoples in Argentina">indigenous peoples in Argentina</a> into three main groups: basic hunters and food gatherers, without development of <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>; advanced gatherers and hunters; and basic farmers with pottery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The second group could be found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pampa" title="Pampa">Pampa</a> and south of <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, and the third one included the <a href="/wiki/Charr%C3%BAa" title="Charrúa">Charrúas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minuane_people" title="Minuane people">Minuane people</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guarani_people" title="Guarani people">Guaraníes</a>.</p>
<p>The major ethnic groups included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selknam_people" title="Selknam people">Onas</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaghan" title="Yaghan">Yámana</a> at the archipelago between the <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel" title="Beagle Channel">Beagle Channel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tehuelches" title="Tehuelches">Tehuelches</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, many peoples at the litoral, guaycurúes and wichis at Chaco. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guarani_people" title="Guarani people">Guaraníes</a> had expanded across large areas of South America, but settled in the northeastern provinces of Argentina. The Toba (Komlek) nation and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diaguita_nation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diaguita nation (page does not exist)">Diaguita</a> which included the Calchaqui and the Quilmes lived in the North and the Comechingones in what is today the province of Cordoba. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charrua_people" title="Charrua people">Charrua</a> (which included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minuane_people" title="Minuane people">Minuane people</a>), <a href="/wiki/Yaro" title="Yaro">yaros</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bohan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bohan (page does not exist)">Bohanes</a> and Chanás (and Chaná-Timbú) were located in the actual territory of Entre Ríos and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Querand%C3%AD_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Querandí people (page does not exist)">Querandí</a> in Buenos Aires.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_colonial_era">Spanish colonial era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spanish colonial era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Viceroyalty of Peru</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Government of the Río de la Plata">Government of the Río de la Plata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata">Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata</a>, and <a href="/wiki/May_Revolution" title="May Revolution">May Revolution</a></div>
<p><b>Europeans</b> first arrived in the region with the 1502 Portuguese voyage of <a href="/wiki/Gon%C3%A7alo_Coelho" title="Gonçalo Coelho">Gonçalo Coelho</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci_(explorer)" title="Amerigo Vespucci (explorer)">Amerigo Vespucci</a>. Around 1512, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Lisboa" title="João de Lisboa">João de Lisboa</a> and Estevão de Fróis discovered the Rio de La Plata in present-day Argentina, exploring its estuary, contacting the Charrúa people, and bringing the first news of the "people of the mountains", the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inca_empire" title="Inca empire">Inca empire</a>, obtained from the local natives. They also traveled as far south as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_San_Matias" title="Gulf of San Matias">Gulf of San Matias</a> at 42ºS, on the northern shores of Patagonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spate_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spate-6">[6]</a></sup> The Spanish, led by <a href="/wiki/Juan_D%C3%ADaz_de_Sol%C3%ADs" title="Juan Díaz de Solís">Juan Díaz de Solís</a>, visited the territory which is now <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> in 1516. In 1536 <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Mendoza" title="Pedro de Mendoza">Pedro de Mendoza</a> established a small settlement at the modern location of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, which was abandoned in 1541.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitre.2C_p._8-9_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitre.2C_p._8-9-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second one was established in 1580 by <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Garay" title="Juan de Garay">Juan de Garay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina" title="Córdoba, Argentina">Córdoba</a> in 1573 by <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Luis_de_Cabrera" title="Jerónimo Luis de Cabrera">Jerónimo Luis de Cabrera</a>. Those regions were part of the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Viceroyalty of Peru</a>, whose capital was Lima, and settlers arrived from that city. Unlike the other regions of South America, the colonization of the Río de la Plata estuary was not influenced by any <a href="/wiki/Gold_rush" title="Gold rush">gold rush</a>, since it lacked any <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a> to mine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitre.2C_p._8-9_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitre.2C_p._8-9-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The natural ports on the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">Río de la Plata</a> <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> could not be used because all shipments were meant to be made through the port of <a href="/wiki/Callao" title="Callao">Callao</a> near <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a>, a condition that led to <a href="/wiki/Contraband" title="Contraband">contraband</a> becoming the normal means of commerce in cities such as <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">Asunción</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Spanish raised the status of this region by establishing the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata">Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata</a> in 1776. This viceroyalty consisted of today's Argentina, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, as well as much of present-day <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. Buenos Aires, now holding the customs of the new political subdivision, became a flourishing port, as the revenues from the <a href="/wiki/Potos%C3%AD" title="Potosí">Potosí</a>, the increasing maritime activity in terms of goods rather than <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>, the production of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">export</a> of <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> and other products, and other political reasons, made it gradually become one of the most important commercial centers of the region.</p>
<p>The viceroyalty was, however, short-lived due to lack of internal cohesion among its many regions and lack of Spanish support. Ships from Spain became scarce again after the Spanish defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">battle of Trafalgar</a>, that gave the British maritime supremacy. The British tried to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_invasions_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="British invasions of the Río de la Plata">invade</a> Buenos Aires and Montevideo in 1806 and 1807, but were defeated both times by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Liniers" title="Santiago de Liniers">Santiago de Liniers</a>. Those victories, achieved without help from mainland Spain, boosted the confidence of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a> in Spain and the capture of the Spanish king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII" title="Ferdinand VII">Ferdinand VII</a> created great concern all around the viceroyalty. It was thought that, without a King, people in America should rule themselves. This idea led to multiple attempts to remove the local authorities at <a href="/wiki/Chuquisaca_Revolution" title="Chuquisaca Revolution">Chuquisaca</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Paz_revolution" title="La Paz revolution">La Paz</a>, Montevideo and <a href="/wiki/Mutiny_of_%C3%81lzaga" title="Mutiny of Álzaga">Buenos Aires</a>, all of which were short-lived. A new successful attempt, the <a href="/wiki/May_Revolution" title="May Revolution">May Revolution</a> of 1810, took place when it was reported that all of Spain, with the exception of Cádiz and León, had been conquered.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_of_independence">War of independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: War of independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Argentine_War_of_Independence" title="Argentine War of Independence">Argentine War of Independence</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Primera_Junta" title="Primera Junta">Primera Junta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Junta_Grande" title="Junta Grande">Junta Grande</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Triumvirate_(Argentina)" title="First Triumvirate (Argentina)">First Triumvirate (Argentina)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Second_Triumvirate_(Argentina)" title="Second Triumvirate (Argentina)">Second Triumvirate (Argentina)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_(retrato,_c.1828).jpg"><img alt="oil paining of Jose' e San Martin" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1573" data-file-width="1199" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_%28retrato%2C_c.1828%29.jpg/220px-Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_%28retrato%2C_c.1828%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_%28retrato%2C_c.1828%29.jpg/330px-Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_%28retrato%2C_c.1828%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_%28retrato%2C_c.1828%29.jpg/440px-Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_%28retrato%2C_c.1828%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn_(retrato,_c.1828).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Portrait of <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn" title="José de San Martín">José de San Martín</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The May Revolution ousted the viceroy. Other forms of government, such as a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">Regency</a> were briefly considered. The viceroyalty was also renamed, and it nominally became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Provinces_of_South_America" title="United Provinces of South America">United Provinces of the Río de la Plata</a>. However, the status of the different territories that had belonged to the viceroyalty changed many times during the course of the war, as some regions would remain loyal to their previous governors and others were captured or recaptured; later these would <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkanize" title="Balkanize">split</a> into several countries.</p>
<p>The first military campaigns against the royalists were waged by <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Belgrano" title="Manuel Belgrano">Manuel Belgrano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Castelli" title="Juan José Castelli">Juan José Castelli</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Primera_Junta" title="Primera Junta">Primera Junta</a>, after expanding to become the <a href="/wiki/Junta_Grande" title="Junta Grande">Junta Grande</a>, was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/First_Triumvirate_(Argentina)" title="First Triumvirate (Argentina)">First Triumvirate</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Second_Triumvirate_(Argentina)" title="Second Triumvirate (Argentina)">Second Triumvirate</a> would replace it years later, calling for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asamblea_del_A%C3%B1o_XIII" title="Asamblea del Año XIII">Assembly of year XIII</a> that was meant to declare independence and write a <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a>. However, it did not do either, and replaced the triumvirates with a single head of state office, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Director_of_the_United_Provinces_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Supreme Director of the United Provinces of the Río de la Plata">Supreme Director</a>.</p>
<p>By this time <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn" title="José de San Martín">José de San Martín</a> arrived in Buenos Aires with other generals of the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>. They gave new strength to the Revolutionary war, which was marked by the defeat of Belgrano and Castelli and the royalist resistance at the Banda Oriental. Alvear took Montevideo, and San Martín started a military campaign that would span an important part of the Spanish territories in America. He created the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Andes" title="Army of the Andes">Army of the Andes</a> in Mendoza and, with the help of <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_O%27Higgins" title="Bernardo O'Higgins">Bernardo O'Higgins</a> and other Chileans, he made the <a href="/wiki/Crossing_of_the_Andes" title="Crossing of the Andes">Crossing of the Andes</a> and liberated Chile. With the Chilean navy at his disposal, he moved to Peru, liberating that country as well. San Martín met <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar" title="Simón Bolívar">Simón Bolívar</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guayaquil_conference" title="Guayaquil conference">Guayaquil</a>, and retired from action.</p>
<p>A new assembly, the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="Congress of Tucumán">Congress of Tucumán</a>, was called while San Martín was preparing the crossing of the Andes. It finally <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Argentine Declaration of Independence">declared independence</a> from Spain or any other foreign power. <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> declared itself independent in 1825, and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> was created in 1828 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Cisplatine_War" title="Cisplatine War">Cisplatine War</a>.</p>
<p>The United Kingdom officially recognized Argentine independence in 1825, with the signing of a <i>Treaty of Friendship, Commerce, and Navigation</i> on February 2; the British <a href="/wiki/Charg%C3%A9_d%27affaires" title="Chargé d'affaires">chargé d'affaires</a> in Buenos Aires, <a href="/wiki/Woodbine_Parish" title="Woodbine Parish">Woodbine Parish</a>, signed on behalf of his country. Spanish recognition of Argentine independence was not to come for several decades.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentine_Civil_Wars">Argentine Civil Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Argentine Civil Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine_Civil_War" title="Argentine Civil War">Argentine Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Confederation" title="Argentine Confederation">Argentine Confederation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

Governor <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_de_Rosas" title="Juan Manuel de Rosas">Juan Manuel de Rosas</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cayetano_Descalzi" title="Cayetano Descalzi">Cayetano Descalzi</a> around 1841</div>

<p>The defeat of the Spanish was followed by a long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine_Civil_War" title="Argentine Civil War">civil war</a> between <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Party" title="Unitarian Party">unitarians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Federales_(Argentina)" title="Federales (Argentina)">federalists</a>, about the organization of the country and the role of Buenos Aires in it. Unitarians thought that Buenos Aires should lead the less-developed provinces, as the head of a strong <a href="/wiki/Centralized_government" title="Centralized government">centralized government</a>. Federalists thought instead that the country should be a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federation</a> of autonomous provinces, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_of_the_United_States" title="States of the United States">states of the United States</a>.</p>
<p>During this period the United Provinces of the Rio de la Plata lacked a head of state, since the unitarian defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cepeda_(1820)" title="Battle of Cepeda (1820)">Battle of Cepeda</a> had ended the authority of the Supreme Directors and the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Constitution_of_1819" title="Argentine Constitution of 1819">1819 Constitution</a>. There was a new attempt in 1826 to write a constitution, leading to the designation of <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_Rivadavia" title="Bernardino Rivadavia">Bernardino Rivadavia</a> as <a href="/wiki/President_of_Argentina" title="President of Argentina">President of Argentina</a>, but it was rejected by the provinces. Rivadavia resigned due to the poor management at the <a href="/wiki/Cisplatine_War" title="Cisplatine War">Cisplatine War</a>, and the 1826 constitution was repealed.</p>
<p>During this time, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Buenos_Aires_Province" title="Governor of Buenos Aires Province">Governors of Buenos Aires Province</a> received the power to manage the international relations of the confederation, including war and debt payment. The dominant figure of this period was the federalist <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_de_Rosas" title="Juan Manuel de Rosas">Juan Manuel de Rosas</a>, who is portrayed from different angles by the diverse historiographic flows in Argentina: liberal history usually considers him a dictator, while revisionists support him on the grounds of his defense of national sovereignty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>He ruled the province of Buenos Aires from 1829 to 1852, facing military threats from secession attempts, neighboring countries, and even European nations. Although Rosas was a Federalist, he kept the customs receipts of Buenos Aires under the exclusive control of the city, whereas the other provinces expected to have a part of the revenue. Rosas considered this a fair measure because only Buenos Aires was paying the <a href="/wiki/External_debt" title="External debt">external debt</a> generated by the <a href="/wiki/Barings_Bank" title="Barings Bank">Baring Brothers</a> loan to Rivadavia, the war of independence and the war against Brazil. He developed a paramilitary force of his own, the <a href="/wiki/Sociedad_Popular_Restauradora" title="Sociedad Popular Restauradora">Popular Restorer Society</a>, commonly known as "<i>Mazorca</i>" ("Corncob").</p>
<p>Rosas' reluctance to call for a new assembly to write a constitution led General <a href="/wiki/Justo_Jos%C3%A9_de_Urquiza" title="Justo José de Urquiza">Justo José de Urquiza</a> from <a href="/wiki/Entre_R%C3%ADos_Province" title="Entre Ríos Province">Entre Ríos</a> to turn against him. Urquiza defeated Rosas during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Caseros" title="Battle of Caseros">battle of Caseros</a> and called for such an assembly. The <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Constitution_of_1853" title="Argentine Constitution of 1853">Argentine Constitution of 1853</a> is, with amendments, still in force to this day. The Constitution was not immediately accepted by Buenos Aires, which seceded from the Confederation; it rejoined a few years later. In 1862 <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Mitre" title="Bartolomé Mitre">Bartolomé Mitre</a> became the first president of the unified country.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liberal_Governments_.281862.E2.80.931880.29">Liberal Governments (1862–1880)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liberal Governments (1862–1880)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Constitution_of_1853" title="Argentine Constitution of 1853">Argentine Constitution of 1853</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_the_Desert" title="Conquest of the Desert">Conquest of the Desert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Generation_of_%2780" title="Generation of '80">Generation of '80</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_American_dreadnought_race" title="South American dreadnought race">South American dreadnought race</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The presidency of <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Mitre" title="Bartolomé Mitre">Bartolomé Mitre</a> saw an economic improvement in Argentina, with agricultural modernization, foreign investment, new railroads and ports and a wave of immigration from Europe. Mitre also stabilized the political system by commanding federal interventions that defeated the personal armies of caudillos <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chacho_Pe%C3%B1aloza" title="Chacho Peñaloza">Chacho Peñaloza</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_S%C3%A1a&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Sáa (page does not exist)">Juan Sáa</a>. Argentina joined Uruguay and Brazil against Paraguay in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Triple_Alliance" title="War of the Triple Alliance">War of the Triple Alliance</a>, which ended during Sarmiento's rule with the defeat of Paraguay and the annexation of part of its territory by Argentina.</p>
<p>Despite victory in the war, Mitre's popularity declined severely because a broad section of the Argentine population was opposed to the war due to the alliance with Brazil (Argentina's historic rival) that took place during the war, and the betrayal of Paraguay (which had been until then one of the country's most important economic allies). One of the major hallmarks of Mitre's presidency was the "Law of Compromise", in which Buenos Aires joined the Argentine Republic and allowed the government to use the City of Buenos Aires as the center of government, but without federalizing the city and by reserving the right of the province of Buenos Aires to secede from the nation if conflict arose.</p>
<p>In 1868 Mitre was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Domingo_Faustino_Sarmiento" title="Domingo Faustino Sarmiento">Domingo Faustino Sarmiento</a>, who promoted public education, culture and <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraphs</a>; as well as the modernization of the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Army" title="Argentine Army">Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Navy" title="Argentine Navy">Navy</a>. Sarmiento managed to defeat the last known caudillos and also dealt with the fallout of the Triple Alliance War, which included a decrease in national production due to the death of thousands of soldiers and an outbreak of diseases, such as <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a>, brought by returning soldiers.</p>
<p>In 1874 <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Avellaneda" title="Nicolás Avellaneda">Nicolás Avellaneda</a> became president and ran into trouble when he had to deal with the economic depression left by the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1873" title="Panic of 1873">Panic of 1873</a>. Most of these economic issues were solved when new land was opened for development after the expansion of national territory through the <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_the_Desert" title="Conquest of the Desert">Conquest of the Desert</a>, led by his war minister <a href="/wiki/Julio_Argentino_Roca" title="Julio Argentino Roca">Julio Argentino Roca</a>. This military campaign took most of the territories under the control of natives, and reduced their population.</p>
<p>In 1880 a trade conflict caused turmoil in Buenos Aires, which led governor <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Tejedor_(politician)" title="Carlos Tejedor (politician)">Carlos Tejedor</a> to declare secession from the republic. Avellaneda denied them this right, breaking the Law of Compromise, and proceeded to send army troops led by Roca to take over the province. Tejedor's secession efforts were defeated and Buenos Aires joined the republic definitively, federalizing the city of Buenos Aires and handing it over to the government as the nation's capital city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Autonomist_hegemony_.281880.E2.80.931916.29">National Autonomist hegemony (1880–1916)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: National Autonomist hegemony (1880–1916)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Argentine%E2%80%93Chilean_naval_arms_race" title="Argentine–Chilean naval arms race">Argentine–Chilean naval arms race</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_American_dreadnought_race" title="South American dreadnought race">South American dreadnought race</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Julio_A_Roca.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1058" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Julio_A_Roca.jpg/200px-Julio_A_Roca.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Julio_A_Roca.jpg/300px-Julio_A_Roca.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Julio_A_Roca.jpg/400px-Julio_A_Roca.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Julio_A_Roca.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
President <a href="/wiki/Julio_Argentino_Roca" title="Julio Argentino Roca">Julio Argentino Roca</a>, the central political figure of the PAN Hegemony years.</div>

<p>After his surge in popularity due to his successful desert campaign, Julio Roca was elected president in 1880 as the candidate for the <a href="/wiki/National_Autonomist_Party" title="National Autonomist Party">National Autonomist Party</a> (Partido Autonomista Nacional - PAN), a party that would remain in power until 1916. During his presidency, Roca created a net of political alliances and installed several measures that helped him retain almost absolute control of the Argentine political scene throughout the 1880s. This keen ability with political strategy earned him his nickname of "<i>The Fox</i>".</p>
<p>The country's economy benefited from a change from <a href="/wiki/Extensive_farming" title="Extensive farming">extensive farming</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" title="Industrial agriculture">industrial agriculture</a> and a huge <a href="/wiki/Great_European_immigration_wave_to_Argentina" title="Great European immigration wave to Argentina">European immigration</a>, but there wasn't yet a strong move towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>. At that time, Argentina received some of the highest levels of foreign investment in Latin America.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the midst of this economic expansion, the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Law_1420" title="Argentine Law 1420">Law 1420 of Common Education</a> of 1884 guaranteed universal, free, non-religious education to all children. This and other government policies were strongly opposed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Argentina" title="Roman Catholicism in Argentina">Roman Catholic Church in Argentina</a>, causing the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> to break off diplomatic relations with the country for several years and setting the stage for decades of continued <a href="/wiki/Church%E2%80%93state_relations_in_Argentina" title="Church–state relations in Argentina">Church–state strain</a>.</p>
<p>In the late 19th and early 20th century, Argentina temporarily resolved its border disputes with Chile with the <a href="/wiki/Puna_de_Atacama_dispute" title="Puna de Atacama dispute">Puna de Atacama dispute</a> of 1899, the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Treaty_of_1881_between_Chile_and_Argentina" title="Boundary Treaty of 1881 between Chile and Argentina">Boundary Treaty of 1881 between Chile and Argentina</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=1902_General_Treaty_of_Arbitration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="1902 General Treaty of Arbitration (page does not exist)">1902 General Treaty of Arbitration</a>. Roca's government and those that followed were aligned with the Argentine <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a>, especially the great land owners.</p>
<p>In 1888, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miguel_Ju%C3%A1rez_Celman" title="Miguel Juárez Celman">Miguel Juárez Celman</a> became president after Roca was constitutionally disqualified from re-election; Celman attempted to reduce Roca's control over the political scene, which earned him his predecessor's opposition. Roca led a great opposition movement against Celman, which coupled with the devastating effects that the <a href="/wiki/Long_Depression" title="Long Depression">Long Depression</a> had on the Argentine economy, allowed the <a href="/wiki/Civic_Union_(Argentina)" title="Civic Union (Argentina)">Civic opposition party</a> to start a coup d'état which would be later known as the <a href="/wiki/Revolution_of_the_Park" title="Revolution of the Park">Revolution of the Park</a>. The Revolution was led by the three main leaders of the Civic Union, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leandro_Alem" title="Leandro Alem">Leandro Alem</a>, former president <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Mitre" title="Bartolomé Mitre">Bartolomé Mitre</a> and moderate socialist <a href="/wiki/Juan_B._Justo" title="Juan B. Justo">Juan B. Justo</a>. Though it failed in its main goals, the revolution forced Juárez Celman's resignation and marked the decline of the <a href="/wiki/Generation_of_%2780" title="Generation of '80">Generation of '80</a>.</p>
<p>In 1891, Roca proposed that the Civic Union elect someone to be vice-president to his own presidency the next time elections came around. One group led by Mitre decided to take the deal, while another more intransigent group led by Alem was opposed. This eventually led to the split of the Civic Union into the <a href="/wiki/National_Civic_Union" title="National Civic Union">National Civic Union</a>, led by Mitre, and the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Civic_Union" title="Radical Civic Union">Radical Civic Union</a>, led by Alem. After this division occurred, Roca withdrew his offer, having completed his plan to divide the Civic Union and decrease their power. Alem would eventually commit suicide in 1896; control of the Radical Civic Union went to his nephew and protégé, <a href="/wiki/Hip%C3%B3lito_Yrigoyen" title="Hipólito Yrigoyen">Hipólito Yrigoyen</a>.</p>
<p>After Celman's downfall, his vice-president <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Pellegrini" title="Carlos Pellegrini">Carlos Pellegrini</a> took over and proceeded to resolve the economic crisis which afflicted the country, earning him the moniker of "<i>The Storm Sailor</i>". Fearing another wave of opposition from Roca like the one imposed on Celman, Pellegrini remained moderate in his presidency ending his predecessor's efforts to distance "<i>The Fox</i>" from political control. The following governments up until 1898 took similar measures and sided with Roca to avoid being politically chastised.</p>
<p>In 1898, Roca became president again in a politically unstable situation, with a large number of social conflicts that included massive strikes and anarchist subversion attempts. Roca handled most of these conflicts by having the police or the army crack down on protestors, rebels and suspected rebels. After the end of his second presidency, Roca fell ill and his role in political affairs began to decrease gradually until his death in late 1914.</p>
<p>In 1904, <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Palacios" title="Alfredo Palacios">Alfredo Palacios</a>, a member of <a href="/wiki/Juan_B._Justo" title="Juan B. Justo">Juan B. Justo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Argentina)" title="Socialist Party (Argentina)">Socialist Party</a> (founded in 1896), became the first Socialist deputy in Argentina, as a representative for the working-class neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/La_Boca" title="La Boca">La Boca</a> in Buenos Aires. He helped create many laws, including the Ley Palacios against sexual exploitation, and others regulating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_laws" title="Labor laws">child and woman labor</a>, working hours and Sunday rest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The hegemony of the PAN ended in 1910 with the election of <a href="/wiki/Roque_S%C3%A1enz_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Roque Sáenz Peña">Roque Sáenz Peña</a> to the presidency. Peña was a progressive member of the PAN who disliked the fraudulent elective system the PAN employed and thus passed the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A1enz_Pe%C3%B1a_Law" title="Sáenz Peña Law">Sáenz Peña Law</a>, which made the political vote mandatory, secret and universal among males aged eighteen or more. Under this law the first non-PAN president since 1880 was elected in 1916, Hipólito Yrigoyen of the Radical Civic Union.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radical_governments_.281916.E2.80.9330.29">Radical governments (1916–30)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Radical governments (1916–30)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Conservative forces dominated Argentine politics until 1916, when the Radicals, led by <a href="/wiki/Hip%C3%B3lito_Yrigoyen" title="Hipólito Yrigoyen">Hipólito Yrigoyen</a>, won control of the government through the first national elections under universal male suffrage. 745,000 citizens were allowed to vote, of a total population of 7.5 million (immigrants, who represented much of the population, were not allowed to vote); of these, 400,000 abstained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yrigoyen, however, only obtained 45% of the votes, which did not allow him a majority in Parliament, where the conservatives remained the leading force. Thus, of 80 draft laws proposed by the executive, only 26 were voted through by the conservative majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Felipe_Pigna.2C_2006.2C_p.42_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Felipe_Pigna.2C_2006.2C_p.42-12">[12]</a></sup> A moderate agricultural reform proposal was rejected by Parliament, as was an income tax on interest, and the creation of a Bank of the Republic (which was to have the missions of the current <a href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Argentina" title="Central Bank of Argentina">Central Bank</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Felipe_Pigna.2C_2006.2C_p.42_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Felipe_Pigna.2C_2006.2C_p.42-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite this conservative opposition, the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Civic_Union" title="Radical Civic Union">Radical Civic Union</a> (UCR), with their emphasis on fair elections and democratic institutions, opened their doors to Argentina's expanding middle class as well as to social groups previously excluded from power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Yrigoyen's policy was to "fix" the system, by enacting necessary reforms which would enable the agroindustrial export model to preserve itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It alternated moderate social reforms with repression of the social movements. In 1918, a student movement started at the <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="National University of Córdoba">University of Córdoba</a>, which eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_university_reform_of_1918" title="Argentine university reform of 1918">University Reform</a>, which quickly spread to the rest of Latin America. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_%2768" title="May '68">May '68</a>, French students recalled the Córdoba movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tragic_Week_(Argentina)" title="Tragic Week (Argentina)">Tragic Week</a> of January 1919, during which the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Regional_Workers%27_Federation" title="Argentine Regional Workers' Federation">Argentine Regional Workers' Federation</a> (FORA, founded in 1901) had called for a general strike after a police shooting, ended with 700 killed and 4,000 injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> General <a href="/wiki/Luis_Dellepiane" title="Luis Dellepiane">Luis Dellepiane</a> marched on Buenos Aires to re-establish civil order. Despite being called on by some to initiate a coup against Yrigoyen, he remained loyal to the President, on the sole condition that the latter would allow him a free hand in the repression of the demonstrations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Social movements thereafter continued in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Forestal" title="La Forestal">Forestal</a></i> British company, and in <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, where Hector Varela headed the military repression, assisted by the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Patriotic_League" title="Argentine Patriotic League">Argentine Patriotic League</a>, killing 1,500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, Yrigoyen's administration enacted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_laws" title="Labor laws">Labor Code</a> establishing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_strike" title="Right to strike">right to strike</a> in 1921, implemented <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a> laws and collective contracts. It also initiated the creation of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direcci%C3%B3n_General_de_Yacimientos_Petrol%C3%ADferos_Fiscales" title="Dirección General de Yacimientos Petrolíferos Fiscales">Dirección General de Yacimientos Petrolíferos Fiscales</a></i> (YPF), the state oil company, in June 1922. Radicalism rejected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a> and advocated social conciliation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, the Radicals continued Argentina's neutrality policy during World War I, despite the United States urging them to declare war against the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a>. Neutrality enabled Argentina to export goods to Europe, in particular to Great Britain, as well as to issue credit to the belligerent powers. Germany sank two Argentine civilian ships, <i>Monte Protegido</i> on April 4, 1917 and the <i>Toro</i>, but the diplomatic incident ended only with the expulsion of the German ambassador, <a href="/wiki/Karl_von_Luxburg" title="Karl von Luxburg">Karl von Luxburg</a>. Yrigoyen organized a Conference of Neutral Powers in Buenos Aires, to oppose the United States' attempt to bring American states in the European war, and also supported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augusto_Sandino" title="Augusto Sandino">Sandino</a>'s resistance in <a href="/wiki/United_States_occupation_of_Nicaragua" title="United States occupation of Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 1922, Yrigoyen's administration refused to follow the <i><a href="/wiki/Cordon_sanitaire" title="Cordon sanitaire">cordon sanitaire</a></i> policy enacted against the Soviet Union, and, basing its policy on the assistance given to Austria after the war, decided to send to the USSR 5 million pesos in assistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The same year, Yrigoyen was replaced by his rival inside the UCR, <a href="/wiki/Marcelo_Torcuato_de_Alvear" title="Marcelo Torcuato de Alvear">Marcelo Torcuato de Alvear</a>, an aristocrat, who defeated <a href="/wiki/Norberto_Pi%C3%B1ero" title="Norberto Piñero">Norberto Piñero</a>'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Concentraci%C3%B3n_Nacional&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Concentración Nacional (page does not exist)">Concentración Nacional</a></i> (conservatives) with 458,457 votes to 200,080. Alvear brought to his cabinet personalities belonging to the traditional ruling classes, such as <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Nicol%C3%A1s_Matienzo" title="José Nicolás Matienzo">José Nicolás Matienzo</a> at the Interior Ministry, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81ngel_Gallardo_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ángel Gallardo (politician) (page does not exist)">Ángel Gallardo</a> in Foreign Relations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_P._Justo" title="Agustín P. Justo">Agustín P. Justo</a> at the War Ministry, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_Domecq_Garc%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manuel Domecq García (page does not exist)">Manuel Domecq García</a> at the Marine and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Herrera_Vegas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rafael Herrera Vegas (page does not exist)">Rafael Herrera Vegas</a> at the Haciendas. Alvear's supporters founded the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uni%C3%B3n_C%C3%ADvica_Radical_Antipersonalista&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Unión Cívica Radical Antipersonalista (page does not exist)">Unión Cívica Radical Antipersonalista</a></i>, opposed to Yrigoyen's party.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the early 1920s, the rise of the anarchist movement, fueled by the arrival of recent émigrés and deportees from Europe, spawned a new generation of left-wing activism in Argentina. The new left, mostly anarchists and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarcho-communists" title="Anarcho-communists">anarcho-communists</a>, rejected the incremental progressivism of the old Radical and Socialist elements in Argentina in favor of immediate action. The extremists, such as <a href="/wiki/Severino_Di_Giovanni" title="Severino Di Giovanni">Severino Di Giovanni</a>, openly espoused violence and '<a href="/wiki/Propaganda_of_the_deed" title="Propaganda of the deed">propaganda by the deed</a>'. A wave of bombings and shootouts with police culminated in an attempt to assassinate U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> on his visit to Argentina in 1928 and a nearly successful attempt to assassinate Yrigoyen in 1929 after he was re-elected to the presidency.</p>
<p>In 1921, the <a href="/wiki/Counter-revolutionary" title="Counter-revolutionary">counter-revolutionary</a> <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Logia_General_San_Mart%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Logia General San Martín (page does not exist)">Logia General San Martín</a></i> was founded, and diffused nationalist ideas in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_Argentina" title="Armed Forces of Argentina">military</a> until its dissolution in 1926. Three years later, the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Liga_Republicana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Liga Republicana (page does not exist)">Liga Republicana</a></i> (Republican League) was founded by <a href="/wiki/Roberto_de_Laferr%C3%A8re" title="Roberto de Laferrère">Roberto de Laferrère</a>, on the model of <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Blackshirts" title="Blackshirts">Blackshirts</a> in Italy. The Argentine Right found its major influences in the 19th-century Spanish writer <a href="/wiki/Marcelino_Men%C3%A9ndez_y_Pelayo" title="Marcelino Menéndez y Pelayo">Marcelino Menéndez y Pelayo</a> and in the French royalist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurras" title="Charles Maurras">Charles Maurras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Also in 1922, the poet <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Lugones" title="Leopoldo Lugones">Leopoldo Lugones</a>, who had turned towards <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a>, made a famous speech in <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a>, known as "the time of the sword", in the presence of the War Minister and future dictator Agustín P. Justo, which called for a military coup and the establishment of a military dictatorship.</p>
<p>In 1928, Yrigoyen was re-elected as president and began a series of reforms to increase workers' rights. This intensified the conservative opposition against Yrigoyen, which grew even stronger after Argentina was devastated by the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash" title="Wall Street Crash">Wall Street Crash</a>. On September 6, 1930, a military coup led by the pro-fascist general <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_F%C3%A9lix_Uriburu" title="José Félix Uriburu">José Félix Uriburu</a> overthrew Yrigoyen's government and began a period in Argentine history known as the <a href="/wiki/Infamous_Decade" title="Infamous Decade">Infamous Decade</a>.</p>
<p>During the Great Depression, exports of frozen beef, especially to Great Britain, provided much needed foreign currency, but trade fell off sharply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infamous_Decade_.281930.E2.80.9343.29">Infamous Decade (1930–43)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Infamous Decade (1930–43)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infamous_Decade" title="Infamous Decade">Infamous Decade</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg/300px-Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg/450px-Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg/600px-Vista_de_Puerto_Madero_-_Edificio_Libertador.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>In 1929, Argentina was wealthy by world standards, but the prosperity ended after 1929 with the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_Latin_America" title="Great Depression in Latin America">Great Depression</a>. In 1930, a military coup, supported by the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Patriotic_League" title="Argentine Patriotic League">Argentine Patriotic League</a>, forced Hipólito Yrigoyen from power, and replaced him with <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_F%C3%A9lix_Uriburu" title="José Félix Uriburu">José Félix Uriburu</a>. Support for the coup was bolstered by the sagging Argentine economy, as well as a string of bomb attacks and shootings involving radical anarchists, which alienated moderate elements of Argentine society and angered the conservative right, which had long been agitating for decisive action by the military forces.</p>
<p>The military coup initiated the period known as the "<a href="/wiki/Infamous_Decade" title="Infamous Decade">Infamous Decade</a>", characterized by <a href="/wiki/Electoral_fraud" title="Electoral fraud">electoral fraud</a>, persecution of the <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(politics)" title="Opposition (politics)">political opposition</a> (mainly against the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Civic_Union" title="Radical Civic Union">UCR</a>) and pervasive government corruption, against the background of the global depression.</p>
<p>During his brief tenure as president, Uriburu cracked down heavily on anarchists and other far-left groups, resulting in 2,000 illegal executions of members of anarchist and communist groups. The most famous (and perhaps most symbolic of anarchism's decay in Argentina at the time) was the execution of Severino Di Giovanni, who was captured in late January 1931 and executed on the first of February of the same year.</p>
<p>After becoming president through the coup, Uriburu attempted to create a constitutional reform that would include <a href="/wiki/Corporatism" title="Corporatism">corporatism</a> in the Argentine Constitution. This move toward fascism was viewed negatively by the conservative backers of the coup and they turned their support to the more moderate conservative general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_P._Justo" title="Agustín P. Justo">Agustín P. Justo</a>, who won the presidency in a 1932 election that was heavily fraudulent.</p>
<p>Justo began a policy of liberal economic moves that primarily benefitted the nation's upper classes and permitted great political and industrial corruption at the expense of national growth. One of the most infamous decisions of Justo's government was the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Roca%E2%80%93Runciman_Treaty" title="Roca–Runciman Treaty">Roca–Runciman Treaty</a> between Argentina and the United Kingdom, which benefitted the British economy and the rich beef producers of Argentina at the expense of national interest.</p>
<p>In 1935, progressive democrat Senator <a href="/wiki/Lisandro_de_la_Torre" title="Lisandro de la Torre">Lisandro de la Torre</a> began an investigation into several corruption allegations within the Argentine beef production industry, during which he attempted to charge Justo's Minister of Agriculture, Luis Duhau, and the Minister of Finance, Federico Pinedo, with political corruption and fraud charges. During the exposition of the investigation in the National Congress, Duhau started a fight among the senators, during which his bodyguard, Ramón Valdez-Cora, tried to kill De La Torre but accidentally ended up shooting De La Torre's friend and political partner <a href="/wiki/Enzo_Bordabehere" title="Enzo Bordabehere">Enzo Bordabehere</a>. The meat investigation was dropped soon afterwards, but not before De La Torre managed to achieve the incarceration of the head of the <i>Anglo</i> meat company for corruption charges. De la Torre would later commit suicide in 1939.</p>
<p>The collapse of international trade led to industrial growth focused on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Import_substitution" title="Import substitution">import substitution</a>, leading to a stronger economic independence. Political conflict increased, marked by confrontation between right-wing <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascists</a> and leftist radicals, while military-oriented conservatives controlled the government. Though many claimed the polls to be fraudulent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roberto_Ortiz" title="Roberto Ortiz">Roberto Ortiz</a> was elected president in 1937 and took office the next year, but due to his fragile health he was succeeded by his vice-president, <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Castillo" title="Ramón Castillo">Ramón Castillo</a>. Castillo effectively took power in 1940; he formally assumed leadership in 1942.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolution_of_.2743_.281943.E2.80.9346.29">Revolution of '43 (1943–46)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Revolution of '43 (1943–46)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1943_Argentine_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1943 Argentine coup d'état">1943 Argentine coup d'état</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Argentina_during_World_War_II" title="Argentina during World War II">Argentina during World War II</a></div>
<p>The civilian government appeared to be close to joining the Allies, but many officers of the Argentine armed forces (and ordinary Argentine citizens) objected due to fear of the spread of communism. There was wide support to stay neutral in the conflict, as during World War I. The government was also questioned for domestic policy reasons, namely, electoral fraud, poor labour rights, and the selection of Patrón Costas to run for the presidency.</p>
<p>On June 4, 1943, the <a href="/wiki/United_Officers%27_Group" title="United Officers' Group">United Officers' Group</a> (GOU), which was a secret alliance between military leaders led by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Pablo_Ram%C3%ADrez" title="Pedro Pablo Ramírez">Pedro Pablo Ramírez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Rawson" title="Arturo Rawson">Arturo Rawson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edelmiro_Farrell" title="Edelmiro Farrell">Edelmiro Farrell</a>, and Farrell's protégé <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">Juan Perón</a>, marched to the <a href="/wiki/Casa_Rosada" title="Casa Rosada">Casa Rosada</a> and demanded the resignation of president Castillo. After hours of threats their goal was achieved and the president resigned. This event is considered by historians to be the official end of the Infamous Decade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the coup, Ramírez took power. Although he did not declare war, he broke off relations with the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis powers</a>. Argentina's largest neighbor, Brazil, had already entered the war on the Allied side in 1942.</p>
<p>In 1944 Ramirez was replaced by Farrell, an army officer of Irish-Argentine origin who had spent two years attached to Mussolini's army in the twenties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Initially, his government continued to maintain a neutral policy. Towards the end of the war, Farrell decided it was in the interests of Argentina to be attached to the winning side. Like several Latin American states, Argentina made a late declaration of war against Germany with no intention of providing any military forces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Juan Perón managed relations with labourers and unions, and become highly popular. He was deposed and detained on <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Garc%C3%ADa_Island" title="Martín García Island">Martín García Island</a>, but a massive demonstration on October 17, 1945, forced the government to free Perón and restore him to office. Perón would win the elections shortly afterwards by a landslide. The US ambassador, <a href="/wiki/Spruille_Braden" title="Spruille Braden">Spruille Braden</a>, took direct action in Argentine politics supporting the antiperonist parties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peronist_years_.281946.E2.80.9355.29">Peronist years (1946–55)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Peronist years (1946–55)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1946 General <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">Juan Perón</a> became president; his populist ideology became known as <a href="/wiki/Peronism" title="Peronism">peronism</a>. His popular wife <a href="/wiki/Eva_Per%C3%B3n" title="Eva Perón">Eva Perón</a> played a leading political role until her death in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Perón established censorship by closing down 110 publications between 1943 and 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> During Juan Perón's rule, the number of unionized workers and government programs increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>His government followed an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isolationist" title="Isolationist">isolationist</a> foreign policy and attempted to reduce the political and economic influence of other nations. Perón expanded government spending. His policies led to ruinous inflation. The peso lost about 70% of its value from early 1948 to early 1950; inflation reached 50% in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-precios_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-precios-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Opposition members were imprisoned and some of them tortured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He dismissed many important and capable advisers, while promoting officials largely on the basis of personal loyalty. A coup (<i><a href="/wiki/Revoluci%C3%B3n_Libertadora" title="Revolución Libertadora">Revolución Libertadora</a></i>) led by <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Lonardi" title="Eduardo Lonardi">Eduardo Lonardi</a>, and supported by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, deposed him in 1955. He went into exile, eventually settling in <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Francoist Spain</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revoluci.C3.B3n_Libertadora_.281955.E2.80.931958.29"><i>Revolución Libertadora</i> (1955–1958)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Revolución Libertadora (1955–1958)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Revoluci%C3%B3n_Libertadora" title="Revolución Libertadora">Revolución Libertadora</a></div>
<p>In Argentina, the 1950s and 1960s were marked by frequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coups_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coups d'état">coups d'état</a>, low economic growth in the 1950s and high growth rates in the 1960s. Argentina faced problems of continued social and labor demands. Argentine painter <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Berni" title="Antonio Berni">Antonio Berni</a>'s works reflected the social tragedies of these times, painting in particular life in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villas_miseria" title="Villas miseria">villas miseria</a></i> (shanty towns).</p>
<p>Following the <i><a href="/wiki/Revoluci%C3%B3n_Libertadora" title="Revolución Libertadora">Revolución Libertadora</a></i> military coup, <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Lonardi" title="Eduardo Lonardi">Eduardo Lonardi</a> held power only briefly and was succeeded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro_Aramburu" title="Pedro Aramburu">Pedro Aramburu</a>, president from November 13, 1955 to May 1, 1958. In June 1956, two Peronist generals, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Valle" title="Juan José Valle">Juan José Valle</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Raul_Tanco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Raul Tanco (page does not exist)">Raul Tanco</a>, attempted a coup against Aramburu, criticizing an important purge in the army, the abrogation of social reforms and persecution of trade-union leaders. They also demanded liberation of all political and labor activists and a return to constitutional order. The uprising was quickly crushed. General Valle and other members of the military were executed, and twenty civilians were arrested at their homes and their bodies thrown in the León Suarez dumping ground.</p>
<p>Along with the <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Plaza_de_Mayo" title="Bombing of Plaza de Mayo">June 1955 Casa Rosada bombing</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Mayo" title="Plaza de Mayo">Plaza de Mayo</a>, the León Suarez massacre is one of the important events that started a cycle of violence. Pedro Aramburu was later kidnapped and executed for this massacre, in 1970, by Fernando Abal Medina, Emilio Angel Maza, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mario_Firmenich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mario Firmenich (page does not exist)">Mario Firmenich</a> and others, who would later form the <i><a href="/wiki/Montoneros" title="Montoneros">Montoneros</a></i> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1956, special elections were held to reform the constitution. The Radical Party under <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Balb%C3%ADn" title="Ricardo Balbín">Ricardo Balbín</a> won a majority, although 25% of all ballots were turned in blank as a protest by the banned Peronist party. Also in support of Peronism, the left wing of the Radical Party, led by <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Frondizi" title="Arturo Frondizi">Arturo Frondizi</a>, left the Constitutional Assembly. The Assembly was severely damaged by this defection and was only able to restore the Constitution of 1853 with the sole addition of the Article 14 <i>bis</i>, which enumerated some social rights.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fragile_radical_administrations_.281958.E2.80.931966.29">Fragile radical administrations (1958–1966)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fragile radical administrations (1958–1966)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frondizi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="555" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Frondizi.jpg/200px-Frondizi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Frondizi.jpg/300px-Frondizi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Frondizi.jpg/400px-Frondizi.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frondizi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
<p>A ban on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peronist" title="Peronist">Peronist</a> expression and representation continued during the fragile civilian governments of the period 1958–1966. Frondizi, UCRI's candidate, won the presidential elections of 1958, obtaining approximately 4,000,000 votes against 2,500,000 for Ricardo Balbín (with 800,000 neutral votes). From <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">Caracas</a>, Perón supported Frondizi and called upon his supporters to vote for him, as a means toward the end of prohibition of the Peronist movement and the re-establishment of the workers' social legislation voted during Perón's leadership.</p>
<p>On one hand, Frondizi appointed <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_Alsogaray" title="Álvaro Alsogaray">Álvaro Alsogaray</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Economy_(Argentina)" title="Minister of Economy (Argentina)">Minister of Economy</a> to placate powerful agrarian interests and other conservatives. A member of the powerful military dynasty <a href="/wiki/Alsogaray" title="Alsogaray">Alsogaray</a>, Álvaro, who had already been Minister of Industry under <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Eugenio_Aramburu" title="Pedro Eugenio Aramburu">Aramburu</a>'s military rule, devalued the peso and imposed credit control.</p>
<p>On the other hand, Frondizi followed a laicist program, which raised concerns among the Catholic nationalist forces, leading to the organization, between 1960 and 1962, of the far-right <a href="/wiki/Tacuara_Nationalist_Movement" title="Tacuara Nationalist Movement">Tacuara Nationalist Movement</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tacuara_Nationalist_Movement" title="Tacuara Nationalist Movement">Tacuara</a>, the "first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_guerrilla" title="Urban guerrilla">urban guerrilla</a> group in Argentina",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> engaged in several anti-Semitic bombings, in particular following <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Eichmann" title="Adolf Eichmann">Adolf Eichmann</a>'s kidnapping by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOSSAD" title="MOSSAD">MOSSAD</a> in 1960. During the visit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower" title="Dwight Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a> to Argentina, in February 1962 (Eisenhower had been until 1961 President of the United States), the Tacuara headed nationalist demonstrations against him, leading to the imprisonment of several of their leaders, among them <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Baxter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joe Baxter (page does not exist)">Joe Baxter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PP_Illia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1299" data-file-width="1182" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/PP_Illia.jpg/200px-PP_Illia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/PP_Illia.jpg/300px-PP_Illia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/PP_Illia.jpg/400px-PP_Illia.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PP_Illia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ousting of President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arturo_Illia" title="Arturo Illia">Arturo Illia</a> was initially broadly supported but later deeply regretted by the Argentine population.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>However, Frondizi's government ended in 1962 with intervention yet again by the military, after a series of local elections were won by the Peronist candidates. <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Guido" title="José María Guido">José María Guido</a>, chairman of the senate, claimed the presidency on constitutional grounds before the deeply divided armed forces were able to agree on a name. Right-wing elements in the Argentine armed forces in favor of direct military rule and the suppression of former Peronist politicians, subsequently attempted to wrest control of the government in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1963_Argentine_Navy_Revolt" title="1963 Argentine Navy Revolt">1963 Argentine Navy Revolt</a> on April 2. The failure of the revolt's plotters to win the loyalty of army units near the capital permitted Guido's government to swiftly put down the revolt at the cost of 21 lives.</p>
<p>In new elections in 1963, neither Peronists nor Communists were allowed to participate. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arturo_Illia" title="Arturo Illia">Arturo Illia</a> of the Radical People's Party won these elections; regional elections and by-elections over the next few years favored Peronists.</p>
<p>On the other hand, the Tacuara were outlawed by Illia in 1965, some of its members ultimately turning to the Peronist Left (such as Joe Baxter) while others remained in their far-right positions (such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Ezcurra_Uriburu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alberto Ezcurra Uriburu (page does not exist)">Alberto Ezcurra Uriburu</a>, who would work with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_A_(Argentina)" title="Triple A (Argentina)">Triple A</a>).</p>
<p>Despite the fact that the country grew and developed economically during Illia's tenure as president, he was eventually ousted in a military coup in 1966.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revoluci.C3.B3n_Argentina_.281966.E2.80.9373.29"><i>Revolución Argentina</i> (1966–73)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Revolución Argentina (1966–73)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Revolution" title="Argentine Revolution">Argentine Revolution</a></div>
<p>Amidst growing worker and student unrest, another coup took place in June 1966, self-designated <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revoluci%C3%B3n_Argentina" title="Revolución Argentina">Revolución Argentina</a></i> (Argentine Revolution), which established General <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_Ongan%C3%ADa" title="Juan Carlos Onganía">Juan Carlos Onganía</a> as <i>de facto</i> president, supported by several leaders of the <a href="/wiki/General_Confederation_of_Labour_(Argentina)" title="General Confederation of Labour (Argentina)">General Confederation of Labour</a> (CGT), among these the general secretary, <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Vandor" title="Augusto Vandor">Augusto Vandor</a>. This led to a series of military-appointed presidents.</p>
<p>While preceding military coups were aimed at establishing temporary, transitional <i><a href="/wiki/Military_dictatorship" title="Military dictatorship">junta</a></i>s, the <i>Revolución Argentina</i> headed by Onganía aimed at establishing a new political and social order, opposed both to <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>, which gave to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_Argentina" title="Armed Forces of Argentina">Armed Forces of Argentina</a> a leading, political role in the economic rationalization of the country. The political scientist <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_O%27Donnell" title="Guillermo O'Donnell">Guillermo O'Donnell</a> named this type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_regime" title="Political regime">regime</a> an "authoritarian-bureaucratic state",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> in reference both to the <i>Revolución Argentina</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_military_government" title="Brazilian military government">Brazilian military regime</a> (1964–85), <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a>'s regime (starting in 1973) and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Mar%C3%ADa_Bordaberry" title="Juan María Bordaberry">Juan María Bordaberry</a>'s regime in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Uruguay" title="History of Uruguay">Uruguay</a>.</p>
<p>Onganía's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Economy_of_Argentina" title="Minister of Economy of Argentina">Minister of Economy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adalbert_Krieger_Vasena" title="Adalbert Krieger Vasena">Adalbert Krieger Vasena</a>, decreed a wage freeze and a 40% devaluation of the currency, which strongly affected the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Argentina" title="Economy of Argentina">state of the Argentine economy</a>, in particular the agricultural sector, favoring foreign capital. Vasena suspended <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective labour conventions</a>, reformed the hydrocarbon law which had established a partial monopoly of the <i><a href="/wiki/YPF" title="YPF">Yacimientos Petrolíferos Fiscales</a></i> (YPF) state enterprise, as well as passing a law facilitating expulsions in case of failure to pay rent. Finally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_strike" title="Right to strike">right to strike</a> was suspended (Law 16,936) and several other laws reversed progress made concerning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_laws" title="Labor laws">labor laws</a> throughout the preceding years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The workers' movement divided itself between Vandoristas, who supported a "Peronism without Peron" line (Vandor declared that "to save Perón, one has to be against Perón") and advocated negotiation with the junta, and Peronists, themselves divided.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In July 1966 Onganía ordered the forcible clearing of five facilities of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Buenos_Aires" title="University of Buenos Aires">University of Buenos Aires</a> (UBA) on July 29, 1966 by the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Federal_Police" title="Argentine Federal Police">Federal Police</a>, an event known as <i><a href="/wiki/La_Noche_de_los_Bastones_Largos" title="La Noche de los Bastones Largos">La Noche de los Bastones Largos</a></i> ("The Night of the Long Batons"). These facilities had been occupied by students, professors and graduates (members of the autonomous government of the university) who opposed the military government's intervention in the universities and revocation of the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_university_reform_of_1918" title="Argentine university reform of 1918">1918 university reform</a>. The university repression led to the exile of 301 university professors, including <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Sadosky" title="Manuel Sadosky">Manuel Sadosky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tulio_Halper%C3%ADn_Donghi" title="Tulio Halperín Donghi">Tulio Halperín Donghi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Bag%C3%BA" title="Sergio Bagú">Sergio Bagú</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Risieri_Frondizi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Risieri Frondizi (page does not exist)">Risieri Frondizi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late May 1968 General <a href="/wiki/Julio_Rodolfo_Alsogaray" title="Julio Rodolfo Alsogaray">Julio Alsogaray</a> dissented from Onganía, and rumors spread about a possible coup d'état, with Algosaray leading the conservative opposition to Onganía. Finally, at the end of the month, Onganía dismissed the leaders of the Armed Forces: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alejandro_Lanusse" title="Alejandro Lanusse">Alejandro Lanusse</a> replaced Julio Alsogaray, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Gnavi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedro Gnavi (page does not exist)">Pedro Gnavi</a> replaced <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Benigno_Varela&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Benigno Varela (page does not exist)">Benigno Varela</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jorge_Mart%C3%ADnez_Zuvir%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jorge Martínez Zuviría (page does not exist)">Jorge Martínez Zuviría</a> replaced <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adolfo_Alvarez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adolfo Alvarez (page does not exist)">Adolfo Alvarez</a>.</p>
<p>On 19 September 1968, two important events affected Revolutionary Peronism. On one hand, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Cooke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John William Cooke (page does not exist)">John William Cooke</a>, former personal delegate of Perón and ideologist of the Peronist Left, as well as a friend of <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>, died from natural causes. On the other hand, a small group (13 men and one woman) who aimed at establishing a <i><a href="/wiki/Foco" title="Foco">foco</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Tucum%C3%A1n_Province" title="Tucumán Province">Tucumán Province</a>, in order to head the resistance against the junta, was captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anzorena48_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anzorena48-32">[32]</a></sup> Among them was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Envar_El_Kadre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Envar El Kadre (page does not exist)">Envar El Kadre</a>, then a leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peronist_Youth" title="Peronist Youth">Peronist Youth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anzorena48_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anzorena48-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1969 the <i><a href="/wiki/General_Confederation_of_Labour_of_the_Argentines" title="General Confederation of Labour of the Argentines">General Confederation of Labour of the Argentines</a></i> (CGTA, headed by the graphist <a href="/wiki/Raimundo_Ongaro" title="Raimundo Ongaro">Raimundo Ongaro</a>) headed social movements, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Cordobazo" title="Cordobazo">Cordobazo</a>, as well as other movements in Tucumán and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_Argentina" title="Santa Fe, Argentina">Santa Fe</a>. While Perón managed a reconciliation with <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Vandor" title="Augusto Vandor">Augusto Vandor</a>, head of the CGT Azopardo, he followed, in particular through the voice of his delegate <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jorge_Paladino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jorge Paladino (page does not exist)">Jorge Paladino</a>, a cautious line of opposition to the military junta, criticizing with moderation the neoliberal policies of the junta but waiting for discontent inside the government ("<i>hay que desencillar hasta que aclare</i>", said Perón, advocating patience). Thus, Onganía had an interview with 46 CGT delegates, among them Vandor, who agreed to cooperate with the military junta, thus uniting themselves with the <i>Nueva Corriente de Opinión</i> headed by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Alonso_(trade_unionist)" title="José Alonso (trade unionist)">José Alonso</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rogelio_Coria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rogelio Coria (page does not exist)">Rogelio Coria</a>.</p>
<p>In December 1969, more than 20 priests, members of the <i><a href="/wiki/Movement_of_Priests_for_the_Third_World" title="Movement of Priests for the Third World">Movement of Priests for the Third World</a></i> (MSTM), marched on the <i><a href="/wiki/Casa_Rosada" title="Casa Rosada">Casa Rosada</a></i> to present to Onganía a petition pleading with him to abandon the planned eradication of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villas_miserias" title="Villas miserias">villas miserias</a></i> (shanty towns).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anzorena49_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anzorena49-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, Onganía implemented <a href="/wiki/Corporatism" title="Corporatism">corporatism</a> policies, experimenting in particular in Córdoba, underneath <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Jos%C3%A9_Caballero" title="Carlos José Caballero">Carlos Caballero</a>'s governance. The same year, the Movement of Priests for the Third World issued a declaration supporting socialist revolutionary movements, which led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism_in_Argentina" title="Catholicism in Argentina">Catholic hierarchy</a>, by the voice of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_Aramburu" title="Juan Carlos Aramburu">Juan Carlos Aramburu</a>, coadjutor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Buenos_Aires" title="Archbishop of Buenos Aires">archbishop of Buenos Aires</a>, to proscribe priests from making political or social declarations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anzorena53_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anzorena53-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growing_instability_.281969.E2.80.9376.29">Growing instability (1969–76)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Growing instability (1969–76)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the de facto government of the <i>Revolución Argentina</i>, the left began to regain power through underground movements. This was mainly through violent guerrilla groups. Later, the return of Peronism was expected to calm down the heated waters but did exactly the opposite, creating a violent breach between right-wing and left-wing Peronism, leading to years of violence and political instability that culminated with the coup d'état of 1976.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subversive_years_.281969.E2.80.9373.29">Subversive years (1969–73)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Subversive years (1969–73)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various armed actions, headed by the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fuerzas_Armadas_de_Liberaci%C3%B3n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fuerzas Armadas de Liberación (page does not exist)">Fuerzas Armadas de Liberación</a></i> (FAL), composed of former members of the <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Communist_Party_of_Argentina" title="Revolutionary Communist Party of Argentina">Revolutionary Communist Party</a>, occurred in April 1969, leading to several arrests among FAL members. These were the first left-wing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_guerrilla" title="Urban guerrilla">urban guerrilla</a> actions in Argentina. Beside these isolated actions, the <a href="/wiki/Cordobazo" title="Cordobazo">Cordobazo</a> uprising that year, called forth by the CGT de los Argentinos, and its Cordobese leader, <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_Tosco" title="Agustín Tosco">Agustín Tosco</a>, prompted demonstrations in the entire country. The same year, the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Army_(Argentina)" title="People's Revolutionary Army (Argentina)">People's Revolutionary Army</a> (ERP) was formed as the military branch of the Trotskyist <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Revolutionary_Party_(Argentina)" title="Workers' Revolutionary Party (Argentina)">Workers' Revolutionary Party</a>, kidnapping high-profile rich Argentines and demanding ransom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nancy_Scheper-Hughes_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nancy_Scheper-Hughes-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul_H._Lewis_125_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paul_H._Lewis_125-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The last of the "de facto" military presidents, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alejandro_Lanusse" title="Alejandro Lanusse">Alejandro Lanusse</a>, was appointed in 1971 and attempted to re-establish democracy amidst an atmosphere of continuing Peronist workers' protests.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="C.C3.A1mpora.27s_tenure_.281973.29">Cámpora's tenure (1973)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cámpora's tenure (1973)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 11, 1973, Argentina held general elections for the first time in ten years. Perón was prevented from running, but voters elected his stand-in, Dr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hector_C%C3%A1mpora" title="Hector Cámpora">Hector Cámpora</a>, as President. Cámpora defeated his <a href="/wiki/Radical_Civic_Union" title="Radical Civic Union">Radical Civic Union</a> opponent. Cámpora won 49.5 percent of the votes in the presidential election following a campaign based on a platform of national reconstruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miguel_Bonasso_1997_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miguel_Bonasso_1997-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riding a wave of mass support, Cámpora inaugurated his period on May 25. He acceded to his functions on May 25, which was saluted by a massive popular gathering of the Peronist Youth movement, <a href="/wiki/Montoneros" title="Montoneros">Montoneros</a>, FAR and FAP ("Fuerzas Armadas Peronistas") in the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Mayo" title="Plaza de Mayo">Plaza de Mayo</a>. Cámpora assumed a strong stance against right-wing Peronists, declaring during his first speech: "La sangre derramada no será negociada" ("Spilled blood will not be negotiated").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miguel_Bonasso_1997_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miguel_Bonasso_1997-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> president <a href="/wiki/Osvaldo_Dortic%C3%B3s_Torrado" title="Osvaldo Dorticós Torrado">Osvaldo Dorticós</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> president <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Allende" title="Salvador Allende">Salvador Allende</a> were present at his inauguration, while <a href="/wiki/William_P._Rogers" title="William P. Rogers">William P. Rogers</a>, U.S. Secretary of State, and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguayan</a> president <a href="/wiki/Juan_Mar%C3%ADa_Bordaberry" title="Juan María Bordaberry">Juan Bordaberry</a>, could not attend, blocked in their respective cars by demonstrators. Political prisoners were liberated on the same day, under the pressure of the demonstrators. Cámpora's government included progressive figures such as Interior Minister Esteban Righi and Education Minister Jorge Taiana, but also included members of the labor and political right-wing Peronist factions, such as <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_L%C3%B3pez_Rega" title="José López Rega">José López Rega</a>, Perón's personal secretary and Minister of Social Welfare, and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_Due" title="Propaganda Due">P2 Masonic lodge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miguel_Bonasso_1997_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Miguel_Bonasso_1997-37">[37]</a></sup> Perón's followers also commanded strong majorities in both houses of Congress.</p>
<p>Hector Cámpora's government followed a traditional Peronist economic policy, supporting the national market and redistributing wealth. One of José Ber Gelbard's first measures as minister of economics was to augment workers' wages. However, the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a> seriously affected Argentina's oil-dependent economy. Almost 600 social conflicts, strikes or occupations occurred in Cámpora's first month. The military conceded Campora's victory, but strikes, as well as government-backed violence, continued unabated. The slogan "<a href="/wiki/Campora" title="Campora">Campora</a> in government, Perón in power" expressed the real source of popular joy, however.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_of_Per.C3.B3n_.281973.E2.80.9374.29">Return of Perón (1973–74)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Return of Perón (1973–74)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amidst escalating terror from right and left alike, Perón decided to return and assume the presidency. On June 20, 1973, two million people waited for him at Ezeiza airport. From Perón's speaking platform, camouflaged far-right gunmen fired on the masses, shooting at the Peronist Youth movement and the <a href="/wiki/Montoneros" title="Montoneros">Montoneros</a>, killing at least thirteen and injuring more than three hundred (this became known as the <a href="/wiki/Ezeiza_massacre" title="Ezeiza massacre">Ezeiza massacre</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cámpora and vice-president Solano Lima resigned on July 13. Deputy <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Alberto_Lastiri" title="Raúl Alberto Lastiri">Raúl Alberto Lastiri</a>, José López Rega's son-in-law and also a <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_Due" title="Propaganda Due">P2 member</a>, was then promoted to the presidency to organize elections. Cámpora's followers such as Chancellor Juan Carlos Puig and Interior Minister Esteban Righi were immediately replaced by Alberto J. Vignes and Benito Llambi, and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Army_(Argentina)" title="People's Revolutionary Army (Argentina)">Ejército Revolucionario del Pueblo</a> (ERP - People's Revolutionary Army) was declared a "dissolved terrorist organization". On September 23, Perón won the elections with 61.85% of the votes, with his third wife, <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Mart%C3%ADnez_de_Per%C3%B3n" title="Isabel Martínez de Perón">Isabel Martínez de Perón</a>, as vice-president.</p>
<p>Peronist right-wing factions won a decisive victory and Perón assumed the Presidency in October 1973, a month after <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Pinochet</a>'s coup in Chile. Violent acts, including by the Triple A, continued to threaten public order. On September 25, 1973, José Ignacio Rucci, <a href="/wiki/General_Confederation_of_Labour_(Argentina)" title="General Confederation of Labour (Argentina)">CGT trade-union</a>'s Secretary General and Perón's friend, was assassinated by the Montoneros. The government resorted to a number of <a href="/wiki/Necessity_and_Urgency_Decree" title="Necessity and Urgency Decree">emergency decrees</a>, including the implementation of special executive authority to deal with violence. This allowed the government to imprison individuals indefinitely without charge.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Perón won 61.9 percent of the vote and, with his wife Maria Estela (Isabel) Martinez de Perón as vice president, their administration was inaugurated on October 12. In his second period in office, Perón was committed to achieving political peace through a new alliance of business and labor to promote national reconstruction. Peron's charisma and his past record with respect to labor helped him maintain his working-class support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-myeducationresearch.org_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-myeducationresearch.org-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Isabel de Perón was inexperienced in politics and only carried Perón's name; Lopez Rega was described as a man with numerous occult interests, including astrology, and a supporter of dissident Catholic groups. Economic policies were directed at restructuring wages and currency devaluations in order to attract foreign investment capital to Argentina. <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_L%C3%B3pez_Rega" title="José López Rega">López Rega</a> was ousted as Isabel de Perón's adviser in June 1975; General Numa Laplane, the commander in chief of the army who had supported the administration through the Lopez Rega period, was replaced by General Jorge Rafael Videla in August 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-myeducationresearch.org_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-myeducationresearch.org-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isabel.27s_government_.281974.E2.80.9376.29">Isabel's government (1974–76)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Isabel's government (1974–76)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perón died on July 1, 1974. His wife succeeded him in office, but her administration was undermined by economic collapse (inflation was skyrocketing and GDP contracted), Peronist intra-party struggles, and growing acts of terrorism by <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">insurgents</a> such as the ERP and paramilitary movements.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Montoneros" title="Montoneros">Montoneros</a>, led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mario_Firmenich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mario Firmenich (page does not exist)">Mario Firmenich</a>, cautiously decided to go underground after Peron's death. Isabel Perón was removed from office by <a href="/wiki/1976_Argentine_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1976 Argentine coup d'état">the military coup</a> on March 24, 1976. This gave way to the last and arguably most violent de facto government in Argentina, the National Reorganization Process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Reorganization_Process_.281976.E2.80.9383.29">National Reorganization Process (1976–83)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: National Reorganization Process (1976–83)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/National_Reorganization_Process" title="National Reorganization Process">National Reorganization Process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dirty_War" title="Dirty War">Dirty War</a></div>
<p>Following the coup against Isabel Perón, the armed forces formally exercised power through a junta led consecutively by <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Rafael_Videla" title="Jorge Rafael Videla">Videla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Eduardo_Viola" title="Roberto Eduardo Viola">Viola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Galtieri" title="Leopoldo Galtieri">Galtieri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reynaldo_Bignone" title="Reynaldo Bignone">Bignone</a> until December 10, 1983. These <i>de facto</i> leaders termed their government program the "<a href="/wiki/National_Reorganization_Process" title="National Reorganization Process">National Reorganization Process</a>".</p>
<p>In the late 1960s and the early 1970s, Marxist–Leninist militias such as the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Army_(Argentina)" title="People's Revolutionary Army (Argentina)">People's Revolutionary Army</a> utilized aggressive tactics that sometimes resulted in violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nancy_Scheper-Hughes_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nancy_Scheper-Hughes-35">[35]</a></sup> Later the military government used these acts as justification for their even more brutal measures. The "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ideological_war&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ideological war (page does not exist)">ideological war</a>" doctrine of the Argentine military focused on eliminating the social base of insurgency. In practice that meant assassinating many middle class students, intellectuals and labor organizers, most of whom had few ties to the guerrillas.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1544" data-file-width="2080" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg/250px-Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg/375px-Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg/500px-Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neuquen_Monumento_Malvinas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monument to the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> fallen, <a href="/wiki/Neuqu%C3%A9n" title="Neuquén">Neuquén</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The costs of what the armed forces called the "Dirty War" were high in terms of lives lost and basic human rights violated. Thousands of deaths may be attributed to various guerrilla attacks and assassinations. The 1984 <a href="/wiki/National_Commission_on_the_Disappearance_of_Persons" title="National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons">Commission on the Disappeared</a> documented the disappearance and probable death at the hands of the military regime of about 11,000 people, relatively few of whom were likely Montonero or ERP cadres. Human rights groups estimate that over 30,000 persons were "<a href="/wiki/Forced_disappearance" title="Forced disappearance">disappeared</a>" (i.e. arrested, tortured, and secretly executed without trial) during the 1976–1983 period; many more went into exile.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The People's Revolutionary Army alone admitted it lost 5,000 militants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Serious economic problems, mounting charges of corruption, public discontent and, finally, the country's 1982 defeat by the United Kingdom in the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> following Argentina's <a href="/wiki/1982_invasion_of_the_Falkland_Islands" title="1982 invasion of the Falkland Islands">unsuccessful attempt</a> to seize the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> all combined to discredit the Argentine military regime. Under strong public pressure, the junta lifted bans on political parties and gradually restored basic political liberties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beagle_conflict">Beagle conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Beagle conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Beagle_conflict" title="Beagle conflict">Beagle conflict</a> began to brew in the 1960s, when Argentina began to claim that the <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel_cartography_since_1881" title="Beagle Channel cartography since 1881">Picton, Lennox and Nueva</a> islands in the <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel" title="Beagle Channel">Beagle Channel</a> were rightfully hers. In 1971, Chile and Argentina signed an agreement formally submitting the Beagle Channel issue to binding <a href="/wiki/Beagle_Channel_Arbitration" title="Beagle Channel Arbitration">Beagle Channel Arbitration</a>. On May 2, 1977 the court ruled that the islands and all adjacent formations belonged to Chile. See the <a class="external text" href="http://legal.un.org/riaa/cases/vol_XXI/53-264.pdf" rel="nofollow">Report and decision of the Court of Arbitration</a>.</p>
<p>On 25 January 1978 the Argentina military junta led by General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jorge_Videla" title="Jorge Videla">Jorge Videla</a> declared the award fundamentally null and intensified their claim over the islands. On 22 December 1978, Argentina started<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Operation_Soberan%C3%ADa" title="Operation Soberanía">Operation Soberanía</a> over the disputed islands, but the invasion was halted due to:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>(The newspaper Clarín explained some years later that such caution was based,) in part, on military concerns. In order to achieve a victory, certain objectives had to be reached before the seventh day after the attack. Some military leaders considered this not enough time due to the difficulty involved in transportation through the passes over the Andean Mountains.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>and in cite 46:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>According to Clarín, two consequences were feared. First, those who were dubious feared a possible regionalization of the conflict. Second, as a consequence, the conflict could acquire great power proportions. In the first case decision makers speculated that Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Brazil might intervene. Then the great powers could take sides. In this case, the resolution of the conflict would depend not on the combatants, but on the countries that supplied the weapons.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>In December that year, moments before Videla signed a declaration of war against Chile, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> agreed to mediate between the two nations. The Pope's envoy, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Samor%C3%A8" title="Antonio Samorè">Antonio Samorè</a>, successfully averted war and proposed a new definitive boundary in which the three disputed islands would remain Chilean. Argentina and Chile both agreed to Samoré's proposal and signed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Peace_and_Friendship_of_1984_between_Chile_and_Argentina" title="Treaty of Peace and Friendship of 1984 between Chile and Argentina">Treaty of Peace and Friendship of 1984 between Chile and Argentina</a>, ending that dispute.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_democracy_.281983.E2.80.93present.29">New democracy (1983–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: New democracy (1983–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1592" data-file-width="1124" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg/200px-Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg/300px-Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg/400px-Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn1983.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Raúl Alfonsín's Presidential assignment, 1983.</div>

<p>On October 30, 1983, Argentines went to the polls to choose a president; vice-president; and national, provincial, and local officials in elections found by international observers to be fair and honest. The country returned to constitutional rule after <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn" title="Raúl Alfonsín">Raúl Alfonsín</a>, candidate of the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Civic_Union" title="Radical Civic Union">Radical Civic Union</a> (<i>Unión Cívica Radical</i>, UCR), received 52% of the popular vote for president. He began a 6-year term of office on December 10, 1983.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alfons.C3.ADn_era_.281983.E2.80.931989.29">Alfonsín era (1983–1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Alfonsín era (1983–1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Ra%C3%BAl_Alfons%C3%ADn" title="Presidency of Raúl Alfonsín">Presidency of Raúl Alfonsín</a></div>
<p>Five days later, he created the <a href="/wiki/National_Commission_on_the_Disappearance_of_Persons" title="National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons">National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons</a> (CONADEP), led by Argentine writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernesto_S%C3%A1bato" title="Ernesto Sábato">Ernesto Sábato</a>. However, it was also under Alfonsín's presidency that the December 24, 1986 "<a href="/wiki/Full_stop_law" title="Full stop law">Full stop law</a>" was voted, granting amnesty to all acts committed before December 10, 1983, amid pressure from the military. It would not be until June 2005's <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Argentina" title="Supreme Court of Argentina">Supreme Court</a> decision to overturn all amnesty laws that investigations could be started again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Alfonsín administration, a <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Peace_and_Friendship_of_1984_between_Chile_and_Argentina" title="Treaty of Peace and Friendship of 1984 between Chile and Argentina">Treaty of Peace and Friendship of 1984 between Chile and Argentina</a> with Chile was signed and the roots of the <a href="/wiki/Mercosur" title="Mercosur">Mercosur</a> trade bloc were established.</p>
<p>In 1985 and 1987, large turnouts for mid-term elections demonstrated continued public support for a strong and vigorous democratic system. The UCR-led government took steps to resolve some of the nation's most pressing problems, including accounting for those who <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desaparecidos" title="Desaparecidos">disappeared</a> during military rule, establishing <a href="/wiki/Civilian_control_of_the_military" title="Civilian control of the military">civilian control of the armed forces</a>, and consolidating democratic institutions. One of the biggest achievements of the Alfonsín administration was the reduction of corruption in public offices, which was reduced by half during his administration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, constant friction with the military, failure to resolve several economic problems inherited from the military dictatorship and great opposition from the labor unions undermined the effectiveness of the Alfonsín government, which left office six months early after Peronist candidate <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Menem" title="Carlos Menem">Carlos Menem</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_general_election,_1989" title="Argentine general election, 1989">1989 presidential elections</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Menemist_decade_.281989.E2.80.9399.29">Menemist decade (1989–99)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Menemist decade (1989–99)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Washington_Consensus" title="Washington Consensus">Washington Consensus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carlos_menem.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1529" data-file-width="1005" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Carlos_menem.jpg/200px-Carlos_menem.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Carlos_menem.jpg/300px-Carlos_menem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Carlos_menem.jpg/400px-Carlos_menem.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carlos_menem.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Carlos_Menem" title="Carlos Menem">Carlos Menem</a>, President throughout the 1990s</div>

</div>
<p>As President, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Menem" title="Carlos Menem">Carlos Menem</a> launched a major overhaul of Argentine domestic policy. Large-scale structural reforms dramatically reversed the role of the state in Argentine economic life. Ironically, the Peronist Menem oversaw the <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> of many of the industries Perón had nationalized.</p>
<p>A decisive leader pressing a controversial agenda, Menem was not reluctant to use the presidency's powers to issue "emergency" decrees (formally <i><a href="/wiki/Necessity_and_Urgency_Decree" title="Necessity and Urgency Decree">Necessity and Urgency Decrees</a></i>) when the Congress was unable to reach consensus on his proposed reforms. Those powers were curtailed somewhat when the constitution was reformed in 1994 as a result of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Pact_of_Olivos" title="Pact of Olivos">Pact of Olivos</a> with the opposition Radical Party. That arrangement opened the way for Menem to seek and win reelection with 50% of the vote in the three-way 1995 presidential race. <a href="/wiki/Piquetero" title="Piquetero">Piquetero</a> movement rose.</p>
<p>The 1995 election saw the emergence of the moderate-left <a href="/wiki/Front_for_a_Country_in_Solidarity" title="Front for a Country in Solidarity">FrePaSo</a> political alliance. This alternative to the two traditional political parties in Argentina was particularly strong in Buenos Aires but lacked the national infrastructure of the Peronists and Radicals. In an important development in Argentina's political life, all three major parties in the 1999 race espoused <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> economic policies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_millennium_crisis_.281999.E2.80.932003.29">New millennium crisis (1999–2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: New millennium crisis (1999–2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%932002_Argentine_great_depression" title="1998–2002 Argentine great depression">1998–2002 Argentine great depression</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="De_La_R.C3.BAa_presidency_.281999.E2.80.932001.29">De La Rúa presidency (1999–2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: De La Rúa presidency (1999–2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_la_R%C3%BAa#Presidency" title="Fernando de la Rúa">Fernando de la Rúa § Presidency</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%932002_Argentine_great_depression#The_crisis" title="1998–2002 Argentine great depression">1998–2002 Argentine great depression § The_crisis</a></div>
<p>In October 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Radical_Civic_Union" title="Radical Civic Union">UCR</a>–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FrePaSo" title="FrePaSo">FrePaSo</a> <a href="/wiki/Alliance_for_Work,_Justice_and_Education" title="Alliance for Work, Justice and Education"><i>Alianza</i></a>'s presidential candidate, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_la_R%C3%BAa" title="Fernando de la Rúa">Fernando de la Rúa</a>, defeated Peronist candidate <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Duhalde" title="Eduardo Duhalde">Eduardo Duhalde</a>. Having taken office in December 1999, De la Rúa followed an <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a>-sponsored program of government spending cuts, revenue increases, and provincial revenue-sharing reforms to get the federal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiscal_deficit" title="Fiscal deficit">fiscal deficit</a> under control, and pursued <a href="/wiki/Labour_market_flexibility" title="Labour market flexibility">labor market flexibilization</a> and business-promotion measures aimed at stimulating foreign investment, so as to avoid <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">defaulting the public debt</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Towards the end of 2001, Argentina faced grave economic problems. The IMF pressed Argentina to service its <a href="/wiki/External_debt" title="External debt">external debt</a>, effectively forcing Argentina to <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">devalue</a> the Argentine peso, which had been pegged to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">U.S. dollar</a>, or alternatively fully <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dollarize" title="Dollarize">dollarize</a> its economy. Deep budget cuts, including a 13% reduction in pay for the nation's 2 million public sector employees, failed to curb the rapidly increasing <a href="/wiki/Country_risk" title="Country risk">country risk</a> on almost U$100 billion in Argentine bonds, increasing debt service costs and further limiting access to international credit, despite a moderately successful debt swap arranged by Cavallo with most bondholders. Voters reacted to the rapidly worsening economy in the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_legislative_election,_2001" title="Argentine legislative election, 2001">October 2001 midterm elections</a> by both depriving the Alliance of its majority in the Lower House, and by casting a record 25% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoiled_ballot" title="Spoiled ballot">spoiled ballots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Corralito_.282001.29">Corralito (2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Corralito (2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="640" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg/220px-Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg/330px-Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg/440px-Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crisis_20_diciembre_2001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Police intervention in the 2001 riots</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On November 1, 2001, as people's fears that the peso would be devalued caused massive withdrawal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_deposit" title="Bank deposit">bank deposits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capital_flight" title="Capital flight">capital flight</a>, de la Rúa's Minister of Economy <a href="/wiki/Domingo_Cavallo" title="Domingo Cavallo">Domingo Cavallo</a> passed regulations severely limiting withdrawals, effectively freezing the peso-denominated assets of the Argentine <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a>, while dollar-denominated foreign accounts were, by its very nature, shielded from devaluation. (The freezing of the bank accounts was informally named <i><a href="/wiki/Corralito" title="Corralito">corralito</a></i>.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The overall economy declined drastically during December 2001. <a href="/wiki/December_2001_riots_in_Argentina" title="December 2001 riots in Argentina">The resulting riots</a> led to dozens of deaths. The Minister of Economy Domingo Cavallo resigned, but that did not prevent the collapse of De la Rúa's administration. On December 20 de la Rúa also resigned, but the political crisis was extremely serious, as a result of the previous resignation of the vice-president <a href="/wiki/Carlos_%C3%81lvarez_(politician)" title="Carlos Álvarez (politician)">Carlos <i>Chacho</i> Álvarez</a> in 2000. The president of the Senate became <a href="/wiki/Ad_interim" title="Ad interim">interim</a> president until the National Congress elected, two days later, <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Sa%C3%A1" title="Adolfo Rodríguez Saá">Adolfo Rodríguez Saá</a> to finish De la Rúa's term. But Rodríguez Saá resigned a week later on December 31, leaving the power to the president of the Chamber of Deputies (as the Senate was undergoing their annual renovation of its president) as interim.</p>
<p>Finally, on January 2, 2002, the National Congress elected the Peronist <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Duhalde" title="Eduardo Duhalde">Eduardo Duhalde</a>, a losing candidate in the most recent presidential election, as president. The peso was first devalued by 29%, and then the dollar peg was abandoned; by July 2002, the national currency had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depreciation_(currency)" title="Depreciation (currency)">depreciated</a> to one-quarter of its former value.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery_.282002.E2.80.9303.29">Recovery (2002–03)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Recovery (2002–03)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>President Duhalde faced a country in turmoil. His administration had to deal with a wave of protests (middle-class <a href="/wiki/Cacerolazo" title="Cacerolazo">cacerolazos</a> and unemployed <a href="/wiki/Piquetero" title="Piquetero">piqueteros</a>), and did so with a relatively tolerant policy, intending to minimize violence. As inflation became a serious issue and the effects of the crisis became apparent in the form of increased unemployment and poverty, Duhalde chose a moderate, low-profile economist, <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Lavagna" title="Roberto Lavagna">Roberto Lavagna</a>, as his Minister of Economy. The economic measures implemented brought inflation under control.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After a year, Duhalde deemed his tasks fulfilled and, pressured by certain political factors, called for elections, which in April 2003 brought <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9stor_Kirchner" title="Néstor Kirchner">Néstor Kirchner</a>, the left-of-centre Peronist governor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Province_(Argentina)" title="Santa Cruz Province (Argentina)">Santa Cruz</a>, to power.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kirchner_governments_.282003.E2.80.932015.29">Kirchner governments (2003–2015)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Kirchner governments (2003–2015)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9stor_Kirchner" title="Néstor Kirchner">Néstor Kirchner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cristina_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Kirchner" title="Cristina Fernández de Kirchner">Cristina Fernández de Kirchner</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Kirchnerism" title="Kirchnerism">Kirchnerism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pink_tide" title="Pink tide">Pink tide</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1461" data-file-width="2100" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg/250px-Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg/375px-Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg/500px-Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cristina_baston_Kirchner.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9stor_Kirchner" title="Néstor Kirchner">Néstor Kirchner</a> hands the presidential mandate to his wife, <a href="/wiki/Cristina_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Kirchner" title="Cristina Fernández de Kirchner">Cristina Fernández de Kirchner</a>.</div>

<p>President Kirchner took office on May 25, 2003. He reshuffled the leadership of the Armed Forces, overturned controversial amnesty laws that protected members of the 1976-1983 dictatorship from prosecution, and kept Lavagna on as economy minister for most of his presidency. Kirchner's administration saw a strong economic rebound,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Argentine_debt_restructuring" title="Argentine debt restructuring">foreign debt restructuring</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Argentine_general_election,_2007" title="Argentine general election, 2007">2007 general election</a> took place in ten provinces in September and Kirchner's <a href="/wiki/Front_for_Victory" title="Front for Victory">Front for Victory</a> won in six provinces. <a href="/wiki/Hermes_Binner" title="Hermes Binner">Hermes Binner</a> was elected <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Santa_Fe" title="Governor of Santa Fe">governor of Santa Fe</a>, becoming the first <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Argentina)" title="Socialist Party (Argentina)">Socialist</a> governor in Argentina's history and the first non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justicialism" title="Justicialism">Justicialist</a> to rule the rather wealthy Santa Fe province, and Center-left <a href="/wiki/Fabiana_R%C3%ADos" title="Fabiana Ríos">Fabiana Ríos</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Support_for_an_Egalitarian_Republic" title="Support for an Egalitarian Republic">ARI</a>) became the first woman to be elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Governor of Tierra del Fuego">governor of Tierra del Fuego</a>, while the right-wing <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Macri" title="Mauricio Macri">Mauricio Macri</a> was elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_of_Government_of_Buenos_Aires_City" title="Chief of Government of Buenos Aires City">Chief of Government of Buenos Aires City</a> in June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mde_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mde-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 10, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Cristina_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Kirchner" title="Cristina Fernández de Kirchner">Cristina Fernández de Kirchner</a> took over the presidency from her husband, after winning elections with 44% of the vote. Néstor Kirchner remained a highly influential politician during the term of Cristina Fernández de Kirchner. The press developed the term "<i>presidential marriage</i>" to make reference to both of them at once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Some political analysts compared this type of government with a <a href="/wiki/Diarchy" title="Diarchy">diarchy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>After proposing a new taxation system for agricultural exports, Cristina Fernández's Government had to face a <a href="/wiki/2008_Argentine_government_conflict_with_the_agricultural_sector" title="2008 Argentine government conflict with the agricultural sector">severe lock out of the sector</a>. The protest, which spread over 129 days, was quickly politicized and marked an inflection point in her administration. The system was finally rejected in the Senate by an opposing vote by the Vice President <a href="/wiki/Julio_Cobos" title="Julio Cobos">Julio Cobos</a>.</p>
<p>The political style of the government changed with the <a href="/wiki/Death_and_state_funeral_of_N%C3%A9stor_Kirchner" title="Death and state funeral of Néstor Kirchner">Death and state funeral of Néstor Kirchner</a>. Cristina slowly distanced herself from the traditional structure of the <a href="/wiki/Justicialist_Party" title="Justicialist Party">Justicialist Party</a> and favored instead <i><a href="/wiki/La_C%C3%A1mpora" title="La Cámpora">La Cámpora</a></i>, a group of young supporters led by her son <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1ximo_Kirchner" title="Máximo Kirchner">Máximo Kirchner</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_conservatism_.282015-Present.29">Return to conservatism (2015-Present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Return to conservatism (2015-Present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Mauricio_Macri" title="Presidency of Mauricio Macri">Presidency of Mauricio Macri</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>On 22 November 2015, the conservatives won the presidency of the country. <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Macri" title="Mauricio Macri">Mauricio Macri</a>, leader of the conservative party <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propuesta_Republicana" title="Propuesta Republicana">PROpuesta Republicana</a></i> was devoted president of the country with an integrated alliance also by social democrats, radicals, etc., (alliance <i><a href="/wiki/Cambiemos" title="Cambiemos">Cambiemos</a></i>) overcoming the former motonauta <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Scioli" title="Daniel Scioli">Daniel Scioli</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Front_for_Victory" title="Front for Victory">Front for Victory</a>. Macri took over as president on 10 December of that year.</p>
