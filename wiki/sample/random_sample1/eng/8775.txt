<p><b>Joseph of Arimathea</b> was, according to all four canonical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">Gospels</a>, the man who assumed responsibility for the burial of Jesus after <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>' <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">crucifixion</a>. A number of stories that developed during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> connect him with both <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury" title="Glastonbury">Glastonbury</a>, where he is supposed to have founded the earliest Christian oratory, and also with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Grail</a> legend.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gospel_narratives">Gospel narratives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gospel narratives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+27%3A57&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Matthew 27:57</a></span> described him simply as a rich man and disciple of Jesus, but according to <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+15%3A43&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Mark 15:43</a></span> Joseph of Arimathea was "a respected member of the council, who was also himself looking for the kingdom of God"; and <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+23%3A50%E2%80%9356&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Luke 23:50–56</a></span> adds that he "had not consented to their decision and action".</p>
<p>According to <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+19%3A38&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">John 19:38</a></span>, upon hearing of Jesus' death, this secret <a href="/wiki/Disciple_(Christianity)" title="Disciple (Christianity)">disciple</a> of Jesus "asked Pilate that he might take away the body of Jesus, and Pilate gave him permission." Joseph immediately purchased a linen shroud (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+15%3A46&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">Mark 15:46</a></span>) and proceeded to <a href="/wiki/Calvary" title="Calvary">Golgotha</a> to take the body of Jesus down from the cross. There, according to <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+19%3A39-40&amp;version=NRSV" rel="nofollow">John 19:39-40</a></span>, Joseph and <a href="/wiki/Nicodemus" title="Nicodemus">Nicodemus</a> took the body and bound it in linen cloths with the spices that Nicodemus had bought. The disciples then conveyed the prepared corpse to the place previously bought for Joseph's own tomb, a man-made cave hewn from rock in a garden of his house nearby. This was done speedily, "for the Sabbath was drawing on".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneration">Veneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Veneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3099" data-file-width="2536" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg/220px-Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg/330px-Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg/440px-Pietro_Perugino_012.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Joseph of Arimathea is venerated as a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a>, and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> churches. The traditional Roman calendar marked his feast day on March 17, but he is now listed, along with Saint <a href="/wiki/Nicodemus" title="Nicodemus">Nicodemus</a>, on August 31 in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Martyrology" title="Roman Martyrology">Martyrologium Romanum</a>. Eastern Orthodox churches commemorate him on the <i>Third Sunday of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Pascha</a></i> (i.e., the second Sunday after Easter) and on July 31, the date shared by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" title="Lutheran Church">Lutheran churches</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although a series of legends developed during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> (perhaps elaborations of early <a href="/wiki/New_Testament_apocrypha" title="New Testament apocrypha">New Testament apocrypha</a>) tied this Joseph to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> as well as the Holy Grail, he is not currently on the abbreviated liturgical calendar of the Church of England, although this Joseph is on the calendars of some churches of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican_communion" title="Anglican communion">Anglican communion</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(USA)" title="Episcopal Church (USA)">Episcopal Church (USA)</a>, which commemorates him on August 1.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Testament_prophecy">Old Testament prophecy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Old Testament prophecy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1555" data-file-width="1166" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg/220px-5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg/330px-5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg/440px-5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:5267-20080122-jerusalem-tomb-of-jesus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tomb of Jesus in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre" title="Church of the Holy Sepulchre">Church of the Holy Sepulchre</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many Christians<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> interpret Joseph's role as fulfilling <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a>'s prediction that the grave of the "Suffering Servant" would be with a rich man (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">Isaiah</a> 53:9), assuming that Isaiah was referring to the <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a>. The prophecy in <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_53" title="Isaiah 53">Isaiah chapter 53</a> is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Man_of_Sorrows" title="Man of Sorrows">Man of Sorrows</a>" passage:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death, though he had done no violence, nor was any deceit in his mouth.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Greek <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> text:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>And I will give the wicked for his burial, and the rich for his death; for he practised no iniquity, nor craft with his mouth.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Since the 2nd century, a mass of legendary detail has accumulated around the figure of Joseph of Arimathea in addition to the New Testament references. Joseph is referenced in apocryphal and non-canonical accounts such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acts_of_Pilate" title="Acts of Pilate">Acts of Pilate</a></i>, a text often appended to the medieval <i>Gospel of Nicodemus</i> and <i>The Narrative of Joseph</i>, and mentioned in the works of <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">early church</a> historians such as <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a> (125–189), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hippolytus_(writer)" title="Hippolytus (writer)">Hippolytus</a> (170–236), <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> (155–222) and <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> (260–340), who added details not found in the canonical accounts. <a href="/wiki/Francis_Gigot" title="Francis Gigot">Francis Gigot</a>, writing in the <i>Catholic Encyclopedia</i>, states that "the additional details which are found concerning him in the apocryphal <i>Acta Pilati</i> ("Acts of Pilate"), are unworthy of credence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gigot_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gigot-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hilary_of_Poitiers" title="Hilary of Poitiers">Hilary of Poitiers</a> (300–367) enriched the legend, and Saint <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a> (347–407), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch of Constantinople">Patriarch of Constantinople</a>, was the first to write<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that Joseph was one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventy_Apostles" title="Seventy Apostles">Seventy Apostles</a> appointed in Luke 10.</p>
<p>During the late 12th century, Joseph became connected with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthurian_cycle" title="Arthurian cycle">Arthurian cycle</a>, appearing in them as the first keeper of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>. This idea first appears in <a href="/wiki/Robert_de_Boron" title="Robert de Boron">Robert de Boron</a>'s <i>Joseph d'Arimathie</i>, in which Joseph receives the Grail from an apparition of Jesus and sends it with his followers to Britain. This theme is elaborated upon in Boron's sequels and in subsequent Arthurian works penned by others. Later retellings of the story contend that Joseph of Arimathea himself travelled to Britain and became the first Christian bishop in the Isles, a claim Gigot charactierizes as a fable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gigot_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gigot-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gospel_of_Nicodemus">Gospel of Nicodemus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gospel of Nicodemus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Nicodemus" title="Gospel of Nicodemus">Gospel of Nicodemus</a></i>, a text appended to the <i>Acts of Pilate</i>, provides additional details about Joseph. For instance, after Joseph asked Pilate for the body of the Christ, and prepared the body with Nicodemus' help, Christ's body was delivered to a new tomb that Joseph had built for himself. In the <i>Gospel of Nicodemus</i>, the Jewish elders express anger at Joseph for burying the body of Christ, saying:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>And likewise Joseph also stepped out and said to them: Why are you angry against me because I begged the body of Jesus? Behold, I have put him in my new tomb, wrapping in clean linen; and I have rolled a stone to the door of the tomb. And you have acted not well against the just man, because you have not repented of crucifying him, but also have pierced him with a spear.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <i>Gospel of Nicodemus</i>. Translated by Alexander Walker.</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The Jewish elders then captured Joseph, and imprisoned him, and placed a seal on the door to his cell after first posting a guard. Joseph warned the elders, "The Son of God whom you hanged upon the cross, is able to deliver me out of your hands. All your wickedness will return upon you."</p>
<p>Once the elders returned to the cell, the seal was still in place, but Joseph was gone. The elders later discover that Joseph had returned to Arimathea. Having a change in heart, the elders desired to have a more civil conversation with Joseph about his actions and sent a letter of apology to him by means of seven of his friends. Joseph travelled back from Arimathea to Jerusalem to meet with the elders, where they questioned him about his escape. He told them this story;<br/></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>On the day of the Preparation, about the tenth hour, you shut me in, and I remained there the whole Sabbath in full. And when midnight came, as I was standing and praying, the house where you shut me in was hung up by the four corners, and there was a flashing of light in mine eyes. And I fell to the ground trembling. Then some one lifted me up from the place where I had fallen, and poured over me an abundance of water from the head even to the feet, and put round my nostrils the odour of a wonderful ointment, and rubbed my face with the water itself, as if washing me, and kissed me, and said to me, Joseph, fear not; but open thine eyes, and see who it is that speaks to thee. And looking, I saw Jesus; and being terrified, I thought it was a phantom. And with prayer and the commandments I spoke to him, and he spoke with me. And I said to him: Art thou Rabbi Elias? And he said to me: I am not Elias. And I said: Who art thou, my Lord? And he said to me: I am Jesus, whose body thou didst beg from Pilate, and wrap in clean linen; and thou didst lay a napkin on my face, and didst lay me in thy new tomb, and roll a stone to the door of the tomb. Then I said to him that was speaking to me: Show me, Lord, where I laid thee. And he led me, and showed me the place where I laid him, and the linen which I had put on him, and the napkin which I had wrapped upon his face; and I knew that it was Jesus. And he took hold of me with his hand, and put me in the midst of my house though the gates were shut, and put me in my bed, and said to me: Peace to thee! And he kissed me, and said to me: For forty days go not out of thy house; for, lo, I go to my brethren into Galilee.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <i>Gospel of Nicodemus</i>. Translated by Alexander Walker</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>According to the <i>Gospel of Nicodemus</i>, Joseph testified to the Jewish elders, and specifically to chief priests <a href="/wiki/Caiaphas" title="Caiaphas">Caiaphas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annas" title="Annas">Annas</a> that Jesus had risen from the dead and ascended to heaven and he indicated that others were raised from the dead at the resurrection of Christ (repeating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthew_27:52" title="Matthew 27:52">Matt 27:52</a>–<a href="/wiki/Matthew_27:53" title="Matthew 27:53">53</a>). He specifically identified the two sons of the high-priest Simeon (again in Luke 2:25–35). The elders Annas, Caiaphas, Nicodemus, and Joseph himself, along with <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel" title="Gamaliel">Gamaliel</a> under whom <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" title="Paul of Tarsus">Paul of Tarsus</a> studied, travelled to Arimathea to interview Simeon's sons Charinus and Lenthius.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_medieval_texts">Other medieval texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other medieval texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medieval interest in Joseph centered on two themes, that of Joseph as the founder of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">British Christianity</a> (even before it had taken hold in Rome), and that of Joseph as the original guardian of the Holy Grail.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Britain">Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Roman_Britain" title="Early centers of Christianity">Early centers of Christianity § Roman Britain</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="974" data-file-width="489" height="438" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg/220px-2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg/330px-2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg/440px-2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2nd_state_-_c1809.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a>'s Illustration "Joseph of Arimathea Among the Rocks of Albion" in its second state after Blake's 1773 original, engraved circa 1809</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Legends about the arrival of Christianity in Britain abounded during the Middle Ages. Early writers do not connect Joseph to this activity, however. <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> (AD 155–222) wrote in <i>Adversus Judaeos</i> that Britain had already received and accepted the Gospel in his lifetime, writing, "all the limits of the Spains, and the diverse nations of the Gauls, and the haunts of the Britons—inaccessible to the Romans, but subjugated to Christ."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tertullian does not say how the Gospel came to Britain before AD 222. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius of Caesarea</a>, (AD 260–340), one of the earliest and most comprehensive of church historians, wrote of Christ's disciples in <i>Demonstratio Evangelica,</i> saying that "some have crossed the Ocean and reached the Isles of Britain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Saint <a href="/wiki/Hilary_of_Poitiers" title="Hilary of Poitiers">Hilary of Poitiers</a> (AD 300–376) also wrote that the Apostles had built churches and that the Gospel had passed into Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The writings of Pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">Hippolytus</a> include a list of the <a href="/wiki/Seventy_disciples" title="Seventy disciples">seventy disciples</a> whom Jesus sent forth in Luke 10, one of which is <a href="/wiki/Aristobulus_of_Britannia" title="Aristobulus of Britannia">Aristobulus</a> of Romans 16:10, called "bishop of Britain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In none of these earliest references to Christianity’s arrival in Britain is Joseph of Arimathea mentioned. <a href="/wiki/William_of_Malmesbury" title="William of Malmesbury">William of Malmesbury</a>'s <i>De Antiquitate Glastoniensis Ecclesiae</i> ("On the Antiquity of the <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Abbey" title="Glastonbury Abbey">Church of Glastonbury</a>", circa 1125) has not survived in its original edition, and the stories involving Joseph of Arimathea are contained in subsequent editions that abound in interpolations placed by the Glastonbury monks "in order to increase the Abbey's prestige – and thus its pilgrim trade and prosperity" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In his <i>Gesta Regum Anglorum</i> ("History of The Kings of England", finished in 1125), William of Malmesbury wrote that Glastonbury Abbey was built by preachers sent by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eleuterus" title="Pope Eleuterus">Pope Eleuterus</a> to Britain, however also adding: "Moreover there are documents of no small credit, which have been discovered in certain places to the following effect: 'No other hands than those of the disciples of Christ erected the church of Glastonbury';" but here William did not explicitly link Glastonbury with Joseph of Arimathea, but instead emphasizes the possible role of <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Apostle" title="Philip the Apostle">Philip the Apostle</a>: "if Philip, the Apostle, preached to the Gauls, as Freculphus relates in the fourth chapter of his second book, it may be believed that he also planted the word on this side of the channel also." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989 A. W. Smith critically examined the accretion of legends around Joseph of Arimathea, by which the poem hymn of <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a> <i><a href="/wiki/And_did_those_feet_in_ancient_time" title="And did those feet in ancient time">And did those feet in ancient time</a></i> is commonly held as "an almost secret yet passionately held article of faith among certain otherwise quite orthodox Christians" and Smith concluded "that there was little reason to believe that an oral tradition concerning a visit made by Jesus to Britain existed before the early part of the twentieth century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sabine_Baring-Gould" title="Sabine Baring-Gould">Sabine Baring-Gould</a> recounted a Cornish story how "Joseph of Arimathea came in a boat to Cornwall, and brought the child Jesus with him, and the latter taught him how to extract the tin and purge it of its <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolfram</a>. This story possibly grew out of the fact that the Jews under the Angevin kings farmed the tin of Cornwall." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In its most developed version, Joseph, a tin merchant, visited Cornwall, accompanied by his nephew, the boy Jesus. C.C. Dobson (1879–1960) made a case for the authenticity of the Glastonbury <i>legenda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The case was argued more recently by Dr Gordon Strachan (1934–2010) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and by Dennis Price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_Grail">Holy Grail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Holy Grail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The legend that Joseph was given the responsibility of keeping the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> was the product of <a href="/wiki/Robert_de_Boron" title="Robert de Boron">Robert de Boron</a>, who essentially expanded upon stories from <i>Acts of Pilate</i>. In Boron's <i>Joseph d'Arimathe</i>, Joseph is imprisoned much as in the <i>Acts</i>, but it is the Grail that sustains him during his captivity. Upon his release he founds his company of followers, who take the Grail to Britain, though Joseph himself does not go. The origin of the association between Joseph and Britain is not entirely clear, though in subsequent romances such as <i><a href="/wiki/Perlesvaus" title="Perlesvaus">Perlesvaus</a></i>, Joseph himself travels to Britain, bringing relics with him. In the <a href="/wiki/Lancelot-Grail" title="Lancelot-Grail">Lancelot-Grail</a> Cycle, a vast Arthurian composition that took much from Robert, it is not Joseph but his son <a href="/wiki/Josephus_of_Arimathea" title="Josephus of Arimathea">Josephus</a> who is considered the primary holy man of Britain.</p>
<p>Later authors sometimes mistakenly or deliberately treated the Grail story as truth. Such stories were inspired by the account of <a href="/wiki/John_of_Glastonbury" title="John of Glastonbury">John of Glastonbury</a>, who assembled a chronicle of the history of Glastonbury Abbey around 1350 and who wrote that Joseph, when he came to Britain, brought with him vessels containing the blood and sweat of Christ (without using the word Grail).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This account inspired the future claims of the Grail, including the claim involving the <a href="/wiki/Nanteos_Cup" title="Nanteos Cup">Nanteos Cup</a> on display in the museum in <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a>; however, it should be noted that there is no reference to this tradition in ancient or medieval text. John of Glastonbury further claims that King Arthur was descended from Joseph, listing the following imaginative pedigree through King Arthur's mother:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Helaius, Nepos Joseph, Genuit Josus, Josue Genuit Aminadab, Aminadab Genuit Filium, qui Genuit Ygernam, de qua Rex Pen-Dragon, Genuit Nobilem et Famosum Regum Arthurum, per Quod Patet, Quod Rex Arthurus de Stirpe Joseph descendit.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> cited Joseph's missionary work in England when she told Roman Catholic bishops that the Church of England pre-dated the Roman Church in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_legends">Other legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Joseph set his walking staff on the ground to sleep, it miraculously took root, leafed out, and blossomed as the "<a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Thorn" title="Glastonbury Thorn">Glastonbury Thorn</a>". The retelling of such miracles encouraged the pilgrim trade at Glastonbury until the Abbey was dissolved in 1539, during the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mytheme" title="Mytheme">mytheme</a> of the staff that Joseph of Arimathea set in the ground at <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury" title="Glastonbury">Glastonbury</a>, which broke into leaf and flower as the <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Thorn" title="Glastonbury Thorn">Glastonbury Thorn</a> is a common miracle in <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiography</a>. Such a miracle is told of the Anglo-Saxon saint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etheldreda" title="Etheldreda">Etheldreda</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Continuing her flight to Ely, Etheldreda halted for some days at Alfham, near Wintringham, where she founded a church; and near this place occurred the "miracle of her staff." Wearied with her journey, she one day slept by the wayside, having fixed her staff in the ground at her head. On waking she found the dry staff had burst into leaf; it became an ash tree, the "greatest tree in all that country;" and the place of her rest, where a church was afterwards built, became known as "Etheldredestow."</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Richard John King, 1862, in: <i>Handbook of the Cathedrals of England; Eastern division: Oxford, Peterborough, Norwich, Ely, Lincoln</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Medieval interest in <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a> raised claims that Joseph was a relative of Jesus; specifically, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Mary</a>'s uncle, or according to some genealogies, Joseph's uncle. A genealogy for the family of Joseph of Arimathea and the history of his further adventures in the east provide material for <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_(heroic_literature)" title="Romance (heroic literature)">romances</a> <i>Estoire del Saint Graal</i>, <i>Perlesvaus</i>, and the <i>Queste del Saint Graal</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another legend, as recorded in <i><a href="/wiki/Flores_Historiarum" title="Flores Historiarum">Flores Historiarum</a></i> is that Joseph is in fact the <a href="/wiki/Wandering_Jew" title="Wandering Jew">Wandering Jew</a>, a man cursed by Jesus to walk the Earth until the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">Second Coming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arimathea">Arimathea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_of_Arimathea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arimathea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arimathea" title="Arimathea">Arimathea</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arimathea" title="Arimathea">Arimathea</a> itself is not otherwise documented, though it was "a town of Judea" according to Luke 23:51. Arimathea is usually identified with either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramleh" title="Ramleh">Ramleh</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ramathaim-Zophim" title="Ramathaim-Zophim">Ramathaim-Zophim</a>, where <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> came to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_(Bible)" title="Samuel (Bible)">Samuel</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Books_of_Samuel" title="Books of Samuel">1 Samuel</a></i> chapter 19).</p>
