<p><b>Flax</b> (also known as <b>common flax</b> or <b>linseed</b>), <i>Linum usitatissimum</i>, is a member of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Linum" title="Linum">Linum</a></i> in the family <a href="/wiki/Linaceae" title="Linaceae">Linaceae</a>. It is a food and fiber crop cultivated in cooler regions of the world. The textiles made from flax are known in the Western countries as <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>, and traditionally used for bed sheets, underclothes, and table linen. The oil is known as <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a>. In addition to referring to the plant itself, the word "flax" may refer to the unspun fibers of the flax plant. The plant species is known only as a cultivated plant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and appears to have been domesticated just once from the wild species <i><a href="/wiki/Linum_bienne" title="Linum bienne">Linum bienne</a></i>, called pale flax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Several other species in the genus <i>Linum</i> are similar in appearance to <i>L. usitatissimum</i>, cultivated flax, including some that have similar blue flowers, and others with white, yellow, or red flowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Some of these are perennial plants, unlike <i>L. usitatissimum</i>, which is an <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual plant</a>.</p>
<p>Cultivated flax plants grow to 1.2 m (3 ft 11 in) tall, with slender stems. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are <a href="/wiki/Glaucous" title="Glaucous">glaucous</a> green, slender <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanceolate" title="Lanceolate">lanceolate</a>, 20–40 mm long, and 3 mm broad.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are pure pale blue, 15–25 mm in diameter, with five petals. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is a round, dry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsule_(fruit)" title="Capsule (fruit)">capsule</a> 5–9 mm in diameter, containing several glossy brown <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> shaped like an <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> pip, 4–7 mm long.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest evidence of humans using wild flax as a <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> comes from the present day <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Republic of Georgia</a>, where spun, dyed, and knotted wild flax fibers were found in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dzudzuana_Cave&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dzudzuana Cave (page does not exist)">Dzudzuana Cave</a> and dated to the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a>, 30,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Flax was first domesticated in the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fu-8">[8]</a></sup> Evidence exists of a domesticated oilseed flax with increased seed size by 9,000 years ago from <a href="/wiki/Tell_Ramad" title="Tell Ramad">Tell Ramad</a> in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fu_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fu-8">[8]</a></sup> Use of the crop steadily spread, reaching as far as <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> by 5,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In China and India, domesticated flax was cultivated also by at least 5,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flax was extensively cultivated in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>, where the temple walls had paintings of flowering flax, and mummies were entombed in linen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Egyptian priests only wore linen, as flax was considered a symbol of purity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a> traded Egyptian linen throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> used it for their <a href="/wiki/Sail" title="Sail">sails</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> declined, so did flax production, but <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> revived the crop in the eighth century CE with laws designed to publicize the <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a> of linen textiles and the health of linseed oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wisseman_S..2C_p.125_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wisseman_S..2C_p.125-14">[14]</a></sup> Eventually, <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> became the major center of the linen industry in the European <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wisseman_S..2C_p.125_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wisseman_S..2C_p.125-14">[14]</a></sup> In North America, flax was introduced by the colonists and it flourished there,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> but by the early 20th century, cheap <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> and rising farm wages had caused production of flax to become concentrated in northern Russia, which came to provide 90% of the world's output. Since then, flax has lost its importance as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_crop" title="Commercial crop">commercial crop</a>, due to the easy availability of more durable fibers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Flax is grown for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flaxseed_oil" title="Flaxseed oil">oil</a>, used as a nutritional supplement, and as an ingredient in many <a href="/wiki/Wood_finishing" title="Wood finishing">wood-finishing</a> products. Flax is also grown as an <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a> in gardens. Flax fibers are used to make linen. The Latin species name <i>usitatissimum</i> means "most useful".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flax fibers are taken from the stem of the plant, and are two to three times as strong as those of cotton. Additionally, flax fibers are naturally smooth and straight. Europe and North America depended on flax for vegetable-based cloth until the 19th century, when cotton overtook flax as the most common plant used for making rag-based paper. Flax is grown on the Canadian prairies for linseed oil, which is used as a drying oil in paints and varnishes and in products such as <a href="/wiki/Linoleum" title="Linoleum">linoleum</a> and printing inks. In India, flax seed oil is also known as <i>alsi</i> in Hindi and <i>javas</i> in Marathi. It is mainly used in <a href="/wiki/Savji" title="Savji">Savji</a> curries such as mutton curries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flaxseeds"><span id="Flax_seeds"></span> Flaxseeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flaxseeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flaxseeds occur in two basic varieties: brown and yellow or golden (also known as golden linseeds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Most types have similar nutritional characteristics and equal numbers of short-chain <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid">omega-3 fatty acids</a>. The exception is a type of yellow flax called solin (trade name <a href="/wiki/Linola" title="Linola">Linola</a>), which has a completely different oil profile and is very low in omega-3 FAs. Flaxseeds produce a <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oil</a> known as flaxseed oil or <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a>, which is one of the oldest commercial oils. It is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edible_oil" title="Edible oil">edible oil</a> obtained by <a href="/wiki/Expeller_pressing" title="Expeller pressing">expeller pressing</a>, sometimes followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvent_extraction" title="Solvent extraction">solvent extraction</a>. Solvent-processed flaxseed oil has been used for many centuries as a <a href="/wiki/Drying_oil" title="Drying oil">drying oil</a> in painting and varnishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although brown flax can be consumed as readily as yellow, and has been for thousands of years, its better-known uses are in paints, for fiber, and for cattle feed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary">Culinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A 100-gram portion of ground flaxseed supplies about 534 calories, 41 g of fat, 28 g of fiber, and 20 g of protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flaxseed <a href="/wiki/Sprouting" title="Sprouting">sprouts</a> are edible, with a slightly spicy flavor. Excessive consumption of flaxseeds with inadequate water can cause bowel obstruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayo_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayo-21">[21]</a></sup> In northern India, flaxseed, called <i>tisi</i> or <i>alsi</i>, is traditionally roasted, powdered, and eaten with boiled rice, a little water, and a little salt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whole flaxseeds are chemically stable, but ground flaxseed can go <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rancidity" title="Rancidity">rancid</a> at room temperature in as little as one week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DieteticAss_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-DieteticAss-23">[23]</a></sup> Refrigeration and storage in sealed containers will keep ground flax from becoming rancid for a longer period; under conditions similar to those found in commercial bakeries, trained sensory panelists could not detect differences between bread made with freshly ground flax and bread made with milled flax stored for four months at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malcolmson_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malcolmson-24">[24]</a></sup> Milled flax is stable to oxidation when stored for nine months at room temperature if packed immediately without exposure to air and light<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-25">[25]</a></sup> and for 20 months at ambient temperatures under warehouse conditions.</p>
<p>Three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_phenol" title="Natural phenol">natural phenolic</a> glucosides, <a href="/wiki/Secoisolariciresinol_diglucoside" title="Secoisolariciresinol diglucoside">secoisolariciresinol diglucoside</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-coumaric_acid_glucoside" title="P-coumaric acid glucoside">p-coumaric acid glucoside</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ferulic_acid_glucoside&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ferulic acid glucoside (page does not exist)">ferulic acid glucoside</a>, can be found in commercial breads containing flaxseed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>In a 100 gram serving, flaxseed contains high levels (&gt; 19% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Value" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a>, DV) of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>, several <a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_minerals" title="Dietary minerals">dietary minerals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nd_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-nd-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-usda-28">[28]</a></sup> Flaxseeds are especially rich in <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">thiamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> (DVs above 90%).</p>
<p>As a percentage of total <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>, flaxseeds contain 54% omega-3 fatty acids (mostly <a href="/wiki/Alpha-Linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-Linolenic acid">ALA</a>), 18% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega-9_fatty_acids" title="Omega-9 fatty acids">omega-9 fatty acids</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a>), and 6% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega-6_fatty_acids" title="Omega-6 fatty acids">omega-6 fatty acids</a> (<a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic acid</a>); the seeds contain 9% <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated fat</a>, including 5% as <a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">palmitic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nd_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-nd-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-usda-28">[28]</a></sup> Flaxseed oil contains 53% 18:3 omega-3 fatty acids (mostly ALA) and 13% 18:2 omega-6 fatty acids.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Consuming flaxseed or its derivatives reduced total and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDL-cholesterol" title="LDL-cholesterol">LDL-cholesterol</a> in the blood, with greater benefits in women and those with high cholesterol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flaxseed and its oil have repeatedly been demonstrated to be nontoxic and are <a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a> for human consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Flax, like many common foods, contains small amounts of cyanogenic <a href="/wiki/Glycoside" title="Glycoside">glycosides</a>,;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> these are nontoxic when consumed in typical amounts, but may be toxic in large quantities from consuming <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staple foods</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banea_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Banea-32">[32]</a></sup> Typical concentrations (for example, 0.48% in a sample of defatted dehusked flaxseed meal) can be removed by special processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flax_fibers">Flax fibers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flax fibers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1712" data-file-width="2288" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG/220px-Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG/330px-Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG/440px-Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heckling_Shop_Irvine.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A heckling shop in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irvine,_Ayrshire" title="Irvine, Ayrshire">Irvine, Ayrshire</a>, used to prepare flax fibers</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Flax fiber is extracted from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bast_(biology)" title="Bast (biology)">bast</a> beneath the surface of the stem of the flax plant. Flax fiber is soft, lustrous, and flexible; bundles of fiber have the appearance of blonde hair, hence the description "flaxen". It is stronger than cotton fiber, but less elastic. The best grades are used for linen fabrics such as <a href="/wiki/Damask" title="Damask">damasks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">lace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bed_sheet" title="Bed sheet">sheeting</a>. Coarser grades are used for the manufacturing of <a href="/wiki/Twine" title="Twine">twine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">rope</a>, and historically for <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webbing_equipment" title="Webbing equipment">webbing equipment</a>. Flax fiber is a raw material used in the high-quality paper industry for the use of printed <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknotes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rolling_paper" title="Rolling paper">rolling paper</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarettes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tea_bag" title="Tea bag">tea bags</a>.</p>
<p>The use of flax fibers dates back tens of thousands of years and <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>, a refined textile made from flax fibers was widely worn by <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> priests over 4,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Industrial-scale flax fiber processing existed in antiquity, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze-age" title="Bronze-age">bronze-age</a> factory dedicated to flax processing discovered in <a href="/wiki/Euonymeia" title="Euonymeia">Euonymeia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaza2015_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaza2015-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Flax_mill" title="Flax mill">Flax mills</a> for spinning flaxen <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">yarn</a> were invented by <a href="/wiki/John_Kendrew_(inventor)" title="John Kendrew (inventor)">John Kendrew</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Porthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Porthouse (page does not exist)">Thomas Porthouse</a> of <a href="/wiki/Darlington" title="Darlington">Darlington</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, in 1787.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> New methods of processing flax have led to renewed interest in the use of flax as an industrial fiber.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> most suitable for flax, besides the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alluvial" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> kind, are deep <a href="/wiki/Loam" title="Loam">loams</a> containing a large proportion of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>. Flax is often found growing just above the waterline in <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberry</a> bogs. Heavy <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a> are unsuitable, as are soils of a <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravelly</a> or dry <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sandy</a> nature. Farming flax requires few <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>. Within eight weeks of sowing, the plant can reach 10–15 cm (3.9–5.9 in) in height and grows several centimeters per day under its optimal growth conditions, reaching 70–80 cm (28–31 in) within 50 days.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_flax_diseases" title="List of flax diseases">List of flax diseases</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting">Harvesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>Maturation</dt>
</dl>
<p>Flax is harvested for fiber production after about 100 days, or a month after the plants flower and two weeks after the seed capsules form. The bases of the plants begin to turn yellow. If the plants are still green, the seed will not be useful, and the fiber will be underdeveloped. The fiber degrades once the plants turn brown.</p>
<p>Flax grown for seed is allowed to mature until the seed capsules are yellow and just starting to split; it is then harvested in various ways. A <a href="/wiki/Combine_harvester" title="Combine harvester">combine harvester</a> may either cut only the heads of the plants, or the whole plant. These are then dried to extract the seed. The amount of weeds in the straw affects its marketability, and this, coupled with market prices, determines whether the farmer chooses to harvest the flax straw. If the flax straw is not harvested, it is typically burned, since the stalks are quite tough and decompose slowly (<i>i.e.</i>, not in a single season). Formed into <a href="/wiki/Windrow" title="Windrow">windrows</a> from the harvesting process, the straw often clogs up tillage and planting equipment. Flax straw that is not of sufficient quality for fiber uses can be baled to build shelters for farm animals, or sold as biofuel, or removed from the field in the spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emile_Claus001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1640" data-file-width="2112" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Emile_Claus001.jpg/220px-Emile_Claus001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Emile_Claus001.jpg/330px-Emile_Claus001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Emile_Claus001.jpg/440px-Emile_Claus001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emile_Claus001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>De vlasoogst</i> (1904) ("<i>Flax harvesting</i>") painting by <a href="/wiki/Emile_Claus" title="Emile Claus">Emile Claus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Museums_of_Fine_Arts_of_Belgium" title="Royal Museums of Fine Arts of Belgium">Royal Museums of Fine Arts of Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a></div>

<p>Two ways are used to harvest flax fiber, one involving mechanized equipment (combines), and a second method, more manual and targeting maximum fiber length.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting_for_fiber">Harvesting for fiber</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Harvesting for fiber">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical">Mechanical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mechanical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The mature plant is cut with mowing equipment, similar to hay harvesting, and raked into windrows. When dried sufficiently, a combine then harvests the seeds similar to wheat or oat harvesting.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manual">Manual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Manual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The plant is pulled up with the roots (not cut), so as to increase the fiber length. After this, the flax is allowed to dry, the seeds are removed, and it is then <a href="/wiki/Retting" title="Retting">retted</a>. Dependent upon climatic conditions, characteristics of the sown flax and fields, the flax remains on the ground between two weeks and two months for retting. As a result of alternating rain and the sun, an enzymatic action degrades the pectins which bind fibers to the straw. The farmers turn over the straw during retting to evenly rett the stalks. When the straw is retted and sufficiently dry, it is rolled up. It is then stored by farmers before extracting the fibers.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Threshing" title="Threshing">Threshing</a> is the process of removing the seeds from the rest of the plant. Separating the usable flax fibers from other components requires pulling the stems through a hackle and/or beating the plants to break them.</p>
<p>Flax processing is divided into two parts: the first part is generally done by the farmer, to bring the flax fiber into a fit state for general or common purposes. This can be performed by three machines: one for threshing out the seed, one for breaking and separating the straw (stem) from the fiber, and one for further separating the broken straw and matter from the fiber.</p>
<p>The second part of the process brings the flax into a state for the very finest purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">lace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambric" title="Cambric">cambric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Damask" title="Damask">damask</a>, and very fine <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>. This second part is performed by a refining machine.</p>

<p>Before the flax fibers can be spun into linen, they must be separated from the rest of the stalk. The first step in this process is <a href="/wiki/Retting" title="Retting">retting</a>, which is the process of rotting away the inner stalk, leaving the outer parts intact. At this point, straw, or coarse outer stem (<a href="/wiki/Cortex_(botany)" title="Cortex (botany)">cortex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epidermis_(botany)" title="Epidermis (botany)">epidermis</a>), is still remaining. To remove this, the flax is "broken", the straw is broken up into small, short bits, while the actual fiber is left unharmed. <a href="/wiki/Scutching" title="Scutching">Scutching</a> scrapes the outer straw from the fiber. The stems are then pulled through "hackles", which act like combs to remove the straw and some shorter fibers out of the long fiber.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retting_flax">Retting flax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Retting flax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several methods are used for retting flax. It can be retted in a pond, stream, field or tank. When the retting is complete, the bundles of flax feel soft and slimy, and quite a few fibers are standing out from the stalks. When wrapped around a finger, the inner woody part springs away from the fibers. Pond retting is the fastest. It consists of placing the flax in a pool of water which will not evaporate. It generally takes place in a shallow pool which will warm up dramatically in the sun; the process may take from a few days to a few weeks. Pond-retted flax is traditionally considered of lower quality, possibly because the product can become dirty, and is easily over-retted, damaging the fiber. This form of retting also produces quite an odor. Stream retting is similar to pool retting, but the flax is submerged in bundles in a stream or river. This generally takes two or three weeks longer than pond retting, but the end product is less likely to be dirty, does not smell as bad, and because the water is cooler, is less likely to be over-retted. Both pond and stream retting were traditionally used less because they pollute the waters used for the process.</p>
<p>In field retting, the flax is laid out in a large field, and dew is allowed to collect on it. This process normally takes a month or more, but is generally considered to provide the highest quality flax fibers,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and it produces the least pollution.</p>
<p>Retting can also be done in a plastic trash can or any type of water-tight container of wood, concrete, earthenware, or plastic. Metal containers will not work, as an acid is produced when retting, and it would <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrode" title="Corrode">corrode</a> the metal. If the water temperature is kept at 80 °F (27 °C), the retting process under these conditions takes 4 or 5 days. If the water is any colder, it takes longer. Scum collects at the top, and an odor is given off the same as in pond retting. 'Enzymatic' retting of flax has been researched as a technique to engineer fibers with specific properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dressing the flax is the process of removing the straw from the fibers. Dressing consists of three steps: breaking, scutching, and heckling. The breaking breaks up the straw. Some of the straw is scraped from the fibers in the scutching process, and finally, the fiber is pulled through heckles to remove the last bits of straw.</p>
<dl>
<dd>Breaking breaks up the straw into short segments.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Scutching" title="Scutching">Scutching</a> removes some of the <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a> from the fiber.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Heckling_(flax)" title="Heckling (flax)">Heckling</a> is pulling the fiber through various sizes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heckling_comb" title="Heckling comb">heckling combs</a> or heckles. A heckle is a bed of "nails"—sharp, long-tapered, tempered, polished steel pins driven into wooden blocks at regular spacing.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_modified_flax_contamination">Genetically modified flax contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Genetically modified flax contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_(%5E)_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1802" data-file-width="3000" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_%28%5E%29_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg/220px-Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_%28%5E%29_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_%28%5E%29_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg/330px-Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_%28%5E%29_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_%28%5E%29_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg/440px-Child_in_a_flax_field._Minnesota_%28%5E%29_-_NARA_-_283899.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In September 2009, it was reported that Canadian flax exports had been contaminated by a deregistered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetically_modified" title="Genetically modified">genetically modified</a> variety called Triffid which had food and feed safety approval in Canada and the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twp_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-twp-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdc-42">[42]</a></sup> However, Canadian growers and the Flax Council of Canada raised concerns about the marketability of this variety in Europe where a <a href="/wiki/Zero_tolerance" title="Zero tolerance">zero tolerance</a> policy regarding unapproved <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">Genetically modified organisms</a> exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Subsequently deregistered in 2010 and never grown commercially in Canada or the U.S.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-44">[44]</a></sup> Triffid stores were destroyed, but future exports and further tests at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan" title="University of Saskatchewan">University of Saskatchewan</a> proved that Triffid persisted among flax crops, possibly affecting future varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-44">[44]</a></sup> Canadian flaxseed cultivars were reconstituted with Triffid-free seed used to plant the 2014 crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twp_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-twp-41">[41]</a></sup> Laboratories are certified to test for the presence of Triffid at a level of one seed in 10,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdc-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolic_images">Symbolic images</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Symbolic images">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flax is the emblem of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and displayed by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>. In a coronet, it appeared on the reverse of the <a href="/wiki/One_pound_(British_coin)" title="One pound (British coin)">British one-pound coin</a> to represent Northern Ireland on coins minted in 1986, 1991, and 2014. Flax also represents Northern Ireland on the badge of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> and on various logos associated with it.</p>
<p>Common flax is the national flower of <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>.</p>
<p>In early tellings of the <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_Beauty" title="Sleeping Beauty">Sleeping Beauty</a> tale, such as "<a href="/wiki/Sun,_Moon,_and_Talia" title="Sun, Moon, and Talia">Sun, Moon, and Talia</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Basile" title="Giambattista Basile">Giambattista Basile</a>, the princess pricks her finger not on a spindle, but on a sliver of flax, which is later sucked out by her children conceived as she sleeps.</p>
