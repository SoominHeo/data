<p><b>Amarna</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">العمارنة <i>al-‘amārnah</i></span>‎‎) is an extensive <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> site that represents the remains of the capital city newly established and built by the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">Pharaoh</a> <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">Akhenaten</a> of the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eighteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eighteenth dynasty of Egypt">Eighteenth Dynasty</a>, and abandoned shortly after his death (1332 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The name for the city employed by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptians</a> is written as <b>Akhetaten</b> (or <b>Akhetaton</b>—transliterations vary) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_(language)" title="English (language)">English</a> transliteration. Akhetaten means "<a href="/wiki/Akhet_(hieroglyph_and_season)" title="Akhet (hieroglyph and season)">Horizon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aten" title="Aten">Aten</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidp125_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-davidp125-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area is located on the east bank of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a> in the modern Egyptian province of <a href="/wiki/Minya_Governorate" title="Minya Governorate">Minya</a>, some 58 km (36 mi) south of the city of <a href="/wiki/Minya,_Egypt" title="Minya, Egypt">al-Minya</a>, 312 km (194 mi) south of the Egyptian capital <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> and 402 km (250 mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Luxor" title="Luxor">Luxor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The city of Deir Mawas lies directly west across from the site of Amarna. Amarna, on the east side, includes several modern villages, chief of which are el-Till in the north and el-Hagg Qandil in the south.</p>
<p>The area was also occupied during later <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Christian</a> times; excavations to the south of the city have found several structures from this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-roman-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name Amarna comes from the Beni Amran tribe that lived in the region and founded a few settlements. The ancient Egyptian name was Akhetaten.</p>
<p>(This site should be distinguished from Tell Amarna in Syria, a <a href="/wiki/Halaf_culture" title="Halaf culture">Halaf period</a> archaeological <a href="/wiki/Tell_(archaeology)" title="Tell (archaeology)">tell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>English Egyptologist, Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Gardner_Wilkinson" title="John Gardner Wilkinson">John Gardner Wilkinson</a> visited Amarna twice in the 1820s and identified it as '<b>Alabastron'</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> following the sometimes contradictory descriptions of Roman-era authors <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> (<i>On Stones</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MatHyer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MatHyer-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MatInd_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MatInd-8">[8]</a></sup> although he was not sure about the identification and suggested <a href="/wiki/Nekhen" title="Nekhen">Kom el-Ahmar</a> as an alternative location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ModEg_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ModEg-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The area of the city was effectively a virgin site, and it was in this city that the Akhetaten described as the Aten's</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"seat of the First Occasion, which he had made for himself that he might rest in it."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>It may be that the Royal Wadi's resemblance to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyph" title="Egyptian hieroglyph">hieroglyph</a> for <i>horizon</i> showed that this was the place to found the city.</p>
<p>The city was built as the new capital of the Pharaoh Akhenaten, dedicated to his <a href="/wiki/Atenism" title="Atenism">new religion</a> of worship to the <a href="/wiki/Aten" title="Aten">Aten</a>. Construction started in or around Year 5 of his reign (1346 BC) and was probably completed by Year 9 (1341 BC), although it became the capital city two years earlier. To speed up construction of the city most of the buildings were constructed out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mud-brick" title="Mud-brick">mud-brick</a>, and white washed. The most important buildings were faced with local stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is the only ancient <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> which preserves great details of its internal plan, in large part because the city was abandoned after the death of Akhenaten, when Akhenaten's son, King <a href="/wiki/Tutankhamun" title="Tutankhamun">Tutankhamun</a>, decided to leave the city and return to his birthplace in Thebes (modern Luxor). The city seems to have remained active for a decade or so after his death, and a shrine to <a href="/wiki/Horemheb" title="Horemheb">Horemheb</a> indicates that it was at least partially occupied at the beginning of his reign,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kemp_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-kemp-11">[11]</a></sup> if only as a source for building material elsewhere. Once it was abandoned it remained uninhabited until Roman settlement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roman_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-roman-4">[4]</a></sup> began along the edge of the Nile. However, due to the unique circumstances of its creation and abandonment, it is questionable how representative of ancient Egyptian cities it actually is. Akhetaten was hastily constructed and covered an area of approximately 8 miles (13 km) of territory on the east bank of the Nile River; on the west bank, land was set aside to provide crops for the city's population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidp125_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-davidp125-2">[2]</a></sup> The entire city was encircled with a total of 14 boundary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stela" title="Stela">stelae</a> detailing Akhenaten's conditions for the establishment of this new capital city of Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidp125_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-davidp125-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest dated stele from Akhenaten's new city is known to be <i>Boundary stele K</i> which is dated to Year 5, IV Peret (or month 8), day 13 of Akhenaten's reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldredp47_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aldredp47-12">[12]</a></sup> (Most of the original 14 boundary stelae have been badly eroded.) It preserves an account of Akhenaten's foundation of this city. The document records the pharaoh's wish to have several temples of the Aten to be erected here, for several royal tombs to be created in the eastern hills of Akhetaten for himself, his chief wife <a href="/wiki/Nefertiti" title="Nefertiti">Nefertiti</a> and his eldest daughter <a href="/wiki/Meritaten" title="Meritaten">Meritaten</a> as well as his explicit command that when he was dead, he would be brought back to Akhetaten for burial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Boundary stela K introduces a description of the events that were being celebrated at Akhetaten:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>His Majesty mounted a great chariot of electrum, like the <a href="/wiki/Aten" title="Aten">Aten</a> when He rises on the horizon and fills the land with His love, and took a goodly road to Akhetaten, the place of origin, which [the Aten] had created for Himself that he might be happy therein. It was His son Wa'enrē [i.e. Akhenaten] who founded it for Him as His monument when His Father commanded him to make it. Heaven was joyful, the earth was glad every heart was filled with delight when they beheld him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldredp48_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-aldredp48-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This text then goes on to state that Akhenaten made a great oblation to the god Aten "and this is the theme [of the occasion] which is illustrated in the <a href="/wiki/Lunette_(stele)" title="Lunette (stele)">lunettes</a> of the stelae where he stands with his queen and eldest daughter before an altar heaped with offerings under the Aten, while it shines upon him rejuvenating his body with its rays."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldredp48_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-aldredp48-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Site_and_plan">Site and plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Site and plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located on the east bank of the Nile, the ruins of the city are laid out roughly north to south along a "Royal Road", now referred to as "Sikhet es-Sultan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterson81_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-waterson81-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grundon92_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-grundon92-16">[16]</a></sup> The Royal residences are generally to the north, in what is known as the <a href="/wiki/North_City,_Amarna" title="North City, Amarna">North City</a>, with a central administration and religious area and the south of the city is made up of residential suburbs..</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_City">North City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="510" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg/125px-Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg/188px-Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg/250px-Egyptian_-_Seal_Ring_with_the_Name_of_Akhenaten_-_Walters_42201_-_Side_A.jpg 2x" width="125"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North_City,_Amarna" title="North City, Amarna">North City, Amarna</a></div>
<p>If one approached the city of Amarna from the north by river the first buildings past the norther boundary stele would be the <a href="/wiki/North_Riverside_Palace" title="North Riverside Palace">North Riverside Palace</a>. This building ran all the way up to the waterfront and was likely the main residence of the Royal Family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Located within the North City area is the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Palace_(Amarna)" title="Northern Palace (Amarna)">Northern Palace</a>, the main residence of the Royal Family. Between this and the central city, the Northern Suburb was initially a prosperous area with large houses, but the house size decreased and became poorer the further from the road they were.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grundon92_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-grundon92-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_City">Central City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Central City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most of the important ceremonial and administrative buildings were located in the central city. Here the <a href="/wiki/Great_Temple_of_the_Aten" title="Great Temple of the Aten">Great Temple of the Aten</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Small_Aten_Temple" title="Small Aten Temple">Small Aten Temple</a> were used for religious functions and between these the Great Royal Palace and Royal Residence were the ceremonial residence of the King and Royal Family, and were linked by a bridge or ramp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Located behind the Royal Residence was the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Correspondence_of_Pharaoh" title="Bureau of Correspondence of Pharaoh">Bureau of Correspondence of Pharaoh</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amarna_Letters" title="Amarna Letters">Amarna Letters</a> were found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>This area was probably the first area to be completed, and had at least two phases of construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterson81_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-waterson81-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_suburbs">Southern suburbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Southern suburbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To the south of the city was the area now referred to as the <i>Southern Suburbs</i>. It contained the estates of many of the city's powerful nobles, including <a href="/wiki/Nakhtpaaten" title="Nakhtpaaten">Nakhtpaaten</a> (Chief Minister), Ranefer, <a href="/wiki/Panehesy" title="Panehesy">Panehesy</a> (High Priest of the Aten) and Ramose (Master of Horses). This area also held the studio of the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Thutmose_(sculptor)" title="Thutmose (sculptor)">Thutmose</a>, where the famous bust of Nefertiti was found in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further to the south of the city was <a href="/wiki/Kom_el-Nana" title="Kom el-Nana">Kom el-Nana</a>, an enclosure, usually referred to as a <i>sun-shade</i>, and was probably built as a sun-temple.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and then the <a href="/wiki/Maru-Aten" title="Maru-Aten">Maru-Aten</a>, which was a palace or sun-temple originally thought to have been constructed for <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">Akhenaten</a>'s queen <a href="/wiki/Kiya" title="Kiya">Kiya</a>, but on her death her name and images were altered to those of <a href="/wiki/Meritaten" title="Meritaten">Meritaten</a>, his daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="City_outskirts">City outskirts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: City outskirts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Surrounding the city and marking its extent, the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Stelae_of_Akhenaten" title="Boundary Stelae of Akhenaten">Boundary Stelae</a> (each a rectangle of carved rock on the cliffs on both sides of the Nile) describing the founding of the city are a primary source of information about it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Away from the city Akhenaten's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Wadi_and_Tombs" title="Royal Wadi and Tombs">Royal necropolis</a> was started in a narrow valley to the east of the city, hidden in the cliffs. Only one tomb was completed, and was used by an unnamed Royal Wife, and Akhenaten's tomb was hastily used to hold him and likely <a href="/wiki/Meketaten" title="Meketaten">Meketaten</a>, his second daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the cliffs to the north and south of the Royal Wadi, the nobles of the city constructed their <a href="/wiki/Tombs_of_the_Nobles_(Amarna)" title="Tombs of the Nobles (Amarna)">Tombs</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_in_ancient_Amarna.2FAkhetaten">Life in ancient Amarna/Akhetaten</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life in ancient Amarna/Akhetaten">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TutankhamunBerlin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="498" data-file-width="371" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/TutankhamunBerlin.jpg/220px-TutankhamunBerlin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/TutankhamunBerlin.jpg/330px-TutankhamunBerlin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/TutankhamunBerlin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TutankhamunBerlin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tutankamun Amarna portrait. <a href="/wiki/Altes_Museum" title="Altes Museum">Altes Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Much of what is known about Amarna's founding is due to the preservation of a series of official boundary stelae (13 are known) ringing the perimeter of the city. These are cut into the cliffs on both sides of the Nile (10 on the east, 3 on the west) and record the events of Akhetaten (Amarna) from founding to just before its fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>To make the move from Thebes to Amarna, Akhenaten needed the support of the military. Ay, one of Akhenaten's principal advisors, exercised great influence in this area because his father <a href="/wiki/Yuya" title="Yuya">Yuya</a> had been an important military leader. Additionally, everyone in the military had grown up together, they had been a part of the richest and most successful period in Egypt's history under Akhenaten's father, so loyalty among the ranks was strong and unwavering. Perhaps most importantly, "it was a military whose massed ranks the king took every opportunity to celebrate in temple reliefs, first at Thebes and later at Amarna."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_life">Religious life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Religious life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>While the reforms of Akhenaten are generally believed to have been oriented towards a sort of monotheism, this may be rather oversimplified. Archaeological evidence shows other deities were also worshipped, even at the centre of the Aten cult – if not officially, then at least by the people who lived and worked there.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>..at Akhetaten itself, recent excavation by <a href="/wiki/Barry_Kemp_(Egyptologist)" title="Barry Kemp (Egyptologist)">Kemp</a> (2008: 41-46) has shown the presence of objects that depict gods, goddesses and symbols that belong to the traditional field of personal belief. So many examples of <a href="/wiki/Bes" title="Bes">Bes</a>, the grotesque dwarf figure who warded off evil spirits, have been found, as well as of the goddess-monster, <a href="/wiki/Taweret" title="Taweret">Taweret</a>, part crocodile, part hippopotamus, who was associated with childbirth. Also in the royal workmen’s village at Akhetaten, stelae dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shed_(deity)" title="Shed (deity)">Shed</a> have been discovered (Watterson 1984: 158 and 208).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Amarna art-style broke with long-established Egyptian conventions. Unlike the strict idealistic <a href="/wiki/Formalism_(art)" title="Formalism (art)">formalism</a> of previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Ancient_Egypt" title="Art of Ancient Egypt">Egyptian art</a>, it depicted its subjects more realistically. These included informal scenes, such as intimate portrayals of affection within the royal family or playing with their children, and no longer portrayed women as lighter coloured than men. The art also had a realism that sometimes borders on caricature.</p>
<p>While the worship of <a href="/wiki/Aten" title="Aten">Aten</a> was later referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amarna_heresy" title="Amarna heresy">Amarna heresy</a> and suppressed, this art had a more lasting legacy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rediscovery_and_excavation">Rediscovery and excavation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amarna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rediscovery and excavation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="440" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png/150px-Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png/225px-Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png/300px-Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amarna_Akkadian_letter.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of the <a href="/wiki/Amarna_letters" title="Amarna letters">Amarna letters</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first western mention of the city was made in 1714 by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Sicard" title="Claude Sicard">Claude Sicard</a>, a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priest</a> who was travelling through the Nile Valley, and described the boundary stela from Amarna. As with much of Egypt, it was visited by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s <i>corps de savants</i> in 1798–1799, who prepared the first detailed map of Amarna, which was subsequently published in <i><a href="/wiki/Description_de_l%27%C3%89gypte" title="Description de l'Égypte">Description de l'Égypte</a></i> between 1821 and 1830.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amarnamapping_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-amarnamapping-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>After this European exploration continued in 1824 when Sir <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Gardiner_Wilkinson" title="John Gardiner Wilkinson">John Gardiner Wilkinson</a> explored and mapped the city remains. The copyist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hay_(Egyptologist)" title="Robert Hay (Egyptologist)">Robert Hay</a> and his surveyor G. Laver visited the locality and uncovered several of the Southern Tombs from sand drifts, recording the reliefs in 1833. The copies made by Hay and Laver languish largely unpublished in the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a>, where an ongoing project to identify their locations is underway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> expedition led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Lepsius" title="Richard Lepsius">Richard Lepsius</a> visited the site in 1843 and 1845, and recorded the visible monuments and topography of Amarna in two separate visits over a total of twelve days, using drawings and paper squeezes. The results were ultimately published in <i>Denkmäler aus Ägypten und Äthiopien</i> between 1849 and 1913, including an improved map of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amarnamapping_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-amarnamapping-28">[28]</a></sup> Despite being somewhat limited in accuracy, the engraved <i>Denkmäler</i> plates formed the basis for scholastic knowledge and interpretation of many of the scenes and inscriptions in the private tombs and some of the Boundary Stelae for the rest of the century. The records made by these early explorers teams are of immense importance since many of these remains were later destroyed or otherwise lost.</p>
<p>In 1887 a local woman digging for <i><a href="/wiki/Sebakh" title="Sebakh">sebakh</a></i> uncovered a cache of over 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform tablets</a> (now commonly known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amarna_Letters" title="Amarna Letters">Amarna Letters</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> These tablets recorded select <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomatic</a> correspondence of the Pharaoh and were predominantly written in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> commonly used during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age" title="Late Bronze Age">Late Bronze Age</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a> for such communication. This discovery led to the recognition of the importance of the site, and lead to a further increase in exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grundon9091_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-grundon9091-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1891 and 1892 <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Barsanti" title="Alessandro Barsanti">Alessandro Barsanti</a> 'discovered' and cleared the king's tomb (although it was probably known to the local population from about 1880).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Around the same time Sir <a href="/wiki/Flinders_Petrie" title="Flinders Petrie">Flinders Petrie</a> worked for one season at Amarna, working independently of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egypt_Exploration_Fund" title="Egypt Exploration Fund">Egypt Exploration Fund</a>. He excavated primarily in the Central City, investigating the <a href="/wiki/Great_Temple_of_the_Aten" title="Great Temple of the Aten">Great Temple of the Aten</a>, the Great Official Palace, the King's House, the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Correspondence_of_Pharaoh" title="Bureau of Correspondence of Pharaoh">Bureau of Correspondence of Pharaoh</a> and several private houses. Although frequently amounting to little more than a sondage, Petrie's excavations revealed additional cuneiform tablets, the remains of several glass factories, and a great quantity of discarded <a href="/wiki/Faience" title="Faience">faience</a>, glass and ceramic in sifting the palace rubbish heaps (including Mycenaean sherds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grundon9091_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-grundon9091-31">[31]</a></sup> By publishing his results and reconstructions rapidly, Petrie was able to stimulate further interest in the site's potential.</p>
<p>The copyist and artist <a href="/wiki/N._de_Garis_Davies" title="N. de Garis Davies">Norman de Garis Davies</a> published drawn and photographic descriptions of private tombs and boundary stelae from Amarna from 1903 to 1908. These books were republished by the EES in 2006.</p>
<p>In the early years of the 20th century (1907 to 1914) the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deutsche_Orientgesellschaft" title="Deutsche Orientgesellschaft">Deutsche Orientgesellschaft</a> expedition, led by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Borchardt" title="Ludwig Borchardt">Ludwig Borchardt</a>, excavated extensively throughout the North and South suburbs of the city. The famous bust of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nefertiti_bust" title="Nefertiti bust">Nefertiti</a>, now in Berlin's <a href="/wiki/Neues_Museum" title="Neues Museum">Ägyptisches Museum</a>, was discovered amongst other sculptural artefacts in the workshop of the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Thutmose_(sculptor)" title="Thutmose (sculptor)">Thutmose</a>. The outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> in August 1914 terminated the German excavations.</p>
<p>From 1921 to 1936 an Egypt Exploration Society expedition returned to excavation at Amarna under the direction of T.E. Peet, Sir <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Woolley" title="Leonard Woolley">Leonard Woolley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Frankfort" title="Henri Frankfort">Henri Frankfort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Glanville" title="Stephen Glanville">Stephen Glanville</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Pendlebury" title="John Pendlebury">John Pendlebury</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mary_Chubb" title="Mary Chubb">Mary Chubb</a> served as the digs administrator. The renewed investigations were focused on religious and royal structures.</p>
<p>During the 1960s the Egyptian Antiquities Organization (now the <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Council_of_Antiquities" title="Supreme Council of Antiquities">Supreme Council of Antiquities</a>) undertook a number of excavations at Amarna.</p>
<p>Exploration of the city continues to the present, currently under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Barry_Kemp_(Egyptologist)" title="Barry Kemp (Egyptologist)">Barry Kemp</a> (Reader in Egyptology, University of Cambridge, England) under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Egypt_Exploration_Society" title="Egypt Exploration Society">Egypt Exploration Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kemp_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-kemp-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1980 a separate expedition led by Geoffrey Martin described and copied the reliefs from the Royal Tomb, later publishing its findings together with objects thought to have come from the tomb. This work was published in 2 volumes by the EES.</p>
<p>In 2007, the continuing EES exploration discovered a <a href="/wiki/Southern_Tombs_Cemetery" title="Southern Tombs Cemetery">cemetery</a> of private individuals, close to the southern tombs of the Nobles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
