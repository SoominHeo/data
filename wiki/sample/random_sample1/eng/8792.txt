<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, the <b>Amazons</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀμαζόνες</span>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">Amazónes</span></i>, singular <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀμαζών</span>, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">Amazōn</span></i>) were a race of women warriors. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scythian" title="Scythian">Scythian</a> women were the real-world basis for the myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> reported that they were related to the <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> (an <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian people</a>) and placed them in a region bordering <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatia</a> (modern territory of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>). Other historiographers place them in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or sometimes <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRBM_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DGRBM-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable queens of the Amazons are <a href="/wiki/Penthesilea" title="Penthesilea">Penthesilea</a>, who participated in the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>, and her sister <a href="/wiki/Hippolyta" title="Hippolyta">Hippolyta</a>, whose magical girdle, given to her by her father <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a>, was the object of one of the <a href="/wiki/Labours_of_Hercules" title="Labours of Hercules">labours of Hercules</a>. Amazon warriors were often depicted in battle with Greek warriors in <a href="/wiki/Amazonomachy" title="Amazonomachy">amazonomachies</a> in classical art.</p>
<p>The Amazons have become associated with many historical people throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Empire_period" title="Roman Empire period">Roman Empire period</a> and <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Roman_historiography" title="Roman historiography">Roman historiography</a>, there are various accounts of Amazon raids in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> From the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, their name has become a term for female warriors in general. Amazons were said to have founded the cities and temples of Smyrna, Sinope, Cyme, Gryne, Ephesus, Pitania, Magnesia, Clete, Pygela, Latoreria and Amastris; according to legend, the Amazons also invented the <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wonder_Woman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wonder_Woman-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of the word is uncertain. It may be derived from an <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">ethnonym</a> <i>*ha-mazan-</i> "warriors", a word attested indirectly through a derivation, a denominal verb in <a href="/wiki/Hesychius_of_Alexandria" title="Hesychius of Alexandria">Hesychius of Alexandria</a>'s gloss <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">«ἁμαζακάραν· πολεμεῖν. Πέρσαι»</span> ("<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">hamazakaran</span></i>: 'to make war' in Persian"), where it appears together with the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">Indo-Iranian</a> root <i>*kar-</i> "make" (from which <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a></i> is also derived).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternatively, a Greek derivation from <i><span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">*ṇ-mṇ-gw-jon-es</span></i> "manless, without husbands" (<a href="/wiki/Privative_a" title="Privative a"><i>a-</i> privative</a> and a derivation of <i><a href="/wiki/Man_(word)" title="Man (word)">*man-</a></i> also found in Slavic <i>muzh</i>) has been proposed, an explanation deemed "unlikely" by <a href="/wiki/Hjalmar_Frisk" title="Hjalmar Frisk">Hjalmar Frisk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> 19th century scholarship also connected the term to the ethnonym <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A further explanation proposes Iranian *<i>ama-janah</i> "virility-killing" as source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Hittite researcher Friedrich Cornelius assumes that there had been the land Azzi with the capital Chajasa in the area of the Thermodon-Iris Delta on the coast of the Black Sea. He brings its residents in direct relation to the Amazons, namely based on its name (woman of the land Azzi = 'Am'+ 'Azzi' = Amazon) and its customs (matriarchal custom of promiscuous sexual intercourse, even with blood relatives). The location of that land as well as his conclusions are controversial. — Gerhard Pollauer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GP107_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-GP107-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Among Classical Greeks, <i>amazon</i> was given a <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">folk etymology</a> as originating from <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">a-</span></i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀ-</span>) and <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">mazos</span></i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μαζός</span>), "without <a href="/wiki/Breast" title="Breast">breast</a>", connected with an <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">etiological</a> tradition once claimed by <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Marcus Justinus</a> who alleged that Amazons had their right <a href="/wiki/Mastectomy" title="Mastectomy">breast cut off</a> or <a href="/wiki/Breast_ironing" title="Breast ironing">burnt out</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There is no indication of such a practice in ancient works of art,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayor-12">[12]</a></sup> in which the Amazons are always represented with both breasts, although the left is frequently covered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mayor-12">[12]</a></sup> Adrienne Mayor suggests the origin of this myth was due to the word's etymology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mayor-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>The legendary Amazons were thought to have lived in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontus" title="Pontus">Pontus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which is part of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> near the southern shore of the Euxine Sea (the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>). There they formed an independent kingdom under the government of a queen named <a href="/wiki/Hippolyta" title="Hippolyta">Hippolyta</a> or Hippolyte ("loose, unbridled mare"). This area is known to have been occupied in the Late Bronze Age by a <a href="/wiki/Transhumance" title="Transhumance">transhumant</a> group known to the Hittites as the <a href="/wiki/Kaskians" title="Kaskians">Kaŝka</a>; though they were not directly known to Greeks, modern archaeologists have determined that they finally defeated their enemies, the Hittites, about 1200 BC; they left no inscriptions. The Amazons were supposed to have founded many towns, amongst them <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">Sinope</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paphos" title="Paphos">Paphos</a>. According to the dramatist <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>, in the distant past they had lived in <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythia</a> (modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" title="Crimean Peninsula">Crimea</a>), at the <i>Palus Maeotis</i> ("Lake Maeotis", the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">Sea of Azov</a>). According to <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, the Amazons lived in and about the <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Don river</a>, which the Greeks called the Tanais; but which was called by the Scythians the "Amazon". The Amazons later moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Themiscyra_(mythology)" title="Themiscyra (mythology)">Themiscyra</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Terme" title="Terme">Terme</a>) on the River <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodon" title="Thermodon">Thermodon</a> (the Terme river in northern Turkey). <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> called them <i>Androktones</i> ("killers of men"), and he stated that in the Scythian language they were called <i>Oiorpata</i>, which he asserted had this meaning.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth">Myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some versions of the myth, no men were permitted to have sexual encounters or reside in Amazon country; but once a year, in order to prevent their race from dying out, they visited the <a href="/wiki/Gargareans" title="Gargareans">Gargareans</a>, a neighbouring tribe. The male children who were the result of these visits were either killed, sent back to their fathers or <a href="/wiki/Child_abandonment" title="Child abandonment">exposed in the wilderness</a> to fend for themselves; the girls were kept and brought up by their mothers, and trained in agricultural pursuits, hunting, and the art of war. In other versions when the Amazons went to war they would not kill all the men. Some they would take as slaves, and once or twice a year they would have sex with their slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The intermarriage of Amazons and men from other tribes was also used to explain the origin of various people; for example, the story of the Amazons settling with the Scythians (Herodotus, <i>Histories</i> 4.110.1-117.1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, the Amazons were referred to as <i>Antianeirai</i> ("those who fight like men").</p>
<p>The Amazons appear in <a href="/wiki/Greek_art" title="Greek art">Greek art</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Greece" title="Archaic Greece">Archaic period</a> and in connection with several Greek legends. They invaded <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">Lycia</a>, but were defeated by <a href="/wiki/Bellerophon" title="Bellerophon">Bellerophon</a>, who was sent against them by <a href="/wiki/Iobates" title="Iobates">Iobates</a>, the king of that country, in the hope that he might meet his death at their hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The tomb of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myrine" title="Myrine">Myrine</a> is mentioned in the <i>Iliad</i>; later interpretation made of her an Amazon: according to <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Queen Myrine led her Amazons to victory against <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a> and much of <a href="/wiki/Gorgon" title="Gorgon">Gorgon</a>.</p>
<p>They attacked the <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygians</a>, who were assisted by <a href="/wiki/Priam" title="Priam">Priam</a>, then a young man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In his later years, however, towards the end of the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>, his old opponents took his side against the Greeks under their queen <a href="/wiki/Penthesilea" title="Penthesilea">Penthesilea</a> "of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thracian</a> birth", who was slain by <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the <a href="/wiki/Labours_of_Hercules" title="Labours of Hercules">tasks imposed upon Hercules</a> by <a href="/wiki/Eurystheus" title="Eurystheus">Eurystheus</a> was to obtain possession of the <a href="/wiki/Girdle" title="Girdle">girdle</a> of the Amazonian queen <a href="/wiki/Hippolyta" title="Hippolyta">Hippolyta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He was accompanied by his friend <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>, who carried off the princess <a href="/wiki/Antiope_(Amazon)" title="Antiope (Amazon)">Antiope</a>, sister of Hippolyta, an incident which led to a retaliatory invasion of <a href="/wiki/Attica" title="Attica">Attica</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> in which Antiope perished fighting by the side of Theseus. In some versions, however, Theseus marries Hippolyta and in others, he marries Antiope and she does not die; by this marriage with the Amazon Theseus had a son <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_(son_of_Theseus)" title="Hippolytus (son of Theseus)">Hippolytus</a>. The battle between the Athenians and Amazons is often commemorated in an entire genre of art, <a href="/wiki/Amazonomachy" title="Amazonomachy">amazonomachy</a>, in marble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bas-relief" title="Bas-relief">bas-reliefs</a> such as from the <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a> or the sculptures of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Halicarnassus" title="Mausoleum of Halicarnassus">Mausoleum of Halicarnassus</a>.</p>

<p>The Amazons are also said to have undertaken an expedition against the <a href="/wiki/Snake_Island_(Black_Sea)" title="Snake Island (Black Sea)">island of Leuke</a>, at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>, where the ashes of Achilles had been deposited by <a href="/wiki/Thetis" title="Thetis">Thetis</a>. The ghost of the dead hero appeared and so terrified the horses, that they threw and trampled upon the invaders, who were forced to retire. <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a> is said to have found them in the army of <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_VI_of_Pontus" title="Mithridates VI of Pontus">Mithridates</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>They are heard of in the time of Alexander, when some of the king's biographers make mention of Amazon Queen <a href="/wiki/Thalestris" title="Thalestris">Thalestris</a> visiting him and becoming a mother by him (the story is known from the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Romance" title="Alexander Romance">Alexander Romance</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, several other biographers of Alexander dispute the claim, including the highly regarded <a href="/wiki/Secondary_source" title="Secondary source">secondary source</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>. In his writing he makes mention of a moment when Alexander's secondary naval commander, <a href="/wiki/Onesicritus" title="Onesicritus">Onesicritus</a>, was reading the Amazon passage of his Alexander history to King <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">Lysimachus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> who was on the original expedition: the king smiled at him and said "And where was I, then?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Roman writer <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s characterization of the <a href="/wiki/Volsci" title="Volsci">Volscian</a> warrior maiden <a href="/wiki/Camilla_(mythology)" title="Camilla (mythology)">Camilla</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i> borrows heavily from the myth of the Amazons.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">Jordanes</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Getica" title="Getica">Getica</a></i> (c. 560), purporting to give the earliest history of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, relates that the Goths' ancestors, descendants of <a href="/wiki/Magog_(Bible)" title="Magog (Bible)">Magog</a>, originally dwelt within Scythia, on the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">Sea of Azov</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Dnieper" title="Dnieper">Dnieper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Don Rivers</a>. After a few centuries, following an incident where the Goths' women successfully fended off a raid by a neighboring tribe, while the menfolk were off campaigning against Pharaoh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesosis" title="Vesosis">Vesosis</a>, the women formed their own army under <a href="/wiki/Marpesia" title="Marpesia">Marpesia</a> and crossed the Don, invading Asia. Her sister <a href="/wiki/Lampedo" title="Lampedo">Lampedo</a> remained in Europe to guard the homeland. They procreated with men once a year. These Amazons conquered Armenia, Syria, and all of Asia Minor, even reaching <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">Ionia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aeolis" title="Aeolis">Aeolia</a>, holding this vast territory for 100 years. Jordanes also mentions that they fought with Hercules, and in the Trojan War, and that a smaller contingent of them endured in the Caucasus Mountains until the time of Alexander. He mentions by name the Queens Menalippe, Hippolyta, and Penthesilea.</p>
<p>In the Grottaferrata Version of <i><a href="/wiki/Digenes_Akritas" title="Digenes Akritas">Digenes Akritas</a></i>, the twelfth century medieval epic of Basil, the Greek-Syrian knight of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> frontier, the hero battles with and kills the female warrior Maximo.</p>

<p>There are several (conflicting) lists of names of Amazons.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quintus_Smyrnaeus" title="Quintus Smyrnaeus">Quintus Smyrnaeus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> lists the attendant warriors of Penthesilea: "Clonie was there, Polemusa, Derinoe, Evandre, and <a href="/wiki/Antandre" title="Antandre">Antandre</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bremusa" title="Bremusa">Bremusa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippothoe" title="Hippothoe">Hippothoe</a>, dark-eyed Harmothoe, <a href="/wiki/Alcibie" title="Alcibie">Alcibie</a>, Derimacheia, Antibrote, and Thermodosa glorying with the spear."</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> lists twelve Amazons who challenged Heracles to single combat during his quest for Hippolyta's girdle and died against him one by one: <a href="/wiki/Aella_(Amazon)" title="Aella (Amazon)">Aella</a>, Philippis, Prothoe, Eriboea, <a href="/wiki/Celaeno" title="Celaeno">Celaeno</a>, Eurybia, <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(mythology)" title="Phoebe (mythology)">Phoebe</a>, Deianeira, <a href="/wiki/Asteria_(mythology)" title="Asteria (mythology)">Asteria</a>, Marpe, Tecmessa, Alcippe. After Alcippe's death, a group attack followed.</p>
<p>Another list of Amazons' names is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyginus" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a>' <i>Fabulae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Along with <a href="/wiki/Hippolyta" title="Hippolyta">Hippolyta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otrera" title="Otrera">Otrera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antiope_(Amazon)" title="Antiope (Amazon)">Antiope</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penthesilea" title="Penthesilea">Penthesilea</a>, it attests the following names: Ocyale, Dioxippe, Iphinome, Xanthe, <a href="/wiki/Hippothoe" title="Hippothoe">Hippothoe</a>, Laomache, <a href="/wiki/Glauce" title="Glauce">Glauce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agave_(mythology)" title="Agave (mythology)">Agave</a>, Theseis, Clymene, Polydora.</p>
<p>Yet another different set of names is found in <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Valerius_Flaccus" title="Gaius Valerius Flaccus">Valerius Flaccus</a>' <i>Argonautica</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> he mentions Euryale, Harpe, Lyce, Menippe and Thoe. Of these Lyce also appears in a fragment preserved in the <i><a href="/wiki/Latin_Anthology" title="Latin Anthology">Latin Anthology</a></i> where she is said to have killed the hero Clonus of <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>, son of Doryclus, with her javelin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Latin_Anthology_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Latin_Anthology-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Tzetzes" title="John Tzetzes">John Tzetzes</a> in <i>Posthomerica</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> enumerates the Amazons who fell at <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a>: Hippothoe, Antianeira, Toxophone, Toxoanassa, Gortyessa, Iodoce, Pharetre, Andro, Ioxeia, Oïstrophe, Androdaïxa, Aspidocharme, Enchesimargos, Cnemis, Thorece, Chalcaor, Eurylophe, Hecate, Anchimache, Andromache the queen. Concerning Antianeira and Andromache, see below; for almost all the other names on the list, this is a unique attestation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephanus_of_Byzantium" title="Stephanus of Byzantium">Stephanus of Byzantium</a> provides an alternate list of the Amazons who fell against Heracles, describing them as "the most prominent" of their people: Tralla, Isocrateia, Thiba, Palla, Coea (Koia), Coenia (Koinia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eustathius_of_Thessalonica" title="Eustathius of Thessalonica">Eustathius</a> gives the same list minus the last two names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Both Stephanus and Eustathius write of these Amazons in connection with the placename Thibais, which they report to have been derived from Thiba's name.</p>
<p>Other names of Amazons from various sources include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aegea" title="Aegea">Aegea</a>, queen of the Amazons who was thought by some to have been the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ainia, presumably accompanied <a href="/wiki/Penthesilea" title="Penthesilea">Penthesilea</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>, killed by <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a>; known only from an Attic terracotta relief fragment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ainippe, an Amazon who confronted <a href="/wiki/Telamon" title="Telamon">Telamon</a> in the battle against Heracles' troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perseus_Digital_Library_-_Description_of_the_Tyrrhenian_amphora_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perseus_Digital_Library_-_Description_of_the_Tyrrhenian_amphora-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>Alce, who was said to have killed the young Oebalus of <a href="/wiki/Arcadia" title="Arcadia">Arcadia</a>, son of Ida (otherwise unknown), with her spear during the Parthian War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Latin_Anthology_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Latin_Anthology-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>Amastris, who was believed to be the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a> of the city previously known as Kromna,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> although the city was also thought to have been named after the historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amastris_(city)" title="Amastris (city)">Amastris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>Anaea, an Amazon whose tomb was shown at the island of <a href="/wiki/Samos" title="Samos">Samos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hero_cults">Hero cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hero cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to ancient sources (<a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>), Amazon tombs could be found frequently throughout what was once known as the ancient Greek world. Some are found in <a href="/wiki/Megara" title="Megara">Megara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaeronea" title="Chaeronea">Chaeronea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalcis" title="Chalcis">Chalcis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skotousa" title="Skotousa">Skotousa</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cynoscephalae" title="Cynoscephalae">Cynoscephalae</a>, and statues of Amazons are all over Greece. At both Chalcis and Athens, <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> tells us that there was an Amazoneum or shrine of Amazons that implied the presence of both tombs and cult. On the day before the Thesea at Athens there were annual sacrifices to the Amazons. In historical times Greek maidens of <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a> performed an annual circular dance with weapons and shields that had been established by <a href="/wiki/Hippolyta" title="Hippolyta">Hippolyta</a> and her Amazons. They had initially set up wooden statues of <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>, a <i>bretas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, (fl.c.160): <i>Description of Greece</i>, Book I: Attica).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gladiatrix_relief.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="300" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gladiatrix_relief.jpg/220px-Gladiatrix_relief.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fd/Gladiatrix_relief.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gladiatrix_relief.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Female_gladiator" title="Female gladiator">female gladiators</a> with their names <i>Amazonia and <a href="/wiki/Achillea" title="Achillea">Achillea</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In works of art, battles between Amazons and Greeks are placed on the same level as – and often associated with – battles of Greeks and <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaurs</a>. The belief in their existence, however, having been once accepted and introduced into the national poetry and art, it became necessary to surround them as far as possible with the appearance of natural beings. Amazons were therefore depicted in the manner of Scythian or Sarmatian horsemen. Their occupation was hunting and war; their arms the bow, spear, axe, a half shield, nearly in the shape of a crescent, called <i>pelta</i>, and in early art a helmet. The model in the Greek mind had apparently been the goddess Athena. In later art they approach the model of Artemis, wearing a thin dress, girt high for speed; while on the later painted vases their dress is often peculiarly <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Persian</a> – that is, close-fitting trousers and a high cap called the <i>kidaris</i>. They were usually on horseback but sometimes on foot. This depiction of Amazons demonstrates just how closely, in the Greek mind, the Amazons were linked to the Scythians. Their manner of dress has been noted to bear a striking similarity to the traditional dress of nomadic peoples from the Crimea to Mongolia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amaz_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-amaz-85">[85]</a></sup> Amazons were described by Herodotus as wearing trousers and having tall stiff caps. The double-sided axe was the most emblematic of their weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wonder_Woman_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wonder_Woman-5">[5]</a></sup> Amazons can also be identified in vase paintings by the fact that they are wearing one earring. The battle between Theseus and the Amazons (<a href="/wiki/Amazonomachy" title="Amazonomachy">Amazonomachy</a>) is a favourite subject on the friezes of temples (e.g. the reliefs from the frieze of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Apollo_at_Bassae" title="Temple of Apollo at Bassae">Temple of Apollo at Bassae</a>, now in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>), vases and sarcophagus reliefs; at <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> it was represented on the shield of the statue of <a href="/wiki/Athena_Parthenos" title="Athena Parthenos">Athena Parthenos</a>, on wall-paintings in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theseum" title="Theseum">Theseum</a> and in the <i><a href="/wiki/Stoa_Poikile" title="Stoa Poikile">Stoa Poikile</a></i>. There were also three standard <a href="/wiki/Amazon_statue_types" title="Amazon statue types">Amazon statue types</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="920" data-file-width="1161" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg/220px-Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg/330px-Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg/440px-Peter_Paul_Rubens_-_The_Battle_of_the_Amazons_-_WGA20272.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<i>Battle of the Amazons</i> by Peter Paul Ruben</div>

<p>Later in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Renaissance" title="The Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, as Amazon myth evolved, artists started to depict warrior women in a new light. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth</a> was often thought of as an Amazon-like warrior during her reign and can be seen in many paintings as such. Though, as explained in <i>Divinia Viagro</i> by Winfried Schleiner, Celeste T. Wright "has given a detailed account of the bad press Amazons had in the Renaissance (with respect to their unwomanly conduct and Scythian cruelty). She notes that she has not found any Elizabethans comparing the queen directly to an Amazon, and suggests that they might have hesitated to do so because of the association of Amazons with enfranchisement of women, which was considered contemptible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Ruben</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder" title="Jan Brueghel the Elder">Jan Brueghel</a> depicted the Battle of the Amazons around 1598, showing many attributes of Renaissance-styled paintings. Amazons also appear in the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> period in another painting titled <i>Battle of the Amazons</i> by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Platzer" title="Johann Georg Platzer">Johann Georg Platzer</a>. As a part of the Romantic period revival, German artist <a href="/wiki/Anselm_Feuerbach" title="Anselm Feuerbach">Anselm Feuerbach</a> painted the Amazons as well. His paintings “engendered all the aspirations of the Romantics: their desire to transcend the boundaries of the ego and of the known world; their interest in the occult in nature and in the soul; their search for a national identity, and the ensuing search for the mythic origins of the Germanic nation; finally, their wish to escape the harsh realities of the present through immersion in an idealized past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>”</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_historiography">In historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1127" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg/220px-S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg/330px-S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg/440px-S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S03_06_01_020_image_2552.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Amazon in combat, infl. Polyclitus, Rome. Brooklyn Museum Archives, Goodyear Archival Collection</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> reported that the Sarmatians were descendants of Amazons and Scythians, and that their wives observed their ancient maternal customs, "frequently hunting on horseback with their husbands; in war taking the field; and wearing the very same dress as the men". Moreover, said Herodotus, "No girl shall wed till she has killed a man in battle". In the story related by Herodotus, a group of Amazons was blown across the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">Maeotian Lake</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">Sea of Azov</a>) into <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythia</a> near the cliff region (today's southeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" title="Crimean Peninsula">Crimea</a>). After learning the Scythian language, they agreed to marry Scythian men, on the condition that they not be required to follow the customs of Scythian women. According to Herodotus, this band moved toward the northeast, settling beyond the <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Tanais</a> (<a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Don</a>) river, and became the ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sauromatians</a>. According to Herodotus, the <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a> fought with the Scythians against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_the_Great" title="Darius the Great">Darius the Great</a> in the 5th century BC.</p>
<p>Hippocrates describes them as: "They have no right breasts...for while they are yet babies their mothers make red-hot a bronze instrument constructed for this very purpose and apply it to the right breast and cauterize it, so that its growth is arrested, and all its strength and bulk are diverted to the right shoulder and right arm."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amazons came to play a role in <a href="/wiki/Roman_historiography" title="Roman historiography">Roman historiography</a>. Caesar reminded the Senate of the conquest of large parts of Asia by <a href="/wiki/Semiramis" title="Semiramis">Semiramis</a> and the Amazons. Successful Amazon raids against Lycia and Cilicia contrasted with effective resistance by Lydian cavalry against the invaders (<a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> 5.504; Nicholas Damascenus). <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Pompeius_Trogus" title="Gnaeus Pompeius Trogus">Gnaeus Pompeius Trogus</a> pays particularly detailed attention to the Amazons. The story of the Amazons as deriving from a Cappadocian colony of two Scythian princes Ylinos and Scolopetos is due to him. <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> records some surprising facts pointing to the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Terme_River" title="Terme River">Terme River</a> as possibly being their home: a mountain named for them (the modern Mason Dagi), as well as a settlement Amazonium; Herodotus (VI.86) first mentions their capital <a href="/wiki/Themiscyra_(Pontus)" title="Themiscyra (Pontus)">Themiscyra</a>, which Pliny locates near the Terme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philostratus" title="Philostratus">Philostratus</a> places the Amazons in the <a href="/wiki/Taurus_Mountains" title="Taurus Mountains">Taurus Mountains</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus</a> places them east of <a href="/wiki/Tanais" title="Tanais">Tanais</a>, as neighbouring the <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a>. <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a> places them in the Caucasus. <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_historica" title="Bibliotheca historica">Bibliotheca historica</a></i> III, chapter 52) mentioned that besides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontus" title="Pontus">Pontus</a> Amazons existed much older race (at that time entirely disappeared) of Amazons from western <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a>, and retells their mythological story which includes <a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantis</a> and Greek mythology.</p>
<p>Although Strabo shows skepticism as to their historicity, the Amazons in general continue to be taken as historical throughout Late Antiquity. Several Church Fathers speak of the Amazons as of a real people. <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Solinus" title="Gaius Julius Solinus">Solinus</a> embraces the account of Pliny. Under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurelianus" title="Aurelianus">Aurelianus</a>, captured <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Gothic</a> women were identified as Amazons (<a href="/wiki/Claudian" title="Claudian">Claudianus</a>). The account of <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justinus</a> was influential, and was used as a source by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulus_Orosius" title="Paulus Orosius">Orosius</a> who continued to be read during the European Middle Ages. Medieval authors thus continue the tradition of locating the Amazons in the North, <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a> placing them at the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulus_Diaconus" title="Paulus Diaconus">Paulus Diaconus</a> in the heart of Germania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_and_Renaissance_literature">Medieval and Renaissance literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medieval and Renaissance literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dahomey_amazon1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="565" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Dahomey_amazon1.jpg/170px-Dahomey_amazon1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Dahomey_amazon1.jpg/255px-Dahomey_amazon1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Dahomey_amazon1.jpg/340px-Dahomey_amazon1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dahomey_amazon1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Dahomey_Amazons" title="Dahomey Amazons">Dahomey Amazons</a> were so named by Western observers due to their similarity to the semi-mythical Amazons</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Amazons continued to be discussed by authors of the European Renaissance, and with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_Exploration" title="Age of Exploration">Age of Exploration</a>, they were located in ever more remote areas. In 1542, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Orellana" title="Francisco de Orellana">Francisco de Orellana</a> reached the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> (<i>Amazonas</i> in Spanish), naming it after a tribe of warlike women he claimed to have encountered and fought on the <a href="/wiki/Nhamund%C3%A1_River" title="Nhamundá River">Nhamundá River</a>, a tributary of the Amazon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Afterwards the whole basin and region of the Amazon (<i>Amazônia</i> in Portuguese, <i>Amazonía</i> in Spanish) were named after the river. Amazons also figure in the accounts of both <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Raleigh" title="Walter Raleigh">Walter Raleigh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Famous medieval traveller <a href="/wiki/John_Mandeville" title="John Mandeville">John Mandeville</a> mentions them in his book:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Beside the land of Chaldea is the land of Amazonia, that is the land of Feminye. And in that realm is all woman and no man; not as some may say, that men may not live there, but for because that the women will not suffer no men amongst them to be their sovereigns."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Medieval and Renaissance authors credit the Amazons with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Corded_Ware_culture" title="Corded Ware culture">battle-axe</a>. This is probably related to the <i><a href="/wiki/Sagaris" title="Sagaris">sagaris</a></i>, an axe-like weapon associated with both Amazons and Scythian tribes by Greek authors (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thracian_tomb_of_Aleksandrovo_kurgan" title="Thracian tomb of Aleksandrovo kurgan">Thracian tomb of Aleksandrovo kurgan</a>). <a href="/wiki/Paulus_Hector_Mair" title="Paulus Hector Mair">Paulus Hector Mair</a> expresses astonishment that such a "manly weapon" should have been invented by a "tribe of women", but he accepts the attribution out of respect for his authority, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Aventinus" title="Johannes Aventinus">Johannes Aventinus</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Ariosto" title="Ludovico Ariosto">Ariosto</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Orlando_Furioso" title="Orlando Furioso">Orlando Furioso</a></i> contains a country of warrior women, ruled by Queen Orontea; the epic describes an origin much like that in Greek myth, in that the women, abandoned by a band of warriors and unfaithful lovers, rallied together to form a nation from which men were severely reduced, to prevent them from regaining power. The Amazons and Queen Hippolyta are also referenced in <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales" title="The Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i> in "<a href="/wiki/The_Knight%27s_Tale" title="The Knight's Tale">The Knight's Tale</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">Classicist</a> Peter Walcot wrote, "Wherever the Amazons are located by the Greeks, whether it is somewhere along the Black Sea in the distant north-east, or in Libya in the furthest south, it is always beyond the confines of <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">the civilized world</a>. The Amazons exist outside the range of normal human experience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nevertheless, there are various proposals for a historical nucleus of the Amazons of Greek historiography, the most obvious candidates being historical <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatia" title="Sarmatia">Sarmatia</a> in line with the account by <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, but some authors prefer a comparison to cultures of Asia Minor or even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minoan_Crete" title="Minoan Crete">Minoan Crete</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scythians_and_Sarmatians">Scythians and Sarmatians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Scythians and Sarmatians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Speculation that the idea of Amazons contains a core of reality is based on archaeological findings from burials, pointing to the possibility that some Sarmatian women may have participated in battle. These findings have led scholars to suggest that the Amazonian legend in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> may have been "inspired by real warrior women".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence of high-ranking warrior women comes from <a href="/wiki/Kurgan" title="Kurgan">kurgans</a> in southern Ukraine and Russia. David Anthony notes, "About 20% of <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythian</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatian" title="Sarmatian">Sarmatian</a> 'warrior graves' on the lower <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">Don</a> and lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volga_river" title="Volga river">Volga</a> contained women dressed for battle similar to how men dress, a phenomenon that probably inspired the Greek tales about the Amazons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anthonyd_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-anthonyd-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1983" data-file-width="2058" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg/220px-Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg/330px-Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg/440px-Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amazone_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2342.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mounted Amazon in <a href="/wiki/Scythia" title="Scythia">Scythian</a> costume, on an <a href="/wiki/Attica" title="Attica">Attic</a> red-figure vase, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr>420 BC</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Up to 25% of military burials were of armed Sarmatian women usually including bows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-97">[97]</a></sup> Russian archaeologist Vera Kovalevskaya points out that when Scythian men were away fighting or hunting, nomadic women would have to be able to defend themselves, their animals and pasture-grounds competently. During the time that the Scythians advanced into Asia and achieved near-<a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> in the Near East, there was a period of twenty-eight years when the men would have been away on campaigns for long periods. During this time the women would not only have had to defend themselves, but to reproduce, and this could well be the origin of the idea that Amazons mated once a year with their neighbours, if Herodotus actually based his accounts on fact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before modern archaeology uncovered some of the Scythian burials of warrior-maidens entombed under <a href="/wiki/Kurgan" title="Kurgan">kurgans</a> in the region of <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a> and Sarmatia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> giving concrete form at last to the Greek tales, the origin of the Amazon story had been the subject of speculation among classics scholars. In the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Eleventh Edition">1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i></a> speculation ranged along the following lines:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>While some regard the Amazons as a purely mythical people, others assume an historical foundation for them. The deities worshipped by them were <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a> (who is consistently assigned to them as a god of war, and as a god of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thracian</a> and generally northern origin) and <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>, not the usual Greek goddess of that name, but an Asiatic deity in some respects her equivalent. It is conjectured that the Amazons were originally the temple-servants and priestesses (<i>hierodulae</i>) of this goddess; and that the removal of the breast corresponded with the self-mutilation of the god <a href="/wiki/Attis" title="Attis">Attis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Galli" title="Galli">galli</a>, Roman priests of <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Rhea Cybele</a>. Another theory is that, as the knowledge of geography extended, travellers brought back reports of tribes ruled entirely by women, who carried out the duties which elsewhere were regarded as peculiar to man, in whom alone the rights of nobility and inheritance were vested, and who had the supreme control of affairs. Hence arose the belief in the Amazons as a nation of female warriors, organized and governed entirely by women. According to J. Viirtheim (<i>De Ajacis</i> origine, 1907), the Amazons were of Greek origin [...] It has been suggested that the fact of the conquest of the Amazons being assigned to the two famous heroes of Greek mythology, Heracles and Theseus [...] shows that they were mythical illustrations of the dangers which beset the Greeks on the coasts of Asia Minor; rather perhaps, it may be intended to represent the conflict between the Greek culture of the colonies on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Euxine</a> and the barbarism of the native inhabitants.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minoan_Crete">Minoan Crete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Minoan Crete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<i>Departure of the Amazons</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Deruet" title="Claude Deruet">Claude Deruet</a>, 1620</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> archeology was still in its infancy, nevertheless, a theory raised in an essay regarding the Amazons contributed by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Richard_Farnell" title="Lewis Richard Farnell">Lewis Richard Farnell</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Myres" title="John Myres">John Myres</a> to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ranulph_Marett" title="Robert Ranulph Marett">Robert Ranulph Marett</a>'s <i>Anthropology and the Classics</i> (1908),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> placed their possible origins in Minoan civilization, drawing attention to overlooked similarities between the two cultures. According to Myres,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> the tradition interpreted in the light of evidence furnished by supposed Amazon cults seems to have been very similar and may have even originated in Minoan culture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_legacy">Modern legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grigory_Potemkin" title="Grigory Potemkin">Grigory Potemkin</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian</a> military leader, statesman, nobleman and <a href="/wiki/Favourite" title="Favourite">favourite</a> of <a href="/wiki/Catherine_the_Great" title="Catherine the Great">Catherine the Great</a> created an <a href="/wiki/Greek_Battalion_of_Balaklava#The_.22Amazons.22_of_the_Battalion" title="Greek Battalion of Balaklava">Amazons battalion</a> on 1787. Wives and daughters of the soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Battalion_of_Balaklava" title="Greek Battalion of Balaklava">Greek Battalion of Balaklava</a> were enlisted and formed this unit.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> Katerina Tarnovska leads a group called the Asgarda which claims to be a new tribe of Amazons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pri.org_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-pri.org-102">[102]</a></sup> Tarnovska believes that the Amazons are the direct ancestors of Ukrainian women, and she has created an all-female martial art for her group, based on another form of fighting called <a href="/wiki/Combat_Hopak" title="Combat Hopak">Combat Hopak</a>, but with a special emphasis on self-defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pri.org_102-1"><a href="#cite_note-pri.org-102">[102]</a></sup> French photographer Guillaume Herbaut lived with the Asgarda and photographed them in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the group consists of 150 women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Samsun" title="Samsun">Samsun</a> in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> features a recently constructed "Amazon Village" museum, created to bring attention to the legacy of the Amazons and to generate both academic interest and popular tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-todayszaman_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-todayszaman-105">[105]</a></sup> An iconic statue of the museum is the prominent figure of a fierce female warrior, flanked by two buildings designed to look like lions.</p>
<p>A festival is held every year in the <a href="/wiki/Terme" title="Terme">Terme</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Samsun_Province" title="Samsun Province">Samsun Province</a> to celebrate the Amazons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-todayszaman_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-todayszaman-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Greece, female <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equestrians" title="Equestrians">equestrians</a> are called "Amazons" (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Αμαζόνες</span>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature_and_popular_media">In literature and popular media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In literature and popular media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Amazon Queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyppolyta" title="Hyppolyta">Hyppolyta</a> appears in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare%27s" title="William Shakespeare's">William Shakespeare's</a> <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">play</a> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i> and also in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Two_Noble_Kinsmen" title="The Two Noble Kinsmen">The Two Noble Kinsmen</a></i>, which Shakespeare co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/John_Fletcher_(playwright)" title="John Fletcher (playwright)">John Fletcher</a>.</li>
<li>The Amazon queen <a href="/wiki/Penthesilea" title="Penthesilea">Penthesilea</a>, and her sexual frenzy, are at the center of the drama <i><a href="/wiki/Penthesilea_(Kleist)" title="Penthesilea (Kleist)">Penthesilea</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_von_Kleist" title="Heinrich von Kleist">Heinrich von Kleist</a> in 1808.</li>
<li>During the period 1905–13, members of the militant <a href="/wiki/Suffragette" title="Suffragette">Suffragette</a> movement were frequently referred to as "Amazons" in books and newspaper articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/DC_Universe" title="DC Universe">DC Universe</a> superheroine <a href="/wiki/Wonder_Woman" title="Wonder Woman">Wonder Woman</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Amazons_(DC_Comics)" title="Amazons (DC Comics)">Amazon</a> princess Diana of <a href="/wiki/Themyscira_(DC_Comics)" title="Themyscira (DC Comics)">Themyscira</a>, created by <a href="/wiki/William_Moulton_Marston" title="William Moulton Marston">William Moulton Marston</a> (the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Systole" title="Systole">systolic</a> <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> test and a scholar with a penchant for women's power).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> She is the only daughter of <a href="/wiki/Hippolyta_(DC_Comics)" title="Hippolyta (DC Comics)">Queen Hippolyta</a>, who, in turn, is Marston's incarnation of the classical Queen Hyppolyta of <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>.</li>

<li>In the Stig Larsson novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Girl_Who_Kicked_the_Hornet%27s_Nest" title="The Girl Who Kicked the Hornet's Nest">The Girl Who Kicked the Hornet's Nest</a></i>, the Amazons appear as the transitional topics between sections of the book.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(series)" title="Diablo (series)">Diablo</a></i>, on the planet of Sanctuary, Askari people, also called Amazons, exist in the Queendom of the Skovos Isles. The Rogues of the Siterhood of the Sightless Eye are descendants of the Askari people.</li>
<li>In "<a href="/wiki/Heroes_Unlimited" title="Heroes Unlimited">Heroes Unlimited</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aliens_Unlimited&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aliens Unlimited (page does not exist)">Aliens Unlimited</a>," there is a race called the Atorians who can be considered Amazons.</li>

</ul>
