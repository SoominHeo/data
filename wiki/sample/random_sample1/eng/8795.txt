<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Amateur radio international operation</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Amateur radio international reciprocal operating agreements</b> permit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_Radio" title="Amateur Radio">Amateur Radio</a> Operators (Hams) from one country to operate a station whilst traveling in another without the need to obtain additional licenses or permits.</p>
<p>When no agreement exists between countries, amateur radio operators are often required to apply for a reciprocal operating permit or a full amateur radio license and <a href="/wiki/Call_sign" title="Call sign">call sign</a> from the host country. Some countries may accept a foreign amateur radio licenses as proof of qualification in lieu of examination requirements<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> whereas other host countries may provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unilateral" title="Unilateral">unilateral</a> reciprocal operating privileges without the need for additional licensing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Conference_of_Postal_and_Telecommunications_Administrations">European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CEPT_License">CEPT License</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CEPT License">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Member Nations of the <a href="/wiki/European_Conference_of_Postal_and_Telecommunications_Administrations" title="European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations">European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations</a> (CEPT)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erodocdb_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-erodocdb-2">[2]</a></sup> all share the same amateur radio reciprocal licensing requirements. Amateurs are permitted to operate from most European countries without the requirement of obtaining additional licensees or permits.</p>
<p>The following countries outside of Europe also participate in CEPT Recommendation T/R 61-01:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogeneratedic_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogeneratedic-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Australia,</li>
<li>Canada,</li>
<li>Israel,</li>
<li>Netherlands Antilles,</li>
<li>New Zealand,</li>
<li>Peru,</li>
<li>South Africa, and,</li>
<li>The United States.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="US_and_Canadian_citizens_visiting_a_CEPT_Country">US and Canadian citizens visiting a CEPT Country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: US and Canadian citizens visiting a CEPT Country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The United States and Canada currently accept CEPT licenses within areas controlled by the US Federal Communications Commission or Industry Canada respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogeneratedic_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogeneratedic-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>CEPT member countries accept:</p>
<ul>
<li>Canadian Basic Class Licences;</li>
<li>US and Canadian Advanced Class Licenses; and,</li>
<li>US Amateur Extra Class Licenses.</li>
</ul>
<p>US General Class licenses are accepted under a separate agreement as CEPT Novice licensees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogeneratedic_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogeneratedic-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>United States licensed stations are required to carry and provide upon request:</p>
<ul>
<li>A US passport;</li>
<li>A copy of the 2009 FCC CEPT notice;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and,</li>
<li>A valid FCC amateur radio license.</li>
</ul>
<p>In Canada, "the Minister of Industry has delegated Radio Amateurs of Canada (RAC) to issue CEPT permits for Canadian licensed stations." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogeneratedic_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogeneratedic-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Canadian licensed stations are required to provide upon request:</p>
<ul>
<li>A Canadian passport;</li>
<li>A copy of the licensee's CEPT permit; and,</li>
<li>A valid Canadian amateur radio licence.</li>
</ul>
<p>As of February 4, 2008, CEPT no longer accepts Technician or General Class Licensees for CEPT reciprocal privileges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CEPT_Novice_License">CEPT Novice License</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: CEPT Novice License">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009 the Electronic Communications Committee (ECC) revised the 'CEPT Novice Radio Amateur license', a separate agreement, to include novice class reciprocal operating privileges in some CEPT countries under modified conditions. European reciprocal privileges have, at least in part, been restored to US General Class Operators as CEPT Novice Operators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erodocdb_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-erodocdb-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CITEL_agreement">CITEL agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: CITEL agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IARP_License">IARP License</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: IARP License">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The International Amateur Radio Permit (IARP) allows foreign operation within <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">North and South American</a> treaty countries without the need to obtain a license or permit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The CITEL agreement allows an IARP<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to be issued, by a member-society of the <a href="/wiki/International_Amateur_Radio_Union" title="International Amateur Radio Union">International Amateur Radio Union</a> (IARU).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_United_States_and_Canada">The United States and Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The United States and Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_Licenses">Domestic Licenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Domestic Licenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="920" data-file-width="1084" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg/300px-Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg/450px-Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg/600px-Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amateur_radio_prefixes_in_Canada.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Canadian amateur radio prefixes/suffixes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Citizens of the United States or Canada may operate in the other country as a domestically licensed station, as if their license had been issued in said country, without the requirement of obtaining any license or permission from the other government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>An American or Canadian amateur may allow third party use of his station and call sign, carry international third party traffic, serve as a temporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_operator" title="Control operator">control operator</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Repeater" title="Repeater">repeater</a> station, and identify themselves as a domestic station using the national call sign system, provided:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The Licensee has citizenship and a valid amateur radio license from the country for their residency;</li>
<li>The Licensee appends the local US/Canadian Zone(Region) to the <b>END</b> of their call sign when identifying their station; and,</li>
<li>The Licensee adheres to the operating powers, frequency (band) allocations, and laws pertaining to the country they are currently operating in.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_CEPT_and_IARP_operators_visiting_the_US_and_Canada">Foreign CEPT and IARP operators visiting the US and Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Foreign CEPT and IARP operators visiting the US and Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ol>
<li>Must have operating privileges in the respective country.</li>
<li>Must identify with the appropriate US or Canadian Country Prefix and Zone(Region) <b>PRIOR</b> to their call sign.</li>
<li>Must observe restrictions concerning the third party use of their equipment.</li>
<li>Must adhere to the operating powers and frequencies of their country's license <b>AND</b> the country they are operating in.</li>
<li>A reciprocal agreement must be present and in good standing between the two countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ol>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Waters_and_Air_Space">International Waters and Air Space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: International Waters and Air Space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amateur radio operators in international waters or airspace are subject to the reciprocal licensing requirements pertaining to the country under which the vessel is flagged. Permission by the vessel's captain for on-board use of amateur radio equipment is often a legal requirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antarctica">Antarctica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Antarctica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_call_signs_of_Antarctica" title="Amateur radio call signs of Antarctica">Amateur radio call signs of Antarctica</a></div>
<p>Although Antarctica is considered international by treaty, Amateur radio operators in Antarctica are often subject to the reciprocal licensing requirements pertaining to the country under which the camp is flagged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial">Extraterrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stations operating from space, defined as an altitude above 50 km above the height of the average terrain, are subject to the terms and conditions established in conjunction with their amateur license grant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DX-pedition">DX-pedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_radio_international_operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: DX-pedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1552" data-file-width="2001" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg/300px-G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg/450px-G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg/600px-G0RTN_operating_amateur_radio.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

A DXer operates during a holiday DXpedition to <a href="/wiki/Muscat,_Oman" title="Muscat, Oman">Muscat</a>, Oman.</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/DX-pedition" title="DX-pedition">DX-pedition</a></div>
<p>A <b>DX-pedition</b> is an expedition to what is considered an exotic place by <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> operators, perhaps because of its remoteness or because there are very few radio amateurs active from that place. This could be an island, a country, or even a particular spot on a geographical grid.</p>
<p>The activity was pioneered by one-time <a href="/wiki/American_Radio_Relay_League" title="American Radio Relay League">ARRL</a> president <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_W._Denniston&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert W. Denniston (page does not exist)">Robert W. Denniston</a>. Mr. Denniston's 1948 DX-pedition was to <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> and was called "Gon-Waki" ala <a href="/wiki/Thor_Heyerdahl" title="Thor Heyerdahl">Thor Heyerdahl's</a> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kon-Tiki" title="Kon-Tiki">Kon-Tiki</a>" expedition the previous year. Arguably there were earlier trips where amateur radio was used that might have qualified as DX-peditions. An example is the voyage of the <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a> <i>Kaimiloa</i>, which traveled the South Pacific in 1924. While the ship's wealthy owners enjoyed the islands an amateur radio operator kept contact with, and sent <a href="/wiki/QSL_card" title="QSL card">QSL cards</a> to, experimenters in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
