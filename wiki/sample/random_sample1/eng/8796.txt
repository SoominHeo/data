<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Amateur Football Alliance</h1>

<p>The <b>Amateur Football Alliance</b> is a <a href="/wiki/County_Football_Association" title="County Football Association">County Football Association</a> in England. It is unusual among County FAs in not serving a particular geographical area. It was founded in 1907, as the <b>Amateur Football Defence Foundation</b>, quickly changed to <b>Amateur Football Association</b>, when <a href="/wiki/The_Football_Association" title="The Football Association">The FA</a> required all county associations to admit professional clubs. Its aim was, as the decline of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateurism" title="Amateurism">amateurism</a> at the highest levels of football set in, to protect and preserve the original amateur spirit. It prides itself on the skill and competitiveness of its leagues, and on its traditions of fair play and respect for opponents and match officials. Many leagues still maintain rules that require clubs to provide food and drink to their opponents and match officials after the match in a clubhouse or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">public house</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_Football_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the general meeting of <a href="/wiki/The_Football_Association" title="The Football Association">The Football Association</a> on 31 May 1907, it was decided by the Amateur Defence Federation that in the best interest of amateur football that a new and separate organisation must be created. The inaugural meeting of the Amateur Football Association was held in the Crown Room of the Holborn Restaurant on 7 July 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nottsform_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nottsform-1">[1]</a></sup> They were addressed by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Lyttelton" title="Alfred Lyttelton">Alfred Lyttelton</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a>, before B.A. Glanville of <a href="/wiki/Clapham_Rovers_F.C." title="Clapham Rovers F.C.">Clapham Rovers</a> proposed the formation of the Association, which was seconded by N.C. Bailey. It was stated that the foundation of the Association wasn't in opposition to professionalism in sport but instead to the "fungus growth which had become attached to the machinery of football management".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formation_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-formation-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Webster,_1st_Viscount_Alverstone" title="Richard Webster, 1st Viscount Alverstone">Lord Alverstone</a> was elected as the first president of the new society,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-formation_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-formation-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_F.C." title="Corinthian F.C.">Corinthians</a> offered to provide a trophy for a new cup competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nottsform_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nottsform-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Football Association responded by banning amateur players from playing for professional clubs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and resulted in the end of the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_of_London_Charity_Shield" title="Sheriff of London Charity Shield">Sheriff of London Charity Shield</a> after the FA refused to provide a professional team for the match, and barred all its members from either playing or providing facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However a later resolution by the FA meant that any player who had played for his school, college or university team which was a member of the Amateur Football Association was not banned from playing for a professional team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Furthermore, the FA asked the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Football_Association" title="Scottish Football Association">Scottish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_Football_Association" title="Welsh Football Association">Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Football_Association" title="Irish Football Association">Irish Football Association</a> not to recognise the formation of the AFA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of teams were forced to choose between one association or the other. <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_A.F.C." title="Cambridge University A.F.C.">Cambridge University</a> pledged their allegiance to the Amateur Football Association and in response, so did <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_A.F.C." title="Oxford University A.F.C.">Oxford University</a> although they would have preferred to remain neutral between the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Both the <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire_and_Rutland_County_Football_Association" title="Leicestershire and Rutland County Football Association">Leicestershire</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essex_Football_Association" title="Essex Football Association">Essex Football Association</a> were early supporters of the actions of the Football Association against the AFA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Meanwhile, both the <a href="/wiki/Army_Football_Association" title="Army Football Association">Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy_Football_Association" title="Royal Navy Football Association">Royal Navy Football Associations</a> took the question of which Association to support by holding a vote of its member clubs; this resulting in both remaining with the Football Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Four current AFA clubs are former <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> winners: <a href="/wiki/Old_Etonians_F.C." title="Old Etonians F.C.">Old Etonians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_Carthusians_F.C." title="Old Carthusians F.C.">Old Carthusians</a>, who both currently play in the <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_League" title="Arthurian League">Arthurian League</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clapham_Rovers_F.C." title="Clapham Rovers F.C.">Clapham Rovers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wanderers_F.C." title="Wanderers F.C.">Wanderers</a>, who play in the <a href="/wiki/Surrey_South_Eastern_Combination" title="Surrey South Eastern Combination">Surrey South Eastern Combination</a>. Past members of the AFA include <a href="/wiki/Ipswich_Town_F.C." title="Ipswich Town F.C.">Ipswich Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barnet_F.C." title="Barnet F.C.">Barnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_City_F.C." title="Cambridge City F.C.">Cambridge City</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Casuals_F.C." title="Casuals F.C.">Casuals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_F.C." title="Corinthian F.C.">the Corinthians</a>. <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Rous" title="Stanley Rous">Sir Stanley Rous</a>, who was president of <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a>, was also the president of the AFA. The AFA's flagship competition is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFA_Senior_Cup" title="AFA Senior Cup">AFA Senior Cup</a> which is contested by AFA-affiliated clubs on Saturday afternoons. Most of these clubs enter one of the three AFA-affiliated Saturday leagues, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Amateur_Football_League" title="Southern Amateur Football League">Southern Amateur League</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Football_Combination" title="Amateur Football Combination">Amateur Football Combination</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_League" title="Arthurian League">Arthurian League</a>, the SAL having been founded in the same year as the AFA (1907) by more or less the same group of people.</p>
<p>The AFA's heartland is in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_Counties" title="Home Counties">Home Counties</a>, but it has member clubs throughout the nation.</p>
