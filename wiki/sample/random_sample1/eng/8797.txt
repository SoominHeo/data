<p><b>Amateur television</b> (<b>ATV</b>) is the transmission of <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_quality" title="Broadcast quality">broadcast quality</a> <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_reproduction" title="Sound reproduction">audio</a> over the wide range of frequencies of <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> allocated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_amateur" title="Radio amateur">radio amateur</a> (Ham) use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> ATV is used for <a href="/wiki/Non-commercial" title="Non-commercial">non-commercial</a> experimentation, pleasure and public service events. Ham TV stations were on the air in many cities before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_television" title="Commercial television">commercial television</a> stations came on the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Various transmission standards are used, these include the broadcast transmission standards of <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">PAL</a> or <a href="/wiki/SECAM" title="SECAM">SECAM</a> elsewhere, utilizing the full <a href="/wiki/Refresh_rate" title="Refresh rate">refresh rates</a> of those standards. ATV includes the study of building of such <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Receiver_(radio)" title="Receiver (radio)">receivers</a>, and the study of <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">radio propagation</a> of signals travelling between transmitting and receiving stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>ATV is an extension of <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a>. It is also called HAM TV or fast-scan TV (FSTV).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_context">North American context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: North American context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_frequency_allocations" title="Amateur radio frequency allocations">amateur radio bands</a> that are suitable for a television signal (<a href="/wiki/Wideband" title="Wideband">wide</a> enough to fit such a signal) are higher in frequency than VHF broadcast TV. The lowest frequency ham band suitable for television transmission is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/70_centimeters" title="70 centimeters">70 centimeters</a>, which is between broadcast channels 13 and 14. While outside of broadcast television channels, this frequency falls into <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">CATV</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_cable_television_frequencies" title="North American cable television frequencies">frequencies</a>, on channels 57 to 61 <a href="/wiki/North_American_television_frequencies#Incrementally-related_carriers_.28IRC.29" title="North American television frequencies">(IRC)</a> (420–450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As such, ATV transmissions can be viewed by setting a television or analog cable-box to cable input and attaching an outdoor antenna. For more sensitive reception, some users may use a purposely-built ATV down-converter, which is a kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set-top-box" title="Set-top-box">set-top-box</a>. Other bands are also used for ATV, most of them in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UHF" title="UHF">UHF</a> region on frequencies higher than UHF broadcast TV. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33_centimeters" title="33 centimeters">33 centimeters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/23_centimeters" title="23 centimeters">23 centimeters</a> are two other commonly used bands for ATV, but reception of these higher bands requires the use of a down-converter.</p>
<p>Most ATV signals are transmitted in either <a href="/wiki/Amplitude_modulation" title="Amplitude modulation">amplitude modulation (AM)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Single-sideband_modulation#Vestigial_sideband_.28VSB.29" title="Single-sideband modulation">vestigial sideband (VSB)</a> <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a> (North American analog TV broadcast modulation standard)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. DSB AM and VSB AM signals are inherently compatible with each other, and most televisions can receive either. DSB-AM signals consists of the carrier and both upper and lower sidebands. VSB-AM is where DSB-AM is filtered and the lower sideband is highly attenuated at frequencies more than 1.25 MHz from the carrier signal. A VSB filter can be added to a DSB-AM transmitter to make it a VSB signal. The filters, depending on power usage, will cost anywhere from US$100–1,000. For practical reasons, most individual ATV users transmit in DSB-AM, and VSB is transmitted by repeater stations. On the 33 cm and higher bands, <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">frequency modulation (FM)</a> ATV may be used, and on the <a href="/wiki/Super_high_frequency" title="Super high frequency">SHF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extremely_high_frequency" title="Extremely high frequency">EHF</a> ham bands, FM is more commonly used than VSB or AM. FM ATV is incompatible with AM/VSB ATV, and a separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demodulator" title="Demodulator">demodulator</a> is necessary to receive signals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2_m_band">2 m band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 2 m band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2-meter_band" title="2-meter band">2-meter band</a> (144-148 MHz), while too narrow in <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> for a video signal, is often used by ATV operators for coordination with each other via FM voice transmissions. Operators seeking an ATV contact might first attempt calling on a regionally recognized ATV liaison-frequency, commonly 144.34 MHz, then agree to an ATV frequency to use for the video transmissions. The 2 meter frequency may be used throughout the contact to talk back to the current station transmitting video. The receiving station(s) may suggest adjustments the sending station can make, such as antenna direction, to improve the quality of the video received.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="70_cm_band">70 cm band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 70 cm band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/70-centimeter_band" title="70-centimeter band">70-centimeter band</a> (420-450 MHz) is the most commonly used ham band for ATV. Signals transmitted on this band usually propagate longer distances than on higher frequency bands, for a given transmitter power and antenna gain. The band falls between <a href="/wiki/North_American_television_frequencies#Channel_frequencies" title="North American television frequencies">broadcast TV channels</a> 13 and 14, which are 210–216 MHz and 470–476 MHz respectively. Propagation is similar to the lowest UHF TV Broadcast channels.</p>
<p>Additionally, this band can be easily received by simply tuning any cable-ready analog television or cable-box to the cable TV channels below and connecting an outdoor TV antenna. Amateur TV signals are much weaker than broadcast TV, so a <a href="/wiki/Preamplifier" title="Preamplifier">preamplifier</a> is often used to improve reception.</p>

<p>Usage notes:</p>
<ol>
<li>In Canada and areas of the US north of a designated "Line A" boundary, amateurs are not allowed to transmit on these channels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITUv1p38_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITUv1p38-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Usually used as an ATV repeater output. Expensive VSB filters must be used on this channel to keep the signal inside the ham band.</li>
<li>Channels 58 and 59 are often offset in frequency to limit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_frequency_interference" title="Radio frequency interference">interference</a> to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Weak-signal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Weak-signal (page does not exist)">weak-signal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_satellite" title="Amateur radio satellite">amateur radio satellite</a> sub-bands (431–433 &amp; 435–438 MHz respectively). Many modern CATV receivers can still lock-on to frequencies offset as much as 1 MHz.</li>
<li>Rarely used today due to heavy FM repeater use in this range.</li>
<li>For technical reasons, a maximum of two channels may be simultaneously used within a given geographic area, and the video carrier frequencies must be at least 12 MHz apart for the signals not to interfere with each other.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="33_cm_band">33 cm band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 33 cm band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/33-centimeter_band" title="33-centimeter band">33-centimeter band</a> (902-928 MHz) is next highest frequency ham band available for ATV in North America. This ham band is unique to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union_region" title="International Telecommunication Union region">ITU Region 2</a>, and it is rarely available for amateur use in ITU Regions 1 or 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITUv1-33cm_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITUv1-33cm-7">[7]</a></sup> This band is also shared with many users, including <a href="/wiki/ISM_band" title="ISM band">ISM</a> devices and unlicensed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Part_15" title="Part 15">Part 15</a> users, so interference issues are more likely than on other bands.</p>
<p>These channels can be received by many newer analog cable-boxes and televisions, which can tune to channels above 125.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="23_cm_band">23 cm band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 23 cm band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/23-centimeter_band" title="23-centimeter band">23-centimeter band</a> (1240-1300 MHz) is the third highest frequency band available for ATV. Analog big-dish satellite television (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TVRO" title="TVRO">TVRO</a>) receivers may be re-purposed for inexpensively receiving ATV in this band. Such receivers can decode FM television when an outdoor antenna is connected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_noise_block-downconverter" title="Low noise block-downconverter">LNB</a> input. Due to the low cost and ease of repurposing old analog satellite receivers, this is the most popular band for FM amateur TV.</p>
<p>Commonly used 23 cm FM channels:</p>
<ul>
<li>1,255 MHz</li>
<li>1,265 MHz</li>
</ul>
<p>This band is also used for AM/VSB television, although this requires a specialized receiver.</p>

<p>Usage notes:</p>
<ol>
<li>Expensive VSB filters must be used on this channel to keep the signal inside the ham band.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_amateur_radio_bands">Other amateur radio bands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other amateur radio bands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the above, there are other ham bands which are less commonly used for ATV:</p>
<ul>
<li>On the <a href="/wiki/13-centimeter_band" title="13-centimeter band">13-centimeter band</a>:
<ul>
<li>2,417.5 MHz is used for ATV links.</li>
<li>2,441.5 MHz is the band's most used FM ATV frequency. A 6.0 MHz audio <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-carrier" title="Sub-carrier">sub-carrier</a> and 4 MHz deviation are used.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/9-centimeter_band" title="9-centimeter band">9 centimeter (3 GHz)</a> and <a href="/wiki/5-centimeter_band" title="5-centimeter band">5 centimeter (5 GHz)</a> bands have ATV links in some areas.</li>
<li>On the <a href="/wiki/3-centimeter_band" title="3-centimeter band">3-centimeter band</a>, 10.4 GHz is a wideband FM channel, and it may be used as an ATV repeater input.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_information">Other information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The distance record for ATV is between Hawaii and California on 434 MHz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Experiments with <a href="/wiki/Digital_television" title="Digital television">digital modes</a> have lagged somewhat behind those in Europe, but have taken on some new urgency given the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DTV_transition_in_the_United_States" title="DTV transition in the United States">transition of broadcast television</a>. WR8ATV currently has an output using <a href="/wiki/DVB-S" title="DVB-S">DVB-S</a>, which is believed to be the first, and currently only, D-ATV repeater in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_context">European context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: European context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, which generally has a narrower <a href="/wiki/70-centimeter_band" title="70-centimeter band">70 cm</a> allocation than the USA, the majority of amateur television operation is currently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_modulated" title="Frequency modulated">frequency modulated</a> on <span class="nowrap">1.2 GHz</span> and above. The frequencies in use depend on national permissions. In most of mainland Europe, the most common frequency is 1255 MHz. Other bands commonly used for ATV are the <a href="/wiki/13cm" title="13cm">13cm</a> (<span class="nowrap">~2.3–2.45 GHz</span>) and <a href="/wiki/3-centimeter_band" title="3-centimeter band">3-cm</a> (<span class="nowrap">~10 GHz</span>) bands, although ATV is used on most of the microwave bands.</p>
<p>In several countries <a href="/wiki/Crossband_operation" title="Crossband operation">cross-band</a> <a href="/wiki/Repeater" title="Repeater">repeaters</a> are used, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amplitude_Modulation" title="Amplitude Modulation">AM</a> inputs on 430 MHz and <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">FM</a> outputs on <span class="nowrap">1255 MHz</span>, others have FM-ATV inputs on <span class="nowrap">13 cm</span> and outputs on <span class="nowrap">3 cm</span>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, much activity occurs using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-band" title="In-band">in-band</a> <a href="/wiki/Repeater" title="Repeater">repeaters</a>. These generally have an input of 1248, 1249 or 1255 MHz and typically output at 1308, 1312 or 1316 MHz, although other frequencies are also used. <a href="/wiki/Simplex" title="Simplex">Simplex</a> operation occurs on these or other frequencies chosen to avoid interference with other users of the band, e.g. <span class="nowrap">1285 MHz</span>. Recent <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> have been done with <a href="/wiki/Digital_television" title="Digital television">digital</a> modes following widely adopted <a href="/wiki/DVB-S" title="DVB-S">DVB-S</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVB-T" title="DVB-T">DVB-T</a> standards. These new DATV transmissions need less spectrum bandwidth than FM-ATV and offer superior picture quality. However, the unavoidable processing delays caused by the temporal compression mean that DATV signals have a second or more of time lag, which can make real-time video conversations feel much less natural than the 'instantaneous' analogue system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission_characteristics">Transmission characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transmission characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_modulated" title="Frequency modulated">frequency modulated</a> <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">TV</a> is used on frequencies above 1,240 MHz (1.24 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">GHz</a>), where there is enough <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> for such <a href="/wiki/Wideband" title="Wideband">wideband</a> <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">transmissions</a>. This is often used as a repeater's input <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>, with output being standard <a href="/wiki/Single-sideband_modulation#Vestigial_sideband_.28VSB.29" title="Single-sideband modulation">VSB</a> on the four channels listed above.</p>
<p>In a nutshell</p>
<ul>
<li>below 1.24 GHz: Vestigial Sideband</li>
<li>above 1.24 GHz: <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">FM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phase-shift_keying" title="Phase-shift keying">PSK</a> etc...</li>
</ul>
<p>The quality of transmission is expressed as a "p level"; "p" standing for "picture". P levels range from zero to five, increasing as the picture becomes more viewable. P-0 signifies a state in which <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sync_bar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sync bar (page does not exist)">sync bars</a> are visible, but the picture is too snowy to be seen; this occurs at a minimum signal strength of 3 dB. Each level represents an increase of 6 dB over the previous; P-5 is 30 dB above P-0 and represents a perfectly clear picture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_television&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As transmission frequency increases, atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Path_loss" title="Path loss">path losses</a> become greater, particularly at frequencies above 10 GHz. Additionally, long-distance propagation by <a href="/wiki/F_region" title="F region">F-layer</a> ionospheric skip over the horizon does not typically occur at higher frequencies, and terrain and man-made structures can affect propagation of signals, blocking or redirecting signals. Factors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-skip" title="E-skip">E-layer skip</a> propagation, <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_propagation" title="Tropospheric propagation">tropospheric enhancement</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Knife-edge_effect" title="Knife-edge effect">knife-edge diffraction</a> can extend the useful range of signals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Content produced by ATV has included:</p>
<ul>
<li>From 1968 to about 2004 amateur TV provided behind-the-scenes co-ordination for the annual New Year's Day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tournament_of_Roses_Parade" title="Tournament of Roses Parade">Tournament of Roses Parade</a> in Pasadena, CA.</li>
<li>HAM TV—as it is also called—provides video co-ordination of many public service events and, along with traditional amateur radio, provides much needed "eyes" in natural disasters.</li>
<li>U.S. stations often retransmitted <a href="/wiki/NASA_TV" title="NASA TV">NASA TV</a> while they were not in use, especially when there were <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">missions</a>.</li>
<li>In recent history, amateur TV has found renewed interest in the radio-controlled hobby world as a method for transmitting <a href="/wiki/First-person_view_(radio_control)" title="First-person view (radio control)">FPV video</a>, for use when flying remote control aircraft or piloting other RC vehicles.</li>
</ul>
