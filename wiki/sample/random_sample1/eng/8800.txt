<p>The <b>AR-10</b> is a <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62×51mm NATO">7.62×51mm NATO</a> <a href="/wiki/Battle_rifle" title="Battle rifle">battle rifle</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Stoner" title="Eugene Stoner">Eugene Stoner</a> in the late 1950s at <a href="/wiki/ArmaLite" title="ArmaLite">ArmaLite</a>, then a division of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairchild_(aircraft_manufacturer)" title="Fairchild (aircraft manufacturer)">Fairchild Aircraft Corporation</a>. When first introduced in 1956, the AR-10 used an innovative straight-line barrel/stock design with <a href="/wiki/Phenol_formaldehyde_resin#Applications" title="Phenol formaldehyde resin">phenolic composite</a> and forged alloy parts resulting in a small arm significantly easier to control in automatic fire and over 1 lb (0.45 kg) lighter than other infantry rifles of the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula.2C_Sam_pp._27-29_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula.2C_Sam_pp._27-29-1">[1]</a></sup> Over its production life, the original AR-10 was built in relatively small numbers, with fewer than 9,900 rifles assembled.</p>
<p>In 1957, the basic AR-10 design was rescaled and substantially modified by ArmaLite to accommodate the <a href="/wiki/.223_Remington" title=".223 Remington">.223 Remington</a> cartridge, and given the designation <a href="/wiki/AR-15" title="AR-15">AR-15</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula88-90_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula88-90-2">[2]</a></sup> ArmaLite licensed the AR-10 and AR-15 designs to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colt_Firearms" title="Colt Firearms">Colt Firearms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArmaLite_History_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArmaLite_History-3">[3]</a></sup> The AR-15 eventually became the <a href="/wiki/M16_rifle" title="M16 rifle">M16 rifle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nodakspud.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nodakspud.com-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m-14parts.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-m-14parts.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AR-10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>ArmaLite began as a small engineering concern founded by George Sullivan, the patent counsel for Lockheed Corporation, and funded by Fairchild Engine and Airplane Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula23-26_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula23-26-7">[7]</a></sup> On October 1, 1954, the company was incorporated as the ArmaLite Corporation, becoming a subdivision of Fairchild. With its limited capital and tiny machine shop, ArmaLite was never intended to be an arms manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula23-26_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula23-26-7">[7]</a></sup> The company focused on producing small arms concepts and designs to be sold or licensed to other manufacturers. Sullivan leased a small machine shop at 6567 Santa Monica Boulevard in Hollywood, California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> hired several employees, and began work on a prototype for a lightweight survival rifle for use by downed aircrew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula23-26_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula23-26-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>While testing the prototype of the <a href="/wiki/Armalite_AR-5" title="Armalite AR-5">Armalite AR-5</a> survival rifle design at a local shooting range, Sullivan met <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Stoner" title="Eugene Stoner">Eugene Stoner</a>, a talented small arms inventor, whom Sullivan immediately hired to be ArmaLite's chief design engineer. At the time, ArmaLite Inc. was a very small organization (as late as 1956 it had only nine employees, including Stoner).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula23-26_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula23-26-7">[7]</a></sup> With Stoner as chief design engineer, ArmaLite quickly released a number of interesting rifle concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula30-36_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula30-36-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first prototypes of the 7.62 mm AR-10 emerged during 1955 and early 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At the time the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> was in the midst of testing several rifles to replace the obsolete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1_Garand_rifle" title="M1 Garand rifle">M1 Garand</a>. <a href="/wiki/Springfield_Armory" title="Springfield Armory">Springfield Armory</a>'s T44E4 and heavier T44E5 were essentially updated versions of the Garand chambered for the new 7.62 mm round, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrique_Nationale" title="Fabrique Nationale">Fabrique Nationale</a> submitted their <a href="/wiki/FN_FAL" title="FN FAL">FN FAL</a> as the T48.</p>
<p>ArmaLite's AR-10 entered the competition late, hurriedly submitting two hand-built 'production' AR-10 rifles based on the fourth prototype in the fall of 1956 to the United States Army's Springfield Armory for testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula36.2C_38_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula36.2C_38-11">[11]</a></sup> The AR-10 prototypes (four in all) featured a straight-line stock design, rugged elevated sights, an oversized aluminum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Flash_suppressor" title="Flash suppressor">flash suppressor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_brake" title="Muzzle brake">recoil compensator</a>, and an adjustable gas system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula27-30_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula27-30-13">[13]</a></sup> In the fourth and final prototype, the upper and lower receiver were hinged with the now-familiar hinge and takedown pins, and the charging handle did not reciprocate and was not attached to the bolt carrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula36.2C_38_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula36.2C_38-11">[11]</a></sup> For a 7.62mm NATO rifle, the AR-10 prototype was incredibly lightweight at only 6.85 lbs. empty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula36.2C_38_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula36.2C_38-11">[11]</a></sup> Initial comments by Springfield Armory test staff were favorable, and some testers commented that the AR-10 was the best lightweight automatic rifle ever tested by the Armory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula39-40_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula39-40-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unfortunately for ArmaLite, the rifle's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>/<a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> composite barrel (an untried prototype design specified for the tests by ArmaLite's president, George Sullivan, over Stoner's vehement objections) burst in a <a href="/wiki/Quality_control#Failure_testing" title="Quality control">torture test</a> conducted by Springfield Armory in early 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> ArmaLite quickly replaced it with a conventional steel barrel, but the damage had been done. The final Springfield Armory report advised against adoption of the rifle, stating that "it would take five years or more to take it through tests to adoption".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula39-40_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula39-40-15">[15]</a></sup> While ArmaLite objected, it was clear that the AR-10, a brand-new rifle still in the prototype stage, was at a disadvantage compared to competing designs with longer development cycles, and by 1957, U.S. Army infantry forces urgently required a modern, magazine-fed infantry rifle to replace the M1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula39-40_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula39-40-15">[15]</a></sup> In the end the Army chose the conventional T44, which entered production as the <a href="/wiki/M14_rifle" title="M14 rifle">M14 rifle</a> in 1957. That same year, ArmaLite completed about 50 production AR-10 rifles at its workshop for use as demonstrator models for its sales agents, including <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Cummings" title="Samuel Cummings">Samuel Cummings</a>, a famous international arms dealer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula46_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula46-17">[17]</a></sup> Attempts to rush completion of these fifty rifles resulted in a few units that were assembled with improperly machined barrel extensions, a defect that went unnoticed at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula46_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula46-17">[17]</a></sup> These production rifles built at ArmaLite's workshop in Hollywood would later become known as the <b>Hollywood</b> model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula36.2C_38_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula36.2C_38-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 4, 1957, Fairchild ArmaLite sold a five-year manufacturing license for the AR-10 to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> arms manufacturer, <b>Artillerie Inrichtingen</b> (A.I.). With its large factory and production facilities, A.I. could produce the ArmaLite rifle in the large quantities for which Fairchild expected orders would be forthcoming.</p>
<p>In 1957 Cummings secured an order of 7,500 AR-10 rifles from Nicaragua, with an initial delivery of 1,000 rifles to be delivered before January 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula45_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula45-18">[18]</a></sup> The order was contingent on a successful completion of a 7,500-round endurance test. With the AR-10 in short supply, Cummings left his personal demonstrator rifle with Nicaragua's chief military commander, General <a href="/wiki/Anastasio_Somoza_Debayle" title="Anastasio Somoza Debayle">Anastasio Somoza</a>, who would personally conduct the endurance test trial. While General Somoza was firing this rifle for the trial, the bolt lug over the ejector sheared off and flew past Somoza's head. The general angrily returned Cummings' AR-10 and canceled the entire Nicaraguan order. The remaining Hollywood rifles were inspected and refitted as necessary with new parts to prevent reoccurrence of the bolt lug failure, but the Nicaraguan order was lost for good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula46_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula46-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A.I. officials meanwhile discovered a number of manufacturing and production issues in the Hollywood version of the AR-10, all of which had to be resolved before large-scale production could commence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula46.2C_50_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula46.2C_50-19">[19]</a></sup> In addition to designing and building tooling for the rifle, the design had to be converted to metric dimensions and subcontractors had to be found to supply materials or manufacture component parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula50_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula50-20">[20]</a></sup> ArmaLite also continued to send A.I. product improvement requests, including an adjustable regulator, a repositioned gas tube, and a new three-prong flash suppressor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula46.2C_50_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula46.2C_50-19">[19]</a></sup> Accessories such as flash hiders, bayonet mounts, sling swivels, and sight graduations had to be designed.</p>
<p>Firearms historians have separated AR-10 production under the A.I. license into at least three basic identifiable versions, along with various sporting, <a href="/wiki/Carbine" title="Carbine">carbine</a>, and other experimental designs and calibers. The three main variants have been termed the <b>Sudanese</b> model, the <b>Transitional</b>, and the <b>Portuguese</b> model AR-10. A.I. built all of these rifles, beginning with the Sudanese model AR-10. The Sudanese version derives its name from the sale of approximately 2,500 AR-10 rifles of to the government of <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> in 1958. The Sudanese model was equipped with a very lightweight, fluted steel barrel fitted with a trim, prong-style flash suppressor, a bayonet lug, lightweight fiberglass furniture, and sight graduations in Arabic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula78_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula78-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula53_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula53-22">[22]</a></sup> The Sudanese model weighed only 3.3 kg (7.3 lb) with an empty magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula78_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula78-21">[21]</a></sup> The price, including cleaning kit and four magazines, was US $225 per rifle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula.2C_Sam_pp._50-51_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula.2C_Sam_pp._50-51-23">[23]</a></sup> All AR-10s, whether produced by ArmaLite or by A.I., used the same Stoner-designed 20-round lightweight aluminum 'waffle' magazine with pressed-in, corrugated sides, intended to be discarded in combat once emptied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>AR-10 production was limited, though <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_Guatemala" title="Armed Forces of Guatemala">Guatemala</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula45_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula45-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Burma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_Armed_Forces" title="Italian Armed Forces">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudan_Armed_Forces" title="Sudan Armed Forces">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Armed_Forces" title="Portuguese Armed Forces">Portugal</a> all purchased AR-10 rifles for limited issue to their military forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula78_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula78-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula72-73_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula72-73-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula75_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula75-26">[26]</a></sup> Sudanese AR-10s were employed in frequent clashes with guerrilla forces and conflicts with neighboring countries, and a few captured rifles eventually turned up in unofficial service with various African and colonial armies, police, and guerrilla forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula81_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula81-27">[27]</a></sup> The AR-10 remained in service with Sudanese Special Forces until 1985.</p>
<p>In 1958, a special <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9739mm" title="7.62×39mm">7.62×39mm</a> caliber variant of the Sudanese AR-10 was produced in very small numbers for evaluation by <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula55_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula55-28">[28]</a></sup> That same year, an AR-10 with a 16" barrel was developed in response to a request by <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> Airlines for a carbine that could be issued to their crew for transpolar flights as part of an Arctic survival kit, and approximately 30 carbines were eventually produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula72-73_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula72-73-25">[25]</a></sup> A number of Transitional AR-10s were also fitted with a folding bipod designed to lie flat under the forearm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Italian Navy acquired the AR-10 for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COMSUBIN" title="COMSUBIN">COMSUBIN</a> underwater commando teams. Germany, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, Finland, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> also purchased small numbers of the AR-10 for test purposes, and Cuba's <a href="/wiki/Fulgencio_Batista" title="Fulgencio Batista">Batista</a> government ordered 100 "Transitional" model rifles in 1958. The Cuban order was delivered to Havana, but in December 1958 <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>'s forces took control of the country, including the warehouse containing the AR-10 shipment. In 1959, in response to a letter from ArmaLite's sales agent Sam Cummings, Castro invited Cummings to Havana to discuss payment for the AR-10 rifles. Fidel, his brother <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Castro" title="Raúl Castro">Raúl</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a> test-fired the AR-10 outside Havana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula72-73_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula72-73-25">[25]</a></sup> Impressed by the weapon's firepower,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Castro paid Cummings for all 100 rifles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula72-73_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula72-73-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, rapidly worsening relations with the United States eliminated any chance of future AR-10 sales to Cuba, and Castro transferred the ex-Batista AR-10s to a group of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communist</a> revolutionaries from the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>. In June 1959, the rebels, led by Cuban officers, invaded the Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula72-73_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula72-73-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brogan89-92_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brogan89-92-31">[31]</a></sup> The invaders were defeated by the Dominican army, and AR-10 rifles from the Batista shipment were found on the bodies of guerrillas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula72-73_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula72-73-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brogan89-92_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brogan89-92-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The final Artillerie Inrichtingen design is known as the <i>Portuguese</i> model AR-10. This final version incorporated all that had been learned to date about the AR-10, including infantry service rifle and field test reports. In addition to a heavier barrel with chrome-lined chamber, optional bipod, and the removable plastic/metal handguards of the Transitional model, the Portuguese variant had wider bolt lugs, a stronger extractor, a new simplified three-position gas regulator, and a cocking handle featuring a forward bolt assist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> It is believed that approximately 4,000-5,000 Portuguese variants were produced; nearly all of them were sold to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_National_Defence_(Portugal)" title="Ministry of National Defence (Portugal)">Portuguese National Defense Ministry</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>-based arms dealer SIDEM International in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula75_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula75-26">[26]</a></sup> The AR-10 was officially adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Parachute_Troops_School" title="Parachute Troops School">Portuguese paratrooper battalions</a> (<i>Caçadores páraquedistas</i>), and the rifle saw considerable combat service in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_Wars" title="Portuguese Colonial Wars">Portugal's counter-insurgency campaigns in Angola and Mozambique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Afonso_2000_pp._183-184_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Afonso_2000_pp._183-184-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In U.S. Army tests at <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Proving_Ground" title="Aberdeen Proving Ground">Aberdeen Proving Ground</a> in November 1960, and later in Portuguese service, the AR-10 gained a reputation for accuracy (some rifles would group into 25 mm (1 inch) at 100 meters with service ammunition).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Portuguese paratroopers found the AR-10 to be not only accurate but reliable in combat, despite rugged service conditions in African jungle and savannah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few Portuguese and Sudanese model AR-10s found their way by various means to nearby African countries; in <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, the AR-10 was much appreciated by members of the <a href="/wiki/French_Foreign_Legion" title="French Foreign Legion">French Foreign Legion</a>. As one police instructor in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congo</a> stated, "It was a good combat weapon that never failed me; a bit too long (but not as bad as the FAL or M14) for house-to-house work or really heavy brush, but great for 400-800 meters, in the flats - and really nice on the body, after wandering around 12-14 hours looking for bad guys."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula81_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula81-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Portuguese-model AR-10s were fitted with A.I.-modified upper receivers in order to mount 3× or 3.6× telescopic sights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula79_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula79-38">[38]</a></sup> These rifles were used by marksmen accompanying small patrols to eliminate individual enemies at extended ranges in open country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula80_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula80-39">[39]</a></sup> Other AR-10s were used by the paratroopers in a secondary role to launch <a href="/wiki/Rifle_grenade" title="Rifle grenade">rifle grenades</a>. The AR-10's built-in gas cutoff design enabled it to fire Energa rifle grenades without adjustment of the gas system, and the self-loading action would even eject the spent blank shells and load the next one, allowing several grenades to be quickly fired. The added recoil took its toll on rifle stocks, and some Portuguese rifles were retrofitted with all-metal buttstocks to better withstand the strain caused by firing the heavy grenades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula80_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula80-39">[39]</a></sup> Additional sales of the AR-10 rifle were stymied after the Netherlands embargoed further shipments of the rifle to Portugal. Paratroopers deploying to Africa in later years were subsequently issued a collapsible stock version of the German <a href="/wiki/Heckler_%26_Koch_G3" title="Heckler &amp; Koch G3">Heckler &amp; Koch G3</a> rifle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Afonso_2000_pp._183-184_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Afonso_2000_pp._183-184-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula79_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula79-38">[38]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the AR-10 continued in service with a few Portuguese airborne units, and was in use as late as 1975 in the Portuguese Timor (East Timor) decolonization emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fairchild-ArmaLite was dissatisfied with the delays in setting up the tooling and production at A.I. for the AR-10, and made it clear that they would not be renewing A.I.'s license to produce the rifle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula88-90_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula88-90-2">[2]</a></sup> By 1960, hampered by Dutch export restrictions and discouraged by the lack of arms sales to major national purchasers, Artillerie Inrichtingen decided to exit the small arms production business altogether, and ceased all production of the AR-10 under its license from Fairchild-ArmaLite. By that time, fewer than 10,000 AR-10s had been produced, mostly military select-fire rifles, with a few semi-automatic only rifles produced for civilian use. All A.I. AR-10 parts inventories, tooling, and prototypes were either sold or scrapped; the barrel tooling was sold to Israel sometime in the early 1960s. All AR-10 production records, design drawings, manuals, literature, and other publications then in inventory were discarded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula88-90_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula88-90-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In later years, some ex-military Sudanese and Portuguese model AR-10s were sold to civilian markets in the U.S., <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Nearly all of the rifles imported to the latter three countries had their full-automatic fire selector disabled. Subsequently, as many as 2,500 Australian AR-10 rifles may have been confiscated and destroyed as a result of more restrictive firearms legislation passed in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the AR-10 ex-military rifles shipped to the USA were in the form of parts kits, having been previously dismantled, though a few were legally imported as <a href="/wiki/National_Firearms_Act" title="National Firearms Act">National Firearms Act</a> (NFA) weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula98-99_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula98-99-42">[42]</a></sup> Large numbers of AR-10 7.62 mm magazines were imported as well. Many of these kit rifles were combined with various semi-auto receivers made by civilian manufacturers in order to permit legal ownership. During the early 1980s, Paragon Sales and Services in <a href="/wiki/Joliet,_Illinois" title="Joliet, Illinois">Joliet, Illinois</a> manufactured new semi-automatic lower receivers while using imported upper AR-10 receivers originally manufactured by Artillerie-Inrichtingen in the Netherlands.</p>
<p>In 1957, in an effort to increase profits from the ArmaLite venture, Fairchild decided to compete in the U.S. Army's CONARC requirement for a new six-pound, high-velocity, .22-caliber selective-fire rifle accurate to 500 yards. In response, ArmaLite engineers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_Stoner" title="Gene Stoner">Gene Stoner</a>, <a href="/wiki/L._James_Sullivan" title="L. James Sullivan">Jim Sullivan</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Fremont&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bob Fremont (page does not exist)">Bob Fremont</a> used the basic AR-10 design to produce the AR-15 in .223 Remington, which was completed as a firing prototype in 1958. Fairchild-Armalite continued its efforts to sell both the AR-10 and AR-15 to various military forces around the world. However, the AR-10 rifle marketed by ArmaLite after 1958 was not the product-improved AR-10 developed by Artillerie Inrichtingen, but rather a design scaled-up from AR-15 plans and specifications, the AR-10a. None of the improvements incorporated by Artillerie Inrichtingen over three years of production was used in the new AR-10A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula89-90_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula89-90-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the AR-10a did not benefit from the modifications undertaken by the Dutch licensee, it had some differences from the AR-15 (besides caliber and part dimensions), including a different bolt, trigger, and cocking handle design, along with a magazine canted forward at a five-degree angle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pikula89-90_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pikula89-90-43">[43]</a></sup> While interest in the AR-15 was considerable, the AR-10a failed to attract any orders from domestic or foreign military customers. In 1959, ArmaLite sold its rights to the AR-10/AR-15 to <a href="/wiki/Colt%27s_Manufacturing_Company" title="Colt's Manufacturing Company">Colt Firearms</a>, who successfully marketed the AR-15 to the U.S. military. Eugene Stoner left the company in 1961. Disappointed with ArmaLite's marketing efforts, Fairchild sold its share of ArmaLite in 1962; George Sullivan bought it with the financial backing of Capital Southwest Corporation of Dallas, Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_details">Design details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AR-10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AR-10 is a lightweight, air-cooled, magazine-fed, <a href="/wiki/Gas-operated_reloading" title="Gas-operated reloading">gas-operated</a> rifle that uses a piston within the bolt carrier with a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_bolt" title="Rotating bolt">rotary bolt</a> locking mechanism. The rifle has a conventional layout; it features an in-line stock, an aluminum alloy receiver and a reinforced <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> pistol grip, handguard, and buttstock. While mostly an original design, the AR-10 built upon previously proven concepts. From the FAL it took the hinged receiver system allowing the rifle to be opened for cleaning much like a break-action shotgun. The ejection port cover is similar to that found on the German <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>-era <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StG44" title="StG44">StG44</a>. The bolt locking mechanism is similar to the <a href="/wiki/M1941_Johnson_rifle" title="M1941 Johnson rifle">M1941 Johnson rifle</a> (itself an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/John_Browning" title="John Browning">Browning</a>-designed <a href="/wiki/Remington_Model_8" title="Remington Model 8">Remington Model 8</a> bolt). From the German <a href="/wiki/FG_42" title="FG 42">FG 42</a> and <a href="/wiki/M1941_Johnson_machine_gun" title="M1941 Johnson machine gun">M1941 Johnson machine gun</a> came the idea of straight-line stocks to reduce muzzle climb in fully automatic fire. The AR-10's method of rotary bolt locking, straight-line recoil, and gas operation enhanced its inherent accuracy.</p>
<p>The gas system, bolt carrier, and bolt-locking design were novel for the time. Most gas-operated rifles divert combustion gases from a port in the barrel to a piston and cylinder arrangement adjacent to the port. In Stoner's design, the gas travels from a port near the middle of the barrel through a steel tube back into the receiver. The gas enters a chamber formed between the rear of the bolt and the inside of the bolt carrier. Once the bolt carrier moves to the rear a small distance, excess gas is vented through holes in its side. This use of the bolt and bolt carrier for the separate actions of a piston and gas cylinder simplified construction and saved weight. Movement of the bolt carrier was in-line with the bore, greatly improving inherent accuracy, as well as keeping the rifle on target as the gun fired. Since the straight-line stock placed the shooter's eye well above the barrel the rifle's sights were mounted high, with the rear sight contained in a carrying handle that also protected the cocking/charging lever.</p>
<p>The receiver is made from forged and machined aluminum to reduce weight. The bolt locks into an extension on the barrel instead of the receiver allowing for a lightweight receiver while not compromising the strength of the bolt locking mechanism. On a few prototype guns, an all-aluminum ("Sullaloy") barrel was fitted at the insistence of George Sullivan – ArmaLite's president – though after the 1957 Springfield Armory tests, all production AR-10s were fitted with standard steel barrels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The stock is made from a phenolic composite reinforced fiberglass with a core of rigid plastic foam. The handguards and pistol grip are also fiberglass. Fairchild was an aircraft manufacturer, and the use of plastics, titanium, and aluminum were common in the aircraft industry at the time, though not generally used in firearms.</p>
<p>Several experimental prototypes of a belt-fed variant of the AR-10 were developed by ArmaLite and A.I. engineers, informally designated the <b>AR-10 LMG</b>. In one version, the belt was fed by a feed-chute connected to a 250-round ammo box carried on the user's back. All of the belt-fed prototypes experienced numerous issues with feed malfunctions and parts breakages, and these problems were never wholly solved during the weapon's development stage, which ended with the termination of A.I.'s production contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIK1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-PIK1-46">[46]</a></sup> As a result, no AR-10 LMG variants were ever sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_AR-10_production">Legacy AR-10 production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AR-10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy AR-10 production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ArmaLite_AR-10B_rifle_series">ArmaLite AR-10B rifle series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AR-10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: ArmaLite AR-10B rifle series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="281" data-file-width="1024" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg/220px-AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg/330px-AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg/440px-AR-10T---Dragunova-20160409.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In 1995, former <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Ordnance Corps (United States Army)">Army Ordnance</a> officer Mark Westrom, owner of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eagle_Arms&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eagle Arms (page does not exist)">Eagle Arms</a>, purchased the ArmaLite brand and the company became ArmaLite Inc. Shortly thereafter, ArmaLite Inc. introduced a completely new rifle, collectively known as the AR-10B rifle series. Notably, the AR-10B was not designed using the original AR-10 drawings from either ArmaLite or Artillerie Inrichtingen, but instead was based on the Colt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AR-15_variants" title="AR-15 variants">AR-15A2</a>, with parts scaled up or redesigned as needed to fire the 7.62×51mm NATO (.308 Winchester) cartridge. The AR-10B prototype was composed of individual sub-components tested on a special lower receiver made of two slabs of aluminum fitted to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_Armament_Company" title="Knights Armament Company">Knights Armament Company</a> <a href="/wiki/SR-25" title="SR-25">SR-25</a> upper receiver assembly, and prototyped using computer analysis. The full prototype AR-10B was the first rifle off the production line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArmaLite_History_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArmaLite_History-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1995, the new ArmaLite company has also incorporated various other design and engineering improvements to the AR-10, including a newly designed steel magazine derived from the model used on the U.S. M14 rifle. The current ArmaLite AR-10 is offered in several versions including an A2 and A4 rifle or carbine with collapsible stock, a target model (AR-10T), a 'retro' AR-10B with Sudanese AR-10-style handguard and cocking lever (limited production) and one version chambered in <a href="/wiki/.300_Remington_SA_Ultra_Mag" title=".300 Remington SA Ultra Mag">300 Remington SAUM</a>. Current users include the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Provincial_Police" title="Ontario Provincial Police">Ontario Provincial Police</a> Tactics and Rescue Unit.</p>
<p>While ArmaLite Inc. holds a US trademark on the name "AR-10",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> other rifle manufacturers currently produce 7.62×51mm NATO auto-loading rifles that are based generally on the AR-10 design: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panther_Arms" title="Panther Arms">DPMS LR-308</a>, Knights Armament Corporation <a href="/wiki/SR-25" title="SR-25">SR-25</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_River_Arms" title="Rock River Arms">Rock River Arms</a> LAR-8, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=JP_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="JP Enterprises (page does not exist)">JP Enterprises</a> LRP-07, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Spirit_Arms&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Spirit Arms (page does not exist)">American Spirit Arms</a> ASA .308, Fulton Armory Titan, LWRC's R.E.P.R., LaRue Tactical's OBR, RND Manufacturing's "The Edge", the German Oberland Arms OA-10, the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Machine_and_Tool_Company" title="Lewis Machine and Tool Company">Lewis Machine and Tool</a> LM308MWS, Bushmaster MOE 16 .308, Palmetto State Armory PA-10, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seekins_SP-10&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seekins SP-10 (page does not exist)">Seekins SP-10</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_M%26P10" title="Smith &amp; Wesson M&amp;P10">Smith &amp; Wesson M&amp;P10</a>, and Windham Weaponry SRC-308. These rifles differ from both the current and original Armalite AR10 in a few minor dimensions that make the Armalite AR10B not as modular as the rest of the AR based firearms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_Calibers">Alternate Calibers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AR-10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternate Calibers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to other manufacturers producing 7.62x51mm NATO AR-10 variants, numerous other chamberings are now being offered. Common examples of these alternate calibers include <a href="/wiki/.243_Winchester" title=".243 Winchester">.243 Winchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/.260_Remington" title=".260 Remington">.260 Remington</a>, <a href="/wiki/6.5mm_Creedmoor" title="6.5mm Creedmoor">6.5mm Creedmoor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/.338_Federal" title=".338 Federal">.338 Federal</a>. Manufacturers of these barrels include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ballistic_Advantage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ballistic Advantage (page does not exist)">Ballistic Advantage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Combat" title="Wilson Combat">Wilson Combat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DPMS_Panther_Arms" title="DPMS Panther Arms">DPMS Panther Arms</a>.</p>
