<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Amalek</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Amalek</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" style="white-space:nowrap;" xml:lang="he">עֲמָלֵק</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Modern</a></small> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="he transliteration">Amalek</span></i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Tiberian_vocalization" title="Tiberian vocalization">Tiberian</a></small> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Hebrew transliteration">ʻĂmālēq</span></i>) occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and refers to a grandson of <a href="/wiki/Esau" title="Esau">Esau</a>, the descendant nation of <i>Amalekites</i>, and the territories of Amalek which they inhabited.</p>
<p>The Old Testament, accepted by Jews and Christians, describes the Amalekites as a nomadic tribe which lived in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Israel" title="Ancient Israel">ancient Israel</a> and in the land called Moab, what the Romans called <a href="/wiki/Arabia_Petraea" title="Arabia Petraea">Arabia Petraea</a> (Moab and the desert of Sinai), a region depopulated in the fourteenth century BCE (maybe by the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">Akhenaten</a>, who introduced a worship centered in Aten-solar deity) and then occupied by Edomites.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Chronicles" title="Books of Chronicles">1 Chronicles</a>, Amalek was the son of <a href="/wiki/Eliphaz" title="Eliphaz">Eliphaz</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a> Timna. Timna was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horite" title="Horite">Horite</a> and sister of <a href="/wiki/Lotan_son_of_Seir" title="Lotan son of Seir">Lotan</a>. Amalek appears in the genealogy of Esau (Gen. 36:12; 1 Chr. 1:36) who was the chief of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edomites" title="Edomites">Edomite</a> tribe (Gen. 36:16). Amalek is described as the "chief of Amalek" in Genesis 36:16, in which it is surmised that he ruled a clan or territory named after him. In the chant of <a href="/wiki/Balaam" title="Balaam">Balaam</a> at <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a>, 24:20, Amalek was called the 'first of the nations', attesting to high antiquity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> states: He was the first of all of them (the other nations) to war against Israel (when they came out of Egypt).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> First-century Roman-Jewish scholar and historian <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Flavius Josephus</a> refers to Amalek as a 'bastard' (νόθος) in a derogatory sense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Old Testament, the <b>Amalekites</b> were a nomadic, or seminomadic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048-4">[4]</a></sup> people who inhabited the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are commonly considered to be Amalek's descendants through the genealogy of Esau. This is probably based on the association of this tribal group with the steppe region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Israel" title="Ancient Israel">ancient Israel</a> and the area of <a href="/wiki/Kadesh" title="Kadesh">Kadesh</a> (Genesis 14:7). As a people, the Amalekites are identified as a recurrent enemy of the Israelites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMercer_Dictionary199021_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMercer_Dictionary199021-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_Amalek">Etymology of Amalek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology of Amalek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Amalek</i> may mean people of lek (עֲם , לֵק), or "dweller in the valley",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEaston189435_.27.27Am.E2.80.99alek.27.27_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEaston189435_.27.27Am.E2.80.99alek.27.27-7">[7]</a></sup> or possibly "war-like", "people of prey", "cave-men".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In some rabbinical interpretations, Amalek is etymologised as a people <i>am</i>, who <i>lick</i> blood,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but most specialists regard the origin to be unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amalekites_in_the_Hebrew_Bible">Amalekites in the Hebrew Bible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amalekites in the Hebrew Bible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amalekite_territory">Amalekite territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amalekite territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Amalekites had a nomadic lifestyle, which led to widespread distribution, mostly along the fringe of southern <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>'s agricultural zone (Num. 13:29, Judg. 12:15, 1 Sam. 15:7; 30:1-2). They also made extensive use of camels (Judg.6:5; 7:12).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048-4">[4]</a></sup> Glen Miller states, "The Amalekites were not part of Canaan...but were a nomadic tribe of marauding bands, living in the southern Negev (desert region). The archaeological data we have of sites in the Negev around the time of this event indicates a very sporadic population--although mostly in the mid-central Negev-- although widely spread out. We have evidence of about 50 'fortresses' at this time, ranging in diameter from 25-70 meters..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exegesis_of_origins">Exegesis of origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exegesis of origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is generally accepted that the Amalekites are descendants of Amalek, a grandson of Esau,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMercer_Dictionary199021_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMercer_Dictionary199021-6">[6]</a></sup> who derive their origins from <a href="/wiki/Edom" title="Edom">Edom</a> (Genesis 36:11–12, 15–16). In <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">exegesis</a> of Genesis 14:7, the use of "Amalekites" seems out of place in a passage that concerns the days of Abraham. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_scholar" title="Bible scholar">Bible scholar</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Noel_Freedman" title="David Noel Freedman">David Noel Freedman</a> considers the <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronism</a> to be an editorial insertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> explains that the writer was making a reference to the country which was afterwards inhabited by the Amalekites. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Knight_(publisher)" title="Charles Knight (publisher)">C. Knight</a> elaborates this concept by making the comparison: "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar" title="Caesar">Caesar</a> went into <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>" because <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> was afterward occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, as Gaul is present day France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight1833411_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight1833411-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternatively, during the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a>, certain Arabic writings claimed that the Amalekites existed long before Abraham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson183250_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson183250-13">[13]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/List_of_Muslim_historians" title="List of Muslim historians">Muslim historians</a> claimed that the Amalekites who fought <a href="/wiki/Joshua" title="Joshua">Joshua</a> were descendants of the inhabitants of North Africa. <a href="/wiki/Al-Masudi" title="Al-Masudi">Al-Masudi</a> said that the Amalekites originated in the region of <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> well before the days of Abraham.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Ebn Arabshah</i> purported that Amalek was a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Ham_(son_of_Noah)" title="Ham (son of Noah)">Ham</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight1833411_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight1833411-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson183250_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson183250-13">[13]</a></sup> Even medieval Jewish historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nachmanides" title="Nachmanides">Nachmanides</a> argued that the Amalekites were not descended from the grandson of Esau but from a man named Amalek, from whom the grandson took his name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is, however, possible that the name Amalek may have been given to two different nations. The Arabians mention <i>Imlik</i>, <i>Amalik</i>, or <i>Ameleka</i> among the aborigines of Arabia, the remains of which were mingled with the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Joktan" title="Joktan">Joktan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adnan" title="Adnan">Adnan</a> and became <i>Mostarabs</i> or <i>Mocarabes</i>, that is, Arabians mixed with foreigners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight1833411_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight1833411-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 19th Century, there was strong support by Western theologians for the idea that the nation of Amalek could have flourished before the time of Abraham. <a href="/wiki/Matthew_George_Easton" title="Matthew George Easton">Matthew George Easton</a> advocated that the Amalekites were not descendants of Amalek, by taking the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_literalism" title="Biblical literalism">literal approach</a> to Genesis 14:7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEaston189435.2C_.27.27Am.27alekite.27.27_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEaston189435.2C_.27.27Am.27alekite.27.27-14">[14]</a></sup> However, the modern biblical scholar <a href="/wiki/David_Noel_Freedman" title="David Noel Freedman">David Freedman</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Textual_criticism" title="Textual criticism">textual analysis</a> to glean that the use of <i>Amalekite</i> in Genesis 14:7 is actually an <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048-4">[4]</a></sup> a chronological inconsistency of (in this case) a group of people in a misplaced time. Also in the early 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Watson_(Methodist)" title="Richard Watson (Methodist)">Richard Watson</a> enumerated several speculative reasons for having a "more ancient Amalek" than Abraham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson183250_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson183250-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the exegesis of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a> 24:20 concerning Balaam's utterance: "Amalek was the first one of the nations, but his end afterward will be even his perishing", Richard Watson attempts to associate this passage to the "first one of the nations" that developed <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_narrative" title="Genesis flood narrative">post-Flood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson183250_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson183250-13">[13]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Cox_(minister)" title="Samuel Cox (minister)">Samuel Cox</a>, the Amalekites were the "first" in their hostility toward the Israelites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECox1884125-126_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECox1884125-126-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many nomadic groups from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabian_desert" title="Arabian desert">Arabian desert</a>, apparently including Amalekites, have collectively been termed "Arab(s)". While considerable knowledge about nomadic Arabs have been recovered through archeological research, no specific artifacts or sites have been linked to Amalek with any certainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200048-4">[4]</a></sup> However, it is possible that some of the fortified settlements in the Negev highlands and even <i>Tel Masos</i> (near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer-sheba" title="Beer-sheba">Beer-sheba</a>) have Amalek connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200049_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEerdmans200049-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Matthew_George_Easton" title="Matthew George Easton">Easton</a> claims that the Babylonian inscription <i>Sute</i> refers to the Amalekites, as well as the Egyptian term <i>Sittiu</i>. Easton also claims that the <i>Armana</i> tablets refer to the Amalekites under the general name <i>Khabbatti</i>, or "plunderers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEaston189435.2C_.27.27Am.27alekite.27.27_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEaston189435.2C_.27.27Am.27alekite.27.27-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaic_views_of_the_Amalekites">Judaic views of the Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Judaic views of the Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, the Amalekites came to represent the archetypal enemy of the Jews. In <a href="/wiki/Jewish_folklore" title="Jewish folklore">Jewish folklore</a> the Amalekites are considered to be the symbol of evil. This concept has been used by some hassidic <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbis</a> (particularly the <a href="/wiki/Baal_Shem_Tov" title="Baal Shem Tov">Baal Shem Tov</a>) to represent <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a> or the rejection of God. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nur_Masalha" title="Nur Masalha">Nur Masalha</a>, Elliot Horowitz and Josef Stern suggest that Amalekites have come to represent an "eternally irreconcilable enemy" that wants to murder Jews, and that Jews in post-biblical times sometimes associate contemporary enemies with Haman or Amalekites, and that some Jews believe that pre-emptive violence is acceptable against such enemies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Purim" title="Purim">Purim festival</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Esther" title="Book of Esther">Book of Esther</a> is read in the commemoration of the saving of the Jewish people from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haman_(Bible)" title="Haman (Bible)">Haman</a> (considered to be an Amalekite) who leads a plot to kill the Jews. On the basis of Exodus 17:14, where the Lord promised to "blot out the name" of Amalek, it is customary for the audience to make noise and shout whenever "Haman" is mentioned, in order to desecrate his name.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extermination_of_the_Amalekites">Extermination of the Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Extermination of the Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/613_mitzvot" title="613 mitzvot">613 mitzvot</a> (commandments) followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Jew" title="Orthodox Jew">Orthodox Jews</a>, three refer to the Amalek: to remember what the Amalekites did to the Israelites, not to forget what the Amalekites did to Israelites, and to destroy the Amalekites utterly. The rabbis derived these from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 25:17–18, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 17:14 and 1 Sam. 15:3. <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> explains the third commandment:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>From man unto woman, from infant unto suckling, from ox unto sheep, so that the name of Amalek not be mentioned even with reference to an animal by saying "This animal belonged to Amalek".</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>As enumerated by <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>, the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitzvot" title="Mitzvot">mitzvot</a> state:</p>
<dl>
<dd>598 <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0525.htm#17" rel="nofollow">Deut. 25:17</a> – Remember what Amalek did to the Israelites</dd>
<dd>599 <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0525.htm#19" rel="nofollow">Deut. 25:19</a> – Wipe out the descendants of Amalek</dd>
<dd>600 <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0525.htm#19" rel="nofollow">Deut. 25:19</a> – Not to forget Amalek's atrocities and ambush on our journey from Egypt in the desert</dd>
</dl>
<p>Some commentators have discussed the ethics of the commandment to exterminate all the Amalekites, including the command to kill all the women, children, and the notion of collective punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> explains that the commandment to destroy the nation of Amalek requires the Jewish people to peacefully request that they accept upon themselves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noachide_laws" title="Noachide laws">Noachide laws</a> and pay a tax to the Jewish kingdom. Only if they refuse must they be physically killed. Some commentators, such as Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayim_Palaggi" title="Hayim Palaggi">Hayim Palaggi</a> (1788–1869) argued that Jews had lost the tradition of distinguishing Amalekites from other people, and therefore the commandment of killing them could not practically be applied ("... We can rely on the maxim that in ancient times, <a href="/wiki/Sennacherib" title="Sennacherib">Sennacherib</a> confused the lineage of many nations." [Eynei Kol Ḥai, 73, on Sanhedrin 96b])</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenians_associated_with_Amalekites">Armenians associated with Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Armenians associated with Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This ascription of Armenians is seen in the context of indifference, which was widely criticized by the Israeli historian <a href="/wiki/Yair_Auron" title="Yair Auron">Ya'ir Oron</a> in his book <i>The Banality of Indifference</i> that dealt with the inaction of overall members of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">Armenian Genocide</a> in the beginning of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oron_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oron-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:124</sup></p>
<p>This indifference was noted inside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jews_of_Turkey" title="Jews of Turkey">Jewish community</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Adana_massacre" title="Adana massacre">1909 massacre of Armenians in Adana</a> by <a href="/wiki/Itamar_Ben-Avi" title="Itamar Ben-Avi">Itamar Ben-Avi</a>, the first native speaker of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Modern Hebrew</a> in the newspaper <a href="/wiki/HaZvi" title="HaZvi">HaZvi</a>. In an editorial named "<i>We</i>", he intervened to speak about the attitude of the Jews during the upheaval and widespread carnage that ensued during the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_countercoup_of_1909" title="Ottoman countercoup of 1909">Ottoman countercoup of 1909</a>, while protesting the general indifference to calls to help alleviate the plight of the Armenians. Outlining the attitude and loyalties of the Ottoman Jews at the time, with regards to other subjects, he wrote: "<i>We did nothing, because we were timid, because the matter did not affect us directly, utterly. Unfortunately these <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turks</a> were not Jews. Unfortunately we had covert sympathy for the enemy of the Turkish Parliament, <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Abd-Hamid II</a>. Sympathy because we believe that Abd-al Hamid would always be our friend, our generous and merciful supporter. That is why we stood aside; that is why we chose to be, in the words of the wise commander, the rearguard; that is why we continue today, two weeks after the revolution and a week after the victory of the '<a href="/wiki/Young_Turks" title="Young Turks">Young Turks</a>' to be indifferent. We are watching from the side and waiting. We are a peculiar people. Yes we!</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oron_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oron-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:125</sup> Regarding the attitude of the Jews towards the Armenians, he wrote: "<i>A slight grimace on their lips, a short heartfelt sigh, and nothing more. The Armenians are not Jews, and according to folk tradition the Armenians are nothing more than Amaleks! Amaleks? We would give them help? To whom? To Amaleks? Heaven forbid!</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oron_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oron-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:126</sup></p>
<p>It is very improbable for the Armenians to be Amalekites, as during the Iron Age they lived in the area of <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">Urartu</a> at the north of <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>. There is no genetic relation between the haplogroup subgroups of the Armenians and the population around Negev.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Jewish_Encyclopedia" title="Jewish Encyclopedia">Jewish Encyclopedia</a></i> of 1906<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> does not mention the Armenians as being Amalekites.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The tradition of identifying Armenians as Amalekites goes back to the 10th century, when it was first attested in the Byzantine Hebrew chronicle <a href="/wiki/Josippon" title="Josippon">Josippon</a> attributed to the Southern Italian Jew Joseph ben Gorion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:122</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EJ_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-EJ-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Italian rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obadiah_ben_Abraham" title="Obadiah ben Abraham">Obadiah ben Abraham</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bertinoro" title="Bertinoro">Bertinoro</a> wrote to his father of the sects in Jerusalem, to which he had made <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">aliyah</a> late in the 15th century, he listed among the Christians "the Latins, Greeks, Jacobites, Amalekites, Abyssinians." It may be that Byzantine Jews made the identification of Armenians and Amalekites to distinguish the former from the Greek Orthodox Christians, and its continued use seems primarily aimed to register the idea that the Amalekites still existed within the realm of Christendom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:122–3</sup></p>
<p>The Byzantine Emperor <a href="/wiki/Leo_V_the_Armenian" title="Leo V the Armenian">Leo V the Armenian</a>, who ruled from 813 AD to 820 AD until his assassination by one of his top generals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_the_Amorian" title="Michael the Amorian">Michael the Amorian</a>, was known as "the Amalekite" apparently because of his approval of the Islamic <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">prohibition against the depiction of sacred images</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1839 the British Jewish-Christian missionary <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wolff" title="Joseph Wolff">Joseph Wolff</a> was struck by what he thought remarkable, namely</p>
<blockquote>
<p>'that the Armenians, who are detested by the Jews as the supposed descendants of the Amalekites, are the only Christian church who have interested themselves for the protection and conversion of Jews.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:10</sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Other missionaries visiting the Holy Land that same year, namely the Scottish missionaries <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bonar" title="Andrew Bonar">Andrew Bonar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Murray_M%27Cheyne" title="Robert Murray M'Cheyne">Robert Murray M'Cheyne</a> suggested that what they saw as “the peculiar hatred which the Jews bear toward the Armenians may arise from a charge often brought against them, namely that Haman was an Armenian, and that the Armenians were the Amalekites of the Bible, attributing this to the fact that Armenians were the first nation to adopt Christianity in 301 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:10–11</sup> Late in the nineteenth century, the Russian traveller of Jewish origin <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Judah_Chorny" title="Joseph Judah Chorny">Joseph Judah Chorny</a> reported hearing from the Jews of Georgia that the Armenians were descendants of the Amalekites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:124</sup> In another anecdote, a Jewish traveler reported that among Armenians who traded with Jews in eastern <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Eastern_Europe)" title="Galicia (Eastern Europe)">Galicia</a>, there was a practice of mourning Haman’s death on Purim, and lighting candles in his memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horowitz_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Horowitz-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:124</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazis_associated_with_Amalekites">Nazis associated with Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nazis associated with Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1717" data-file-width="1445" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg/220px-Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg/330px-Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg/440px-Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Davidster_Dick_Stins_The_Hague.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Davidster" (<a href="/wiki/Star_of_David" title="Star of David">Star of David</a>) by Dick Stins is a <a href="/wiki/War_memorial" title="War memorial">World War II memorial</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>. The text at the side (in Dutch and Hebrew) is from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 25:17, 19 – "Remember what Amalek has done to you ... do not forget."</div>

<p>The Nazis and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> have been referred to as Amalekites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>A prominent 19th and early 20th century rabbi, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yosef_Chaim_Sonnenfeld" title="Yosef Chaim Sonnenfeld">Yosef Chaim Sonnenfeld</a>, claimed upon Kaiser Wilhelm's visit to Palestine in 1898, three decades before Hitler's rise to power, he had a tradition from his teachers that the Germans are descended from the ancient Amalekites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Samuel's words to <a href="/wiki/Agag" title="Agag">Agag</a>: "As your sword bereaved women, so will your mother be bereaved among women." (Samuel 1:15:33) were quoted by Israeli President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itzhak_Ben-Zvi" title="Itzhak Ben-Zvi">Itzhak Ben-Zvi</a> in his handwriting in response to a telegram sent by <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazi</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_criminal" title="War criminal">war criminal</a> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Eichmann" title="Adolf Eichmann">Adolf Eichmann</a>'s wife pleading for clemency after he was taken to Israel and sentenced to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Hebrew Bible, Amalek lived in <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a>: "Amalek dwells in the land of the Negev" (Numbers 13:29). The Israelites were instructed to kill all those who dwelled in Canaan: "thou shalt save alive nothing that breathes" (Deuteronomy 19:16) otherwise "it shall come to pass, that I shall do to you, as I thought to do to them" (Numbers 33:56). The Hebrew Bible ascribes Haman, who tried to commit a genocide of the Jewish people, to Agag, whom the Israelites, led by Saul, failed to kill. According to these verses Hitler may be seen as a result of this failure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zionists_associated_with_Amalekites">Zionists associated with Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Zionists associated with Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The anti-Zionist Haredi rabbi <a href="/wiki/Zalman_Teitelbaum" title="Zalman Teitelbaum">Zalman Teitelbaum</a> denounced the proposed draft of Haredi men by the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a> by saying "The Zionists came from the seed of Amalek. There has never been such a sect that caused so much damage to the Jewish people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nodraft_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-nodraft-29">[29]</a></sup> A senior rabbi in Israel's <a href="/wiki/Shas" title="Shas">Shas</a> party, Shalom Cohen, publicly labeled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Zionists" title="Religious Zionists">Religious Zionists</a> as Amalek, but later clarified that his remarks were aimed only at <a href="/wiki/The_Jewish_Home" title="The Jewish Home">The Jewish Home</a> party, not all Religious Zionists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-30">[30]</a></sup> Another rabbi associated with Shas, Shimon Badani, referred to Finance Minister <a href="/wiki/Yair_Lapid" title="Yair Lapid">Yair Lapid</a> and The Jewish Home party as Amalek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badani_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-badani-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neturei_Karta" title="Neturei Karta">Neturei Karta</a> are a Haredi group known for their radical opposition to the state of Israel and extreme wariness with regard to non-Haredi Jews. Historically, Neturei Karta equated <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a> with Amalek and Nazism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> For some Neturei Karta rabbis the very word 'amalek' is read in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gematriya" title="Gematriya">gematriya</a> to mean 'politics', which in their view is something pious Jews should never engage in, since politics for them constitutes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galut" title="Galut">galut</a>, or exile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Kahanism">In Kahanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In Kahanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the religious teaching of the Jewish ultra-nationalist <a href="/wiki/Meir_Kahane" title="Meir Kahane">Meir Kahane</a> (1932–1990), Amalek is a mythical enemy of Israel, embodied in different actual enemies throughout Jewish history. Since God and the Jewish people, according to Kahane, are ontologically related, the contemporary enemies of Israel are allegedly threatening existence of God himself. In the contemporary meaning, "Amalek" refers to the hostile Gentiles who are to be revenged for the near annihilation of Jews and their God, or calls for the analogous actions in the future. Metaphysics of Kahane, borrowed from the earlier Jewish authors (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judah_Halevy" title="Judah Halevy">Judah Halevy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Isaac_Kook" title="Abraham Isaac Kook">Abraham Isaac Kook</a>), provided justification for the radical movement known as <a href="/wiki/Kahanism" title="Kahanism">Kahanism</a>, presently banned in Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iranians_as_Amalekites">Iranians as Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iranians as Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Netanyahu" title="Benjamin Netanyahu">Benjamin Netanyahu</a> Prime Minister of Israel has compared Iran and its conflict with Israel as new Amaleks on several occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabs_as_Amalekites"><a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> as Amalekites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Arabs as Amalekites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NabateensRoutes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="814" data-file-width="837" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/NabateensRoutes.png/220px-NabateensRoutes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/NabateensRoutes.png/330px-NabateensRoutes.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/NabateensRoutes.png/440px-NabateensRoutes.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the Hebrew Bible, the Amalekites were a people, in the land called Moab,in what the Roman called <a href="/wiki/Arabia_Petraea" title="Arabia Petraea">Arabia Petraea</a> (Southern Syria with Moab and the desert of Sinai), a region depopulated in the XIV BCE (maybe by the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">Akhenaten</a>, who introduced a worship centered in Aten-solar deity), and then occupied by Edomites. The brief Babylonian captivity of the Hebrews that began in 586 BCE opened a minor power vacuum in Judah and as Edomites moved into open Judaean grazing lands, leaving "Nabataean" inscriptions (in 312/311 BC in the Third War of the Diadochi; a Seleucid officer, mentioned the Nabataeans in a battle report). The Nabatean are in some degree related too to the Nabatu in Arabia, East of Edom. The Arabic alphabet itself developed out of cursive variants of the Nabataean script in the 5th century. The Edomite Religion was to a stone cube representation of God. The <a href="/wiki/Arabah" title="Arabah">Arabah</a> Edomite-Nabatean Trade Route was centered in the Myrrh trade from Yemen towards Egypt (other products were bitumen from the Dead Sea and copper from Moab), from Ma’in-Yemen, to La Mecque (Mecca), Yatrib-Medine, Dedan, Tayma, Hegra, Petra, Aqaba and finally to Egypt. Balaam lists Moab, Edom (Idumea), Seir (<a href="/wiki/Horites" title="Horites">Horites</a>), Kedar, all were Arabah tribes included in the Roman province of Arabia Petra, west of Nabatei tribe, a long term confront as described in the <a href="/wiki/Mesha" title="Mesha">Mesha</a> of the Moab of the people of <a href="/wiki/Chemosh" title="Chemosh">Chemosh</a>. Kedar, Edom, Moab, Amalkites were for the Roman the Arabah.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the Arabic sources, the Biblical Amalek changed to <i><b>Al-′Amālīq</b></i> (العماليق) which means the Giants, the Arab genealogies consider the Canaanites, Assyrians, Egypt's Pharaohs descended from Amalek. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> according to <a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_al-Athir" title="Ali ibn al-Athir">Ali ibn al-Athir</a> the Amalekites king Jalloot was killed by the Israelite prophet Dawood and the Bani Israel tribe took over the Holy Land .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss3_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ss3-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
