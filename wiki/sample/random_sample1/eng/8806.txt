<p>The <b>New World porcupines</b>, family <b>Erethizontidae</b>, are large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a> <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, distinguished by their <a href="/wiki/Spine_(zoology)" title="Spine (zoology)">spiny</a> coverings from which they take their name. They inhabit <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> and wooded regions across North America, and into northern South America. Although both the New World and <a href="/wiki/Old_World_porcupine" title="Old World porcupine">Old World porcupine</a> families belong to the <a href="/wiki/Hystricognathi" title="Hystricognathi">Hystricognathi</a> branch of the vast <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> Rodentia, they are quite different and are not closely related.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_porcupine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New World porcupines are stout animals, with blunt, rounded heads, fleshy, mobile snouts, and coats of thick, cylindrical or flattened spines ("quills"). The spines are mixed with long, soft hairs. They vary in size from the relatively small <a href="/wiki/Prehensile-tailed_porcupine" title="Prehensile-tailed porcupine">prehensile-tailed porcupines</a>, which are around 30 cm (12 in) long, and weigh about 900 g (32 oz), to the much larger <a href="/wiki/North_American_porcupine" title="North American porcupine">North American porcupine</a>, which has a body length of 86 cm (34 in), and weighs up to 18 kg (40 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are distinguished from the Old World porcupines in that they have rooted <a href="/wiki/Molar_(tooth)" title="Molar (tooth)">molars</a>, complete collar bones, entire upper lips, tuberculated soles, no trace of first front toes, and four teats.</p>
<p>They are less strictly nocturnal than Old World species in their habits, and some types live entirely in trees, while others have dens on the ground. Their long and powerful <a href="/wiki/Prehensile_tail" title="Prehensile tail">prehensile tails</a> help them balance when they are in the tree tops. Their diets consist mainly of bark, leaves, and conifer needles, but can also include roots, stems, berries, fruits, seeds, nuts, grasses, and flowers. Some species also eat insects and small reptiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Their teeth are similar to those of Old World porcupines, with the <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dental formula</a> <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;" title="upper dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">1.0.1.3</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;" title="lower dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">1.0.1.3</span></span></span></p>
<p>Solitary offspring (or, rarely, twins) are born after a gestation period of up to 210 days, depending on the species. The young are born fully developed, with open eyes, and are able to climb trees within a few days of birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoM_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genera_and_species">Genera and species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_World_porcupine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genera and species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They include three genera, of which the first is represented by the North American porcupine (<i>Erethizon dorsatum</i>), a stout, heavily built animal, with long hairs almost or quite hiding its spines, four front and five hind toes, and a short, stumpy tail. It is a native of the greater part of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where any remnant of the original forest is left.</p>
<p>The tree porcupines (<i><a href="/wiki/Prehensile-tailed_porcupine" title="Prehensile-tailed porcupine">Coendou</a></i>) contain 16 species. They are found throughout tropical <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, with two extending into <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. They are of a lighter build than the ground porcupines, with short, close, many-coloured spines, often mixed with hairs, and prehensile tails. The hind feet have only four toes, owing to the suppression of the first, in place of which they have a fleshy pad on the inner side of the foot; between this pad and the toes, branches and other objects can be firmly grasped as with a hand. These three genera are often united into a single genus <i>Coendou</i>.</p>
<p>Genus <i>Chaetomys</i>, distinguished by the shape of its skull and the greater complexity of its teeth, contains <a href="/wiki/Bristle-spined_rat" title="Bristle-spined rat"><i>C. subspinosus</i></a>, a native of the hottest parts of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. This animal is often considered a member of <a href="/wiki/Echimyidae" title="Echimyidae">Echimyidae</a> on the basis of its <a href="/wiki/Premolar" title="Premolar">premolars</a>. However, a molecular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogeny</a> based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial" title="Mitochondrial">mitochondrial</a> gene coding for <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome b</a> combined to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karyology" title="Karyology">karyological</a> evidence actually suggests the <a href="/wiki/Bristle-spined_rat" title="Bristle-spined rat">bristle-spined rat</a> is more closely related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erethizontidae" title="Erethizontidae">Erethizontidae</a> than to the Echimyidae, and is the sister group of all other Erethizontidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
