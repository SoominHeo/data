<p>The <b>American River</b> (<i><b>Río de los Americanos</b></i> during the Mexican-ruled period before 1846) is a 119 mile long <a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_of_California" title="List of rivers of California">California river</a> which runs from the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a> to its confluence with the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River" title="Sacramento River">Sacramento River</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Valley" title="Sacramento Valley">Sacramento Valley</a>. Via the Sacramento River, it is part of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> and Pacific Ocean watershed. The river is fed by the melting snowpack of the Sierra Nevada and its many headwaters and tributaries, including the <a href="/wiki/North_Fork_American_River" title="North Fork American River">North</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Fork_American_River" title="Middle Fork American River">Middle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_American_River" title="South Fork American River">South Forks</a>.</p>
<p>The river is known for the 1848 discovery of gold at <a href="/wiki/Coloma,_California" title="Coloma, California">Coloma</a> which started the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a> and contributed to the initial large-scale settlement of California by European immigrants. Today, the river maintains high water quality and is the main source of drinking water for <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is dammed extensively for irrigation, flood control and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> generation. The American River watershed supports Mediterranean, temperate and montane ecosystems, and is home to a diverse array of fish and wildlife.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Maidu, Miwok, Nisenan and Wintun peoples inhabited the American River in Sacramento for at least 5,000 years before foreigners settled the region, although human habitation in Northern California is believed to date back as much as 12,000 years. They utilized the vast amount of resources of the American River for shelter, clothes, baskets, and other goods before Europeans arrived in the late 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maidu.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-maidu.com-10">[10]</a></sup> The Nisenan called the river <i>Kum Mayo</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_(dwelling)" title="Roundhouse (dwelling)">roundhouse</a> river".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spanish explorer <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Moraga" title="Gabriel Moraga">Gabriel Moraga</a> named the river "Rio de las Llagas" (River of Sorrows) when he passed through the area in the early 1800s, perhaps due to hostile relations with local native peoples. Another member of the expedition recorded the name as "Rio de los Lagos" (River of the Lakes) which may or may not have been an error, as in those times the area of the Central Valley surrounding the American River was home to vast <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, which would have given the river the appearance of a series of lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1820s, <a href="/wiki/Jedediah_Smith" title="Jedediah Smith">Jedediah Smith</a> led an expedition to the American River with the goal of finding a safe route across the Sierra Nevada. After a failed attempt to cross the mountains via the South Fork of the American River, Smith's group managed to cross via <a href="/wiki/Ebbetts_Pass" title="Ebbetts Pass">Ebbetts Pass</a> on the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_River" title="Stanislaus River">Stanislaus River</a>, becoming the first non-Native Americans to do so. In Smith's honor the Spanish settlers and Native Americans named the river "Rio de los Americanos", <i>American River</i>. During this time, <a href="/wiki/Alta_California" title="Alta California">Alta California</a> was part of <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a>; however in 1822, Mexico gained its independence from Spain and took control of California.</p>
<p>In the 1830s fur trappers of the <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">Hudson's Bay Company</a> (HBC) visited the area to trap beaver and otter. During one of these expeditions, smallpox or malaria were accidentally introduced to the local Native Americans, who had no natural immunity to Old World diseases. Some accounts suggest as much as 70 percent of the indigenous population was wiped out. The surviving natives became hostile to European settlers and traders for quite some time, and prevented the HBC from establishing a permanent outpost here.</p>
<p>In 1839, German immigrant <a href="/wiki/John_Sutter" title="John Sutter">John Sutter</a> established the <a href="/wiki/New_Helvetia" title="New Helvetia">New Helvetia</a> settlement on the American River, near the present-day location of central Sacramento. In 1848, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican-American_War" title="Mexican-American War">Mexican-American War</a>, California was ceded to the United States in the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maidu.com_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-maidu.com-10">[10]</a></sup> Just weeks later, James Marshall, an employee of Sutter, discovered gold on the South Fork, starting the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although miners looking for gold worked all three forks of the American River, the South Fork held the richest deposits. However, as the easily accessible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placer_gold" title="Placer gold">placer gold</a> was played out, large companies used <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_mining" title="Hydraulic mining">hydraulic mining</a> to access gold buried deeper in the soil. Unfortunately, this large and extensive mining practice washed away entire mountainsides and heavily polluted all the waterways, including the American River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Flood_of_1862" title="Great Flood of 1862">Great Flood of 1862</a> the American River flooded massively, putting much of Sacramento under water for three months. Newly elected Governor <a href="/wiki/Leland_Stanford" title="Leland Stanford">Leland Stanford</a> had to travel to his inauguration by rowboat; shortly after, the state government moved temporarily to San Francisco. A significant contributor to the flood damage was the debris washed down by hydraulic mining, which had choked the river channel and reduced its capacity to drain floodwaters. In response, the city of Sacramento undertook a massive project to raise its streets and buildings as much as 9.5 feet (2.9 m). Many of original sidewalks and the first floors of buildings remain as subterranean spaces underneath today's streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lower American River has been one of seven California rivers to achieve the designation "Recreational River" under both the California Wild and Scenic Rivers Act (1972) and the National Wild and Scenic Rivers Act (1980). This status provides state and national recognition to protect the river's outstanding scenic, fish and wildlife, historic, cultural, and recreational values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American River is fed by its North, Middle, and South forks, which are located in <a href="/wiki/El_Dorado_County,_California" title="El Dorado County, California">El Dorado County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placer_County,_California" title="Placer County, California">Placer County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_County,_California" title="Sacramento County, California">Sacramento County</a>. The river's three forks originate in the <a href="/wiki/Tahoe_National_Forest" title="Tahoe National Forest">Tahoe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eldorado_National_Forest" title="Eldorado National Forest">Eldorado National Forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The North and Middle Forks join near <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_California" title="Auburn, California">Auburn</a>, and continue downstream as the North Fork, although the Middle Fork carries a higher volume of water. The North and South Forks join in <a href="/wiki/Folsom_Lake" title="Folsom Lake">Folsom Lake</a>. All three forks are known for their verdant canyons, forested ridges, massive rock formations, trails, backcountry winter adventuring among snowy peaks, fishing and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_water_rafting" title="White water rafting">white water rafting</a>. There are various fish species that live within the American River such as Chinook Salmon and Steelhead Trout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American River headwaters lie along about 50 miles (80 km) along the Sierra Crest from Mount Lincoln in the north (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=American_River&amp;params=39_17_16_N_120_19_41_W_region:US_type:landmark"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">39°17′16″N</span> <span class="longitude">120°19′41″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">39.28778°N 120.32806°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">39.28778; -120.32806</span></span></span></a></span>) where it adjoins the watersheds of the <a href="/wiki/South_Yuba_River" title="South Yuba River">South Yuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Truckee_River" title="Truckee River">Truckee Rivers</a>, to Winnemucca Lake in the <a href="/wiki/Mokelumne_Wilderness" title="Mokelumne Wilderness">Mokelumne Wilderness</a>, where it meets the watersheds of the <a href="/wiki/Mokelumne_River" title="Mokelumne River">Mokelumne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carson_River" title="Carson River">Carson Rivers</a>. Lands east of the American River watershed divide drain into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tahoe" title="Lake Tahoe">Lake Tahoe</a>, which flows into the Truckee River. During most years, a significant snowpack builds up in the Sierra Nevada which provides water during the hot, dry summers. In warmer winters much of the precipitation may fall as rain instead, causing flooding, but resulting in lower summer flows due to the lack of snow. Due to California's highly variable weather patterns, runoff volumes in the American River can rise and fall drastically from one year to the next.</p>

<p>Below <a href="/wiki/Folsom_Dam" title="Folsom Dam">Folsom Dam</a>, the river passes through an urbanized area but is buffered by a riparian park, the <a href="/wiki/American_River_Parkway" title="American River Parkway">American River Parkway</a>. Containing fishing and family-oriented rafting, and paved bicycling and multi-use trails, it runs 30.6 miles (49.2 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-6">[6]</a></sup> from Folsom Lake to the river's confluence with the Sacramento River. The American River Parkway incorporates historic <a href="/wiki/William_Leidesdorff" title="William Leidesdorff">Leidesdorff</a> Ranch, a 35,000-acre (140 km<sup>2</sup>) cattle and wheat agribusiness owned by the "African Founding Father of California." <a href="/wiki/Jedediah_Smith_Memorial_Trail" title="Jedediah Smith Memorial Trail">Jedediah Smith Memorial Trail</a>, a meandering, uninterrupted 32-mile (51 km) cycle path, hugs the river bank from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Sacramento" title="Old Sacramento">Old Sacramento</a> to <a href="/wiki/Folsom_Lake" title="Folsom Lake">Folsom Lake</a>. The trail was named for <a href="/wiki/Jedediah_Smith" title="Jedediah Smith">Smith</a> and his men, who camped and trapped along its banks in 1828.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chittenden_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chittenden-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Fork">North Fork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North Fork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North_Fork_American_River" title="North Fork American River">North Fork American River</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Fork_American_River" title="North Fork American River">North Fork</a> is the longest tributary of the American River, at 88 miles (142 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-6">[6]</a></sup> It begins at an elevation of about 7,900 feet (2,400 m) near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tahoe" title="Lake Tahoe">Lake Tahoe</a> in Placer County at Mountain Meadow Lake, just northeast of <a href="/wiki/Granite_Chief" title="Granite Chief">Granite Chief</a> and immediately due west of <a href="/wiki/Squaw_Valley_Ski_Resort" title="Squaw Valley Ski Resort">Squaw Valley Ski Resort</a>. It flows westward through remote wilderness areas.</p>
<p>The North Fork and its tributaries provides one of the most biologically diverse habitats in North America.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The North Fork features scenic multi-use trails along forested ridgetops and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian" title="Riparian">riparian</a> corridors. It flows freely as a designated <a href="/wiki/National_Wild_and_Scenic_Rivers_System" title="National Wild and Scenic Rivers System">National Wild and Scenic Rivers System</a> river until reaching the North Fork Dam, which was built to contain mining debris. This dam creates the small Lake Clementine just north of the <a href="/wiki/Foresthill_Bridge" title="Foresthill Bridge">Foresthill Bridge</a> and upstream of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confluence_(geography)" title="Confluence (geography)">confluence</a> with the Middle Fork American River at <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_California" title="Auburn, California">Auburn</a>. Below the confluence, the North Fork continues several miles until reaching Folsom Lake. Both the North Fork and Middle Fork feature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic" title="Historic">historic</a> sites of ancient <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> culture and 1850s Gold Rush habitation.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Middle_Fork_American_River" title="Middle Fork American River">Middle Fork</a> is 62 miles (100 km) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-6">[6]</a></sup> It originates a mere 1.7-mile (2.7 km) from the source of the North Fork on the south face of <a href="/wiki/Granite_Chief" title="Granite Chief">Granite Chief</a>, between the summit and Emigrant Pass. It is characterized by somewhat broader steep canyons interspersed with <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a>, waterfalls, and quiet <a href="/wiki/Riparian_zone" title="Riparian zone">riparian</a> areas. The Middle Fork is used extensively for both motorized and non-motorized recreation, including fishing, white water adventuring, bicycling (mountain and road), horseback riding, trail running and hiking. It contains areas used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> generation, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, and agricultural timber cultivation and harvesting. The Middle Fork features part of the <a href="/wiki/Auburn_State_Recreation_Area" title="Auburn State Recreation Area">Auburn State Recreation Area</a>. The Western States Trail hosts multiple annual endurance events, including the <a href="/wiki/Tevis_Cup" title="Tevis Cup">Tevis Cup</a> equestrian trail ride, and the world-famous Western States 100- Mile Trail Run, both beginning at Squaw Valley and leading to Auburn via remote wilderness trails.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Fork">South Fork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: South Fork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/South_Fork_American_River" title="South Fork American River">South Fork</a> is 87 miles (140 km) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-6">[6]</a></sup> It originates at Echo Summit near <a href="/wiki/Echo_Lake_(California)" title="Echo Lake (California)">Echo Lake</a> south of Lake Tahoe in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Dorado_County" title="El Dorado County">El Dorado County</a>, just south of where <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_50_in_California" title="U.S. Route 50 in California">U.S. Route 50</a> makes a sharp curve northward to descend into Tahoe Valley. The South Fork has multi-use recreational areas, including the Rubicon Trail for motorized adventuring and whitewater rafting venues. The South Fork also features the historic town of <a href="/wiki/Coloma,_California" title="Coloma, California">Coloma</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a> began in 1848. Trout fishing and recreational <a href="/wiki/Gold_panning" title="Gold panning">gold panning</a> are popular activities on the South Fork.</p>
<p>The <b>Silver Fork American River</b> is a 20-mile-long (32 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-6">[6]</a></sup> tributary of the South Fork American River, that has its origin at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_Fork_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Silver Fork Lake (page does not exist)">Silver Fork Lake</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American River watershed crosses multiple climate zones due to the large range in elevations. The climate of the lower American River valley is Mediterranean and temperate grassland. Sacramento County in particular, where the main stem American River is located, is known for its cool winters and hot summers, with low average precipitation. The North, Middle and South Forks originate in alpine zones along the Sierra Crest and flow through subalpine, montane and temperate forests.</p>
<p>The region is prone to seasonal drought conditions, as about 90 percent of precipitation falls as rain and snow between the months of November and April. Much of the Central Valley was historically wetlands, which made the ground very fertile and attractive for agriculture, which is now what the Central Valley is known for. The American flows through this region, providing Sacramento County with its main source of drinking water, and houses a multitude of plants and wildlife.</p>
<p>There is an abundance of flora and fauna found at the American River that creates an immensely bio diverse ecosystem. Native plants along the American River Parkway are adapted to a Mediterranean climate, are drought tolerant, and help support a balanced, healthy ecosystem. Non-native, and sometimes invasive, species have been introduced to the American River ecosystem. These exotic species have no natural predators and combat with native species for sun, space, and nutrients, causing substantial problems for the native plant life and the entire bionetwork. The American River Parkway Foundation, in collaboration with Sacramento County Parks, manages the invasive plants on the American River.</p>
<p>Fourteen environmental <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> groups are shown to be active in the Upper American River Watershed by the Adopt A Watershed Program of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._EPA" title="U.S. EPA">U.S. EPA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Upper American River Foundation is a prominent one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="River_modifications">River modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: River modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:American_River_North.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1232" data-file-width="2048" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/American_River_North.jpg/220px-American_River_North.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/American_River_North.jpg/330px-American_River_North.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/American_River_North.jpg/440px-American_River_North.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:American_River_North.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
North Fork of American River</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Historically, the American River, like many other rivers in California, were modified by the dams of beavers, until European trappers removed many of the beavers from their native habitat for their fur. Since the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a> was centered in an area that included the American River basin, it was one of the earlier California rivers to be populated, beginning at Leidesdorff Ranch, owned by <a href="/wiki/William_Leidesdorff" title="William Leidesdorff">William Leidesdorff</a>. Water was used to drive <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">grist mills</a>. Gold miners dredged the riverbed and constructed <a href="/wiki/Diversion_dam" title="Diversion dam">diversion dams</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_mining" title="Hydraulic mining">hydraulic mining</a>. Mining activity caused sediment and mercury waste to accumulate in the rivers. The Natoma Company completed its <a href="/wiki/Folsom_Powerhouse_State_Historic_Park" title="Folsom Powerhouse State Historic Park">Folsom Powerhouse</a> in 1895 and began delivering power 22 miles (35 km) away to the city of Sacramento to power a streetcar system.</p>
<p>Major flooding of the Sacramento area led to calls for a large dam on the American River by the late 19th century. During the 20th century, the American River was extensively developed for flood control, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> production and irrigation. <a href="/wiki/Folsom_Dam" title="Folsom Dam">Folsom Dam</a> is the primary flood-control facility for Sacramento. The numerous hydroelectric dams upstream on the Middle and South Forks are not constrained by flood control requirements; rather, the needs of electricity and water supply govern their operation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Valley_Project">Central Valley Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Central Valley Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_Project" title="Central Valley Project">Central Valley Project</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Folsom_Dam" title="Folsom Dam">Folsom Dam</a> was built in 1955 as a principal part of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_Project" title="Central Valley Project">Central Valley Project</a> by the Army Corps of Engineers, and is operated by the Bureau of Reclamation. The concrete and earth embankment structure is more than 5 miles (8.0 km) long and creates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folsom_lake" title="Folsom lake">Folsom lake</a>, impounding 1.1 million acre-feet (1.4 km<sup>3</sup>) of water when full.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The dam is a multipurpose facility that acts as a reservoir for agricultural, domestic and industrial use, as well as hydropower generation, recreation, and fish and wildlife habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Nimbus Dam is 7 miles (11 km) downstream from Folsom Dam and helps to stabilize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peaking_power" title="Peaking power">peaking power</a> releases from Folsom Dam, and divert water into the <a href="/wiki/Folsom_South_Canal" title="Folsom South Canal">Folsom South Canal</a> for irrigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_American_River_Project">Upper American River Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Upper American River Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Upper_American_River_Project" title="Upper American River Project">Upper American River Project</a></div>
<p>Eight hydroelectric plants on the South Fork are operated by <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Municipal_Utility_District" title="Sacramento Municipal Utility District">Sacramento Municipal Utility District</a> (SMUD) as the <a href="/wiki/Upper_American_River_Project" title="Upper American River Project">Upper American River Project</a>. These plants are fed by a series of reservoirs including <a href="/wiki/Loon_Lake_(California)" title="Loon Lake (California)">Loon Lake</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_House_Reservoir" title="Ice House Reservoir">Ice House Reservoir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Union_Valley_Reservoir" title="Union Valley Reservoir">Union Valley Reservoir</a>. With the exception of Folsom Lake, Union Valley is the largest in the American River watershed, storing 277,000 acre feet (0.342 km<sup>3</sup>) of water. The Upper American River Project produces 1.8 billion <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kilowatt hours</a> in an average year, enough for about 20 percent of Sacramento's electricity needs. The SMUD plants are run on a <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking</a> basis, although recreational boating and environmental flow requirements constrain their operation slightly.</p>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">run-of-the-river hydroelectric plants</a> are located further downstream on the South Fork. While they are not operated by SMUD, they depend on the releases from SMUD reservoirs to generate electricity. The El Dorado Irrigation District owns the Akin Powerhouse, and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Gas_and_Electric_Company" title="Pacific Gas and Electric Company">PG&amp;E</a> owns the Chili Bar Powerhouse. The Rock Creek Powerhouse is owned by Sithe Energies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Fork_Project">Middle Fork Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Middle Fork Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Placer County Water Agency operates five hydroelectric power plants on remote sections of the Middle Fork as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_Fork_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Middle Fork Project (page does not exist)">Middle Fork Project</a>, whose primary reservoirs are situated at <a href="/wiki/French_Meadows_Reservoir" title="French Meadows Reservoir">French Meadows Reservoir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hell_Hole_Reservoir" title="Hell Hole Reservoir">Hell Hole Reservoir</a>. The Middle Fork Project generates 1.03 billion kilowatt hours each year and also provides 120,000 acre feet (0.15 km<sup>3</sup>) of domestic water supply.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_and_restoration_projects">Conservation and restoration projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Conservation and restoration projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American River Conservancy works to conserve the wildlife and their aquatic and terrestrial habitats and resources around the American River and Consumnes watersheds. They have successfully protected almost 25,000 acres since their inception in 1989. The land they protect helps the American River water quality by conserving and resorting wet meadows, wetlands, and riparian areas. They conserve the diversity of habitats and biodiversity by supporting protected native or endemic species that are listed as endangered or threatened. The American River Conservancy protects the American River watershed to preserve the cultural and historic values of the river systems and the landscapes surround them. They are also invested in keeping the American River for recreational activities as well as its remarkable natural scenic views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American River Parkway is a 23-mile stretch along the American River and includes about 5,000 acres of embankment between the Nimbus Fish Hatchery and where the American River and the Sacramento River converge. The Sacramento City Regional Parks owns most of the land that is operated by the American River Parkway. This area serves the citizens of Sacramento and visitors from away with access to the river and parks, picnic areas, biking and jogging paths, trails for hiking, and habitat for fish and wildlife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chinook Salmon are a threatened anadromous fish species that have historically used the American River as a location for an important part of their life cycle while in freshwater. The American River, however, has lost some of its natural flow and riverbed because of water projects and diversions. The American River Salmonid Spawning and Rearing Habitat Restoration project, which started in 2008, entails the placement of gravel to provide adequate habitat conducive to the salmonid’s needs for spawning and rearing to reach a maturity level fit for the ocean. From 2008 to 2012, the placement totaled 85,880 tons of gravel. The gravel will increase the number of fish redds, spawning nests, along the American River, which will decrease egg retention, and increase birth and survivorship rate. The project also included the introduction of small islands, loose woody debris, and larger boulders to advance the rearing habitat for the anadromous fish and increase biodiversity of the American River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American River is utilized for a variety of recreational uses. Locals and travelers alike use the river’s scenic landscape for bird watching, hiking, biking, fishing, river rafting, kayaking, and more. Although the American River allows small motor boats for part of the year, it remains free from speed boats or boats that use fuel, which makes it a clean, biodiverse river that people can safely swim in and do other recreational activities in.</p>
<p>The American River is specifically known for its whitewater rafting. The South Fork has Class III rapids, the Middle Fork has Class IV rapids, and the North Fork has Class IV spring run-off rapids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup> All three rivers also feature Class II sections. The areas with rapids still feature beautiful scenic views and wildlife, which is what makes it the most popular whitewater rafting location in California <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
