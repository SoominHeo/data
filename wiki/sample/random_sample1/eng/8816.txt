<p><i><b>Amistad</b></i> is a 1997 American <a href="/wiki/Historical_period_drama" title="Historical period drama">historical drama</a> film directed by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>, based on the true story of the 1839 mutiny aboard the slave ship <i><a href="/wiki/La_Amistad" title="La Amistad">La Amistad</a></i>, during which <a href="/wiki/Mende_people" title="Mende people">Mende</a> tribesmen abducted for the slave trade managed to gain control of their captors' ship off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and the international <a href="/wiki/United_States_v._The_Amistad" title="United States v. The Amistad">legal battle</a> that followed their capture by a U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revenue_cutter" title="Revenue cutter">revenue cutter</a>. The case was ultimately resolved by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">United States Supreme Court</a> in 1841.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Morgan_Freeman" title="Morgan Freeman">Morgan Freeman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Hawthorne" title="Nigel Hawthorne">Nigel Hawthorne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hopkins" title="Anthony Hopkins">Anthony Hopkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Djimon_Hounsou" title="Djimon Hounsou">Djimon Hounsou</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_McConaughey" title="Matthew McConaughey">Matthew McConaughey</a> had starring roles. <a href="/wiki/David_Franzoni" title="David Franzoni">David Franzoni</a>'s screenplay was based on the book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutiny_on_the_Amistad:_The_Saga_of_a_Slave_Revolt_and_Its_Impact_on_American_Abolition,_Law,_and_Diplomacy" title="Mutiny on the Amistad: The Saga of a Slave Revolt and Its Impact on American Abolition, Law, and Diplomacy">Mutiny on the Amistad: The Saga of a Slave Revolt and Its Impact on American Abolition, Law, and Diplomacy</a></i> (1987), by the historian Howard Jones.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amistad_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Amistad</i> is the name of a slave ship traveling from Cuba to the United States in 1839. It is carrying African people as its cargo. As the ship is crossing from Cuba to the United States, Cinqué, a leader of the Africans, leads a mutiny and takes over the ship. The mutineers spare the lives of two Spanish navigators to help them sail the ship back to Africa. Instead, the navigators play out the Africans and sail north to the east coast of the United States, where the ship is stopped by the American Navy, and the 53 living Africans imprisoned as runaway slaves.</p>
<p>In an unfamiliar country and not speaking a single word of English, the Africans find themselves in a legal battle. District Attorney <a href="/wiki/William_S._Holabird" title="William S. Holabird">William S. Holabird</a> brings charges of piracy and murder. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State" title="Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Forsyth_(Georgia)" title="John Forsyth (Georgia)">John Forsyth</a>, on behalf of President <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a> (who is campaigning for re-election), represents the claim of Queen Isabella II of Spain that the Africans are slaves and are property of Spain based on a treaty. Two Naval officers claim them as salvage while the two Spanish navigators produce proof of purchase. A lawyer named Roger Sherman Baldwin, hired by the abolitionist Tappan and his black associate Joadson (a fictional character<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) decides to defend the Africans.</p>
<p>Baldwin argues that the Africans had been captured in Africa to be sold in the Americas illegally. Baldwin proves through documents found hidden on Amistad that the African people were initially cargo belonging to a Portuguese slave ship, The Tecora. Therefore, the Africans were free citizens of another country and not slaves at all. In light of this evidence, the staff of President Van Buren has the judge presiding over the case replaced by Judge Coglin, who is younger and believed to be impressionable and easily influenced. Consequently, seeking to make the case more personal, on the advice of former American president (and lawyer) John Quincy Adams, Baldwin and Joadson find James Covey, a former slave who speaks both Mende and English. Cinque tells his story at trial.</p>
<p>District Attorney Holabird attacks Cinqué’s “tale” of being captured and kept in a Lomboko slave fortress and especially questions the throwing of precious cargo overboard. However, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s fervent abolitionist Captain Fitzgerald of the <a href="/wiki/West_Africa_Squadron" title="West Africa Squadron">West Africa Squadron</a> backs up Cinqué’s account. Baldwin shows from The Tecora's inventory that the number of African people taken as slaves was reduced by 50. Fitzgerald explains that some slave ships when interdicted do this to get rid of the evidence for their crime. But in The Tecora's case, they had underestimated the amount of provisions necessary for their journey. As the tension rises, Cinqué stands up from his seat and repeatedly cries, "Give us, us free!"</p>
<p>Judge Coglin rules in favor of the Africans. After pressure from Senator Calhoun on President Van Buren, the case is appealed to the Supreme Court. Despite refusing to help when the case was initially presented, Adams agrees to assist with the case. At the Supreme Court, he makes an impassioned and eloquent plea for their release, and is successful.</p>
<p>The Lomboko slave fortress is liberated by the Royal Marines under the command of Captain Fitzgerald. After all the slaves were hurried out of the fortress, Fitzgerald orders the ship's cannon to destroy it. He then dictates a letter to Forsyth saying that he was right—the slave fortress doesn't exist.</p>
<p>Because of the release of the Africans, Van Buren loses his re-election campaign, and tension builds between the North and the South, which would eventually culminate in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>.</p>

<p>Actress and director <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Allen" title="Debbie Allen">Debbie Allen</a> had run across some books about the mutiny on <i><a href="/wiki/La_Amistad" title="La Amistad">La Amistad</a></i> and brought the subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HBO_films" title="HBO films">HBO films</a>, which chose to make a film adaptation of the subject. She later presented the project to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DreamWorks_SKG" title="DreamWorks SKG">DreamWorks SKG</a> to release the film, which agreed. Steven Spielberg, who wanted to stretch his artistic wings after making <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></i> (1997), was interested in directing it for DreamWorks, which he also co-founded, as well. Spielberg was an unlikely person to tackle the Amistad story since his previous picture about black characters, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Color_Purple_(1985_film)" title="The Color Purple (1985 film)">The Color Purple</a></i>, had been badly received by the black community.</p>
<p>Filming of the exterior and interior court scenes took place at the Old Colony House in Newport, RI, and then moved to Sonalyst Studios. The opening scene was filmed on a sound stage in <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a>. Production then went to Puerto Rico for the scenes set in Africa, including those with the slave fortress.</p>
<p>Post-production was done rarely with Spielberg, due to his commitment to another DreamWorks film, <i><a href="/wiki/Saving_Private_Ryan" title="Saving Private Ryan">Saving Private Ryan</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_accuracy">Historical accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amistad_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many academics, including <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> professor <a href="/wiki/Eric_Foner" title="Eric Foner">Eric Foner</a>, have criticized <i>Amistad</i> for historical inaccuracy and the misleading characterizations of the Amistad case as a "turning point" in the American perspective on slavery. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Foner wrote:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">In fact, the <i>Amistad</i> case revolved around the Atlantic slave trade — by 1840 outlawed by international treaty — and had nothing whatsoever to do with slavery as a domestic institution. Incongruous as it may seem, it was perfectly possible in the nineteenth century to condemn the importation of slaves from Africa while simultaneously defending slavery and the flourishing slave trade within the United States.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>

<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><i>Amistad</i>'s problems go far deeper than such anachronisms as President Martin Van Buren campaigning for re-election on a whistle-stop train tour (in 1840, candidates did not campaign), or people constantly talking about the impending <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, which lay twenty years in the future.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>

<p>Other reported inaccuracies include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Despite what the film suggests, the actual Supreme Court decision reversed District and Circuit decrees regarding the Africans' conveyance back to Africa; they were to be deemed free, but the U.S. government could not take them back to Africa, as they had arrived on American soil as free people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>The film version of Adams' closing speech before the Supreme Court and the court's decision as read by Justice Joseph Story bear no resemblance to the much longer historical versions; they are not even fair summaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>During the scene depicting the destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Lomboko" title="Lomboko">Lomboko</a> slave fortress by a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a>, the vessel's captain refers to another officer as "<a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">ensign</a>". This rank has never been used by the Royal Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amistad_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amistad_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Amistad</i> received mainly positive reviews. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film receives an approval rating of 75% based on reviews from 61 critics, with an average score of 6.9/10. its consensus reads: "Heartfelt without resorting to preachiness, <i>Amistad</i> tells an important story with engaging sensitivity and absorbing skill."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Susan Wloszczyna of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> summed up the feelings of many reviewers when she wrote: "as Spielberg vehicles go, <i>Amistad</i> — part mystery, action thriller, courtroom drama, even culture-clash comedy — lands between the disturbing lyricism of <i><a href="/wiki/Schindler%27s_List" title="Schindler's List">Schindler's List</a></i> and the storybook artificiality of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Color_Purple_(1985_film)" title="The Color Purple (1985 film)">The Color Purple</a></i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> awarded the film three out of four stars, writing:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Amistad," like Spielberg's "Schindler's List," is [...] about the ways good men try to work realistically within an evil system to spare a few of its victims. [...] "Schindler's List" works better as narrative because it is about a risky deception, while "Amistad" is about the search for a truth that, if found, will be small consolation to the millions of existing slaves. As a result, the movie doesn't have the emotional charge of Spielberg's earlier film — or of "The Color Purple," which moved me to tears. [...] What is most valuable about "Amistad" is the way it provides faces and names for its African characters, whom the movies so often make into faceless victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amistad_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film earned $44,229,441 at the box office in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, debuting at <abbr title="Number">No.</abbr> 5 on December 10, 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
