<p><b>Amis</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formosan_language" title="Formosan language">Formosan language</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Amis_people" title="Amis people">Amis</a> (or Ami), an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">indigenous tribal people</a> living along the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_aborigines" title="Taiwanese aborigines">Taiwanese aborigines</a>). Currently the largest of the Formosan languages, it is spoken from <a href="/wiki/Hualien_City" title="Hualien City">Hualien</a> in the north to <a href="/wiki/Taitung_City" title="Taitung City">Taitung</a> in the south, with another population near the southern end of the island, though the northern varieties are considered to be separate languages.</p>
<p>Government services in counties where many Amis people live in Taiwan such as the Hualien and Taitung train stations broadcast in Amis alongside Mandarin. However, few Amis under the age of 20 in 1995 spoke the language, and it is not known how many of the 138,000 ethnic Amis are speakers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amis_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amis is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialect_cluster" title="Dialect cluster">dialect cluster</a>. The northern varieties, Sakizaya and Nataoran, are divergent enough to be classified as separate languages. Within Amis proper, there are Tavalong-Vata'an, Middle, and Southern dialects, the latter including Malan and Hengchun Amis.</p>

<p>The voiceless plosives <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p t k ʡ/</span> and the affricate <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t͡s/</span> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unreleased_stop" title="Unreleased stop">released</a> in clusters, so that <i>cecay</i> "one" is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t͡sᵊt͡saj]</span>; as is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/s/</span>: <i>sepat</i> "four" is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[sᵊpatʰ]</span>. The glottal stop is an exception, frequently having no audible release in final position. The <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voiced</a> fricatives, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/v ɮ ɣ/</span> (the latter found only in loanwords) are devoiced to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[f ɬ x]</span> in utterance-final and sometimes initial position. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɮ/</span> may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdental" title="Interdental">interdental</a> or post-dental. The sibilants, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t͡s s/</span>, are optionally palatalized (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t͡ɕ ɕ]</span>) before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span>. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> does not occur in word-initial position. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɺ/</span> is often post-alveolar, and in final position it is released: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʡuʡuɺ̠ᵊ]</span> "fog".</p>
<p><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɮ/</span> shows dramatic dialectical variation. In <a href="/wiki/Fengbin,_Hualien" title="Fengbin, Hualien">Fengbin</a>, a town in the center of Amis territory, it is pronounced as a <a href="/wiki/Central_consonant" title="Central consonant">central</a> dental fricative, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ð̪]</span>, whereas in the town of Kangko, only 15 km away, it is a <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">lateral</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɮ̪]</span>. In Northern Amis, it is a plosive <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d̪]</span>, which may be laxed to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ð̪]</span> intervocalically. The epiglottals are also reported to have different pronunciations in the north, but the descriptions are contradictory. In Central Amis, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʜ/</span> is always voiceless and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʡ/</span> is often accompanied by vibrations that suggest it involves an epiglottal trill <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Voiced_epiglottal_trill" title="Voiced epiglottal trill">ʢ</a>]</span>. Edmondson and Elsing report that these are true epiglottals initially and medially, but in utterance-final position they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiglotto-pharyngeal_consonant" title="Epiglotto-pharyngeal consonant">epiglotto–pharyngeal</a>.</p>
<p>Sakizaya, considered to be a separate language, contrasts a voiced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/z/</span> with voiceless <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/s/</span>.</p>
<p>In the practical orthography, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ts/</span> is written ⟨c⟩, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> ⟨y⟩, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʡ/</span> ⟨'⟩, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span> ⟨^⟩, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɮ/</span> ⟨d⟩, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋ/</span> ⟨ng⟩, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʜ/</span> ⟨x⟩.</p>

<p>Amis has three common vowels, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i a u/</span>. Despite the fact that a great deal of latitude is afforded by only needing to distinguish three vowels, Amis vowels stay close to their cardinal values, though there is more movement of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> toward each other (tending to the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[o]</span> range) than there is in front-vowel space (in the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[e]</span> range).</p>
<p>A voiceless epenthetic schwa optionally breaks up consonant clusters, as noted above. However, there are a small number of words where a short schwa (written <i>e)</i> may be phonemic. However, no contrast involving the schwa is known, and if it is also epenthetic, then Amis has words with no phonemic vowels at all. Examples of this <i>e</i> are <i>malmes</i> "sad", pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[maɺə̆mːə̆s]</span>, and <i>’nem</i> "six", pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʡnə̆m]</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʡə̆nə̆m]</span>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amis_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Verbs in the Amis language have some inflections including <a href="/wiki/Existential_clause" title="Existential clause">existential clause</a>, <a href="/wiki/Active_voice" title="Active voice">active voice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_voice" title="Grammatical voice">passive voice</a>, disposal sentence, <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative mood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optative_mood" title="Optative mood">optative mood</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prohibitive_mood" title="Prohibitive mood">prohibitive mood</a>.</p>
<p>There are two <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word orders</a> in Amis language, called <b>"General" Word Order</b> and <b>"Special" Word Order</b>.</p>
