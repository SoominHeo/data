<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Abaddon</h1>

<p>The Hebrew term <i><b>Abaddon</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">אֲבַדּוֹן</span>‎‎, <i><span lang="he-Latn" xml:lang="he-Latn">'Ǎḇaddōn</span></i>), and its Greek equivalent <i><b>Apollyon</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Ἀπολλύων</span>, <i>Apollyon</i>), appears in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> as both a place of destruction and by two different angel names (hades and lucifer.) In the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, <i>abaddon</i> is used with reference to a <a href="/wiki/Abyss_(religion)" title="Abyss (religion)">bottomless pit</a>, often appearing alongside the place שאול (<i><a href="/wiki/Sheol" title="Sheol">sheol</a></i>), meaning the realm of the dead. In the New Testament <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a>, an angel called Abaddon is described as the king of an army of <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>; his name is first transcribed in Greek (Revelation 9:11—"whose name in Hebrew is Abaddon, The Angel of Death." (Ἀβαδδὼν), and then translated ("which in Greek means the Destroyer" (Ἀπολλύων, <i>Apollyon</i>)). The Latin <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douay_Rheims_Bible" title="Douay Rheims Bible">Douay Rheims Bible</a> have additional notes (not present in the Greek text), "in Latin Exterminans", <i>exterminans</i> being the Latin word for "destroyer".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_Driver_Briggs" title="Brown Driver Briggs">Brown Driver Briggs</a> lexicon, the Hebrew <i>abaddon</i> (Hebrew: אבדון; avadon) is an intensive form of the <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">Semitic root</a> and verb stem <i>abad</i> (אָבַד) "perish" (transitive "destroy"), which occurs 184 times in the Hebrew Bible. The <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>, an early Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, renders "abaddon" as "ἀπώλεια",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while the Greek <i>Apollyon</i> comes from <i>apollumi</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CF%80%CF%8C%CE%BB%CE%BB%CF%85%CE%BC%CE%B9" title="wikt:ἀπόλλυμι">ἀπόλλυμι</a>), "to destroy". The Greek term <i>Apollyon</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%88%CF%80%CE%BF%CE%BB%CE%BB%CF%8D%CF%89%CE%BD" title="wikt:Ἀπολλύων">Ἀπολλύων</a>, "the destroyer"), is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_participle" title="Active participle">active participle</a> of <i>apollumi</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CF%80%CF%8C%CE%BB%CE%BB%CF%85%CE%BC%CE%B9" title="wikt:ἀπόλλυμι">ἀπόλλυμι</a>, "to destroy"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and is not used as a name in classical Greek texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew_Bible">Hebrew Bible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hebrew Bible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term <i>abaddon</i> appears six times in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masoretic_text" title="Masoretic text">Masoretic text</a> of the Hebrew Bible; <i>abaddon</i> means destruction or "place of destruction", or the realm of the dead, and is accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Sheol" title="Sheol">Sheol</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Job</a> 26:6: the grave (Sheol) is naked before Him, and destruction (Abaddon) has no covering.</li>
<li>Job 28:22: destruction (Abaddon) and death say..</li>
<li>Job 31:12: it is a fire that consumes to destruction (Abaddon)...</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Psalms" title="Book of Psalms">Psalm</a> 88:11: Shall thy loving kindness be declared in the grave (Sheol) or thy faithfulness in destruction (Abaddon)?</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Proverbs" title="Book of Proverbs">Proverbs</a> 15:11: Hell (Sheol) and Destruction (Abaddon) are before the LORD, how much more than the hearts of the children of men?</li>
<li>Proverbs 27:20: Hell (Sheol) and Destruction (Abaddon) are never full; so the eyes of man are never satisfied. (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authorised_King_James_Version" title="Authorised King James Version">KJV</a>, 1611)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Temple_era_texts">Second Temple era texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second Temple era texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The text of the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Hymns" title="Thanksgiving Hymns">Thanksgiving Hymns</a>—which was found in the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls" title="Dead Sea Scrolls">Dead Sea Scrolls</a>—tells of "the Sheol of Abaddon" and of the "torrents of <a href="/wiki/Belial" title="Belial">Belial</a> [that] burst into Abaddon". The <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Philo" title="Pseudo-Philo">Biblical Antiquities</a></i> (misattributed to <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">Philo</a>) mentions Abaddon as a place (destruction) rather than an individual. Abaddon is also one of the compartments of <a href="/wiki/Gehenna" title="Gehenna">Gehenna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-4">[4]</a></sup> By extension, it can mean an underworld abode of lost souls, or <a href="/wiki/Gehenna" title="Gehenna">Gehenna</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rabbinical_literature">Rabbinical literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rabbinical literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some legends, Abaddon is identified as a realm where the <a href="/wiki/Damnation" title="Damnation">damned</a> lie in fire and snow, one of the places in <a href="/wiki/Gehenna" title="Gehenna">Gehenna</a> that <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> visited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ginzberg_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ginzberg-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Testament">New Testament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: New Testament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scriptures#Christianity" title="Scriptures">Christian scriptures</a> contain the first known depiction of <i>Abaddon</i> as an individual entity instead of a place.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Revelation 9:11 And they had a king over them, which is the angel of the bottomless pit, whose name in the Hebrew tongue is Abaddon, but in the Greek tongue hath his name Apollyon. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KJV" title="KJV">KJV</a>, 1611</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Revelation</a> 9:11, Abaddon is described as "Destroyer",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biblegateway_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-biblegateway-6">[6]</a></sup> the angel of the <a href="/wiki/Abyss_(religion)" title="Abyss (religion)">abyss</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biblegateway_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-biblegateway-6">[6]</a></sup> and as the king of a plague of locusts resembling horses with crowned human faces, women's hair, lions' teeth, wings, iron breast-plates, and a tail with a scorpion's stinger that torments for five months anyone who does not have the seal of God on their foreheads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The symbolism of Revelation 9:11 leaves the identity of Abaddon open to interpretation. Protestant commentator <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Henry" title="Matthew Henry">Matthew Henry</a> (1708) believed Abaddon to be the <a href="/wiki/Antichrist" title="Antichrist">Antichrist</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henry_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-henry-8">[8]</a></sup> whereas the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamieson-Fausset-Brown_Commentary" title="Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Commentary">Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Commentary</a> (1871) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_H._Halley" title="Henry H. Halley">Henry H. Halley</a> (1922) identified the angel as Satan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jfb_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-jfb-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halley_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-halley-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter-Day Saints</a> believe that the use of "Abaddon" in Revelation 9 refers to the devil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> publication <i>The Interpreter's Bible</i> states: "Abaddon, however, is an angel not of Satan but of God, performing his work of destruction at God's bidding", citing the context at Revelation chapter 20, verses 1 through 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (March 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> as well cite Revelation 20:1-3 where the angel having "the key of the abyss" is actually shown to be a representative of God, one from heaven, and, rather than being "satanic", is the one that binds Satan and hurls him into the abyss; concluding that "Abaddon" is another name for Jesus Christ after his resurrection. To give further understanding, the book "Insight from The Scriptures, Vol. 1 presents the following information: "ABADDON: (A·badʹdon) [from Heb., meaning “Destruction”]. At Revelation 9:11 this Hebrew word is transliterated into the English text. There we read concerning the symbolic plague of locusts that they have “a king, the angel of the abyss. In Hebrew his name is Abaddon, but in Greek he has the name Apollyon.” In Hebrew the word ʼavad·dohnʹ means “destruction” and may also refer to “the place of destruction.” It appears in the original Hebrew text a total of five times, and in four of the occurrences it is used to parallel “the burial place,” “Sheol,” and “death.” (Ps 88:11; Job 26:6; 28:22; Pr 15:11) The word ʼavad·dohnʹ in these texts evidently refers to the destructive processes that ensue with human death, and these scriptures indicate that decay or destruction takes place in Sheol, the common grave of mankind. At Job 31:12 ʼavad·dohnʹ designates the damaging effect of an adulterous course. Job declared: “That [adulterous course] is a fire that would eat clear to destruction [ʽadh-ʼavad·dohnʹ], and among all my produce it would take root.”—Compare Pr 6:26-28, 32; 7:26, 27. Abaddon, the angel of the abyss—who is he? At Revelation 9:11, however, the word “Abaddon” is used as the name of “the angel of the abyss.” The corresponding Greek name Apollyon means “Destroyer.” In the 19th century there were efforts made to show that this text prophetically applied to individuals such as Emperor Vespasian, Muhammad, and even Napoleon, and the angel was generally regarded as “satanic.” It should be noted, however, that at Revelation 20:1-3 the angel having “the key of the abyss” is shown to be God’s representative from heaven, and rather than being “satanic,” he binds and hurls Satan into the abyss. Commenting on Revelation 9:11, The Interpreter’s Bible says: “Abaddon, however, is an angel not of Satan but of God, performing his work of destruction at God’s bidding.” In the Hebrew scriptures just considered, it is evident that ʼavad·dohnʹ is paralleled with Sheol and death. At Revelation 1:18 we find Christ Jesus stating: “I am living forever and ever, and I have the keys of death and of Hades.” His power with regard to the abyss is shown at Luke 8:31. That he has destroying power, including the power of destruction over Satan, is evident from Hebrews 2:14, which says that Jesus partook of blood and flesh in order that “through his death he might bring to nothing the one having the means to cause death, that is, the Devil.” At Revelation 19:11-16 he is clearly represented as God’s appointed Destroyer or Executioner.—See APOLLYON.</p>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gnostic_texts">Gnostic texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abaddon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gnostic texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 3rd century <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Thomas" title="Acts of Thomas">Acts of Thomas</a>, Abaddon is the name of a demon, or the <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">devil</a> himself.</p>
<p>Abaddon is given particularly important roles in two sources, a homily entitled "The Enthronement of Abbaton" by pseudo-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timothy_of_Alexandria" title="Timothy of Alexandria">Timothy of Alexandria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus_Christ_(by_Bartholomew)" title="Resurrection of Jesus Christ (by Bartholomew)">Apocalypse of Bartholomew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aziz_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aziz-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (March 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the homily by Timothy, Abbaton was first named <i>Muriel</i>, and had been given the task by <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> of collecting the earth that would be used in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>. Upon completion of this task, the angel was appointed as a guardian. Everyone, including the angels, demons, and corporeal entities feared him. Abbaton was promised that any who venerated him in life could be saved. Abaddon is also said to have a prominent role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_Judgement" title="Last Judgement">Last Judgement</a>, as the one who will take the souls to the <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_Josaphat" title="Valley of Josaphat">Valley of Josaphat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aziz_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aziz-15">[15]</a></sup> He is described in the Apocalypse of Bartholomew as being present in the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Jesus" title="Tomb of Jesus">Tomb of Jesus</a> at the moment of his <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">resurrection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
