<p>The <i><b>Ave Maria by Bach/Gounod</b></i> is a popular and much-recorded setting of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> text <i><a href="/wiki/Hail_Mary#Latin_version" title="Hail Mary">Ave Maria</a></i>, originally published in 1853 as <i><b>Méditation sur le Premier Prélude de Piano de S. Bach</b></i>. The piece consists of a melody by the French Romantic composer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gounod" title="Charles Gounod">Charles Gounod</a> that he <a href="/wiki/Superimposition" title="Superimposition">superimposed</a> over an only very slightly changed version of the <a href="/wiki/Prelude_and_Fugue_in_C_major,_BWV_846" title="Prelude and Fugue in C major, BWV 846">Prelude No. 1 in C major, BWV 846</a>, from Book I of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">J.S. Bach</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Well-Tempered_Clavier" title="The Well-Tempered Clavier">The Well-Tempered Clavier</a></i>, written 137 years earlier.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ave_Maria_(Bach/Gounod)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gounod improvised the melody, and his future father-in-law <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph-Guillaume_Zimmermann" title="Pierre-Joseph-Guillaume Zimmermann">Pierre-Joseph-Guillaume Zimmermann</a> transcribed the improvisation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and in 1853 made an arrangement for <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pump_organ" title="Pump organ">harmonium</a>. The same year it appeared with the words of <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_de_Lamartine" title="Alphonse de Lamartine">Alphonse de Lamartine</a>'s poem <i>Le livre de la vie</i> ("The Book of Life").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1859 <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ditions_Alphonse_Leduc#Heugel" title="Éditions Alphonse Leduc">Jacques Léopold Heugel</a> published a version with the familiar Latin text. The version of Bach's prelude used by Gounod includes the "Schwencke measure" (m.23), a measure allegedly added by Christian Friedrich Gottlieb Schwencke in an attempt to correct what he or someone else erroneously deemed a "faulty" progression, even though this sort of progression was standard in Bach's music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bach_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bach-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alongside <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Schubert</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ave_Maria_(Schubert)" title="Ave Maria (Schubert)"><i>Ave Maria</i></a>, the Bach/Gounod <i>Ave Maria</i> has become a fixture at wedding Masses, funerals and <a href="/wiki/Quincea%C3%B1era" title="Quinceañera">quinceañeras</a>. There are many different instrumental <a href="/wiki/Arrangement" title="Arrangement">arrangements</a> including for violin and guitar, string quartet, piano solo, cello, and even trombones. <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">Opera</a> singers, such as <a href="/wiki/Franco_Corelli" title="Franco Corelli">Franco Corelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Pavarotti" title="Luciano Pavarotti">Luciano Pavarotti</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choirs</a> have recorded it hundreds of times during the twentieth century.</p>
<p>Later in his career, Gounod composed an unrelated setting of <i>Ave Maria</i> for a four-part <a href="/wiki/SATB" title="SATB">SATB</a> choir.</p>
