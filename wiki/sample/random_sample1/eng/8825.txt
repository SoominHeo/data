<p><b>Abedi Ayew</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/juː/ long 'u' in 'cute'">juː</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>a-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BAYDI</span></span></b></i></a></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>a-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">YOU</span></span></b></i></a></span>; born 5 November 1964), known professionally as <b>Abedi Pele</b>, is a retired <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghanaian</a> footballer who played as an <a href="/wiki/Midfielder#Attacking_midfielder" title="Midfielder">attacking midfielder</a>, and who served as <a href="/wiki/Captain_(association_football)" title="Captain (association football)">captain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ghana_national_football_team" title="Ghana national football team">Ghana national team</a>. He is regarded as one of the greatest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> footballers of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottmar_Hitzfeld_picks_Abedi_Pele_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ottmar_Hitzfeld_picks_Abedi_Pele-3">[3]</a></sup> He played for several European clubs and found his fame in France's <a href="/wiki/Ligue_1" title="Ligue 1">Ligue 1</a> with <a href="/wiki/Lille_OSC" title="Lille OSC">Lille OSC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympique_Marseille" title="Olympique Marseille">Olympique Marseille</a>, where he won the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> in 1993, among other titles.</p>
<p>Three of his sons, <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim_Ayew" title="Ibrahim Ayew">Ibrahim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Ayew" title="André Ayew">André</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Ayew" title="Jordan Ayew">Jordan</a>, have also become internationals for Ghana; Ibrahim and André were selected for the <a href="/wiki/2010_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2010 FIFA World Cup">2010 FIFA World Cup</a>, while André and Jordan played at the <a href="/wiki/2014_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2014 FIFA World Cup">2014 FIFA World Cup</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abedi_Pele&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abedi Ayew was born into a family in the town of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kyebi_in_the_Eastern_Region_of_Ghana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kyebi in the Eastern Region of Ghana (page does not exist)">Kibi</a> and grew up in the town of <a href="/wiki/Dome,_Ghana" title="Dome, Ghana">Dome</a> at the northern outskirts of the city <a href="/wiki/Accra" title="Accra">Accra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tawiah_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tawiah-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abedi_Pele&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abedi Pele was one of the pioneers of African football in Europe. Abedi Pele was one of the first great African players to make an impact on European club football. He played for teams in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and most famously France where he was very instrumental in Marseille's prominence in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">European Champions League</a> during the early 1990s. Amongst his international accolades, he was often included in FIFA "All-Star" selections and captained the African All-Stars in their victory over their European counterparts in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA-CAF_Meridian_Cup" title="UEFA-CAF Meridian Cup">Meridian Cup</a> of 1997.</p>
<p>He left Ghana after the 1982 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Cup_of_Nations" title="African Cup of Nations">African Cup of Nations</a> to join <a href="/wiki/Al_Sadd_SC" title="Al Sadd SC">Al Sadd</a> in <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a> for a transfer fee of $1,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After a short spell with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FFC_Z%C3%BCrich" title="FFC Zürich">FFC Zürich</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> he returned to Ghana but, after both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asante_Kotoko_SC" title="Asante Kotoko SC">Kotoko</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accra_Hearts_of_Oak_SC" title="Accra Hearts of Oak SC">Hearts of Oak</a> failed to sign him, joined <a href="/wiki/AS_Dragons_FC_de_l%27Ou%C3%A9m%C3%A9" title="AS Dragons FC de l'Ouémé">AS Dragons FC de l'Ouémé</a> of <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>. He would later return to Ghana and play for <a href="/wiki/Real_Tamale_United" title="Real Tamale United">Real Tamale United</a> for a season. He started his career in Europe with French association football club <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamois_Niort" title="Chamois Niort">Chamois Niort</a>, subsequently joining <a href="/wiki/Montpellier_HSC" title="Montpellier HSC">Montpellier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lille_OSC" title="Lille OSC">Lille</a> before transferring to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympique_Marseille" title="Olympique Marseille">Olympique Marseille</a>. He later joined <a href="/wiki/Olympique_Lyonnais" title="Olympique Lyonnais">Lyon</a>. He also played for <a href="/wiki/Torino_F.C." title="Torino F.C.">Torino</a> of Italy and finally rounded out his European career with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TSV_1860_M%C3%BCnchen" title="TSV 1860 München">1860 München</a>.</p>
<p>Abedi Pele went on to sign a two-year contract with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Ain_Club" title="Al Ain Club">Al Ain Club</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> and was nominated one of the best foreign players to play in the UAE league.</p>
<p>He was the captain of Ghana's national football team for six years (1992–1998) and was one of the first African football players to earn a top placing in <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Player_of_the_Year" title="FIFA World Player of the Year">FIFA World Player of the Year</a> voting in 1991 and 1992. He is arguably Africa's most decorated and honoured football player ever, winning the <a href="/wiki/France_Football" title="France Football">France Football</a> Magazine African Player of the Year Award three times, was the inaugural winner of the BBC African Sports Star of the Year in 1992, and the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_African_Football" title="Confederation of African Football">Confederation of African Football</a> award twice. He was also awarded the golden ball award for being the best player at the <a href="/wiki/1992_African_Cup_of_Nations" title="1992 African Cup of Nations">1992 African Cup of Nations</a>, and was the "man of the match" in <a href="/wiki/Olympique_de_Marseille" title="Olympique de Marseille">Marseille's</a> historic <a href="/wiki/1993_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="1993 UEFA Champions League Final">European Champions League final</a> win over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC_Milan" title="AC Milan">Milan</a> in 1993.</p>
<p>Abedi Pele played for <a href="/wiki/Ghana_national_football_team" title="Ghana national football team">Ghana</a> 73 times and is considered the greatest football player in his country's history, and among the best in Africa. Until 7 June 2013 when he was surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Asamoah_Gyan" title="Asamoah Gyan">Asamoah Gyan</a>, he was the top goal scorer for Ghana's Black Stars with 33 goals. He was a fixture in the African Championships of the 1980s and 90s with his national team, and a member of Ghana's victorious team in the <a href="/wiki/1982_African_Cup_of_Nations" title="1982 African Cup of Nations">1982 African Cup of Nations</a>, but he never had an opportunity to play in the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a>, as the <i>Black Stars</i> failed to qualify for the competition during his career. However, he was arguably the most dominant figure on the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_African_Football" title="Confederation of African Football">African football</a> scene for nearly a decade. His performance in the <a href="/wiki/1992_African_Cup_of_Nations" title="1992 African Cup of Nations">1992 African Cup of Nations</a> is often cited as one of the most outstanding football displays by any player in a single tournament. His native Ghana reached the finals of the Cup that year, only to lose on penalties in the final to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire_national_football_team" title="Côte d'Ivoire national football team">Côte d'Ivoire</a> after Abedi was suspended because of a <a href="/wiki/Penalty_card#Yellow_card" title="Penalty card">Yellow card</a> he received in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-final" title="Semi-final">semi-final</a> match against <a href="/wiki/Nigeria_national_football_team" title="Nigeria national football team">Nigeria</a>. Prior to that, his three spectacular goals against <a href="/wiki/Zambia_national_football_team" title="Zambia national football team">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Congo_national_football_team" title="Congo national football team">Congo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria_national_football_team" title="Nigeria national football team">Nigeria</a> proved crucial in putting Ghana through to their seventh appearance in a CAN final – and their first final in ten years. The performance earned him the added nickname of "The African <a href="/wiki/Diego_Maradona" title="Diego Maradona">Maradona</a>". His solo run goal against Congo in the quarterfinals is often compared to Maradona's second goal against England in the 1986 World Cup. His backheader goal against Nigeria from the edge of the opponent's penalty box also heralded international acclaim.</p>

<p>Abedi holds the record for most appearances at the Nations Cup Finals, his record even surpassing the legendary <a href="/wiki/Roger_Milla" title="Roger Milla">Roger Milla</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_national_football_team" title="Cameroon national football team">Cameroon</a>. He made his first appearance at the 13th Nations Cup Finals in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> in 1982 and for the next 16 years (ending at the <a href="/wiki/1998_African_Cup_of_Nations" title="1998 African Cup of Nations">21st Finals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>) continued to grace the most prestigious football tournament on the continent, first as a member of Ghana's squad rising to become captain in 1991. Aside from his acclaimed exploits at the 1992 competition, Abedi also earned much acclaim for his three goals at the 1996 competition – where he led Ghana to the semifinals of the competition despite critics expecting him to be in the twilight of his career.</p>
<p>At the club level, he was a key figure in Olympique de Marseille's dominance of the French league – resulting in four league championships and two European Champions League finals appearances. An attacking <a href="/wiki/Midfielder" title="Midfielder">midfielder</a>, Abedi became as famous for his sublime dribbling skills as he did for his knack for scoring spectacular and often very important goals. Many such goals became regular "Goals of the Week" on ITN's weekly "European Football" program. At Marseille, he was a member of the team's "Magical Trio", along with <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Papin" title="Jean-Pierre Papin">Jean-Pierre Papin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Waddle" title="Chris Waddle">Chris Waddle</a> – spearheading perhaps Europe's strongest league side of the early 1990s. Abedi was the only remaining member of the trio still with the side when Marseille defeated Milan in the <a href="/wiki/1993_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="1993 UEFA Champions League Final">1993 UEFA Champions League Final</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.</p>
<p>Ayew has the singular honour of participating in more FIFA organized charity matches than any other African player. He is very popular in many African countries, especially the French-speaking countries because of his exploits in France. In countries such as <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and South Africa, football fans still wear T-shirts bearing his name. He has become one of the continent’s most respected and important ambassadors. Abedi Pele is a member of <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a>'s Football Committee, and of the player status committees of both FIFA and CAF. That explains why the South African FA made him a Spokesperson for their 2006 World Cup bid. Abedi is considered one of the best players in World History not to have appeared at the FIFA World Cup, and perhaps the greatest African football player ever.</p>
<p>In appreciation of Abedi's devout services to the country, the Ghanaian government awarded him the country's highest honour, the <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_Ghana" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of Ghana">Order of the Volta</a> (civil division). He thus became the first Ghanaian sportsman to be so honoured.</p>
<p>On 29 January 1997, the first UEFA-CAF Meridian Cup All-Star Match between <a href="/wiki/Europe_XI_v_Africa_XI_(1997)" title="Europe XI v Africa XI (1997)">Europe and Africa</a> was played in <a href="/wiki/S.L._Benfica" title="S.L. Benfica">S.L. Benfica</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_da_Luz_(1954)" title="Estádio da Luz (1954)">Estádio da Luz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and was televised in 100 countries worldwide, including 30 in Africa, for an audience of 60 million viewers. Former <a href="/wiki/Olympique_de_Marseille" title="Olympique de Marseille">Olympique de Marseille</a> midfielder Abedi Pele was on target with a stunning goal early in the first half and, after <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Gu%C3%A9rin" title="Vincent Guérin">Vincent Guérin</a> had equalised for Europe just before half-time, it was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Player_of_the_Year" title="African Player of the Year">1998 African Player of the Year</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustapha_Hadji" title="Mustapha Hadji">Mustapha Hadji</a>, who struck Africa’s 78th-minute winner in the 2–1 win.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uefa_meridian_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-uefa_meridian-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_retirement">After retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abedi_Pele&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>CAF-UEFA All Star</dt>
</dl>
<p>In 2001, the UEFA-CAF Meridian Cup All-Star Match format was changed slightly for the second All-Star Match to bring together great players aged between 35 and 45 who now revel in their ‘veteran’ status and play the game purely for pleasure. The squad sparked off memories of great footballing moments at club and international level<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uefa_meridian_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-uefa_meridian-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Football Ambassador</dt>
</dl>
<p>In June 2001 he was nominated by the present government of Ghana to serve as the next Chairman of the FA, an opportunity he later gave up for a more experienced former coach of Ghana for which in his own words said that this was to be an opportunity to learn from his superiors.</p>
<p>At present he owns a second division club, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nania_F.C." title="Nania F.C.">Nania F.C.</a>, with the future hopes of nurturing the young talent to augment the fledging league of the country. He has also been involved with various charity work across the African continent.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Controversy</dt>
</dl>
<p>He was recently embroiled in a serious alleged Second Division Promotion Play-off bribery scandal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Middle_League_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-National_Middle_League-8">[8]</a></sup> for which the Football Association found him and others guilty. The guilty verdict attracted fines and suspensions of Abedi and others, but these have been quashed by the Appeals Committee of the Football Association after determining that there were irregularities in the initial judgement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appeals_Decision_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Appeals_Decision-9">[9]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Ghana_Football_Association" title="Ghana Football Association">Ghana Football Association</a>. The allegations stem from an astonishing 31–0 victory<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> recorded by his club, Nania FC over a much respected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okwawu_United" title="Okwawu United">Okwawu United</a> side. A similarly farcical 28–0 result<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> was recorded in another second division match played between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Mariners" title="Great Mariners">Great Mariners</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tudu_Mighty_Jets" title="Tudu Mighty Jets">Tudu Mighty Jets</a> on the same weekend. The clubs involved in that Second Division Promotion Play-off Zone III match were also investigated and subject to the prospect of stiff penalties and demotions. Despite his vehement denials,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Abedi had been chastised by some members of the <a href="/wiki/Media_of_Ghana" title="Media of Ghana">Ghanaian media</a>, who were demanding that strong punitive actions be taken against him, by Ghana's football governing body as well as the legal system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghana_Football_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ghana_Football-13">[13]</a></sup> His wife Maha Ayew was on 3 November 2008 banned from football against this Manipulations Scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abedi_Pele&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abedi is the brother of <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Ayew" title="Kwame Ayew">Kwame</a> and Sola Ayew (ex-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accra_Hearts_of_Oak_SC" title="Accra Hearts of Oak SC">Hearts of Oak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghana_national_under-23_football_team" title="Ghana national under-23 football team">Black Meteors</a>). He is also the father of <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Ayew" title="André Ayew">André</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Ayew" title="Jordan Ayew">Jordan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdul_Rahim_Ayew" title="Abdul Rahim Ayew">Rahim</a>, Imani, and is married to Maha. Two of his children - Andre and Rahim - represented Ghana in the FIFA 2010 World Cup in South Africa. Jordan and Andre represented Ghana in the FIFA 2014 World Cup in Brazil.</p>
