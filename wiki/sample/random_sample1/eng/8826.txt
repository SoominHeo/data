<p><b>Aversa</b> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[aËvÉrsa]</a></span> (<a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">Neapolitan</a>: <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap"><i>Averza</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">comune</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Caserta" title="Province of Caserta">Province of Caserta</a> in <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">southern Italy</a>, about 5 kilometres (3 miles) north of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>. It is the centre of an agricultural district, the <i>agro aversano</i>, producing wine and cheese (famous for the typical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_buffalo" title="Domestic buffalo">buffalo</a> <a href="/wiki/Mozzarella" title="Mozzarella">mozzarella</a>). Aversa is also the main seat of the <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(division)" title="Faculty (division)">faculties</a> of <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">Architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">Engineering</a> of the <i>Seconda universitÃ  degli studi di Napoli</i> (Second University of Naples).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aversa is located near the city of Naples, from which it is separated by only 5 kilometres (3 miles), and only 16 kilometres (10 miles) from Caserta, the capital of the province to which it belongs, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caserta_Province" title="Caserta Province">Caserta Province</a>.</p>
<p>It is located in a fertile coastal plain north of Naples, thus serving as a market for agricultural products to the city. The plain on which it sits was known in ancient Roman times as the Campania Felix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecere_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cecere-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although some archaeological sites excavated near Aversa have revealed human presence in the area since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_period" title="Neolithic period">Neolithic period</a>, the first known inhabitants in the area were the <a href="/wiki/Liburnians" title="Liburnians">Liburnians</a>, people related to the <a href="/wiki/Illyrians" title="Illyrians">Illyrians</a>, whose territory was bounded on the south by <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, west by the Tyrrhenian Sea and north along the Clanio river. However, some say that the founding of the city took place with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscans" title="Etruscans">Etruscans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecere_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cecere-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In any case, because of endemic malaria that ravaged the region, the primitive city was abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecere_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cecere-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lower_middle_ages">Lower middle ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lower middle ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Only a small military fortification, a castellum, still stands in the area, which is linked to a chapel in memory of the current alleged passage through Aversa by the Apostle Paul in the year 61. A.D., via the Roman road that ran towards Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecere_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cecere-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aversa_County">Aversa County</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aversa County">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Aversa, which replaced the nearby city of <a href="/wiki/Atella" title="Atella">Atella</a> that had been laid waste during the <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Gothic_Wars_(disambiguation)" title="Gothic Wars (disambiguation)">Gothic Wars</a>, was the first of the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> territories in the Mediterranean. In 1030, the site was ceded to <a href="/wiki/Rainulf_Drengot" title="Rainulf Drengot">Rainulf Drengot</a>, a cadet of the lords of Quarrel near <a href="/wiki/Alen%C3%A7on" title="AlenÃ§on">AlenÃ§on</a> in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>; he was invested as count by Duke <a href="/wiki/Sergius_IV_of_Naples" title="Sergius IV of Naples">Sergius IV of Naples</a> and confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Conrad_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Conrad II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Conrad II</a>. By offering a generous principle of asylum for the persecuted, Rainulf enlarged the power and importance of his little <i>borgo</i>, which became the base from which the Normans forged a state in Sicily and Italy. The diplomacy of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Guiscard" title="Robert Guiscard">Robert Guiscard</a>, who built the fortifications, led to the investiture of a bishop responsible to the Pope at Aversa, which was nominally territory of the Eastern Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> One of the first bishops was the Norman <a href="/wiki/Guitmund" title="Guitmund">Guitmund</a> (died c. 1090â95), a Benedictine monk, theologian, and opponent of <a href="/wiki/Berengar_of_Tours" title="Berengar of Tours">Berengar of Tours</a>.</p>
<p>The count of Aversa, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_Aversa" title="Richard I of Aversa">Richard I</a>, was one of the chief leaders in the struggle against the Papal forces which culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Civitate" title="Battle of Civitate">Battle of Civitella del Fortore</a> (1053) in <a href="/wiki/Benevento" title="Benevento">Beneventan territory</a>; even <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_IX" title="Pope Leo IX">Pope Leo IX</a> himself was captured at what turned into a rout in favour of the Normans. The astute Richard did not treat the pope as a prisoner, however, but escorted him back to Rome with full honours, a gesture that led to the conciliation of the Normans with the Church, the lifting of the ban of excommunication that had been laid upon Aversa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angevins">Angevins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Angevins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the Norman dynasty Aversa declined in importance: the Angevin kings of Naples came to Aversa mostly to hunt and hold court in the citadel, of which a few traces remain in via Roma in Aversa's historic centre. In particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joan_I_of_Naples" title="Joan I of Naples">Queen Joanna I</a> chose Aversa for her preferred seat. There a group of nobles threw her husband <a href="/wiki/Andrew,_Duke_of_Calabria" title="Andrew, Duke of Calabria">Andrew</a> from a window with a rope around his neck. His brother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Louis_I_of_Hungary" title="King Louis I of Hungary">King Louis I of Hungary</a>, head of the <a href="/wiki/Capetian_House_of_Anjou" title="Capetian House of Anjou">Capetian House of Anjou</a>, marched into Italy and at Aversa took his vengeance at a banquet of reconciliation, as Joanna escaped to Avignon.</p>
<p>The presence of the court also benefitted Aversa by the institution of the Real Casa dell'Annunziata (about 1315) an orphanage and hospice that occupied a central place in Aversan public life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_of_Aragon">Crown of Aragon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crown of Aragon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_V_of_Aragon" title="Alfonso V of Aragon">Alfonso V of Aragon</a> permanently enthroned the kingdom of Naples within the domains of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>, Aversa continued to maintain the privileges it had enjoyed. Soon the epidemics and subdivisions of land caused it to be relegated as a peripheral urban center of Naples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecere_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cecere-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aversa_DOC">Aversa DOC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aversa DOC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Italian_wine" title="Italian wine">Italian wine</a>, both white and sparkling, under the Aversa <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DOC_appellation" title="DOC appellation">DOC appellation</a> comes from this area. Grapes destined for DOC product must be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvested_(wine)" title="Harvested (wine)">harvested</a> to a maximum <a href="/wiki/Yield_(wine)" title="Yield (wine)">yield</a> of 14 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> with the finished wines <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermented_(wine)" title="Fermented (wine)">fermented</a> to a minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_level" title="Alcohol level">alcohol level</a> of 10.5% for still and 11% for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spumante" title="Spumante">spumante</a></i> style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saunders_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saunders-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_variety" title="Grape variety">grape variety</a> of the region is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asprinio" title="Asprinio">Asprinio</a> which must constitute at least 85% of the wines, with other local white grape varieties, such as <a href="/wiki/Fiano_(grape)" title="Fiano (grape)">Fiano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trebbiano" title="Trebbiano">Trebbiano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greco_(grape)" title="Greco (grape)">Greco</a> permitted to fill in the remainder. <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">Viticulture</a> in Aversa is unique for its use of growing the grapevines with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poplar_trees" title="Poplar trees">poplar trees</a> acting as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trellises_(wine)" title="Trellises (wine)">trellises</a>. This traditional method of trellising means that almost all harvesting is done by hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saunders_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saunders-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aversa, the second in historic importance of the dioceses of Campania, is the "city of a hundred churches" in its extensive historic center. Among its monuments:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Romanesque <a href="/wiki/Aversa_Cathedral" title="Aversa Cathedral">Duomo</a>, dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" title="Paul of Tarsus">Saint Paul</a>, has a spectacular <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a> and a majestic octagonal <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a>. <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Solimena" title="Francesco Solimena">Francesco Solimena</a>'s <i>Madonna of the Gonfalone</i>and the Quattrocento painter <a href="/wiki/Angiolillo_Arcuccio" title="Angiolillo Arcuccio">Angiolillo Arcuccio</a>'s <i>Martyrdom of St Sebastian</i> are in the Duomo. The pre-Romanesque sculpture of St George and the Dragon is one of the few surviving free-standing sculptures of its date. An outstanding collection of Baroque liturgical silver is kept in the Treasury.</li>
<li>The Baroque <i>Church of San Francesco delle Monache</i>.</li>
<li>The <i>Ospedale Psichiatrico Santa Maria Maddalena</i> founded by <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Murat" title="Joachim Murat">Joachim Murat</a> in 1813 which was the oldest Judicial Psychiatric Hospital in Italy and the center of many accusations of abuse.</li>
<li>The <i>Real Casa dell'Annunziata</i>.</li>
<li>The Benedictine Abbey of <i>San Lorenzo</i>, founded in the 10th century, with a fine Renaissance cloister.</li>
<li>The Church of <i>Santa Maria a Piazza</i>, founded in the 10th century, has frescoes of the school of <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>.</li>
<li>Other churches in the city conserve paintings by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guido_da_Siena" title="Guido da Siena">Guido da Siena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polidoro_da_Caravaggio" title="Polidoro da Caravaggio">Polidoro da Caravaggio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marco_Pino_da_Siena" title="Marco Pino da Siena">Marco Pino da Siena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietro_da_Cortona" title="Pietro da Cortona">Pietro da Cortona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Negroni" title="Pietro Negroni">Pietro Negroni</a> <i>il Giovane Zingaro</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Ribera" title="Giuseppe Ribera">Giuseppe Ribera</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cornelius_Smeet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cornelius Smeet (page does not exist)">Cornelius Smeet</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abram_Vink&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abram Vink (page does not exist)">Abram Vink</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teodoro_d%27Errico" title="Teodoro d'Errico">Teodoro d'Errico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_de_Mura" title="Francesco de Mura">Francesco de Mura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Stanzione" title="Massimo Stanzione">Massimo Stanzione</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paolo_de_Maio" title="Paolo de Maio">Paolo de Maio</a>.</li>
<li>The Historic Former Railway station (Stazione Ferrovia Napoli Piedimonte D'Alife) of a long closed 1913 railway</li>
<li>The Historic Aragonese Castle which now houses the Italian Penitentiary Police (Italy's Correction Officers) training facility</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aversa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aversa_railway_station" title="Aversa railway station">Aversa railway station</a> is a major station on the <a href="/wiki/Rome%E2%80%93Formia%E2%80%93Naples_railway" title="RomeâFormiaâNaples railway">RomeâFormiaâNaples railway</a>. Most of the traffic is operated by <a href="/wiki/Trenitalia" title="Trenitalia">Trenitalia</a>, although some trains run under the aegis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MetroCampania_NordEst" title="MetroCampania NordEst">MetroCampania NordEst</a> (successor to the former <a href="/wiki/Ferrovia_Alifana" title="Ferrovia Alifana">Ferrovia Alifana</a>). Aversa is served by a suburban train connection to the Piscinola station of northern Naples.</p>
<p>The nearest airport is that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoli-Capodichino" title="Napoli-Capodichino">Napoli-Capodichino</a>, 10 kilometres (6 miles) away.</p>
<p>Aversa is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Autostrada_A1_(Italy)" title="Autostrada A1 (Italy)">A1 Motorway</a> by the SP 335-VI Provincial Road (former SS 265 State Road) and the SS 7 bis. Public bus transport is responsibility of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Compagnia_Trasporti_Pubblici_Napoli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Compagnia Trasporti Pubblici Napoli (page does not exist)">CTP</a> (Compagnia Trasporti Pubblici Napoli).</p>
