<p><b>Abu al-Fida</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><big>أبو الفداء</big></span>‎‎; November 1273 – October 27, 1331), fully <b>Abu Al-fida' Isma'il Ibn 'ali ibn Mahmud Al-malik Al-mu'ayyad 'imad Ad-din</b> and better known in English as <b>Abulfeda</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[a]</a></sup> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_people" title="Kurdish people">Kurdish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographer</a> and local governor of <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> He was a prince of the <a href="/wiki/Ayyubid_dynasty" title="Ayyubid dynasty">Ayyubid dynasty</a> and the author of <i>The memoirs of a Syrian prince: Abu'l-Fidāʼ, Sultan of Ḥamāh</i>. The crater <a href="/wiki/Abulfeda_(crater)" title="Abulfeda (crater)">Abulfeda</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, is named after him.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu%27l-Fida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abu'l-Fida was born in <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> where his father <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Malik_ul-Afdal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Malik ul-Afdal (page does not exist)">Malik ul-Afdal</a>, brother of Emir <a href="/wiki/Al-Mansur_Muhammad_II" title="Al-Mansur Muhammad II">Al-Mansur Muhammad II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a>, had fled from the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a>.</p>
<p>In his boyhood he devoted himself to the study of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> and the sciences, but from his twelfth year onward, he was almost constantly engaged in military expeditions, chiefly against the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">crusaders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1285 he was present at the assault of a stronghold of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_St._John" title="Knights of St. John">Knights of St. John</a>, and took part in the sieges of <a href="/wiki/Tripoli,_Lebanon" title="Tripoli, Lebanon">Tripoli</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akko" title="Akko">Acre</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qal%27at_ar-Rum" title="Qal'at ar-Rum">Qal'at ar-Rum</a>. In 1298 he entered the service of the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk" title="Mamluk">Mamluk</a> <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malik_al-Nasir" title="Malik al-Nasir">Malik al-Nasir</a> and after twelve years was invested by him with the governorship of <a href="/wiki/Hama" title="Hama">Hama</a>. In 1312 he became prince with the title <b>Malik us-Salhn</b>, and in 1320 received the hereditary rank of sultan with the title <b>Malik ul-Mu'ayyad</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>For more than twenty years all together he reigned in tranquillity and splendour, devoting himself to the duties of government and to the composition of the works to which he is chiefly indebted for his fame. He was a munificent patron of men of letters, who came in large numbers to his court. He died in 1331.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abu%27l-Fida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Concise_History_of_Humanity_or_Chronicles_(book)" title="The Concise History of Humanity or Chronicles (book)">The Concise History of Humanity or Chronicles</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><i>المختصر في أخبار البشر - تاريخ أبى الفداء</i></span>‎‎) (History of Abu al-Fida); his chief historical work is <i>An Abridgment of the History at the Human Race,</i> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Annals" title="Annals">annals</a> extending from the creation of the world to the year 1329 (<a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, 2 vols. 1869).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-6">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>A Sketch of the Countries</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><i>تقويم البلدان</i></span>‎‎). His <i>Geography</i> is, like much of the history, founded on the works of his predecessors, including the works of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Idrisi" title="Muhammad al-Idrisi">Muhammad al-Idrisi</a>. A long introduction on various geographical matters is followed by twenty-eight sections dealing in tabular form with the chief towns of the world. After each name are given the longitude, latitude, climate, spelling, and then observations generally taken from earlier authors. Parts of the work were published and translated as early as 1650 in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-6">[5]</a></sup> In his works Abu'l-Fida correctly mentions the latitude and longitude of the city of <a href="/wiki/Quanzhou" title="Quanzhou">Quanzhou</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>A book about medicine named <i>Kunash</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><i>الكُنّاش</i></span>‎‎)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abu%27l-Fida_Mosque" title="Abu'l-Fida Mosque">Abu'l-Fida Mosque</a></li>
</ul>
