<p><b>Mount Asama</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">浅間山</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Asama-yama</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is an active <a href="/wiki/Complex_volcano" title="Complex volcano">complex volcano</a> in central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>, the main island of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The volcano is the most active on Honshū.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> classifies Mount Asama as rank A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qvoj-list_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-qvoj-list-3">[3]</a></sup> It stands 2,568 metres (8,425 ft) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> on the border of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunma_prefecture" title="Gunma prefecture">Gunma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagano_prefecture" title="Nagano prefecture">Nagano prefectures</a>. It is included in <i><a href="/wiki/100_Famous_Japanese_Mountains" title="100 Famous Japanese Mountains">100 Famous Japanese Mountains</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asama_class_cruiser" title="Asama class cruiser">A cruiser class</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a> was named after it, including <a href="/wiki/Lead_ship" title="Lead ship">lead ship</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Asama" title="Japanese cruiser Asama">Asama</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mount Asama sits at the conjunction of the <a href="/wiki/Izu-Bonin-Mariana_Arc" title="Izu-Bonin-Mariana Arc">Izu-Bonin-Mariana Arc</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeastern_Japan_Arc" title="Northeastern Japan Arc">Northeastern Japan Arc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-2">[2]</a></sup> The mountain is built up from non-alkali mafic and pyroclastic volcanic rocks dating from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semless_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Semless-1">[1]</a></sup> The main rock type is <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dacite" title="Dacite">dacite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QVoJ_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-QVoJ-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AsamaYamaS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/AsamaYamaS.jpg/200px-AsamaYamaS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/AsamaYamaS.jpg/300px-AsamaYamaS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/AsamaYamaS.jpg/400px-AsamaYamaS.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AsamaYamaS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Viewed from the North</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Scientists from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nagoya_University" title="Nagoya University">Nagoya University</a> completed their first successful imaging experiment of the interior of the volcano in April 2007. By detecting sub-atomic particles called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muons" title="Muons">muons</a> as they passed through the volcano after arriving from space, the scientists were able gradually to build up a picture of the interior, creating images of cavities through which lava was passing deep inside the volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A University of Tokyo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcanology" title="Vulcanology">volcano observatory</a> is located on the mountain's east slope. Volcanic gas emissions from this volcano are measured by a <a href="/wiki/Multi-Component_Gas_Analyzer_System" title="Multi-Component Gas Analyzer System">Multi-Component Gas Analyzer System</a>, which detects pre-eruptive degassing of rising magmas, improving <a href="/wiki/Prediction_of_volcanic_activity" title="Prediction of volcanic activity">prediction of volcanic activity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eruptive_history">Eruptive history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eruptive history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_(a),_SRTM-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="900" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_%28a%29%2C_SRTM-1.jpg/170px-Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_%28a%29%2C_SRTM-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_%28a%29%2C_SRTM-1.jpg/255px-Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_%28a%29%2C_SRTM-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_%28a%29%2C_SRTM-1.jpg/340px-Mount_Asama_Relief_Map_%28a%29%2C_SRTM-1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>The geologic features of this active volcano are closely monitored with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismograph" title="Seismograph">seismographs</a> and strategically positioned video cameras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup> Scientists have noted a range of textural variety in the ash which has been deposited in the region during the serial eruptions since the <i>Tennin</i> eruption of 1108.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009_eruptions">2009 eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2009 eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mount Asama erupted in early February 2009, sending ash to a height of 2 km (6,600 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-9">[9]</a></sup> and throwing rocks up to 1 km (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>5</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> mi) from the crater. Ash fall was reported in Tokyo, 145 km (90 mi) southeast of the volcano crater. On 16 February there were 13 recorded volcanic earthquakes and an eruption emitting smoke and ash in a cloud 400 m (1,300 ft) high.</p>
<p>Mount Asama continued to have small eruptions, tremors and earthquakes in February and remained on level-3 alert (with a danger zone within 4 km (<span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> mi) of the crater).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Japanese_Meteorological_Agency_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Japanese_Meteorological_Agency-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_eruptions">2008 eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008 eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three small ash eruptions occurred at Asama volcano in August 2008. This was the first activity at the volcano since 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_eruption">2004 eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2004 eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A single vulcanian eruption occurred at Asama volcano at 11:02 UT on 1 September 2004. Incandescent blocks were ejected from the summit and caused many fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup> The eruption sent ash and rock as far away as 200 km (120 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1995_earthquakes">1995 earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1995 earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 1995, more than 1000 earthquakes were detected at the volcanic mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1983_eruptions">1983 eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1983 eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An explosive eruption occurred on 8 April. Incandescent tephra was ejected, and ash fell 250 km (160 mi) from the volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1982_eruption">1982 eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1982 eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Explosive eruptions occurred at the summit of Asama volcano on 26 April. Fine ash fell in Tokyo, 130 km (80 mi) to the SE, for the first time in 23 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tenmei_eruption_.281783.29"><i>Tenmei</i> eruption (1783)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tenmei eruption (1783)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="620" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg/200px-Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg/300px-Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg/400px-Onioshidashi_Lava_flow.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>Mount Asama erupted in 1783 (<i><a href="/wiki/Tenmei" title="Tenmei">Tenmei</a> 3</i>), causing widespread damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The three-month-long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plinian" title="Plinian">plinian</a> eruption that began on 9 May 1783, produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andesitic" title="Andesitic">andesitic</a> pumice falls, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyroclastic" title="Pyroclastic">pyroclastic</a> flows, lava flows, and enlarged the cone. The climactic eruption began on 4 August and lasted for 15 hours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards2003_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richards2003-12">[12]</a></sup> and contained pumice falls and pyroclastic flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup> The complex features of this eruption are explained by rapid deposits of coarse pyroclastic ash near the vent and the subsequent flows of lava; and these events which were accompanied by a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eruption_plume" title="Eruption plume">eruption plume</a> which generated further injections of pumice into the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Titsingh" title="Isaac Titsingh">Isaac Titsingh</a>'s account of the Asama-yama eruption was posthumously published in French in Paris in 1820;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and an English translation was published in London in 1822.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> These books were based on Japanese sources; and the work represented first of its kind to be disseminated in Europe and the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The volcano's devastation exacerbated what was already known as the "Great <i>Tenmei</i> Famine". Much of the agriculturally productive land in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shinano_province" title="Shinano province">Shinano</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dzuke_province" title="Kōzuke province">Kōzuke</a> provinces would remain fallow or under-producing for the next four or five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The effects of this eruption were made worse because, after years of near or actual famine, neither the authorities nor the people had any remaining reserves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The 4 August eruption killed up to 1,400 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StoltmanLidstone2007_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-StoltmanLidstone2007-19">[19]</a></sup> with an additional 20,000 more deaths caused by the famine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowman2000_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowman2000-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tennin_eruption_.281108.29"><i>Tennin</i> eruption (1108)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tennin eruption (1108)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eruption of Mount Asama in 1108 (<i><a href="/wiki/Tennin_(era)" title="Tennin (era)">Tennin</a> 1</i>) has been the subject of studies by modern science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Records suggest that the magnitude of this plinian eruption was twice as large as that of the <i>Tenmei</i> catastrophe in 1783.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marking_the_span_of_Japan.27s_history">Marking the span of Japan's history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Marking the span of Japan's history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eruptions of Mount Asama mark the span of Japan's recorded history, including: <i><b>2009</b></i>, <i><b>2008</b></i>, <i><b>2004</b></i>, 2003, <i><b>1995</b></i>, 1990, <i><b>1983</b></i>, <i><b>1982</b></i>, 1973, 1965, 1961, 1958–59, 1953–55, 1952, 1952, 1950–51, 1949, 1947, 1946, 1944–45, 1938–42, 1935–37, 1934, 1934, 1933, 1931–32, 1930, 1929, 1929, 1927–28, 1924, 1922, 1920–21, 1919, 1918?, 1917, 1916, 1915, 1914, 1909–14, 1908, 1908, 1907, 1907, 1906, 1905?, 1904, 1903, 1902, 1902, 1900–01, 1899, 1899, 1894, 1889, 1879, 1878?, 1875, 1869, 1815, 1803, 1803, <i><b>1783</b></i>, 1779?, 1777, 1776, 1769, 1762, 1755, 1754, 1733, 1732, 1731, 1729, 1729, 1728, 1723, 1723, 1722, 1721, 1720, 1719, 1718, 1717, 1711, 1710, 1708–09, 1706, 1704, 1703, 1669, 1661, 1661, 1660, 1659, 1658, 1657, 1656, 1655, 1653, 1652, 1651, 1650?, 1649, 1648, 1648, 1647, 1645, 1644, 1609, 1605, 1604, 1600, 1598, 1597, 1596, 1596, 1595?, 1591, 1590, 1532, 1528, 1527, 1518, 1427?, 1281, <i><b>1108</b></i>, 887, 685.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seach1_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-seach1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Note: The dates of eruptions featured in this article appear in bold italics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Popular_Culture">In Popular Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Asama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In Popular Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mount Asama served as the backdrop to Japan's first color film, <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Comes_Home" title="Carmen Comes Home">Carmen Comes Home</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Several references are made to Mount Asama throughout the film, including a song composed by a blind composer, Mr. Taguchi.</p>
