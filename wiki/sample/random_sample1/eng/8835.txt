<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Asabiyyah</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>`Asabiyya</b> or <b>asabiyyah</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>: عصبيّة) refers to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_solidarity" title="Social solidarity">social solidarity</a> with an emphasis on <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unity" title="wikt:unity">unity</a>, group consciousness and sense of shared purpose, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_cohesion" title="Social cohesion">social cohesion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zua_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-zua-1">[1]</a></sup> originally in a context of "<a href="/wiki/Tribalism" title="Tribalism">tribalism</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clanism</a>". It was a familiar term in the pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> era, but became popularized in <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Muqaddimah" title="Muqaddimah">Muqaddimah</a></i> where it is described as the fundamental bond of human society and the basic motive force of history. `Asabiyya is neither necessarily <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomadic</a> nor based on blood relations; rather, it resembles philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Classical_republicanism" title="Classical republicanism">classical republicanism</a>. In the modern period, the term is generally analogous to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_solidarity" title="Social solidarity">solidarity</a>. However, it is often negatively associated because it can sometimes suggest loyalty to one's group regardless of circumstances, or <a href="/wiki/Partisan_(political)" title="Partisan (political)">partisanship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asabiyyah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> uses the term <i>Asabiyyah</i> to describe the bond of cohesion among humans in a group forming <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>. The bond, Asabiyyah, exists at any level of civilization, from nomadic society to states and empires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibi_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tibi-3">[3]</a></sup> Asabiyyah is most strong in the nomadic phase, and decreases as civilization advances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibi_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tibi-3">[3]</a></sup> As this Asabiyyah declines, another more compelling Asabiyyah may take its place; thus, civilizations rise and fall, and history describes these cycles of Asabiyyah as they play out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibi_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-tibi-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun argues that each <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynasty</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>) has within itself the seeds of its own downfall. He explains that ruling houses tend to emerge on the peripheries of great <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empires</a> and use the much stronger `asabiyya present in those areas to their advantage, in order to bring about a change in leadership. This implies that the new rulers are at first considered "<a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarians</a>" by comparison to the old ones. As they establish themselves at the center of their empire, they become increasingly lax, less coordinated, disciplined and watchful, and more concerned with maintaining their new power and lifestyle at the centre of the empire—i.e, their internal cohesion and ties to the original peripheral group, the `asabiyya, dissolves into factionalism and individualism, diminishing their capacity as a political unit. Thus, conditions are created wherein a new dynasty can emerge at the periphery of their control, grow strong, and effect a change in leadership, beginning the cycle anew.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asabiyyah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nomadic invaders have on many occasions ended up adopting the religion and culture of the civilizations they conquered, which was true for various <a href="/wiki/Circassians" title="Circassians">Circassians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a>, some of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongol</a> invaders that invaded the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Islamic world</a> and ended up adopting <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> religion and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_culture" title="Muslim culture">culture</a>.</p>
<p>According to Khaldun, the Asabiyyah cycle was also true for every other pre-modern civilization, whether in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> whose <a href="/wiki/Dynastic_cycle" title="Dynastic cycle">dynastic cycles</a> resemble the Asabiyyah cycles described by Ibn Khaldun, in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> where waves of <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarian</a> invaders adopted <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> culture, or in <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">India</a> or <a href="/wiki/History_of_Iran" title="History of Iran">Persia</a> where nomadic invaders assimilated into those civilizations.</p>
