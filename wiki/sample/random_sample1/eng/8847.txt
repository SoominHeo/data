<p><b>Arthur Cayley</b> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society#Fellows" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">F.R.S.</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 16 August 1821 – 26 January 1895) was a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>. He helped found the modern British school of <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>.</p>
<p>As a child, Cayley enjoyed solving complex maths problems for amusement. He entered <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>, where he excelled in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. He worked as a <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a> for 14 years.</p>
<p>He postulated the <a href="/wiki/Cayley%E2%80%93Hamilton_theorem" title="Cayley–Hamilton theorem">Cayley–Hamilton theorem</a>—that every <a href="/wiki/Square_matrix" title="Square matrix">square matrix</a> is a root of its own <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_polynomial" title="Characteristic polynomial">characteristic polynomial</a>, and verified it for matrices of order 2 and 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was the first to define the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a> in the modern way—as a set with a <a href="/wiki/Binary_function" title="Binary function">binary</a> operation satisfying certain laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Formerly, when mathematicians spoke of "groups", they had meant <a href="/wiki/Permutation_group" title="Permutation group">permutation groups</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cayley%27s_theorem" title="Cayley's theorem">Cayley's theorem</a> is named in honour of Cayley.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Cayley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arthur Cayley was born in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_London" title="Richmond, London">Richmond, London</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, on 16 August 1821. His father, Henry Cayley, was a distant cousin of <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">Sir George Cayley</a> the <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a> engineer innovator, and descended from an ancient <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> family. He settled in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a>. His <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> was Maria Antonia Doughty, daughter of William Doughty. According to some writers she was Russian, but her father's name indicates an English origin. His brother was the linguist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Bagot_Cayley" title="Charles Bagot Cayley">Charles Bagot Cayley</a>. Arthur spent his first eight years in Saint Petersburg. In 1829 his parents were settled permanently at <a href="/wiki/Blackheath,_London" title="Blackheath, London">Blackheath</a>, near London. Arthur was sent to a private school. At age 14 he was sent to <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_School" title="King's College School">King's College School</a>. The school's master observed indications of mathematical genius and advised the father to educate his son not for his own business, as he had intended, but to enter the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Cayley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the unusually early age of 17 Cayley began residence at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>. The cause of the <a href="/wiki/Analytical_Society" title="Analytical Society">Analytical Society</a> had now triumphed, and the <i>Cambridge Mathematical Journal</i> had been instituted by Gregory and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Leslie_Ellis" title="Robert Leslie Ellis">Robert Leslie Ellis</a>. To this journal, at the age of twenty, Cayley contributed three papers, on subjects that had been suggested by reading the <i>Mécanique analytique</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph Louis Lagrange">Lagrange</a> and some of the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laplace" title="Laplace">Laplace</a>.</p>
<p>Cayley's tutor at Cambridge was <a href="/wiki/George_Peacock" title="George Peacock">George Peacock</a> and his private coach was <a href="/wiki/William_Hopkins" title="William Hopkins">William Hopkins</a>. He finished his undergraduate course by winning the place of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senior_Wrangler" title="Senior Wrangler">Senior Wrangler</a>, and the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smith%27s_prize" title="Smith's prize">Smith's prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His next step was to take the M.A. degree, and win a Fellowship by competitive examination. He continued to reside at Cambridge University for four years; during which time he took some pupils, but his main work was the preparation of 28 memoirs to the <i>Mathematical Journal</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_lawyer">As a lawyer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Cayley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As a lawyer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the limited tenure of his fellowship it was necessary to choose a profession; like <a href="/wiki/Augustus_De_Morgan" title="Augustus De Morgan">De Morgan</a>, Cayley chose law, and at age 25 entered at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Inn,_London" title="Lincoln's Inn, London">Lincoln's Inn, London</a>. He made a specialty of <a href="/wiki/Conveyancing" title="Conveyancing">conveyancing</a>. It was while he was a pupil at the <a href="/wiki/Bar_examination" title="Bar examination">bar examination</a> that he went to <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> to hear <a href="/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton" title="William Rowan Hamilton">Hamilton</a>'s lectures on <a href="/wiki/Quaternion" title="Quaternion">quaternions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>His friend <a href="/wiki/James_Joseph_Sylvester" title="James Joseph Sylvester">J. J. Sylvester</a>, his senior by five years at Cambridge, was then an <a href="/wiki/Actuary" title="Actuary">actuary</a>, resident in London; they used to walk together round the courts of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Inn" title="Lincoln's Inn">Lincoln's Inn</a>, discussing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_invariants" title="Theory of invariants">theory of invariants</a> and covariants. During this period of his life, extending over fourteen years, Cayley produced between two and three hundred papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_professor">As a professor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Cayley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As a professor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At Cambridge University the ancient professorship of pure mathematics is denominated by the <a href="/wiki/Lucasian_Professor_of_Mathematics" title="Lucasian Professor of Mathematics">Lucasian</a>, and is the chair that had been occupied by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>. Around 1860, certain funds bequeathed by Lady Sadleir to the University, having become useless for their original purpose, were employed to establish another professorship of pure mathematics, called the <a href="/wiki/Sadleirian_Professor_of_Pure_Mathematics" title="Sadleirian Professor of Pure Mathematics">Sadleirian</a>. The duties of the new professor were defined to be <i>"to explain and teach the principles of pure mathematics and to apply himself to the advancement of that science."</i> To this chair Cayley was elected when 42 years old. He gave up a lucrative practice for a modest salary; but he never regretted the exchange, for the chair at Cambridge enabled him to end the divided allegiance between law and mathematics, and to devote his energies to the pursuit that he liked best. He at once married and settled down in Cambridge. More fortunate than Hamilton in his choice, his home life was one of great happiness. His friend and fellow investigator, Sylvester, once remarked that Cayley had been much more fortunate than himself; that they both lived as bachelors in London, but that Cayley had married and settled down to a quiet and peaceful life at Cambridge; whereas he had never married, and had been fighting the world all his days.</p>
<p>At first the teaching duty of the Sadleirian professorship was limited to a course of lectures extending over one of the terms of the academic year; but when the University was reformed about 1886, and part of the college funds applied to the better endowment of the University <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professors</a>, the lectures were extended over two terms. For many years the attendance was small, and came almost entirely from those who had finished their career of preparation for competitive examinations; after the reform the attendance numbered about fifteen. The subject lectured on was generally that of the memoir on which the professor was for the time engaged.</p>
<p>The other duty of the chair — the advancement of mathematical science — was discharged in a handsome manner by the long series of memoirs that he published, ranging over every department of pure mathematics. But it was also discharged in a much less obtrusive way; he became the standing referee on the merits of mathematical papers to many societies both at home and abroad.</p>
<p>In 1872 he was made an honorary fellow of Trinity College, and three years later an ordinary fellow, which meant stipend as well as honour. About this time his friends subscribed for a presentation portrait. <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">Maxwell</a> wrote an address to the committee of subscribers who had charge of the Cayley portrait fund. The verses refer to the subjects investigated in several of Cayley's most elaborate memoirs; such as, Chapters on the Analytical Geometry of <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>

<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>n</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>

</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="n" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.405ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span> dimensions; On the theory of <a href="/wiki/Determinant" title="Determinant">Determinants</a>; Memoir on the theory of Matrices; Memoirs on skew surfaces, otherwise Scrolls; On the delineation of a Cubic Scroll, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to his work on <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a>, Cayley made fundamental contributions to <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>. Cayley and <a href="/wiki/George_Salmon" title="George Salmon">Salmon</a> discovered the 27 lines on a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_surface" title="Cubic surface">cubic surface</a>. Cayley constructed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chow_variety" title="Chow variety">Chow variety</a> of all curves in projective 3-space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He founded the algebro-geometric theory of <a href="/wiki/Ruled_surface" title="Ruled surface">ruled surfaces</a>.</p>
<p>In 1876 he published a <i>Treatise on <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_function" title="Elliptic function">Elliptic Functions</a></i>. He took great interest in the movement for the University education of women. At Cambridge the women's colleges are Girton and Newnham. In the early days of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girton_College" title="Girton College">Girton College</a> he gave direct help in teaching, and for some years he was chairman of the council of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newnham_College" title="Newnham College">Newnham College</a>, in the progress of which he took the keenest interest to the last.</p>
<p>In 1881 he received from the <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>, Baltimore, where Sylvester was then professor of mathematics, an invitation to deliver a course of lectures. He accepted the invitation, and lectured at Baltimore during the first five months of 1882 on the subject of the <i>Abelian and Theta Functions</i>.</p>
<p>In 1893 Cayley became a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BAE">BAE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Cayley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: BAE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1883 Cayley was President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association for the Advancement of Science</a>. The meeting was held at Southport, in the north of England. As the President's address is one of the great popular events of the meeting, and brings out an audience of general culture, it is usually made as little technical as possible. <a href="#CITEREFCayley1996">Cayley (1996)</a> took for his subject the Progress of Pure Mathematics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Collected_Papers">The <i>Collected Papers</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Cayley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Collected Papers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1889 the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">Cambridge University Press</a> requested him to prepare his mathematical papers for publication in a collected form—a request which he appreciated very much. They are printed in magnificent <a href="/wiki/Bookbinding" title="Bookbinding">quarto volumes</a>, of which seven appeared under his own editorship. While editing these volumes, he was suffering from a painful internal malady, to which he succumbed on 26 January 1895, in the 74th year of his age. When the funeral took place, a great assemblage met in Trinity Chapel, comprising members of the University, official representatives of Russia and America, and many of the most illustrious philosophers of <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>.</p>
<p>The remainder of his papers were edited by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Forsyth" title="Andrew Forsyth">Andrew Forsyth</a>, his successor in the Sadleirian Chair. The Collected Mathematical papers number thirteen quarto volumes, and contain 967 papers. Cayley retained to the last his fondness for novel-reading and for travelling. He also took special pleasure in paintings and architecture, and he practiced <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">water-color painting</a>, which he found useful sometimes in making mathematical diagrams.</p>
