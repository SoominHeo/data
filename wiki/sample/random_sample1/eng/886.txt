<p>"<b>Cleanin' Out My Closet</b>" is a song by American rapper <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> from his album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Eminem_Show" title="The Eminem Show">The Eminem Show</a></i> (2002). "Cleanin' Out My Closet" was the second single released off the album following "<a href="/wiki/Without_Me" title="Without Me">Without Me</a>". However, unlike the preceding single which was as his humorous Slim Shady <a href="/wiki/Persona" title="Persona">persona</a>, "Cleanin' Out My Closet" was a serious yet sarcastic song based on his childhood and relationships. It also depicts Eminem venting his anger towards his mother, Debbie Mathers, for the way she raised him. In the chorus, Eminem sarcastically apologizes to his mother for hurting her and making her cry, but takes it back by showing the world what his mother, according to him, was like.</p>
<p>It became the second Top 10 single from the album, reaching number four on the United States <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart, and one of the highest-charting singles of his career. The song did not reach number one on any foreign charts, but was certified Platinum in Australia. "Cleanin' Out My Closet" was also used in the initial theatrical trailer to Eminem's film <i><a href="/wiki/8_Mile_(film)" title="8 Mile (film)">8 Mile</a></i>, released in November 2002. With the release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marshall_Mathers_LP_2" title="The Marshall Mathers LP 2">The Marshall Mathers LP 2</a></i> in 2013, Eminem apologized to his mother on the track "<a href="/wiki/Headlights_(Eminem_song)" title="Headlights (Eminem song)">Headlights</a>". He stated (among other things such as wrongfully mocking her drug problem, regretting that he never let his daughters meet their grandmother) that he cringes when he hears "Cleanin' Out My Closet" on the radio, and that he does not play the song in his concerts anymore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrical_content">Lyrical content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanin%27_Out_My_Closet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyrical content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In the introduction, Eminem says, "Where's my snare. I have no snare in my headphones." Using his verified Genius account, he explained that this was a true story: "the engineer had it muted for some reason."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the first verse, Eminem deals with the criticism and activism against his lyrics, and how these people do not understand his background and personality.</li>
<li>The second verse then moves on to how his father abandoned him and his mother when Eminem was an infant, and then goes on to say how he would not do the same to his own daughter. The verse ends with a reference to Eminem's arrest for an alleged pistol whip on a bouncer who kissed his wife, a theme prevalent throughout the album.</li>
<li>At the start of the third verse, Eminem states that his comments regarding his mother are not made for the sake of public attention "now I would never dis my own mama just to get recognition", before alleging her for abuse of medicinal drugs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnchausen_syndrome_by_proxy" title="MÃ¼nchausen syndrome by proxy">MÃ¼nchausen syndrome by proxy</a> as stated in the line, "Victim of MÃ¼nchausen's syndrome. My whole life I was made to believe I was sick when I wasn't." He also mentions the demo that his mother put out for him in hopes of repairing their estranged relationship called "Set the Record Straight" and claimed she made it to try to justify the way she treated him. He then goes on to insult his mother by telling her he will not take his daughter(s) to her funeral and is enraged that she will not admit she was a bad mother and calls her out for suing him and says: "But how dare you try and take what you didn't help me to get / You selfish bitch, I hope you fucking burn in hell for this shit!" Finally, he mentions that at his uncle Ronnie's funeral, his mother told him he should have died instead, giving way to the final lyric: "Well, guess what? I am dead / Dead to you as can be!"</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanin%27_Out_My_Closet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Allmusic highlighted this song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> David Browne was lukewarm: "The unhealed scars of his childhood are pored over in <i>Cleanin' Out My Closet</i>: In the chorus, he apologizes for making his mama cry, but in the verses, he lashes out at her (<i>you selfish bitch</i>) and vows to be a better dad than his own absentee father (<i>I wonder if he even kissed me goodbye</i>). The song is both fragile and furious, and the syncopated music-box arrangement matches it in tension."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> J-23 was positive: "Cleanin' Out My Closet" features the album's most personal rhymes as he lets us know just how he feels about his parents. His third verse, dealing with his mother, is truly something to behold."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> NME also praised the single: "Cleanin' Out My Closet", a stunning outpouring of grief-stricken anger against his mother."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> RapReviews agreed: "The jokes don't last very long though, because Eminem's next song "Cleanin' Out My Closet" is a direct attack on his mother for not really being one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Rolling Stone praised production: "Cleanin' Out My Closet" feature electric-guitar rhythms fraternizing with hip-hop-sensible drum patterns" and he noted that his (Eminem's) relationship with his estranged mother creates "Cleanin' Out My Closet," possibly the record's most powerful moment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remixes_.26_Mashups">Remixes &amp; Mashups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanin%27_Out_My_Closet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Remixes &amp; Mashups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two remixes of the song feature as European bonus tracks on the <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_from_the_Lab" title="Straight from the Lab">Straight from the Lab</a></i> mixtape later released as a compilation: a <a href="/wiki/Drum_and_bass" title="Drum and bass">Drum and bass</a> remix (which was produced by <a href="/wiki/DJ_Green_Lantern" title="DJ Green Lantern">DJ Green Lantern</a>) and a "Religious remix" entitled "(God is) Cleanin' Out My Closet".</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mashup_(video)" title="Mashup (video)">mashup video</a> "Atomic Closet" (a fusion of Eminem's original "Cleaning Out My Closet" and Blondie's "<a href="/wiki/Atomic_(song)" title="Atomic (song)">Atomic</a>"), Blondie's hit was heavily sampled as the background song, with Eminem rapping throughout. The video was directed by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Atwell" title="Philip Atwell">Philip Atwell</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_side" title="B side">B side</a> "Stimulate" appears on the deluxe edition bonus disc of the soundtrack <i><a href="/wiki/Music_from_and_Inspired_by_the_Motion_Picture_8_Mile" title="Music from and Inspired by the Motion Picture 8 Mile">Music from and Inspired by the Motion Picture 8 Mile</a></i>, and also appears on <i>Straight from the Lab</i>.</p>
<p>The song was mashed up with "<a href="/wiki/Hotel_California_(Eagles_album)" title="Hotel California (Eagles album)">Hotel California</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Eagles_(UK_band)" title="The Eagles (UK band)">The Eagles</a>, which appears on <a href="/wiki/DJ_Vlad" title="DJ Vlad">DJ Vlad</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Roc_Raida" title="Roc Raida">Roc Raida</a>'s mashup mixtape, <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Phenomenon" title="Rock Phenomenon">Rock Phenomenon</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jon_Connor" title="Jon Connor">Jon Connor</a>'s 2012 mixtape <i>The People's Rapper LP</i> included a remix of the song, as well as other Eminem remixes.</p>
