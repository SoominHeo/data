<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Asuka Kiyomihara Code</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Asuka Kiyomihara Code</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">飛鳥浄御原令</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Asuka Kiyomihara-ryō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> refers to a collection of governing rules compiled and promulgated in 689, one of the first, if not the first collection of <a href="/wiki/Ritsury%C5%8D" title="Ritsuryō">Ritsuryō</a> laws in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Japan" title="Classical Japan">classical Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This also marks the initial appearance of the central administrative body called the <i><a href="/wiki/Daij%C5%8D-kan" title="Daijō-kan">Daijō-kan</a></i> (Council of State) composed of the three ministers—the <i><a href="/wiki/Daij%C5%8D-daijin" title="Daijō-daijin">Daijō-daijin</a></i> (Chancellor), the <i><a href="/wiki/Sadaijin" title="Sadaijin">Sadaijin</a></i> (Minister of the Left) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Udaijin" title="Udaijin">Udaijin</a></i> (Minister of the Right).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall232_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hall232-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 662, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Tenji" title="Emperor Tenji">Emperor Tenji</a> is said to have compiled the first Japanese legal code known to modern historians. The <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cmi_Code" title="Ōmi Code">Ōmi-ryō</a>, consisting of 22 volumes, was promulgated in the last year of Tenji's reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This legal codification is no longer extant, but it is said to have been refined in what is known as the Asuka Kiyomihara <i>ritsu-ryō</i> of 689.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall232_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-hall232-2">[2]</a></sup> The compilation was commenced in 681 under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Temmu" title="Emperor Temmu">Emperor Temmu</a>. The Emperor died in 686, but the finalization of the Code took a few more years. It was promulgated in 689. These are understood to have been a forerunner of the <i>Taihō ritsu-ryō</i> of 701.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although not "finalized" (not incorporating a penal code, a <i>ritsu</i>, for instance), the code already incorporated several important regulations (for instance compulsory registration for citizens), which paved the way for the more complete <a href="/wiki/Taih%C5%8D_Code" title="Taihō Code">Taihō Code</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hall232_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-hall232-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
