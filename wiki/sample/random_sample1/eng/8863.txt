<p>The <b>Aster</b> missile series, primarily comprising the <b>Aster 15</b> and <b>Aster 30</b> are a family of vertically launched <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missiles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurosam.com_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-eurosam.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The name "<i>Aster</i>" originates from the mythical Greek archer named <i><a href="/wiki/Asterion" title="Asterion">Asterion</a></i> (in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>), Asterion likewise receiving his name from the ancient Greek word aster (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">ἀστήρ</span>), meaning "star". Aster is manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Eurosam" title="Eurosam">Eurosam</a>, a European consortium consisting of <a href="/wiki/MBDA" title="MBDA">MBDA</a> France, MBDA Italy (combined 66%) and the <a href="/wiki/Thales_Group" title="Thales Group">Thales Group</a> (33%). The missile is designed to intercept and destroy a wide range of air threats, such as <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile#Supersonic" title="Cruise missile">supersonic anti-ship cruise missiles</a> at very low altitude (<a href="/wiki/Sea_skimming" title="Sea skimming">Sea-skimming</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aster_30_hits_sea-skimming_target_at_supersonic_speeds_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aster_30_hits_sea-skimming_target_at_supersonic_speeds-5">[5]</a></sup> and fast flying, high performance <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">aircraft</a> or missiles.</p>
<p>Aster is primarily operated by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and is an integrated component of the <a href="/wiki/PAAMS" title="PAAMS">PAAMS</a> air-defence missile system, known in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> as Sea Viper. As the principal weapon of the PAAMS system, Aster equips the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_45_destroyers" title="Type 45 destroyers">Type 45 destroyers</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizon_class_frigate" title="Horizon class frigate">Horizon class frigates</a>. Aster also equips the French and Italian <a href="/wiki/FREMM_multipurpose_frigate" title="FREMM multipurpose frigate">FREMM multipurpose frigates</a>, though they will not be operating as part of a PAAMS air-defence suite.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 1980s, the predominant missiles in Franco-Italian service were short-range systems such as the French <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crotale_missile" title="Crotale missile">Crotale</a></i>, Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selenia_Aspide" title="Selenia Aspide">Aspide</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Sparrow" title="Sea Sparrow">Sea Sparrow</a>, with ranges up to a dozen kilometres. Some vessels were also equipped with the American medium/long range <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_missile" title="Standard missile">Standard</a>. France and Italy decided to start development of a domestic medium/long range surface-to-air missile to enter service in the first decade of this millennium, that would give them comparable range but superior interception capability to the American <i><a href="/wiki/RIM-66_Standard" title="RIM-66 Standard">Standard</a></i> or British <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Dart_missile" title="Sea Dart missile">Sea Dart</a></i> already in service. Thought was given in particular to the new missile's ability to intercept next-generation supersonic anti-ship missiles, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmos" title="Brahmos">Brahmos</a> missile developed jointly by <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. This allowed the actual systems to have the characteristic of being specialised either in short-to-medium range "point defence" (ships, for instance), or in medium-to-long range "zone defence" (<a href="/wiki/Naval_fleet" title="Naval fleet">fleets</a>).</p>
<p>In May 1989, a <a href="/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding" title="Memorandum of understanding">Memorandum of understanding</a> was signed between France and Italy for the development of a family of future surface-to air-missiles. Eurosam was formed shortly afterwards. By July 1995 development had taken shape in the form of the Aster missile and test firing of the first Aster 30 took place. The missile successfully intercepted a target at an altitude of 15,000 m and at speeds of 1000 km/h. A Phase 2 contract was awarded in 1997 at US$1 billion for pre-production and development of the French-Italian land and naval systems.</p>
<p>During development trials between 1993 and 1994 all flight sequences, altitudes and ranges, were validated. This was also the period during which the launch sequence of Aster 30 was validated. In May 1996, trials of the Aster 15 active electromagnetical final guidance system against live targets began. All six attempts were successful. Again during 1997 Aster was extensively tested, this time being pitted against targets such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospatiale_C.22" title="Aerospatiale C.22">C22 target</a> and first generation <a href="/wiki/Exocet" title="Exocet">Exocet</a> anti-ship missiles. In numerous engagements Aster scored direct impacts on its targets. During one such engagement on 13 November 1997 in a strong <a href="/wiki/Countermeasure" title="Countermeasure">countermeasures environment</a>, the Aster was not armed with its military warhead so that the distance between the Aster and the target could be recorded. The target (a C22) was recovered bearing two strong cuts due to the fins of the Aster missile.</p>
<p>In May 2001 Aster had again successfully completed the "manufacturer's validation firing test" and was deployed for the first time on the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor_technology" title="Nuclear reactor technology">nuclear-powered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_carriers" title="Aircraft carriers">aircraft carriers</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_aircraft_carrier_Charles_de_Gaulle_(R91)" title="French aircraft carrier Charles de Gaulle (R91)">Charles de Gaulle (R91)</a>. Again on the 29 June 2001 achieved a successful interception of a <i>Arabel</i> missile at low altitude in less than five seconds. During the same year a target simulating an aircraft flying at speeds of Mach 1 and at an altitude of 100 meters was intercepted by an Aster 15. The first ever operational firing of the Aster missile took place during October 2002 on board Charles de Gaulle. Finally in November 2003 Eurosam was awarded the 3 billion euro Phase 3 production contract which saw full production commence and exports to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The resulting Aster surface-to-air missile meets inter-service and international requirements, addressing the needs of the land, air and naval forces of France, Italy and the United Kingdom. The decision to base the missile around a common terminal intercept 'dart' to which different sized boosters can be attached has made it modular and extensible.</p>
<p>From 2002 to 2005, the Italian experimental frigate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpino_class_frigate" title="Alpino class frigate"><i>Carabiniere</i> (F581)</a> provided a test bed for live firing trials of the Aster 15 from Sylver A43 launchers with <a href="/wiki/EMPAR" title="EMPAR">EMPAR</a> and SAAM-it systems, and the trials of Aster 30 from Sylver A50 launchers with EMPAR and <a href="/wiki/PAAMS" title="PAAMS">PAAMS</a>(E) systems. As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, France has spent €4.1bn at 2010 prices on 10 SAMP/T launchers, 375 Aster 30 missiles and 200 Aster 15 missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francebudget2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francebudget2012-7">[7]</a></sup> Another 80 Aster 30 and 40 Aster 15 have been bought for France's Horizon-class frigates under a separate programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francebudget2012_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Francebudget2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aster is best described as an anti-missile missile that is capable of intercepting all types of high performance air threats such as; aircraft, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UAV" title="UAV">UAVs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise missiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-ship_missile" title="Anti-ship missile">anti-ship missiles</a> at ranges of up to 120 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBDA_PDF_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-MBDA_PDF-1">[1]</a></sup> There are currently two versions of the Aster missile family, the short-medium range version, Aster 15, and the long range version, Aster 30. The missile bodies are identical; Their difference in range and intercept speed is because Aster 30 uses a much larger booster. Total weights of the Aster 15 and Aster 30 are 310 kg and 450 kg respectively. Aster 15 has a length of 4.2 meters, rising to just under 5 meters for Aster 30. Aster 15 has a diameter of 180mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBDA_PDF_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-MBDA_PDF-1">[1]</a></sup> Given the larger dimensions of the Aster 30, a naval based system requires the longer tubes of the <a href="/wiki/Sylver_Vertical_Launching_System" title="Sylver Vertical Launching System">Sylver</a> A50 or A70 <a href="/wiki/Vertical_launching_system" title="Vertical launching system">vertical launching system</a> (VLS). Additionally the American <a href="/wiki/Mark_41_Vertical_Launching_System" title="Mark 41 Vertical Launching System">Mark 41 Vertical Launching System</a> can accommodate Aster 30.</p>
<p>Aster 30 is capable of reaching speeds of Mach 4.5 while reaching altitudes of 20 km and is capable of performing aerial manoeuvres greater than 60 <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">Gs</a> giving it a very high degree of manoeuvrability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurosam.com_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-eurosam.com-2">[2]</a></sup> This is thanks to the combination of aerodynamic control and the direct thrust vector control called "PIF-PAF".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBDA_PDF_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-MBDA_PDF-1">[1]</a></sup> Thrust vectors are intentionally located at the missile's centre of gravity maximising responsiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurosam.com_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-eurosam.com-2">[2]</a></sup> This system also prevents a rupture of the missile under high-<i>g</i> maneuvers during trajectory corrections, and allows such maneuvers to be performed without losing aerodynamic performances, improving the precision of the impact on target. A standard launch of the Aster can include 90-degree trajectory changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Eurosam describes Aster as a "hit-to-kill Missile Interceptor".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurosam.com_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-eurosam.com-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Aster missile is autonomously guided enabling it to cope with saturated attacks and is equipped with an active RF seeker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBDA_PDF_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-MBDA_PDF-1">[1]</a></sup> The shipboard radar fulfills roles of sentry, meteo, target discrimination, acquisition and chase. When coupled with the advanced <a href="/wiki/PAAMS" title="PAAMS">PAAMS</a> air-defence system utilizing the <a href="/wiki/SAMPSON" title="SAMPSON">SAMPSON</a> and <a href="/wiki/S1850M" title="S1850M">S1850M</a> radars (as found on the British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_45_destroyers" title="Type 45 destroyers">Type 45 destroyers</a>), Aster is capable of simultaneously targeting and engaging multiple targets at once. MBDA states Aster is capable of "Multiple engagement capability with high rate of fire".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBDA_PDF_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-MBDA_PDF-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>Aster 15</b> - Ship point and local area defence</li>
<li><b>Aster 30 Block 0</b> - Ship local and wide area defence</li>
<li><b>Aster 30 Block 1</b> - Ground-based wide area defence capable against 600 km-range ballistic missiles such as <a href="/wiki/R-17_Elbrus" title="R-17 Elbrus">Scud-B</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tran20130512_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tran20130512-9">[9]</a></sup></li>

</ul>
<p>Block 1 is used in the Eurosam SAMP/T system operated by the French Air Force and the Italian Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the development of Block 1NT is being funded by France and Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBDAspec_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-MBDAspec-10">[10]</a></sup> Block 2 would not be delivered until after 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tran20130512_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tran20130512-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2016, the United Kingdom showed interest in acquiring the Block 1NT version for its Type 45 Destroyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_systems">Land systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Land systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3456" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg/220px-Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg/330px-Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg/440px-Salon_du_Bourget_20090619_110.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Aster 30 has been successfully incorporated into a land based <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">SAM</a> system, fulfilling the "<i>Ground-based area defence</i>" mission requirement. It comes in the form of the Eurosam <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SAMP/T_Mamba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SAMP/T Mamba (page does not exist)"><b>SAMP</b>/<b>T</b></a> (<b>S</b>urface-to-<b>A</b>ir <b>M</b>issile <b>P</b>latform/<b>T</b>errain). The system uses a network of sophisticated radars and sensors - including 3D phased array radar - enabling it to be highly effective against all types of air threats. SAMP/T uses an upgraded version of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arabel_(radar)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arabel (radar) (page does not exist)">Arabel</a> long range radar, with improved performance developed under the Aster 30 block 1 upgrade program, in order to extend the system's capability against higher speed targets and higher altitude targets. The system can intercept missiles with a 600 km range (short range ballistic missile targets).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAMP.2FT_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAMP.2FT-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<li>In April 2008, RSS <i>Intrepid</i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formidable_class_frigate" title="Formidable class frigate">Formidable class frigate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Singapore_Navy" title="Republic of Singapore Navy">Republic of Singapore Navy</a>, shot down an aerial drone off the French port of <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a> during a naval exercise. Then again in 2010, a frigate of the same class, RSS <i>Supreme</i> fired an Aster 15 and shot down an aerial drone off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> as part of exercise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIMPAC#RIMPAC_2010" title="RIMPAC">RIMPAC 2010</a>.</li>
<li>Beginning with <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dauntless_(D33)" title="HMS Dauntless (D33)">HMS <i>Dauntless</i></a> in September 2010, all of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s Type 45 destroyers have successfully intercepted Mirach drones with Aster missiles at the <a href="/wiki/Benbecula" title="Benbecula">Benbecula</a> ranges off the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. Mirach is a 13 ft jet which flies at speeds of up to 600 mph (966 km/h) at altitudes as low as 10 ft (3 m) or as high as 14,000 ft (4 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>In December 2011, an Aster 30 missile downed an <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sparrow_(target_missile)" title="Sparrow (target missile)">Black Sparrow</a></i> ballistic missile target, the first time an Aster missile had attempted such an engagement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aster_30_hit_BM_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aster_30_hit_BM-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>In April 2012, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizon_class_frigate" title="Horizon class frigate">Horizon-class</a> frigate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_frigate_Forbin_(D620)" title="French frigate Forbin (D620)"><i>Forbin</i></a>, of the <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a> downed an American <a href="/wiki/GQM-163_Coyote" title="GQM-163 Coyote">GQM-163 Coyote</a> target simulating a <a href="/wiki/Sea_skimming" title="Sea skimming">sea-skimming</a> <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile#Supersonic" title="Cruise missile">supersonic anti-ship cruise missile</a> traveling at Mach 2.5 (3000 km/h) with an altitude of less than 5 metres. It was the first time a <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> missile defence system destroyed a supersonic sea-skimming "missile". The trial was described as a "complex operational scenario".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aster_30_hits_sea-skimming_target_at_supersonic_speeds_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aster_30_hits_sea-skimming_target_at_supersonic_speeds-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aster_(missile_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Aster 30 is <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">anti-ballistic missile</a> capable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aster_30_hit_BM_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aster_30_hit_BM-17">[17]</a></sup> an <b>Aster Block 2 BMD</b> (<b>B</b>allistic <b>M</b>issile <b>D</b>efence) variant of the Aster 30 missile, with hit-to-kill technology and exo-atmospheric capability, is currently being developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janes2016_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janes2016-19">[19]</a></sup> It will have wide-area defence capable of intercepting 3000 km-range <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRBM" title="IRBM">IRBM</a> ballistic missiles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tran20130512_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tran20130512-9">[9]</a></sup> The speed of the missile will be increased from Mach 4.5 to Mach 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It will be developed by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
