<p>The <b>Aztec Empire</b>, or the <b>Triple Alliance</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuatl_language" title="Nahuatl language">Nahuatl</a>: <span lang="nah" xml:lang="nah"><i><b>Ēxcān Tlahtōlōyān</b></i></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA for Nahuatl">[ˈjéːʃkaːn̥ t͡ɬaʔtoːˈlóːjaːn̥]</a>), began as an alliance of three <a href="/wiki/Nahua_peoples" title="Nahua peoples">Nahua</a> "<a href="/wiki/Altepetl" title="Altepetl">altepetl</a>" city-states: <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">Mexico-Tenochtitlan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texcoco_(altepetl)" title="Texcoco (altepetl)">Mexico-Texcoco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tlacopan" title="Tlacopan">Mexico-Tlacopan</a>. These three city-states ruled the area in and around the <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_Mexico" title="Valley of Mexico">Valley of Mexico</a> from 1428 until they were defeated by the combined forces of the Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadores</a></i> and their native allies under <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> in 1521.</p>
<p>The Triple Alliance was formed from the victorious faction in a civil war fought between the city of <a href="/wiki/Azcapotzalco_(altepetl)" title="Azcapotzalco (altepetl)">Azcapotzalco</a> and its former tributary provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite the initial conception of the empire as an alliance of three self-governed city-states, <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">Tenochtitlan</a> quickly became dominant militarily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hassig_1988_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hassig_1988-3">[3]</a></sup> By the time the Spanish arrived in 1519, the lands of the Alliance were effectively ruled from Tenochtitlan, while the other partners in the alliance had taken subsidiary roles.</p>
<p>The alliance waged wars of conquest and expanded rapidly after its formation. At its height, the alliance controlled most of central <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> as well as some more distant territories within <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Soconusco" title="Soconusco">Xoconochco province</a>, an Aztec <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> near the present-day <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemalan</a> border. Aztec rule has been described by scholars as "hegemonic" or "indirect".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2001-4">[4]</a></sup> The Aztec left rulers of conquered cities in power so long as they agreed to pay semi-annual <a href="/wiki/Tribute" title="Tribute">tribute</a> to the Alliance, as well as supply military forces when needed for the Aztec war efforts. In return, the imperial authority offered protection and political stability, and facilitated an integrated economic network of diverse lands and peoples who had significant local autonomy.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">state religion</a> of the empire was <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">polytheistic</a>, worshiping a diverse pantheon that included dozens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deities" title="Deities">deities</a>. Many had officially recognized cults large enough so that the deity was represented in the central temple precinct of the capital Tenochtitlan. The imperial cult, specifically, was that of <a href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">Huitzilopochtli</a>, the distinctive warlike patron god of the Mexica Aztecs. Peoples in conquered provinces were allowed to retain and freely continue their own religious traditions, so long as they added the imperial god Huitzilopochtli to their local pantheons.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "<a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztec</a>" in modern usage would not have been used by the people themselves. It has variously been used to refer to the Triple Alliance empire, the <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">Nahuatl</a>-speaking people of central Mexico prior to the Spanish conquest, or specifically the <a href="/wiki/Mexica" title="Mexica">Mexica</a> ethnicity of the Nahuatl-speaking peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p3-4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p3-4-5">[5]</a></sup> The name comes from a Nahuatl word meaning "people from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztlan" title="Aztlan">Aztlan</a>," reflecting the mythical place of origin for Nahua peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith1984_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith1984-6">[6]</a></sup> For the purpose of this article, "Aztec" refers only to those cities that constituted or were subject to the Triple Alliance. For the broader use of the term, see the article on <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztec civilization</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_the_Aztec_Empire">Before the Aztec Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Before the Aztec Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nahua peoples descended from <a href="/wiki/Chichimeca" title="Chichimeca">Chichimec peoples</a> who migrated to central Mexico from the north in the early 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies1973p3-22_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies1973p3-22-7">[7]</a></sup> According to the pictographic codices in which the Aztecs recorded their history, the place of origin was called <a href="/wiki/Aztl%C3%A1n" title="Aztlán">Aztlán</a>. Early migrants settled the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basin_of_Mexico" title="Basin of Mexico">Basin of Mexico</a> and surrounding lands by establishing a series of independent city-states. These early Nahua cities were ruled by petty kings called <i><a href="/wiki/Tlatoani" title="Tlatoani">tlahtohqueh</a></i> (singular, <i>tlatoāni</i>). Most of the existing settlements, which had been established by other indigenous peoples before the Nahua migration, were assimilated into Nahua culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p37_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p37-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>These early city-states fought various small-scale wars with each other, but due to shifting alliances, no individual city gained dominance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mexica" title="Mexica">Mexica</a> were the last of Aztlan migrants to arrive in Central Mexico. They entered the Basin of Mexico around the year 1250 AD, and by then most of the good agricultural land had already been claimed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies1973_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies1973-10">[10]</a></sup> The Mexica persuaded the king of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culhuac%C3%A1n_(altepetl)" title="Culhuacán (altepetl)">Culhuacan</a> to allow them to settle in a relatively infertile patch of land called <a href="/wiki/Chapultepec" title="Chapultepec">Chapultepec</a> (<i>Chapoltepēc,</i> "in the hill of grasshoppers"). The Mexica served as hired mercenaries for Culhuacan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tezozomocp49-51_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tezozomocp49-51-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After they served Culhuacan in battle, the king appointed one of his daughters to rule over the Mexica. According to mythological native accounts, the Mexica instead sacrificed her by flaying her skin, on the command of their god <a href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">Huitzilopochtli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tezozomocp52-60_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tezozomocp52-60-12">[12]</a></sup> When the king of Culhuacan learned of this, he attacked and used his army to drive the Mexica from Tizaapan by force. The Mexica moved to an island in the middle of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Texcoco" title="Lake Texcoco">Lake Texcoco</a>, where an eagle nested on a <a href="/wiki/Nopal" title="Nopal">nopal</a> cactus. The Mexica interpreted this as a sign from their god and founded their new city, <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">Tenochtitlan</a>, on this island in the year <i>ōme calli,</i> or "Two House" (1325 AD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new Mexica city allied with the city of <a href="/wiki/Azcapotzalco_(altepetl)" title="Azcapotzalco (altepetl)">Azcapotzalco</a> and paid tribute to its king, <a href="/wiki/Tezozomoc_(Azcapotzalco)" title="Tezozomoc (Azcapotzalco)">Tezozomoc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p44_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p44-13">[13]</a></sup> With Mexica assistance, Azcopotzalco began to expand into a small tributary empire. Until this point, the Mexica ruler was not recognized as a legitimate king. Mexica leaders successfully petitioned one of the kings of Culhuacan to provide a daughter to marry into the Mexica line. Their son, <a href="/wiki/Acamapichtli" title="Acamapichtli">Acamapichtli</a>, was enthroned as the first tlatoani of Tenochtitlan in the year 1372.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tezozomoc_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tezozomoc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the Tepanecs of Azcapotzalco expanded their kingdom with help from the Mexica, the <a href="/wiki/Acolhua" title="Acolhua">Acolhua</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Texcoco_(altepetl)" title="Texcoco (altepetl)">Texcoco</a> grew in power in the eastern portion of the lake basin. Eventually, war erupted between the two states, and the Mexica played a vital role in the conquest of Texcoco. By then, Tenochtitlan had grown into a major city and was rewarded for its loyalty to the Tepanec by receiving Texcoco as a tributary province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p46_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p46-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Tepanec_War_and_the_Triple_Alliance">The Tepanec War and the Triple Alliance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Tepanec War and the Triple Alliance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1426, the Tepanec king Tezozomoc died, and the resulting succession crisis precipitated a civil war between potential successors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p46_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p46-15">[15]</a></sup> The Mexica supported Tezozomoc's preferred heir, Tayahauh, who was initially enthroned as king. But his son, Maxtla, soon usurped the throne and turned against factions that opposed him, including the Mexica ruler <a href="/wiki/Chimalpopoca" title="Chimalpopoca">Chimalpopoca</a>. The latter died shortly thereafter, possibly assassinated by Maxtla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies1973_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davies1973-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new Mexica ruler <a href="/wiki/Itzcoatl" title="Itzcoatl">Itzcoatl</a> continued to defy Maxtla; he blockaded Tenochtitlan and demanded increased tribute payments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p47_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p47-16">[16]</a></sup> Maxtla similarly turned against the <a href="/wiki/Acolhua" title="Acolhua">Acolhua</a>, and the king of Texcoco, <a href="/wiki/Nezahualcoyotl" title="Nezahualcoyotl">Nezahualcoyotl</a>, fled into exile. Nezahualcoyotl recruited military help from the king of Huexotzinco, and the Mexica gained the support of a dissident Tepanec city, <a href="/wiki/Tlacopan" title="Tlacopan">Tlacopan</a>. In 1427, Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, Tlacopan, and Huexotzinco went to war against Azcapotzalco, emerging victorious in 1428.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p47_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p47-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, Huexotzinco withdrew, and the three remaining cities formed a treaty known today as the Triple Alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p47_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p47-16">[16]</a></sup> The Tepanec lands were carved up among the three cities, whose leaders agreed to cooperate in future wars of conquest. Land acquired from these conquests was to be held by the three cities together. Tribute was to be divided so that two-fifths each went to Tenochtitlan and Texcoco, and one-fifth went to Tlacopan. Each of the three kings of the alliance in turn assumed the title "huetlatoani" ("Elder Speaker", often translated as "Emperor"). In this role, each temporarily held a <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> position above the rulers of other city-states ("tlatoani").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p460_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p460-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the next 100 years, the Triple Alliance of Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, and Tlacopan came to dominate the Valley of Mexico and extend its power to the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_ocean" title="Pacific ocean">Pacific</a>. Tenochtitlan gradually became the dominant power in the alliance. Two of the primary architects of this alliance were the half-brothers <a href="/wiki/Tlacaelel" title="Tlacaelel">Tlacaelel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moctezuma_I" title="Moctezuma I">Moctezuma</a>, nephews of Itzcoatl. Motecuzoma eventually succeeded Itzcoatl as the Mexica <i>huetlatoani</i> in 1440. Tlacaelel occupied the newly created title of "<a href="/wiki/Cihuacoatl_(position)" title="Cihuacoatl (position)">Cihuacoatl</a>", equivalent to something between "Prime Minister" and "Viceroy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p47_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p47-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_Reforms">Imperial Reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Imperial Reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jaguar_warrior.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="890" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Jaguar_warrior.jpg/220px-Jaguar_warrior.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Jaguar_warrior.jpg/330px-Jaguar_warrior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Jaguar_warrior.jpg/440px-Jaguar_warrior.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jaguar_warrior.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaguar_Warrior" title="Jaguar Warrior">Jaguar Warrior</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Codex_Magliabechiano" title="Codex Magliabechiano">Codex Magliabechiano</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Shortly after the formation of the Triple Alliance, Itzcoatl and Tlacopan instigated sweeping reforms on the Aztec state and <a href="/wiki/Aztec_religion" title="Aztec religion">religion</a>. It has been alleged that Tlacaelel ordered the burning of some or most of the extant Aztec books, claiming that they contained lies and that it was "not wise that all the people should know the paintings".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leon-Portilla1963p155_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leon-Portilla1963p155-19">[19]</a></sup> Even if he did order such book-burnings, it was probably limited primarily to documents containing political propaganda from previous regimes; he thereafter rewrote the history of the Aztecs, naturally placing the Mexica in a more central role.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Moctezuma_I" title="Moctezuma I">Moctezuma I</a> succeeded Itzcoatl as the Mexica emperor, more reforms were instigated to maintain control over conquered cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p48_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p48-20">[20]</a></sup> Uncooperative kings were replaced with puppet rulers loyal to the Mexica. A new imperial tribute system established Mexica tribute collectors that taxed the population directly, bypassing the authority of local dynasties. Nezahualcoyotl also instituted a policy in the Acolhua lands of granting subject kings tributary holdings in lands far from their capitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p462_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p462-21">[21]</a></sup> This was done to create an incentive for cooperation with the empire; if a city's king rebelled, he lost the tribute he received from foreign land. Some rebellious kings were replaced by <i>calpixqueh</i>, or appointed governors rather than dynastic rulers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p462_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p462-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moctezuma issued new laws that further separated nobles from commoners and instituted the death penalty for adultery and other offenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duran1994p209-210_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duran1994p209-210-22">[22]</a></sup> By royal decree, a religiously-supervised school was built in every neighborhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duran1994p209-210_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Duran1994p209-210-22">[22]</a></sup> Commoner neighborhoods had a school called a "telpochcalli" where they received basic religious instruction and military training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p456-457_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p456-457-23">[23]</a></sup> A second, more prestigious type of school called a "calmecac" served to teach the nobility, as well as commoners of high standing seeking to become priests or artisans. Moctezuma also created a new title called "quauhpilli" that could be conferred on commoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p48_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p48-20">[20]</a></sup> This title was a form of non-hereditary lesser nobility awarded for outstanding military or civil service (similar to the English <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>). In some rare cases, commoners that received this title married into royal families and became kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p462_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p462-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>One component of this reform was the creation of an institution of regulated warfare called the <a href="/wiki/Flower_war" title="Flower war">Flower Wars</a>. Mesoamerican warfare overall is characterized by a strong preference for capturing live prisoners as opposed to slaughtering the enemy on the battlefield, which was considered sloppy and gratuitous. The Flower Wars are a potent manifestation of this approach to warfare. These highly ritualized wars ensured a steady, healthy supply of experienced Aztec warriors as well as a steady, healthy supply of captured enemy warriors for <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice_in_Aztec_culture" title="Human sacrifice in Aztec culture">sacrifice</a> to the gods. Flower wars were pre-arranged by officials on both sides and conducted specifically for the purpose of each polity collecting prisoners for sacrifice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p451_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p451-24">[24]</a></sup> According to native historical accounts, these wars were instigated by Tlacaelel as a means of appeasing the gods in response to a massive drought that gripped the Basin of Mexico from 1450-1454.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duran1994_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duran1994-25">[25]</a></sup> The flower wars were mostly waged between the Aztec Empire and the neighboring cities of their arch-enemy <a href="/wiki/Tlaxcala_(Nahua_state)" title="Tlaxcala (Nahua state)">Tlaxcala</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Years_of_Expansion">Early Years of Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early Years of Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aztecexpansion.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1055" data-file-width="1892" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Aztecexpansion.png/450px-Aztecexpansion.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Aztecexpansion.png/675px-Aztecexpansion.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Aztecexpansion.png/900px-Aztecexpansion.png 2x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aztecexpansion.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the expansion of the empire, showing the areas conquered by the Aztec rulers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the defeat of the Tepanecs, Itzcoatl and Nezahualcoyotl rapidly consolidated power in the Basin of Mexico and began to expand beyond its borders. The first targets for imperial expansion were Coyoacan in the Basin of Mexico and Cuauhnahuac and Huaxtepec in the modern Mexican state of <a href="/wiki/Morelos" title="Morelos">Morelos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p47-48_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p47-48-27">[27]</a></sup> These conquests provided the new empire with a large influx of tribute, especially agricultural goods.</p>
<p>On the death of Itzcoatl, Motecuzoma I was enthroned as the new Mexica emperor. The expansion of the empire was briefly halted by a major four-year drought that hit the Basin of Mexico in 1450, and several cities in Morelos had to be re-conquered after the drought subsided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Motecuzoma and Nezahualcoyotl continued to expand the empire east towards the Gulf of Mexico and south into <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a>. In 1468, Motecuzoma I died and was succeeded by his son, <a href="/wiki/Axayacatl" title="Axayacatl">Axayacatl</a>. Most of Axayacatl's thirteen-year-reign was spent consolidating the territory acquired under his predecessor. Motecuzoma and Nezahualcoyotl had expanded rapidly and many provinces rebelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies1973_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davies1973-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the same time as the Aztec Empire was expanding and consolidating power, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pur%C3%A9pecha_Empire" title="Purépecha Empire">Purépecha Empire</a> in West Mexico was similarly expanding. In 1455, the Purépecha under their king Tzitzipandaquare had invaded the Toluca Valley, claiming lands previously conquered by Motecuzoma and Itzcoatl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard1993p169_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard1993p169-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1472, Axayacatl re-conquered the region and successfully defended it from Purépecha attempts to take it back. In 1479, Axayacatl launched a major invasion of the Purépecha Empire with 32,000 Aztec soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard1993p169_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard1993p169-29">[29]</a></sup> The Purépecha met them just across the border with 50,000 soldiers and scored a resounding victory, killing or capturing over 90% of the Aztec army. Axayacatl himself was wounded in the battle, retreated to Tenochtitlan, and never engaged the Purépecha in battle again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p51_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p51-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1472, Nezahualcoyotl died and his son <a href="/wiki/Nezahualpilli" title="Nezahualpilli">Nezahualpilli</a> was enthroned as the new huetlatoani of Texcoco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2008p450_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans2008p450-31">[31]</a></sup> This was followed by the death of Axayacatl in 1481.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p51_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p51-30">[30]</a></sup> Axayacatl was replaced by his brother <a href="/wiki/Tizoc" title="Tizoc">Tizoc</a>. Tizoc's reign was notoriously brief. He proved to be ineffectual and did not significantly expand the empire. Apparently due to his incompetence, Tizoc was likely assassinated by his own nobles five years into his rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p51_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p51-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Years_of_Expansion">Later Years of Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later Years of Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Tizoc was succeeded by his brother <a href="/wiki/Ahuitzotl" title="Ahuitzotl">Ahuitzotl</a> in 1486. Like his predecessors, the first part of Ahuitzotl's reign was spent suppressing rebellions that were commonplace due to the indirect nature of Aztec rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p51_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p51-30">[30]</a></sup> Ahuitzotl then began a new wave of conquests including the <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca_Valley" title="Oaxaca Valley">Oaxaca Valley</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soconusco" title="Soconusco">Soconusco Coast</a>. Due to increased border skirmishes with the Purépechas, Ahuitzotl conquered the border city of Otzoma and turned the city into a military outpost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p54_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p54-32">[32]</a></sup> The population of Otzoma was either killed or dispersed in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard1993p169_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard1993p169-29">[29]</a></sup> The Purépecha subsequently established fortresses nearby to protect against Aztec expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard1993p169_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard1993p169-29">[29]</a></sup> Ahuitzotl responded by expanding further west to the Pacific Coast of <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a>.</p>
<p>By the reign of Ahuitzotl, the Mexica were the largest and most powerful faction in the Aztec Triple Alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Building on the prestige the Mexica had acquired over the course of the conquests, Ahuitzotl began to use the title "huehuetlatoani" ("Eldest Speaker") to distinguish himself from the rulers of Texcoco and Tlacopan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p51_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p51-30">[30]</a></sup> Even though the alliance still technically ran the empire, the Mexica Emperor now assumed nominal if not actual seniority.</p>
<p>Ahuitzotl was succeeded by his nephew <a href="/wiki/Moctezuma_II" title="Moctezuma II">Motecuzoma II</a> in 1502. Motecuzoma II spent most of his reign consolidating power in lands conquered by his predecessors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p54_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p54-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1515, Aztec armies commanded by the Tlaxcalan general Tlahuicole invaded the Purépecha Empire once again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard1993p169-170_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard1993p169-170-34">[34]</a></sup> The Aztec army failed to take any territory and was mostly restricted to raiding. The Purépechas defeated them and the army withdrew.</p>
<p>Motecuzoma II instituted more imperial reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p54_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p54-32">[32]</a></sup> After the death of Nezahualcoyotl, the Mexica Emperors had become the <i>de facto</i> rulers of the alliance. Motecuzoma II used his reign to attempt to consolidate power more closely with the Mexica Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> He removed many of Ahuitzotl's advisors and had several of them executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p54_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p54-32">[32]</a></sup> He also abolished the "quauhpilli" class, destroying the chance for commoners to advance to the nobility. His reform efforts were cut short by the Spanish Conquest in 1519.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_Conquest">Spanish Conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spanish Conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Aztec_Empire" title="Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire">Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

The <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_Mexico" title="Valley of Mexico">Valley of Mexico</a> at the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Conquest_of_Mexico" title="Spanish Conquest of Mexico">Spanish Conquest</a>.</div>

<p>Spanish expedition leader <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> landed in Yucatán in 1519 with approximately 630 men (most armed with only a sword and shield). Cortés had actually been removed as the expedition's commander by the governor of Cuba, <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez_de_Cu%C3%A9llar" title="Diego Velázquez de Cuéllar">Diego Velásquez</a>, but had stolen the boats and left without permission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiazdelCastillo2003pp35-40_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiazdelCastillo2003pp35-40-36">[36]</a></sup> At the island of Cozumel, Cortés encountered a shipwrecked Spaniard named <a href="/wiki/Ger%C3%B3nimo_de_Aguilar" title="Gerónimo de Aguilar">Gerónimo de Aguilar</a> who joined the expedition and translated between Spanish and Mayan. The expedition then sailed west to Campeche, where after a brief battle with the local army, Cortés was able to negotiate peace through his interpreter, Aguilar. The King of Campeche gave Cortés a second translator, a bililngual Nahua-Maya slave woman named <a href="/wiki/La_Malinche" title="La Malinche">Malintzin</a>. Aguilar translated from Spanish to Mayan and Malintzin translated from Mayan to Nahuatl. Once Malinche learned Spanish, she became Cortés's translator for both language and culture, and was a key figure in interactions with <a href="/wiki/Nahua_peoples" title="Nahua peoples">Nahua</a> rulers. An important article, "Rethinking Malinche" by <a href="/wiki/Frances_Karttunen" title="Frances Karttunen">Frances Karttunen</a> examines her role in the conquest and beyond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cortés then sailed from Campeche to <a href="/wiki/Cempoala" title="Cempoala">Cempoala</a>, a tributary province of the Aztec Triple Alliance. Nearby, he founded the town of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> where he met with ambassadors from the reigning Mexica emperor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motecuhzoma_Xocoyotzin" title="Motecuhzoma Xocoyotzin">Motecuzoma II</a>. When the ambassadors returned to Tenochtitlan, Cortés went to Cempoala to meet with the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totonac" title="Totonac">Totonac</a> leaders. After the Totonac ruler told Cortés of his various grievances against the Mexica, Cortés convinced the Totonacs to imprison an imperial tribute collector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiazdelCastillo2003pp92-94_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiazdelCastillo2003pp92-94-38">[38]</a></sup> Cortés subsequently released the tribute collector after persuading him that the move was entirely the Totonac's idea and that he had no knowledge of it. Having effectively declared war on the Aztecs, the Totonacs provided Cortés with 20 companies of soldiers for his march to Tlaxcala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiazdelCastillo2003p120_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiazdelCastillo2003p120-39">[39]</a></sup> At this time several of Cortés's soldiers attempted to mutiny. When Cortés discovered the plot, he had his ships scuttled and sank them in the harbor to remove any possibility of escaping to Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="491" data-file-width="645" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg/220px-Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg/330px-Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg/440px-Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Codex_azcatitlan222.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Codex_Azcatitlan" title="Codex Azcatitlan">Codex Azcatitlan</a> depicting the Spanish army, with Cortez and Malinche in front</div>

<p>The Spanish-led Totonac army crossed into <a href="/wiki/Tlaxcala_(Nahua_state)" title="Tlaxcala (Nahua state)">Tlaxcala</a> to seek the latter's alliance against the Aztecs. However, the Tlaxcalan general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xicotencatl_the_Younger" title="Xicotencatl the Younger">Xicotencatl the Younger</a> believed them to be hostile, and attacked. After fighting several close battles, Cortés eventually convinced the leaders of Tlaxcala to order their general to stand down. Cortés then secured an alliance with the people of Tlaxcala, and traveled from there to the Basin of Mexico with a smaller company of 5,000-6,000 Tlaxcalans and 400 Totonacs, in addition to the Spanish soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup> During his stay in the city of Cholula, Cortés claims he received word of a planned ambush against the Spanish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup> In a pre-emptive response, Cortés directed his troops attack and kill a large number of unarmed Cholulans gathered in the main square of the city.</p>
<p>Following the massacre at Cholula, Hernan Cortés and the other Spaniards entered Tenochtitlan, where they were greeted as guests and given quarters in the palace of former emperor Axayacatl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p275_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p275-41">[41]</a></sup> After staying in the city for six weeks, two Spaniards from the group left behind in Veracruz were killed in an altercation with an Aztec lord named Quetzalpopoca. Cortés claims that he used this incident as an excuse to take Motecuzoma prisoner under threat of force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup> For several months, Motecuzoma continued to run the kingdom as a prisoner of Hernan Cortés. Then, in 1520, a second, larger Spanish expedition arrived under the command of <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nfilo_de_Narv%C3%A1ez" title="Pánfilo de Narváez">Pánfilo de Narváez</a> sent by Diego Velásquez with the goal of arresting Cortés for treason. Before confronting Narváez, Cortés secretly persuaded Narváez's lieutenants to betray him and join Cortés.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Cortés was away from Tenochtitlan dealing with Narváez, his second in command <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Alvarado" title="Pedro de Alvarado">Pedro de Alvarado</a> massacred a group of Aztec nobility in response to a ritual of human sacrifice honoring <a href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">Huitzilopochtli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-5"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup> The Aztecs retaliated by attacking the palace where the Spanish were quartered. Cortés returned to Tenochtitlan and fought his way to the palace. He then took Motecuzoma up to the roof of the palace to ask his subjects to stand down. However, by this point the ruling council of Tenochtitlan had voted to depose Motecuzoma and had elected his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuitlahuac" title="Cuitlahuac">Cuitlahuac</a> as the new emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p275_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p275-41">[41]</a></sup> One of the Aztec soldiers struck Motecuzoma in the head with a sling stone, and he died several days later – although the exact details of his death, particularly who was responsible, are unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p275_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p275-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3359" data-file-width="3845" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg/220px-Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg/330px-Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg/440px-Aztec_Indians_Mexico_Tlaxcalan_Cortez.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Spaniards and their allies, realizing they were vulnerable to the hostile Mexica in Tenochtitlan following Moctezuma's death, attempted to retreat without detection in what is known as the "Sad Night" or <a href="/wiki/La_Noche_Triste" title="La Noche Triste">La Noche Triste</a>. Spaniards and their Indian allies were discovered clandestinely retreating, and then were forced to fight their way out of the city, with heavy loss of life. Some Spaniards lost their lives by drowning, loaded down with gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> They retreated to <a href="/wiki/Tlacopan" title="Tlacopan">Tlacopan</a> (now Tacuba) and made their way to <a href="/wiki/Tlaxcala" title="Tlaxcala">Tlaxcala</a>, where they recovered and prepared for the second, successful assault on Tenochtitlan. After this incident, a <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> <a href="/wiki/Outbreak" title="Outbreak">outbreak</a> hit Tenochtitlan. As the indigenous of the New World had no previous exposure to smallpox, this outbreak alone killed more than 50% of the region's population, including the emperor, Cuitlahuac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2009p279_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2009p279-43">[43]</a></sup> While the new emperor <a href="/wiki/Cuauht%C3%A9moc" title="Cuauhtémoc">Cuauhtémoc</a> dealt with the smallpox outbreak, Cortés raised an army of Tlaxcalans, Texcocans, Totonacs, and others discontent with Aztec rule. With a combined army of up to 100,000 warriors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-6"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup> the overwhelming majority of which were indigenous rather than Spanish, Cortés marched back into the Basin of Mexico. Through numerous subsequent battles and skirmishes, he captured the various indigenous city-states or <a href="/wiki/Altepetl" title="Altepetl">altepetl</a> around the lake shore and surrounding mountains, including the other capitals of the Triple Alliance, Tlacopan and <a href="/wiki/Texcoco_(altepetl)" title="Texcoco (altepetl)">Texcoco</a>. Texcoco in fact had already become firm allies of the Spaniards and the city-state, and subsequently petitioned the Spanish crown for recognition of their services in the conquest, just as Tlaxcala had done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Using boats constructed in Texcoco from parts salvaged from the scuttled ships, Cortés blockaded and laid siege to Tenochtitlan for a period of several months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cort.C3.A9s_40-7"><a href="#cite_note-Cort.C3.A9s-40">[40]</a></sup> Eventually, the Spanish-led army assaulted the city both by boat and using the elevated causeways connecting it to the mainland. Although the attackers took heavy casualties, the Aztecs were ultimately defeated. The city of Tenochtitlan was thoroughly destroyed in the process. Cuauhtémoc was captured as he attempted to flee the city. Cortés kept him prisoner and tortured him for a period of several years before finally executing him in 1525.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Restall2004p148_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Restall2004p148-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Aztec Empire was an example of an <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empire</a> that ruled by indirect means. Like most European empires, it was <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnically</a> very diverse, but unlike most European empires, it was more of a system of tribute than a single unitary of government. In the theoretical framework of imperial systems posited by Alexander J. Motyl<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motyl_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-motyl-46">[46]</a></sup> the Aztec empire was an "informal" empire in that the Alliance did not claim supreme authority over the tributary provinces; it merely expected tributes to be paid. It was also a discontinuous empire because not all dominated territories were connected, for example the southern peripheral zones of <a href="/wiki/Soconusco" title="Soconusco">Xoconochco</a> were not in immediate contact with the center. The hegemonic nature of the Aztec empire can be seen in the fact that generally local rulers were restored to their positions once their city-state was conquered and the Aztecs did not interfere in local affairs as long as the tribute payments were made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the form of government is often referred to as an empire, in fact most areas within the empire were organized as city-states (known as an <i><a href="/wiki/Altepetl" title="Altepetl">altepetl</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">Nahuatl</a>, the language of the Aztecs). These were small polities ruled by a king or <i>tlatoani</i> (literally "speaker", plural <i>tlatoque</i>) from a dynasty. The Early Aztec period was a time of growth and competition among altepeme. Even after the empire was formed (1428) and began its program of expansion through conquest, the altepetl remained the dominant form of organization at the local level. The efficient role of the altepetl as a regional political unit was largely responsible for the success of the empire's hegemonic form of control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>It should be remembered that the term "Aztec empire" is a modern one, not one used by the Aztec themselves. The Aztec realm was at its core composed of three <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">Nahuatl</a>-speaking city states in the densely-populated Valley of Mexico. Over time, asymmetries of power elevated one of those city states, Tenochtitlan, above the other two. The "Triple Alliance" came to establish <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> over much of central Mesoamerica, including areas of great linguistic and cultural diversity. Administration of the empire was performed through largely traditional, indirect means. However, over time something of a nascent <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a> may have been beginning to form insofar as the state organization became increasingly centralized.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_administration">Central administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Central administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tlacochcalcatl.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="459" data-file-width="248" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Tlacochcalcatl.jpg/180px-Tlacochcalcatl.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/20/Tlacochcalcatl.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tlacochcalcatl.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <i><a href="/wiki/Tlacochcalcatl" title="Tlacochcalcatl">tlacochcalcatl</a></i> pictured in the <a href="/wiki/Codex_Mendoza" title="Codex Mendoza">Codex Mendoza</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before the reign of <a href="/wiki/Nezahualcoyotl" title="Nezahualcoyotl">Nezahualcoyotl</a> (1429–1472), the Aztec empire operated as a confederation along traditional <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerican</a> lines. Independent altepetl were led by tlatoani (lit., "speakers"), who supervised village headmen, who in turn supervised groups of households. A typical Mesoamerican confederation placed a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huey_Tlatoani" title="Huey Tlatoani">Huey Tlatoani</a></i> (lit., "great speaker") at the head of several tlatoani. Following Nezahualcoyotl, the Aztec empire followed a somewhat divergent path, with some tlatoani of recently conquered or otherwise subordinated altepetl becoming replaced with <i>calpixque</i> stewards charged with collecting tribute on behalf of the Huetlatoani rather than simply replacing an old tlatoque with new ones from the same set of local nobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yet the Huey tlatoani was not the sole executive. It was the responsibility of the Huey tlatoani to deal with the <i>external</i> issues of empire; the management of tribute, war, diplomacy, and expansion were all under the purview of the Huey tlatoani. It was the role of the <i><a href="/wiki/Cihuacoatl" title="Cihuacoatl">Cihuacoatl</a></i> to govern the city of Tenochtitlan itself. The Cihuacoatl was always a close relative of the Huey tlatoani; <a href="/wiki/Tlacaelel" title="Tlacaelel">Tlacaelel</a>, for example, was the brother of Moctezuma I. Both the title "Cihuacoatl", which means "female snake" (it is the name of a <a href="/wiki/Nahua_peoples" title="Nahua peoples">Nahua</a> deity), and the role of the position, somewhat analogous to a European <a href="/wiki/Viceroy" title="Viceroy">Viceroy</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a>, reflect the <a href="/wiki/Dualism" title="Dualism">dualistic</a> nature of Nahua <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a>. Neither the position of Cihuacoatl nor the position of Huetlatoani were priestly, yet both did have important ritual tasks. Those of the former were associated with the "female" wet season, those of the latter with the "male" dry season. While the position of Cihuacoatl is best attested in Tenochtitlan, it is known that the position also existed the nearby altepetl of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atzcapotzalco" title="Atzcapotzalco">Atzcapotzalco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culhuac%C3%A1n_(altepetl)" title="Culhuacán (altepetl)">Culhuacan</a>, and Tenochtitlan's ally <a href="/wiki/Texcoco_(altepetl)" title="Texcoco (altepetl)">Texcoco</a>. Despite the apparent lesser status of the position, a Cihuacoatl could prove both influential and powerful, as in the case of Tlacaelel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early in the history of the empire, Tenochtitlan developed a four-member military and advisory Council which assisted the Huey tlatoani in his decision-making: the <i>tlacochcalcatl</i>; the <i>tlaccatecatl</i>; the <i>ezhuahuacatl</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BerdanAnawalt1992p196_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-BerdanAnawalt1992p196-52">[52]</a></sup> and the <i>tlillancalqui</i>. This design not only provided advise for the ruler, it also served to contain ambition on the part of the nobility, as henceforth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huey_Tlatoani" title="Huey Tlatoani">Huey Tlatoani</a> could only be selected from the Council. Moreover, the actions of any one member of the Council could easily be blocked by the other three, providing a simple system of checks on the ambition higher officials. These four Council members were also generals, members of various military societies. The ranks of the members were not equal, with the tlacochcalcatl and tlaccatecatl having a higher status than the others. These two Councillors were members of the two most prestigious military societies, the <i>cuauhchique</i> ("shorn ones") and the <i>otontin</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Otomi_(military)" title="Otomi (military)">Otomies</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provincial_administration">Provincial administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Provincial administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, provinces and altepetl were governed by hereditary tlatoani. As the empire grew, the system evolved further and some tlatoani were replaced by other officials. As has already been mentioned, directly-appointed stewards (singular <i>calpixqui</i>, plural <i>calpixque</i>) were sometimes imposed on altepetl instead of the selection of provincial nobility to the same position of tlatoani. At the height of empire, the organization of the state into tributary and strategic provinces saw an elaboration of this system. The 38 tributary provinces fell under the supervision of high stewards, or <i>huecalpixque</i>, whose authority extended over the lower-ranking calpixque. These calpixque and huecalpixque were essentially managers of the provincial tribute system which was overseen and coordinated in the paramount capital of Tenochtitlan not by the <i>huetlatoani</i>, but rather by a separate position altogether: the <i>petlacalcatl</i>. On the occasion that a recently conquered altepetl was seen as particularly restive, a military governor, or <i>cuauhtlatoani</i>, was placed at the head of provincial supervision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> During the reign of Moctezuma I, the calpixque system was elaborated, with two calpixque assigned per tributary province. One was stationed in the province itself, perhaps for supervising the collection of tribute, and the other in Tenochtitlan, perhaps for supervising storage of tribute. Tribute was drawn from commoners, the <i>macehualtin</i>, and distributed to the nobility, be they 'kings' (<i>tlatoque</i>), lesser rulers (<i>teteuctin</i>), or provincial nobility (<i>pipiltin</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tribute collection was supervised by the above officials and relied upon the coercive power of the Aztec military, but also upon the cooperation of the <i>pipiltin</i> (the local nobility who were themselves exempt from and recipient to tribute) and the hereditary class of merchants known as <i>pochteca</i>. These pochteca had various gradations of ranks which granted them certain trading rights and so were not necessarily pipiltin themselves, yet they played an important role in both the growth and administration of the Aztec tributary system nonetheless. The power, political and economic, of the pochteca was strongly tied to the political and military power of the Aztec nobility and state. In addition to serving as diplomats (<i>teucnenenque</i>, or "travelers of the lord") and spies in the prelude to conquest, higher-ranking pochteca also served as judges in market plazas and were to certain degree autonomous <a href="/wiki/Corporate_group_(sociology)" title="Corporate group (sociology)">corporate groups</a>, having administrative duties within their own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_Realm" title="Estates of the Realm">estate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provincial_Structure">Provincial Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Provincial Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2265" data-file-width="3613" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png/220px-Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png/330px-Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png/440px-Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Territorial_Organization_of_the_Aztec_Empire_1519.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Territorial Organization of the Aztec Empire 1519</div>

<p>Originally, the Aztec empire was loose alliance between three cities: <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">Tenochtitlan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texcoco_(altepetl)" title="Texcoco (altepetl)">Texcoco</a>, and the most junior partner, <a href="/wiki/Tlacopan" title="Tlacopan">Tlacopan</a>. As such, they were known as the 'Triple Alliance.' This political form was very common in Mesoamerica, where alliances of city-states were ever fluctuating. However, overtime it was Tenochtitlan which assumed paramount authority in the alliance, and although each partner city shared spoils of war and rights to regular tribute from the provinces and were governed by their own Huetlatoani, it was Tenochtitlan which became the largest, most powerful, and most influential of the three cities. It was the de facto and acknowledged center of empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though they were not described by the Aztec this way, there were essentially two types of provinces: Tributary and Strategic. Strategic provinces were essentially subordinate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Client_states" title="Client states">client states</a> which provided tribute or aid to the Aztec state under "mutual consent". Tributary provinces, on the other hand, provided regular tribute to the empire; obligations on the part of Tributary provinces were mandatory rather than consensual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans470-471_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans470-471-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith1-2_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith1-2-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_and_State">Ideology and State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ideology and State">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Aztec_religion" title="Aztec religion">Aztec religion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tovar_Codex_(folio_134).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="831" data-file-width="1180" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Tovar_Codex_%28folio_134%29.png/270px-Tovar_Codex_%28folio_134%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Tovar_Codex_%28folio_134%29.png/405px-Tovar_Codex_%28folio_134%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Tovar_Codex_%28folio_134%29.png/540px-Tovar_Codex_%28folio_134%29.png 2x" width="270"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tovar_Codex_(folio_134).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This page from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codex_Tovar" title="Codex Tovar">Codex Tovar</a> depicts a scene of <a href="/wiki/Xipe_Totec#Human_sacrifice" title="Xipe Totec">gladiatorial sacrificial</a> rite, celebrated on the festival of <i>Tlacaxipehualiztli</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rulers, be they local teteuctin or tlatoani, or central Huetlatoani, were seen as representatives of the gods and therefore ruled by <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right</a>. <i>Tlatocayotl</i>, or the principle of rulership, established that this divine right was inherited by descent. Political order was therefore also a cosmic order, and to kill a tlatoani was to transgress that order. For that reason, whenever a tlatoani was killed or otherwise removed from their station, a relative and member of the same bloodline was typically placed in their stead. The establishment of the office of Huetlatoani understood through the creation of another level of rulership, <i>hueitlatocayotl</i>, standing in superior contrast to the lesser <i>tlatocayotl</i> principle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Expansion of the empire was guided by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Militaristic" title="Militaristic">militaristic</a> interpretation of Nahua religion, specifically a devout veneration of the sun god, <a href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">Huitzilopochtli</a>. Militaristic state rituals were performed throughout the year according to a ceremonial calendar of events, rites, and mock battles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The time period they lived in was understood as the <i>Ollintonatiuh</i>, or Sun of Movement, which was believed to be the final age after which humanity would be destroyed. It was under Tlacaelel that Huitzilopochtli assumed his elevated role in the state pantheon and who argued that it was through blood sacrifice that the Sun would be maintained and thereby stave off the end of the world. It was under this new, militaristic interpretation of Huitzilopochtli that Aztec soldiers were encouraged to fight wars and capture enemy soldiers for sacrifice. Though blood sacrifice was common in Mesoamerica, the scale of <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">human sacrifice</a> under the Aztecs was likely unprecedented in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aztec_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_(law)" title="Code (law)">code of law</a> seems to have been established under the reign of <a href="/wiki/Moctezuma_I" title="Moctezuma I">Moctezuma I</a>. These laws served to establish and govern relations between the state, classes, and individuals. Punishment was to be meted out solely by state authorities. Nahua mores were enshrined in these laws, criminalizing public acts of homosexuality, drunkenness, and nudity, not to mention more universal proscriptions against theft, murder, and property damage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As stated before, pochteca could serve as judges, often exercising judicial oversight of their own members. Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">military courts</a> dealt with both cases within the military and without during wartime. There was an <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a> process, with appellate courts standing between local, typically market-place courts, on the provincial level and a <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme court</a> and two special higher appellate courts at Tenochtitlan. One of those two special courts dealt with cases arising within Tenochtitlan, the other with cases originating from outside the capital. The ultimate judicial authority laid in hands of the Huetlatoani, who had the right to appoint lesser judges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
