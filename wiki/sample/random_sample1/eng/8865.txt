<p><b>Prince</b> or <b>Princess of Asturias</b> (Spanish: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Príncipe/Princesa de Asturias</i></span>), was the title of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">heir</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Kingdoms of Castile</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Leon" title="Kingdom of Leon">Leon</a> and, after the union of the peninsular Kingdoms, the main one used by the heir of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Spain" title="Kingdom of Spain">Kingdom of Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">Spanish Constitution of 1978</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Article 57 [...]</p>
<p>2. The Crown Prince, from his birth or since the event that gives rise to the appeal, will have the dignity of Prince of Asturias and other titles traditionally linked to the successor of the Crown of Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constitution_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Constitution-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The origin of the title took place in 1388, when King <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Castile" title="John I of Castile">John I of Castile</a> granted the dignity –who also included jurisdiction over the territory–<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez_Gonz.C3.A1lez_2000.2C_p._395_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez_Gonz.C3.A1lez_2000.2C_p._395-3">[3]</a></sup> to his first-born son <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">Henry</a>. In an attempt to ended the dynastic struggle between the heirs of both Kings <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Castile" title="Peter of Castile">Peter I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_Castile" title="Henry II of Castile">Henry II of Castile</a>, was chosen the principality as the higher jurisdictional lordship which the King can grant and that had not been given to nobody before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez394_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez394-4">[4]</a></sup> The custom of granting unique titles to royal heirs had already been in use in the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">Aragon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Girona" title="Prince of Girona">Prince of Girona</a>), <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> (<a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">France</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dauphin_of_France" title="Dauphin of France">Dauphin de Viennois</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas53_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas53-5">[5]</a></sup> The title, therefore, was the object of two uses: generic to name the heir of the Castilian Kingdom and specific to apply it to the prince first in the line of succession when the King transmitted to him effectively the territory of the Principality, with its government and its rents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez394_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez394-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the dynastic union between the Kingdoms of Castile and Aragon under the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, the title was incorporated to the generals of the Hispanic Monarchy, the crown prince accumulating the titles of <i>Prince of Asturias, Girona, Spain and the New World</i>, and being titled already in times of the House of Habsburg <i>Prince of these Kingdoms, Prince of the Spains and the New World</i> ("Príncipe de estos Reynos, príncipe de las Españas y del Nuevo Mundo").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas6162_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas6162-6">[6]</a></sup> From 1705, with the advent of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> on the Spanish throne, the title was further promoted following the decisive help of Castile to the French in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas64_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas64-7">[7]</a></sup> At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/19th_century" title="19th century">19th century</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1812" title="Spanish Constitution of 1812">Spanish Constitution of 1812</a> consecrated the title to the heir of the Crown, but in the following Constitutions, was eliminated the synonymy between the title and the dignity of heir to the Crown;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas6768_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas6768-8">[8]</a></sup> in the successive Constitutions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> was used in the second half of the 19th and in the first half of the <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>, and finally with the restoration of the monarchy (this time <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">parliamentary</a>) in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas73_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas73-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The jurisdictional lordships, forms of government –not of ownership or possession– which were consolidated in the <a href="/wiki/14th_century" title="14th century">14th</a> and <a href="/wiki/15th_century" title="15th century">15th centuries</a>, were subrogations of the royal power for the administration of towns, usually those with geographical or structural difficulties that generated income. From King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso XI of Castile">Alfonso XI</a> the rulers created these lordships to generally give to their allies a proper way to maintained their position and to be able to govern and to administer zones that were impossible to attend by the traditional channels of the monarchy. From its origins, there have been buying and selling operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez_Gonz.C3.A1lez_2000.2C_p._395_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez_Gonz.C3.A1lez_2000.2C_p._395-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins:_Counties_of_Nore.C3.B1a_and_Gij.C3.B3n">Origins: Counties of Noreña and Gijón</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins: Counties of Noreña and Gijón">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="400" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png/250px-Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png/375px-Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castillo_de_Nore%C3%B1a.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Noreña Castle, engraving of 19th century.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The origins of the Principality of Asturias could be traced to the Counties of <a href="/wiki/Nore%C3%B1a" title="Noreña">Noreña</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gij%C3%B3n" title="Gijón">Gijón</a> –located in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Asturias_de_Oviedo" title="Asturias de Oviedo">Asturias de Oviedo</a>–, territories endowed with seignorial jurisdiction that belonged to Rodrigo Álvarez, who was called <i>of the Asturias</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These lordships had an extraordinary singularity: they were territories that in remote times formed the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">Kingdom of Asturias</a>, the one identified with the origins of the monarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Rodrigo died without succession in 1333, he bequeathed his domains to <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_Castile" title="Henry II of Castile">Henry, Count of Trastámara</a> and illegitimate half-brother of King <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Castile" title="Peter of Castile">Peter I</a>, during whose reign a "true civil war" –in words of <a href="/wiki/Luis_Su%C3%A1rez_Fern%C3%A1ndez" title="Luis Suárez Fernández">Luis Suárez Fernández</a>– took place in Asturias de Oviedo because a group of knights settled in small dominions believed that the consolidation of the "states" that were being occupied the Count of Trastámara (in civil war against the King), would affect their power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Henry, once King, ceded the Counties to his illegitimate son Alfonso Enríquez.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> During the reign of his half-brother King <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Castile" title="John I of Castile">John I</a>, the Count of Noreña and Gijón revolted against him several times; for this reason, the King decided to confiscate the Counties and incorporate them to the Crown,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> promising by writing dated 18 July 1383 that always remained as part of the royal demesne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_the_Principality">Creation of the Principality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Creation of the Principality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enrique_III_de_Castilla_(Museo_del_Prado).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2952" data-file-width="1819" height="357" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Enrique_III_de_Castilla_%28Museo_del_Prado%29.jpg/220px-Enrique_III_de_Castilla_%28Museo_del_Prado%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Enrique_III_de_Castilla_%28Museo_del_Prado%29.jpg/330px-Enrique_III_de_Castilla_%28Museo_del_Prado%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Enrique_III_de_Castilla_%28Museo_del_Prado%29.jpg/440px-Enrique_III_de_Castilla_%28Museo_del_Prado%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enrique_III_de_Castilla_(Museo_del_Prado).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Imaginative portrait of King Henry III of Castile, by Calixto Ortega, 1848. He was the first Prince of Asturias, proclaimed in 1388.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas53_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas53-5">[5]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the assassination of <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Castile" title="Peter of Castile">King Peter I</a> in 1369, there began a series of disputes and long rivalries between <a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt" title="John of Gaunt">John, Duke of Lancaster</a> (who claimed the Castilian throne as the husband of <a href="/wiki/Constance_of_Castile,_Duchess_of_Lancaster" title="Constance of Castile, Duchess of Lancaster">Constance</a>, eldest surviving daughter of King Peter I and his mistress <a href="/wiki/Mar%C3%ADa_de_Padilla" title="María de Padilla">María de Padilla</a> but recognized as legitimate and in line of succession by the Cortes of 1362), and the two successive Trastámara claimants, <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_Castile" title="Henry II of Castile">Henry II</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Castile" title="John I of Castile">John I</a>. After two decades of conflicts of varying intensity, the parties arrived at a compromise through means of the marriage between <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">Prince Henry</a> (son and heir of King John I) and <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Lancaster" title="Catherine of Lancaster">Catherine of Lancaster</a> (only surviving child of John and Constance).</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Otrosi pusieron é ordenaron los dichos Rey Don Juan é Duque de Alencastre en sus tratos , que el dicho Infante Don Enrique oviese título de se llamar Príncipe de Asturias , é la dicha Doña Catalina Princesa : é fué ordenado que á dia cierto fuese venida la dicha Doña Catalina en Castilla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 8 July 1388, the Treaty of Bayonne was signed between John of Lancaster and King John I of Castile, establishing the final dynastic reconciliation after the assassination of King Peter I. By this treaty, the Duke of Lancaster and his wife Constance renounced to all their rights over the Castilian throne on behalf of the marriage of their daughter Catherine with the first-born son of King Juan I of Castile, the future Henry III, who was granted as heir the dignity of Prince of Asturias.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas53_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas53-5">[5]</a></sup> The title was granted with a certain ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The premature death of John I and the minority of Henry III prevented the institutional and juridical conformation of the principality, whereas Alfonso Enríquez rebelled again after obtaining his freedom by royal decree. Besieged by the King's troops, he was submitted to the arbitration of King <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI_of_France" title="Charles VI of France">Charles VI of France</a>, who imposed on the count the return of the territories he held in Asturias. The territory was pacified and confirmed his royal status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early days of his creation, the title of Prince of Asturias wasn't just a simple honorific title, as it included the ownership of the territory of <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a>: the Prince ruled it in representation of the King and was able to appoint judges, mayors, etc. King <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Castile" title="John II of Castile">John II</a> by decree dated in <a href="/wiki/Tordesillas" title="Tordesillas">Tordesillas</a> on 3 March 1444 declared the conversion of the Principality in a jurisdictional lordship, linking the cities, towns and places of Asturias de Oviedo with their rents and jurisdictions to the <a href="/wiki/Majorat" title="Majorat">Majorat</a> of the heirs of the Crown<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> –however, this document was in some case disobeyed and ignored by the Asturian towns for being considered against their traditional <a href="/wiki/Fuero" title="Fuero"><i>fueros</i></a>–.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On 31 May of that same year the future <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_Castile" title="Henry IV of Castile">Henry IV</a> tried to make the Majorat effective and remember to Oviedo and the twenty-one principal Asturian villages that rightfully belonged to his lordship although he had not "executed or used [the principality] because of my minority's and the great debates and scandals who taken place in these kingdoms".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the legal conformation, the duality principality-lordship was recovered and would last under the jurisdiction of the Prince until the time of <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> who limited the scope of the title making it merely honorary; this decision was upheld by the members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> until the present day.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">

John was the only son of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> and heir of all his domains during his lifetime.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although all the heirs of the Crown of Castile have traditionally been considered Princes of Asturias, not all had a formal act by which the jurisdictional lordship was granted;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in consequence, strictly, only were Princes of Asturias <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">Henry</a> during 1388–1390, <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_Castile" title="Henry IV of Castile">Enrique</a> during 1444–1453, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Isabella</a> during 1468–1474 and <a href="/wiki/John,_Prince_of_Asturias" title="John, Prince of Asturias">John</a> briefly during 1496–1497.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez395_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez395-25">[25]</a></sup> In the periods in which no Prince was proclaimed, the Principality didn't disappear, but was directly governed by the monarch, to whose treasury were send the jurisdictional rents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez395_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez395-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the Catholic Monarchs began a policy of reintegration of the royal patrimony that gave rise to a long fight with the principality, begun in 1483 and finished in 1490 with the rubric of a concord for which the House of Quiñones handed over to the Crown the districts of <a href="/wiki/Cangas_del_Narcea" title="Cangas del Narcea">Cangas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tineo" title="Tineo">Tineo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Llanes" title="Llanes">Llanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ribadesella" title="Ribadesella">Ribadesella</a> in exchange for five million of <a href="/wiki/Maraved%C3%AD" title="Maravedí">maravedis</a> and the leonese Babias.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1496 it was tried to revitalize the Principality by Real Letter dated 20 May, in which the monarchs, "wishing to observe the ancient custom" of their Kingdoms –in allusion to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">Aragon</a>–, gave to Prince John the rents and jurisdictions of the Asturian places that previously reverted to the Crown, reserving to them the majority of the justice and the condition of not to alienate the patrimony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decadence_under_the_Habsburgs">Decadence under the Habsburgs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decadence under the Habsburgs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With Prince John the title was incorporated to the generals of the Hispanic Monarchy, accumulating him the titles of Prince of Asturias, Girona (1496), Spain and the New World. The imperialist aspirations are observed in the new title of the heir of the Catholic Monarchs: "Prince of these Kingdoms, Prince of the Spains and the New World" (<i>Príncipe de estos Reynos, Príncipe de las Españas y del Nuevo Mundo</i>). The title lives from that moment a time of partial decadence with the establishment of House of Habsburg in the Spanish throne<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas6162_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas6162-6">[6]</a></sup> –for example, <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> was educated to take the functions of Regent during the absences of <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">his father</a> and not like a Prince of Asturias–.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Su.C3.A1rez395_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Su.C3.A1rez395-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/16th_century" title="16th century">16th</a> and <a href="/wiki/17th_century" title="17th century">17th centuries</a> were characterized by various conflicts between the King and the Principality because of the titles and dignities granted and referring to the territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Only during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a> was fixed a proper ceremonial for the Prince's oath as heir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas64_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas64-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absolutism_under_the_Bourbons">Absolutism under the Bourbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Absolutism under the Bourbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> to the Spanish throne after the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> begins a new period for the Principality of Asturias, whose population saw with hope the new dynasty. The new royal house promoted an identification of the Principality with the Spanish heir following the aid given by the Crown of Castile during the contest, and the Principality of Asturias, which until then had been that of the heir of Castile, tends to be considered properly Spanish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas64_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas64-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[a]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century_Constitutions">19th century Constitutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 19th century Constitutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1168" data-file-width="862" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG/250px-Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG/375px-Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG/500px-Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Const._C%C3%A1diz.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of the first edition of the Political Constitution of the Spanish monarchy. Cadiz, 19 March 1812</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Another period for the title was opened at the beginning of the 19th century with the arrival of the constitutional regime. For <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_Arg%C3%BCelles" title="Agustín Argüelles">Agustín Argüelles</a>, an Asturian deputy in the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz_Cortes" title="Cádiz Cortes">Cádiz Cortes</a>, the draft of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1812" title="Spanish Constitution of 1812">Constitution of 1812</a> preserved more by "custom than by utility or precision" the title of Prince of Asturias to the heir of the Crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas67_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas67-30">[29]</a></sup> The commission responsible for the project, equating the Crown Prince with that of Asturias, proposed that the Cortes should recognize him immediately after announcing his birth and that upon reaching the age of 14, the prince swear before the Cortes the defence of the Catholic faith, the preservation of the Constitution and obedience to the King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas67_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas67-30">[29]</a></sup> During the discussion of the project some deputies proposed that the Prince should be renamed <i>of the Spains</i> and not <i>of Asturias</i>, while others wanted that he used the dignity only after his oath and not from his birth. The Asturians Argüelles, Pedro Inguanzo Rivero and Alonso Cañedo Vigil, with opposing ideologies, defended a title of honor or purely nominal, without any royal rights, but consecrated by history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas6768_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas6768-8">[8]</a></sup> The project remained unchanged and was finally approved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[b]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Constitutions of 1837 and 1845 was eliminated the synonymy of the title with the heir of the throne, speaking of "immediate heir to the crown" (Article 20 of the Constitution of 1837) and "immediate successor to the Crown", " immediate successor, "and" first-born son of the King "(articles 39, 47 and 61 of the Constitution of 1845).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> The Royal Decree of 30 May 1850 attributes "to the inmediate successors to the Crown, according to the Constitution of the Monarchy, without distinction of men or women," the title of "Princes of Asturias".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1869" title="Spanish Constitution of 1869">Constitution of 1869</a> was kept the traditional denomination of Prince of Asturias thanks to the influence of the Asturian <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Posada_Herrera" title="José Posada Herrera">José Posada Herrera</a>, although in the following Constitution of 1876 omits the title like those of 1837 and 1845.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> However, the Real Order of 25 March 1875 ratified the doctrine of 1850 by granting <a href="/wiki/Isabella,_Princess_of_Asturias_(1851%E2%80%931931)" title="Isabella, Princess of Asturias (1851–1931)">Infanta María Isabel Francisca de Asís</a> the title of Princess of Asturias.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas70_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas70-36">[34]</a></sup> A new decree dated 1 August 1880, fixed the ceremonial for the presentation of the "Prince or Infanta" that Queen <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_Austria" title="Maria Christina of Austria">Maria Christina of Austria</a> was close to gave birth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas70_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas70-36">[34]</a></sup> a title immediately appealed by a commission of the Provincial Delegation of Oviedo that asked for the return of the title based on the validity of the decree of 1850. This exhibition motivated the publication of the "Decree of ceremonial for the presentation of the royal child", published in the <i>Gazette of Madrid</i> of 1 September 1880, in which the heir was alluded as Prince of Asturias.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas70_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas70-36">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Royal Decree of 22 August 1880 tried to clarify all the confusion and established the titles and honors of the Prince and Infantes. It was preceded by a statement of reasons in which the historian <a href="/wiki/Antonio_C%C3%A1novas_del_Castillo" title="Antonio Cánovas del Castillo">Antonio Cánovas del Castillo</a> –ultimately responsible as head of government–, sought to establish the true profile of the institution, arguing that there was an "unnecessary and inaccurate" confusion between the right of succession and the title of Prince of Asturias, that the Castilian investiture of the Principality of Asturias could not be confused with the succession to the Spanish Crown, reserving the single denomination of "Prince" or "Prince of these Kingdoms", and that the legislators of Cadiz exceeded in his functions, promoting confusion in the constitutional articles. The decree established that the title had not been a creation of the Cortes, but of the King's will, and restored the "secular uses", maintaining the title of Prince for the first-born sons of the monarch, using the denomination of <i>Asturias</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boe1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-boe1-38">[36]</a></sup> The decree stated that any other immediate successor, male or female, had to be granted the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boe1_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-boe1-38">[36]</a></sup> After considerable criticism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup> the new liberal government of <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A1xedes_Mateo_Sagasta" title="Práxedes Mateo Sagasta">Práxedes Mateo Sagasta</a> was limited to restore the doctrine of the decree of 1850, granting the title of Princess of Asturias to <a href="/wiki/Mercedes,_Princess_of_Asturias" title="Mercedes, Princess of Asturias">Infanta Maria de las Mercedes</a> by Royal Decree dated 10 March 1881.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_democracy">Current democracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current democracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the restoration of the monarchy in 1975, the Royal Decree of 21 January 1977, supported by the Provincial Delegation of Oviedo, ordered that the son of King <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos I of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felipe_VI_of_Spain" title="Felipe VI of Spain">Prince Felipe</a>, bears the title of Prince of Asturias, in addition to those traditionally associated with the heir of the throne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup> The process culminated in the promulgation of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">Constitution of 1978</a>, whose article 57 says that the Crown Prince will be Prince of Asturias and could use the rest of titles linked to his person, embodying in a symbolic way the Spanish dynastic union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas73_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas73-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_and_functions">Titles and functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_of_Asturias&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Titles and functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The Prince of Asturias, as <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">Crown prince</a>, is the first in the <a href="/wiki/Line_of_succession_to_the_Spanish_throne" title="Line of succession to the Spanish throne">Line of succession to the Spanish throne</a>, receives the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a> by Royal Decree 1368/1987<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup> and holds the titles inherents to that position, recognized in the article 57.1 of the Constitution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constitution_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Constitution-2">[2]</a></sup> and which symbolize the Spanish dynastic union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas73_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas73-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition to the principal of Prince of Asturias (as heir of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coronas53_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Coronas53-5">[5]</a></sup> the heir also had the following titles:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Girona" title="Prince of Girona">Prince of Girona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Montblanc" title="Duke of Montblanc">Duke of Montblanc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Cervera" title="Count of Cervera">Count of Cervera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Balaguer" title="Lord of Balaguer">Lord of Balaguer</a> as the heir of the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anguera86_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anguera86-44">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> with origins in 1351, 1387, 1353 and 1418, respectively.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Viana" title="Prince of Viana">Prince of Viana</a>, as the heir of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">Kingdom of Navarre</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anguera86_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anguera86-44">[42]</a></sup> with origin in 1424.</li>
</ul>
<p>He also presides over the Princess of Asturias and Princess de Girona Foundations –named Prince of Asturias and Prince of Girona when the heir is male–.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon reaching the age of majority, he must take before the <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">Cortes Generales</a> an oath to faithfully carry out his duties, to keep and enforce the Constitution and laws and to respect the rights of citizens and autonomous communities, as well as fidelity to the King, according to the Article 61 of the Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constitution_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Constitution-2">[2]</a></sup> This procedure was introduced in 1978, since before the promulgation of this Constitution the Princes of Asturias had to receive the oath of allegiance from the Cortes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rtve_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-rtve-48">[46]</a></sup> The current titular of the Principality is <a href="/wiki/Leonor,_Princess_of_Asturias" title="Leonor, Princess of Asturias">Leonor</a>, who took that dignity on 19 June 2014, when her father King <a href="/wiki/Felipe_VI_of_Spain" title="Felipe VI of Spain">Felipe VI</a> ascended to the throne following the abdication of her grandfather King <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rtve_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-rtve-48">[46]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-Lozoya-61"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-Lozoya_61-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-Lozoya_61-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">At the Ceremony of the Val de Lozoya of 25 October 1470, Henry IV annulled the Treaty of the Bulls of Guisando and proclaimed his daughter Joanna as Princess of Asturias. <li id="cite_note-62"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-62">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">After the death of Henry IV on 11 December 1474 began the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Castilian_Succession" title="War of the Castilian Succession">War of the Castilian Succession</a> between Isabella (who proclaimed herself Queen on 13 December) and Joanna (who did the same on 30 May 1475). The dynastic fight was finally settled on 4 September 1479 in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Alc%C3%A1%C3%A7ovas" title="Treaty of Alcáçovas">Treaty of Alcáçovas</a>, leaving Isabella as the legitimate Queen of Castile.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-63"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-63">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Proclaimed Princess of Asturias by a Royal Decree of 13 October 1830 and sworn as the heir to the throne on 20 June 1833.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-64"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-64">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">With the arrival of the Second Spanish Republic in 1931, royal titles and nobiliary titles are abolished in Spain. On 11 June 1933, Prince Alfonso renounced to his dynastic rights in order to contracted a <a href="/wiki/Morganatic_marriage" title="Morganatic marriage">morganatic marriage</a>. The new heir and titular Prince of Asturias should be Infante <a href="/wiki/Infante_Jaime,_Duke_of_Segovia" title="Infante Jaime, Duke of Segovia">Jaime</a>, but the fact that he was <a href="/wiki/Deaf-mute" title="Deaf-mute">deaf-mute</a> led him to be pressured to also resign his rights, which he did on 23 June. The title of Prince of Asturias <i>in pretense</i> then passed on the third of the sons of King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XIII of Spain">Alfonso XIII</a>, Infante <a href="/wiki/Infante_Juan,_Count_of_Barcelona" title="Infante Juan, Count of Barcelona">Juan</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-65"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-65">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">On 15 January 1941, a few weeks before his death (on 28 February 28), the exiled King Alfonso XIII abdicated in his son and heir, Prince Juan, who became in the new King in exile; however, he preferred to use the title <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Barcelona" title="Count of Barcelona">Count of Barcelona</a>, one of the titles of the king of Spain. His first son, Prince <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos</a> would then be recognized as the new Prince of Asturias. He renounced his claim to the throne in favor of his son on 14 May 1977.</span></li>
