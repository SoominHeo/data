<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Assignat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_(1792)_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3769" data-file-width="6169" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_%281792%29_2.jpg/300px-FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_%281792%29_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_%281792%29_2.jpg/450px-FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_%281792%29_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_%281792%29_2.jpg/600px-FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_%281792%29_2.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FRA-A73-R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise-400_livres_(1792)_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Assignat</i> from the 1792 issue: 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_livres" title="French livres">livres</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Assignats were paper money issued by the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(French_Revolution)" title="National Assembly (French Revolution)">National Assembly</a> in France from 1789 to 1796, during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. Backed by the value of properties formerly held by the Catholic Church, the assignats were immediately a source of political controversy. While their proponents, like other eighteenth-century advocates of "land banks," argued that land was a more stable source of value than was gold or silver, the assignats' opponents saw them as based on an illegitimate seizure of property.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_value_of_assignats(1789-1796).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="886" data-file-width="1299" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/The_value_of_assignats%281789-1796%29.png/220px-The_value_of_assignats%281789-1796%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/The_value_of_assignats%281789-1796%29.png/330px-The_value_of_assignats%281789-1796%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/The_value_of_assignats%281789-1796%29.png/440px-The_value_of_assignats%281789-1796%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_value_of_assignats(1789-1796).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The value of Assignats (1789-1796)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Originally meant as <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a>, the assignats were re-defined as <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a> (assignats-monnaie) in April 1790 to address the liquidity crisis provoked by the political, social, and cultural instability of the Revolution. When the cost of reimbursing Old Regime venal office holders for their properties (judgeships, military ranks etc.) added yet more to the Revolution's inherited debts, the National Assembly voted by a narrow margin to issue additional assignats (Sept. 1790).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The properties backing the assignats were renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Biens_nationaux" title="Biens nationaux">biens nationaux</a></i> (“national goods”) and auctioned by district-level authorities. Through the sale of these properties, assignats were used to successfully retire a significant portion of the <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">national debt</a>. However, since these land sales were their original intent, the assignats were issued only in large denominations (50, 100, 200, and 1000 livres) that worked poorly as a daily medium of exchange. Moreover, the National Assembly never mandated that assignats and Old Regime coins (which remained in circulation) had to be exchanged on par. Already in fall 1790, the National Assembly itself was paying a 7.5% commission to exchange large denomination assignats for smaller coins. By the end of 1791, the discount rate was often 20% or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These limits on the bills' practical use, coupled with the organized opposition of counter-revolutionaries, led to their losing value. Patriotic revolutionaries blamed the assignats' depreciation on foreign conspiracies. There was some basis for these suspicions. The British, Belgian, and Swiss counterfeited the currency industrially - according to Dillaye: “Seventeen manufacturing establishments were in full operation in London, with a force of four hundred men devoted to the production of false and forged Assignats.”</p>
<p>After the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">war</a>, the fall of the <a href="/wiki/10_August_(French_Revolution)" title="10 August (French Revolution)">monarchy</a>, and the declaration of a <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">Republic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Convention" title="National Convention">National Convention</a> mandated that bills and coins exchange on par, but this law could never be enforced. Instead, the assignats continued depreciating. Rising prices and food shortages exacerbated public unrest. Bills such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_Price_Act" title="Maximum Price Act">Maximum Price Act</a></i> of 1793 aimed to address this situation. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermidorian_Convention" title="Thermidorian Convention">Thermidorian Convention</a> lifted the Maximum Price Act in the name of "economic freedom" and the assignats lost almost all value over the next year. In 1796, the <a href="/wiki/French_Directory" title="French Directory">Directoire</a> issued <a href="/wiki/Mandats_territoriaux" title="Mandats territoriaux">Mandats</a>, a currency in the form of land warrants to replace the assignats, although these too quickly failed.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="970" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg/200px-Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg/300px-Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg/400px-Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Assignat_de_15_sols.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Assignat</i> of 4 Jan 1792, still bearing Royal markings: 15 <a href="/wiki/French_sol" title="French sol">sols</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a> opposed all forms of fiat currency. By the 1830s-1840s, the assignats and other papers issued during the Revolution had become collectors' items.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Assignat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div style="width: 220px; height: 165px; overflow: hidden;">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ITA-S538-Italian_States_assignat-8_Paoli_(1798).jpg" title="File:ITA-S538-Italian States assignat-8 Paoli (1798).jpg">File:ITA-S538-Italian States assignat-8 Paoli (1798).jpg</a></div>

<p>Between 1798 and 1799, the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">revolutionary French forces</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic_(18th_century)" title="Roman Republic (18th century)">Roman Republic</a>, which also issued <i>assignats</i> (Italian: <i>assegnati</i>). They were issued by the law of 23 <a href="/wiki/Fructidor" title="Fructidor">Fructidor</a> VI (14 Sept 1798). The currency used was <i>paolo</i> or <i>giulio</i>, the older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_States_scudo" title="Papal States scudo">currency of the Papal States</a>. Roman Republic also issued coins denominated in <i>baiocco</i> and <i>scudo</i>.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The term <i>assignat</i> is similar to the Russian word <i>assignatsia</i> which means "banknote". <i>Assignatsionny rubl</i> (<a href="/wiki/Assignation_ruble" title="Assignation ruble">assignation ruble</a>) was used in Russia from 1769 until 1 January 1849. This had no connection to the French Revolution.</p>
