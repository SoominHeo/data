<p>'<b>Asir</b> or '<b>Aseer</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪər/ 'ear' in 'near'">ɪər</span></span>/</a></span></span> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">عسير</span>‎‎ <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">ʿAsīr</span></i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Regions of Saudi Arabia">region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> located in the southwest of the country, named after the tribe of Asir. It has an area of 81,000 km² and an estimated population of 2,190,000 It shares a short border with <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>. Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Abha" title="Abha">Abha</a>. Other towns include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khamis_Mushayt" title="Khamis Mushayt">Khamis Mushayt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qal%27at_Bishah" title="Qal'at Bishah">Qal'at Bishah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bareq" title="Bareq">Bareq</a>. The regional governor is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faisal_bin_Khalid_bin_Abdul_Aziz_Al_Saud" title="Faisal bin Khalid bin Abdul Aziz Al Saud">Faisal bin Khalid</a> (appointed 16 May 2007), a son of <a href="/wiki/Khalid_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Khalid of Saudi Arabia">King Khalid bin Abdulaziz</a>. He replaced his cousin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khalid_Al_Faisal" title="Khalid Al Faisal">Khalid Al Faisal</a> in the post, who was made governor of <a href="/wiki/Makkah_Region" title="Makkah Region">Makkah Region</a> on the same day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%27Asir_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Asir_Mountains" title="Asir Mountains">Asir Mountains</a></div>
<p>Geographically, the 'Asir Region is situated on a high plateau that receives more rainfall than the rest of the country and contains the country's highest peaks, which rise to almost 3,000 metres at Jebel Sawdah near Abha. Though data is exceedingly sparse and unreliable, the average annual rainfall in the highlands probably ranges from 300 to 500 millimetres (12 to 20 inches) falling in two rainy seasons, the chief one being in March and April with some rain in the summer. Temperatures are very extreme, with diurnal temperature ranges in the highlands the greatest in the world. It is common<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What time of year? Summer, winter, spring, fall? (September 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for afternoon temperatures to be over 30 °C (85 °F) yet mornings can be extremely frosty and <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> can cut visibility to near zero percent. As a result, there is much more natural vegetation in 'Asir than in any other part of Saudi Arabia, with sheltered areas even containing areas of dense coniferous forests, though more exposed ridges still are very dry. 'Asir is home to many farmers who chiefly grow wheat and fruit crops, though irrigation has greatly expanded production in modern times.</p>

<p>The population belongs almost entirely to ancient tribes native to the area such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%27Asir_(tribe)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="'Asir (tribe) (page does not exist)">'Asir</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qahtan" title="Qahtan">Qahtan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bariq" title="Bariq">Bariq</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bal-Garn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bal-Garn (page does not exist)">Bal-Garn</a>, Elyan, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Khatha%27m&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Khatha'm (page does not exist)">Khatha'm</a>, Bayafandi, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rijal_Alma_Asir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rijal Alma Asir (page does not exist)">Rijal Alma Asir</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rijal_Al-Hajr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rijal Al-Hajr (page does not exist)">Rijal Al-Hajr</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Amro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Amro (page does not exist)">Amro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bani_Shehr" title="Bani Shehr">Shehr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shahran" title="Shahran">Shahran</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bal-Asmar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bal-Asmar (page does not exist)">Bal-Asmar</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bal-Ahmar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bal-Ahmar (page does not exist)">Bal-Ahmar</a>, (or 'Asaryah), after whom the region was named. Their religion is a majority <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> rite in the main, but also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shafii" title="Shafii">Shafii</a> rite. <a href="/wiki/Zaidiyyah" title="Zaidiyyah">Zaidi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismaili" title="Ismaili">Ismaili</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shia</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a> form the large non-Sunni minority in Asir, becoming the majority in the south bordering on Yemen, but thinning out towards the north.</p>
<p>The inhabitants speak a conservative group of <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> dialects. Most tribes in 'Asir are divided into three sub-parts based on their geographical location. The tribes of the central highlands are called <i>sarat</i>, the tribes of the western coastal plain are called <i>tihama</i>, and the tribes of the eastern desert region of 'Asir are referred to as <i>badiyah</i>. Thus many tribes are divided into <i>sarat</i>, <i>tihama</i>, and <i>badiyah</i> components.</p>
<p>The region's culture shares much in common with neighboring Yemen in its architectural style, which is adapted to cope with the occasional heavy downpours by deflecting them from houses, but "[t]he ways of the mountaineers tend towards those of <a href="/wiki/Najd" title="Najd">Najd</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-1">[1]</a></sup> 'Asir has been much less affected by the <a href="/wiki/Oil_boom" title="Oil boom">oil boom</a> than has the rest of Saudi Arabia, and the modern urban development of cities like <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a> is largely absent except to some extent in Abha, which serves as a resort town for many Saudis during the extremely hot summers. Tribal laws and customs are still exceedingly strong among the peoples of this region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%27Asir_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_Islam">Before Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%27Asir_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Before Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 25 B.C. <a href="/wiki/Aelius_Gallus" title="Aelius Gallus">Aelius Gallus</a> marched his legions south from Egypt on a 1,300-mile expedition to take control of the ancient overland trade routes between the Mediterranean and what is now <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadhramaut" title="Hadhramaut">Hadhramaut</a>. The Romans wanted control of those routes because they were desperate for money and hoped to raise some by capturing Marib, capital of Yemen, and taking control of the trade in incense - then a priceless commodity - and other valuable aromatics. As it turned out, however, the expedition was a disaster and little information about 'Asir emerged.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saudi_Rule">Saudi Rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%27Asir_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Saudi Rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1920, however, 'Abd al-'Aziz, founder of <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, had begun to recoup the losses of the House of Sa'ud and to unify most of the Peninsula under his rule. As part of this campaign, he sent his Bedouin warriors also known as the <a href="/wiki/Ikhwan" title="Ikhwan">Ikhwan</a> to occupy 'Asir, and from then on 'Asir was controlled by the House of Sa'ud - a situation formalized in 1934 with the signing of the Treaty of Taif between Saudi Arabia and Yemen.</p>
<p>Even then the region was still largely unknown to the West. In 1932, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._St._John_Philby" title="H. St. John Philby">H. St. John Philby</a>, one of the first Europeans to explore and map the Peninsula, did enter 'Asir, but as he did not publish his observations until 1952, the area remained one of the blank spots on the world's map.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%27Asir_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, 'Asir was known for producing <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senna_(plant)" title="Senna (plant)">senna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frankincense" title="Frankincense">frankincense</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero83_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero83-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero84_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero84-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero86_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero86-4">[4]</a></sup> Wheat was grown in the summer and <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a> has been grown in wetter areas of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero84_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero84-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero85_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero85-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">Straw</a> was used to make mats, hats, and <a href="/wiki/Basket" title="Basket">baskets</a>, with specific skills being credited to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somali_people" title="Somali people">Somali</a> women in the area. Tribes in the area also weaved tents from straw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero99_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero99-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
