<p><b>Hagia Irene</b> or <b>Hagia Eirene</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἁγία Εἰρήνη</span>, <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Greek" title="Medieval Greek">Byzantine</a> <small>Greek pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">[aˈʝia iˈrini]</a></span>, "Holy Peace", <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Aya İrini</i></span>), sometimes known also as <b>Saint Irene</b>, is an Greek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a> located in the outer courtyard of <a href="/wiki/Topkap%C4%B1_Palace" title="Topkapı Palace">Topkapı Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. The Hagia Irene today operates as a museum and concert hall.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hagia_Irene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hagia_Irene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The church was dedicated by Constantine to the peace of God, and is one of the three shrines which the Emperor devoted to God's attributes, together with <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> (Wisdom) and Hagia Dynamis (Power).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ja106_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ja106-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Church">Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hagia_Irene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The building reputedly stands on the site of a pre-Christian temple. It ranks, in fact, as the first church built in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a> commissioned the first Hagia Irene [Reference?] church in the 4th century. It served as the church of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarchate of Constantinople">Patriarchate</a> before <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> was completed in 360. From May to July 381 the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Constantinople" title="First Council of Constantinople">First Council of Constantinople</a> took place in the church. It was burned down during the <a href="/wiki/Nika_riots" title="Nika riots">Nika revolt</a> in 532.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> had the church restored in 548.</p>
<p>Heavily damaged by an earthquake in the 8th century, it dates in its present form largely from the repairs made at that time. The Emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantine_V" title="Constantine V">Constantine V</a> ordered the restorations and had its interior decorated with <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a>. Hagia Irene is the only example of a <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> church in the city which retains its original <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(architecture)" title="Atrium (architecture)">atrium</a>. A great <a href="/wiki/Cross" title="Cross">cross</a> in the half-dome above the main <a href="/wiki/Narthex" title="Narthex">narthex</a>, where the image of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pantocrator" title="Pantocrator">Pantocrator</a> or <a href="/wiki/Theotokos" title="Theotokos">Theotokos</a> was usually placed in Byzantine tradition, is a unique vestige of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Iconoclasm" title="Byzantine Iconoclasm">Iconoclastic</a> art; presumably it replaced earlier decoration. The church was enlarged during the 11th and 12th centuries.</p>
<p>The church measures 100 m × 32 m. It has the typical form of a Roman <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>, consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Aisle" title="Aisle">aisles</a>, divided by columns and pillars. It comprises a main space, a <a href="/wiki/Narthex" title="Narthex">narthex</a>, galleries and an atrium. The dome is 15m wide and 35m high and has twenty windows.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arsenal">Arsenal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hagia_Irene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arsenal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">conquest of Constantinople</a> in 1453 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mehmed_II" title="Mehmed II">Mehmed II</a>, the church was enclosed inside the walls of the Topkapi palace. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Janissary" title="Janissary">Janissaries</a> used the church as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armory_(military)" title="Armory (military)">armoury</a>. It was also used as a warehouse for war booty. During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmet_III" title="Ahmet III">Ahmet III</a> (1703–1730) it was converted into a weapons museum.</p>
<p>In 1846, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Marshal" title="Field Marshal">Marshal</a> of the Imperial Arsenal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmed_Fethi_Pa%C5%9Fa" title="Ahmed Fethi Paşa">Ahmed Fethi Paşa</a>, made the church into a military antiques museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was used as the Military Museum from 1908 until 1978 when it was turned over to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkish_Ministry_of_Culture" title="Turkish Ministry of Culture">Turkish Ministry of Culture</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concert_hall">Concert hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hagia_Irene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concert hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, the Hagia Irene serves mainly as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_hall" title="Concert hall">concert hall</a> for <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a> performances, due to its extraordinary <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustic</a> characteristics and impressive atmosphere. Many of the concerts of the <a href="/wiki/Istanbul_International_Music_Festival" title="Istanbul International Music Festival">Istanbul International Music Festival</a> have been held here every summer since 1980.</p>
<p>In 2000, the Turkish <a href="/wiki/Haute_couture" title="Haute couture">haute couture</a> designer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Faruk_Sara%C3%A7&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Faruk Saraç (page does not exist)">Faruk Saraç</a> produced a special show here. A collection of 700 designed pieces inspired by the Ottoman sultans, including the robes of 36 sultans ranging from <a href="/wiki/Osman_I" title="Osman I">Osman Gazi</a>, the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> to the last sultan, <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_VI" title="Mehmed VI">Mehmed VI</a>, were on display. The show was accompanied by music and the story of the sultans' lives and demonstrations of Ottoman-era dancing.</p>
<p>For many years, the Hagia Irene was only accessible during events or by special permission, but the museum has been open to the public every day except Tuesday since January 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
