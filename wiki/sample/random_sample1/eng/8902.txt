<p><b>Yu Aoi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">蒼井 優</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Aoi Yū</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, born August 17, 1985 in <a href="/wiki/Kasuga,_Fukuoka" title="Kasuga, Fukuoka">Kasuga, Fukuoka</a>)</span> is a Japanese actress and model. She made her film debut as Shiori Tsuda in <a href="/wiki/Shunji_Iwai" title="Shunji Iwai">Shunji Iwai</a>'s 2001 film <i><a href="/wiki/All_About_Lily_Chou-Chou" title="All About Lily Chou-Chou">All About Lily Chou-Chou</a></i>. She subsequently portrayed Tetsuko Arisugawa in <i><a href="/wiki/Hana_and_Alice" title="Hana and Alice">Hana and Alice</a></i> (2004), also directed by Iwai, Kimiko Tanigawa in the hula dancing film <i><a href="/wiki/Hula_Girls" title="Hula Girls">Hula Girls</a></i> and Hagumi Hanamoto in the 2006 live-action adaptation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Honey_and_Clover" title="Honey and Clover">Honey and Clover</a></i> manga series.</p>
<p>She has won numerous awards for her performances on screen, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Academy_Prize_(film)" title="Japan Academy Prize (film)">Japan Academy Prize</a> and Kinema Junpo Awards for best supporting actress in 2007 for <i><a href="/wiki/Hula_Girls" title="Hula Girls">Hula Girls</a></i> and Rookie of the Year for continued performances in the field of <i>Films in Media and Fine Arts</i> by the Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology of Japan in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y%C5%AB_Aoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y%C5%AB_Aoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yu Aoi made her stage debut as Polly in the 1999 rendition of <i><a href="/wiki/Annie_(musical)" title="Annie (musical)">Annie</a></i>, followed by her appearance as a regular on TV Tokyo's <i><a href="/wiki/Oha_Suta" title="Oha Suta">Oha Suta</a></i> (The Super Kids Station) in 2000. A year later, she debuted in Shunji Iwai's <i>All About Lily Chou-Chou</i> playing Shiori Tsuda alongside <a href="/wiki/Hayato_Ichihara" title="Hayato Ichihara">Hayato Ichihara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shugo_Oshinari" title="Shugo Oshinari">Shugo Oshinari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miwako_Ichikawa" title="Miwako Ichikawa">Miwako Ichikawa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ayumi_Ito" title="Ayumi Ito">Ayumi Ito</a>. Aoi would later work in <i>Ao to Shiro de Mizuiro</i> and <i>Gaichu</i> with friend <a href="/wiki/Aoi_Miyazaki" title="Aoi Miyazaki">Aoi Miyazaki</a>. With her first roles on the small and big screen came TV commercials and endorsements for <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamaha" title="Yamaha">Yamaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/NTT_DoCoMo" title="NTT DoCoMo">DoCoMo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coca_Cola" title="Coca Cola">Coca Cola</a>.</p>
<p>In 2003, commemorating the 30th anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Kit_Kat" title="Kit Kat">Kit Kat</a> in Japan, Shunji Iwai shot a series of short films starring Yu Aoi and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Suzuki" title="Anne Suzuki">Anne Suzuki</a>, which later was expanded into the feature film called <i>Hana &amp; Alice</i>, which earned Aoi the Best Actress award at the Japanese Professional Movie Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005.E2.80.932007">2005–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y%C5%AB_Aoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2005–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Aoi played her first lead on the big screen in <i>Letters from Kanai Nirai</i>, which was sold in Korea with the alternate title of <i>Aoi Yu's Letter</i> due to her popularity. She also had supporting roles in the Satoshi Miki film <i><a href="/wiki/Turtles_Are_Surprisingly_Fast_Swimmers" title="Turtles Are Surprisingly Fast Swimmers">Turtles Swim Faster than Expected</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Juri_Ueno" title="Juri Ueno">Juri Ueno</a>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otoko-tachi_no_Yamato" title="Otoko-tachi no Yamato">Yamato</a></i> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shido_Nakamura" title="Shido Nakamura">Shido Nakamura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenichi_Matsuyama" title="Kenichi Matsuyama">Kenichi Matsuyama</a>. This supporting role would earn her one of her double-nomination as Best Supporting Actress at the 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Academy_Prize_(film)" title="Japan Academy Prize (film)">Japanese Academy Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She won against herself for her work as Kimiko Tanikawa in the Japanese hit Hula Girls, which was sent to the Academy Awards as the Japanese official selection that year.</p>
<p>To this date, her role as the hula dancing girl from small town Iwaki remains her most successful role yet, earning her a dozen awards as Best Actress and Best Supporting Actress,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> alongside her other smaller roles that year as Hagu in Honey &amp; Clover, and Kana Sato in the Shunji-Iwai-produced and Nirai-Kanai-directed <i><a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Song" title="Rainbow Song">Rainbow Song</a></i>. Aoi also lent her voice to play Shiro in the animated film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tekkon_Kinkreet" title="Tekkon Kinkreet">Tekkon Kinkreet</a></i>, the adaptation to the <a href="/wiki/Taiy%C5%8D_Matsumoto" title="Taiyō Matsumoto">Taiyō Matsumoto</a> manga, Black and White, directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Arias" title="Michael Arias">Michael Arias</a>.</p>
<p>During these years, she made commercials for <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_(company)" title="Canon (company)">Canon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shiseido" title="Shiseido">Shiseido Cosmetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shueisha" title="Shueisha">Shueisha Publishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirin_Brewery_Company" title="Kirin Brewery Company">Kirin</a> Beverage and continued endorsing DoCoMo. Aoi also released two photobooks with <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Takahashi" title="Yoko Takahashi">Yoko Takahashi</a> as <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographer</a>, and distributed by Rockin'on: <i>Travel Sand</i> in 2005 and <i>Dandelion</i> in 2007.</p>
<p>In 2007, she participated in the live-action adaptation of the manga series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bugmaster" title="Bugmaster">Mushishi</a></i> alongside <a href="/wiki/Joe_Odagiri" title="Joe Odagiri">Joe Odagiri</a>, as well as WOWOW's <i>Don't Laugh at My Romance</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_the_Quiet_Room" title="Welcome to the Quiet Room">Welcome to the Quiet Room</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Yuki_Uchida" title="Yuki Uchida">Yuki Uchida</a>, and going back to the stage to play <a href="/wiki/Desdemona" title="Desdemona">Desdemona</a> in a rendition of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Othello" title="Othello">Othello</a></i>. For these last two roles, Aoi lost 7 kg for her role as eating disorder patient, Miki.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.93present">2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y%C5%AB_Aoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Aoi began 2008 with the release of <i>Don't Laugh at My Romance</i>, which earned her a nomination as Best Supporting Actress at the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Film_Awards" title="Asian Film Awards">Asian Film Awards</a> 2009. She appeared in the experimental drama <i>Camouflage</i> (aka. Aoi Yu x 4 Lies), in which she collaborated with four different directors exploring the theme of lies. The series lasted for 12 episodes, and included work with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ry%C5%8D_Kase" title="Ryō Kase">Ryō Kase</a>, Yoichi Nukumizu, Shoko Ikezu, <a href="/wiki/Nobuhiro_Yamashita" title="Nobuhiro Yamashita">Nobuhiro Yamashita</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yuki_Tanada" title="Yuki Tanada">Yuki Tanada</a>.</p>
<p>A couple of months later, NTV signed Aoi to play her first TV leading role as Handa Sen in the live-action adaptation of Shota Kikuchi's manga series <i>Osen</i>, which aired until the end of June with ten episodes.</p>
<p>Next, Aoi released <i>One Million Yen Girl</i> written and directed by <i>Camouflage</i> director Yuki Tanada, and also released by WOWOW. This was her latest leading film role since Nirai Kanai in 2005. She briefly participated in the Japanese World-War-II-jury-themed film <i><a href="/wiki/Best_Wishes_for_Tomorrow" title="Best Wishes for Tomorrow">Best Wishes for Tomorrow</a></i>, as well as the international <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_!_(film)" title="Tokyo ! (film)">Tokyo!</a></i> - a three-short-film collection by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Gondry" title="Michel Gondry">Michel Gondry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leos_Carax" title="Leos Carax">Leos Carax</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bong_Joon_Ho" title="Bong Joon Ho">Bong Joon Ho</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009, The Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology of Japan named Yu Aoi Rookie of the Year in the field of Films in Media and Fine Arts, citing her work in her film debut in <i>All About Lily Chou Chou</i>, until her work in <i>One Million Yen Girl</i>.</p>
<p>In June 2009, the film <i>Ikechan and Me</i>, a live-action adaptation of the picture book of the same name by Rieko Saibara, was released. In it she lends her voice to Ikechan, as well as playing supporting roles in <i>Honokaa Boy</i> and <a href="/wiki/Yoji_Yamada" title="Yoji Yamada">Yoji Yamada</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ot%C5%8Dto_(2010_film)" title="Otōto (2010 film)">Ototo</a></i>.</p>
<p>She starred in <a href="/wiki/Ry%C5%ABichi_Hiroki" title="Ryūichi Hiroki">Ryūichi Hiroki</a>'s 2010 film <i>The Lightning Tree</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> She has also appeared in the films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Vampire_(2011_film)" title="Vampire (2011 film)">Vampire</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rurouni_Kenshin_(2012_film)" title="Rurouni Kenshin (2012 film)">Rurouni Kenshin</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>She appeared in <a href="/wiki/Kiyoshi_Kurosawa" title="Kiyoshi Kurosawa">Kiyoshi Kurosawa</a>'s 2012 television drama <i><a href="/wiki/Penance_(TV_series)" title="Penance (TV series)">Penance</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Y%C5%AB_Aoi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aoi has won numerous awards, including the prestigious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Academy_Prize_(film)" title="Japan Academy Prize (film)">Japan Academy Prize</a> for best supporting actress in 2007, for her portrayal of Kimiko Tanigawa in <i><a href="/wiki/Hula_Girls" title="Hula Girls">Hula Girls</a></i>, for which she was critically acclaimed.</p>
<p>During the same year, she also received both the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ribbon_Awards" title="Blue Ribbon Awards">Blue Ribbon Award</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yokohama_Film_Festival" title="Yokohama Film Festival">Yokohama Film Festival</a> award for best actress, both for her performance in <i>Hula Girls</i> and her portrayal of Hagumi Hanamoto in the <i><a href="/wiki/Honey_and_Clover" title="Honey and Clover">Honey and Clover</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">live action</a> movie. She also received the <a href="/wiki/Hochi_Film_Award" title="Hochi Film Award">Hochi Film Award</a>, the Kinema Junpo Award and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainichi_Film_Concours" title="Mainichi Film Concours">Mainichi Film Concours</a> for best supporting actress for her performances in <i>Hula Girls</i>, <i>Honey and Clover</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Song" title="Rainbow Song">Rainbow Song</a></i>. She also received the <a href="/wiki/Nikkan_Sports_Film_Award" title="Nikkan Sports Film Award">Nikkan Sports Film Award</a> for best new talent for her performance in <i>Hula Girls</i>.</p>
<p>Earlier, in 2005, she had also won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Professional_Movie_Award" title="Japanese Professional Movie Award">Japanese Professional Movie Award</a> for Best Actress for her portrayal of Tetsuko (Alice) Arisugawa in <i><a href="/wiki/Hana_and_Alice" title="Hana and Alice">Hana and Alice</a></i>.</p>
