<p><b>Kazuo Aoki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">青木 一男</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Aoki Kazuo</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, November 28, 1889 – June 25, 1982)</span>, was a bureaucrat and cabinet minister in the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a>, serving as <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(Japan)" title="Minister of Finance (Japan)">Minister of Finance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Greater_East_Asia" title="Ministry of Greater East Asia">Minister of Greater East Asia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kazuo_Aoki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aoki was born to a farming family in <a href="/wiki/Sarashina_District,_Nagano" title="Sarashina District, Nagano">Sarashina District</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagano_prefecture" title="Nagano prefecture">Nagano prefecture</a> (now part of the city of <a href="/wiki/Nagano,_Nagano" title="Nagano, Nagano">Nagano</a>), and was trained as a lawyer, graduating from the Law School of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_Imperial_University" title="Tokyo Imperial University">Tokyo Imperial University</a> in 1916. On graduation, he entered the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Finance (Japan)">Ministry of Finance</a>. Rising rapidly through the ranks, Aoki became chief of the Financial Bureau under <a href="/wiki/Takahashi_Korekiyo" title="Takahashi Korekiyo">Takahashi Korekiyo</a>, who made use of his legal background to have Aoki draft a Foreign Exchange Management Act, which was passed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diet_of_Japan" title="Diet of Japan">Diet of Japan</a> in 1933. Up until that time, Japan had not attempted to implement comprehensive state control over <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">foreign exchange</a>. Aoki followed up on this law with the Rice Control Act (1933) and the Petroleum Control Act (1934), which set the stage for increasing state control over strategic sectors of the economy. He was also on the committee which drafted the <a href="/wiki/National_Mobilization_Law" title="National Mobilization Law">National Service Draft Ordinance</a>, which placed the Japanese economy on a <a href="/wiki/War_economy" title="War economy">war economy</a> footing after the start of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a>. <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Fumimaro_Konoe" title="Fumimaro Konoe">Fumimaro Konoe</a> asked Aoki to become deputy director of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cabinet_Planning_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cabinet Planning Board (page does not exist)">Cabinet Planning Board</a> in 1937, and he became its chairman in 1939. The same year, Aoki was nominated to a seat in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Peers_(Japan)" title="House of Peers (Japan)">Upper House</a> in the Diet.</p>
<p>Under Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Nobuyuki_Abe" title="Nobuyuki Abe">Abe</a>, Aoki was <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(Japan)" title="Minister of Finance (Japan)">Minister of Finance</a> in 1939, while retaining his post as chairman of the Cabinet Planning Board. After the fall of the Abe administration, Aoki was assigned as a special envoy to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reorganized_National_Government_of_China" title="Reorganized National Government of China">Reorganized National Government of China</a> to guide economic policy. He was recalled to Japan under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hideki_T%C5%8Dj%C5%8D" title="Hideki Tōjō">Tōjō administration</a> to the newly created cabinet post of <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Greater_East_Asia" title="Ministry of Greater East Asia">Minister of Greater East Asia</a> In November 1942, in which position he oversaw the <a href="/wiki/Greater_East_Asia_Conference" title="Greater East Asia Conference">Greater East Asia Conference</a>.</p>
<p>Aoki visited Japanese-occupied <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Batavia</a> in May 1943, meeting with <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Hatta" title="Mohammad Hatta">Mohammad Hatta</a>, who as representative for the Indonesian nationalists, advised him that unless there was a shift in Japanese policy towards granting independence for Indonesia (as it had for <a href="/wiki/State_of_Burma" title="State of Burma">Burma</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Philippine_Republic" title="Second Philippine Republic">Philippines</a>), it would be increasingly difficult to maintain popular support for Japan. Aoki promised to raise the issue with Tōjō, who mentioned his intent to grant independence to <a href="/wiki/British_Malaya" title="British Malaya">Malaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a> within a year in his June 1943 parliamentary speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan" title="Surrender of Japan">surrender of Japan</a>, Aoki was arrested (as were all former government members) by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Commander_of_the_Allied_Powers" title="Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers">Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers</a> and held in <a href="/wiki/Sugamo_Prison" title="Sugamo Prison">Sugamo Prison</a> on charges of <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crimes</a>. However, he was released in 1948 without coming to trial. Afterwards, Aoki established a private legal practice. In 1953, he ran for a seat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Councilors" title="House of Councilors">House of Councilors</a> with the support of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Japan,_1945)" title="Liberal Party (Japan, 1945)">Liberal Party</a> on a nationwide ticket. He was subsequently reelected to the same seat three more times as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democratic_Party_(Japan)" title="Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)">Liberal Democratic Party (LDP)</a>. He joined a right-wing faction within the LDP in 1960 which was adamantly opposed to Japan’s normalization of relations with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%E2%80%99s_Republic_of_China" title="People’s Republic of China">People’s Republic of China</a> and supported Japan’s continued recognition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> on <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. He was also a strong supporter of building a nationwide network of highways in Japan, especially the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%AB%C5%8D_Expressway" title="Chūō Expressway">Chūō Expressway</a>.</p>
<p>After his retirement from politics, Aoki joined the Board of Directors of <a href="/wiki/Shin-etsu_Broadcasting" title="Shin-etsu Broadcasting">Shin-etsu Broadcasting</a>, and in 1968 was one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Nagano_Broadcasting_Systems" title="Nagano Broadcasting Systems">Nagano Broadcasting Systems</a>. In 1971, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Rising_Sun" title="Order of the Rising Sun">Order of the Rising Sun</a>, 1st class. He published his memoirs in 1981, shortly before his death in 1982.</p>
