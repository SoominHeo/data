<p><b>Ferdinand August Bebel</b> (22 February 1840 – 13 August 1913) was a German socialist politician, writer, and orator. He is best remembered as one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Workers%27_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Workers' Party of Germany">Social Democratic Workers' Party of Germany</a> (SDAP) in 1869, which in 1875 merged with the <a href="/wiki/General_German_Workers%27_Association" title="General German Workers' Association">General German Workers' Association</a> into the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="History of the Social Democratic Party of Germany">Socialist Workers' Party of Germany</a> (SAPD). During the repression under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Socialist_Laws" title="Anti-Socialist Laws">Anti-Socialist Laws</a>, Bebel became the leading figure of the <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a> movement in Germany and from 1892 until his death served as chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Bebel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Bebel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ferdinand August Bebel, known to all by his middle name, was born on 22 February 1840, in <a href="/wiki/Deutz,_Cologne" title="Deutz, Cologne">Deutz</a>, Germany, now a part of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. He was the son of a <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noncommissioned_officer" title="Noncommissioned officer">noncommissioned officer</a> in the Prussian infantry, initially from <a href="/wiki/Ostr%C3%B3w_Wielkopolski" title="Ostrów Wielkopolski">Ostrowo</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Posen" title="Province of Posen">Province of Posen</a>, and was born in military barracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-americana-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a young man, Bebel apprenticed as a carpenter and joiner in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup> Like most German workmen at that time, he traveled extensively in search of work and he thereby obtained a first-hand knowledge of the difficulties facing the working people of the day.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>, where he lived for some time, he joined a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> workmen's club. When in <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">Tyrol</a> in 1859 he volunteered for service in the war against Italy, but was rejected; and in his own country he was rejected likewise as physically unfit for the army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-americana-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1860 he settled in Leipzig as a master turner, making horn buttons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-americana-1">[1]</a></sup> He joined various labor organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colliers_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-colliers-3">[3]</a></sup> Although initially an opponent of socialism, Bebel gradually was won over to socialist ideas through pamphlets of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Lassalle" title="Ferdinand Lassalle">Ferdinand Lassalle</a>, which popularized the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenin297_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenin297-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1865 he came under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Liebknecht" title="Wilhelm Liebknecht">Wilhelm Liebknecht</a> and was thereafter committed fully to the socialist cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Bebel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:August_Bebel_c1910.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1008" data-file-width="840" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/August_Bebel_c1910.jpg/240px-August_Bebel_c1910.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/August_Bebel_c1910.jpg/360px-August_Bebel_c1910.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/August_Bebel_c1910.jpg/480px-August_Bebel_c1910.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:August_Bebel_c1910.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
August Bebel c. 1910</div>

<p>Following the death of Lassalle, Bebel was among the group of Socialists that refused to follow new party leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_von_Schweitzer" title="Johann Baptist von Schweitzer">Johann Baptist von Schweitzer</a> at the Eisenach Conference of 1867, an action which gave rise to the name "Eisenachers" for this Marxist faction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup> Together with Liebknecht, he founded the <i>Sächsische Volkspartei</i> ("Saxon People's Party"). Bebel was also President of the Union of German Workers' Associations from 1867 and a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_International" title="First International">First International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bebel was elected to the <a href="/wiki/North_German_Confederation" title="North German Confederation">North German</a> <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(North_German_Confederation)" title="Reichstag (North German Confederation)">Reichstag</a> as a member from <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> in that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1869 he helped found the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Workers%27_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Workers' Party of Germany">Social Democratic Workers' Party of Germany</a> (SDAP), which later merged with another organization in 1875 to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAPD" title="SAPD">Socialist Workers' Party of Germany</a> (SAPD), which in turn became the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a> (SPD) in 1890.</p>
<p>Bebel's great organizing talent and oratorical power quickly made him one of the leaders of the socialists and their chief spokesman in parliament. He remained a member of the North German Parliament, and later of its counterpart for the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(German_Empire)" title="Reichstag (German Empire)">Reichstag</a>, until his death, except for the interval of 1881–83.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6">[6]</a></sup> He represented successively the districts of <a href="/wiki/Glauchau" title="Glauchau">Glauchau</a>-<a href="/wiki/Meerane" title="Meerane">Meerane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strassburg" title="Strassburg">Strassburg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nie_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nie-7">[7]</a></sup> Later in his life, he acted as chairman of the SPD. Representing as he did Marxian principles, he was bitterly opposed by certain factions of his party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colliers_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-colliers-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1870 he spoke in parliament against the continuance of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">war with France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-americana-1">[1]</a></sup> Bebel and Liebknecht were the only members who did not vote the extraordinary subsidy required for the war with France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup> Bebel was the only socialist who was elected to the Reichstag in 1871, and he used his position to protest against the annexation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alsace_and_Lorraine" title="Alsace and Lorraine">Alsace and Lorraine</a> and to express his full sympathy with the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Commune" title="Paris Commune">Paris Commune</a>. German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a> afterwards said that this speech of Bebel's was a “ray of light” showing him that socialism was an enemy to be fought against and crushed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6">[6]</a></sup> Falsely accused of being in league with the French and part of a conspiracy to free French <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoners of war</a> held in Germany and to lead them in an attack from the rear, Bebel and Liebknecht were arrested for <a href="/wiki/High_treason" title="High treason">high treason</a>, but no prosecution was possible for lack of evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not wanting to release such important opponents of the war effort, old charges of preaching dangerous doctrines and plotting against the state were levied against Bebel and Liebknecht in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-People_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-People-2">[2]</a></sup> The pair were convicted and sentenced to two years in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Festungshaft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Festungshaft (page does not exist)">Festungshaft</a></i> (imprisonment in a fortress), which was spent at the famous <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigstein_Fortress" title="Königstein Fortress">Königstein Fortress</a>. For insulting the German emperor, Bebel was additionally sentenced to nine months' ordinary imprisonment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6">[6]</a></sup> This incarceration served to increase Bebel's prestige among his party associates and the sympathetic public at large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nie_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nie-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1874 Bebel took a partner and founded a small button factory, for which he acted as salesman, but in 1889 he gave up his business to devote himself wholly to politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-americana-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1868 he became connected with the staff of the <i>Volksstaat</i> ("The People's State") at Leipzig, and in 1891 with that of the <i>Vorwärts</i> ("Forward") at <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nie_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-nie-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his release from prison, he helped to organize, at the congress of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gotha_(town)" title="Gotha (town)">Gotha</a>, the united party of Social Democrats, which had been formed during his imprisonment. After the passing of the Socialist Law he continued to show great activity in the debates of the Reichstag, and was also elected a member of the Saxon parliament; when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_siege" title="State of siege">state of siege</a> was proclaimed in Leipzig he was expelled from the city, and in 1886 condemned to nine months' imprisonment for taking part in a secret society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In party meetings of 1890 and 1891, Bebel's policies were severely attacked, first by the extremists, the “young” Socialists from Berlin, who wished to abandon parliamentary action; against these Bebel won a complete victory. On the other side he was involved in a quarrel with Volmar and his school, who desired to put aside from immediate consideration the complete attainment of the socialist ideal, and proposed that the party should aim at bringing about, not a complete overthrow of society, but a gradual amelioration. This conflict of tendencies continued, and Bebel came to be regarded as the chief exponent of the traditional views of the orthodox Marxist party. Though a strong opponent of militarism, he publicly stated that foreign nations attacking Germany must not expect the help or the neutrality of the Social Democrats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6">[6]</a></sup> Already in 1911 amid the rising tensions between the European powers, Bebel publicly predicted an upcoming <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">great war</a> with millions of soldiers confronting each other<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> followed by a great collapse, "mass bankruptcy, mass misery, mass unemployment and great famine."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Class.2C_race.2C_pacifism.2C_and_.27prurient.27_sex">Class, race, pacifism, and 'prurient' sex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Bebel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Class, race, pacifism, and 'prurient' sex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bebel particularly distinguished himself by his denunciation of the maltreatment of soldiers by officers and still more frequently by non-commissioned officers. His efforts in this matter had received great encouragement when King <a href="/wiki/Albert_of_Saxony" title="Albert of Saxony">Albert of Saxony</a> issued an edict dealing with the maltreatment of soldiers in the Saxon contingent, thus cutting the ground from under the feet of the Imperial Government, which had persistently attempted to deny or to explain away the cases put forward by Bebel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Speaking before the <i>Reichstag</i>, Bebel criticized the war to crush the <a href="/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion" title="Boxer Rebellion">Boxer Rebellion</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in 1900, saying:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>No, this is no crusade, no holy war; it is a very ordinary war of conquest...A campaign of revenge as barbaric as has never been seen in the last centuries, and not often at all in history...not even with the Huns, not even with the Vandals...That is no match for what the German and other troops of foreign powers, together with the Japanese troops, have done in China…<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Bebel is also famed for his outrage at the news of German mistreatment of indigenous people in her South-West African colony, the <a href="/wiki/Herero_people" title="Herero people">Herero</a> nation in particular. In 1904 following a revolt by the Herero people who were being pushed off their land to make way for German settlers, the government launched the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herero_and_Namaqua_Genocide" title="Herero and Namaqua Genocide">Herero and Namaqua Genocide</a> to crush the revolt by waging a "war of extermination" against the Herero. He and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Social_Democratic_Party" title="German Social Democratic Party">German Social Democratic Party</a> thus became the only party in the Reichstag to oppose increased colonial expenditures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deas2009_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deas2009-12">[12]</a></sup> and in a speech in March 1904 Bebel classified the policy in <a href="/wiki/German_West_Africa" title="German West Africa">German West Africa</a> as ‘not only barbaric, but bestial.’ This caused some sections of the contemporary German press to scathingly classify Bebel as 'Der hereroische Bebel' (<i>Coburger Zeitung</i>, 17 January 1904).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deas2009_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deas2009-12">[12]</a></sup> Bebel was not deterred; he later followed this up with strongly worded warnings against the rising tide of theories of <a href="/wiki/Racial_hierarchy" title="Racial hierarchy">racial hierarchy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racial_purity" title="Racial purity">racial purity</a>, causing the general election to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Reichstag" title="German Reichstag">German Reichstag</a> in 1907 to go over in history as the ‘<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hottentot" title="Hottentot">Hottentot</a> Election.’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bebel's book, <i>Women and Socialism</i> was translated into English by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_DeLeon" title="Daniel DeLeon">Daniel DeLeon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Labor_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Labor Party of America">Socialist Labor Party of America</a> as <i>Woman under Socialism</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It figured prominently in the Connolly-DeLeon controversy after <a href="/wiki/James_Connolly" title="James Connolly">James Connolly</a>, then a member of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Labor_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Labor Party of America">SLP</a>, denounced it as a "quasi-prurient" book that would repel potential recruits to the socialist movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The book contained an attack on the institution of marriage which identified Bebel with the most extreme forms of socialism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6">[6]</a></sup> In the preface to DeLeon's translation, 'Woman Under Socialism', DeLeon distanced himself from Bebel on this point, holding that monogamy was the most desirable form of social organisation.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Bebel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>August Bebel died on 13 August 1913 of a heart attack during a visit to a sanatorium in <a href="/wiki/Churwalden" title="Churwalden">Passugg</a>, Switzerland. He was 73 years old at the time of his death. His body was buried in <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>.</p>
<p>At the time of his death Bebel was eulogized by Russian Marxist leader <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> as a "model workers' leader," who had proven himself able to "break his own road" from being an ordinary worker into becoming a political leader in the struggle for a "better social system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The well-known saying "Anti-Semitism is the socialism of fools" ("<i>Der Antisemitismus ist der Sozialismus der dummen Kerle</i>") is frequently attributed to Bebel, but probably originated with the Austrian democrat <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ferdinand_Kronawetter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ferdinand Kronawetter (page does not exist)">Ferdinand Kronawetter</a>; it was in general use among German Social Democrats by the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bebel, along with <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Lassalle" title="Ferdinand Lassalle">Ferdinand Lassalle</a>, was among the socialist icons included in bas relief portraits on the facade of <a href="/wiki/The_Forward" title="The Forward">The Forward</a> building, erected in 1912 as the headquarters of the New York Yiddish-language socialist newspaper.</p>
