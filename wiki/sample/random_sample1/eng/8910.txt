<p><b>Auðumbla</b> (also spelled <i>Auðumla</i>, <i>Auðhumbla</i>, and <i>Auðhumla</i>) is a primeval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a> appearing in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>. It is attested in <i><a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a></i>, a part of <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i>, in association with <a href="/wiki/Ginnungagap" title="Ginnungagap">Ginnungagap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ymir" title="Ymir">Ymir</a>. Auðumbla is not mentioned again in the <i>Prose Edda</i>, and apart from one mention in <i><a href="/wiki/Nafna%C3%BEulur" title="Nafnaþulur">Nafnaþulur</a></i>, its name does not occur in other ancient sources. Nevertheless, it is generally accepted by scholars as an authentic part of Norse mythos and not dismissed as an invention of Snorri Sturluson.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Au%C3%B0umbla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ymir.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="577" data-file-width="736" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ymir.jpg/250px-Ymir.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ymir.jpg/375px-Ymir.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ymir.jpg/500px-Ymir.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>Auðumbla's name appears in different variations in <i>Prose Edda</i> manuscripts. Its meaning is unclear. The word <i>auð</i> might be related to words meaning 'wealth', 'ease', 'fate' or 'emptiness', with 'wealth' perhaps being a likelier candidate. The word <i>um(b)la</i> is unclear, but judging from apparent cognates in other <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>, it could mean 'polled cow'. Other vision says <i>þumb</i> could be the word stem.</p>
<p>Another theory links it with the name <i>Ymir</i>. The name may have been obscure and interpreted differently even in pagan times.</p>
<p>The name can be <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_orthography" title="Old Norse orthography">represented or Anglicized</a> as <i>Audumbla</i>, <i>Audumla</i>, <i>Audhumbla</i>, <i>Audhumla</i>, <i>Authumbla</i>, <i>Authumla</i>, <i>Authhumbla</i>, <i>Authhumla</i>, <i>Audhhumbla</i> or <i>Audhhumla</i>. In Swedish it is also <i>Ödhumla</i>. In this case, the letter <i>i</i> in <i>bird</i> and <i>girl</i> has a pronunciation very similar to <i>ö</i> (o with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umlaut_(diacritic)" title="Umlaut (diacritic)">umlaut</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Au%C3%B0umbla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Swedish scholar <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Rydberg" title="Viktor Rydberg">Viktor Rydberg</a>, writing in the late 19th century, drew a parallel between the Norse <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation myths</a> and accounts in <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vedic_mythology" title="Vedic mythology">Vedic mythology</a>, postulating a common <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">Proto-Indo-European</a> origin. While many of Rydberg's theories were dismissed as fanciful by later scholars his work on <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">comparative mythology</a> was sound to a large extent. Zoroastrian mythology does have a primeval ox which is variously said to be male or female and comes into existence in the middle of the earth along with the primeval man.</p>
