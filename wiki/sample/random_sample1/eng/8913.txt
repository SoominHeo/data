<p><b>Sir Marc Aurel Stein</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Indian_Empire" title="Order of the Indian Empire">KCIE</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Asiatic_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Asiatic Society">FRAS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>Stein Márk Aurél</i></span>) (26 November 1862 – 26 October 1943) was a Hungarian-British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist">archaeologist</a>, primarily known for his explorations and archaeological discoveries in Central Asia. He was also a professor at <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> universities.</p>
<p>Stein was also an ethnographer, geographer, linguist and surveyor. His collection of books and manuscripts taken from <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a> caves is important for the study of the history of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and the art and literature of <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. He wrote several volumes on his expeditions and discoveries which include <i>Ancient Khotan</i>, <i>Serindia</i> and <i>Innermost Asia</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurel_Stein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stein was born to Nathan Stein and Anna Hirschler, a Jewish couple residing in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>. His parents and his sister retained their Jewish faith but Stein and his brother, Ernst Eduard, were baptised as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutherans</a>, apparently to free them from the anti-semitism which would have denied them access to education and advancement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMirsky1977.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Dq3YKfArws2QC.26printsec.3Dfrontcover.23v.3Donepage.26q.3Danti-semitism.26f.3Dfalse_3-4.5D_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMirsky1977.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Dq3YKfArws2QC.26printsec.3Dfrontcover.23v.3Donepage.26q.3Danti-semitism.26f.3Dfalse_3-4.5D-2">[2]</a></sup> At home the family spoke German and <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, the language of <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_nationalism" title="Hungarian nationalism">Hungarian nationalist revival</a> in the 19th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and Stein was proud of this heritage for the rest of his life. He attended Catholic and Lutheran gymnasiums in Budapest, where he mastered Greek, Latin, French, and English before going on for advanced study at Universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leipzig" title="University of Leipzig">Leipzig</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Tubingen" title="University of Tubingen">Tübingen</a>. He graduated in Sanskrit and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_Language" title="Persian Language">Farsi</a> and received his Ph.D. from Tübingen in 1883.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMirsky1977.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Dq3YKfArws2QC.26q.3DMagyar.23v.3Dsnippet.26q.3DMagyar.26f.3Dfalse_5-6.5D_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMirsky1977.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Dq3YKfArws2QC.26q.3DMagyar.23v.3Dsnippet.26q.3DMagyar.26f.3Dfalse_5-6.5D-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1884 he went to England to study oriental languages and archaeology. He became a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> citizen in 1904<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and made his famous expeditions with British sponsorship. In 1887, Stein went to India, where he joined the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Punjab" title="University of the Punjab">Punjab University</a> as Registrar. Later, between 1888 and 1899, he was the Principal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_College,_Lahore" title="Oriental College, Lahore">Oriental College, Lahore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_.E2.80.93_British_Academy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio_.E2.80.93_British_Academy-6">[6]</a></sup> Stein was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Sven_Hedin" title="Sven Hedin">Sven Hedin</a>'s 1898 work <i>Through Asia</i>. Realizing the importance of Central Asian history and archaeology he sent a proposal to the government to explore, map and study the people of Central Asia. In May 1900 he received the approval to lead an expedition to Chinese Turkestan which was strategically located in High Asia where the Russians and Germans were already taking interest.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expeditions">Expeditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurel_Stein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expeditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Stein made four major expeditions to <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>—in 1900–1901, 1906–1908, 1913–1916 and 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He brought to light the hidden treasure of a great civilization which by then was practically lost to the world. One of his significant finds during his first journey during 1900–1901 was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taklamakan" title="Taklamakan">Taklamakan</a> Desert oasis of <a href="/wiki/Dandan_Oilik" title="Dandan Oilik">Dandan Oilik</a> where he was able to uncover a number of relics. During his third expedition in 1913–1916, he excavated at <a href="/wiki/Khara-Khoto" title="Khara-Khoto">Khara-Khoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a>'s Stein collection of Chinese, Tibetan and Tangut manuscripts, Prakrit wooden tablets, and documents in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khotanese_language" title="Khotanese language">Khotanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uyghur_language" title="Uyghur language">Uyghur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sogdian_language" title="Sogdian language">Sogdian</a> and Eastern Turkic is the result of his travels through central Asia during the 1920s and 1930s. Stein discovered manuscripts in the previously lost <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian languages</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a> at <a href="/wiki/Miran_(Xinjiang)" title="Miran (Xinjiang)">Miran</a> and other oasis towns, and recorded numerous archaeological sites especially in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balochistan_(region)" title="Balochistan (region)">Balochistan</a>.</p>
<p>When Stein visited Khotan he was able to render in Persian a portion of the Shahnama after he came across a local reading the Shahnama in Turki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mirsky1998_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mirsky1998-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 1901 Stein was responsible for exposing forgeries of <a href="/wiki/Islam_Akhun" title="Islam Akhun">Islam Akhun</a>.</p>
<p>Stein's greatest discovery was made at the <a href="/wiki/Mogao_Caves" title="Mogao Caves">Mogao Caves</a> also known as "Caves of the Thousand Buddhas", near <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a> in 1907. It was there that he discovered a printed copy of the <i><a href="/wiki/Diamond_Sutra" title="Diamond Sutra">Diamond Sutra</a></i>, the world's oldest printed text, dating to AD 868, along with 40,000 other scrolls (all removed by gradually winning the confidence and bribing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> caretaker).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He acquired 24 cases of manuscripts and 4 cases of paintings and relics. He was knighted for his efforts, but Chinese nationalists dubbed him a burglar and staged protests against him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His discovery inspired other French, Russian, Japanese, and Chinese treasure hunters and explorers who also took their toll on the collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his expedition of 1906–1908 while surveying in the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_China" title="Western China">western China</a>, Stein suffered frostbite and lost several toes on his right foot.</p>
<p>When he was resting from his extended journeys into Central Asia, he spent most of his time living in a tent in the spectacularly beautiful alpine meadow called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mohanmarg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mohanmarg (page does not exist)">Mohanmarg</a> which lies at the mouth atop the <a href="/wiki/Sind_Valley" title="Sind Valley">Sind Valley</a> where from he translated <i><a href="/wiki/Rajatarangini" title="Rajatarangini">Rajatarangini</a></i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">sanskrit</a></i> to <i>English</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Stein was a lifelong bachelor, but was always accompanied by a dog named "Dash" (of which there were seven).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AurelSteinGrave.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/AurelSteinGrave.jpg/220px-AurelSteinGrave.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/AurelSteinGrave.jpg/330px-AurelSteinGrave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/AurelSteinGrave.jpg/440px-AurelSteinGrave.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The fourth expedition to Central Asia, however, ended in failure. Stein did not publish any account, but others have written of the frustrations and rivalries between British and American interests in China, between Harvard's Fogg Museum and the British Museum, and finally, between <a href="/wiki/Paul_J._Sachs" title="Paul J. Sachs">Paul J. Sachs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Langdon_Warner" title="Langdon Warner">Langdon Warner</a>, the two Harvard sponsors of the expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stein died in <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a> on 26 October 1943 and is buried in Kabul's British Cemetery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Game">Great Game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurel_Stein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Great Game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stein, as well as his rivals <a href="/wiki/Sven_Hedin" title="Sven Hedin">Sven Hedin</a>, Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Younghusband" title="Francis Younghusband">Francis Younghusband</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolai_Przhevalsky" title="Nikolai Przhevalsky">Nikolai Przhevalsky</a>, were active players in the British-Russian struggle for influence in Central Asia, the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Game" title="Great Game">Great Game</a>. Their explorations were supported by the British and Russian Empires as they filled in the remaining "blank spots" on the maps, providing valuable information and creating "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spheres_of_influence" title="Spheres of influence">spheres of influence</a>" for archaeological exploration as they did for political influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyerBrysac1999.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DSsv-GONnxTsC.26pg.3DPA611.26dq.3DA.2BTournament.2Bof.2BShadows.2Baurel.2Bstein.26hl.3Den.26sa.3DX.26ei.3D4hKXVOWcNonroAS-0oLQBg.26ved.3D0CB8Q6AEwAA.23v.3Donepage.26q.3DA.2520Tournament.2520of.2520Shadows.2520aurel.2520stein.26f.3Dfalse_367-368.5D_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyerBrysac1999.5Bhttps:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DSsv-GONnxTsC.26pg.3DPA611.26dq.3DA.2BTournament.2Bof.2BShadows.2Baurel.2Bstein.26hl.3Den.26sa.3DX.26ei.3D4hKXVOWcNonroAS-0oLQBg.26ved.3D0CB8Q6AEwAA.23v.3Donepage.26q.3DA.2520Tournament.2520of.2520Shadows.2520aurel.2520stein.26f.3Dfalse_367-368.5D-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The art objects he collected are divided between the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Srinagar" title="Srinagar">Srinagar</a> Museum, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum,_New_Delhi" title="National Museum, New Delhi">National Museum, New Delhi</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurel_Stein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stein received a number of honours during his career. In 1909, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Medal_(RGS)" title="Gold Medal (RGS)">Founder's Medal</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Geographical_Society" title="Royal Geographical Society">Royal Geographical Society</a> 'for his extensive explorations in Central Asia, and in particular his archaeological work'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RGS_.E2.80.93_Gold_Medal_Recipients_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-RGS_.E2.80.93_Gold_Medal_Recipients-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1909, he was awarded the first Campbell Memorial Gold Medal by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asiatic_Society_of_Mumbai" title="Asiatic Society of Mumbai">Royal Asiatic Society of Bombay</a>. He was awarded a number of other Gold Medals: the Gold Medal of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_de_G%C3%A9ographie" title="Société de Géographie">Société de Géographie</a> in 1923; the <i>Grande Médaille d’or</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Asiatic_Society_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Royal Asiatic Society of Great Britain and Ireland">Royal Asiatic Society of Great Britain and Ireland</a> in 1932; and the Gold Medal of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Antiquaries_of_London" title="Society of Antiquaries of London">Society of Antiquaries of London</a> in 1935. In 1934, he was awarded the Huxley Memorial Medal of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Anthropological_Institute_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Royal Anthropological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland">Royal Anthropological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sir_Aurel_Stein.E2.80.99s_Medals_at_the_Royal_Geographical_Society_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sir_Aurel_Stein.E2.80.99s_Medals_at_the_Royal_Geographical_Society-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1910 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King%27s_Birthday_Honours" title="King's Birthday Honours">King's Birthday Honours</a>, he was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_the_Order_of_the_Indian_Empire" title="Companion of the Order of the Indian Empire">Companion of the Order of the Indian Empire</a> (CIE) for his service as Inspector-General Of Education and Archaeological Surveyor in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North-West_Frontier_Province_(1901%E2%80%931955)" title="North-West Frontier Province (1901–1955)">North-West Frontier Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LG_23_June_1910_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-LG_23_June_1910-22">[22]</a></sup> Two years later, in the 1912 King's Birthday Honours, he was promoted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_Commander" title="Knight Commander">Knight Commander</a> of the Order of the Indian Empire (KCIE) for his service as Superintendent of the Archaeological Department, North-West Frontier Circle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LG_11_June_1912_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-LG_11_June_1912-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was made an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary</a> <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Letters" title="Doctor of Letters">Doctor of Letters</a> (DLitt) by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> in 1909. He was made an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a> (DSc) by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> in 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sir_Aurel_Stein.E2.80.99s_Medals_at_the_Royal_Geographical_Society_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sir_Aurel_Stein.E2.80.99s_Medals_at_the_Royal_Geographical_Society-21">[21]</a></sup> He made an honorary <a href="/wiki/Legum_Doctor" title="Legum Doctor">Doctor of Laws</a> (LLD) by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">University of St Andrews</a> in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sir_Aurel_Stein.E2.80.99s_Medals_at_the_Royal_Geographical_Society_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sir_Aurel_Stein.E2.80.99s_Medals_at_the_Royal_Geographical_Society-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Who_Was_Who_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Who_Was_Who-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1919, Stein became a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1921, he was elected <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">Fellow of the British Academy</a> (FBA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_.E2.80.93_British_Academy_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio_.E2.80.93_British_Academy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
