<p>The <b>Autostrade</b> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[autoˈstraːde]</a></span>; singular <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><b>Autostrada</b></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[autoˈstraːda]</a></span>) are roads forming the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> national system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorway" title="Motorway">motorways</a>. The total length of the system is about 6,758 kilometres (4,199 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In North and Central Italy this is mainly as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tollways" title="Tollways">tollways</a>, with the biggest portion in concession to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantia_(company)" title="Atlantia (company)">Atlantia</a> group (via <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_per_l%27Italia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Autostrade per l'Italia (page does not exist)">Autostrade per l'Italia</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autostrade_per_l%27Italia" title="it:Autostrade per l'Italia">it</a>)</span>) which operates some 3,408 km. Other operators include ASTM, ATP and <a href="/wiki/Autostrade_Lombarde" title="Autostrade Lombarde">Autostrade Lombarde</a> in the north-west, <a href="/wiki/Autostrada_del_Brennero_(company)" title="Autostrada del Brennero (company)">Autostrada del Brennero</a>, <a href="/wiki/A4_Holding" title="A4 Holding">A4 Holding</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Concessioni_Autostradali_Venete&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Concessioni Autostradali Venete (page does not exist)">Concessioni Autostradali Venete</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Autovie_Venete&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Autovie Venete (page does not exist)">Autovie Venete</a> in the north-east, SALT and Autocisa in the center, <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">all under the supervision of the state-owned <a href="/wiki/ANAS" title="ANAS">ANAS</a>.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Italy was the first country in the world to build motorways, i.e., fast roads, reserved for motor vehicles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Milano-Laghi motorway (connecting <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Varese" title="Varese">Varese</a>) was devised by Piero Puricelli, a civil engineer and entrepreneur. He received the first authorization to build a public-utility fast road in 1921, and completed the construction (one lane each direction) between 1924 and 1926. By the end of the 1930s, over 400 kilometers of multi- and dual-single-lane motorways were constructed throughout Italy, linking cities and rural towns.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed">Speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:82px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Autostrada.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="990" data-file-width="510" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Autostrada.jpg/80px-Autostrada.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Autostrada.jpg/120px-Autostrada.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Autostrada.jpg/160px-Autostrada.jpg 2x" width="80"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Autostrada.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Autostrada sign</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Italy's autostrade have a standard speed limit of 130 km/h (81 mph) for cars. Limits for other vehicles (or during foul weather and/or low visibility) are lower. Legal provisions allow operators to set the limit to 150 km/h (93 mph) on their concessions on a voluntary basis if these conditions are met: three lanes in each direction and a working <a href="/wiki/SPECS_(speed_camera)" title="SPECS (speed camera)">SICVE</a>, or Safety Tutor, speed-camera system that measure the average speed. In 2016, no road was taking advantage of this possibility.</p>
<p>The first speed limit, to 120 km/h (75 mph), was enacted in November 1973 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-law_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-law-2">[2]</a></sup> In October 1977, a gradated system was introduced, where cars of above 1,300 cc (79 cu in) were given a 140 km/h (87 mph) speed limit, cars of 900-1299 cc could drive at 130 km/h (81 mph), those of 600-899 cc could drive at 110 km/h (68 mph), and those of 599 cc (36.6 cu in) or less were given a max speed of 90 km/h (56 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-law_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-law-2">[2]</a></sup> In July 1988 a blanket speed limit of 110 km/h (68 mph) was imposed on all cars above 600 cc (the lower limit was kept for smaller cars) by the short lived <a href="/wiki/Italian_Democratic_Socialist_Party" title="Italian Democratic Socialist Party">PSDI</a> government, but in September 1989 this was increased to 130 km/h (81 mph) for cars above 1,100 cc (67 cu in) and 110 km/h (68 mph) for smaller ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-libera_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-libera-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Until 1990, the designation <b>A1</b> referred only to the Milan-Rome section of the current A1; the Rome-Naples section was known as <b>A2</b>. After a link was built bypassing Rome, the designation <b>A2</b> was withdrawn and now the A1 designation refers to the whole route. The residual connections to the "Grande Raccordo Anulare" (Great Ring Road, around Rome) were designated as <i>raccordi</i> (see later). Until 1973, the designation <b>A17</b> referred to the current A16, and the section Canosa-Bari of the current A14.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_bretelle_and_raccordi_autostradali">List of <i>bretelle</i> and <i>raccordi autostradali</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: List of bretelle and raccordi autostradali">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some <i>autostrade</i> are called <i>bretelle, diramazioni</i> or <i>raccordi</i> because they are short and have few exits.</p>
<p><i>Bretelle</i>, <i>diramazioni</i> or <i>raccordi</i> are generally connections between two motorways, or connections between motorways and important cities without a motorway.</p>
<p>They have the same number (sometimes with the suffix <i>dir</i>) as one of the two autostrade linked, a combination of the numbers of the two autostrade linked, or the number of the main autostrada.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trafori_.28T.29">Trafori (T)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trafori (T)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Important <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">alpine</a> <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a> (<span class="languageicon" style="font-size:0.95em; font-weight:bold; color:#555;">(Italian)</span> <i>trafori</i>) are identified by the capital letter "T" followed by a single digit number. Currently there are only three T-classified tunnels: <a href="/wiki/Mont_Blanc_Tunnel" title="Mont Blanc Tunnel">Mont Blanc Tunnel</a> (T1), <a href="/wiki/Great_St_Bernard_Tunnel" title="Great St Bernard Tunnel">Great St Bernard Tunnel</a> (T2) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frejus_Road_Tunnel" title="Frejus Road Tunnel">Frejus Road Tunnel</a> (T4). Tunnels that cross the border between Italy and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (T1, T4) or <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (T2), are treated as motorways (green signage, access control, and so on), although they are not proper motorways. The code T3 was once assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Bargagli-Ferriere_Tunnel" title="Bargagli-Ferriere Tunnel">Bargagli-Ferriere Tunnel</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_Appennines" title="Ligurian Appennines">Ligurian Appennines</a> before it was reclassified as SP 226.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Raccordi_autostradali_.28RA.29">Raccordi autostradali (RA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Raccordi autostradali (RA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>RA stands for <i>Raccordo autostradale</i> (translated as "motorway connection"), a relatively short <a href="/wiki/Spur_route" title="Spur route">spur route</a> that connects an <i>autostrada</i> to a nearby city or tourist resort not directly served by the motorway. These spurs are owned and managed by ANAS (with some exceptions, such as the RA7 that became <a href="/wiki/Autostrada_A53_(Italy)" title="Autostrada A53 (Italy)">A53</a> when assigned to a private company for maintenance). Some spurs are toll-free motorways (type-A), but most are type-B or type-C roads. All RA have separate carriageways with two lanes in each direction. Generally, they do not have an emergency lane.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strade_extraurbane_principali">Strade extraurbane principali</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autostrade_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Strade extraurbane principali">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:82px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="200" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg/80px-Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg/120px-Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg/160px-Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg.png 2x" width="80"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Italian_traffic_signs_-_strada_extraurbana_principale.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Strada extraurbana principale</i> sign</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Type B</i> highway (or <i>strada extraurbana principale</i>), commonly but unofficially known as <i>superstrada</i> (Italian equivalent for expressway), is a divided highway with at least two lanes in each direction, paved shoulder on the right, no cross-traffic and no at-grade intersections. Access restrictions on such highways are exactly the same as autostrade. Signage at the beginning and the end of the highways is the same, except the background color is blue instead of green. The general speed limit on <i>strade extraurbane principali</i> is 110 km/h. <i>Strade extraurbane principali</i> are not tolled. All <i>strade extraurbane principali</i> are owned and managed by <a href="/wiki/ANAS" title="ANAS">ANAS</a>, and directly controlled by the Italian government or by the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Italy" title="Regions of Italy">regions</a>.</p>
