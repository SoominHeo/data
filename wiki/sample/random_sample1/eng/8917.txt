<p><i><b>Out of the Furnace</b></i> is a 2013 American <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller film</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Cooper_(director)" title="Scott Cooper (director)">Scott Cooper</a>, from a screenplay written by Cooper and Brad Ingelsby. Produced by <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_DiCaprio" title="Leonardo DiCaprio">Leonardo DiCaprio</a>, the film stars <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">Christian Bale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casey_Affleck" title="Casey Affleck">Casey Affleck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woody_Harrelson" title="Woody Harrelson">Woody Harrelson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoe_Saldana" title="Zoe Saldana">Zoe Saldana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest_Whitaker" title="Forest Whitaker">Forest Whitaker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willem_Dafoe" title="Willem Dafoe">Willem Dafoe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Shepard" title="Sam Shepard">Sam Shepard</a>.</p>
<p>The film is about a Pennsylvania steel mill worker Russell Baze (Bale) and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraq_war" title="Iraq war">Iraq war</a> veteran brother Rodney (Affleck), who cannot adjust to civilian life. While Rodney makes some money doing bareknuckle fights for bar owner and small-time criminal John Petty (Dafoe), who runs illegal gambling operations, Rodney becomes so indebted due to his own gambling losses that he begs Petty to let him do a big money fight. After Petty reluctantly arranges for Rodney to do a fight for a ruthless criminal gang in the backwoods, Rodney disappears, and his brother tries to find out what has happened to him. The film received a <a href="/wiki/Limited_release" title="Limited release">limited release</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> on December 4, 2013, followed by a wide theatrical release on December 6. The film earned $15.7 million against its $22 million budget and it received mixed reviews.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After getting off work at a <a href="/wiki/North_Braddock,_Pennsylvania" title="North Braddock, Pennsylvania">North Braddock, Pennsylvania</a>, steel mill, Russell Baze catches his brother Rodney at a horse racing simulcast, where Rodney had just bet on a losing horse. Rodney reveals John Petty loaned the money to him. Petty owns a bar and runs several illegal games. Russell visits Petty, pays off some of Rodney's debt, and promises to pay Petty the rest with his next paycheck if Rodney has not yet paid it off. Driving home intoxicated, Russell hits another car, killing its occupants, including a little boy. He is incarcerated for vehicular manslaughter. While in prison he is informed that his ailing father has died and his girlfriend Lena has left him for the small town police chief, Wesley Barnes. Upon his release from prison, Russell returns home and resumes his job at the mill.</p>
<p>The same day, Rodney participates in an illegal bare-knuckle prizefight. Rodney was supposed to take a "dive" for Petty as a way to repay some of the gambling debt he owes Petty, but during the fight, Rodney becomes enraged against his opponent and wins the fight. The next morning, Russell finds Rodney's bloodied knuckle tapes in the trash and confronts him about it. Russell wants him to work in the mill, but Rodney, a four-tour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraq_war" title="Iraq war">Iraq war</a> veteran, is too mentally scarred to take a regular job.</p>
<p>Rodney tells Petty that these "nickel and dime" fights will never earn him enough money to pay Petty back. Rodney then insists that Petty call and organize a more lucrative fight. Petty reluctantly arranges a fight with Harlan DeGroat, a sociopathic drug dealer from rural New Jersey to whom Petty owes a lot of money. Russell wants Lena back, but she is pregnant with Wesley's baby. Russell unsuccessfully feigns happiness to Lena, saying she will be a great mom, but both know that her pregnancy makes their getting back together an impossibility.</p>
<p>Rodney is told he must purposely lose the fight in New Jersey. When DeGroat seeks assurances Rodney will lose, Petty promises he will. Rodney briefly knocks out his opponent, but when hearing Petty pleading with him, Rodney helps the fighter get up, takes a dive, and lets the man pummel his face into a bloody mess. After the fight, DeGroat asks for the rest of his money, but Petty reminds DeGroat they had agreed in advance that this fight made them even, and DeGroat drops the subject. While driving back home, DeGroat and his men ambush Petty and Rodney. DeGroat first shoots and kills Petty, has Rodney dragged into the woods, and kills him, too. Unknown to anyone, Petty accidentally dialed his cell phone, and it fell onto the car seat. The call connected to his bartender Dan's voice mail and provided proof that DeGroat was the killer.</p>
<p>That night, Russell finds a letter from Rodney, stating this will be his last fight and he wants to work with Russell at the mill. Wesley informs Russell about Rodney's disappearance, and Russell and his uncle "Red" set off to find him. In DeGroat's town, Russell and Red are stopped by the sheriff, who informs them that DeGroat's men would kill them if they knew why the two were in town, and, as a favor to Sheriff Wesley, he will escort them to the state line rather than searching and arresting them for illegally carrying concealed weapons.</p>
<p>Upon returning to the mill, Wesley visits Russell and confirms Rodney's death. Russell goes to Petty's office, finds a phone number for DeGroat, and calls him without identifying himself, enticing him to come collect Petty's debt. At the bar, Russell sabotages DeGroat's van to prevent his escape and confronts him. DeGroat escapes to a nearby, shutdown mill, where Russell shoots him in the thigh. Russell then follows DeGroat as he hobbles off and shoots him in the back. Russell informs DeGroat that he is Rodney's brother, as Wesley approaches the mill in a squad car. Wesley pleads for Russell to put down his gun, but Russell proceeds to carefully aim his hunting rifle and shoots DeGroat in the head. The film cuts to Russell sitting at home at the dining table and fades to black.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was produced by <a href="/wiki/Relativity_Media" title="Relativity Media">Relativity Media</a>, <a href="/wiki/Appian_Way" title="Appian Way">Appian Way</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Granite_Pictures" title="Red Granite Pictures">Red Granite Pictures</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Free_Productions" title="Scott Free Productions">Scott Free Productions</a>, with Jeff Waxman, Tucker Tooley, Brooklyn Weaver, <a href="/wiki/Riza_Aziz" title="Riza Aziz">Riza Aziz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joey_McFarland" title="Joey McFarland">Joey McFarland</a>, Joe Gatta, Danny Dimbort, and Christian Mercuri serving as executive producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wadas_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wadas-3">[3]</a></sup> Director <a href="/wiki/Scott_Cooper_(director)" title="Scott Cooper (director)">Scott Cooper</a> read an article about <a href="/wiki/Braddock,_Pennsylvania" title="Braddock, Pennsylvania">Braddock, Pennsylvania</a>, a declining <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> industry town outside of <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, and the efforts to revitalize it, led by mayor <a href="/wiki/John_Fetterman_(politician)" title="John Fetterman (politician)">John Fetterman</a>. After visiting, Cooper was inspired to use the borough as the backdrop for a film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corsaro2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-corsaro2012-4">[4]</a></sup> Cooper developed the story from <i>The Low Dweller</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">spec script</a> written by Brad Ingelsby that had actor <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_DiCaprio" title="Leonardo DiCaprio">Leonardo DiCaprio</a> and director <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a> attached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fleming_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fleming-5">[5]</a></sup> The studio offered the script to Cooper, which he rewrote, drawing on his experience of growing up in <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a> and losing a sibling at a young age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> DiCaprio and Scott stayed on as producers of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fleming_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-fleming-5">[5]</a></sup> The story has no relation to <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_This_Furnace" title="Out of This Furnace">Out of This Furnace</a></i>, a 1941 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_novel" title="Historical novel">historical novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bell_(novelist)" title="Thomas Bell (novelist)">Thomas Bell</a>, set in Braddock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corsar02013_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-corsar02013-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> reported the film's budget was $22 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclintock_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcclintock-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Principal photography began in the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_metropolitan_area" title="Pittsburgh metropolitan area">Pittsburgh metropolitan area</a> on April 13, 2012, and wrapped on June 1, 2012. The majority of filming took place in Braddock, and additional filming was in nearby <a href="/wiki/North_Braddock,_Pennsylvania" title="North Braddock, Pennsylvania">North Braddock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperial,_Pennsylvania" title="Imperial, Pennsylvania">Imperial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rankin,_Pennsylvania" title="Rankin, Pennsylvania">Rankin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swissvale,_Pennsylvania" title="Swissvale, Pennsylvania">Swissvale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Cinematographer Masanobu Takayanagi shot the feature in <a href="/wiki/Anamorphic_format" title="Anamorphic format">anamorphic format</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" title="Eastman Kodak">Kodak</a> <a href="/wiki/35_mm_film" title="35 mm film">35 mm film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Prison scenes were filmed in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Panhandle_of_West_Virginia" title="Northern Panhandle of West Virginia">Northern Panhandle</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, at the former <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_State_Penitentiary" title="West Virginia State Penitentiary">State Penitentiary</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moundsville,_West_Virginia" title="Moundsville, West Virginia">Moundsville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wadas_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-wadas-3">[3]</a></sup> Filming also took place in rural <a href="/wiki/Beaver_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Beaver County, Pennsylvania">Beaver County</a>, including a <a href="/wiki/Deer_hunting" title="Deer hunting">deer hunting</a> scene in <a href="/wiki/Raccoon_Creek_State_Park" title="Raccoon Creek State Park">Raccoon Creek State Park</a> and a mill scene in <a href="/wiki/Koppel,_Pennsylvania" title="Koppel, Pennsylvania">Koppel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Independence_Township,_Beaver_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Independence Township, Beaver County, Pennsylvania">Independence Township</a> doubled for <a href="/wiki/Bergen_County,_New_Jersey" title="Bergen County, New Jersey">Bergen County, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Carrie_Furnace" title="Carrie Furnace">Carrie Furnace</a>, an abandoned <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> near Braddock, served as the location for the film's finale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corsaro2012_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-corsaro2012-4">[4]</a></sup> Christian Bale wore a tattoo of Braddock's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZIP_code" title="ZIP code">ZIP code</a>, 15104, on his neck as a homage to the town's mayor <a href="/wiki/John_Fetterman_(politician)" title="John Fetterman (politician)">John Fetterman</a>, who has the same design on his arm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The musical score to <i>Out of the Furnace</i> was composed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dickon_Hinchliffe" title="Dickon Hinchliffe">Dickon Hinchliffe</a>. Originally, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Iglesias" title="Alberto Iglesias">Alberto Iglesias</a> had reached an agreement to compose the score for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, Hinchliffe later took over scoring duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Jam" title="Pearl Jam">Pearl Jam</a> frontman <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Vedder" title="Eddie Vedder">Eddie Vedder</a> re-recorded the song <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Release_(Pearl_Jam_song)" title="Release (Pearl Jam song)">Release</a> from Pearl Jam's debut album <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearl_Jam_Ten" title="Pearl Jam Ten">Ten</a> for the film, which can be heard during the opening scenes and end credits. A soundtrack album featuring Hinchliffe's score was released digitally on December 3, 2013 by Relativity Music Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film premiered at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TCL_Chinese_Theatre" title="TCL Chinese Theatre">TCL Chinese Theatre</a> on November 9, 2013 in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood, California</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>'s AFI Fest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It received a <a href="/wiki/Limited_release" title="Limited release">limited release</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> on December 4, 2013, followed by a wide theatrical release in the U.S. on December 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Director Scott Cooper won the award for best first or second film for <i>Out of the Furnace</i> at the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Rome_Film_Festival" title="Rome Film Festival">Rome Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Jam" title="Pearl Jam">Pearl Jam</a> song "Release" is featured during the opening title and features in a newly recorded edition during the end credits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Out of the Furnace</i> was the only new film to receive a <a href="/wiki/Wide_release" title="Wide release">wide release</a> in the U.S. on December 6, 2013, and earned an estimated $1.8 million on its opening day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The film took in an estimated $5.3 million over its opening weekend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> characterized it as a <a href="/wiki/Box_office_bomb" title="Box office bomb">box office bomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclintock_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcclintock-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaufman_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaufman-21">[21]</a></sup> The film came in third behind the animated <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Pictures" title="Walt Disney Pictures">Disney</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Frozen_(2013_film)" title="Frozen (2013 film)">Frozen</a></i>, which brought in $31.6 million, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games:_Catching_Fire" title="The Hunger Games: Catching Fire">The Hunger Games: Catching Fire</a></i>, which had $27 million in ticket sales that weekend. Relativity Media had pre-sold the film to foreign distributors for $16 million, which offset its costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaufman_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-kaufman-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the film has an average approval rating of 53% based on 182 reviews and an average rating of 5.9/10. The general consensus for the site says: "While it may not make the most of its incredible cast, <i>Out of the Furnace</i> is still so packed with talent that it's hard to turn away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The film holds a score of 63 (indicating "generally favorable reviews") out of 100 on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, based on 40 critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit">Lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Out_of_the_Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Town officials from <a href="/wiki/Mahwah,_New_Jersey" title="Mahwah, New Jersey">Mahwah, New Jersey</a>, urged a boycott of the film due to negative depictions of the <a href="/wiki/Ramapough_Mountain_Indians" title="Ramapough Mountain Indians">Ramapough Mountain Indians</a>, an indigenous people living around the <a href="/wiki/Ramapo_Mountains" title="Ramapo Mountains">Ramapo Mountains</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> characterizing the film as a <a href="/wiki/Hate_crime" title="Hate crime">hate crime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Relativity_Media" title="Relativity Media">Relativity Media</a> responded that the film "is not based on any one person or group" and is "entirely fictional".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Nine members of the group, eight of whom have the surname of the film's lead character, DeGroat, filed suit against the makers and other involved parties, claiming that <i>Out of the Furnace</i> portrays a gang of "inbreds" living in the Ramapo Mountains who are "lawless, drug-addicted, impoverished and violent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The lawsuit asserts that "The Defendants, and each of them, knew or should have known that their actions would place Plaintiffs, and/or any person so situated in a false light." The suit continues, "The connection between the ethnic slur of 'Jackson Whites', with the location of the Ramapo Mountains of New Jersey', with a <a href="/wiki/Bergen_County_Police_Department" title="Bergen County Police Department">Bergen County Police</a> patrol car, with the surnames 'DeGroat' and 'Van Dunk', is too specific to the Ramapough plaintiffs to be chance, coincidence or happenstance, and implies an element of knowledge on the part of the Defendants, or some of them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 16, 2014, U.S. District Court Judge William Walls, sitting in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a>, dismissed the lawsuit, saying that the film did not refer directly to any of the plaintiffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
