<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Eine kleine Nachtmusik</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><b><span lang="de">Eine kleine Nachtmusik</span></b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (Serenade No. 13 for strings in G major), <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6chel_catalogue" title="KÃ¶chel catalogue">K.</a> 525, is a 1787 composition for a <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber ensemble</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>. The German title means "a little <a href="/wiki/Serenade" title="Serenade">serenade</a>," though it is often rendered more literally but less accurately as "a little night music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The work is written for an ensemble of two <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> with optional <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>, but is often performed by <a href="/wiki/String_orchestra" title="String orchestra">string orchestras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H397_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-H397-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition.2C_publication.2C_and_reception">Composition, publication, and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eine_kleine_Nachtmusik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition, publication, and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The serenade was completed in Vienna on 10 August 1787,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H397_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-H397-3">[3]</a></sup> around the time Mozart was working on the second act of his opera <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">Don Giovanni</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is not known why it was composed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Hildesheimer, noting that most of Mozart's serenades were written on commission, suggests that this serenade, too, was a commission, whose origin and first performance were not recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-H-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="catalog"></span>The traditionally used name of the work comes from the entry Mozart made for it in his personal catalog, which begins, "Eine kleine Nacht-Musik."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Neal_Zaslaw" title="Neal Zaslaw">Zaslaw</a> and Cowdery point out, Mozart almost certainly was not giving the piece a special title, but only entering in his records that he had completed a little serenade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaslaw_and_Cowdery_1991.2C_250_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zaslaw_and_Cowdery_1991.2C_250-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The work was not published until about 1827, long after Mozart's death, by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Anton_Andr%C3%A9" title="Johann Anton AndrÃ©">Johann AndrÃ©</a> in <a href="/wiki/Offenbach_am_Main" title="Offenbach am Main">Offenbach am Main</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H397_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-H397-3">[3]</a></sup> It had been sold to this publisher in 1799 by Mozart's widow <a href="/wiki/Constanze_Mozart" title="Constanze Mozart">Constanze</a>, part of a large bundle of her husband's compositions.</p>
<p>Today the serenade is widely performed and recorded; indeed both Jacobson and Hildesheimer opine that the serenade is the most popular of all Mozart's works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Of the music, Hildesheimer writes, "even if we hear it on every street corner, its high quality is undisputed, an occasional piece from a light but happy pen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-H-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Movements">Movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eine_kleine_Nachtmusik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work has four <a href="/wiki/Movement_(music)" title="Movement (music)">movements</a>:</p>
<div>
<ol style="list-style-type:upper-roman">

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegro">Allegro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eine_kleine_Nachtmusik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Allegro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>This first movement is in <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata-allegro form</a>. It opens with an ascending <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_school#Musical_innovations" title="Mannheim school">Mannheim rocket</a> theme. The second theme is more graceful and in <a href="/wiki/D_major" title="D major">D major</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominant_key" title="Dominant key">dominant key</a> of <a href="/wiki/G_major" title="G major">G major</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Exposition_(music)" title="Exposition (music)">exposition</a> closes in D major and is repeated. The <a href="/wiki/Musical_development" title="Musical development">development</a> section begins on D major and touches on <a href="/wiki/D_minor" title="D minor">D minor</a> and <a href="/wiki/C_major" title="C major">C major</a> before the work returns to G major for the <a href="/wiki/Recapitulation_(music)" title="Recapitulation (music)">recapitulation</a>. There is a brief <a href="/wiki/Coda_(music)" title="Coda (music)">coda</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanze">Romanze</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eine_kleine_Nachtmusik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Romanze">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second movement, with the tempo marked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andante" title="Andante">Andante</a>, is a "Romanze" in the subdominant key of <a href="/wiki/C_major" title="C major">C major</a>. A feeling of intimacy and tenderness prevails throughout this movement. It is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rondo_form" title="Rondo form">rondo form</a>, taking the shape AâBâAâCâA plus a final <a href="/wiki/Coda_(music)" title="Coda (music)">coda</a>. The keys of the sections are C major for A and B, C minor for C. The middle appearance of A is truncated, consisting of only the first half of the theme. Heartz describes the movement as evoking <a href="/wiki/Gavotte" title="Gavotte">gavotte</a> rhythm: each of its sections begins in the middle of the measure, with a double upbeat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Menuetto">Menuetto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eine_kleine_Nachtmusik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Menuetto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The third movement, marked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allegretto" title="Allegretto">Allegretto</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Minuet" title="Minuet">minuet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ternary_form" title="Ternary form">trio</a>, both in <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><b>3</b><br/>
<b>4</b></span></span></span> time. The minuet is in the home key of G major, the contrasting trio in the dominant key of D major. As is normal in this form, the minuet is played again <a href="/wiki/Da_capo" title="Da capo">da capo</a> following the trio.</p>

<p>The fourth and last movement is in lively <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, marked Allegro; the key is again G major. The movement is written in <a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata form</a>. Mozart specifies repeats not just for the exposition section but also for the following <a href="/wiki/Musical_development" title="Musical development">development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recapitulation_(music)" title="Recapitulation (music)">recapitulation</a> section. The work ends with a long coda.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_extra_movement">Possible extra movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eine_kleine_Nachtmusik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Possible extra movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the catalog entry mentioned <a href="#catalog">above</a>, Mozart listed the work as having five movements ("Allegro â Minuet and Trio â Romance â Minuet and Trio â Finale.").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaslaw_and_Cowdery_1991.2C_250_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zaslaw_and_Cowdery_1991.2C_250-8">[8]</a></sup> The second movement in his listing, a minuet and trio, was long thought lost and no one knows if it was Mozart or someone else who removed it. Musicologist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Einstein" title="Alfred Einstein">Alfred Einstein</a> suggested, however, that a minuet in <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_in_B-flat_major,_K._498a" title="Piano Sonata in B-flat major, K. 498a">Piano Sonata in B-flat major, K. 498a</a>, is the missing movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The sonata's minuet has been recorded in an arrangement for strings made by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Del_Mar" title="Jonathan Del Mar">Jonathan Del Mar</a> for <a href="/wiki/Nimbus_Records" title="Nimbus Records">Nimbus Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
