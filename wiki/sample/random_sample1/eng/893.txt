<p><b>Crass</b> were an English collective and <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> band formed in 1977<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERimbaud199969_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERimbaud199969-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which promoted <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a> as a political ideology, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lifestylism" title="Lifestylism">way of life</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance movement</a>. Crass popularised the <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-punk" title="Anarcho-punk">anarcho-punk</a> movement of the <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punk subculture</a>, advocating <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>. The band used and advocated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DIY_punk_ethic" title="DIY punk ethic">DIY punk ethic</a> approach to its <a href="/wiki/Sound_collage" title="Sound collage">sound collages</a>, leaflets, albums and films.</p>
<p>Crass spray-painted stencilled <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> messages in the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> system and on advertising billboards, coordinated <a href="/wiki/Squatting" title="Squatting">squats</a> and organised political action. The band expressed its ideals by dressing in black, military-surplus-style clothing and using a stage backdrop amalgamating <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icons</a> of perceived authority such as the <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">Christian cross</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Union_Jack" title="Union Jack">Union Jack</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ouroboros" title="Ouroboros">ouroboros</a>.</p>
<p>The band was critical of punk subculture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Youth_culture" title="Youth culture">youth culture</a> in general. Crass promoted an anarchism which became more common in the punk-music scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They are considered <a href="/wiki/Art_punk" title="Art punk">art punk</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-5">[5]</a></sup> in their use of tape collages, graphics, spoken word releases, poetry and improvisation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1977:_Origins">1977: Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1977: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crass_steve_1981.jpg"><img alt="Steve Ignorant onstage, June 1981" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="487" data-file-width="612" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Crass_steve_1981.jpg/220px-Crass_steve_1981.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Crass_steve_1981.jpg/330px-Crass_steve_1981.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Crass_steve_1981.jpg/440px-Crass_steve_1981.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The band was based around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dial_House_(Essex,_England)" title="Dial House (Essex, England)">Dial House</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Commune" title="Commune">open-house community</a> near <a href="/wiki/Epping,_Essex" title="Epping, Essex">Epping</a>, Essex, and formed when Dial House founder <a href="/wiki/Penny_Rimbaud" title="Penny Rimbaud">Penny Rimbaud</a> began jamming with <a href="/wiki/Steve_Ignorant" title="Steve Ignorant">Steve Ignorant</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> (who was staying in the house at the time). Ignorant was inspired to form a band after seeing <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a> perform at Colston Hall in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> whilst Rimbaud, a veteran of avant garde <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a> groups such as <a href="/wiki/EXIT_(performance_art_group)" title="EXIT (performance art group)">EXIT</a> and Ceres Confusion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> was working on his book <i>Reality Asylum</i>. They produced "So What?" and "Do They Owe Us A Living?" as a drum-and-vocal duo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They briefly called themselves Stormtrooper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200714_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200714-10">[10]</a></sup> before choosing Crass in reference to a line in the <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Ziggy_Stardust_(song)" title="Ziggy Stardust (song)">Ziggy Stardust</a>" ("The kids was just crass").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERimbaud199999_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERimbaud199999-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other friends and household members joined (including <a href="/wiki/Gee_Vaucher" title="Gee Vaucher">Gee Vaucher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Wright_(musician)" title="Pete Wright (musician)">Pete Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/N._A._Palmer" title="N. A. Palmer">N. A. Palmer</a> and Steve Herman), and Crass played their first live gig at a squatted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_party" title="Street party">street festival</a> in Huntley Street, <a href="/wiki/North_London" title="North London">North London</a>. They planned to play five songs, but a neighbour "pulled the plug" after three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger200683_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger200683-12">[12]</a></sup> Guitarist Steve Herman left the band soon afterwards, and was replaced by Phil Clancey, aka <a href="/wiki/Phil_Free" title="Phil Free">Phil Free</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger200686_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger200686-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joy_De_Vivre" title="Joy De Vivre">Joy De Vivre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eve_Libertine" title="Eve Libertine">Eve Libertine</a> also joined around this time. Other early Crass performances included a four-date tour of New York City,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger200693_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger200693-14">[14]</a></sup> a festival gig in <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-punk77.co.uk_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-punk77.co.uk-15">[15]</a></sup> and regular appearances with the <a href="/wiki/U.K._Subs" title="U.K. Subs">U.K. Subs</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_White_Lion,_Putney" title="The White Lion, Putney">The White Lion, Putney</a> and Action Space in central London. The latter performances were often poorly attended: "The audience consisted mostly of us when the Subs played and the Subs when we played".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rimbaudsleevenote_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rimbaudsleevenote-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crass played two gigs at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roxy_Club" title="Roxy Club">Roxy Club</a> in Covent Garden, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-punk77.co.uk_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-punk77.co.uk-15">[15]</a></sup> According to Rimbaud, the band arrived drunk at the second show and were ejected from the stage; this inspired their song, "Banned from the Roxy",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and Rimbaud's essay for Crass' self-published magazine <i>International Anthem</i>, "Crass at the Roxy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> After the incident the band took themselves more seriously, avoiding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcohol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a> before shows and wearing black, <a href="/wiki/Military_surplus" title="Military surplus">military surplus</a>-style clothing on and offstage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006103_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006103-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Crass_logo.png"><img alt="Crass logo" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="376" data-file-width="376" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/The_Crass_logo.png/150px-The_Crass_logo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/The_Crass_logo.png/225px-The_Crass_logo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/The_Crass_logo.png/300px-The_Crass_logo.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Crass_logo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Crass logo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>They introduced their stage backdrop, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logotype" title="Logotype">logo</a> designed by Rimbaud's friend Dave King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200723_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200723-20">[20]</a></sup> This gave the band a <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">militaristic</a> image, which led to accusations of fascism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006104_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006104-21">[21]</a></sup> Crass countered that their uniform appearance was intended to be a statement against the "<a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a>", so (in contrast to many rock bands) no member would be identified as the "leader".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006104_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006104-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conceived and intended as cover artwork for a self-published pamphlet version of Rimbaud's <i>Christ's Reality Asylum</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200713_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200713-22">[22]</a></sup> the Crass logo was an amalgam of several "<a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icons</a> of authority" including the <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">Christian cross</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Union_Jack" title="Union Jack">Union Jack</a> and a two-headed <a href="/wiki/Ouroboros" title="Ouroboros">Ouroboros</a> (symbolising the idea that power will eventually destroy itself).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERimbaud199990_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERimbaud199990-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Using such deliberately mixed messages was part of Crass' strategy of presenting themselves as a "barrage of contradictions",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> challenging audiences to (in Rimbaud's words) "make your own fucking minds up".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcKay199690_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKay199690-26">[26]</a></sup> This included using loud, aggressive music to promote a <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifist</a> message,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcKay199689_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKay199689-27">[27]</a></sup> a reference to their <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dadaist</a>, performance-art backgrounds and <a href="/wiki/Situationist_International" title="Situationist International">situationist</a> ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcKay199688_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKay199688-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band eschewed elaborate <a href="/wiki/Stage_lighting" title="Stage lighting">stage lighting</a> during live sets, preferring to play under 40-watt household light bulbs; the technical difficulties of filming under such lighting conditions partly explains why there is little live footage of Crass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006108_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006108-29">[29]</a></sup> They pioneered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multimedia_art" title="Multimedia art">multimedia</a> presentation, using video technology (back-projected films and <a href="/wiki/Video_art" title="Video art">video collages</a> by Mick Duffield and <a href="/wiki/Gee_Vaucher" title="Gee Vaucher">Gee Vaucher</a>) to enhance their performances, and also distributed leaflets and handouts explaining anarchist ideas to their audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006185_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006185-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1978.E2.80.931979:_The_Feeding_of_the_5000_and_Crass_Records">1978–1979: <i>The Feeding of the 5000</i> and Crass Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1978–1979: The Feeding of the 5000 and Crass Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crass_Records" title="Crass Records">Crass Records</a></div>
<p>Crass' first release was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Feeding_of_the_5000_(album)" title="The Feeding of the 5000 (album)">The Feeding of the 5000</a></i> (an 18-track, 12" <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">45 rpm</a> EP on the <a href="/wiki/Small_Wonder_Records" title="Small Wonder Records">Small Wonder</a> label) in 1978. Workers at the record-pressing plant refused to handle it due to the allegedly <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy" title="Blasphemy">blasphemous</a> content of the song "Asylum",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and the record was released without it. In its place were two minutes of silence, entitled "The Sound of Free Speech". This incident prompted Crass to set up their own <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">independent record label</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crass_Records" title="Crass Records">Crass Records</a>, to prevent Small Wonder from being placed in a compromising position and to retain editorial control over their material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A re-recorded, extended version of "Asylum", renamed "Reality Asylum", was shortly afterwards released on Crass Records as a <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">7</a>" single and Crass were investigated by the police due to the song's lyrics. The band were interviewed at their Dial House home by <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a>'s vice squad, and threatened with prosecution; however, the case was dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rimbaudsleevenote_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-rimbaudsleevenote-16">[16]</a></sup> "Reality Asylum" retailed at 45<a href="/wiki/Coins_of_the_pound_sterling" title="Coins of the pound sterling">p</a> (when most other singles cost about 90p),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006137_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006137-34">[34]</a></sup> and was the first example of Crass' "pay no more than..." policy: issuing records as inexpensively as possible. The band failed to factor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_added_tax" title="Value added tax">value added tax</a> into their expenses, causing them to lose money on every copy sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006138_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006138-35">[35]</a></sup> A year later Crass Records released new pressings of "The Feeding of the 5000" (subtitled "The Second Sitting"), restoring the original version of "Asylum".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980:_Stations_of_the_Crass_and_Bloody_Revolutions">1980: <i>Stations of the Crass</i> and <i>Bloody Revolutions</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1980: Stations of the Crass and Bloody Revolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crass3.jpg"><img alt="Guitarist and singer on stage" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="260" data-file-width="326" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Crass3.jpg/220px-Crass3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8d/Crass3.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crass3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Crass, 1981; <a href="/wiki/N._A._Palmer" title="N. A. Palmer">N. A. Palmer</a> (left) and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Ignorant" title="Steve Ignorant">Steve Ignorant</a> pictured at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digbeth_Civic_Hall" title="Digbeth Civic Hall">Digbeth Civic Hall</a>, Birmingham</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1979 the band released their second album (<i><a href="/wiki/Stations_of_the_Crass" title="Stations of the Crass">Stations of the Crass</a></i>), financed with a loan from <a href="/wiki/Poison_Girls" title="Poison Girls">Poison Girls</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> a band with whom they regularly appeared. This was a double album, with three sides of new material and a fourth side recorded live at the Pied Bull in <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a>.</p>
<p>The next Crass single, 1980's "Bloody Revolutions", was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fund_raising" title="Fund raising">benefit</a> release with Poison Girls which raised £20,000 to fund the <a href="/wiki/Wapping_Autonomy_Centre" title="Wapping Autonomy Centre">Wapping Autonomy Centre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200723_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200723-20">[20]</a></sup> The words were a critique (from an anarchist-pacifist perspective) of the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> view of <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolutionary struggle</a>, and were (in part) a response to violence marring a gig at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conway_Hall" title="Conway Hall">Conway Hall</a> in London's <a href="/wiki/Red_Lion_Square" title="Red Lion Square">Red Lion Square</a> at which both bands performed in September 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006169_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006169-37">[37]</a></sup> The show was intended as a benefit for the so-called "Persons Unknown", a group of anarchists facing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(crime)" title="Conspiracy (crime)">conspiracy</a> charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006145_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006145-38">[38]</a></sup> During the performance <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_(UK)" title="Socialist Workers Party (UK)">Socialist Workers Party</a> supporters and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-fascists" title="Anti-fascists">anti-fascists</a> attacked <a href="/wiki/British_Movement" title="British Movement">British Movement</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Nazi" title="Neo-Nazi">neo-Nazis</a>, triggering violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Crass afterwards argued that the leftists were largely to blame for the fighting, and organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Rock_Against_Racism" title="Rock Against Racism">Rock Against Racism</a> were causing audiences to become polarised into left- and right-wing factions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Others (including the anarchist organisation <a href="/wiki/Class_War" title="Class War">Class War</a>) were critical of Crass's position, stating that "like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kropotkin" title="Kropotkin">Kropotkin</a>, their politics are up shit creek".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Many of the band's punk followers felt that they failed to understand the violence to which they were subjected from the right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006149_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006149-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crass_Joy.jpg"><img alt="Crass singer Joy De Vivre" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="590" data-file-width="566" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Crass_Joy.jpg/170px-Crass_Joy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Crass_Joy.jpg/255px-Crass_Joy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Crass_Joy.jpg/340px-Crass_Joy.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crass_Joy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Crass singer <a href="/wiki/Joy_De_Vivre" title="Joy De Vivre">Joy De Vivre</a>, 1984</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>"Rival Tribal Rebel Revel", a <a href="/wiki/Flexi_disc" title="Flexi disc">flexi disc</a> single given away with the <i>Toxic Grafity</i> [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] <a href="/wiki/Fanzine" title="Fanzine">fanzine</a>, was also a commentary about the events at Conway Hall attacking the mindless violence and <a href="/wiki/Tribalism" title="Tribalism">tribalistic</a> aspects of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Youth_culture" title="Youth culture">youth culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This was followed by the single, "Nagasaki Nightmare/Big A Little A". The strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear" title="Anti-nuclear">anti-nuclear</a> lyrics of the first song were reinforced by the fold-out-sleeve artwork. It featured an article by Mike Holderness of <i><a href="/wiki/Peace_News" title="Peace News">Peace News</a></i> magazine connecting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_power" title="Atomic power">atomic power</a> industry and the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and a large poster-style map of nuclear installations in the UK. The other side of the record, "Big A Little A", was a statement of the band's anti-statist and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchist" title="Individualist anarchist">individualist anarchist</a> philosophy:</p>
<p>"Be exactly who you want to be, do what you want to do / I am he and she is she but you're the only you" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1981:_Penis_Envy">1981: <i>Penis Envy</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1981: Penis Envy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png"><img alt="Magazine record ad" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="439" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png/150px-Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png/225px-Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png/300px-Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crass_in_Loving_magazine.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Loving</i> ad for "Our Wedding"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Crass released their third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Penis_Envy_(album)" title="Penis Envy (album)">Penis Envy</a></i>, in 1981. This marked a departure from the hardcore-punk image <i>The Feeding of the 5000</i> and <i>Stations of the Crass</i> had given the group. It featured more-complex musical arrangements and female vocals by <a href="/wiki/Eve_Libertine" title="Eve Libertine">Eve Libertine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joy_De_Vivre" title="Joy De Vivre">Joy De Vivre</a> (singer Steve Ignorant was credited as "not on this recording"). The album addressed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> issues, attacking marriage and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_repression" title="Sexual repression">sexual repression</a>.</p>
<p>The last track on <i><a href="/wiki/Penis_Envy_(album)" title="Penis Envy (album)">Penis Envy</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Middle_of_the_road_(music)" title="Middle of the road (music)">MOR</a> <a href="/wiki/Love_song" title="Love song">love song</a> entitled "Our Wedding", was made available as a white <a href="/wiki/Flexi_disc" title="Flexi disc">flexi disc</a> to readers of <i>Loving</i>, a teenage romance magazine. Crass tricked the magazine into offering the disc, posing as "Creative Recording And Sound Services". <i>Loving</i> accepted the offer, telling their readers that the free Crass flexi would make "your wedding day just that bit extra special".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid</a> controversy resulted when the hoax was exposed, with the <i><a href="/wiki/News_of_the_World" title="News of the World">News of the World</a></i> stating that the title of the flexi's originating album was "too obscene to print".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Despite <i>Loving</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s annoyance, Crass had broken no laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album was banned by the retailer HMV,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud-49">[49]</a></sup> and in 1984 copies of the album were seized from the Eastern Bloc record shop by <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester_Police" title="Greater Manchester Police">Greater Manchester Police</a> under the direction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Constable" title="Chief Constable">Chief Constable</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Anderton" title="James Anderton">James Anderton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The shop owners were charged with displaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obscene_Publications_Act" title="Obscene Publications Act">"obscene articles for publication for gain"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The judge ruled against Crass in the ensuing court case, although the decision was overturned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal" title="Court of Appeal">Court of Appeal</a> (except the lyrics to one song, "Bata Motel", which were upheld as "sexually provocative and obscene").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_a_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud_a-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1982.E2.80.931983:_Christ.C2.A0.E2.80.93_The_Album_and_strategy_change"><span id="1982.E2.80.931983:_.27.27Christ.C2.A0.E2.80.93_The_Album.27.27_and_a_change_of_strategy"></span>1982–1983: <i>Christ – The Album</i> and strategy change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1982–1983: Christ – The Album and strategy change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg"><img alt="Steve Ignorant and N.A. Palmer" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="375" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg/200px-Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg/300px-Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/48/Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crass_steve_dec1981.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>The band's fourth LP, 1982's double set <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ_-_The_Album" title="Christ - The Album">Christ - The Album</a></i>, took almost a year to record, produce and mix (during which the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> broke out and ended). This caused Crass to question their approach to making records. As a group whose primary purpose was <a href="/wiki/Political_criticism" title="Political criticism">political commentary</a>, they felt overtaken and redundant by world events:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"The speed with which the Falklands War was played out and the devastation that Thatcher was creating both at home and abroad, forced us to respond far faster than we had ever needed to before. <i>Christ – The Album</i> had taken so long to produce that some of the songs in it, songs that warned of the imminence of riots and war, had become almost redundant. Toxteth, Bristol, Brixton and the Falklands were ablaze by the time that we released. We felt embarrassed by our slowness, humbled by our inadequacy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Subsequent releases (including the singles "How Does It Feel? (to Be the Mother of a Thousand Dead)" and "Sheep Farming in the Falklands" and the album <i><a href="/wiki/Yes_Sir,_I_Will" title="Yes Sir, I Will">Yes Sir, I Will</a></i>) saw the band's sound go back to basics and were issued as "tactical responses" to political situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006220_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006220-53">[53]</a></sup> They anonymously produced 20,000 copies of a flexi-disc with a live recording of "Sheep Farming...", copies of which were randomly inserted into the sleeves of other records by sympathetic workers at the <a href="/wiki/Rough_Trade_Records" title="Rough Trade Records">Rough Trade Records</a> distribution warehouse to spread their views to those who might not otherwise hear them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006215_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006215-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_Action_and_internal_debates">Direct Action and internal debates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Direct Action and internal debates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stations.jpeg"><img alt="Album cover, featuring graffiti" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="221" data-file-width="306" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/34/Stations.jpeg/150px-Stations.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/34/Stations.jpeg/225px-Stations.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/34/Stations.jpeg/300px-Stations.jpeg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stations.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail from front cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Stations_of_the_Crass" title="Stations of the Crass">Stations of the Crass</a></i>, illustrating Crass' stenciled graffiti</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From their early days of spraying stencilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-war" title="Anti-war">anti-war</a>, anarchist, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-consumerist" title="Anti-consumerist">anti-consumerist</a> <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> messages in the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> and on billboards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcKay199687_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKay199687-55">[55]</a></sup> Crass was involved in politically motivated <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> and musical activities. On 18 December 1982, the band helped co-ordinate a 24-hour squat in the empty west London Zig Zag club to prove "that the underground punk scene could handle itself responsibly when it had to and that music really could be enjoyed free of the restraints imposed upon it by corporate industry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200725_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200725-56">[56]</a></sup> About 500 people attended; the bands playing (in running order) were Faction, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=D_and_V&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="D and V (page does not exist)">D and V</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omega_Tribe" title="Omega Tribe">Omega Tribe</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lack_of_Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lack of Knowledge (page does not exist)">Lack of Knowledge</a>, Sleeping Dogs, <a href="/wiki/The_Apostles" title="The Apostles">the Apostles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amebix" title="Amebix">Amebix</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Null_%26_Void&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Null &amp; Void (page does not exist)">Null &amp; Void</a>, Soldiers of Fortune, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mob_(British_punk_band)" title="The Mob (British punk band)">the Mob</a>, Polemic Attack, <a href="/wiki/Poison_Girls" title="Poison Girls">Poison Girls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conflict_(band)" title="Conflict (band)">Conflict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flux_of_Pink_Indians" title="Flux of Pink Indians">Flux of Pink Indians</a>, Crass and <a href="/wiki/DIRT_(band)" title="DIRT (band)">DIRT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200726_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200726-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1983 and 1984, Crass were part of the <a href="/wiki/Stop_the_City" title="Stop the City">Stop the City</a> actions co-ordinated by <a href="/wiki/London_Greenpeace" title="London Greenpeace">London Greenpeace</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006247_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006247-58">[58]</a></sup> which foreshadowed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-globalisation" title="Anti-globalisation">anti-globalisation</a> rallies of the early 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006248_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006248-59">[59]</a></sup> Support for these activities was provided in the lyrics and sleeve notes of the band's last single, "You're Already Dead", expressing doubts about their commitment to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-violence" title="Non-violence">non-violence</a>. It was also a reflection of disagreements within the group, as explained by Rimbaud; "Half the band supported the pacifist line and half supported direct and if necessary violent action. It was a confusing time for us, and I think a lot of our records show that, inadvertently".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcKay199699_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKay199699-60">[60]</a></sup> This led to introspection within the band, with some members becoming embittered and losing sight of their essentially positive stance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERimbaud1999249_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERimbaud1999249-61">[61]</a></sup> Reflecting this debate, the next release under the Crass name was <i><a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Love" title="Acts of Love">Acts of Love</a></i>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_classical_music" title="European classical music">classical-music</a> settings of 50 poems by Penny Rimbaud, described as "songs to my other self" and intended to celebrate "the profound sense of unity, peace and love that exists within that other self".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<p>Another Crass <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a> was known as the "<a href="/wiki/Thatchergate" title="Thatchergate">Thatchergate</a> tapes",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killyourpetpuppy-thatchergate_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-killyourpetpuppy-thatchergate-63">[63]</a></sup> a recording of an apparently accidentally overheard telephone conversation (due to crossed lines). The tape was constructed by Crass from edited recordings of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>. On the 'rather clumsily' forged tape, they appear to discuss the sinking of the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Sheffield_(D80)" title="HMS Sheffield (D80)">HMS <i>Sheffield</i></a> during the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> and agree that Europe would be a target for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> in a conflict between the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killyourpetpuppy-thatchergate_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-killyourpetpuppy-thatchergate-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Copies were leaked to the press via a Dutch news agency during the 1983 general election campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._State_Department" title="U.S. State Department">U.S. State Department</a> and British Government believed the tape to be propaganda produced by the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> (as reported by the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(UK)" title="The Sunday Times (UK)">The Sunday Times</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killyourpetpuppy-thatchergate_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-killyourpetpuppy-thatchergate-63">[63]</a></sup> Although the tape was produced anonymously, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> linked the tape with the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006238_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006238-66">[66]</a></sup> Previously classified government documents made public in January 2014 under the UK's '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Year_Rule" title="Thirty Year Rule">Thirty Year Rule</a>' reveal that the prime minister was personally aware of the tape and had discussed it with her cabinet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1984:_Breakup"><span id="1984:_Dissolution"></span>1984: Breakup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1984: Breakup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crass_evelibertine.png"><img alt="Eve Libertine, May 1984" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="894" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Crass_evelibertine.png/170px-Crass_evelibertine.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Crass_evelibertine.png/255px-Crass_evelibertine.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Crass_evelibertine.png/340px-Crass_evelibertine.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crass_evelibertine.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Eve Libertine, May 1984</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Crass had become a thorn in the side of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>'s government after the Falklands War. Questions about the band in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> and an attempted prosecution by <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament_(UK)" title="Member of Parliament (UK)">MP</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timothy_Eggar" title="Timothy Eggar">Timothy Eggar</a> under the UK's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obscene_Publications_Act" title="Obscene Publications Act">Obscene Publications Act</a> for their single, "How Does It Feel...",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> made them question their purpose:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"We found ourselves in a strange and frightening arena. We had wanted to make our views public, had wanted to share them with like minded people, but now those views were being analysed by those dark shadows who inhabited the corridors of power (…) We had gained a form of political power, found a voice, were being treated with a slightly awed respect, but was that really what we wanted? Was that what we had set out to achieve all those years ago?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The band had also incurred heavy legal expenses for the <i><a href="/wiki/Penis_Envy_(album)" title="Penis Envy (album)">Penis Envy</a></i> prosecution;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_a_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud_a-52">[52]</a></sup> this, combined with exhaustion and the pressures of living and operating together, finally took its toll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud-49">[49]</a></sup> On 7 July 1984 the band played a benefit gig at <a href="/wiki/Aberdare" title="Aberdare">Aberdare</a>, Wales, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1984-1985)" title="UK miners' strike (1984-1985)">striking miners</a>, and on the return trip guitarist <a href="/wiki/N._A._Palmer" title="N. A. Palmer">N. A. Palmer</a> announced that he intended to leave the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006254_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006254-69">[69]</a></sup> This confirmed Crass's previous intention to quit in 1984, and the band split up.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crass_Collective.2C_Crass_Agenda_and_Last_Amendment"><span id="2002_onwards:_The_Crass_Collective.2FCrass_Agenda.2FLast_Amendment"></span>Crass Collective, Crass Agenda and Last Amendment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Crass Collective, Crass Agenda and Last Amendment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2002 several former members arranged Your Country Needs You, a concert of "voices in opposition to war", as the <a href="/wiki/Last_Amendment" title="Last Amendment">Crass Collective</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_Hall" title="Queen Elizabeth Hall">Queen Elizabeth Hall</a> on London's <a href="/wiki/South_Bank" title="South Bank">South Bank</a>, Your Country Needs You included Benjamin Britten's <i><a href="/wiki/War_Requiem" title="War Requiem">War Requiem</a></i> and performances by <a href="/wiki/Goldblade" title="Goldblade">Goldblade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fun-Da-Mental" title="Fun-Da-Mental">Fun-Da-Mental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_MacKaye" title="Ian MacKaye">Ian MacKaye</a> and Pete Wright's post-Crass project, Judas 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In October 2003 the Crass Collective changed their name to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crass_Agenda" title="Crass Agenda">Crass Agenda</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> with Rimbaud, Libertine and Vaucher working with <a href="/wiki/Matt_Black_(DJ)" title="Matt Black (DJ)">Matt Black</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coldcut" title="Coldcut">Coldcut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> musicians such as <a href="/wiki/Julian_Siegel" title="Julian Siegel">Julian Siegel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Shortt" title="Kate Shortt">Kate Shortt</a>. In 2004 Crass Agenda spearheaded a campaign to save the <i><a href="/wiki/Vortex_Jazz_Club" title="Vortex Jazz Club">Vortex</a></i> Jazz Club in <a href="/wiki/Stoke_Newington" title="Stoke Newington">Stoke Newington</a>, north London<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> (where they regularly played). In June 2005 Crass Agenda was declared to be "no more", changing its name to the "more pertinent" Last Amendment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> After a five-year hiatus, Last Amendment performed at the Vortex in June 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Rimbaud has also performed and recorded with <a href="/wiki/Japanther" title="Japanther">Japanther</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Charlatans_(UK_band)" title="The Charlatans (UK band)">the Charlatans</a>. A "new" Crass track (a remix of 1982's "Major General Despair" with new lyrics), "The Unelected President", is available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007:_Ignorant.27s_The_Feeding_of_the_5000"><span id="2007:_The_Feeding_of_the_5000_.28revisited.29"></span>2007: Ignorant's <i>The Feeding of the 5000</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2007: Ignorant's The Feeding of the 5000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg"><img alt="Concert poster with photo of deformed, outstretched hand" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="374" data-file-width="265" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/06/Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg/150px-Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/06/Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg/225px-Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/06/Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ignorant_5000gig_nov2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Poster for the <i>5000</i> performance, November 2007</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 24 and 25 November 2007, Steve Ignorant performed Crass' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Feeding_of_the_5000_(album)" title="The Feeding of the 5000 (album)">The Feeding of the 5000</a></i> album live at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepherds_Bush_Empire" title="Shepherds Bush Empire">Shepherds Bush Empire</a> with a band of "selected guests".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Other members of Crass were not involved in these concerts. Initially Rimbaud refused Ignorant permission to perform Crass songs he had written, but later changed his mind: "I acknowledge and respect Steve's right to do this, but I do regard it as a betrayal of the Crass ethos".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian07_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian07-78">[78]</a></sup> Ignorant had a different view: "I don't have to justify what I do...Plus, most of the lyrics are still relevant today. And remember that three-letter word, "fun"?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian07_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian07-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010:_Crassical_Collection_reissues"><span id="2010:_The_Crassical_Collection_reissues"></span>2010: Crassical Collection reissues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2010: Crassical Collection reissues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010 it was announced that Crass would release <i>The Crassical Collection</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> remastered reissues of their back catalogue. Three former members objected, threatening legal action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Despite their concerns the project went ahead, and the remasters were eventually released. First in the series was <i>The Feeding of the 5000</i>, released in August 2010. <i>Stations of the Crass</i> followed in October, with new editions of <i>Penis Envy</i>, <i>Christ – The Album</i>, <i>Yes Sir, I Will</i> and <i>Ten Notes on a Summer's Day</i> released in 2011 and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Critics praised the improved sound quality and new packaging of the remastered albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011:_The_Last_Supper">2011: The Last Supper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2011: The Last Supper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011 Steve Ignorant embarked on an international tour, entitled "The Last Supper". He performed Crass material, culminating with a final performance at the Shepherds Bush Empire on 19 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steveignorantblogpost_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-steveignorantblogpost-86">[86]</a></sup> Ignorant said that this was the last time he would sing the songs of Crass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> with Rimbaud's support; the latter joined him onstage for a drum-and-vocal rendition of "Do They Owe Us A Living", bringing the band's career full circle after 34 years: "And then Penny came on...and we did it, 'Do They Owe Us A Living' as we'd first done it all those years ago. As it started, so it finished".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steveignorantblogpost_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-steveignorantblogpost-86">[86]</a></sup> Ignorant's lineup for the tour were <a href="/wiki/Gizz_Butt" title="Gizz Butt">Gizz Butt</a>, Carol Hodge, Pete Wilson and Spike T. Smith, and he was joined by Eve Libertine for a number of songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steveignorantblogpost_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-steveignorantblogpost-86">[86]</a></sup> The set list included a cover of "West One (Shine on Me)" by <a href="/wiki/The_Ruts" title="The Ruts">The Ruts</a>, when Ignorant was joined onstage by the Norfolk-based <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">lifeboat</a> crew with whom he volunteers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steveignorantblogpost_86-3"><a href="#cite_note-steveignorantblogpost-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gee_Vaucher">Gee Vaucher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gee Vaucher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Artwork by Vaucher and Penny Rimbaud, including a recording of the original 'Thatchergate Tape', featured as part of the 'Peculiar People' show at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Focal_Point_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Focal Point Gallery (page does not exist)">Focal Point Gallery</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southend_on_Sea" title="Southend on Sea">Southend on Sea</a> during the Spring of 2016, part of a series of events celebrating the history of 'Radical Essex'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Vaucher's painting 'Oh America', featuring an image of the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a> hiding her face with her hands, was used as the front page of the UK <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a> newspaper to mark the election of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> as US President on November 9, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> From November 2016 to February 2017 the <a href="/wiki/Firstsite" title="Firstsite">Firstsite</a> art gallery in <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a>, hosted a retrospective of Gee Vaucher's artwork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_and_Influence">Influences and Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Influences and Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For Rimbaud the initial inspiration for founding Crass was the death of his friend <a href="/wiki/Wally_Hope" title="Wally Hope">Phil 'Wally Hope' Russell</a>, as detailed in his book <i>The Last of the Hippies: An Hysterical Romance</i>. Russell had been placed in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychiatric" title="Psychiatric">psychiatric</a> hospital after helping to set up the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stonehenge_free_festival" title="Stonehenge free festival">Stonehenge free festival</a> in 1974, and died shortly afterwards. Rimbaud believed that Russell was murdered by the State for political reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Band members have also cited influences ranging from <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situationism" title="Situationism">situationism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger200633_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger200633-92">[92]</a></sup> the poetry of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baudelaire" title="Baudelaire">Baudelaire</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> British working class '<a href="/wiki/Kitchen_sink_realism" title="Kitchen sink realism">kitchen sink</a>' literature and films such as <a href="/wiki/Kes_(film)" title="Kes (film)">Kes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> and the films of <a href="/wiki/Anthony_McCall" title="Anthony McCall">Anthony McCall</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger200633_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger200633-92">[92]</a></sup> (McCall's <i>Four Projected Movements</i> was shown as part of an early Crass performance).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerger2006146_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2006146-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crass have said that their musical influences were seldom drawn from rock, but more from classical music (particularly <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>, on whose work, Rimbaud states, some of Crass' riffs are based),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcKay199695_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKay199695-96">[96]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Free_jazz" title="Free jazz">free jazz</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_a_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud_a-52">[52]</a></sup> European <a href="/wiki/Atonality" title="Atonality">atonality</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_a_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud_a-52">[52]</a></sup> and avant-garde composers such as <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Stockhausen" title="Karlheinz Stockhausen">Karlheinz Stockhausen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rimbaud_a_52-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rimbaud_a-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crass influenced the anarchist movement in the UK, the US and beyond. The growth of anarcho-punk spurred interest in anarchist ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> The band have also claimed credit for revitalising the <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movement</a> and the UK <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a> during the late 1970s early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERimbaud1999109_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERimbaud1999109-99">[99]</a></sup> Others contend that they overestimated their influence, their radicalising effect on militants notwithstanding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> Crass' philosophical and aesthetic influences on 1980s punk bands were far-reaching, although few mimicked their later <a href="/wiki/Free_improvisation" title="Free improvisation">free-form</a> style (heard on <i>Yes Sir, I Will</i> and their final recording, <i>Ten Notes on a Summer's Day</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGlasper200717_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlasper200717-101">[101]</a></sup> Their painted and <a href="/wiki/Collage" title="Collage">collage</a> black-and-white record sleeves (by <a href="/wiki/Gee_Vaucher" title="Gee Vaucher">Gee Vaucher</a>) may have influenced later artists such as <a href="/wiki/Banksy" title="Banksy">Banksy</a> (with whom Vaucher collaborated)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Subvertising" title="Subvertising">subvertising</a> movement. <a href="/wiki/Anti-folk" title="Anti-folk">Anti-folk</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Lewis" title="Jeffrey Lewis">Jeffrey Lewis</a>'s 2007 album, <i><a href="/wiki/12_Crass_Songs" title="12 Crass Songs">12 Crass Songs</a></i>, features acoustic covers of Crass material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2011, artist <a href="/wiki/Toby_Mott" title="Toby Mott">Toby Mott</a> exhibited a portion of his Crass ephemera collection at the Roth Gallery in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> The exhibit featured artwork, albums (including 12" LPs and EPs), 7" singles from Crass Records and a complete set of Crass' self-published <a href="/wiki/Zine" title="Zine">zine</a>, <i>Inter-National Anthem</i>.</p>
