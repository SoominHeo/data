<p><b>Ayran</b>, <b>Doogh</b> or <b>Tan</b> (<a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <span lang="sq" xml:lang="sq"><i>Dhallë</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">دوغ</span>‎‎, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_language" title="Azerbaijani language">Azerbaijani</a>: <span lang="az" xml:lang="az"><i>ayran</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>: <span lang="hy" xml:lang="hy">թան</span> <i>tan</i>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">شنينة</span> <i>shinēna</i> <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>ayran</i></span>) is a cold <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a> beverage mixed with <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is popular in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gastro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gastro-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, Armenia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgystan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> North Caucasus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the Balkans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Afghanistan (by the Kirghiz)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and Lebanon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Its primary ingredients are water and yogurt, and ayran has been variously described as "diluted yogurt"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gastro_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-gastro-3">[3]</a></sup> and "a most refreshing drink made by mixing yogurt with iced water".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ayran is served chilled and often as an accompaniment to grilled meat or rice<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-culturesite_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-culturesite-10">[10]</a></sup> especially during summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar beverages include the Iranian <i><a href="/wiki/Doogh" title="Doogh">doogh</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-functionaldiary_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-functionaldiary-12">[12]</a></sup> but yogurt drinks are popular beyond the Middle East region—ayran has been likened by some to the South Asian <i><a href="/wiki/Lassi" title="Lassi">lassi</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ayran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Shirin Simmons, <i>doogh</i> has long been a popular drink and was consumed in ancient Persia (modern-day Iran).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Described by an 1886 source as a cold drink of curdled milk and water seasoned with mint,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> its name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> word for <a href="/wiki/Milking" title="Milking">milking</a>, <i>dooshidan</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-un-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Nevin Halıcı, <i>ayran</i> is a traditional Turkish drink and was consumed by nomadic Turks prior to 1000 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gastro_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-gastro-3">[3]</a></sup> According to Celalettin Koçak and Yahya Kemal Avşar (Professor of Food Engineering at <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_University" title="Mustafa Kemal University">Mustafa Kemal University</a>), <i>ayran</i> was first developed thousands of years ago by the <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6kt%C3%BCrks" title="Göktürks">Göktürks</a>, who would dilute bitter yogurt with water in an attempt to improve its flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a> 1000 CE Turkish dictionary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C4%ABw%C4%81n_ul-Lughat_al-Turk" title="Dīwān ul-Lughat al-Turk">Dīwān ul-Lughat al-Turk</a>, defines ayran as a "drink made out of milk."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etim_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-etim-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkish_national_drink_status">Turkish national drink status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ayran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Turkish national drink status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Recep_Tayyip_Erdo%C4%9Fan" title="Recep Tayyip Erdoğan">Recep Tayyip Erdoğan</a>, a Turkish politician who has held the posts of <a href="/wiki/President_of_Turkey" title="President of Turkey">President</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Turkey" title="Prime Minister of Turkey">Prime Minister</a>, has promoted ayran as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_liquors" title="List of national liquors">national drink</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erdogan_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-erdogan-19">[19]</a></sup> Speaking at a 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" title="World Health Organisation">WHO</a> Global Alcohol Policy Conference held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%B0stanbul" title="İstanbul">İstanbul</a>, Erdoğan contrasted ayran with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcohol</a>, which he suggested was a recent introduction to Turkey. Stating that in the early years of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Republic_of_Turkey" title="History of the Republic of Turkey">modern Turkish republic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a> 1920-1950), alcoholic beverages were "part of the radical top-down modernization program embarked upon by the elites,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erdogan_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-erdogan-19">[19]</a></sup> Erdoğan expressed regret that alcohol was widely promoted during this period even in school <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbooks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erdogan_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-erdogan-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Still, sales of ayran in Turkey may lag behind other <a href="/wiki/Non-alcoholic_beverage" title="Non-alcoholic beverage">non-alcoholic beverages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-sales-20">[20]</a></sup> According to a 2015 joint statement from the Soft Drink Producers Association, the Sparkling Water Producers Association, and the Milk Producers and Exporters Union of Turkey, ayran consumption during the holy month of <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a> has declined every year for the years 2010-15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sales_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-sales-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Turkey’s Customs and Trade Ministry imposed a 220,000 Turkish Lira fine (approx. $70,000) to state-owned Çaykur manufacturers for “insulting ayran” in one of their advertisement for iced tea, in which the protagonist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rap_music" title="Rap music">raps</a> that ayran makes him sleepy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fine_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-fine-21">[21]</a></sup> and halted advertisements of Çaykur's competing, ice-tea product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fine_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-fine-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">Salt</a> (and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">pepper</a>) is added, and dried <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mentha_pulegium" title="Mentha pulegium">pennyroyal</a> can be mixed in as well. One variation includes diced cucumbers to provide a crunchy texture to the beverage. Some varieties of <i>doogh</i> have carbonation.</p>
