<p>The <b>Icelandic Phallological Museum</b> (<a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>: <span lang="is" xml:lang="is"><i>Hið Íslenzka Reðasafn</i></span>), located in <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>, Iceland, houses the world's largest display of <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penises</a> and penile parts. The collection of 280 specimens from 93 species of animals includes 55 penises taken from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whales" title="Whales">whales</a>, 36 from <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a> and 118 from <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">land mammals</a>, allegedly including <a href="/wiki/Hulduf%C3%B3lk" title="Huldufólk">Huldufólk</a> (Icelandic elves) and <a href="/wiki/Troll" title="Troll">trolls</a>. In July 2011, the museum obtained its first <a href="/wiki/Human_penis" title="Human penis">human penis</a>, one of four promised by would-be donors. Its detachment from the donor's body did not go according to plan and it was reduced to a greyish-brown shriveled mass that was pickled in a jar of <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formalin</a>. The museum continues to search for "a younger and a bigger and better one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Founded in 1997 by since-then retired teacher Sigurður Hjartarson and now run by his son Hjörtur Gísli Sigurðsson, the museum grew out of an interest in penises that began during Sigurður's childhood when he was given a cattle whip made from a bull's penis. He obtained the organs of Icelandic animals from sources around the country, with acquisitions ranging from the 170 cm (67 in) front tip of a <a href="/wiki/Blue_whale_penis" title="Blue whale penis">blue whale penis</a> to the 2 mm (0.08 in) <a href="/wiki/Baculum" title="Baculum">baculum</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Hamster" title="Hamster">hamster</a>, which can only be seen with a magnifying glass. The museum claims that its collection includes the penises of elves and trolls, though, as Icelandic folklore portrays such creatures as being invisible, they cannot be seen. The collection also features phallic art and crafts such as lampshades made from the scrotums of bulls.</p>
<p>The museum has become a popular tourist attraction with thousands of visitors a year and has received international media attention, including a Canadian documentary film called <i>The Final Member</i>, which covers the museum's quest to obtain a human penis. According to its mission statement, the museum aims to enable "individuals to undertake serious study into the field of phallology in an organized, scientific fashion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metro-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Icelandic_Phallological_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="800" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg/200px-Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg/300px-Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg/400px-Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sigur%C3%B0ur_Hjartarson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sigurður Hjartarson, founder of the Icelandic Phallological Museum</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The museum's founder Sigurður Hjartarson worked as a teacher and principal for 37 years, teaching history and Spanish at Reykjavík's <a href="/wiki/Hamrahlid_College" title="Hamrahlid College">Hamrahlid College</a> before his retirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRO31May2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IRO31May2010-4">[4]</a></sup> As a child, he owned a bull's <a href="/wiki/Pizzle" title="Pizzle">pizzle</a>, which was given to him to use as a cattle whip. He began collecting penises after a friend heard the story of the bull's penis in 1974 and gave him four new ones, three of which Sigurður gave to friends. Acquaintances at whaling stations began bringing him <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whale_penis" title="Whale penis">whale penises</a> as well, and the collection grew from there, expanding through donations and acquisitions from various sources around Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organs of farm animals came from <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse" title="Slaughterhouse">slaughterhouses</a>, while fishermen supplied those of <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">pinnipeds</a> and the smaller whales. The penises of larger whales came from <a href="/wiki/Whaling_in_Iceland" title="Whaling in Iceland">commercial whaling stations</a>, although this source dried up after the <a href="/wiki/International_Whaling_Commission" title="International Whaling Commission">International Whaling Commission</a> implemented a global ban on commercial whaling in 1986. Sigurður was able to continue to collect whale penises by harvesting them from the 12–16 whales that fall victim to stranding on the Icelandic coast each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-5">[5]</a></sup> He also obtained the penis of a <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a> shot by fishermen who found the animal drifting on <a href="/wiki/Drift_ice" title="Drift ice">drift ice</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Westfjords" title="Westfjords">Westfjords</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sigurður was assisted by his family, though not without some occasional embarrassment. His daughter Þorgerður recalls that she was once sent to a slaughterhouse to collect a specimen but arrived just as the workers were taking a lunch break: "Someone asked, 'What's in the basket?' I had to say, 'I'm collecting a frozen goat penis.' After that I said, 'I will never collect for you again.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-6">[6]</a></sup> According to Sigurður, "Collecting penises is like collecting anything. You can never stop, you can never catch up, you can always get a new one, a better one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skoch_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skoch-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="center">
<div class="thumb tnone">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ochsenziemer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="2725" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Ochsenziemer.jpg/400px-Ochsenziemer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Ochsenziemer.jpg/600px-Ochsenziemer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Ochsenziemer.jpg/800px-Ochsenziemer.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ochsenziemer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A bull's penis similar to this one, intended for use as a <a href="/wiki/Bullwhip" title="Bullwhip">bullwhip</a>, was given to the museum's founder when he was a boy and sparked a lasting interest in penises.</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The collection was at first housed in Sigurður's office at the college until he retired from his teaching job. He decided, more as a hobby than a job, to put it on public display in Reykjavík and was awarded a grant from the city council of <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_kr%C3%B3na" title="Icelandic króna">ISK</a> 200,000 to support the opening of a museum in August 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters1999_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters1999-8">[8]</a></sup> By 2003, it was attracting 5,200 visitors a year, of which 4,200 were from abroad. He put the museum up for sale in 2003, but also offered it to the city of Reykjavík as a gift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, he was unsuccessful in obtaining financial support from the state or city. When he retired in 2004, he could no longer afford the rent on the museum's premises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="446" data-file-width="742" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg/220px-Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg/330px-Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg/440px-Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Husavik_Phallusmuseum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The former exhibition room in Húsavík, 2008</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After his retirement, he moved along with his collection to <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BAsav%C3%ADk" title="Húsavík">Húsavík</a>, a fishing village with a population of about 2,200 people located 298 miles (480 km) northeast of the capital. The museum was housed in a small building, formerly a restaurant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-10">[10]</a></sup> that was marked with a giant wooden penis and a stone phallus standing outside on the street. The village's inhabitants were at first skeptical of the new arrival, but came to accept it when they were persuaded that there was nothing pornographic about the museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 he handed over the collection to his son, Hjörtur Gísli Sigurðsson (described by <i><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)">Slate</a></i> as "the world's only hereditary penis-museum operator"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-6">[6]</a></sup>). It was relocated from Húsavík to Reykjavík's main shopping street at <a href="/wiki/Laugavegur_(Reykjav%C3%ADk)" title="Laugavegur (Reykjavík)">Laugavegur</a> 116.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Its former location in Húsavík is now home to <a href="/wiki/The_Exploration_Museum" title="The Exploration Museum">The Exploration Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> An offer from a wealthy German to buy the museum for ISK 30 million (US $232,000 / €186,000) and a proposal to move it to the United Kingdom were both turned down, as Hjörtur insists that "the museum has to be in Iceland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He intends to continue acquiring new penises because you can "always get a better, newer one ... a bigger size or better shape, you know?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cummins_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cummins-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iceland" title="University of Iceland">University of Iceland</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">anthropologist</a> Sigurjón Baldur Hafsteinsson, Icelanders' tolerance of the museum is an indicator of how Icelandic society has changed since the 1990s, when a newly elected <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal</a> government fostered a more open outlook on entertainment, creativity and tourism that has "enabled new ideas to emerge publicly".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skoch_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Skoch-7">[7]</a></sup> He has documented the significance of the museum's role in Icelandic culture in a book, <i>Phallological museum</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9783643904706">ISBN 9783643904706</a>) <a href="/wiki/OCLC" title="OCLC">OCLC</a> <a class="external text" href="//www.worldcat.org/oclc/888575994" rel="nofollow">888575994</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Icelandic_Phallological_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>According to the museum's website, the collection comprises 280 specimens from 93 species of animals. They range from some of the largest to some of the smallest penises in the animal world. Its largest exhibit is a portion of a blue whale's penis measuring 170 cm (67 in) long and weighing 70 kilograms (150 lb),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> which <i>Iceland Review</i> has dubbed "a real Moby Dick".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafsteinsson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hafsteinsson-17">[17]</a></sup> The specimen is just the tip, as the entire organ, when intact, would have been about 5 m (16 ft) long and weighed about 350–450 kilograms (770–990 lb). The baculum of a hamster, only 2 mm (0.08 in) long, is the smallest item in the collection and needs a magnifying glass to be viewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> Sigurður has described the collection as the product of "37 years of collecting penises. Somebody had to do it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skoch_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Skoch-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum also has a "folklore section" exhibiting mythological penises; its online catalogue lists specimens taken from elves, trolls, <a href="/wiki/Kelpie" title="Kelpie">kelpies</a>, and "The Nasty Ghost of <a href="/wiki/Sn%C3%A6fellsj%C3%B6kull" title="Snæfellsjökull">Snæfell</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catalogue_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Catalogue-18">[18]</a></sup> Sigurður says that the elf's penis, which the museum's catalogue describes as "unusually big and old", is among his favourites. It cannot be seen, as Icelandic folklore holds that elves and trolls are invisible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-10">[10]</a></sup> The folkloric penises also include those of a <a href="/wiki/Merman" title="Merman">merman</a>, a one-legged, one-armed and one-eyed monster called a Beach-Murmurer, an Enriching Beach Mouse (said to draw "money from the sea to enrich her owner"), and an <a href="/wiki/Yule_Lads" title="Yule Lads">Icelandic Christmas Lad</a> found dead at the foot of a mountain in 1985 and whose penis was presented to the museum by a former mayor of Reykjavík.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catalogue_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Catalogue-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum's website states that it enables "individuals to undertake serious study into the field of phallology in an organized, scientific fashion", giving due prominence to a field that until now has only been "a borderline field of study in other academic disciplines such as history, art, psychology, literature and other artistic fields like music and ballet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The museum aims to collect penis specimens from every <a href="/wiki/List_of_mammals_of_Iceland" title="List of mammals of Iceland">mammal in Iceland</a>. It also exhibits phallic artwork and penis-related objects or "phallobilia" such as lampshades made from the <a href="/wiki/Scrotum" title="Scrotum">scrotums</a> of bulls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metro_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metro-3">[3]</a></sup> Other exhibits range "from an 18th-century engraving depicting the circumcision of Christ to a 20th-century plastic penis pacifier."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hafsteinsson_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hafsteinsson-17">[17]</a></sup> Most of the collection has been donated, and the only purchase to date has been an <a href="/wiki/Penis#elephants" title="Penis">elephant's penis</a> measuring nearly 1 m (3.3 ft) long. The penises are either preserved in <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and displayed in jars or are dried and hung or mounted on the walls of the museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sigurður has used a variety of techniques to preserve the penises, including preservation in formaldehyde, pickling, drying, stuffing and salting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOL_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-IOL-20">[20]</a></sup> One particularly large penis taken from a bull has been converted into a walking stick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skoch_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Skoch-7">[7]</a></sup> Many of the museum's exhibits are illuminated by lamps made by Sigurður from rams' testicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOL_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-IOL-20">[20]</a></sup> Sigurður has also carved wooden phalluses, which can be found adorning various objects around the museum,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup> and has a bow tie decorated with images of phalluses that he wears on special occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Josh Schonwald of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon.com</a> described his impressions of the museum when he visited in 1998:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>They were hanging on the walls, stuffed in jars, displayed with curatorial love – dried penises, penises embalmed in formaldehyde, massive penises displayed like hunting trophies. A tanned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull%27s_penis" title="Bull's penis">bull's penis</a>, a smoked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse%27s_penis" title="Horse's penis">horse's penis</a>. There were runty, shriveled <a href="/wiki/Deer_penis" title="Deer penis">penises of reindeer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_penis" title="Fox penis">foxes</a>, minks and rats. There were seal and walrus penises with stiff <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penis_bone" title="Penis bone">penis bones</a> – ensuring a perpetually erect state. There was the Big Penis – a 3-foot-long blue whale penis (which could have been an oar for a canoe).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The museum is open every day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and by July 2011 was attracting up to 11,000 visitors annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup> Sixty percent of the museum's visitors are reported to be women,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> though according to the authors of the <i>Rough Guide to Iceland</i>, mentioning the museum "causes the staff at the tourist office to blush with embarrassment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rough_Guide_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rough_Guide-22">[22]</a></sup> The museum's guest book includes comments such as, "I've never seen so many penises–and I went to boarding school!" (from a New Zealand visitor), "They're bigger in the USA," (from someone from Wisconsin) and "Is there a vagina museum?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-6">[6]</a></sup> On this point, Sigurður has said, "I'm only collecting the male organ. Somebody else has to do the other job. I'd be interested in how they would preserve it. I think vaginas are better alive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nerve_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nerve-23">[23]</a></sup> (There is in fact a "Museum of Vaginal Imagination" in Rotterdam in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_penis">Human penis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Icelandic_Phallological_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Human penis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For many years, the museum sought to obtain a human penis. Sigurður was able to obtain human testicles and a foreskin from two separate donors;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> the foreskin was donated by Iceland's National Hospital after an emergency <a href="/wiki/Circumcision" title="Circumcision">circumcision</a> operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rough_Guide_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rough_Guide-22">[22]</a></sup> The museum also contains sculptures of 15 penises based on the <a href="/wiki/Iceland_national_handball_team" title="Iceland national handball team">Iceland national handball team</a>. As the team had <a href="/wiki/Handball_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_tournament" title="Handball at the 2008 Summer Olympics – Men's tournament">won the silver medal at the 2008 Beijing Olympic Games</a>, the penises were made from a silvered material. Sigurður claims that although they are not displayed in the same order as the individuals shown in the photograph that accompany them, "their wives would recognise them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup> According to <i>Slate</i>, these sculptures were created by Sigurður's daughter, Þorgerður Sigurðardóttir, and were based on her own experience rather than any knowledge of the team. The team's goalkeeper denies that the sculptures are casts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum has so far received pledges from four men—an Icelander, a German, an American and a Briton—to donate their penises. Canadian film-maker Zach Math comments that the American, Tom Mitchell, "is an ordinary guy but he has this quirk where he thinks of his penis as a separate entity from his body — Elmo. He has this dream that he wants it to be the most famous penis in the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hot_Docs_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hot_Docs-26">[26]</a></sup> According to Sigurður, Mitchell "wanted to have his penis cut off even during his lifetime and then visit the museum."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-10">[10]</a></sup> Mitchell sent a cast of his penis to serve as a substitute in the meantime,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> along with photographs of it dressed up as <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">Santa Claus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The donor also tattooed his penis with the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the United States">Stars and Stripes</a> to make it look more appealing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hot_Docs_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hot_Docs-26">[26]</a></sup> He says that "I've always thought it'd be really cool for my penis to be the first true penis celebrity" and has made it the star of its own comic book, <i>Elmo: Adventures of a Superhero Penis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Icelandic donor was a 95-year-old man from nearby <a href="/wiki/Akureyri" title="Akureyri">Akureyri</a> who was said to have been a womaniser in his youth and wanted to donate his penis to the museum to ensure his "eternal fame".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> Sigurður said that, even at the age of 95, the donor remained active, "both vertically and horizontally".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salon_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Salon-5">[5]</a></sup> However, the donor was said to be concerned that "his penis is shrinking as he gets older and he is worried it might not make a proper exhibit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> His penis was given priority over those of the non-Icelandic donors in accordance with the museum's mission to display the organs of <a href="/wiki/List_of_mammals_of_Iceland" title="List of mammals of Iceland">Icelandic mammals</a>. Removing and preserving it was not an easy proposition, as Sigurður explained: "The donor and the doctors are in agreement, it must be taken while the body is warm. Then bleed it and pump it up. If it cools you can't do anything, so [the donor] is eager to have it taken warm and treated to be preserved with dignity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOL_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-IOL-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2011, the Icelandic donor died and his penis was surgically removed so that it could be added to the museum's collection. The <a href="/wiki/Penectomy" title="Penectomy">penectomy</a> was not entirely successful and left the penis "a greyish-brown, shrivelled mass". According to Sigurður, "I should have stretched it and sewn it at the back to keep it in more or less a normal position". Instead, it "went directly into the formaldehyde". Although disappointed with the results, Sigurður expressed confidence that "I will get a younger and a bigger and better one soon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup> Visitors' most common reaction to the preserved human penis is "that it's very old, you know, a bit shrunken, and the male members [<i>sic</i>] say 'oh, I hope mine will not look like this when I get old.<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cummins_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cummins-14">[14]</a></sup> Sigurður has considered donating his own penis to the museum when he dies but said that it depends on his wife: "If she dies first, my specimen would go in here. If I die first, well I can't say. She might say no."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>American writer and actor <a href="/wiki/Jonah_Falcon" title="Jonah Falcon">Jonah Falcon</a>, known for his impressively sized penis, was invited by the museum via <i>The Huffington Post</i> to donate his member after his death. It was announced in May 2014 that Falcon had accepted the proposal, suggesting a display alongside a sperm whale to be called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jonah_and_the_Whale" title="Jonah and the Whale">Jonah and the whale</a>", after the biblical story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Icelandic_Phallological_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum is the subject of <i>The Final Member</i>, a film by Canadian documentarians Zach Math and Jonah Bekhor. It profiles Sigurður and his quest to obtain a human penis for the museum, telling the story of the American and Icelandic donors and examining the quasi-taboo nature of the museum's collection. Bekhor says: "I wouldn't say it's a <a href="/wiki/Rorschach_test" title="Rorschach test">Rorschach test</a>, but depending on how you react to it really says a lot about what your relationship is with that element of the human anatomy. It's a really interesting phenomenon and we're really curious to see how audiences respond." The film premiered on 1 May 2012 at the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Docs_Canadian_International_Documentary_Festival" title="Hot Docs Canadian International Documentary Festival">Hot Docs Canadian International Documentary Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CTV_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CTV-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
