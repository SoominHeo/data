<p>The <b>Iron Bridge</b> is a bridge that crosses the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shropshire,_England" title="Shropshire, England">Shropshire, England</a>. Opened in 1781, it was the first <a href="/wiki/Arch_bridge" title="Arch bridge">arch bridge</a> in the world to be made of <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>, and was greatly celebrated after construction owing to its use of the new material.</p>
<p>In 1934 it was designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_Monument" title="Scheduled Monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a> and closed to vehicular traffic. <a href="/wiki/Toll_bridge" title="Toll bridge">Tolls</a> for pedestrians were collected until 1950, when ownership of the bridge was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Shropshire_County_Council" title="Shropshire County Council">Shropshire County Council</a>. It now belongs to <a href="/wiki/Telford_and_Wrekin" title="Telford and Wrekin">Telford and Wrekin</a> Borough Council. The bridge, the adjacent settlement of <a href="/wiki/Ironbridge" title="Ironbridge">Ironbridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ironbridge_Gorge" title="Ironbridge Gorge">Ironbridge Gorge</a> form the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> Ironbridge Gorge <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. The bridge is a Grade I <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>, and a waypoint on the <a href="/wiki/South_Telford_Heritage_Trail" title="South Telford Heritage Trail">South Telford Heritage Trail</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Iron_Bridge_(Aerial).JPG"><img alt="An aerial photo of the bridge, showing the close proximity of the main road alongside the river and the many building dotted nearby." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/The_Iron_Bridge_%28Aerial%29.JPG/220px-The_Iron_Bridge_%28Aerial%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/The_Iron_Bridge_%28Aerial%29.JPG/330px-The_Iron_Bridge_%28Aerial%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/The_Iron_Bridge_%28Aerial%29.JPG/440px-The_Iron_Bridge_%28Aerial%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Iron_Bridge_(Aerial).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Iron Bridge from above</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Abraham_Darby_I" title="Abraham Darby I">Abraham Darby I</a> first smelted local <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> with <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a> coal in 1709, and in the coming decades <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a> became a centre for industry due to the low price of fuel from local mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-p3-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a> was used as a key trading route, but it was also a barrier to travel around the deep <a href="/wiki/Ironbridge_Gorge" title="Ironbridge Gorge">Severn Gorge</a>, especially between the then important industrial parishes of <a href="/wiki/Broseley" title="Broseley">Broseley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeley,_Shropshire" title="Madeley, Shropshire">Madeley</a>, the nearest bridge being at <a href="/wiki/Buildwas" title="Buildwas">Buildwas</a> two miles away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p3-4_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-p3-4-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-eh-3">[3]</a></sup> The use of the river by boat traffic and the steep sides of the gorge meant that any bridge should ideally be of a single span, and sufficiently high to allow tall ships to pass underneath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-eh-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bros_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bros-4">[4]</a></sup> The steepness and instability of the banks was problematic for building a bridge, and there was no point where roads on opposite sides of the river converged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p4-5_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-p4-5-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Iron Bridge was the first of its kind to be constructed, although not the first to be considered or the first iron bridge of any kind. An iron bridge was partly constructed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyons" title="Lyons">Lyons</a> in 1755, but was abandoned for reasons of cost,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and a 22.2 metres (73 ft) span <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> footbridge over an ornamental waterway was erected in Yorkshire in 1769.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p9_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-p9-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposal">Proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1773, architect <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Farnolls_Pritchard" title="Thomas Farnolls Pritchard">Thomas Farnolls Pritchard</a> wrote to his 'iron mad' friend and local <a href="/wiki/Ironmaster" title="Ironmaster">ironmaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkinson_(industrialist)" title="John Wilkinson (industrialist)">John Wilkinson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Broseley" title="Broseley">Broseley</a>, to suggest building a bridge out of <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ib1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ib1-8">[8]</a></sup> Pritchard had previous experience with the design of both wooden and stone bridges, and it is possible that he had integrated into these designs elements of iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p10-11_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-p10-11-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the winter of 1773–74, local newspapers advertised a proposal to petition Parliament for leave to construct an iron bridge with a single 120 feet (37 m) span.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECossonsTrinder200219_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECossonsTrinder200219-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1775, a subscription of between three and four thousand pounds was raised, and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Darby_III" title="Abraham Darby III">Abraham Darby III</a>, the grandson of Abraham Darby I and an ironmaster working at <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a>, was appointed treasurer to the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECossonsTrinder200219_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECossonsTrinder200219-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 1776, the Act to build a bridge received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a>. It had been drafted by Thomas Addenbrooke, secretary of the trustees, and John Harries a London barrister<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[i]</a></sup> then presented to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Great_Britain" title="House of Commons of Great Britain">House of Commons</a> by Charles Baldwyn, MP for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salop" title="Salop">Salop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECossonsTrinder200223_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECossonsTrinder200223-11">[11]</a></sup> Abraham Darby III was commissioned to <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">cast</a> and build the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-eh-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> In May 1776, the trustees withdrew Darby's commission, and instead advertised for plans for a single arch bridge to be built in "stone, brick or timber."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p15_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-p15-14">[13]</a></sup> No satisfactory proposal was made, and the trustees agreed to go with Pritchard's design, but there was continued uncertainty about the use of iron, and conditions were set on the cost and duration of the construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p15_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-p15-14">[13]</a></sup> In July 1777 the span of the bridge was decreased to 90 feet (27 m), and then increased again to 100 feet 6 inches (30.63 m), possibly in order to accommodate a <a href="/wiki/Towpath" title="Towpath">towpath</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p16_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-p16-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[ii]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IronBridge2.JPG"><img alt="A view looking North along the roadway across the bridge, the curve  of its arch obscuring much of the town of Ironbridge behind. <div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IronBridge2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The site, adjacent to where a ferry had run between <a href="/wiki/Madeley,_Shropshire" title="Madeley, Shropshire">Madeley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benthall,_Shropshire" title="Benthall, Shropshire">Benthall</a>, was chosen for its high approaches on each side and the relative solidity of the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-eh-3">[3]</a></sup> The Act of Parliament described how the bridge was to be built from a point in <a href="/wiki/Benthall,_Shropshire" title="Benthall, Shropshire">Benthall parish</a> near the house of Samuel Barnett to a point on the opposite shore near the house of Thomas Crumpton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p23_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-p23-17">[15]</a></sup> Pritchard died on 21 December 1777 in his tower-house at <a href="/wiki/Eyton_on_Severn" title="Eyton on Severn">Eyton on Severn</a>, only a month after work had begun, having been ill for over a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-eh-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ib1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ib1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p17_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-p17-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The masonry and abutments were constructed between 1777 and 1778, and the ribs were lifted into place in the summer of 1779.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s4_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-s4-19">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup> The bridge first spanned the river on 2 July 1779, and it was opened to traffic on 1 January 1781.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-21">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-do_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-do-22">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997 a small watercolour by <a href="/wiki/Elias_Martin" title="Elias Martin">Elias Martin</a> in a Stockholm museum, which shows the bridge under construction in 1779.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-b1-21">[19]</a></sup> A half-size replica of the main section of the bridge was built in 2001 as part of the research for the BBC <a href="/wiki/Timewatch" title="Timewatch">Timewatch</a> programme, which was shown the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-b1-21">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bridge is built from five cast iron ribs that give a span of 100 feet 6 inches (30.63 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup> Exactly 378 long tons 10 cwt (847,800 lb or 384.6 t) of iron was used in the construction of the bridge, and there are almost 1700 individual components, the heaviest weighing 5.5 long tons (5.6 t).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap7_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap7-24">[22]</a></sup> Components were cast individually to fit with each other, rather than being of standard sizes, with discrepancies of up to several centimeters between 'identical' components in different locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sean_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-sean-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decorative rings and <a href="/wiki/Ogee" title="Ogee">ogees</a> between the structural ribs of the bridge suggest that the final design was Pritchard's, as the same elements appear in a <a href="/wiki/Gazebo" title="Gazebo">gazebo</a> he rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s4_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-s4-19">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> A foreman at the foundry, Thomas Gregory, drew the detailed designs for the members, resulting in the use of carpentry jointing details such as <a href="/wiki/Mortise_and_tenon" title="Mortise and tenon">mortise and tenon</a> joints and <a href="/wiki/Dovetail_joint" title="Dovetail joint">dovetails</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-eh-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two supplemental arches, of similar cast iron construction, carry a towpath on the south bank and also act as <a href="/wiki/Flood_arch" title="Flood arch">flood arches</a>. A stone arch carries a small path on the north, town, bank.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">Cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darby had agreed to construct the bridge with a budget of <span style="white-space: nowrap">GB£3,250</span> and <span style="white-space: nowrap">GB£3,250</span> was raised by subscribers to the project, mostly from Broseley. While the actual cost of the bridge is unknown, contemporary records suggest it was as high as <span style="white-space: nowrap">GB£6,000</span>, the excess being born by Darby, who was highly indebted from other ventures as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p29_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-p29-28">[26]</a></sup> However, by the mid-1790s the bridge was highly profitable, and tolls were giving the shareholders an annual <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividend</a> of 8 per cent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p30_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-p30-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The opening of the bridge resulted in changes in the pattern of settlement in the Gorge, and roads around the bridge were improved in the years after its construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p31-33_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-p31-33-30">[28]</a></sup> The town of <a href="/wiki/Ironbridge" title="Ironbridge">Ironbridge</a>, taking its name from the bridge, developed at the northern end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p31-33_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-p31-33-30">[28]</a></sup> The trustees, as well as local hotel keepers and coach operators, promoted interest in the bridge among members of polite society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p31-33_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-p31-33-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repairs">Repairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Repairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 1783, a 35-yard (32 m) wall was built in order to prevent the north bank from slipping into the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p47_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-p47-31">[29]</a></sup> Cracks were found in the stone land arch on the south side in December 1784, and the neighbouring abutment showed signs of movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup> The Gorge is very prone to landslides, and over 20 are recorded in the <a href="/wiki/British_Geological_Survey" title="British Geological Survey">British Geological Survey</a>'s National Landslide Database in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> It was suspected that the sides of the gorge were moving towards the river, forcing the feet of the arch towards each other, and consequently repairs were carried out in 1784, 1791 and 1792.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p47_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-p47-31">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was the only bridge on the River Severn to survive the flood of February 1795 undamaged, due to its strength and small profile against the floodwaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> The medieval bridge at <a href="/wiki/Buildwas" title="Buildwas">Buildwas</a> was replaced with a cast iron bridge by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a>, which, by virtue of superior design, required half the quantity of iron despite a longer span of 39 metres (128 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p272_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-p272-34">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> The Buildwas bridge survived until 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p272_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-p272-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1800 the trustees commissioned repairs which lasted for several years, which involved the replacement of the stone land arches with wooden ones to relieve pressure on the main span.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p47_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-p47-31">[29]</a></sup> A proposal to build a rigid support between the abutments to keep them part was found to be impossible with the available technology, but was achieved during the later restoration of the bridge in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p48_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-p48-36">[34]</a></sup> In 1812, its construction was described as "very bad" by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hutton" title="Charles Hutton">Charles Hutton</a>, and he predicted that it would not last for long, "though not from any deficiency in the iron-work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> The timber arches were replaced with cast iron ones in December 1820, and further repairs were necessary throughout the remainder of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-7"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p50_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-p50-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 August 1902, a 30 feet (9.1 m) length of parapet collapsed into the river, and a section of deck plate weighing around 5 cwt fell from the bridge in July 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p50_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-p50-38">[36]</a></sup> The opening of a toll-free concrete bridge in 1909 caused concern among the trustees, but it continued to be used by vehicles and pedestrians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p50-51_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-p50-51-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closure">Closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>A 1923 report by <a href="/wiki/Mott,_Hay_and_Anderson" title="Mott, Hay and Anderson">Mott, Hay and Anderson</a> suggested that other than the paintwork, the main span of the bridge was in good condition. It was suggested that the metal deck of the bridge was dangerously heavy, and that after removing the dead weight the bridge should be reopened to vehicles no heavier than 2 tonnes (2.0 long tons; 2.2 short tons) and restricted to the centre of the roadway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p51_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-p51-41">[39]</a></sup> A weight limit of 4 tonnes (3.9 long tons; 4.4 short tons) was imposed, but the housing boom of the 1930s meant that drivers distributing tiles produced at <a href="/wiki/Jackfield" title="Jackfield">Jackfield</a> were insistent that they should be allowed to use the bridge, so the trustees took the decision to close the bridge to vehicular traffic with effect from 18 June 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p51_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-p51-41">[39]</a></sup> That same year, it was designated a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_Ancient_Monument" title="Scheduled Ancient Monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-8"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Toll_bridge" title="Toll bridge">Tolls</a> for pedestrians were collected until 1950, when ownership of the bridge was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Shropshire_County_Council" title="Shropshire County Council">Shropshire County Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap8_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap8-42">[40]</a></sup> The tolls collected only marginally covered the cost of collection, leaving no money for conservation, and the bridge had not been cleaned or painted for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p51_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-p51-41">[39]</a></sup> In 1956 the County Council made a proposal to demolish the bridge and replace it with a new one, but this plan did not come to fruition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bros_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bros-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Iron_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg/220px-The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg/330px-The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg/440px-The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_bridge_tollhouse_-_geograph.org.uk_-_267001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The brick tollhouse at one end of the bridge</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After negotiations to raise the required funds, a programme of repairs took place on the foundations of the bridge at a cost of <span style="white-space: nowrap">GB£147,000</span> between 1972 and 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup> The consulting engineers <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sandford,_Fawcett,_Wilton_and_Bell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sandford, Fawcett, Wilton and Bell (page does not exist)">Sandford, Fawcett, Wilton and Bell</a> elected to place <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">ferro-concrete</a> <a href="/wiki/Inverted_arch" title="Inverted arch">inverted arch</a> under the river to counter inward movement of the bridge abutments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_20-9"><a href="#cite_note-et-20">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p52_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-p52-44">[42]</a></sup> Construction of the arch was carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Tarmac_Group" title="Tarmac Group">Tarmac Construction Company</a> starting in the spring of 1973, but unusually high summer floods washed over the cofferdam, frustrating hopes that the work could be done in a single summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p53-54_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-p53-54-45">[43]</a></sup> Filling material was removed from the south abutment to reduce its weight, and the arch through it was reinforced with concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p52-53_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-p52-53-46">[44]</a></sup> The road surface was replaced with a lighter <a href="/wiki/Macadam" title="Macadam">macadam</a>, the stone of the abutments was renewed and the toll-house was restored as an information centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p54_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-p54-47">[45]</a></sup> In 1980, the structure was painted for the first time in the 20th century, and the work was complete for the bicentenary of the opening, which was celebrated with a <a href="/wiki/Pig_roast" title="Pig roast">pig roast</a> on 1 January 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p54-55_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-p54-55-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999–2000, the bridge was scaffolded to allow examination by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a>. The bridge was also repainted and minor repairs carried out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p55_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-p55-49">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>Over fifty painters and engravers came to the area around <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a> during the period 1750–1830 to witness and record the rise of industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s4_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-s4-19">[17]</a></sup> Possibly the first artist to depict the bridge was <a href="/wiki/William_Williams_(artist)" title="William Williams (artist)">William Williams</a>, who was paid 10 guineas in October 1780 by Darby for a "drawing" of the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p33-34_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-p33-34-50">[48]</a></sup> An engraving by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Angelo_Rooker" title="Michael Angelo Rooker">Michael Angelo Rooker</a> proved popular, and a copy was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p35-36_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-p35-36-51">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy</a> held an exhibition entitled "A View from the Iron Bridge" to commemorate the bicentenary of the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[50]</a></sup></p>
