<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Iodised salt</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Iodised salt</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-ise.2C_-ize_.28-isation.2C_-ization.29" title="American and British English spelling differences">also spelled</a> <b>iodized salt</b>) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_salt" title="Table salt">table salt</a> mixed with a minute amount of various salts of the element <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. The ingestion of iodine prevents <a href="/wiki/Iodine_deficiency" title="Iodine deficiency">iodine deficiency</a>. Worldwide, iodine deficiency affects about two billion people and is the leading preventable cause of <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_disability" title="Intellectual disability">intellectual and developmental disabilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Deficiency also causes <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> gland problems, including "endemic <a href="/wiki/Goitre" title="Goitre">goitre</a>". In many countries, iodine deficiency is a major public health problem that can be cheaply addressed by purposely adding small amounts of iodine to the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> salt.</p>
<p>Iodine is a <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrient</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral">dietary mineral</a> that is naturally present in the food supply in some regions, especially near sea coasts, but is generally quite rare in the Earth's crust, since iodine is a so-called "heavy" element and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abundance_of_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of chemical elements">abundance of chemical elements</a> generally declines with greater atomic mass. Where natural levels of iodine in the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> are low and the iodine is not taken up by vegetables, iodine added to salt provides the small but essential amount of iodine needed by humans.</p>
<p>An opened package of table salt with <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> may rapidly lose its iodine content through the process of oxidation and iodine <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry.2C_biochemistry_and_nutritional_aspects">Chemistry, biochemistry and nutritional aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemistry, biochemistry and nutritional aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iodised_salt.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Iodised_salt.JPG/220px-Iodised_salt.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Iodised_salt.JPG/330px-Iodised_salt.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Iodised_salt.JPG/440px-Iodised_salt.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iodised_salt.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A pile of iodised salt.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compounds</a> are used as iodide sources, depending on the producer: <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodate" title="Potassium iodate">potassium iodate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodide" title="Potassium iodide">potassium iodide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodate" title="Sodium iodate">sodium iodate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a>. Any of these compounds supplies the body with its iodine required for the biosynthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroxine" title="Thyroxine">thyroxine</a> (T<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Triiodothyronine" title="Triiodothyronine">triiodothyronine</a> (T<sub>3</sub>) hormones by the thyroid gland. Animals also benefit from iodine supplements, and the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_iodide" title="Hydrogen iodide">hydrogen iodide</a> derivative of <a href="/wiki/Ethylenediamine" title="Ethylenediamine">ethylenediamine</a> is the main supplement to livestock feed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Salt is an effective vehicle for distributing iodine to the public because it does not spoil and is consumed in more predictable amounts than most other commodities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, the concentration of iodine in salt has gradually increased in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>: 3.75 mg/kg in 1952, 7.5 mg/kg in 1962, 15 mg/kg in 1980, 20 mg/kg in 1998, and 25 mg/kg in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These increases were found to improve iodine status in the general Swiss population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Salt that is iodised with iodide may slowly lose its iodine content by exposure to excess air over long periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edible <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> can be iodised by spraying it with a potassium iodate or potassium iodide solution. 57 grams of potassium iodate, costing about US$1.15 (in 2006), is required to iodise a ton of salt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextrose" title="Dextrose">Dextrose</a> is added as a stabilizer to prevent potassium iodide from oxidizing and evaporating. Anti-caking agents such as calcium silicate are commonly added to table salt to prevent clumping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_public_health_initiatives">In public health initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In public health initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Worldwide, <a href="/wiki/Iodine_deficiency" title="Iodine deficiency">iodine deficiency</a> affects two billion people and is the leading preventable cause of <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_disability" title="Intellectual disability">intellectual and developmental disabilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> According to public health experts, iodisation of salt may be the world's simplest and most cost-effective measure available to improve health, only costing US$0.05 per person per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/World_Summit_for_Children" title="World Summit for Children">World Summit for Children</a> in 1990, a goal was set to eliminate iodine deficiency by 2000. At that time, 25% of households consumed iodised salt, a proportion that increased to 66% by 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Salt producers are often, although not always, supportive of government initiatives to iodise edible salt supplies. Opposition to iodisation comes from small salt producers who are concerned about the added expense, private makers of iodine pills, concerns about promoting salt intake, and unfounded rumours that iodisation causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> or other illnesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> recommends<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 150 <a href="/wiki/Microgram" title="Microgram">micrograms</a> (0.15 mg) of iodine per day for both men and women.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Australian children were identified as being iodine deficient is a survey conducted between 2003 and 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As a result of this study the Australian Government mandated that all bread with the exception of "organic " bread must use iodized salt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>There remains concern that this initiative is not sufficient for pregnant and lactating women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Iodine Deficiency Disorders were detected as a major public health issue by Brazilian authorities in the 1950s, when about 20% of the population had goiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The National Agency for Sanitary Vigilance (ANVISA) is responsible for setting the mandatory iodine content of table salt. The Brazilian diet averages 12 g of table salt per day, more than twice the recommended value of 5 g a day. To avoid excess consumption of iodine, the iodizing of Brazilian table salt was reduced to 15–45 mg/kg in July 2013. Specialists criticized the move, saying that it would be better for the government to promote reduced salt intake, which would solve the iodine problem as well as reduce the incidence of high blood pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of the Chinese population lives inland, far from sources of dietary iodine. In 1996, the Ministry of Public Health estimated that iodine deficiency was responsible for 10 million cases of mental retardation in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Chinese government had held a <a href="/wiki/Salt_in_Chinese_history" title="Salt in Chinese history">legal monopoly on salt production</a> since 119 BCE and began iodizing salt in the 1960s, but <a href="/wiki/Chinese_economic_reform" title="Chinese economic reform">market reforms</a> in the 1980s led to widespread smuggling of non-iodized salt from private producers. In the inland province of <a href="/wiki/Ningxia" title="Ningxia">Ningxia</a>, only 20% of salt consumed was sold by the <a href="/wiki/China_National_Salt_Industry_Corporation" title="China National Salt Industry Corporation">China National Salt Industry Corporation</a>. The Chinese government responded by cracking down on smuggled salt, establishing a salt police with 25,000 officers to enforce the salt monopoly. Consumption of iodized salt reached 90% of the Chinese population by 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kazakhstan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, a country in Central Eurasia in which local food supplies seldom contain sufficient iodine, has drastically reduced iodine deficiency through salt iodisation programmes. Campaigns by the government and non-profit organisations to educate the public about the benefits of iodised salt began in the mid 1990s, with iodisation of edible salt becoming legally mandatory in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcneil_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-mcneil-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the U.S. in the early 20th century, goitre was especially prevalent in the region around the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Murray_Cowie" title="David Murray Cowie">David Murray Cowie</a>, a professor of <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">paediatrics</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>, led the U.S. to adopt the <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> practice of adding <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodide" title="Potassium iodide">potassium iodide</a> to table and cooking salt. On May 1, 1924, iodised salt was sold commercially in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17796701_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17796701-18">[18]</a></sup> By the fall of 1924, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morton_Salt_Company" title="Morton Salt Company">Morton Salt Company</a> began distributing iodised salt nationally. There was a gradual increase in average intelligence of 1 standard deviation, 15 points, in iodine-deficient areas and 3.5 points nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BI72213_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BI72213-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 20, 1995, Philippine President Fidel V. Ramos signed Republic Act 8172: An Act for Salt Iodization Nationwide(ASIN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The South African government instructed that all salt for sale was to be iodised after December 12, 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="No-additive_salts_for_canning_and_pickling">No-additive salts for canning and pickling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: No-additive salts for canning and pickling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In contrast to table salt, which often has iodide as well as anticaking ingredients, special canning and <a href="/wiki/Pickling_salt" title="Pickling salt">pickling salt</a> is made for producing the brine to be used in pickling vegetables and other foodstuffs. This salt has no iodine added because the iodide can be oxidised by the foods and darken them—a harmless but aesthetically undesirable effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortification_of_salt_with_other_elements">Fortification of salt with other elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fortification of salt with other elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double-fortified_salt">Double-fortified salt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Double-fortified salt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Salt can also be double-fortified with iron and iodine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The iron is microencapsulated with stearin to prevent it from reacting with the iodine in the salt. By providing iron in addition to iodine in the convenient delivery vehicle of salt, it could serve as a sustainable approach to combating both iodine and iron deficiency disorders in areas where both deficiencies are prevalent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adding iron to iodised salt is complicated by a number of chemical, technical, and organoleptic issues. Since a viable DFS premix became available for scale-up in 2001, a body of scientific literature has been emerging to support the DFS initiative including studies conducted in Ghana, India, Côte d'Ivoire, Kenya and Morocco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluoridated_salt">Fluoridated salt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fluoridated salt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some countries, table salt is treated with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_fluoride" title="Potassium fluoride">potassium fluoride</a> to enhance dental health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aigueperse_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aigueperse-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diethylcarbamazine">Diethylcarbamazine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iodised_salt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Diethylcarbamazine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diethylcarbamazine" title="Diethylcarbamazine">diethylcarbamazine</a> has been added to salt to combat <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_filariasis" title="Lymphatic filariasis">lymphatic filariasis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
