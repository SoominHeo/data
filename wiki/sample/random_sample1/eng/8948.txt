<p><b>Potassium iodide</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a>, medication, and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">dietary supplement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-St2002-2">[2]</a></sup> As a medication it is used to treat <a href="/wiki/Hyperthyroidism" title="Hyperthyroidism">hyperthyroidism</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_emergencies" title="Radiation emergencies">radiation emergencies</a>, and to protect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_gland" title="Thyroid gland">thyroid gland</a> when certain types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceuticals" title="Radiopharmaceuticals">radiopharmaceuticals</a> are used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS2017_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS2017-3">[3]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">developing world</a> it is also used to treat skin <a href="/wiki/Sporotrichosis" title="Sporotrichosis">sporotrichosis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phycomycosis" title="Phycomycosis">phycomycosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS2017_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS2017-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2008-4">[4]</a></sup> As a supplement it is used in those who have low intake of <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> in the diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St2002_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-St2002-2">[2]</a></sup> It is given by mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS2017_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS2017-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Common side effects include vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, rash, and swelling of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salivary_glands" title="Salivary glands">salivary glands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS2017_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS2017-3">[3]</a></sup> Other side effects include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergic_reactions" title="Allergic reactions">allergic reactions</a>, headache, <a href="/wiki/Goitre" title="Goitre">goitre</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2008_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2008-4">[4]</a></sup> While use during <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> may harm the baby this use is still recommended in radiation emergencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS2017_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS2017-3">[3]</a></sup> Potassium iodide has the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K</a><a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Commercially it is made by mixing <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> with iodine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Potassium iodide has been used medically since at least 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Or2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Or2012-7">[7]</a></sup> It is on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization%27s_List_of_Essential_Medicines" title="World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines">World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines</a>, the most effective and safe medicines needed in a <a href="/wiki/Health_system" title="Health system">health system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO19th_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO19th-8">[8]</a></sup> Potassium iodide is available as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_medication" title="Generic medication">generic medication</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over_the_counter" title="Over the counter">over the counter</a>. In the United States a course of treatment is less than 25 USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ric2015_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ric2015-9">[9]</a></sup> Potassium iodide is also used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodization_of_salt" title="Iodization of salt">iodization of salt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St2002_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-St2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_uses">Medical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Medical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_supplement">Dietary supplement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dietary supplement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The major uses of KI include as a nutritional supplement in animal feeds and also the human diet. For the latter, it is the most common additive used to "iodize" <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">table salt</a> (a public health measure to prevent <a href="/wiki/Iodine_deficiency" title="Iodine deficiency">iodine deficiency</a> in populations that get little seafood). The oxidation of iodide causes slow loss of iodine content from <a href="/wiki/Iodised_salt" title="Iodised salt">iodised salts</a> that are exposed to excess air. The alkali metal iodide salt, over time and exposure to excess oxygen and carbon dioxide, slowly oxidizes to metal carbonate and elemental iodine, which then evaporates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodate" title="Potassium iodate">Potassium iodate</a> is used to add iodine to some salts so that the iodine is not lost by oxidation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextrose" title="Dextrose">Dextrose</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_thiosulfate" title="Sodium thiosulfate">sodium thiosulfate</a> are often added to iodized table salt to stabilize potassium iodide thus reducing loss of the volatile chemical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thyroid_protection">Thyroid protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thyroid protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Thyroid iodine uptake blockade with potassium iodide is used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> <a href="/wiki/Scintigraphy" title="Scintigraphy">scintigraphy</a> and therapy with some radioiodinated compounds that are not targeted to the thyroid, such as <a href="/wiki/Iobenguane" title="Iobenguane">iobenguane</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIBG" title="MIBG">MIBG</a>), which is used to image or treat neural tissue tumors, or iodinated <a href="/wiki/Fibrinogen" title="Fibrinogen">fibrinogen</a>, which is used in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fibrinogen_scan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fibrinogen scan (page does not exist)">fibrinogen scans</a> to investigate clotting. These compounds contain iodine, but not in the iodide form. However, since they may be ultimately metabolized or break down to radioactive iodide, it is common to administer non-radioactive potassium iodide to ensure that iodide from these radiopharmaceuticals is not sequestered by the normal affinity of the thryoid for iodide.</p>
<p>U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a>-approved dosing of potassium iodide for this purpose with iobenguane, is as follows (per 24 hours): infants less than 1 month old, 16 mg; children 1 month to 3 years, 32 mg; children 3 years to 18 years, 65 mg; adults 130 mg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, some sources recommend alternative dosing regimens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eanm.org_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-eanm.org-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not all sources are in agreement on the necessary <i>duration</i> of thyroid blockade, although agreement appears to have been reached about the <i>necessity</i> of blockade for both scintigraphic and therapeutic applications of iobenguane. Commercially available iobenguane is labeled with <a href="/wiki/Iodine-123" title="Iodine-123">iodine-123</a>, and product labeling recommends administration of potassium iodide 1 hour prior to administration of the radiopharmaceutical for all age groups,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> while the European Associated of Nuclear Medicine recommends (for iobenguane labeled with either isotope,) that potassium iodide administration begin one day prior to <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">radiopharmaceutical</a> administration, and continue until the day following the injection, with the exception of new-borns, who do not require potassium iodide doses following radiopharmaceutical injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eanm.org_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-eanm.org-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUREG1250_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUREG1250-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Product labeling for diagnostic iodine-131 iobenguane recommends potassium iodide administration one day before injection and continuing 5 to 7 days following administration, in keeping with the much longer half-life of this isotope and its greater danger to the thyroid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Iodine-131 iobenguane used for therapeutic purposes requires a different pre-medication duration, beginning 24–48 hours prior to iobenguane injection and continuing 10–15 days following injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_accidents">Nuclear accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nuclear accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> approved potassium iodide to protect <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> glands from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioiodine" title="Radioiodine">radioactive iodine</a> involving accidents or fission emergencies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In an accidental event or attack on a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plant</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">nuclear bomb fallout</a>, volatile fission product radionuclides may be released. Of these products, <a href="/wiki/Iodine-131" title="Iodine-131"><sup>131</sup>I</a> is one of the most common and is particularly dangerous to the thyroid gland because it may lead to <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_cancer" title="Thyroid cancer">thyroid cancer</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By saturating the body with a source of stable iodide prior to exposure, inhaled or ingested <sup>131</sup>I tends to be excreted, which prevents radioiodine uptake by the thyroid. According to one 2000 study "KI administered up to 48 h before <sup>131</sup>I exposure can almost completely block thyroid uptake and therefore greatly reduce the thyroid absorbed dose. However, KI administration 96 h or more before <sup>131</sup>I exposure has no significant protective effect. In contrast, KI administration after exposure to radioiodine induces a smaller and rapidly decreasing blockade effect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> For optimal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophylaxis#Prophylaxis" title="Prophylaxis">prevention</a>, KI must be dosed daily until a risk of significant exposure to radioiodine by either inhalation or ingestion no longer exists.</p>
<p>Emergency 130 milligrams potassium iodide doses provide 100 mg iodide (the other 30 mg is the potassium in the compound), which is roughly 700 times larger than the normal nutritional need (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recommended_dietary_allowance" title="Recommended dietary allowance">recommended dietary allowance</a>) for iodine, which is 150 micrograms (0.15 mg) of iodine (as iodide) per day for an adult. The typical tablet actually weighs 160 mg. 130 mg of which is potassium iodide, and 30 mg being excipients, such as binding agents.</p>
<p>Potassium iodide cannot protect against any other causes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">radiation poisoning</a>, nor can it provide any degree of protection against <a href="/wiki/Dirty_bomb" title="Dirty bomb">dirty bombs</a> that produce radionuclides other than radionuclides of iodine. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_products" title="Fission products">fission products</a> and the external links for more details concerning radionuclides.</p>

<p>The potassium iodide in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodized_salt" title="Iodized salt">iodized salt</a> is insufficient for this use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who-japan_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-who-japan-20">[20]</a></sup> A likely <a href="/wiki/Lethal_dose" title="Lethal dose">lethal dose</a> of salt (more than a kilogram<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfr_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfr-21">[21]</a></sup>) would be needed to equal the potassium iodide in one tablet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nacl-msds_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nacl-msds-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> does not recommend KI prophylaxis for adults over 40 years, unless the radiation dose from inhaled radioiodine is expected to threaten thyroid function, because the KI side effects increase with age and may exceed the KI protective effects; "...unless doses to the thyroid from inhalation rise to levels threatening thyroid function, that is of the order of about 5 <a href="/wiki/Gray_(unit)" title="Gray (unit)">Gy</a>. Such radiation doses will not occur far away from an accident site."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_Guidelines_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_Guidelines-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services restated these two years later as "The downward KI (potassium iodide) dose adjustment by age group, based on body size considerations, adheres to the principle of minimum effective dose. The recommended standard (daily) dose of KI for all school-age children is the same (65 mg). However, adolescents approaching adult size (i.e., &gt;70 kg [154 lbs]) should receive the full adult dose (130 mg) for maximal block of thyroid radioiodine uptake. Neonates ideally should receive the lowest dose (16 mg) of KI."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HHS_Guidelines_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HHS_Guidelines-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>SSKI (i.e., the solution of KI rather than tablets) may be used in radioiodine-contamination emergencies (i.e., nuclear accidents) to "block" the thyroid's uptake of radioiodine, at a dose of two drops of SSKI per day for an adult. This is not the same as blocking the thyroid's release of thyroid hormone, for which the adult dose is different (and is actually higher by a factor of 7 or 8), and for which KI anti-radiation pills (not a common medical treatment form of KI) are not usually available in pharmacies, or normally used in hospitals, or by physicians. Although the two forms of potassium iodide are completely interchangeable, normally in practice the SSKI solution, which is the historical medical form of high dose iodine, is generally used for all medical purposes save for radioiodine prophylaxis. For protection of the thyroid against radioiodine (iodine-131) contamination, the convenient standard 130 mg KI pill is used, if available. As noted, the equivalent two drops of SSKI (equaling the dose of one KI pill) may be used for this purpose, if the pills are not available.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Side_effects">Side effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Side effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is reason for caution with prescribing the ingestion of high doses of potassium iodide and iodate, as their unnecessary use can cause conditions such as the <a href="/wiki/Jod-Basedow_phenomenon" title="Jod-Basedow phenomenon">Jod-Basedow phenomena</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolff-Chaikoff_effect" title="Wolff-Chaikoff effect">Wolff-Chaikoff effect</a>, trigger and/or worsen <a href="/wiki/Hyperthyroidism" title="Hyperthyroidism">hyperthyroidism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>, and then cause temporary or even permanent thyroid conditions. It can also cause <a href="/wiki/Sialadenitis" title="Sialadenitis">sialadenitis</a> (an inflammation of the salivary gland), gastrointestinal disturbances, allergic reactions and rashes. Potassium iodide is also not recommended for those who have had an allergic reaction to iodine, and people with dermatitis herpetiformis and hypocomplementemic vasculitis, conditions that are linked to a risk of iodine sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>There have been some reports of potassium iodide treatment causing swelling of the <a href="/wiki/Parotid_gland" title="Parotid gland">parotid gland</a> (one of the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glands" title="Glands">glands</a> which secrete <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a>), due to its stimulatory effects on saliva production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pathophysiology_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pathophysiology-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>A saturated solution of KI (SSKI) is typically given orally in adult doses several times a day (5 drops of SSKI assumed to be ⅓ ml) for thyroid blockade (to prevent the thyroid from excreting thyroid hormone) and occasionally this dose is also used, when iodide is used as an expectorant (the total dose is about one gram KI per day for an adult). The anti-radioiodine doses used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I-131" title="I-131">I-131</a> uptake blockade are lower, and range downward from 100 mg a day for an adult, to less than this for children (see table). All of these doses should be compared with the far lower dose of iodine needed in normal nutrition, which is only 150 μg per day (150 micrograms, not milligrams).</p>
<p>At maximal doses, and sometimes at much lower doses, side effects of iodide used for medical reasons, in doses of 1000 times the normal nutrional need, may include: acne, loss of appetite, or upset stomach (especially during the first several days, as the body adjusts to the medication). More severe side effects which require notification of a physician are: fever, weakness, unusual tiredness, swelling in the neck or throat, mouth sores, skin rash, nausea, vomiting, stomach pains, irregular heartbeat, numbness or tingling of the hands or feet, or a metallic taste in the mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of a particular 'Iodine tablet' used in <a href="/wiki/Portable_water_purification" title="Portable water purification">portable water purification</a> has also been determined as somewhat effective at reducing radioiodine uptake. In a small study on human subjects, who for each of their 90-day trial, ingested four 20 milligram tetraglycine hydroperiodide(TGHP) water tablets, with each tablet releasing 8 milligrams (ppm) of free titratable iodine;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> it was found that the biological uptake of radioactive iodine in these human subjects dropped to, and remained at, a value of less than 2% the radioiodine uptake rate of that observed in control subjects who went fully exposed to radioiodine without treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the event of a radioiodine release the ingestion of prophylaxis potassium iodide, if available, or even iodate, would rightly take precedence over perchlorate administration, and would be the first line of defense in protecting the population from a radioiodine release. However, in the event of a radioiodine release too massive and widespread to be controlled by the limited stock of iodide and iodate prophylaxis drugs, then the addition of perchlorate ions to the water supply, or distribution of perchlorate tablets would serve as a cheap, efficacious, second line of defense against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a> radioiodine bioaccumulation.</p>
<p>The ingestion of goitrogen drugs is, much like potassium iodide also not without its dangers, such as <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>. In all these cases however, despite the risks, the prophylaxis benefits of intervention with iodide, iodate or perchlorate outweigh the serious cancer risk from radioiodine <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a> in regions were radioiodine has sufficiently contaminated the environment.</p>
<p>Potassium iodide in its raw form is a mild irritant and should be handled with gloves. Chronic overexposure can have adverse effects on the thyroid. Potassium iodide is a possible <a href="/wiki/Teratology" title="Teratology">teratogen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_uses">Industrial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>KI is used with silver nitrate to make <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a> (AgI) an important chemical in film photography. KI is a component in some disinfectants and hair treatment chemicals. KI is also used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescence_quenching" title="Fluorescence quenching">fluorescence quenching</a> agent in biomedical research, an application that takes advantage of collisional quenching of fluorescent substances by the iodide ion. However, for several fluorophores addition of KI in µM-mM concentrations results in increase of fluorescence intensity, and iodide acts as fluorescence enhancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Potassium iodide is a component in the electrolyte of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dye_sensitized_solar_cell" title="Dye sensitized solar cell">dye sensitized solar cells</a> (DSSC) along with iodine.</p>
<p>Potassium iodide finds its most important applications in organic synthesis mainly in the preparation of aryl iodides in the <a href="/wiki/Sandmeyer_reaction" title="Sandmeyer reaction">Sandmeyer reaction</a>, starting from aryl amines. Aryl iodides are in turn used to attach aryl groups to other organics by nucleophilic substitution, with iodide ion as the leaving group.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potassium iodide is <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic</a>, K<sup>+</sup>I<sup>−</sup>. It crystallises in the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> structure. It is produced industrially by treating KOH with iodine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is a white <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>, which is the most commercially significant iodide compound, with approximately 37,000 tons produced in 1985. It absorbs water less readily than <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a>, making it easier to work with.</p>
<p>Aged and impure samples are yellow because of the slow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of the salt to <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a> and elemental <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>4 KI + 2 CO<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 K<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + 2 I<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inorganic_chemistry">Inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> ion is a mild <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing agent</a>, <span class="texhtml">I<sup>−</sup></span> is easily oxidised to <span class="texhtml"><a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">I<sub>2</sub></a></span> by powerful <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidising agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">2 KI(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous" title="Aqueous">aq</a>) + Cl<sub>2</sub>(aq) → 2 <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">KCl</a>(aq) + I<sub>2</sub>(aq)</span></dd>
</dl>
<p>This reaction is employed in the isolation of iodine from natural sources. Air will oxidize iodide, as evidenced by the observation of a purple extract when aged samples of KI are rinsed with <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a>. As formed under acidic conditions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydriodic_acid" title="Hydriodic acid">hydriodic acid</a> (HI) is a stronger reducing agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like other iodide salts, <span class="texhtml">KI</span> forms <span class="texhtml"><a href="/wiki/Triiodide" title="Triiodide">I<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup></a></span> when combined with elemental <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">KI(aq) + <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">I<sub>2</sub></a>(<a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">s</a>) → KI<sub>3</sub>(aq)</span></dd>
</dl>
<p>Unlike <span class="texhtml">I<sub>2</sub></span>, <span class="texhtml">I<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup></span> salts can be highly water-soluble. Through this reaction, <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titrations</a>. Aqueous <span class="texhtml">KI<sub>3</sub></span>, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lugol%27s_solution" title="Lugol's solution">Lugol's solution</a>", is used as a disinfectant and as an etchant for gold surfaces.</p>
<p>Potassium iodide and <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a> are used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver(I)_iodide" title="Silver(I) iodide">silver(I) iodide</a>, which is used for high speed <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a> and for cloud-seeding:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml">KI(aq) + <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">AgNO<sub>3</sub></a>(aq) → AgI(s) + <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">KNO<sub>3</sub></a>(aq)</span></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_chemistry">Organic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>KI serves as a source of iodide in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>. A useful application is in the preparation of aryl iodides from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arenediazonium_salts" title="Arenediazonium salts">arenediazonium salts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> For example:</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KI_Sandmeyer.png"><img alt="KI Sandmeyer.png" data-file-height="489" data-file-width="1778" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/KI_Sandmeyer.png/400px-KI_Sandmeyer.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/KI_Sandmeyer.png/600px-KI_Sandmeyer.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/KI_Sandmeyer.png/800px-KI_Sandmeyer.png 2x" width="400"/></a></p>
<p>KI, acting as a source of iodide, may also act as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophilic_catalyst&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nucleophilic catalyst (page does not exist)">nucleophilic catalyst</a> for the alkylation of alkyl <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chlorides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromide" title="Bromide">bromides</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mesylate" title="Mesylate">mesylates</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potassium iodide has been used medically since at least 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Or2012_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Or2012-7">[7]</a></sup> Some of the earliest uses included for <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Or2012_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Or2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chernobyl">Chernobyl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chernobyl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl nuclear reactor disaster</a> in April, 1986, a saturated solution of potassium iodide (SSKI) was administered to 10.5 million children and 7 million adults in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HHS_Guidelines_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-HHS_Guidelines-23">[23]</a></sup> as a preventative measure against accumulation of radioactive <a href="/wiki/Iodine-131" title="Iodine-131">iodine-131</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> gland. People in the areas immediately surrounding Chernobyl itself, however, were not given the supplement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Potassium iodide's (KI) value as a radiation protective (thyroid blocking) agent was demonstrated at the time of the Chernobyl nuclear accident when Soviet authorities distributed it in a 30 km zone around the plant. The purpose was to protect residents from radioactive iodine, a highly carcinogenic material found in nuclear reactors which had been released by the damaged reactor. Only a limited amount of KI was available, but those who received it were protected. Later, the US Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) reported, "thousands of measurements of I-131 (radioactive iodine) activity...suggest that the observed levels were lower than would have been expected had this prophylactic measure not been taken. The use of KI...was credited with permissible iodine content in 97% of the evacuees tested."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUREG1250_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUREG1250-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Poland, 300 miles from Chernobyl, also distributed KI to protect its population. Approximately 18 million doses were distributed, with follow-up studies showing no known thyroid cancer among KI recipients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJM93_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-AJM93-37">[37]</a></sup> With the passage of time, people living in irradiated areas where KI was not available have developed thyroid cancer at epidemic levels, which is why the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) reported "The data clearly demonstrate the risks of thyroid radiation... KI can be used [to] provide safe and effective protection against thyroid cancer caused by irradiation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA_Talk_Paper_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDA_Talk_Paper-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chernobyl also demonstrated that the need to protect the thyroid from radiation was greater than expected. Within ten years of the accident, it became clear that thyroid damage caused by released radioactive iodine was virtually the only adverse health effect that could be measured. As reported by the NRC, studies after the accident showed that "As of 1996, except for thyroid cancer, there has been no confirmed increase in the rates of other cancers, including leukemia, among the... public, that have been attributed to releases from the accident."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRC-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>But equally important to the question of KI is the fact that radioactivity releases are not "local" events. Researchers at the World Health Organization accurately located and counted the cancer victims from Chernobyl and were startled to find that "the increase in incidence [of thyroid cancer] has been documented up to 500 km from the accident site... significant doses from radioactive iodine can occur hundreds of kilometers from the site, beyond emergency planning zones."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_Guidelines_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_Guidelines-19">[19]</a></sup> Consequently, far more people than anticipated were affected by the radiation, which caused the United Nations to report in 2002 that "The number of people with thyroid cancer... has exceeded expectations. Over 11,000 cases have already been reported."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCHA_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-OCHA-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nagasaki">Nagasaki</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nagasaki">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These findings were consistent with studies of the effects of previous radioactivity releases. In 1945, millions of Japanese were exposed to radiation from nuclear weapons, and the effects can still be measured. Today, nearly half (44.8%) the survivors of Nagasaki studied have identifiable thyroid disease, with the American Medical Association reporting "it is remarkable that a biological effect from a single brief environmental exposure nearly 60 years in the past is still present and can be detected."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMA_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMA-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_weapons_testing">Nuclear weapons testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nuclear weapons testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The development of thyroid cancer among residents in the North Pacific from radioactive fallout following the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Proving_Grounds" title="Pacific Proving Grounds">United States' nuclear weapons testing</a> in the 1950s (on islands nearly 200 miles downwind of the tests) were instrumental in the decision by the FDA in 1978 to issue a request for the availability of KI for thyroid protection in the event of a release from a commercial nuclear power plant or weapons-related nuclear incident. Noting that KI's effectiveness was "virtually complete" and finding that iodine in the form of potassium iodide (KI) was substantially superior to other forms including <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodate" title="Potassium iodate">iodate (KIO<sub>3</sub>)</a> in terms of safety, effectiveness, lack of side effects, and speed of onset, the FDA invited manufacturers to submit applications to produce and market KI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_Fed._Reg._42-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_Fed._Reg.-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fukushima">Fukushima</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fukushima">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was reported on March 16, 2011, that potassium iodide tablets were given preventatively to U.S. Naval air crew members flying within 70 nautical miles of the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant">Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant</a> damaged in the earthquake (8.9/9.0 magnitude) and ensuing tsunami on March 11, 2011. The measures were seen as precautions, and the Pentagon said no U.S. forces have shown signs of radiation poisoning. By March 20, the US Navy instructed personnel coming within 100 miles of the reactor to take the pills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formulations">Formulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Formulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three companies (Anbex, Inc., Fleming Co, and Recip of Sweden) have met the strict FDA requirements for manufacturing and testing of KI, and they offer products (IOSAT, ThyroShield, and Thyro-Safe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> respectively) which are available for purchase. In 2012, Fleming Co. sold all its product rights and manufacturing facility to other companies and no longer exists. ThyroShield is currently not in production. The Swedish manufacturing facility for Thyrosafe, a half-strength potassium iodide tablet for thyroid protection from radiation manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Recipharm" title="Recipharm">Recipharm</a> AB, was mentioned on the secret US 2008 <a href="/wiki/Critical_Foreign_Dependencies_Initiative" title="Critical Foreign Dependencies Initiative">Critical Foreign Dependencies Initiative</a> leaked by Wikileaks in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tablets of potassium iodide are supplied for emergency purposes related to blockade of radioiodine uptake, a common form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">radiation poisoning</a> due to environmental contamination by the short-lived fission product <a href="/wiki/Iodine-131" title="Iodine-131">iodine-131</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Potassium iodide may also be administered pharmaceutically for <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_storm" title="Thyroid storm">thyroid storm</a>.</p>
<p>For reasons noted above, therapeutic drops of SSKI, or 130 mg tablets of KI as used for nuclear fission accidents, are not used as nutritional supplements, since an SSKI drop or nuclear-emergency tablet provides 300 to 700 times more iodine than the daily adult nutritional requirement. Dedicated nutritional iodide tablets containing 0.15 mg (150 micrograms (µg)) of iodide, from KI or from various other sources (such as kelp extract) are marketed as supplements, but they are not to be confused with the much higher pharmaceutical dose preparations.</p>
<p>Potassium iodide can be conveniently prepared as a saturated solution, abbreviated SSKI. This method of delivering potassium iodide does not require a method to weigh out the potassium iodide so it can be used in an emergency situation. KI crystals are simply added to water until no more KI will dissolve and instead sits at the bottom of the container. With pure water, the concentration of KI in the solution depends only on the temperature. Potassium iodide is highly soluble in water so SSKI is a concentrated source of KI. At 20 degrees Celsius the solubility of KI is 140-148 grams per 100 grams of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Because the volumes of KI and water are approximately additive, the resulting SSKI solution will contain about 1.40 gram (1400 mg) KI per milliliter (mL) of solution. This is 100% weight/volume (note units of <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(chemistry)" title="Mass concentration (chemistry)">mass concentration</a>) of KI (one gram KI per mL solution), which is possible because SSKI is significantly more dense than pure water—about 1.72 g/mL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Because KI is about 76.4% iodide by weight, SSKI contains about 764 mg iodide per mL. This <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(chemistry)" title="Mass concentration (chemistry)">concentration</a> of iodide allows the calculation of the iodide dose per drop, if one knows the number of drops per milliliter. For SSKI, a solution more viscous than water, there are assumed to be 15 drops per mL; the iodide dose is therefore approximately 51 mg per drop, assuming 15 drops/mL. It is conventionally rounded to 50 mg per drop.</p>
<p>The term SSKI is also used, especially by pharmacists, to refer to a U.S.P. pre-prepared solution formula, made by adding exactly KI to water to prepare a solution containing of 1000 mg KI per mL solution (100% wt/volume KI solution), to closely approximate the concentration of SSKI made by saturation. This is essentially interchangeable with SSKI made by saturation, and also contains about 50 mg iodide per drop.</p>
<ul>
<li>Saturated solutions of potassium iodide can be an emergency treatment for <a href="/wiki/Hyperthyroidism" title="Hyperthyroidism">hyperthyroidism</a> (so-called <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_storm" title="Thyroid storm">thyroid storm</a>), as high amounts of iodide temporarily suppress secretion of thyroxine from the thyroid gland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The dose typically begins with a loading dose, then 1/3 mL SSKI (5 drops or 250 mg iodine as iodide), three times per day.</li>
<li>Iodide solutions made from a few drops of SSKI added to drinks have also been used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expectorant" title="Expectorant">expectorants</a> to increase the water content of respiratory secretions and encourage effective coughing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>SSKI has been proposed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topical" title="Topical">topical</a> treatment for <a href="/wiki/Sporotrichosis" title="Sporotrichosis">sporotrichosis</a>, but no trials have been conducted to determine the efficacy or side effects of such treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Potassium iodide has been used for symptomatic treatment of <a href="/wiki/Erythema_nodosum" title="Erythema nodosum">erythema nodosum</a> patients for persistent lesions whose cause remains unknown. It has been used in cases of erythema nodosum associated with <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn's disease">Crohn's disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall1997_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall1997-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>Due to its high potassium content, SSKI is extremely bitter, and if possible it is administered in a sugar cube or small ball of bread. It may also be mixed into much larger volumes of juices.</li>
<li>Neither SSKI or KI tablets are used as nutritional supplements, since the nutritional requirement for iodine is only 150 micrograms or 0.15 mg of iodide per day. Thus, a drop of SSKI provides 50/0.15 = 333 times the daily iodine requirement, and a standard KI tablet provides twice this much.</li>
</ul>
