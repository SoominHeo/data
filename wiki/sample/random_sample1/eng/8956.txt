<p>The <b>Aikikai</b> is the original school of <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">Aikido</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is centered on the <a href="/wiki/Aikikai#Aikikai_Foundation" title="Aikikai">Aikikai Foundation</a> in Japan, and its figurehead is the <a href="/wiki/Aikikai#Doshu" title="Aikikai">Doshu</a> (the family heir of the founder of Aikido). It is represented globally through the <a href="/wiki/Aikikai#International_Aikido_Federation" title="Aikikai">International Aikido Federation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aikikai_Foundation">Aikikai Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikikai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aikikai Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="200" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b2/AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif/100px-AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b2/AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif/150px-AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/b2/AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AIKIKAI-logo-07CBA50695-seeklogo_com.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One logo of the Aikikai Foundation</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Aikikai Foundation</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">財団法人合気会</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Zaidan Hōjin Aikikai</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is the original aikido organization. It has been an incorporated entity in Japan since 1940. It is headed by the doshu, the living successor of the founder of aikido. In its name, <i>Kai</i> (会) simply means assembly or club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dict_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dict-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Aikikai Foundation operates <a href="/wiki/Aikikai#Hombu_dojo" title="Aikikai">Hombu dojo</a>, which is also named Aikido World Headquarters. It is sometimes called the Aikikai Hombu to distinguish it from the headquarters of later aikido organisations. It is located in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. The term "Hombu" may sometimes be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metonym" title="Metonym">used loosely</a> to refer to the upper echelons of instructors at Hombu dojo, or to the Aikikai Foundation itself.</p>
<p>The Aikikai Foundation also currently manages one satellite dojo, the historical <a href="/wiki/Iwama_dojo" title="Iwama dojo">Iwama dojo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ibaraki_Prefecture" title="Ibaraki Prefecture">Ibaraki</a> (about 100 km northeast of Tokyo).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Aikikai Foundation sends instructors around Japan on a continual basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup> It also issues certificates of grading and instructor titles legitimated by the Doshu throughout the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Doshu">Doshu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikikai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Doshu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;max-width:208px">

<p><b>Doshu</b> (道主 / どうしゅ / Dōshu) literally translates as 'Master of the Way'. It is the title denoting the head of the Aikikai. Although the same term could be used as a title within other disciplines, in Japanese its most common meaning is specific to the Aikikai Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dict_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dict-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The system of having the heir of a <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">martial arts</a> school be the previous headmaster's son (either natural or adopted for the sake of succession) was common in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kory%C5%AB" title="Koryū">koryū</a></i> (traditional schools) and such heirs often are referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Iemoto" title="Iemoto">iemoto</a></i>. Other arts commonly use the title <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dke" title="Sōke">Sōke</a> for the headmaster.</p>
<ul>
<li>Founder (開祖 <i>Kaiso</i>) <a href="/wiki/Morihei_Ueshiba" title="Morihei Ueshiba">Morihei Ueshiba</a> (lived 1883-1969) originated the art of Aikido. As such he was also the first doshu, but is more commonly referred to as O-sensei (grand teacher).</li>
<li>Second Doshu (二代道主) <a href="/wiki/Kisshomaru_Ueshiba" title="Kisshomaru Ueshiba">Kisshomaru Ueshiba</a> (lived 1921–1999) took over the leadership of the organisation and assumed the title of doshu in 1969 upon the death of his father Morihei Ueshiba. Kisshomaru was the first person in aikido to be widely referred to as Doshu. He was succeeded by his son Moriteru.</li>
<li>Third Doshu (三代道主) <a href="/wiki/Moriteru_Ueshiba" title="Moriteru Ueshiba">Moriteru Ueshiba</a> (born 1951) is the current doshu. He is grandson of the founder of aikido and assumed the title of doshu in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Moriteru is expected to be succeeded as doshu by his son, <a href="/wiki/Mitsuteru_Ueshiba" title="Mitsuteru Ueshiba">Mitsuteru Ueshiba</a> (born 1980),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> who is currently referred to as "Waka(若) Sensei".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hombu_dojo">Hombu dojo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikikai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hombu dojo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Aikikai&amp;params=35_41_56.9_N_139_42_51.5_E_type:landmark"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°41′56.9″N</span> <span class="longitude">139°42′51.5″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.699139°N 139.714306°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.699139; 139.714306</span></span></span></a></span></span></span></p>
<p>The Hombu <a href="/wiki/Dojo" title="Dojo">dojo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> is the headquarters of the Aikikai.</p>
<p>The Hombu dojo is officially named Aikido World Headquarters, and is entirely operated by the Aikikai Foundation. It is sometimes called the Aikikai Hombu to distinguish it from the headquarters of later aikido organisations. Hombu (本部 <i>honbu</i>) is a common word meaning "headquarters".</p>
<p>Hombu dojo has about 30 instructors, and most are <a href="/wiki/Shihan" title="Shihan">shihan</a>. The instructors include the Doshu and <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Tada" title="Hiroshi Tada">Hiroshi Tada</a> 9th dan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The location of Hombu dojo is in Wakamatsu-cho, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shinjuku,_Tokyo" title="Shinjuku, Tokyo">Shinjuku</a>, Tokyo, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. (This same location was formerly the <a href="/wiki/Ushigome" title="Ushigome">Ushigome</a> District of Shinjuku.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dojo was established in April 1931 by the founder of Aikido. It was originally named Kobukan Dōjō (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%9A%87" title="wikt:皇">皇</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%AD%A6" title="wikt:武">武</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A4%A8" title="wikt:館">館</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%81%93" title="wikt:道">道</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A0%B4" title="wikt:場">場</a>, imperial warrior hall dojo) but was called "Hombu Dōjō" after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1967 the original single-story wooden structure was replaced by a five story modern building. Training takes place in three rooms which total about 250 <a href="/wiki/Tatami" title="Tatami">tatami</a> in area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup> The main training area has 105 tatami mats (170m<sup>2</sup>). The other two training areas, containing 72 and 42 tatami mats respectively, are often used for beginner's classes, children's classes, women's classes, or fixed term and enrollment "academy" (<i>Gakko</i>) courses.</p>
<p>Classes are held seven days a week. Beginner's and regular classes are open to all Aikido practitioners that are or become Aikikai members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A small number of students also reside in the dōjō as part of a <a href="/wiki/Uchi-deshi" title="Uchi-deshi">live-in training</a> program.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aikikai_school_of_Aikido">Aikikai school of Aikido</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikikai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aikikai school of Aikido">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aikido_keyhole.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="536" data-file-width="800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Aikido_keyhole.jpg/220px-Aikido_keyhole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Aikido_keyhole.jpg/330px-Aikido_keyhole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Aikido_keyhole.jpg/440px-Aikido_keyhole.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aikido_keyhole.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Doshu demonstrating Aikido to students from around the globe, at the IAF Congress in <a href="/wiki/Tanabe,_Wakayama" title="Tanabe, Wakayama">Tanabe</a> (birthtown of O'Sensei)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Within Japan, there are about 1800 other training locations affiliated with the Hombu. These are united under the All-Japan Aikido Federation. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Overseas, there are about 100 foreign aikido organisations recognised by the Hombu. These are ostensibly national aikido organisations that each represent many dojos and many students. The gradings of students in these organisations are codified specifically by the Hombu. (The Hombu has procedures for examinations and recommendations of aikido grades and instructor titles, and ideally this ensures a degree of international consistency and serves to prevent local instructors from boastfully exaggerating their own ranks.) However, regardless of recognition, all foreign aikido groups remain organisationally independent from the Aikikai Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikikai_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-aikikai-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The students of recognised dojo worldwide are loosely referred to collectively as the Aikikai school of aikido, although this "school" is not an official organisation. Their aikido technique may be referred to as Aikikai style, although this encompasses a wider spectrum of technical styles characteristically than later (and smaller) schools of aikido.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Aikido_Federation">International Aikido Federation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikikai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International Aikido Federation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:International_Aikido_Federation_(logo).gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="154" data-file-width="150" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/International_Aikido_Federation_%28logo%29.gif/100px-International_Aikido_Federation_%28logo%29.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/ae/International_Aikido_Federation_%28logo%29.gif 1.5x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:International_Aikido_Federation_(logo).gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
International Aikido Federation logo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The International Aikido Federation is the primary global aikido organisation. It is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbrella_organisation" title="Umbrella organisation">umbrella organisation</a> with member organisations from more than 40 nations (for example, the All-Japan Aikido Federation is one member). All members must be recognised by the Hombu, so the IAF exclusively represents the Aikikai school. The IAF is a nominally democratic organisation but special roles are given to the Doshu and a council of senior instructors, to safeguard the "technical and moral" integrity of aikido. The IAF currently admits only one member organisation per country and gives each such member equal vote (it was modelled on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a>; it does not necessarily give every Aikikai student equal representation). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IAF demonstrates aikido at the <a href="/wiki/World_Games" title="World Games">World Games</a>, and represents aikido to the globe. The IAF also organises International Aikido Congresses every four years, facilitating direct training between aikido students of different countries, sharing instruction by the world's most senior aikido instructors, and also providing a channel for official communication with the Hombu.</p>
<p>The IAF was formed in 1976. The IAF chair (1996–present) is Peter Goldsbury, who is also a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">comparative mythology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima_University" title="Hiroshima University">Hiroshima</a>. Goldsbury publishes numerous aikido articles at AikiWeb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iaf_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-iaf-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aikiweb_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-aikiweb-10">[10]</a></sup> The previous chair was Giorgio Veneri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IAF publishes a <a class="external text" href="https://www.aikido-international.org/membership-advice/member-nations" rel="nofollow">list of member nations and organisations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aikikai_style_of_aikido">Aikikai style of aikido</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikikai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aikikai style of aikido">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aikikai is not one style of aikido but instead encompasses a diversity of technical styles. This is associated with the broad base of first generation instructors, who each had their own interpretation of the art, and other influences. Nonetheless Aikikai is often described as a style for comparison with separate aikido organisations. Aikikai is described as the most traditional; Aikikai has stayed loyal to the Ueshiba house and is sometimes called Ueshiba Aikido. It has retained many of the aspects which Morihei Ueshiba valued, such as the non-competitive training system (as contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Tomiki" title="Kenji Tomiki">Tomiki's</a> development). Spiritual interpretations are not emphasised compared to in <a href="/wiki/Ki_Society" title="Ki Society">Ki Society</a>.</p>
<p>Morihei Ueshiba's own style evolved significantly over his lifetime. The pre-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">war</a> <i>aikibudo</i> techniques taught by <a href="/wiki/Morihei_Ueshiba" title="Morihei Ueshiba">Morihei Ueshiba</a> were generally closer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aikijujutsu" title="Aikijujutsu">aikijujutsu</a> whilst flowing technique was more emphasised as he became older, and this is reflected in the aikido of people who studied at different periods in time.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seiseki_Abe" title="Seiseki Abe">Seiseki Abe</a> said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"As far as the Aikido [O-Sensei] practiced in his later years, even young girls, old people and children could do [the techniques]. That is a big difference. I suppose you could say that it was a difference in the severity or the strictness of the training. Before the war, it was severity and strong technique, as opposed to the (kind of) techniques that invigorate our partners as we have now."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kazuo_Chiba" title="Kazuo Chiba">Kazuo Chiba</a> has implied that the style of training in Hombu concentrated less on physical rigor because of its demographic:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"A large portion of the membership at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iwama_Dojo" title="Iwama Dojo">Iwama Dojo</a> consisted of local farmers, hard workers who spent all day in the fields. They had thick bones and great physical strength, combined with a peculiar local character known as "Mito kishitsu," a type of manliness close to gallantry. Altogether, it was quite an opposite culture from Hombu Dojo in Tokyo. Because it is in the capital of Japan, Hombu’s membership consists of white-collar workers, intellectuals, businessmen, politicians and university students."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
