<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Beer in Ireland</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">Brewing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> has a long history. Production currently stands at over 8 million hectolitres, and approximately half the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> consumed is beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abfi2010_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-abfi2010-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lager accounts for 60% of the beer sold, stout 34% and ale 6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iba_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-iba-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the beginning of the nineteenth century, there were over two hundred breweries in the country, fifty-five of them in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. In the nineteenth century, the number of breweries fell to about fifty, and by 2007 only about twelve remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, Ireland produced <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ale</a>, without the use of <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>, as the plant is not native to Ireland. Large quantities of hops were imported from England in the 18th century. In 1752, more than 500 tons of English hops were imported through Dublin alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the second half of the 18th century, beer, mostly porter, was imported from England in increasing quantities: 15,000 barrels in 1750, 65,000 in 1785, and over 100,000 in 1792.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> In the 1760s about 600,000 barrels of beer were brewed annually in Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 18th century, the Irish Parliament used taxation to encourage brewing at the expense of distilling, reasoning that beer was less harmful than whiskey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> In the 1760s, the Royal Dublin Society offered prizes to brewers who used the most Irish hops and those that produced the most porter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brewing prospered in the early decades of the 19th century and by 1814 Ireland was exporting more beer to England than it imported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iia-p457_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-iia-p457-7">[7]</a></sup> Irish exports to England accelerated as the century progressed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iia-p457_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-iia-p457-7">[7]</a></sup> and from a modest 11,328 barrels in 1828, exported 689,796 barrels by 1901.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beer_market">Beer market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beer market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>in 1998, Ireland produced approximately 8.5m hectolitres of beer per annum; this rose to 8.7m in 2002. Exports were 3.5m hectolitres in 1998 and fell to 2.4m in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Whilst Ireland lies 6th in the world for beer consumption per capita, it ranks 4th in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_alcohol_consumption_per_capita" title="List of countries by alcohol consumption per capita">consumption of alcohol</a>, with 11.7 litres per head in 2011.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lager_brewing">Lager brewing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lager brewing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Advertising_hoarding,_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="640" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Advertising_hoarding%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg/220px-Advertising_hoarding%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Advertising_hoarding%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg/330px-Advertising_hoarding%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Advertising_hoarding%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg/440px-Advertising_hoarding%2C_Belfast_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1125414.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">lager</a> brewery in Ireland was set up in <a href="/wiki/Dartry" title="Dartry">Dartry</a>, Dublin, in 1891, but did not survive very long. Lager was later brewed for a short period at the Regal Brewery, <a href="/wiki/Kells,_County_Meath" title="Kells, County Meath">Kells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harp_Lager" title="Harp Lager">Harp Lager</a> has been brewed in <a href="/wiki/Dundalk" title="Dundalk">Dundalk</a> since 1968.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heineken" title="Heineken">Heineken</a> Ireland, based at the Murphy Brewery in Cork, have the largest share of the lager market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition to Heineken, they brew Amstel and Coors Light, as well as the brands they acquired from Beamish &amp; Crawford including Fosters and Carling.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_red_ale">Irish red ale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Irish red ale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ales produced in Ireland are now largely in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_red_ale" title="Irish red ale">Irish red ale</a> style, with a slight red colour, generally in the 3.8–4.4% ABV range (although export versions are often stronger). The largest national brand is <a href="/wiki/Smithwick%27s" title="Smithwick's">Smithwick's</a>, produced by the <a href="/wiki/Diageo" title="Diageo">Diageo</a> multinational. Others include Diageo's <a href="/wiki/Macardle_Moore_Brewery" title="Macardle Moore Brewery">Macardle's</a>, Franciscan Well's Rebel Red, Carlow Brewing Company's O'Hara's Irish Red and Messrs Maguire Rusty. Ireland's second-largest brewer, Heineken, no longer makes a red ale in Ireland, having discontinued local production of Murphy's Red and Beamish Red. Dungarvan Brewing Company's Copper Coast red ale was released in 2010, along with Clanconnel Brewery's McGrath's Irish Red. Eight Degrees brewery launched their Sunburnt Irish Red Ale in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stout">Stout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guinness_brewery.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Guinness_brewery.jpg/200px-Guinness_brewery.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Guinness_brewery.jpg/300px-Guinness_brewery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Guinness_brewery.jpg/400px-Guinness_brewery.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_stout" title="Irish stout">Irish stout</a></div>
<p>In 1756, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Guinness" title="Arthur Guinness">Arthur Guinness</a> set up a small brewery, moving to <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in 1759. Having initially brewed ale, he switched to producing <a href="/wiki/Porter_(beer)" title="Porter (beer)">porter</a>, which was a style from London. In the early twentieth century, Guinness became the largest brewer in the world, exporting the Irish style to many countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Although no longer the largest brewer in the world, it remains the largest brewer of stout. In 2014, Guinness sales amounted to 19% of all beer sales in Ireland.</p>
<p>Stout brewed by <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a> (and the smaller brewers <a href="/wiki/Murphy%27s_Irish_Stout" title="Murphy's Irish Stout">Murphy's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beamish_and_Crawford" title="Beamish and Crawford">Beamish</a>) once dominated domestic beer consumption in Ireland, with <a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">lager</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ale</a> having much smaller shares. Lager has subsequently grown in popularity with Carlsberg taking 8% of the market and Budweiser 7% in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMbeer_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EMbeer-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Draught Irish stout is normally served <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen#Nitrogenated_beer" title="Nitrogen">nitrogenated</a>, to create a creamy texture with a long-lasting head.</p>

<p>Craft stouts available in Ireland include Shandon Stout by the Franciscan Well in Cork, O'Hara's Irish stout by Carlow Brewing Company, Black Rock Irish Stout by Dungarvan Brewing Company and Irish stout by Galway Hooker Brewery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_craft_beer_and_real_ale">Irish craft beer and real ale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Irish craft beer and real ale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beginning in the 1990s, brewpubs and microbreweries began to emerge. While some, such as the Biddy Early Brewery, Dublin Brewing Company and Dwan's, have since ceased production, the Franciscan Well Brewpub in <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> and Dublin's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Porterhouse_Brewing_Company" title="The Porterhouse Brewing Company">Porterhouse</a> have both celebrated ten years in business, while the Hilden Brewery in <a href="/wiki/Lisburn" title="Lisburn">Lisburn</a> is Ireland's oldest independent brewer, having been established in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Carlow Brewing Company, established in 1996 and makers of the O'Hara's range, is another survivor of the "first wave" of Irish craft brewing.</p>
<p>The "second wave" began in the mid-2000s and has included the Beoir Chorca Dhuibhne brewery in <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>, Galway Hooker brewery in <a href="/wiki/County_Galway" title="County Galway">County Galway</a>, Dungarvan Brewing Company, Clanconnel Brewery, <a href="/wiki/Trouble_Brewing_(brewery)" title="Trouble Brewing (brewery)">Trouble Brewing</a>, Metalman Brewing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Dingle Brewing Company in <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>, Bo Bristle (formerly Breweyed), Eight Degrees Brewing, and the Galway Bay Brewery, which, like the Porterhouse Brewing Company, also runs its own chain of pubs.</p>
<p>The "third wave" of Irish craft brewing began in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The surge of new breweries is largely a result of changes in excise requirements, access to EU funding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and an increase in government-funded brewing education courses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Many second- and third-wave brewers are also involved in a professional brewers' association, Beer Ireland, which has provided members with networking opportunities and information on setting up a brewery.</p>
<p>In 2014, there were approximately fifty Irish craft brewing businesses either in production or in planning. Despite this growth, in 2014 craft beers represented only 1.5% of beer sales by volume in Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMbeer_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-EMbeer-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British-based pub chain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J_D_Wetherspoon" title="J D Wetherspoon">J D Wetherspoon</a> has about nine outlets in Northern Ireland selling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_ale" title="Real ale">real ale</a> and opened pubs in Cork and Dublin in early 2014, with three more planned for 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional.2C_Trade_and_Consumer_Associations">Professional, Trade and Consumer Associations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Professional, Trade and Consumer Associations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three brewing associations operating in Ireland.</p>
<p>The <b>Irish Brewers Association</b> was founded in 1904 and is now a division of <a href="/wiki/Ibec" title="Ibec">IBEC</a>. While it claims to be "the representative voice for the brewing industry in Ireland" it has only five members which include Diageo, Heineken, and Molson Coors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> all macro breweries.</p>
<p>The <b>Independent Craft Brewers &amp; Distillers of Ireland</b> is a group founded in 2013 by Carlow brewing director Seamus O'Hara. The group was initially established to support craft brewers and distillers in Ireland, but has since re-focused its efforts solely on brewing to become the Independent Craft Brewers of Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Membership of the group is open to anyone operating a microbrewery in Ireland within the definition of same by the Irish Revenue Commissioners.</p>
<p><b>Beer Ireland</b> was founded in 2012 by a group of third wave brewers hoping to set up their own microbreweries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By the end of 2013 the group had a hindred members, including brewers at approximately twenty Irish craft breweries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The group has embraced the spirit of comradery and collaboration typical of the craft brewing industry. The organisation's goal is to improve the quality and proliferation of Irish craft beer.</p>
<p><b>Beoir</b> (the Irish for "beer"), founded in July 2010, is an independent group of consumers which seeks greater choice, quality and value-for-money for beer and cider drinkers on the island of Ireland. Their primary goal is to support and raise awareness of Ireland's native independent microbreweries and craft cider-makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudo-Irish_beer">Pseudo-Irish beer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer_in_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pseudo-Irish beer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of beers claim an Irish provenance or are commissioned by Irish companies, but are produced outside Ireland. In the past these have included Árainn Mhór beers and Time Lager. Today, the Strangford Lough Brewing Company produces a concentrated wort which they export to the UK and US where contract breweries turn it into finished beer.</p>
<p>Many breweries outside Ireland produce Irish-themed beers which are not commonly available in Ireland, such as Killian's Irish Red and Wexford Cream Ale.</p>

Several spirit groceries can still be found in rural towns and villages.</p>
