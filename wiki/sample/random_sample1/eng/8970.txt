<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Irish phonology</h1>

<p>The <b>phonology of the Irish language</b> varies from <a href="/wiki/Irish_language#Dialects" title="Irish language">dialect to dialect</a>; there is no <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard pronunciation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>. Therefore, this article focuses on phenomena that pertain generally to most or all dialects, and on the major differences among the dialects. Detailed discussion of the dialects can be found in the specific articles: <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Irish" title="Ulster Irish">Ulster Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connacht_Irish" title="Connacht Irish">Connacht Irish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Munster_Irish" title="Munster Irish">Munster Irish</a>.</p>
<p>Irish <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> has been studied as a discipline since the late 19th century, with numerous researchers publishing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_linguistics" title="Descriptive linguistics">descriptive</a> accounts of dialects from all regions where the language is spoken. More recently, Irish phonology has been the focus of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_linguist" title="Theoretical linguist">theoretical linguists</a>, who have produced a number of books, articles, and doctoral theses on the topic.</p>
<p>One of the most important aspects of Irish phonology is that almost all <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonants</a> come in pairs, with one having a "broad" pronunciation and the other a "slender" one. Broad consonants are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velarized" title="Velarized">velarized</a>, that is, the back of the tongue is pulled back and slightly up in the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">soft palate</a> while the consonant is being articulated. Slender consonants are <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">palatalized</a>, which means the tongue is pushed up toward the <a href="/wiki/Hard_palate" title="Hard palate">hard palate</a> during the articulation. The contrast between broad and slender consonants is crucial in Irish, because the meaning of a word can change if a broad consonant is substituted for a slender consonant or vice versa. For example, the only difference in pronunciation between the words <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bó</i></span> ('cow') and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>beo</i></span> ('alive') is that <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bó</i></span> is pronounced with a broad <i>b</i> sound, while <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>beo</i></span> is pronounced with a slender <i>b</i> sound. The contrast between broad and slender consonants plays a critical role not only in distinguishing the individual consonants themselves, but also in the pronunciation of the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a>, in the determination of which consonants can stand next to which other consonants, and in the behavior of words that begin with a vowel. This broad/slender distinction is similar to the hard/soft one of several Slavic languages, like <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>.</p>
<p>The Irish language shares a number of phonological characteristics with its nearest linguistic relatives, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>, as well as with <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a>, the language with which it is most closely in <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">contact</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_discipline">History of the discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Die_araner_mundart.djvu&amp;page=7"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3509" data-file-width="2257" height="352" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Die_araner_mundart.djvu/page7-220px-Die_araner_mundart.djvu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Die_araner_mundart.djvu/page7-330px-Die_araner_mundart.djvu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Die_araner_mundart.djvu/page7-440px-Die_araner_mundart.djvu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Die_araner_mundart.djvu?page=7" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Title page of <i>Die araner Mundart. Ein Beitrag zur Erforschung des Westirischen</i> (The Aran Dialect: A Contribution to the Study of West Irish) (<a href="#CITEREFFinck1899">Finck 1899</a>).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Until the end of the nineteenth century, linguistic discussions of Irish focused either on the traditional grammar of the language (issues like the <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflection</a> of nouns, verbs and adjectives) or on the <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">historical</a> development of sounds from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" title="Proto-Celtic">Proto-Celtic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a>. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_linguistics" title="Descriptive linguistics">descriptive</a> analysis of the phonology of an Irish dialect was <a href="#CITEREFFinck1899">Finck (1899)</a>, which was based on <a href="/wiki/Franz_Nikolaus_Finck" title="Franz Nikolaus Finck">the author</a>'s fieldwork in the <a href="/wiki/Aran_Islands" title="Aran Islands">Aran Islands</a>. This was followed by <a href="#CITEREFQuiggin1906">Quiggin (1906)</a>, a phonetic description of the dialect of Meenawannia near <a href="/wiki/Glenties" title="Glenties">Glenties</a>, County Donegal. <a href="#CITEREFPedersen1909">Pedersen (1909)</a> is predominantly a historical account, but has some description of modern dialects as well. <a href="/wiki/Alf_Sommerfelt" title="Alf Sommerfelt">Alf Sommerfelt</a> published early descriptions of both <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Irish" title="Ulster Irish">Ulster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munster_Irish" title="Munster Irish">Munster varieties</a> (<a href="#CITEREFSommerfelt1922">Sommerfelt 1922</a> and <a href="#CITEREFSommerfelt1965">Sommerfelt 1965</a> for the village of Torr in <a href="/wiki/Gweedore" title="Gweedore">Gweedore</a>, <a href="#CITEREFSommerfelt1927">Sommerfelt 1927</a> for Munster, and <a href="#CITEREFSommerfelt1929">Sommerfelt 1929</a> for the now extinct dialect of South <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">Armagh</a>). The dialect of <a href="/wiki/Dunquin" title="Dunquin">Dunquin</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Dingle_Peninsula" title="Dingle Peninsula">Dingle Peninsula</a> was described by <a href="#CITEREFSjoestedt1931">Sjoestedt (1931)</a>. From 1944 to 1968 the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Institute_for_Advanced_Studies" title="Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies">Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies</a> published a series of monographs, each describing the phonology of one local dialect: <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_Cu.C3.ADv1944">Ó Cuív (1944)</a> for West Muskerry in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ballyvourney" title="Ballyvourney">Ballyvourney</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coolea" title="Coolea">Coolea</a> and vicinity), <a href="#CITEREFde_Bhaldraithe1966">de Bhaldraithe (1966)</a> (first published 1945) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cois_Fhairrge" title="Cois Fhairrge">Cois Fhairrge</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Galway" title="County Galway">County Galway</a> (<a href="/wiki/Barna" title="Barna">Barna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiddal" title="Spiddal">Spiddal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inverin" title="Inverin">Inverin</a> and vicinity), <a href="#CITEREFBreatnach1947">Breatnach (1947)</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An_Rinn" title="An Rinn">An Rinn</a>, County Waterford, <a href="#CITEREFde_B.C3.BArca1958">de Búrca (1958)</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tourmakeady" title="Tourmakeady">Tourmakeady</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Mayo" title="County Mayo">County Mayo</a>, <a href="#CITEREFWagner1959">Wagner (1959)</a> for <a href="/wiki/Teelin" title="Teelin">Teelin</a>, County Donegal, <a href="#CITEREFMhac_an_Fhailigh1968">Mhac an Fhailigh (1968)</a> for <a href="/wiki/Erris" title="Erris">Erris</a> in County Mayo. More recent descriptive phonology has been published by <a href="#CITEREFLucas1979">Lucas (1979)</a> for <a href="/wiki/Rosguill" title="Rosguill">Rosguill</a> in northern Donegal, <a href="#CITEREFHughes1986">Hughes (1986)</a> for Tangaveane and Commeen (also near Glenties), <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_Curn.C3.A1in1996">Ó Curnáin (1996)</a> for Iorras Aithneach in <a href="/wiki/Connemara" title="Connemara">Connemara</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kilkieran" title="Kilkieran">Kilkieran</a> and vicinity), and <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_S.C3.A92000">Ó Sé (2000)</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Dingle_Peninsula" title="Dingle Peninsula">Dingle Peninsula</a> in County Kerry.</p>
<p>Research into the theoretical phonology of Irish began with <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_SiadhailWigger1975">Ó Siadhail &amp; Wigger (1975)</a>, which follows the principles and practices of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_Pattern_of_English" title="The Sound Pattern of English">The Sound Pattern of English</a></i> and which formed the basis of the phonology sections of <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_Siadhail1989">Ó Siadhail (1989)</a>. Dissertations examining Irish phonology from a theoretical point of view include <a href="#CITEREFN.C3.AD_Chios.C3.A1in1991">Ní Chiosáin (1991)</a>, <a href="#CITEREFGreen1997">Green (1997)</a> in <a href="/wiki/Optimality_theory" title="Optimality theory">optimality theory</a>, and <a href="#CITEREFCyran1997">Cyran (1997)</a> and <a href="#CITEREFBloch-Rozmej1998">Bloch-Rozmej (1998)</a> in <a href="/wiki/Government_phonology" title="Government phonology">government phonology</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On-_and_offglides">On- and offglides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: On- and offglides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Broad (velar or velarized) consonants have a noticeable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velar_approximant" title="Velar approximant">velar offglide</a> (a very short vowel-like sound) before <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">front vowels</a>, which sounds like the English <i>w</i> but made without rounding the lips. The IPA symbol for this sound is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɰ]</span>. Thus <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>naoi</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n̪ˠiː/</span> ('nine') and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>caoi</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kiː/</span> ('way, manner') are pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[n̪ˠɰiː]</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kɰiː]</span>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This velar offglide is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labialized" title="Labialized">labialized</a> (pronounced with lip-rounding, like <i>w</i>) after <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">labial consonants</a>, so <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>buí</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bˠiː/</span> ('yellow') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠwiː]</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, slender (palatal or palatalized) consonants have a <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">palatal offglide</a> (like English <i>y</i>) before <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">back vowels</a>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tiubha</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʲuː/</span> ('thick') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʲjuː]</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When a broad consonant follows a front vowel, there is a very short vowel sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə̯]</span> (called an onglide) just before the consonant, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>díol</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʲiːl̪ˠ/</span> ('sell') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʲiːə̯l̪ˠ]</span>. Similarly, when a slender consonant follows a back vowel, there is an onglide <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i̯]</span> before the consonant, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>áit</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aːtʲ/</span> ('place') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[aːi̯tʲ]</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>óil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/oːlʲ/</span> ('drinking' <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">gen.</a>) is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[oːi̯lʲ]</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>meabhair</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mʲəuɾʲ/</span> ('understanding') is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[mʲəui̯ɾʲ]</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dúinn</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d̪ˠuːn̠ʲ/</span> ('to us') is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d̪ˠuːi̯n̠ʲ]</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allophones">Allophones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Allophones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> (which can be written as ⟨bh⟩, ⟨mh⟩, or ⟨v⟩) has two basic <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophones</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labiovelar_approximant" title="Labiovelar approximant">labiovelar approximant</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w]</span> and the velarized <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">voiced labiodental fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[vˠ]</span>. The distribution of these allophones varies from dialect to dialect. In Munster, generally only <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[vˠ]</span> is found,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and in Ulster generally only <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w]</span> is found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In Connacht, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w]</span> is found word-initially before vowels (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bhfuil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[wɪlʲ]</span> 'is') and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[vˠ]</span> in other positions (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>naomh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[n̪ˠiːvˠ]</span> 'saint', <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fómhar</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈfˠuːvˠəɾˠ]</span> 'autumn', and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bhrostaigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈvˠɾˠɔsˠt̪ˠə]</span> 'hurried'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>The remaining labial fricatives are typically <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">labiodental</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[fˠ, fʲ, vʲ]</span>, but they as well as the fricative allophone <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[vˠ]</span> of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> have <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">bilabial</a> allophones <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɸˠ, ɸʲ, βˠ, βʲ]</span> in many dialects; the distribution depends partly on environment (bilabials are more likely to be found adjacent to rounded vowels) and partly on the individual speaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the coronals, most are <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">alveolar</a>, but the broad stops and lateral are typically <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t̪ˠ, d̪ˠ, n̪ˠ, l̪ˠ]</span>, and the slender coronal fricative is typically <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">postalveolar</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʃ]</span>. The slender coronal stops <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʲ, dʲ/</span> may be realized as <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">alveolo-palatal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">affricates</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tɕ, dʑ]</span> in a number of dialects, including Tourmakeady,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Erris,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and Teelin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The slender dorsal stops <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/c, ɟ, ɲ/</span> may be articulated as true <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">palatals</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[c, ɟ, ɲ]</span> or as <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">palatovelars</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[k̟, ɡ˖, ŋ˖]</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phoneme <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> has three allophones in most dialects: a <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">palatal approximant</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[j]</span> before vowels besides <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span> and at the ends of <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllables</a> (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dheas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[jasˠ]</span> 'nice', <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>beidh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bʲɛj]</span> 'will be'); a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative" title="Voiced palatal fricative">voiced (post)palatal fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʝ]</span> before consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ghrian</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʝɾʲiən̪ˠ]</span> 'sun'); and an intermediate sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[j˔]</span> (with more <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">frication</a> than <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[j]</span> but less frication than <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʝ]</span>) before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span> (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dhírigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[j˔iːɾʲə]</span> 'straightened').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>As in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voiceless</a> <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stops</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">aspirated</a> (articulated with a puff of air immediately upon release) at the start of a word, while voiced stops may be incompletely voiced but are never aspirated. Voiceless stops are unaspirated after <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠ/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scanradh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[sˠkauɾˠə]</span><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_scanradh"><a href="#endnote_scanradh">1</a></sup> 'terror'); however, stops remain aspirated after the <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">clitic</a> <i>is</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠ/</span> (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>is cam</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[sˠkʰaum]</span> 'it's crooked').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Several researchers (e.g. <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_Cu.C3.ADv1944">Ó Cuív 1944</a>, <a href="#CITEREFWagner1959">Wagner 1959</a>, <a href="#CITEREFde_Bhaldraithe1966">de Bhaldraithe 1966</a>, <a href="#CITEREFMhac_an_Fhailigh1968">Mhac an Fhailigh 1968</a>, and <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_S.C3.A92000">Ó Sé 2000</a>) use transcriptions like <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sb sd sɡ xd/</span>, etc., indicating they consider the stops that occur after voiceless fricatives to be devoiced allophones of the voiced stops rather than unaspirated allophones of the voiceless stops (as is actually the case in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>), but this is a minority view.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortis_and_lenis_sonorants">Fortis and lenis sonorants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fortis and lenis sonorants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">sonorants</a> (those spelled ⟨l n r m⟩) were divided not only into broad and slender types, but also into <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">fortis and lenis</a> types. The precise phonetic definition of these terms is somewhat vague, but the coronal fortis sounds (those spelled ⟨l n r⟩) were probably longer in duration and may have had a larger area of contact between the tongue and the roof of the mouth than the lenis sounds. Fortis <i>m</i> was probably a normal <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[m]</span>, while lenis <i>m</i> was a nasalized semivowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w̃]</span>, perhaps tending towards a nasalized fricative <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ṽ]</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[β̃]</span> when palatalized. By convention, the fortis coronals are transcribed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_letters" title="Capital letters">capital letters</a> ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">L N R</span>⟩, the lenis with lower case ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">l n r</span>⟩ (some authors, such as <a href="#CITEREFStifter2006">Stifter 2006</a>, instead use Latin ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">l n r m</span>⟩ for fortis and Greek ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">λ ν ρ μ</span>⟩ for lenis). Thus Old Irish had four <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_consonant" title="Rhotic consonant">rhotic</a> phonemes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/Rˠ, Rʲ, rˠ, rʲ/</span>, four <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">lateral</a> phonemes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/Lˠ, Lʲ, lˠ, lʲ/</span>, and four coronal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">nasal</a> phonemes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/Nˠ, Nʲ, nˠ, nʲ/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Fortis and lenis sonorants contrasted with each other between vowels and word-finally after vowels in Old Irish, for example <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>berraid</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bʲeRˠɨðʲ/</span> ('he shears') vs. <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>beraid</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bʲerˠɨðʲ/</span> ('he may carry'); <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>coll</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/koLˠ/</span> ('hazel') vs. <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>col</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kolˠ/</span> ('sin'); <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>sonn</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠoNˠ/</span> ('stake') vs. <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>son</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠonˠ/</span> ('sound').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Word-initially, only the fortis sounds were found, but they became lenis in environments where <a href="/wiki/Irish_initial_mutations" title="Irish initial mutations">morphosyntactically triggered lenition</a> was found: <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>rún</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/Rˠuːnˠ/</span> ('mystery') vs. <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>a rún</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a rˠuːnˠ/</span> ('his mystery'), <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>lón</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/Lˠoːnˠ/</span> ('provision') vs. <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga"><i>a lón</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a lˠoːnˠ/</span> ('his provision').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the modern language, the four rhotics have been reduced to two in all dialects, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/Rˠ, Rʲ, rˠ/</span> having merged as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾˠ/</span>. For the laterals and nasals, some dialects have kept all four distinct, while others have reduced them to three or two distinct phonemes, as summarized in the following table.</p>

<p>As for fortis and lenis <i>m</i>, in time the lenis version (nasalized semivowel or labial fricative) came to be pronounced as a regular semivowel or fricative along with nasalization of the preceding vowel. The later loss of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> between vowels has resulted in phonemically nasalized vowels in some modern dialects (see <a href="#Nasalized_vowels">below</a>), but these are not robustly maintained in any dialect; the strong tendency is to eliminate the nasalization entirely. The original nasalized semivowel is still reflected as ⟨mh⟩ in the spelling, however.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowel</a> sounds vary from dialect to dialect, but in general Connacht and Munster at least agree in having the <a href="/wiki/Monophthong" title="Monophthong">monophthongs</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uː/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʊ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/eː/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɛ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/oː/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɔ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span>, and <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span>), which is found only in <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">unstressed</a> syllables; and the falling <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əi/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əu/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iə/</span>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uə/</span>.</p>
<p>The vowels of <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Irish" title="Ulster Irish">Ulster Irish</a> are more divergent and are not discussed in this article.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel_backness">Vowel backness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vowel backness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vowel_backness" title="Vowel backness">backness</a> of vowels (that is, the horizontal position of the highest point of the tongue) depends to a great extent on the quality (broad or slender) of adjacent consonants. Some researchers (e.g. <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_SiadhailWigger1975">Ó Siadhail &amp; Wigger 1975</a>:80–82, <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_Siadhail1989">Ó Siadhail 1989</a>:35–37, <a href="#CITEREFN.C3.AD_Chios.C3.A1in1994">Ní Chiosáin 1994</a>) have argued that <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ]</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʊ]</span> are actually allophones of the same phoneme, as are <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ]</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔ]</span>, as in a <a href="/wiki/Vertical_vowel_system" title="Vertical vowel system">vertical vowel system</a>. Under this view, these phonemes are <a href="/wiki/Underspecification" title="Underspecification">not marked</a> at an abstract level as either <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">front vowels</a> or <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">back vowels</a>. Rather, they acquire a specification for frontness or backness from the consonants around them. In this article, however, the more traditional assumption that <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ, ʊ, ɛ, ɔ/</span> are four distinct phonemes will be followed. The descriptions of the allophones in this section come from <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_S.C3.A92000">Ó Sé (2000</a>:20–24); the pronunciations therefore reflect the Munster accent of the <a href="/wiki/Dingle_Peninsula" title="Dingle Peninsula">Dingle Peninsula</a>. Unless otherwise noted, however, they largely hold for other Munster and Connacht accents as well.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Close_vowels">Close vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Close vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The four <a href="/wiki/Close_vowel" title="Close vowel">close vowel</a> phonemes of Irish are the fully close <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uː/</span>, and the <a href="/wiki/Near-close_vowel" title="Near-close vowel">near-close</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʊ/</span>. Their exact pronunciation depends on the quality of the surrounding consonants. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span> is realized as a <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">front</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[iː]</span> between two slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tír</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʲiːrʲ]</span> 'country'). Between a slender and a broad consonant, the tongue is retracted slightly from this position (for which the IPA symbol is <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i̠ː]</span>), e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>díol</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʲi̠ːl̪ˠ]</span> ('sale'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>caoire</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ki̠ːɾʲə]</span> ('berry' gen.). Between two broad consonants, the tongue is retracted even further, almost to the point of being a <a href="/wiki/Central_vowel" title="Central vowel">central vowel</a> (in IPA, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ïː]</span>): <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>caora</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kïːɾˠə]</span> ('sheep'). <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uː/</span> is a fully back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[uː]</span> between broad consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dún</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d̪ˠuːn̪ˠ]</span> 'fort'), but between a broad and a slender consonant, the tongue is somewhat advanced (IPA <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[u̟ː]</span>), e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>triúr</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʲɾʲu̟ːɾˠ]</span> ('three people'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>súil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[sˠu̟ːlʲ]</span> ('eye'). Between two slender consonants, it is advanced even further, to a centralized vowel (IPA <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[üː]</span>): <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ciúin</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[cüːnʲ]</span> ('quiet').</p>

<p>The near-close vowels <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʊ/</span> show a similar pattern. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ/</span> is realized between slender consonants as a front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i̞]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʲi̞ɟ]</span> ('house' <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dat.</a>). After a slender consonant and before a broad one, it is a <a href="/wiki/Near-front_vowel" title="Near-front vowel">near-front</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>giota</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈɟɪt̪ˠə]</span> ('piece'). After a broad consonant and before a slender one, it is a more retracted <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ̈]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga">tuigeann</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈt̪ˠɪ̈ɟən̪ˠ]</span> ('understands'). Finally, between two broad consonants it is a central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɨ̞]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>goirt</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡɨ̞ɾˠtʲ]</span><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_goirt"><a href="#endnote_goirt">2</a></sup> ('salty'). <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʊ/</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Near-back_vowel" title="Near-back vowel">near-back</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʊ]</span> when all adjacent consonants are broad, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dubh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d̪ˠʊvˠ]</span> ('black'), and a more centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʊ̟]</span> after a slender consonant, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>giobal</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈɟʊ̟bˠəl̪ˠ]</span> ('rag').</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid_vowels">Mid vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mid vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ga_mid_allophones.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="882" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Ga_mid_allophones.svg/220px-Ga_mid_allophones.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Ga_mid_allophones.svg/330px-Ga_mid_allophones.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Ga_mid_allophones.svg/440px-Ga_mid_allophones.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ga_mid_allophones.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The realization of the long <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_vowel" title="Close-mid vowel">close-mid vowels</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/eː/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/oː/</span> varies according to the quality of the surrounding consonants. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/eː/</span> is a front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[eː]</span> between two slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>béic</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bʲeːc]</span> 'yell'), a centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ëː]</span> between a broad and a slender consonant (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>glaoigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡl̪ˠëːɟ]</span> 'call'), and a more open centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ̝̈ː]</span> between two broad consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>baol</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠɛ̝̈ːl̪ˠ]</span> 'danger'). <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/oː/</span> ranges from a back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[oː]</span> between two broad consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fód</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[fˠoːd̪ˠ]</span> 'turf') to an advanced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[o̟ː]</span> between a broad and a slender consonant (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fóid</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[fˠo̟ːdʲ]</span> 'turf' [gen.]) to a centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[öː]</span> between two slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceoil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[cöːlʲ]</span> 'music' [gen.]).</p>
<p>The short <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_vowel" title="Open-mid vowel">open-mid vowels</a> also vary depending on their environment. Short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɛ/</span> ranges from a front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ̝]</span> between slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>beidh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bʲɛ̝ɟ]</span> 'will be') to a retracted <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ̝̈]</span> between a broad and a slender consonant (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bead</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bʲɛ̝̈d̪ˠ]</span> 'I will be', <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>raibh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɾˠɛ̝̈vʲ]</span> 'was') to a central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɘ̞]</span> when the only adjacent consonant is broad (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>croich</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kɾˠɘ̞]</span> 'cross' [dat.]). Short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɔ/</span> between two broad consonants is usually a back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔ̝]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cloch</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kl̪ˠɔ̝x]</span> ('stone'), but it is a centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ö]</span> adjacent to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">nasal consonants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">labial consonants</a>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ansan</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ən̪ˠˈsˠön̪ˠ]</span> ('there') and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bog</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠöɡ]</span> ('soft'). Between a broad and a slender consonant, it is a more open <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔ̝̈]</span>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scoil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[skɔ̝̈lʲ]</span> ('school'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>deoch</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʲɔ̝̈x]</span> ('drink').</p>
<p>Unstressed <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span> is realized as a near-close, near-front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ]</span> when adjacent to a <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">palatal consonant</a>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>píce</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈpʲiːcɪ]</span> ('pike'). Next to other slender consonants, it is a mid-centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ̽]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sáile</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈsˠaːlʲɪ̽]</span> ('salt water'). Adjacent to broad consonants, it is usually a mid central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>eolas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈoːl̪ˠəsˠ]</span> ('information'), but when the preceding syllable contains one of the close back vowels <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uː, ʊ/</span>, it is realized as a mid-centralized back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʊ̽]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dúnadh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈd̪ˠuːn̪ˠʊ̽]</span> ('closing'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>muca</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈmˠʊkʊ̽]</span> ('pigs').</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_vowels">Open vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Open vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The realization of the <a href="/wiki/Open_vowel" title="Open vowel">open vowels</a> varies according to the quality of the surrounding consonants; there is a significant difference between Munster dialects and Connacht dialects as well. In Munster, long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span> and short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> have approximately the same range of realization: both vowels are relatively back in contact with broad consonants and relatively front in contact with slender consonants. Specifically, long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span> in word-initial position and after broad consonants is a back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑː]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>áit</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑːtʲ]</span> ('place'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>trá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t̪ˠɾˠɑː]</span> ('beach'). Between a slender and a broad consonant, it is a retracted front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[a̠ː]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gearrfaidh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈɟa̠ːɾˠhəɟ]</span> ('will cut'), while between two slender consonants it is a fully front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[aː]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>a Sheáin</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə çaːnʲ]</span> ('John' <a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">voc.</a>). In Dingle, the back allophone is rounded to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɒː]</span> after broad labials, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠɒːn̪ˠ]</span> ('white'), while in <a href="/wiki/Ring,_County_Waterford" title="Ring, County Waterford">Ring, County Waterford</a>, rounded <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɒː]</span> is the usual realization of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span> in all contexts except between slender consonants, where it is a centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɒ̈ː]</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> between two slender consonants is a front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[a]</span>, as in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gairid</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɟaɾʲədʲ]</span><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_gairid"><a href="#endnote_gairid">3</a></sup> ('short'). Between a broad and a slender consonant, it is in most cases a retracted <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[a̠]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fear</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[fʲa̠ɾˠ]</span> ('man'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>caite</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈka̠tʲə]</span> ('worn'), but after broad labials and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̪ˠ/</span> it is a centralized front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ä]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>baile</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠälʲə]</span> ('town'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>loit</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[l̪ˠätʲ]</span><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_loit"><a href="#endnote_loit">4</a></sup> ('injure'). When it is adjacent only to broad consonants, it is a centralized back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑ̈]</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>mac</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[mˠɑ̈k]</span> ('son'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>abair</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑ̈bˠəɾʲ]</span> ('say').</p>

</div>
<p>In Connacht varieties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> the allophones of short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> are consistently further front than the allophones of long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span>. In Erris, for example, short <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> ranges from a <a href="/wiki/Near-open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-open front unrounded vowel">near-open front vowel</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[æ]</span> before slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sail</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[sˠælʲ]</span> 'earwax') to an open <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[a]</span> after slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>geal</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɟalˠ]</span> 'bright') to a centralized back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑ̈]</span> between broad consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>capall</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈkɑ̈pˠəl̪ˠ]</span> 'horse'). Long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span>, on the other hand, ranges from a back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑː]</span> between broad consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bád</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠɑːd̪ˠ]</span> 'boat') to an advanced back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑ̟ː]</span> before slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fáil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[fˠɑ̟ːlʲ]</span> 'to get') to a centralized back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɑ̈ː]</span> after slender consonants (e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>breá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bʲɾʲɑ̈ː]</span> 'fine'). In <a href="/wiki/Toormakeady" title="Toormakeady">Toormakeady</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> the back allophone is rounded to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɒː]</span> after broad labials, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠɒːn̪ˠ]</span> ('white'). In Connemara, the allophones of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> are lengthened in duration, so that only vowel quality distinguishes the allophones of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> from those of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diphthongs">Diphthongs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diphthongs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="882" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg/220px-Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg/330px-Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg/440px-Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ga_ai-au_allophones.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Approximate ranges of the allophones of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əi/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əu/</span></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The starting point of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əi/</span> ranges from a near-open central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɐ]</span> after broad consonants to an open-mid centralized front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ̈]</span> after slender consonants, and its end point ranges from a near-close near-front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ]</span> before slender consonants to a centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ̈]</span> before broad consonants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Examples include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cladhaire</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kl̪ˠɐɪɾʲə]</span> ('rogue'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gadhar</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡɐɪ̈ɾˠ]</span> ('dog'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cill</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[cɛ̈ɪlʲ]</span> ('church'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>leigheas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[lʲɛ̈ɪ̈sˠ]</span> ('cure').</p>
<p>The starting point of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əu/</span> ranges from a near-open central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɐ]</span> after broad consonants to an open-mid advanced central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɜ̟]</span> after slender consonants, and its end point ranges from a near-close near-back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʊ]</span> before broad consonants to a centralized <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʊ̈]</span> before slender consonants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Examples include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bodhar</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠɐʊɾˠ]</span> ('deaf'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>feabhas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[fʲɜ̟ʊsˠ]</span> ('improvement'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>labhairt</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[l̪ˠɐʊ̈ɾʲtʲ]</span> ('speak'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>meabhair</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[mʲɜ̟ʊ̈ɾʲ]</span> ('memory'). In West Muskerry and the Dingle Peninsula, however, the starting point of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əu/</span> is rounded and further back after broad consonants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gabhar</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡɔʊɾˠ]</span> ('goat').</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="882" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg/220px-Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg/330px-Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg/440px-Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ga_ia-ua_allophones.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Approximate ranges of the allophones of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iə/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uə/</span></div>

<p>The starting point of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iə/</span> ranges from a close front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i]</span> after slender consonants to a retracted <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i̠]</span> after word-initial broad <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾˠ/</span> (the only context in which it appears after a broad consonant). Its end point ranges from a mid central <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span> before broad consonants to a close-mid centralized front <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ë]</span> before slender consonants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Examples include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ciall</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ciəl̪ˠ]</span> ('sense'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>riamh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɾˠi̠əvˠ]</span> ('ever'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>diabhail</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʲiëlʲ]</span> ('devils').</p>
<p>The starting point of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uə/</span> is consistently a close back <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[u]</span> while the end point ranges from <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɐ]</span> to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ̽]</span>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>thuas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[huɐsˠ]</span> ('above'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>uan</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[uən̪ˠ]</span> ('lamb'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>buail</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bˠuɪ̽lʲ]</span> ('strike').</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nasalized_vowels">Nasalized vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nasalized vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In general, vowels in Irish are <a href="/wiki/Nasalization" title="Nasalization">nasalized</a> when adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">nasal consonants</a>. For some speakers, there are reported to be <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">minimal pairs</a> between <a href="/wiki/Nasal_vowel" title="Nasal vowel">nasal vowels</a> and oral vowels, indicating that nasal vowels are also separate phonemes; these generally result from an earlier nasalized semivowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w̃]</span> (historically the lenited version of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/m/</span>), that has since been lost. <p>In addition, where a vowel is nasalized because it is adjacent to a nasal consonant, it often retains its nasalization in related forms where the consonant is no longer nasal. For example, the nasal <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/m/</span> of <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>máthair</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈmãːhəɾʲ]</span> ('mother') is replaced by nonnasal <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> in the phrase <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>a mháthair</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə ˈwãːhəɾʲ]</span> ('his mother'), but the vowel remains nasalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Similarly, in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sneachta</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈʃnʲãxt̪ˠə]</span> ('snow') the vowel after the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nʲ/</span> is nasalized, while in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>an tsneachta</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə ˈtʲɾʲãxt̪ˠə]</span> ('the snow' gen.), the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nʲ/</span> is replaced by <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾʲ/</span> in some northern dialects, but the nasalized vowel remains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonotactics">Phonotactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Phonotactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most interesting aspects of Irish <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">phonotactics</a> revolve around the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">consonant clusters</a>. Here it is important to distinguish between clusters that occur at the beginnings of words and those that occur after vowels, although there is overlap between the two groups.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word-initial_consonant_clusters">Word-initial consonant clusters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Word-initial consonant clusters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>

</div>
<p>Irish words can begin with clusters of two or three consonants. In general, all the consonants in a cluster agree in their quality, i.e. either all are broad or all are slender. Two-consonant clusters consist of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obstruent_consonant" title="Obstruent consonant">obstruent consonant</a> followed by a <a href="/wiki/Liquid_consonant" title="Liquid consonant">liquid</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">nasal consonant</a> (however, labial obstruents may not be followed by a nasal); examples (from <a href="#CITEREFN.C3.AD_Chios.C3.A1in1999">Ní Chiosáin 1999</a>) include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bleán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bʲlʲaːnˠ/</span> ('milking'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>breá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bʲɾʲaː/</span> ('fine'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cnaipe</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈkn̪ˠapʲə/</span> ('button'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dlí</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʲlʲiː/</span> ('law'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gnáth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡn̪ˠaː/</span> ('usual'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>pleidhce</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈpʲlʲəicə/</span> ('idiot'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>slios</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃlʲɪsˠ/</span> ('slice'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sneachta</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈʃnʲaxt̪ˠə/</span> ('snow'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tlúth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t̪ˠl̪ˠuː/</span> ('poker'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tnúth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t̪ˠn̪ˠuː/</span> ('long for'). In addition, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠ/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> may be followed by a voiceless stop, as in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sparán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈsˠpˠaɾˠaːn̪ˠ/</span> ('purse') and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scéal</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃceːl̪ˠ/</span> ('story'). Further, the cluster <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mˠn̪ˠ/</span> occurs in the word <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>mná</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mˠn̪ˠaː/</span> ('women') and a few forms related to it. Three-consonant clusters consist of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠ/</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> plus a voiceless stop plus a liquid. Examples include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scliúchas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈʃclʲuːxəsˠ/</span> ('rumpus'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scread</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃcɾʲad̪ˠ/</span> ('scream'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>splanc</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠpˠl̪ˠaŋk/</span> ('flash'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>spraoi</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠpˠɾˠiː/</span> ('fun'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>stríoc</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃtʲɾʲiːk/</span> ('streak').</p>
<p>One exception to quality agreement is that broad <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠ/</span> is found before slender labials (and for some speakers in Connemara and Dingle before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/c/</span> as well<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>). Examples include: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sméara</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠmʲeːɾˠə/</span> ('berries'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>speal</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠpʲal/</span> ('scythe'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>spleách</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠpʲlʲaːx/</span> ('dependent'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>spreag</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠpʲɾʲaɡ/</span> ('inspire'), and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scéal</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃceːl̪ˠ/ ~ /sˠceːl̪ˠ/</span> ('story').</p>
<p>In the environment of an initial <a href="/wiki/Consonant_mutation" title="Consonant mutation">consonant mutation</a>, there is a much wider range of possible onset clusters;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> for example, in a lenition environment the following occur: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bhlas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/wl̪ˠasˠ/</span> ('tasted'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bhris</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/vʲɾʲɪʃ/</span> ('broke'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>chleacht</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/çlʲaxt̪ˠ/</span> ('practiced'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>chrom</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xɾˠɔmˠ/</span> ('bent'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ghreamaigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈjɾʲamˠə/</span> ('stuck'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ghníomhaigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈjnʲiːwə/</span> ('acted'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>shleamhnaigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/hlʲəun̪ˠə/</span> ('slipped'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>shnámh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/hn̪ˠaːw/</span> ('swam'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>shroich</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/hɾˠɪç/</span> ('reached'). In an eclipsis environment, the following are found: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>mbláth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mˠl̪ˠaː/</span> ('flower'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>mbliana</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈmʲlʲiən̪ˠə/</span> ('years'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>mbrisfeá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈmʲɾʲɪʃaː/</span> ('you would break'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ndlúth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n̪ˠl̪ˠuː/</span> ('warp'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ndroichead</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈn̪ˠɾˠɔhəd̪ˠ/</span> ('bridge'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ndréimire</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈnʲɾʲeːmʲəɾʲə/</span> ('ladder'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ngléasfá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈɲlʲeːsˠaː/</span> ('you would dress'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ngreadfá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈɲɾʲat̪ˠaː/</span> ('you would leave'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ngníomhófá</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈɲnʲiːwoːhaː/</span> ('you would act').</p>
<p>In Donegal, Mayo, and Connemara dialects (but not usually on the Aran Islands), the coronal nasals <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nˠ, nʲ/</span> can follow only <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠ, ʃ/</span> respectively in a word-initial cluster. After other consonants, they are replaced by <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾˠ, ɾʲ/</span>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cnoc</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kɾˠʊk/</span> ('hill'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>mná</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mˠɾˠaː/</span> ('women'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gnaoi</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡɾˠiː/</span> ('liking'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tnúth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t̪ˠɾˠuː/</span> ('long for').</p>
<p>Under lenition, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠn̪ˠ, ʃnʲ/</span> become <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/hn̪ˠ, hnʲ/</span> as expected in these dialects, but <a href="/wiki/Irish_initial_mutations#After_proclitics" title="Irish initial mutations">after the definite article <i>an</i></a> they become <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t̪ˠɾˠ, tʲɾʲ/</span>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sneachta</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃnʲaxt̪ˠə/</span> ('snow'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>shneachta</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/hnʲaxt̪ˠə/</span> ('snow' [lenited form]), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>an tsneachta</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə tʲɾʲaxt̪ˠə/</span> ('the snow' gen.).</p>

<p>Like word-initial consonant clusters, post-vocalic consonant clusters usually agree in broad or slender quality. The only exception here is that broad <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾˠ/</span>, not slender <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾʲ/</span>, appears before the slender coronals <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʲ, dʲ, ʃ, nʲ, lʲ/</span>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>beirt</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bʲɛɾˠtʲ/</span> ('two people'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceird</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ceːɾˠdʲ/</span> ('trade'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>doirse</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈd̪ˠoːɾˠʃə/</span> ('doors'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>doirnín</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d̪ˠuːɾˠˈnʲiːnʲ/</span> ('handle'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>comhairle</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈkuːɾˠlʲə/</span> ('advice').</p>
<p>A cluster of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾˠ, ɾʲ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̪ˠ, lʲ/</span>, or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n̪ˠ, nʲ/</span> followed by a labial or dorsal consonant (except the voiceless stops <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pˠ, pʲ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k, c/</span>) is broken up by an <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">epenthetic</a> vowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>borb</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈbˠɔɾˠəbˠ/</span> ('abrupt'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gorm</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈɡɔɾˠəmˠ/</span> ('blue'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dearmad</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈdʲaɾˠəmˠəd̪ˠ/</span> ('mistake'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dearfa</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈdʲaɾˠəfˠə/</span> ('certain'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>seirbhís</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈʃɛɾʲəvʲiːʃ/</span> ('service'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fearg</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈfʲaɾˠəɡ/</span> ('anger'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dorcha</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈd̪ˠɔɾˠəxə/</span> ('dark'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dalba</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈd̪ˠal̪ˠəbˠə/</span> ('bold'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>colm</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈkɔl̪ˠəmˠ/</span> ('dove'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>soilbhir</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈsˠɪlʲəvʲəɾʲ/</span> ('pleasant'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>gealbhan</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈɟal̪ˠəwən̪ˠ/</span> ('sparrow'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>binb</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈbʲɪnʲəbʲ/</span> ('venom'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Banba</i></span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈbˠan̪ˠəbə/</span> (a name for Ireland), <span <p>There is no epenthesis, however, if the vowel preceding the cluster is long or a diphthong: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fáirbre</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈfˠaːɾʲbʲɾʲə/</span> ('wrinkle'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>téarma</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈtʲeːɾˠmˠə/</span> ('term'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>léargas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈlʲeːɾˠɡəsˠ/</span> ('insight'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>dualgas</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈd̪ˠuəl̪ˠɡəsˠ/</span> ('duty'). There is also no epenthesis into words that are at least three syllables long: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>firmimint</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈfʲɪɾʲmʲəmʲənʲtʲ/</span> ('firmament'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>smiolgadán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈsˠmʲɔl̪ˠɡəd̪ˠaːn̪ˠ/</span> ('throat'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>caisearbhán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈkaʃəɾˠwaːn̪ˠ/</span> ('dandelion'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Cairmilíteach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈkaɾʲmʲəlʲiːtʲəx/</span> ('Carmelite').</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonological_processes">Phonological processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Phonological processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel-initial_words">Vowel-initial words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Vowel-initial words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vowel-initial words in Irish exhibit behavior that has led linguists to suggest that the vowel sound they begin with on the surface is not actually the first sound in the word at a more abstract level. Specifically, when a clitic ending in a consonant precedes a word beginning with the vowel, the consonant of the clitic surfaces as either broad or slender, depending on the specific word in question. For example, the <i>n</i> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">definite article</a> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>an</i></span> ('the') is slender before the word <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>iontais</i></span> ('wonder') but broad before the word <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>aois</i></span> ('age'):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>an iontais</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ənʲ ˈiːn̪ˠt̪ˠəʃ/</span> ('the wonder' gen.) vs. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>an aois</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ən̪ˠ ˈiːʃ/</span> ('the age').</p>
<p>One analysis of these facts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> is that vowel-initial words actually begin, at an abstract level of representation, with a kind of "empty" consonant that consists of nothing except the information "broad" or "slender". Another analysis is that vowel-initial words, again at an abstract level, all begin with one of two <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">semivowels</a>, one triggering palatalization and the other triggering velarization of a preceding consonant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lengthening_before_fortis_sonorants">Lengthening before fortis sonorants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Lengthening before fortis sonorants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Where reflexes of the Old Irish <a href="#Fortis_and_lenis_sonorants">fortis sonorants</a> appear in syllable-final position (in some cases, only in word-final position), they trigger a lengthening or diphthongization of the preceding vowel in most dialects of Irish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The details vary from dialect to dialect.</p>
<p>In Donegal and Mayo, lengthening is found only before <i>rd, rl, rn</i>, before <i>rr</i> (except when a vowel follows), and in a few words also before word-final <i>ll</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> for example, <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>barr</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bˠaːɾˠ/</span> ('top'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ard</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aːɾˠd̪ˠ/</span> ('tall'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>orlach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈoːɾˠl̪ˠax/</span> ('inch'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tuirne</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈt̪uːɾˠn̠ʲə/</span> ('spinning wheel'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>thall</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/haːl̪ˠ/</span> ('yonder').</p>
<p>In Connemara,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> the Aran Islands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and Munster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> lengthening is found generally not only in the environments listed above, but also before <i>nn</i> (unless a vowel follows) and before <i>m</i> and <i>ng</i> at the end of a word. For example, the word <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>poll</i></span> ('hole') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pˠəul̪ˠ/</span> in all of these regions, while <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>greim</i></span> ('grip') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɟɾʲiːmʲ/</span> in Connemara and Aran and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɟɾʲəimʲ/</span> in Munster.</p>
<p>Because vowels behave differently before broad sonorants than before slender ones in many cases, and because there is generally no lengthening (except by <a href="/wiki/Analogy#Linguistics" title="Analogy">analogy</a>) when the sonorants are followed by a vowel, there is a variety of vowel <a href="/wiki/Alternation_(linguistics)" title="Alternation (linguistics)">alternations</a> between different related word-forms. For example, in Dingle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceann</i></span> ('head') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/cəun̪ˠ/</span> with a diphthong, but <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cinn</i></span> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">genitive</a> singular of the same word) is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ciːnʲ/</span> with a long vowel, while <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceanna</i></span> (the plural, meaning 'heads') is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈcan̪ˠə/</span> with a short vowel.</p>
<p>This lengthening has received a number of different explanations within the context of theoretical phonology. All accounts agree that some property of the fortis sonorant is being transferred to the preceding vowel, but the details about what property that is vary from researcher to researcher. <a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_SiadhailWigger1975">Ó Siadhail &amp; Wigger (1975</a>:89–90)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> argue that the fortis sonorant is <a href="/wiki/Tenseness" title="Tenseness">tense</a> (a term only vaguely defined phonetically) and that this tenseness is transferred to the vowel, where it is realized phonetically as vowel length and/or diphthongization. <a href="#CITEREFN.C3.AD_Chios.C3.A1in1991">Ní Chiosáin (1991</a>:188–95) argues that the triggering consonant is underlyingly associated with a unit of <a href="/wiki/Syllable_weight" title="Syllable weight">syllable weight</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)"><i>mora</i></a>; this mora then shifts to the vowel, creating a long vowel or a diphthong. <a href="#CITEREFCarnie2002">Carnie (2002)</a> expands on that analysis to argue that the fortis sonorants have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_tongue_root" title="Advanced tongue root">advanced tongue root</a> (that is, the bottom of the tongue is pushed upward during articulation of the consonant) and that diphthongization is an <a href="/wiki/Articulatory_phonetics" title="Articulatory phonetics">articulatory</a> effect of this tongue movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Devoicing">Devoicing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Devoicing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Where a voiced <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruent</a> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> comes into contact with <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span>, the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> is absorbed into the other sound, which then becomes voiceless (in the case of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span>, devoicing is to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/fˠ/</span>). Devoicing is found most prominently in the <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">future</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_verbs#First_conjugation" title="Irish verbs">first conjugation</a> verbs (where the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> sound is represented by the letter <i>f</i>) and in the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbal_adjective" title="Verbal adjective">verbal adjectives</a> (where the sound is spelled <i>th</i>). For example, the verb <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scuab</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠkuəbˠ/</span> ('sweep') ends in the voiced consonant <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bˠ/</span>, but its future tense <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scuabfaidh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈsˠkuəpˠəɟ/</span> ('will sweep') and verbal adjective <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scuabtha</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈsˠkuəpˠə/</span> ('swept') have the voiceless consonant <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pˠ/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sandhi">Sandhi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sandhi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Irish exhibits a number of external <a href="/wiki/Sandhi" title="Sandhi">sandhi</a> effects, i.e. phonological changes across word boundaries, particularly in rapid speech. The most common type of sandhi in Irish is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">assimilation</a>, which means that a sound changes its pronunciation in order to become more similar to an adjacent sound. One type of assimilation in Irish is found when a coronal consonant (one of <i>d, l, n, r, s, t</i>) changes from being broad to being slender before a word that begins with a slender coronal consonant, or from being slender to being broad before a word that begins with a broad coronal consonant. For example, <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>feall</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/fʲal̪ˠ/</span> ('deceive') ends with a broad <i>ll</i>, but in the phrase <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>d'fheall sé orm</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʲal̠ʲ ʃə ɔɾˠəmˠ]</span> ('it deceived me'), the <i>ll</i> has become slender because the following word, <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sé</i></span>, starts with a slender coronal consonant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>The consonant <i>n</i> may also assimilate to the <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a> of a following consonant, becoming labial before a labial consonant, palatal before a palatal consonant, and velar before a velar consonant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> For example, the <i>nn</i> of <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceann</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/can̪ˠ/</span> ('one') becomes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[mˠ]</span> in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceann bacach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[camˠ ˈbˠakəx]</span> ('a lame one') and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋ]</span> in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ceann carrach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[caŋ ˈkaɾˠəx]</span> ('a scabbed one'). A voiced consonant at the end of a word may become voiceless when the next word begins with a voiceless consonant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> as in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>lúb sé</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[l̪ˠuːpˠ ʃeː]</span> ('he bent'), where the <i>b</i> sound of <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>lúb</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̪ˠuːbˠ/</span> ('bent') has become a <i>p</i> sound before the voiceless <i>s</i> of <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sé</i></span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_facts_of_stress_placement">General facts of stress placement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: General facts of stress placement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An Irish word normally has only one <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stressed</a> syllable, namely the first syllable of the word. In IPA transcription, a stressed syllable is marked with the symbol <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ ˈ ]</span> to the left of the syllable. Examples include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>d'imigh</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈdʲɪmʲiː/</span> ('left' [past tense of <i>leave</i>]) and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>easonóir</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈasˠən̪ˠoːɾʲ/</span> ('dishonor').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> However, certain words, especially <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a>, have stress on a noninitial syllable, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>amháin</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/əˈwaːnʲ/</span> ('only'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>tobac</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/təˈbak/</span> ('tobacco').</p>
<p>In most <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compound</a> words, primary stress falls on the first member and a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_stress" title="Secondary stress">secondary stress</a> (marked with <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ ˌ ]</span>) falls on the second member, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>lagphórtach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈl̪ˠaɡˌfˠɔɾˠt̪ˠəx/</span> ('spent bog'). Some compounds, however, have primary stress on both the first and the second member, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>deargbhréag</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈdʲaɾˠəɡˌvʲɾʲeːɡ/</span> ('a terrible lie').</p>
<p>In Munster, stress is attracted to a long vowel or diphthong in the second or third syllable of a word, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cailín</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kaˈlʲiːnʲ/</span> ('girl'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>achainí</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/axəˈnʲiː/</span> ('request').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> In the now-extinct accent of East Mayo, stress was attracted to a long vowel or diphthong in the same way as in Munster; in addition, stress was attracted to a short vowel before word-final <i>ll</i>, <i>m</i>, or <i>nn</i> when that word was also final in its utterance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> For example, <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>capall</i></span> ('horse') was pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kaˈpˠɞl̪ˠ]</span> in isolation or as the last word of a sentence, but as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈkapˠəl̪ˠ]</span> in the middle of a sentence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_nature_of_unstressed_vowels">The nature of unstressed vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: The nature of unstressed vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In general, short vowels are all reduced to <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span>) in unstressed syllables, but there are some exceptions. In Munster, if the third syllable of a word is stressed and the preceding two syllables are short, the first of the two unstressed syllables is <i>not</i> reduced to schwa; instead it receives a secondary stress, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>spealadóir</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˌsˠpʲal̪ˠəˈd̪ˠoːɾʲ/</span> ('scythe-man').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Also in Munster, an unstressed short vowel is not reduced to schwa if the following syllable contains a stressed <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/iː/</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/uː/</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>ealaí</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aˈl̪ˠiː/</span> ('art'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bailiú</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bˠaˈlʲuː/</span> ('gather').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> In Ulster, long vowels in unstressed syllables are shortened but are not reduced to schwa, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cailín</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈkalʲinʲ/</span> ('girl'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>galún</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈɡalˠunˠ/</span> ('gallon').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes_relating_to_.2Fx.2F">Processes relating to /x/</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Processes relating to /x/">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">voiceless velar fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span>, spelled ⟨ch⟩, is associated with some unusual patterns in many dialects of Irish. For one thing, its presence after the vowel <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> triggers behavior atypical of short vowels; for another, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> and its slender counterpart <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ç/</span> interchange with the <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">voiceless glottal fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> in a variety of ways, and can sometimes be deleted altogether.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_of_.2Fax.2F">Behavior of /ax/</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Behavior of /ax/">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Munster, stress is attracted to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> in the second syllable of a word if it is followed by <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span>, <i>provided</i> the first syllable (and third syllable, if there is one) contains a short vowel (<a href="#CITEREF.C3.93_Cu.C3.ADv1944">Ó Cuív 1944</a>:66). Examples include <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>bacach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/bˠəˈkax/</span> ('lame') and <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>slisneacha</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃlʲəˈʃnʲaxə/</span> ('chips'). However, if the first or third syllable contains a long vowel or diphthong, stress is attracted to that syllable instead, and the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> is reduced to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span> as normal, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>éisteacht</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈeːʃtʲəxt̪ˠ/</span> ('listen'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>moltachán</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˌmˠɔl̪ˠhəˈxaːn̪ˠ/</span><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_moltach.C3.A1n"><a href="#endnote_moltach.C3.A1n">5</a></sup> ('wether').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Ulster, unstressed <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> is not reduced to <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga">eallach</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈal̪ˠax/</span> ('cattle').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_of_.2Fx.2F_and_.2F.C3.A7.2F_with_.2Fh.2F">Interaction of /x/ and /ç/ with /h/</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Interaction of /x/ and /ç/ with /h/">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many dialects of Irish, the voiceless dorsal fricatives <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ç/</span> alternate with <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> under a variety of circumstances. For example, as the lenition of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʲ/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> is replaced by <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ç/</span> before back vowels, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>thabharfainn</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈçuːɾˠhən̠ʲ/</span><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_thabharfainn"><a href="#endnote_thabharfainn">6</a></sup> ('I would give'), <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>sheoil</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/çoːlʲ/</span> ('drove').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> In Munster, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ç/</span> becomes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> after a vowel, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>fiche</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈfʲɪhə/</span> ('twenty').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> In Ring, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> becomes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> at the end of a monosyllabic word, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>scáth</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sˠkaːx/</span> ('fear').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> In some Ulster dialects, such as that of <a href="/wiki/Tory_Island" title="Tory Island">Tory Island</a>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> can be replaced by <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span>, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>cha</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ha/</span> ('not'), and can even be deleted word-finally, as in <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>santach</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈsˠan̪ˠt̪ˠah ~ ˈsˠan̪ˠt̪ˠa/</span> ('greedy')<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> In other Ulster dialects, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> can be deleted before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t̪ˠ/</span> as well, e.g. <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>seacht</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃat̪ˠ/</span> ('seven').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_languages">Comparison with other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Comparison with other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Gaelic_and_Manx">Scottish Gaelic and Manx</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Scottish Gaelic and Manx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the phonological processes found in Irish are found also in its nearest relatives, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>. For example, both languages contrast "broad" and "slender" consonants, but only at the coronal and dorsal <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">places of articulation</a>; both Scottish Gaelic and Manx have lost the distinction in labial consonants. The change of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kn̪ˠ ɡn̪ˠ mn̪ˠ/</span> etc. to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kɾˠ ɡɾˠ mɾˠ/</span> etc. is found in Manx and in most Scottish dialects. Evidence from written manuscripts suggests it had begun in Scottish Gaelic as early as the sixteenth century and was well established in both Scottish Gaelic and Manx by the late 17th to early 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Lengthening or diphthongization of vowels before fortis sonorants is also found in both languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> The stress pattern of Scottish Gaelic is the same as that in Connacht and Ulster Irish, while in Manx, stress is attracted to long vowels and diphthongs in noninitial syllables, but under more restricted conditions than in Munster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<p>Manx and many dialects of Scottish Gaelic share with Ulster Irish the property of not reducing unstressed <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span> before <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_phonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Hiberno-English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Irish pronunciation has had a significant influence on the features of <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Hiberno-English</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> For example, most of the vowels of Hiberno-English (with the exception of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɔɪ/</span>) correspond to vowel <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">phones</a> (which may or may not be phonemes) of Irish. The Irish stops <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t̪ˠ d̪ˠ]</span> have been taken over (though without distinctive velarization) into Hiberno-English as common realizations of the English phonemes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/θ ð/</span>. Hiberno-English also allows <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> to appear in positions where it is permitted in Irish but excluded in other <a href="/wiki/List_of_dialects_of_the_English_language" title="List of dialects of the English language">dialects of English</a>, such as before an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unstressed_vowel" title="Unstressed vowel">unstressed vowel</a> (e.g. <i>Haughey</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈhɑhi/</span>) and at the end of a word (e.g. <i>McGrath</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/məˈɡɹæh/</span>). Another feature of Hiberno-English pronunciation taken from Irish is epenthesis in words like <i>film</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈfɪləm]</span> and <i>form</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈfɒːɹəm]</span>.</p>
