<p><b>Adai</b> (1390–1438) was the Mongol <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty" title="Northern Yuan dynasty">Northern Yuan dynasty</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. After the prominent eastern Mongolian chancellor, <a href="/wiki/Arughtai" title="Arughtai">Arughtai</a>, threw his allegiance to him, he briefly reunited most of the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> under his banner.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lineage_and_early_life">Lineage and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adai_Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lineage and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of Adai’s family lineage traced back to <a href="/wiki/Kadan" title="Kadan">Kadan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hasar" title="Hasar">Hasar</a> due to the interfamily marriages within the <a href="/wiki/Borjigin" title="Borjigin">Borjigin</a> clan. Mongolian sources also say Adai was a son of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96r%C3%BCg_Tem%C3%BCr_Khan" title="Örüg Temür Khan">Örüg Temür Khan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Adai’s family was a member of eastern Mongol clans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khorchin" title="Khorchin">Khorchin</a>, originated in the region of <a href="/wiki/Nen_River" title="Nen River">Nen River</a> to the east of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khingan" title="Greater Khingan">Greater Khingan</a> Range. Even before his proclamation as the Khagan, Adai almost succeeded in unifying western Mongol territory by defeating the <a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirats</a>. Alarmed by the possible resurgence of another <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a>’s era, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> provided support to the <a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirats</a> and their allies among the western Mongol clans, successfully turning the tide by first recovering and then launching counteroffensives against eastern and central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> clans.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arughtai" title="Arughtai">Arughtai</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chingsang" title="Chingsang">chingsang</a> installed Adai on the throne as <a href="/wiki/Khagan" title="Khagan">Khagan</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty" title="Northern Yuan dynasty">Great Yuan</a> after discussing the matter with the family of Khasar.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adai_Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his rule, Adai was able to consolidate and expand his power, eventually unifying both the central and eastern Mongol territories in 1425. However, his conquest of western Mongol territory was checked by the <a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirats</a> and both sided continued the war for unification for decades. Riding on the success of unifying central and eastern Mongol territories, Adai proclaimed himself as Khagan with support of central and eastern Mongol clans in 1425, the same year his rivalry khan in the west, <a href="/wiki/Oyiradai" title="Oyiradai">Oyiradai</a> was killed. Adai and Arughtai crushed the Oirats and killed several leaders of them. After capturing the Oirat nobles, Adai, married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulichi" title="Gulichi">Gulichi</a>'s wife, Öljeitü the Beauty, who had been a consort of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elbeg_Khan" title="Elbeg Khan">Elbeg Khan</a> (r.1392–1399) and enslaved Bahamu's son (future Toghan taishi). Although Adai Khan’s authority did not fully reach western Mongols who did not recognize him as supreme ruler, western Mongol clans did not have a khan of their own so Adai Khan remained as the sole Mongol ruler for the next eight years at least in name. Elbeg's daughter and the Oirat taishi Bahamu's widow, Samar, persuaded the Khan to release her son now named Toghan to western Mongolia. However, in 1433, Toghan raised a rebellion and western Mongols finally crowned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tayisung_Khan" title="Tayisung Khan">Toghtoa Bukha</a> (or Toγtoγa Buqa) with the title of Tayisung Khan as their next new khan, which resulted in half a decade of the simultaneous existence of two khans supported by opposing Mongol clans.</p>
<p>After two decisive campaigns in 1422 and 1423, Adai Khan lost all of the territory gained in the past and in 1430, the third Oirat victory wiped out his major strength, after which he could no longer mount any effective offensives and was forced on the defensive. Both sides attempted to utilize the stalemate to prepare for the next stage but Adai Khan could not recover from the previous loss. The Oirats soon launched another round of offensives and in the fourth decisive Oirat victory in 1434 in which Adai Khan’s major advisors, Arughtai and others, were killed, Adai Khan’s downfall was certain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adai_Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1438, Adai Khan was defeated in Ejene when his territory was overran by the Oirats and their allies. Adai sought refuge in the <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Genghis_Khan" title="Mausoleum of Genghis Khan">Eight White Tents</a> of <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a>, however, being unarmed, he was murdered by Togoon <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_nobility" title="Mongolian nobility">taishi</a> of the Oirat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
