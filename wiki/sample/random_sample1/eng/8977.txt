<p>The <i><b>ajaeng</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string instrument</a>. It is a wide <a href="/wiki/Zither" title="Zither">zither</a> with strings made of twisted <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, played by means of a slender stick made of <a href="/wiki/Forsythia" title="Forsythia">forsythia</a> wood, which is scraped against the strings in the manner of a <a href="/wiki/Bow_(music)" title="Bow (music)">bow</a>. The original version of the instrument, and that used in court music (called the <i><a href="/wiki/Jeongak" title="Jeongak">jeongak</a> ajaeng</i>), has seven strings, while the <i>ajaeng</i> used for <i><a href="/wiki/Sanjo_(music)" title="Sanjo (music)">sanjo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sinawi" title="Sinawi">sinawi</a></i> (called the <i>sanjo ajaeng</i>) has eight strings; some instruments may have up to nine strings.</p>
<p>The <i>ajaeng</i> is generally played while seated on the floor. It has a deep tone similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a>, but more raspy. Some contemporary players prefer to use an actual <a href="/wiki/Horsehair" title="Horsehair">horsehair</a> bow rather than a stick, believing the sound to be smoother. The instrument is used in court, aristocratic, and folk music, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_classical_music" title="Contemporary classical music">contemporary classical music</a> and film scores.</p>
<p>The <i>ajaeng</i> is derived from the Chinese <i><a href="/wiki/Yazheng" title="Yazheng">yazheng</a></i> (simplified: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%BD%A7" title="wiktionary:轧">轧</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AD%9D" title="wiktionary:筝">筝</a>; traditional: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%BB%8B" title="wiktionary:軋">軋</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AE%8F" title="wiktionary:箏">箏</a>).</p>
