<p><b>Uncle</b> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>avunculus</i></span> the diminutive of <i>avus</i> "grandfather") is a male <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> relationship or <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> within an extended or immediate family. An uncle is the <a href="/wiki/Brother" title="Brother">brother</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-brother" title="Half-brother">half-brother</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Step-brother" title="Step-brother">step-brother</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brother-in-law" title="Brother-in-law">brother-in-law</a> of one's parent. A biological uncle is a second degree male relative and shares 25% genetic overlap. However people who are not a biological uncle, are sometimes affectionately called as an uncle, as a title of admiration and respect.</p>
<p>A woman with the equivalent relationship of an uncle is an <a href="/wiki/Aunt" title="Aunt">aunt</a>. The reciprocal relationship to both of these is that of a <a href="/wiki/Nephew_and_niece" title="Nephew and niece">nephew</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nephew_and_niece" title="Nephew and niece">niece</a>.</p>
<p>A great-uncle (sometimes written as great uncle, grand-uncle or granduncle) is the brother or brother-in-law of one's grandparent.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_variations">Cultural variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cultural variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some cultures and families, children may refer to the cousins of their parents as "aunt" or "uncle". It is also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_of_respect" title="Title of respect">title of respect</a> for elders (for example older cousins, neighbors, acquaintances, close family friends, and even sometimes total strangers). Using the term in this way is a form of <a href="/wiki/Fictive_kinship" title="Fictive kinship">fictive kinship</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Albanian.2C_Slavic.2C_and_Persian">Albanian, Slavic, and Persian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Albanian, Slavic, and Persian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some cultures, like Albanian, Slavic, or Persian, no single inclusive term describing both a person's <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> to their parental male sibling or parental male in-law exists. Instead, there are specific terms describing a person's kinship to their mother's brother ("dajë" in <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a> language, "daiyee" in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>) or a person's kinship to their father's brother ("xhajë" in Albanian, "amou" in Persian). An analogous differentiation exists using separate terms to describe a person's kinship to their mother's female sibling, ("teze" in Albanian, "khaleh" in Persian), and a person's kinship to their father's female sibling, ("hallë" in Albanian, "ammeh" in Persian).</p>
<p>Furthermore, in Persian culture the terms used to describe a person's kinship to their maternal or paternal in-laws bear clear and unambiguous descriptions of that relationship, differentiating the parental in-laws from blood-relatives. For example, there is a specific term describing a person's kinship to the spouse of their paternal uncle (i.e. "zan-amou", literally 'wife-of-' amou). This clarifies that kinship is to the spouse of the person's paternal male sibling, as opposed to a blood-relationship.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish">Irish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Irish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uncles and aunts are considered important in modern Irish culture and are usually chosen to be <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godfather or godmother</a> of children during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>. A young Irish person might seek the counsel of their favourite aunt or uncle before making an important decision and the opinion of the respective aunt or uncle is treated seriously.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uncles_in_popular_culture">Uncles in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uncle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uncles in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the loving image of an old but wise and friendly uncle in many cultures the word has been used as a loving nickname for many people. In <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_mythology" title="Tibetan mythology">Tibetan mythology</a> <a href="/wiki/Akhu_T%C3%B6npa" title="Akhu Tönpa">Akhu Tönpa</a> (<i>Uncle Tompa</i>) is a familiar and well-beloved figure. The American national personification <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Sam" title="Uncle Sam">Uncle Sam</a> serves as an allegorical fatherly figure to many Americans. The American wrestler Stanley C. Frazier used <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Elmer" title="Uncle Elmer">Uncle Elmer</a></i> as his nickname. Various children's TV hosts have used <i>uncle</i> as their nickname, including <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> (<i>Uncle Walt</i>), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Davidse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bob Davidse (page does not exist)">Bob Davidse</a> (<i>Nonkel Bob</i>, literally <i>Uncle Bob</i>), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Rutten&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edwin Rutten (page does not exist)">Edwin Rutten</a> (who hosted a children's show named <i>De Show van Ome Willem</i> (<i>The Show of Uncle Willem</i>). The Dutch poet Ome Ko also used <i>uncle</i> as part of his pseudonym.</p>
<p>Rich, wise or otherwise eccentric uncles are also popular in works of fiction.</p>
