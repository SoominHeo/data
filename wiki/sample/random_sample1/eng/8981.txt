<p><b>Azerbaijan Airlines</b> (<a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_language" title="Azerbaijani language">Azerbaijani</a>: <span lang="az"><i>Azərbaycan Hava Yolları</i></span>), also known as <b>AZAL</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> and largest <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> of <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>. Based in <a href="/wiki/Baku" title="Baku">Baku</a>, adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Heydar_Aliyev_International_Airport" title="Heydar Aliyev International Airport">Heydar Aliyev International Airport</a>, the carrier operates to destinations across <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">CIS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a>. Azerbaijan Airlines is a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association" title="International Air Transport Association">International Air Transport Association</a>.</p>

<p>On 20 October 1910, a plane flew over Baku for the first time. After this introduction of aviation to Azerbaijan, it took only 13 years for the new mode of transportation to acquire an official status in the region: the Caucasus Joint Stock Company of Civil Aviation - ZAKAVIA - was established in 1923. It was founded by Mughan Melioration Construction, the Caspian Shipping Company, and AzNeft (Azerbaijan Oil Company). ZAKAVIA planes were intended to conduct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_surveys" title="Aerial surveys">aerial surveys</a>, deliver emergency goods, and carry mail.</p>
<p>In 1926, regular flights began on the <a href="/wiki/Mineralnye_Vody" title="Mineralnye Vody">Mineralnye Vody</a>–<a href="/wiki/Grozny" title="Grozny">Grozny</a>–<a href="/wiki/Makhachkala" title="Makhachkala">Makhachkala</a>–Baku–<a href="/wiki/Yevlakh" title="Yevlakh">Yevlakh</a>–Tbilisi route. Airports were opened in Baku, Yevlakh, and <a href="/wiki/Shaki,_Azerbaijan" title="Shaki, Azerbaijan">Shaki</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Dornier_Komet" title="Dornier Komet">Dornier Komet III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dornier_Komet" title="Dornier Komet">Dornier Merkur</a> planes were based. The successful completion of a flight largely depended on the skill of the pilots, as the aircraft had no navigation equipment at the time. Pilots used roads, railway lines, local terrain and other features as landmarks. Despite the imperfections, the international air-route <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a>-Baku-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandar-e_Pahlavi" title="Bandar-e Pahlavi">Bandar-e Pahlavi</a> was opened in 1929, while in 1933, the first flight to <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> ridge was carried out, shortening the distance of the route by 1100 km. A regular air route between Baku and Moscow was opened in 1937; it was operated by local aircraft <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kharkov_KhAI-1" title="Kharkov KhAI-1">Kharkov KhAI-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Putilov_Stal-3" title="Putilov Stal-3">Putilov Stal-3</a>, which carried 15 passengers a day.</p>
<p>The Baku-Kharkiv, Baku-<a href="/wiki/Grozny" title="Grozny">Grozny</a>, Baku-<a href="/wiki/Rostov-on-Don" title="Rostov-on-Don">Rostov-on-Don</a>, Baku-<a href="/wiki/Astrakhan" title="Astrakhan">Astrakhan</a>, and Baku-Tbilisi flights were introduced the following year, while the Baku airport received the status of a separate economic unit by a resolution of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_People%27s_Commissars" title="Council of People's Commissars">Council of People's Commissars</a>. On 2 June 1938, the first aviation group was established in Azerbaijan.</p>
<p>Economic development in Azerbaijan, the key oil industry centre of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, was very rapid at the time, enabling Azerbaijani aviation great confidence in the sky. It was playing an important role in the national economy, but following the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, it was largely reoriented to military ends.</p>
<p>After the war ended, Azerbaijani pilots resumed carrying passengers and cargo. New aircraft emerged on air routes originating from Baku, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lisunov" title="Lisunov">Lisunov</a> <a href="/wiki/Lisunov_Li-2" title="Lisunov Li-2">Li-2</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin" title="Ilyushin">Ilyushin</a> <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-14" title="Ilyushin Il-14">Il-14</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-18" title="Ilyushin Il-18">Il-18</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Antonov" title="Antonov">Antonov</a> <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-24" title="Antonov An-24">An-24</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-2" title="Antonov An-2">An-2</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mil_Moscow_Helicopter_Plant" title="Mil Moscow Helicopter Plant">Mil</a> <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-1" title="Mil Mi-1">Mi-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-4" title="Mil Mi-4">Mi-4</a>.</p>
<p>A defining new stage in the history of Azerbaijan's civil aviation fleet occurred in the 1970-80s. This was a time when airports fitted with soft-surface runways were built in <a href="/wiki/Ganja,_Azerbaijan" title="Ganja, Azerbaijan">Ganja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yevlakh" title="Yevlakh">Yevlakh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naftalan,_Azerbaijan" title="Naftalan, Azerbaijan">Naftalan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakhchivan_(city)" title="Nakhchivan (city)">Nakhchivan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zaqatala_(city)" title="Zaqatala (city)">Zaqatala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lankaran" title="Lankaran">Lankaran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agdam" title="Agdam">Agdam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agstafa" title="Agstafa">Agstafa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaki,_Azerbaijan" title="Shaki, Azerbaijan">Shaki</a>. They could accommodate <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev" title="Yakovlev">Yakovlev</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yak-40" title="Yak-40">Yak-40</a> jets. A total of 11 districts were linked to Baku by air routes.</p>
<p>Azerbaijan's aviation fleet was among the first to receive the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev" title="Tupolev">Tupolev</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu-134" title="Tu-134">Tu-134</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-154" title="Tupolev Tu-154">Tu-154</a> jet planes and <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-2" title="Mil Mi-2">Mil Mi-2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-8" title="Mil Mi-8">Mi-8</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helicopters" title="Helicopters">helicopters</a>.</p>
<p>In 1990, Azerbaijan announced that it was setting up its own airline, and that it would be independent of <a href="/wiki/Aeroflot" title="Aeroflot">Aeroflot</a>, the long-time provider of air services for the Soviet republics. Azerbaijan Airlines (AZAL) was officially established on August 17, 1992. Its first president was Vagif Sadykhly.</p>
<p>Formed from the regional branch of <a href="/wiki/Aeroflot" title="Aeroflot">Aeroflot</a>, Azerbaijan Airlines, also known as Azerbaijan Hava Yollari, soon spread its wings into the world outside the Soviet Union, which had been Aeroflot's exclusive domain. A scheduled Baku-<a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> route was launched in January 1991 in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Airlines" title="Turkish Airlines">Turkish Airlines</a>, and the cargo enterprise Aviasharg was created in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>.</p>
<p>AZAL inherited a huge fleet from Aeroflot, including more than 20 Soviet-made Tupolev airliners, some regional airliners and freighters, 90 light aircraft, and 50 helicopters. It was quick to lease a pair of <a href="/wiki/Boeing_727" title="Boeing 727">Boeing 727s</a>, however, that once belonged to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PanAm" title="PanAm">PanAm</a> (and were built in 1968). AZAL had an extensive involvement with Aviation Leasing Group (ALG), the U.S.-based lessor of these Boeing 727s. It had a transatlantic charter cargo joint venture with ALG's <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Airways" title="Buffalo Airways">Buffalo Airways</a>, which was also training AZAL aircrews to Western standards in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.</p>
<p>Azerbaijan worked out a deal to acquire a number of <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> airliners, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">737</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing_757" title="Boeing 757">757</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing_767" title="Boeing 767">767</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777" title="Boeing 777">777</a> models, in the mid-1990s in exchange for the amount of fuel the more efficient planes were expected to save compared with the old Tupolevs. AZAL was also operating ten smaller Yakovlev Yak-40 tri-jets, an <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin" title="Ilyushin">Ilyushin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il-76" title="Il-76">Il-76</a> freighter, and several <a href="/wiki/Antonov" title="Antonov">Antonov</a> turboprops.</p>
<p>In November 1994, AZAL began a route to <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, which, along with <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, was a key source of Western goods. It was soon also flying to <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Service to several regional destinations was suspended in mid-1998, due to low margins and the need to repair three Yak-40 aircraft.</p>
<p>With the exception of a few major cities, service to neighboring <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">CIS</a> countries was suspended in January 1999, due to debt. These routes were generally unprofitable as well, and were facing new competition from trains. According to the <a href="/wiki/Trend_News_Agency" title="Trend News Agency">Trend News Agency</a>, domestic flights accounted for only about 16 percent of AZAL's traffic in 1998.</p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_International" title="Flight International">Flight International</a></i>, the airline's fortunes took a leap forward in May 1996 when Jahangir Askerov became AZAL's director-general. A pilot himself, Askerov reorganized AZAL into five units: the airline, airports, <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a>, in-flight catering, and cargo.</p>
<p>The country invested in a new air traffic infrastructure, including a new airport terminal and new air traffic control facilities (air traffic control had to deal not only with arrivals and departures, but with more than 1,600 overflights a month, noted Flight International.) The national airline was running out of aircraft, however, due to a lack of spare parts, an official told AssA-Irada news agency. In 1999, AZAL was down to seven Tupolevs and two Boeing 727s.</p>
<p>In spite of the new infrastructure, leading foreign airlines, specifically <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a>, were shutting down their operations in the country due to high fees, poor service, and the widespread practice of bribery, reported Russia's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ITAR-TASS" title="ITAR-TASS">ITAR-TASS</a> News Agency. Azerbaijan President <a href="/wiki/Heydar_Aliyev" title="Heydar Aliyev">Heydar Aliyev</a> promised a crackdown on corruption.</p>
<p>Azerbaijan's border dispute with <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> over <a href="/wiki/Nagorno-Karabakh" title="Nagorno-Karabakh">Nagorno-Karabakh</a> had delayed financing for two new <a href="/wiki/Boeing_757" title="Boeing 757">Boeing 757s</a> from the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex-Im_Bank" title="Ex-Im Bank">Ex-Im Bank</a>. The $66 million loan guarantee was the Ex-Im Bank's first transaction for Azerbaijan, according to Air Transport Intelligence. The financing also was guaranteed by the Azerbaijan government and the <a href="/wiki/International_Bank_of_Azerbaijan" title="International Bank of Azerbaijan">International Bank of Azerbaijan</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Export_Credits_Guarantee_Department" title="Export Credits Guarantee Department">Export Credits Guarantee Department</a> guaranteed financing for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" title="Rolls-Royce plc">Rolls-Royce engines</a>, utilized by both Boeing 757s.</p>

<p>The first of the 757s was delivered in the fall of 2000. The planes offered the carrier unprecedented range, comfort, and efficiency on long-haul international routes. They also helped project a modern image to the world. Interestingly, the second Boeing 757 to be delivered arrived in December laden with medical supplies due to a recent earthquake in Azerbaijan. In January 2001, AZAL used one of the planes to begin operating a <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>-Baku route in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>.</p>
<p>Azerbaijan, a predominantly <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> country, experienced a reduction in air traffic following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9/11_terrorist_attacks" title="9/11 terrorist attacks">9/11 terrorist attacks</a> on the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. AZAL was able to remain profitable through 2001, however, and even make progress toward paying off its debt. The airline was soon shopping for more new aircraft as it retired its aging Soviet models. AZAL ordered its first Western-made helicopters in October 2002, purchasing six for EUR 52 million from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurocopter" title="Eurocopter">Eurocopter</a>. AZAL used helicopters to ferry personnel and equipment out to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_rigs" title="Oil rigs">oil rigs</a> in the Caspian Sea.</p>
<p>In July 2004, two of AZAL's airliners were impounded by <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> over a 12-year-old debt owed by Azerbaijan's Agriculture Ministry to a Turkish company. In the same month, AZAL ordered new <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukrainian</a>-made, 52-passenger Antonov <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An-140" title="An-140">An-140</a> turboprops to replenish its regional fleet, paying about $36 million for four planes.</p>
<p>The company was also ordering four aircraft from <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a>: three <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A319</a> mid-size airliners and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A319CJ" title="Airbus A319CJ">Airbus Corporate Jetliner</a>, for use as an <a href="/wiki/Air_transports_of_heads_of_state_and_government" title="Air transports of heads of state and government">official state aircraft</a>. These are all powered by <a href="/wiki/CFM_International" title="CFM International">CFM International</a> engines.</p>
<p>The construction of the <a href="/wiki/Baku_Cargo_Terminal" title="Baku Cargo Terminal">Baku Cargo Terminal</a> (BCT), completed in March 2005, has made Baku one of the major transfer points for cargo proceeding from the west eastwards and from the north southwards. The same year, the BCT became a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IATA" title="IATA">IATA</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_ground_handling" title="Aircraft ground handling">Ground Handling</a> Council and a participant in the World Business Partners Program of the <a href="/wiki/Airports_Council_International" title="Airports Council International">Airports Council International</a>.</p>
<p>Commissioned shortly afterwards were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nakhchivan_Airport" title="Nakhchivan Airport">Nakhchivan International Airport</a>, a new terminal in the Zabrat airport, and the <a href="/wiki/Ganja_International_Airport" title="Ganja International Airport">Ganja International Airport</a>. A five-star hotel was built at the <a href="/wiki/Heydar_Aliyev_International_Airport" title="Heydar Aliyev International Airport">Heydar Aliyev International Airport</a> in Baku. International airports in <a href="/wiki/Lankaran_International_Airport" title="Lankaran International Airport">Lankaran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zaqatala_(city)" title="Zaqatala (city)">Zaqatala</a> were opened in 2008.</p>
<p>The civil aviation fleet welcomed such aircraft as the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A319</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A320</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATR_42-500" title="ATR 42-500">ATR 42-500</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATR_72-500" title="ATR 72-500">ATR 72-500</a>.</p>
<p>On 22 July 2010, Boeing and Azerbaijan Airlines signed an agreement to substitute two Next-Generation 737 airplanes for one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/767-300ER" title="767-300ER">767-300ER</a> (extended range) and two 767 Freighters. Including this announcement, Azerbaijan Airlines had a total of eight Boeing airplanes on order: two <a href="/wiki/Boeing_767" title="Boeing 767">767-300ERs</a>, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_767-300F" title="Boeing 767-300F">767 Freighters</a>, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_737-900ER" title="Boeing 737-900ER">Next-Generation 737s</a> and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_787-8" title="Boeing 787-8">787-8s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2010, AZAL cancelled an order for the remaining two Next-Generation 737s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 December 2010, AZAL stopped using the Russian-made Tupolev planes.</p>
<p>In January 2011, Azerbaijan Airlines received the European service standard from the European Economic Chamber of Trade, Commerce and Industry (EEIG) by unanimous decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 2, 2011, Azerbaijan Airlines took delivery of the first of two Boeing 767-300ERs which is expected to serve the South-East Asia routes. The remaining B763 and two Boeing 767-300Fs are expected to be delivered in 2012.</p>
<p>On 23 and 24 December 2014, Azerbaijan Airlines took delivery of the 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_787-8" title="Boeing 787-8">Boeing 787 Dreamliners</a> it had on order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The airline also launched its <a href="/wiki/Premium_economy" title="Premium economy">Premium economy</a> product along with the introduction of the 787.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZAL_787_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AZAL_787-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azerbaijan_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div>
<ul>
<li>On 30 November 1995, at about 19:10 LT, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_707-320" title="Boeing 707-320">Boeing 707-323C</a>, which was registered as 4K-401, was en route from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urumqi" title="Urumqi">Urumqi</a> (<a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi_Diwopu_International_Airport" title="Ürümqi Diwopu International Airport">URC</a>), but encountered some problems with the left main <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a>. After a low pass over Baku's runway, the aircraft made a left turn in preparation for landing at the airport. Shortly afterwards, however, the plane struck light <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanchions" title="Stanchions">stanchions</a> on a road bridge and crashed into a field, killing two crew members and injuring four other people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 5 December 1995, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan_Airlines_Flight_56" title="Azerbaijan Airlines Flight 56">Azerbaijan Airlines Flight 56</a> was on a return flight between Baku and <a href="/wiki/Nakhchivan_Autonomous_Republic" title="Nakhchivan Autonomous Republic">Nakhchivan</a>. The flight departed Baku at 15:28, arriving at Nakhchivan at 16:37 after an uneventful flight. The plane was refueled and prepared for the flight back to Baku, and departed at 17:52. While climbing through a height of 60 meters after takeoff and at an airspeed of 317 km/h, the no. 1 engine failed. The co-pilot reacted by countering the left bank. The captain then assumed control of the airplane. Because the co-pilot had countered the left bank. the captain did not have the sensory information which could have alerted him that it was the left-hand engine that had failed. The airplane continued to climb through 140m, passing through a layer of <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>, after which the captain ordered the right-hand engine to be shut down. The flight engineer noticed that the power on the operating engine was decreasing. He brought the throttle back to the original position, but the engine had already stopped. Eight seconds later, the flight engineer reported that both engines had failed. The airplane had reached an elevation of 197 m and the speed had decreased to 290 km/h. The captain then decided to carry out a <a href="/wiki/Forced_landing" title="Forced landing">forced landing</a>. The plane was then maneuvered for a forced landing during which a sharp right turn was made to avoid an apartment block. In a 37-degree right bank with a 10 m/s (1960 feet/min) descent rate, the airplane crashed into a field in the southwestern outskirts Nakhchivan, 3,850 m from the runway, killing 2 crew members and 50 passengers; 30 were injured. Investigation revealed that, following the mechanical failure of the left-hand engine, the crew shut down the working right-hand engine. The Tu-134B flight deck ergonomics contributed in that the flight engineers' engine parameters are difficult to monitor in conditions of weak illumination. It was later concluded that the engine failed because it was operated for more than 30 hours with an unrepaired defect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 15 May 1997, a number of Azerbaijani soldiers were returning to camp near Ganja after a small arms practice and had begun shooting at a road sign. At the same time a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakovlev_40" title="Yakovlev 40">Yakovlev 40</a> 4K-87504 was returning from a training flight and was above the area on its approach to <a href="/wiki/Ganja_International_Airport" title="Ganja International Airport">Ganja Airport</a>. A number of stray bullets struck the Yak-40 and hit an oxygen cylinder. A fire broke out and control was lost. The aircraft crashed 5160m short of the runway, 95m left of the extended centerline. All 6 crew members died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 18 August 2000, a passenger <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_hijacking" title="Aircraft hijacking">hijacked</a> Azerbaijan Airlines Flight 154, a <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-154" title="Tupolev Tu-154">Tupolev Tu-154</a>, while the plane was on a domestic trip between Nakhichevan and Baku. The hijacker claimed to be armed with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_grenade" title="Hand grenade">hand grenade</a> and a bottle of flammable liquid. He demanded that the aircraft, which was carrying 164 passengers, be flown to Istanbul, Turkey, where he wanted to visit a hospitalized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azeri" title="Azeri">Azeri</a> political leader. The hijacker agreed to the pilot’s recommendation to refuel in Tbilisi, Georgia. However, the hijacker was overpowered by two Ministry of National Security officials who were on the plane. The plane landed in Baku, and the hijacker was arrested. He did not have a grenade but reportedly carried two bottles - one containing <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> and the other an unidentified liquid. The hijacker was identified as the chairman of the Nakhichevan branch of the opposition <a href="/wiki/Musavat" title="Musavat">Musavat Party</a>. A number of the hijacker’s largely political demands, including postponing parliamentary elections until December, were printed in an opposition newspaper the following day. Several days after the hijacking, the editor-in-chief of this paper was arrested and charged with <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a> for having maintained contacts with the hijacker. Documents relating to the hijacking were reportedly in his apartment. The hijacker, however, claimed to have acted alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 23 December 2005, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan_Airlines_Flight_217" title="Azerbaijan Airlines Flight 217">Azerbaijan Airlines Flight 217</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-140" title="Antonov An-140">Antonov An-140</a>, registered as 4K-AZ48 and en route to <a href="/wiki/Aktau" title="Aktau">Aktau</a>, plunged into the Caspian Sea about 20 miles north of Baku. All 18 passengers and five crew members died. The airline grounded its other remaining An-140 airplanes and postponed plans to purchase more of the type from Ukraine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Investigators discovered that three independent gyroscopes were not providing stabilised heading and attitude performance information to the crew early in the flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 12 August 2010, Azerbaijan Airlines Flight 75, an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A319-111</a> registered as 4K-AZ04 and bearing the name "Guba", suffered a collapse of the undercarriage when the aircraft exited runway 05-23 following arrival at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atat%C3%BCrk_International_Airport" title="Atatürk International Airport">Atatürk International Airport</a> in Istanbul. The aircraft was substantially damaged, but all 127 passengers and crew escaped unharmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</div>
