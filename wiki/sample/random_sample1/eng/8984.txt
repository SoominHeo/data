<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Azumaya algebra</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, an <b>Azumaya algebra</b> is a generalization of <a href="/wiki/Central_simple_algebra" title="Central simple algebra">central simple algebras</a> to <i>R</i>-algebras where <i>R</i> need not be a <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field</a>. Such a notion was introduced in a 1951 paper of <a href="/wiki/Goro_Azumaya" title="Goro Azumaya">Goro Azumaya</a>, for the case where <i>R</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutative_local_ring" title="Commutative local ring">commutative local ring</a>. The notion was developed further in <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">ring theory</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck" title="Alexander Grothendieck">Alexander Grothendieck</a> made it the basis for his geometric theory of the <a href="/wiki/Brauer_group" title="Brauer group">Brauer group</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbaki_seminar" title="Bourbaki seminar">Bourbaki seminars</a> from 1964-5. There are now several points of access to the basic definitions.</p>

<p>An Azumaya algebra on a scheme <i>X</i> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structure_sheaf" title="Structure sheaf">structure sheaf</a> <i>O</i><sub><i>X</i></sub>, according to the original Grothendieck seminar, is a sheaf <i>A</i> of <i>O</i><sub><i>X</i></sub>-algebras that is étale locally isomorphic to a matrix algebra sheaf; one should, however, add the condition that each matrix algebra sheaf is of positive rank. Milne, <i>Étale Cohomology</i>, starts instead from the definition that it is a sheaf <i>A</i> of <i>O</i><sub><i>X</i></sub>-algebras whose stalk <i>A</i><sub><i>x</i></sub> at each point <i>x</i> is an Azumaya algebra over the <a href="/wiki/Local_ring" title="Local ring">local ring</a> <i>O</i><sub><i>X,x</i></sub> in the sense given above. <p>where End(<i>E</i><sub>i</sub>) is the endomorphism sheaf of <i>E</i><sub><i>i</i></sub>. The Brauer group of <i>X</i> (an analogue of the <a href="/wiki/Brauer_group" title="Brauer group">Brauer group</a> of a field) is the set of equivalence classes of Azumaya algebras. The group operation is given by tensor product, and the inverse is given by the opposite algebra.</p>
<p>There have been significant applications of Azumaya algebras in <a href="/wiki/Diophantine_geometry" title="Diophantine geometry">diophantine geometry</a>, following work of <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Manin" title="Yuri Manin">Yuri Manin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Manin_obstruction" title="Manin obstruction">Manin obstruction</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Hasse_principle" title="Hasse principle">Hasse principle</a> is defined using the Brauer group of schemes.</p>
