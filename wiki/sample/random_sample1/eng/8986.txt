<p><b>Azteca Trece</b> (or Azteca 13), is a Mexican national broadcast television network owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_Azteca" title="TV Azteca">Azteca</a>, with more than 100 transmitters across the country. Azteca Trece is named for its flagship, <a href="/wiki/XHDF-TDT" title="XHDF-TDT">XHDF</a> (formerly channel 13; the network now broadcasts on channel 1.1 nationwide). Azteca Trece programming is available in Mexico through <a href="/wiki/SKY_M%C3%A9xico" title="SKY México">SKY México</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dish_M%C3%A9xico" title="Dish México">Dish México</a>, as well as all Mexican cable systems, and some Azteca Trece programming can be seen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azteca_America" title="Azteca America">Azteca America</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azteca_Mexico" title="Azteca Mexico">Azteca Mexico</a> on channel 442 on <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment_of_XHDF">Establishment of XHDF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment of XHDF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/XHDF-TDT" title="XHDF-TDT">XHDF-TDT</a></div>
<p>Azteca Trece takes its historic channel number (13) from XHDF-TV, which signed on in 1968 on channel 13. It was owned by Francisco Aguirre's <a href="/wiki/Grupo_Radio_Centro" title="Grupo Radio Centro">Organización Radio Centro</a> through concessionaire Corporación Mexicana de Radio y Televisión, S.A. de C.V. The station had fewer resources compared to its Mexico City competitors, <a href="/wiki/Telesistema_Mexicano" title="Telesistema Mexicano">Telesistema Mexicano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Televisi%C3%B3n_Independiente_de_M%C3%A9xico" title="Televisión Independiente de México">Televisión Independiente de México</a>, and relied on foreign films and series to fill out its broadcast day.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationalization_and_government_operation">Nationalization and government operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nationalization and government operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="278" data-file-width="270" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg/200px-Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg/300px-Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg/400px-Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Logo_Canal_13_Imevision_1974-1985.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This Canal 13 logo was used between 1974 and 1985</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1972, due to debts owed to the state-owned <i>Sociedad Mexicana de Crédito Industrial</i> (Mexican Industrial Credit Society or SOMEX), XHDF and concessionaire Corporación Mexicana de Radio y Televisión were nationalized.</p>
<p>The first director of the government-owned Canal 13 was Antonio Menéndez González, and after his death, he was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Gonz%C3%A1lez_Pedrero" title="Enrique González Pedrero">Enrique González Pedrero</a>, senator of the state of Tabasco from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partido_Revolucionario_Institucional" title="Partido Revolucionario Institucional">PRI</a>. Corporación Mexicana de Radio y Televisión, along with another state-owned enterprise, Tele-Radio Nacional, began receiving new television concessions as part of a national expansion of the Mexico City station into a national television network.</p>
<p>One of the first orders of business for Canal 13 was a relocation. On July 14, 1976, Canal 13's new facilities in the <a href="/wiki/Ajusco" title="Ajusco">Ajusco</a> area of Mexico City were formally inaugurated by President <a href="/wiki/Luis_Echeverr%C3%ADa" title="Luis Echeverría">Luis Echeverría</a>. The event was attended by various figures from the political and business sectors of the country, including Secretary of the Interior <a href="/wiki/Mario_Moya_Palencia" title="Mario Moya Palencia">Mario Moya Palencia</a> and Secretary of Communications and Transportation <a href="/wiki/Eugenio_M%C3%A9ndez_Docurro" title="Eugenio Méndez Docurro">Eugenio Méndez Docurro</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Azc%C3%A1rraga_Milmo" title="Emilio Azcárraga Milmo">Emilio Azcárraga Milmo</a>, Romulo O'Farrill and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miguel_Aleman_Velasco" title="Miguel Aleman Velasco">Miguel Aleman Velasco</a>, who served as directors of <a href="/wiki/Televisa" title="Televisa">Televisa</a>.</p>
<p>In 1983, the Mexican government reorganized its broadcast holdings. The result was the creation of the Mexican Television Institute, which changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Imevisi%C3%B3n" title="Imevisión">Imevisión</a> in 1985. Imevisión comprised not only Canal 13, now known as <i>Red Nacional 13</i>, but the former Televisión de la República Mexicana, with its <a href="/wiki/XEIMT-TDT" title="XEIMT-TDT">channel 22 station</a>, and a new network known as <i><a href="/wiki/Azteca_7" title="Azteca 7">Red Nacional 7</a></i> and broadcast in Mexico City by the brand-new <a href="/wiki/XHIMT-TDT" title="XHIMT-TDT">XHIMT-TV</a> channel 7.</p>
<p>During the Imevisión years, Red Nacional 13 continued to broadcast commercial programming, although it featured some programs with a cultural focus, such as <i>Temas de Garibay</i>, <i>Entre Amigos</i> with Alejandro Aura, and several programs with journalist Jorge Saldaña.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privatization">Privatization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Privatization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="199" data-file-width="188" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg/200px-Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg/300px-Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg/400px-Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Logo_Azteca_trece_XHDF_1998-2007.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This Azteca Trece logo, with variations, was used between 1994 and 2009 and is based off the Mayan numeral for 13</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1990, Imevisión collapsed the 7 and 13 national networks into one, retaining the stronger channel 13 branding. At this time, the first of two attempts to privatize Imevisión was made, meeting with no bidders.</p>
<p>In 1993, the administration of <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Salinas_de_Gortari" title="Carlos Salinas de Gortari">Carlos Salinas de Gortari</a> auctioned off Imevisión and some other government-owned media ventures in various packages. Radio Televisión del Centro, headed by electronics store owner <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Salinas_Pliego" title="Ricardo Salinas Pliego">Ricardo Salinas Pliego</a>, bought all of the TV stations. The result was the creation of Televisión Azteca, which took its name from the holding company created for the largest of the packages: the Red Nacional 13, including XHDF.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Azteca Trece is the home of most of TV Azteca's domestic output, especially <a href="/wiki/Telenovela" title="Telenovela">telenovelas</a>, entertainment programs, and news.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entertainment">Entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Azteca Trece features two entertainment programs on its weekday schedule. Its morning show, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Venga_la_Alegr%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Venga la Alegría (page does not exist)">Venga la Alegría</a></i>, airs from 7:55 to 11am and competes against similar offerings from <a href="/wiki/Las_Estrellas" title="Las Estrellas">Las Estrellas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imagen_Televisi%C3%B3n" title="Imagen Televisión">Imagen Televisión</a>. An afternoon show, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ventaneando&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ventaneando (page does not exist)">Ventaneando</a></i>, is more focused on entertainment news.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="News">News</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: News">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hechos" title="Hechos">Hechos</a></div>
<p>Azteca Trece airs three editions of <i>Hechos</i> (Facts), Azteca's primary newscast, in the morning, at lunchtime and at 9pm. All three beat Televisa's competing newscasts in the ratings in September 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Azteca_Trece_Internacional">Azteca Trece Internacional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Azteca Trece Internacional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The network also operates an international version of Azteca Trece as <b>Azteca Trece Internacional</b>, reaching 13 countries in Central and South America. On July 15, 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Radio-television_and_Telecommunications_Commission" title="Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission">CRTC in Canada</a> denied a request for Azteca Trece Internacional to be broadcast via digital cable and satellite. However, a second subsequent request was approved on January 20, 2006. The channel is available in Canada on <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Cable" title="Rogers Cable">Rogers Digital Cable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vid%C3%A9otron" title="Vidéotron">Vidéotron</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Bell_Fibe_TV" title="Bell Fibe TV">Bell Fibe TV</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Azteca_Trece_transmitters">Azteca Trece transmitters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azteca_Trece&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Azteca Trece transmitters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Azteca Trece is available on 90 of its own transmitters as well as on a subchannel of 13 <a href="/wiki/Azteca_7" title="Azteca 7">Azteca 7</a> transmitters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mexico-VC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mexico-VC-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mexico-inf-TV_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mexico-inf-TV-3">[3]</a></sup> The latter only carry Azteca 13 in standard definition.</p>
<p>As part of the national virtual channel realignment of October 2016, Azteca Trece, including in Mexico City, moved from channel 13 to channel 1. The move allowed it to leapfrog <a href="/wiki/Las_Estrellas" title="Las Estrellas">Las Estrellas</a>, its primary competitor, which remained on channel 2.</p>
