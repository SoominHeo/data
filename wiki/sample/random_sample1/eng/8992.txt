<p>The <b>akaname</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">垢嘗</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Y%C5%8Dkai" title="Yōkai">yōkai</a> depicted in <a href="/wiki/Toriyama_Sekien" title="Toriyama Sekien">Toriyama Sekien</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gazu_Hyakki_Yagy%C5%8D" title="Gazu Hyakki Yagyō">Gazu Hyakki Yagyō</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murakami_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-murakami-3">[3]</a></sup> Meaning filth (aka) licker (name), they are stated to lick the filth that collects in bathtubs and bathrooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kodansha_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kodansha-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classics">Classics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akaname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In classical yōkai depictions, children with clawed feet and cropped heads are depicted by the bath place sticking out a long tongue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These depictions do not feature any kind of explanation, so anything related to them can only be inferred, but in the Edo period <a href="/wiki/Kaidan" title="Kaidan">kaidan</a> book <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kokon_Hyakumonogatari_Hy%C5%8Dban&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kokon Hyakumonogatari Hyōban (page does not exist)">Kokon Hyakumonogatari Hyōban</a>, there are writings about a yōkai called "akaneburi" ("neburi" meaning "to lick") and it is inferred that the akaname is a depiction of this akaneburi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utah_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-utah-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murakami_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-murakami-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Kokon Hyakumonogatari Hyōban, the akaneburi is a monster that lives in old bathhouses and are said to lurk in dilapidated estates. In those times, it was believed that fish were born from water and lice were born from dirt, and seeing how fish intake water and lice eat dirt, all things were thus believed to eat the material that spawns them, the akaneburi being the ones that transform from the air of the places that gather dust and filth and therefore live by eating filth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Showa.2C_Heisei.2C_and_beyond">Showa, Heisei, and beyond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akaname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Showa, Heisei, and beyond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In literature about yōkai from the periods of Shōwa, Heisei, and beyond, akaname and akaneburi were interpreted the same way as above. These interpretations state that the akaname is a yōkai that lives in old bathhouses and dilapidated buildings<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shonensha_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-shonensha-7">[7]</a></sup> that would sneak into places at night when people are asleep<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shonensha_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-shonensha-7">[7]</a></sup> to lick using a long tongue at the filth and grime sticking to bath places and bathtubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kodansha_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-kodansha-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwai_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-iwai-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tada_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-tada-9">[9]</a></sup> It doesn't do anything other than lick at the filth, but since yōkai were considered creepy to see in any case, it is said that people worked hard to ensure that the bath places and bathtubs are washed clean so that the akaname wouldn't come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kodansha_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-kodansha-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwai_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-iwai-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There were none who saw what the akaname truly were, but since "aka" can remind people of the color red ("aka" in Japanese), they are said to have red faces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwai_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-iwai-8">[8]</a></sup> or be entirely red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tada_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-tada-9">[9]</a></sup> Also, "aka" (meaning filth) also has connotations to the idea of "impurities" such as "depravities," "sins," or "worldly desires" and other things that are not necessary, which leads to the theory that it wasn't simply a lesson to keep bath places clean, but also to keep such impurities from lurking in one's own self.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_media">In other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akaname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Filthlicker (Akaname)" is a song by the UK <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> band Sa-da-ko from their 2013 album, <i>Awakenings</i>.</p>
<p>In the video game <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Majora%27s_Mask" title="The Legend of Zelda: Majora's Mask">The Legend of Zelda: Majora's Mask</a></i>, there is a creature who lives in the hotel toilet in Clock Town that may or may not be inspired by this folk legend.</p>
<p>It has also spawned a fandom as a creature in the <i>Monster Girl Encyclopedia</i>, written by Japanese author Kenkou Cross.</p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Girl" title="Lost Girl">Lost Girl</a></i> episode "Big in Japan" (<a href="/wiki/Lost_Girl_(season_5)" title="Lost Girl (season 5)">season 5</a>, episode 3) a character almost becomes an akaname.</p>
<p>In an episode of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yo-Kai_Watch" title="Yo-Kai Watch">Yo-Kai Watch</a></i> the protagonist, Keita, ends up possessed by an Akaname and has an uncontrollable urge to lick food clean and eventually people's hair. It's only after his friend Fumi shares her salty-sweet candy with him that the Akaname leaves him alone and goes down to the store to buy more candy.</p>
<p>Akaname appear in the <a href="/wiki/Glu_Mobile" title="Glu Mobile">Glu Mobile</a> videogame <i>Samurai vs Zombies Defense</i>, in which they fight for the zombies and use their tongues to attack.</p>
