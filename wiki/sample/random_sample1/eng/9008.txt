<p><b>ARCO Arena</b> (originally called the <b>Sacramento Sports Arena</b> and later called the <b>Original ARCO Arena</b> or <b>ARCO Arena I</b> to distinguish it from its successor) was an indoor <a href="/wiki/Arena" title="Arena">arena</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>. It was the NBA's smallest arena as it held just 10,333 people and was built in 1985 to temporarily accommodate the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Kings" title="Sacramento Kings">Sacramento Kings</a>, who had relocated from <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a>.</p>
<p>Located north of Sacramento's downtown, ARCO Arena was nicknamed "The Madhouse on Market Street", and Kings games in this small venue were 100% sold out. Its official name of "ARCO Arena" is believed to be the first example of an NBA team selling <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> to a brand new facility: in this case, rights were sold to the <a href="/wiki/ARCO" title="ARCO">Atlantic Richfield Company</a>, which is now a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a>.</p>
<p>The Kings left this building in 1988 to move to the new ARCO Arena (now known as the soon-to-be-defunct <a href="/wiki/Sleep_Train_Arena" title="Sleep Train Arena">Sleep Train Arena</a>), built one mile (1.6 km) to the west. The structure survived as an office building for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sprint_Communications" title="Sprint Communications">Sprint Communications</a>. On December 19, 2005, the <a href="/wiki/California_Department_of_Consumer_Affairs" title="California Department of Consumer Affairs">California Department of Consumer Affairs</a> moved their headquarters into the building.</p>
