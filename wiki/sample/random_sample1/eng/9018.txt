<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Joseph Louis Cook</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Joseph Louis Cook</b> or <b>Akiatonhar√≥nkwen</b> (died October 1814) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_nation" title="Mohawk nation">Mohawk</a>) was an <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> leader and commissioned officer in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> during the American Revolution. Born to an African father and an <a href="/wiki/Abenaki" title="Abenaki">Abenaki</a> mother in what is now <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, he and his mother moved to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_nation" title="Mohawk nation">Mohawk</a> village in New York, where he was adopted by a family. He became an influential leader among the Mohawk and distinguished himself as a warrior for the French during the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, Cook supported the rebel colonists and joined their fight. He became the highest-ranking <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> officer in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>, achieving the rank of lieutenant colonel. He led <a href="/wiki/Oneida_people" title="Oneida people">Oneida</a> warriors, who were allied with the rebels, against the British in some actions. After the war, he settled in central New York State, where he became an important adviser to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oneida_(tribe)" title="Oneida (tribe)">Oneida</a>. He represented them and the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Nations_of_Canada" title="Seven Nations of Canada">Seven Nations of Canada</a> to negotiate with the government of New York state in trying to achieve more justice in postwar land deals. He later settled at <a href="/wiki/Akwesasne" title="Akwesasne">Akwesasne</a>, eventually a formal Mohawk reserve that straddles the New York, USA-Quebec, Canada borders.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Louis_Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cook was born as <i>Nia-man-rigounant</i> to an <a href="/wiki/Abenaki" title="Abenaki">Abenaki</a> mother and black father in Quebec. The family was taken captive in a French raid in 1745.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte-2">[2]</a></sup> A French officer planned to keep the boy as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave" title="Slave">slave</a>, but the Mohawk intervened and gave him back to his mother.</p>
<p>Out of gratitude, she took her son and followed the Mohawk warriors back to their village of <a href="/wiki/Fonda,_New_York" title="Fonda, New York">Caughnawaga</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_F.26I_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte_F.26I-3">[3]</a></sup> Cook was formally adopted by a family into the tribe and grew up learning their culture and language. In the <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_language" title="Mohawk language">Mohawk language</a>, he was named <i>Akiatonhar√≥nkwen</i>, translates as "he unhangs himself from the group."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte-2">[2]</a></sup> Over the years, Cook also learned French, as he was educated by <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> Catholic missionaries in the village.</p>
<p>Later he learned English as well. Among English-language records, he is most often referred to as <b>Louis Cook</b> or <b>Colonel Louis</b>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="French_and_Indian_War">French and Indian War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Louis_Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: French and Indian War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cook lived in the Mohawk village of Caughnawaga. He fought with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_nation" title="Mohawk nation">Mohawk nation</a> on the side of the French against the British colonists in the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>, the North American front of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A friend and minister, <a href="/wiki/Eleazer_Williams" title="Eleazer Williams">Eleazer Williams</a>, later wrote that Cook was at the battle against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Braddock_expedition" title="Braddock expedition">Braddock expedition</a> in 1755 (the Braddock party included the young <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>), and served under <a href="/wiki/Louis-Joseph_de_Montcalm" title="Louis-Joseph de Montcalm">General Montcalm</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Oswego_(1756)" title="Battle of Fort Oswego (1756)">Battle of Fort Oswego</a> in 1756.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_F.26I_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte_F.26I-3">[3]</a></sup> The same year, he was wounded in a skirmish with <a href="/wiki/Rogers%27_Rangers" title="Rogers' Rangers">Rogers' Rangers</a> near <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Fort Ticonderoga">Fort Ticonderoga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_F.26I_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte_F.26I-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cook was given his first command in the 1758 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Carillon" title="Battle of Carillon">Battle of Carillon</a>, where he was praised by General Montcalm. He was also present at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sainte-Foy" title="Battle of Sainte-Foy">Battle of Sainte-Foy</a> in 1760, serving under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_de_Gaston,_Chevalier_de_Levis" title="Francis de Gaston, Chevalier de Levis">Chevalier de Levis</a>.</p>
<p>Following the war, Cook returned to Caughnawaga and married Marie-Charlotte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCBO_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCBO-5">[5]</a></sup> As he never fully accepted the British victory and found his homeland increasingly overrun by British colonists, he moved with his family to <a href="/wiki/Akwesasne" title="Akwesasne">Akwesasne</a>, a Mohawk village on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a> in what was then Quebec.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Revolution">American Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Louis_Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: American Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Louis_Cook.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="395" data-file-width="346" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Joseph_Louis_Cook.jpg/220px-Joseph_Louis_Cook.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Joseph_Louis_Cook.jpg/330px-Joseph_Louis_Cook.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/55/Joseph_Louis_Cook.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Colonel Louis defiantly raises his <a href="/wiki/Tomahawk_(axe)" title="Tomahawk (axe)">Tomahawk</a> in John Trumbull's <i><a class="new" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Upload?wpDestFile=The_Death_of_General_Montgomery_in_the_Attack_on_Quebec_December_31,_1775.jpeg" title="The Death of General Montgomery in the Attack on Quebec December 31, 1775.jpeg">The Death of General Montgomery in the Attack on Quebec</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although the Mohawk and three other of the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> nations sided with the British during the American Revolution, hoping to expel the colonists from their lands, Cook allied with the Thirteen Colonies, as did the Oneida and Tuscarora. As early as 1775, he offered his services to General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Cook was with <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a> on his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnold%27s_expedition_to_Quebec" title="Arnold's expedition to Quebec">expedition into Quebec</a>, when he was already known as "Colonel Louis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Washington met again with Cook in 1776.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In New York, Louis Cook was present at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Oriskany" title="Battle of Oriskany">Battle of Oriskany</a>, and participated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saratoga_Campaign" title="Saratoga Campaign">Saratoga Campaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCBO_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DCBO-5">[5]</a></sup> He led a large body of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oneida_tribe" title="Oneida tribe">Oneida</a> warriors under General <a href="/wiki/Robert_Van_Rensselaer" title="Robert Van Rensselaer">Robert Van Rensselaer</a>. Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Klock%27s_Field" title="Battle of Klock's Field">Battle of Klock's Field</a>, Cook forded a river in pursuit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_Johnson" title="Sir John Johnson">Sir John Johnson</a> while General Rensselaer delayed. Infuriated, Colonel Louis shook his sword at Rensselaer and accused him of being a <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Tory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cook was with the Continental Army at <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a> in the winter of 1777. In spring 1778, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Stephen_DuPonceau" title="Peter Stephen DuPonceau">Peter Stephen DuPonceau</a> wrote of meeting Cook, dressed in American regimentals, after hearing the officer singing a French aria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In March of that year, General <a href="/wiki/Philip_Schuyler" title="Philip Schuyler">Philip Schuyler</a> sent Cook to destroy British ships at Niagara to prevent another Canadian expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nickname of "Colonel Louis" was made fact on June 15, 1779, when Cook received a commission from the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Colonel" title="Lieutenant Colonel">Lieutenant Colonel</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCBO_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-DCBO-5">[5]</a></sup> This commission was the highest rank awarded to an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">American Indian</a> during the Revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It is the only known Continental Army commission given to a man of known African descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor173_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor173-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Colonel Louis was with Lieutenant-Colonel <a href="/wiki/Marinus_Willett" title="Marinus Willett">Marinus Willett</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Johnstown" title="Battle of Johnstown">Battle of Johnstown</a> in 1781, one of the last North American battles of the Revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCBO_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-DCBO-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the war, Cook became a personal enemy of Captain <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Brant" title="Joseph Brant">Joseph Brant</a>, a Mohawk who supported the British. When each returned to their homes after the war, their personal conflict divided the Mohawk nation and brought the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Nations_of_Canada" title="Seven Nations of Canada">Seven Nations</a> of Canada and the Iroquois at what would be the Six Nations Reserve to the brink of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Louis_Cook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cook settled in the area of <a href="/wiki/Sterling,_New_York" title="Sterling, New York">Sterling, New York</a> following the war. He became an influential adviser to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oneida_tribe" title="Oneida tribe">Oneida tribe</a> because he could speak both French and English in addition to Oneida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> While living at <a href="/wiki/Onondaga,_New_York" title="Onondaga, New York">Onondaga</a>, Cook married Marguerite Thewanihattha. They had several children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCBO_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-DCBO-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cook convinced the Oneida to lease their lands, using the example of the Mohawk tribe at <a href="/wiki/Kahnawake" title="Kahnawake">Kahnawake</a>. The Oneida leased nearly 5¬†million acres (20,000¬†km<sup>2</sup>) to Colonel John Livingston for 999 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor173_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor173-15">[15]</a></sup> Conflicting claims were made on many of the Iroquois lands, and much of the land was lost to the state of New York because the Iroquois were forced to cede land, as the majority had been allies of the defeated British.</p>
<p>The Oneida named Cook and Peter Otsequette to negotiate with Governor <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(vice_president)" title="George Clinton (vice president)">George Clinton</a> for a return of- or compensation for- their land. Governor Clinton made some minor concessions to the Oneida in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Treaty_of_Fort_Schuyler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Treaty of Fort Schuyler (page does not exist)">Treaty of Fort Schuyler</a>, but generally did not yield much to the Oneida representatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Today, Cook has been criticized for negotiating bad land deals for the Oneida.</p>
<p>Despite his shortcomings in the Oneida land negotiations, between 1792 and 1796 Cook was also selected by the Seven Nations of Canada on six separate occasions to represent them in land negotiations with New York state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The negotiations were related to lands sold by the people of two villages, Grand River and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyendinaga" title="Tyendinaga">Tyendinaga Mohawk Territory, Ontario</a>, who were led by Joseph Brant. The Mohawk peoples of <a href="/wiki/Akwesasne" title="Akwesasne">Akwesasne</a> and Kahnawake, two of the Seven Nations, denied that those villages had the right to sell what was common Mohawk land in New York. Ultimately, New York prevailed in keeping control of the land, and the division between Cook and Brant was deepened.</p>
<p>By 1789, Cook had settled at Akwesasne, where he became an influential chief. The Mohawk reserve eventually established there spans the New York (US)-Quebec (Canada) border and the St. Lawrence River. He argued that the St. Regis Indians (as they were called in New York) and the Seven Nations should remain neutral in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> between the US and Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The United States forgot Cook's earlier service and detained the Mohawk at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Niagara" title="Fort Niagara">Fort Niagara</a>; he was released after producing proof of his commission in the Continental Army, as well as letters from George Washington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although too elderly to participate, Cook followed the United States Army into Canada during the war and was present at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lundy%27s_Lane" title="Battle of Lundy's Lane">Battle of Lundy's Lane</a>. He was involved in a skirmish and fell from his horse. The injuries proved fatal; he died in the American camp in October 1814. Cook was given a military salute at his funeral,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-21">[21]</a></sup> and was buried near <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCBO_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-DCBO-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
