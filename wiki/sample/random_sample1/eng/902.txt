<p>The <b>CJ-10</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">长剑-10</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">長劍-10</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Cháng Jiàn 10</span></i>; literally: "long sword 10") is a second-generation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jfq-75_p102_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-jfq-75_p102-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">Chinese</a> ground based land-attack <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">missile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmpr-2015_p39_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmpr-2015_p39-5">[5]</a></sup> It is a copy of the <a href="/wiki/Kh-55" title="Kh-55">Kh-55</a> missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is reportedly manufactured by the <a href="/wiki/China_Aerospace_Science_and_Industry_Corporation" title="China Aerospace Science and Industry Corporation">China Aerospace Science and Industry Corporation</a> Third Academy and the China Haiying Electro-Mechanical Technology Academy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jfq-75_p102_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-jfq-75_p102-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, the CJ-10 was identified as a <b>DH-10</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">东海-10</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Dong Hai 10</span></i>; literally: "east sea 10") missile by Western media and analysts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-apa-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proj2049_p1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-proj2049_p1-8">[8]</a></sup> In the annual reports submitted to the United States Congress from the United States Department of Defense concerning the Chinese missile, which was classified as a variance of "DH-10" until 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmpr-2011_p2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmpr-2011_p2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmpr-2011_p31_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmpr-2011_p31-10">[10]</a></sup> when it was recognized as the "CJ-10" in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmpr-2012_p21_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmpr-2012_p21-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmpr-2012_p42_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmpr-2012_p42-12">[12]</a></sup> Current publications may use both terms interchangeably to identify the missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jfq-75_p102_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-jfq-75_p102-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missilethreat-2013_p27_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-missilethreat-2013_p27-13">[13]</a></sup> Ian Easton believes that the CJ-10 is the same missile as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HN-2" title="HN-2">HN-2</a>, and that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HN-3" title="HN-3">HN-3</a> is the "DH-10A".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proj2049_p3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-proj2049_p3-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CJ-10_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the September 2014 edition of Joint Forces Quarterly, an article reportedly described CJ-10 as a subsonic missile with a range of more than 1,500 km and a 500 kg. payload. The article attributes the missile having a guidance package using <a href="/wiki/Inertial_navigation_system" title="Inertial navigation system">inertial navigation system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">satellite navigation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrain_Contour_Matching" title="Terrain Contour Matching">Terrain Contour Matching</a>, and a likely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Scene-Mapping_Area_Correlator" title="Digital Scene-Mapping Area Correlator">Digital Scene-Mapping Area Correlator</a> for terminal guidance. Ships and ground <a href="/wiki/Transporter_erector_launcher" title="Transporter erector launcher">transporter erector launchers</a> were listed as launch platforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jfq-75_p102_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-jfq-75_p102-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the United States believes that the missile has a range of more than 1,500 km, and can potentially carry either conventional or nuclear payloads;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-missilethreat-2013_p29_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-missilethreat-2013_p29-15">[15]</a></sup> other sources claim the missile has ranges of 2,000 km (1,200 mi; 1,100 nmi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> 2,500 km (1,600 mi; 1,300 nmi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsci10march15_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-popsci10march15-1">[1]</a></sup> or as much as 4,000 km (2,500 mi; 2,200 nmi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2004, the CJ-10 was credited with a <a href="/wiki/Circular_error_probable" title="Circular error probable">CEP</a> of 10 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_2004-09-21_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-janes_2004-09-21-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The YJ-100 is a subsonic <a href="/wiki/Anti-ship_missile" title="Anti-ship missile">anti-ship missile</a> version of the CJ-10 with a range of 800 km (500 mi; 430 nmi). The missile can be air-launched by the <a href="/wiki/Xian_H-6" title="Xian H-6">H-6 bomber</a> and fired from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_launch_system" title="Vertical launch system">vertical launch system</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Type_055_destroyer" title="Type 055 destroyer">Type 055 destroyer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinamai4feb15_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinamai4feb15-19">[19]</a></sup> The YJ-100 will have an onboard radar and is potentially a counter to the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Range_Anti-Ship_Missile" title="Long Range Anti-Ship Missile">Long Range Anti-Ship Missile</a> (LRASM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsci10march15_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-popsci10march15-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CJ-10_(missile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The development of the CJ-10 could have potentially benefited significantly from Chinese acquisition of Western and Soviet missile technology in the 1990s, notably the <a href="/wiki/Kh-55" title="Kh-55">Kh-55</a> (purchased from <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Tomahawk_(missile)" title="Tomahawk (missile)">Tomahawk missiles</a> (that were unexploded and parts purchased from <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proj2049_p3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-proj2049_p3-14">[14]</a></sup> The detailed production engineering data packages of the Kh-55SM/Korshun LACM were bought from Kiev by 2001 which had by then been developed by Ukraine’s Dnipropetrovsk-based Yuzhnoye State Design Bureau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatf-7_Babur_GLCM_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatf-7_Babur_GLCM-20">[20]</a></sup> A 1995 Russian document suggested a complete production facility had been transferred to Shanghai, for the development of a nuclear-armed cruise missile. Originally it was thought that this was based on the 300 km-range <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raduga_Kh-15" title="Raduga Kh-15">Raduga Kh-15</a> (AS-16 'Kickback'), but it now appears that it was the Kh-55 that was transferred to China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jane%27s_Information_Group" title="Jane's Information Group">Jane's Information Group</a> reported the CJ-10 was tested 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_2004-09-21_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-janes_2004-09-21-18">[18]</a></sup> An August 2012 report by Jane's indicated that a shipborne variance of the missile may have been tested on <i><a href="/wiki/Type_909_weapon_trials_ship" title="Type 909 weapon trials ship">Bi Sheng</a></i>, a Chinese weapons trial ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janes_2012-10-14_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-janes_2012-10-14-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United States in 2008 estimated that 50–250 missiles were in service,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> increasing to 150–350 in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
