<p>The <b>Atari 7800 ProSystem</b>, or simply the <b>Atari 7800</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Home_video_game_console" title="Home video game console">home video game console</a> officially released by <a href="/wiki/Atari_Corporation" title="Atari Corporation">Atari Corporation</a> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is almost fully <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backward-compatible</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Atari_2600" title="Atari 2600">Atari 2600</a>, the first console to have backward compatibility without the use of additional modules. It was considered affordable at a price of US$140.</p>
<p>The 7800 has significantly improved graphics hardware over the 2600, but uses the same audio chip. It also shipped with a different model of joystick than the 2600-standard CX40.</p>
<p>The 1986 launch is sometimes referred to as a "re-release" or "relaunch" because the Atari 7800 had originally been announced on May 21, 1984, to replace Atari Inc.'s <a href="/wiki/Atari_5200" title="Atari 5200">Atari 5200</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_release_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-press_release-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but a general release was shelved due to the sale of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A few units were released to test markets in June 1984 though.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Atari 7800 ProSystem was the first game system from Atari Inc. designed by an outside company, <a href="/wiki/General_Computer_Corporation" title="General Computer Corporation">General Computer Corporation</a> (GCC). The system was designed in 1983-84 with an intended mass market rollout in June 1984, but was canceled shortly thereafter due to the sale of the company to <a href="/wiki/Jack_Tramiel" title="Jack Tramiel">Tramel Technology Ltd</a> on July 2, 1984. The project was originally called the Atari 3600, though was later renamed the Atari 7800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Atari had been facing mounting pressure in the form of competition from the <a href="/wiki/ColecoVision" title="ColecoVision">ColecoVision</a>, which boasted graphics that more closely mirrored <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade games</a> of the time than Atari’s 2600 system. At the same time, the Atari 5200 (the original intended successor to the Atari 2600) had been widely criticized for not being able to play Atari 2600 games without an adapter.</p>
<p>GCC, which had a background in creating arcade games, designed their new system with a graphical architecture similar to arcade machines of the time. The 7800 allows a large number of moving objects (75 to 100) that far exceeds previous consoles. Powering the system is a slightly customized 6502 processor, the Atari SALLY (sometimes described as a "6502C"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGM62_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EGM62-8">[8]</a></sup> running at 1.79 MHz.</p>
<p>In contrast to the Atari 5200, the Atari 7800 can play almost all Atari 2600 games out of the box, without the need for an adapter. In addition, it features a return to a digital controller.Then as an added bonus, GCC's programmers would also do almost all of the Atari 2600, 5200 games in 1983-1984 for Atari.To make sure the system had every bell and whistle possible, the system was slated to be released with not only a computer keyboard, but also a High Score cartridge (Designed by GCC), and a new add-on module for the Atari 5200 which would have given the Atari 5200 system full Atari 7800/2600 compatibility to ensure its existing base of 5200 owners could immediately take advantage of all the hot new games that the 7800 was capable of producing &lt;<a class="external free" href="http://www.atarimuseum.com/videogames/consoles/7800/7800menu/" rel="nofollow">http://www.atarimuseum.com/videogames/consoles/7800/7800menu/</a>&gt;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>To address the concerns of parents that home computers were a better investment than consoles, the system was designed to be upgraded to a full-fledged home computer. A keyboard was developed, and the keyboard had an expansion port (which was the SIO port from Atari's 8-bit computer line, though the 7800 could not run Atari computer programs) that allowed for the addition of peripherals such as disk drives and printers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>To further enhance the gaming experience, GCC had also designed a "high score cartridge", a battery-backed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAM" title="RAM">RAM</a> cartridge designed for storing game scores. On the side of the 7800 was an expansion port, reportedly for a planned connection with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laserdisc" title="Laserdisc">laserdisc</a> player.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 7800 was initially released in southern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in June 1984<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, following an announcement on May 21, 1984 at the Summer <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show" title="Consumer Electronics Show">Consumer Electronics Show</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_release_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-press_release-4">[4]</a></sup> 13 games were announced for the system's launch, including <i><a href="/wiki/Ms._Pac-Man" title="Ms. Pac-Man">Ms. Pac-Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pole_Position_II" title="Pole Position II">Pole Position II</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Centipede_(video_game)" title="Centipede (video game)">Centipede</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Joust_(video_game)" title="Joust (video game)">Joust</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dig_Dug" title="Dig Dug">Dig Dug</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Desert_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Desert Falcon (page does not exist)">Desert Falcon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Robotron:_2084" title="Robotron: 2084">Robotron: 2084</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Galaga" title="Galaga">Galaga</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Xevious" title="Xevious">Xevious</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Food_Fight_(video_game)" title="Food Fight (video game)">Food Fight</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ballblazer" title="Ballblazer">Ballblazer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rescue_on_Fractalus!" title="Rescue on Fractalus!">Rescue on Fractalus!</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Track_%26_Field_(video_game)" title="Track &amp; Field (video game)">Track &amp; Field</a></i>. Atari was a sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a> and planned to push the 7800 aggressively in time for Christmas that year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On July 2, 1984, <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Warner Communications</a> sold Atari's Consumer Division to <a href="/wiki/Jack_Tramiel" title="Jack Tramiel">Jack Tramiel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> All projects were halted during an initial evaluation period. Modern publications have often incorrectly asserted that Jack Tramiel mothballed the Atari 7800, feeling video games were a past fad, and subsequently asserted that he dusted off the Atari 7800 once the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">NES</a> became successful. The reality was that a contractual issue arose in that GCC had not been paid for their development of the 7800. Warner and Tramiel battled back and forth over who was accountable, with Tramiel believing that the 7800 should have been covered as part of his acquisition deal. In May 1985, Jack relented and paid GCC the overdue payment. This led to additional negotiations regarding the initial launch titles that GCC had developed and then an effort to find someone to lead their new video game division, which was completed in November 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original production run of the Atari 7800 languished on warehouse shelves until it was re-introduced in January 1986, after strong 2600 sales the previous Christmas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The console was released nationwide in May 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CE2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Atari's launch of the 7800 under Tramiel was far more subdued than Warner had planned for the system in 1984 with a marketing budget of just $300,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Additionally, the keyboard and high score cartridge were canceled, the expansion port was removed from later production runs of the system and, in lieu of new titles, the system was launched with titles intended for the 7800's debut in 1984.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the end of 1986, Computer Entertainer claimed the Atari 7800 had sold 100,000 consoles in the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-CE2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CE1-14">[14]</a></sup> less than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Master_System" title="Sega Master System">Sega Master System</a>'s 125,000 and the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a>'s 1.1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE2_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-CE2-1">[1]</a></sup> According to Atari, due to manufacturing problems, they only managed to produce and sell 100,000 units by 1986, including units that had been in a warehouse since 1984. A common complaint in 1986 was a lack of games, including a gap of months between new releases (<i><a href="/wiki/Galaga" title="Galaga">Galaga</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s release in August was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Xevious" title="Xevious">Xevious</a></i> in November).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-CE1-14">[14]</a></sup> By the end of 1986, the 7800 had 10 games, compared to Sega's 20 and Nintendo's 36; nine of the NES games were third-party, whereas the 7800 and Master System had no third-party games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE2_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-CE2-1">[1]</a></sup> A reason cited for the lack of third-party interest in the 7800 was its small 100,000 install base and low market penetration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE1_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-CE1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketplace_challenges">Marketplace challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marketplace challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Atari's lineup for the 7800 emphasized high-quality versions of popular arcade games like <i><a href="/wiki/Joust_(video_game)" title="Joust (video game)">Joust</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Asteroids_(video_game)" title="Asteroids (video game)">Asteroids</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgw198808_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgw198808-15">[15]</a></sup> This had been a primary reason for the success of the Atari 2600 VCS against systems like the <a href="/wiki/Intellivision" title="Intellivision">Intellivision</a>.</p>
<p>During the Atari 7800’s life cycle, Atari found themselves struggling to get developers to create 7800 versions of then-popular arcade titles because of a controversial policy employed by Nintendo. When Nintendo revived the industry, they signed up software development companies to create <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a> games under a strict license agreement which imposed serious restrictions on what they were allowed to do. One of the key clauses was that companies who made Nintendo games were not allowed to make that game on a competing system for a period of two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Because of the market success of the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a>, companies chose to develop for it first and were thus barred from developing the same games on competing systems for two years. The software libraries of the Atari 7800 and Sega Master System suffered tremendously as a result.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Eleven titles were developed and sold by three third-party companies under their own labels for the 7800 (<a href="/wiki/Absolute_Entertainment" title="Absolute Entertainment">Absolute Entertainment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Froggo" title="Froggo">Froggo</a>) with the rest published by Atari themselves. However, most Atari development was contracted out.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some NES titles were developed by companies who had licensed their title from a different arcade manufacturer. While the creator of the NES version would be restricted from making a competitive version of an NES game, the original arcade copyright holder was not precluded from licensing out rights for a home version of an arcade game to multiple systems. Through this loophole, Atari 7800 conversions of <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Bros." title="Mario Bros.">Mario Bros.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Double_Dragon" title="Double Dragon">Double Dragon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Commando_(video_game)" title="Commando (video game)">Commando</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rampage_(video_game)" title="Rampage (video game)">Rampage</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Xenophobe_(video_game)" title="Xenophobe (video game)">Xenophobe</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ikari_Warriors" title="Ikari Warriors">Ikari Warriors</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Kung-Fu_Master" title="Kung-Fu Master">Kung-Fu Master</a></i> were licensed and developed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By June 1988, the Atari 7800 had sold 1 million units worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuation">Discontinuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Discontinuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Atari 7800 remained officially active in the United States between 1986 and 1991 and in Europe between 1989 and 1991. On January 1, 1992, Atari Corp. formally announced that production of the Atari 7800, the Atari 2600, the Atari 8-bit computer line, and the Atari XE Game System would cease. (It has since been discovered that Atari Corp. continued to develop games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Toki_(video_game)" title="Toki (video game)">Toki</a></i> for the Atari 7800 until all development was shut down in May 1993.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://betaphasegames.com/7800_Toki_Screens.html" rel="nofollow">[2]</a>) By the time of the cancellation, Nintendo's NES dominated the North American market, controlling 80% while Atari Corp. controlled just 12%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite trailing the Nintendo Entertainment System in terms of number of units sold, the 7800 was a profitable enterprise for Atari Corp., benefiting largely from Atari’s name and the system's 2600 compatibility. Profits were strong owing to low investment in game development and marketing. Nonetheless, the 7800 failed to help Atari regain its dominance in the video game industry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphics">Graphics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Graphics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The graphics are generated by a custom graphics chip called MARIA, which uses an approach to graphics commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade game</a> <a href="/wiki/Arcade_system_board" title="Arcade system board">system boards</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-guide-24">[24]</a></sup> It was very different from other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_video_game_consoles_(second_generation)" title="History of video game consoles (second generation)">second</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_video_game_consoles_(third_generation)" title="History of video game consoles (third generation)">third generation</a> consoles. Instead of a limited number of hardware sprites, MARIA allows for a much larger number of sprites described in a series of <a href="/wiki/Display_list" title="Display list">display lists</a>. Each display list contains sprite entries with pointers to graphics data, color information, and horizontal positioning. The same display list is used for multiple rasters with the pointers being automatically adjusted. However, managing and displaying a large number of sprites required much more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPU" title="CPU">CPU</a> time (both directly and indirectly since the MARIA would halt the CPU when drawing sprites) than consoles with hardware sprites and backgrounds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>MARIA has a number of different graphics modes which are either 160 pixels wide or 320 pixels wide. While the 320 pixel modes theoretically enable the 7800 to create games at higher resolution than the 256 pixel wide graphics found in the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Master_System" title="Sega Master System">Sega Master System</a>, the intense processing demands of MARIA typically meant that programmers created their games using the lower 160 pixel modes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The 7800 features a broad (for its time) palette of 256 colors. Depending on various parameters, each individual sprite can use from 1 to 12 colors, with 3 colors (plus a 4th "transparency" color) being the most common. In this format, the sprite is referenced to one of 8 palettes, where each palette holds 3 assignable colors. There is also an assignable background color, which will be visible wherever another object has not covered it up. In total the system can utilize 25 colors on a scanline at one time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atarimuseum.com_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-atarimuseum.com-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The graphics resolution, color palette assignments, and background color can be adjusted in between scanlines. This technique is documented in the original 1983 "Atari 3600 Software Guide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atarimuseum.com_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-atarimuseum.com-25">[25]</a></sup> Games often used this feature to render high resolution text in one area of the screen, while displaying more colorful graphics with less resolution in the gameplay area. Demos also exist which use this feature to place all 256 colors on the screen at the same time.</p>
<p>The MARIA’s approach had advantages and disadvantages when it came to generating graphics in software during the lifespan of the 7800. It excelled at moving around large numbers of sprites on a static screen without the screen flickering that plagued other 8-bit systems. Its flexible design enabled it to play games which used display list manipulation to generate a pseudo 3D appearance such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ballblazer" title="Ballblazer">Ballblazer</a></i> (1987) and <i>F-18 Hornet</i> (1988). While side-scrolling games in the vein of <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Mario_Bros." title="Super Mario Bros.">Super Mario Bros.</a></i> are possible on the system (1990's <i>Scrapyard Dog</i> is the best example), it is significantly harder to develop such a title than on a tile-based system such as the Nintendo Entertainment System.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sound">Sound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A common criticism of the 7800 regards its use of the TIA to provide 2-channel sound effects and music, resulting in sound quality that is virtually identical to the Atari 2600 VCS from 1977. While the inclusion of 2600 hardware is required to maintain compatibility with the older system, this drove up production costs and reduced available space on the 7800’s motherboard. As such, the 7800 does not include additional hardware for generating sound as it does with graphics and the sound hardware is considered the weakest part of the system.</p>
<p>To compensate for this, GCC’s engineers allowed games to include a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atari_POKEY" title="Atari POKEY">POKEY audio chip</a> in the cartridge which substantially improved the audio quality. To ensure software developers had an economical means of producing better sound than TIA, GCC had originally planned to make a low-cost, high performance sound chip, GUMBY, which could also be placed in 7800 cartridges to enhance its sound capabilities further. This project was cancelled when Atari was sold to Jack Tramiel.</p>
<p>Despite having the capability to support sound chips in cartridges, almost no 7800 cartridges feature POKEY hardware for enhanced sound. <i>Ballblazer</i>, released in 1987, uses the POKEY to generate all music and sound effects. Similarly, <i><a href="/wiki/Commando_(video_game)" title="Commando (video game)">Commando</a></i>, released in 1989, uses a POKEY to generate in-game music while the TIA generates the game's sound effects for a total of 6 channels of sound.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lockout_features">Lockout features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lockout features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the debate over <i><a href="/wiki/Custer%27s_Revenge" title="Custer's Revenge">Custer's Revenge</a></i>, an Atari 2600 VCS title with adult themes, Atari had concerns over similar adult titles finding their way onto the 7800 and displaying adult graphics on the significantly improved graphics of the MARIA chip.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> To combat this, they included a digital signature protection method which prevented unauthorized 7800 games from being played on the system.</p>
<p>When a cartridge was inserted into the system, the 7800 BIOS included code which would generate a digital signature of the cartridge ROM and compare it to the signature stored on the cartridge. If a correct signature was located on the cartridge, the 7800 would operate in 7800 mode, granting the game access to MARIA and other features. If a signature was not located, the 7800 remained in 2600 mode and MARIA was unavailable. All 7800 games released in North America had to be digitally signed by Atari. This digital signature code is not present in PAL 7800s, which use various heuristics to detect 2600 cartridges, due to export restrictions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The signing utility was found and released by Classic Gaming Expo in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Backward_compatibility">Backward compatibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Backward compatibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Atari 7800 differs from the 2600 in several key areas. It features a full Atari SALLY 6502 processor whereas the 2600 VCS has a stripped-down 6507 processor running at a slower speed. It has additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAM" title="RAM">RAM</a> (Random Access Memory) and the ability to access more cartridge data at one time than the 2600. The most substantial difference, however, is a graphics architecture which differs markedly from either the Atari 2600 VCS or Atari’s 8-bit line of computers.</p>
<p>The 7800's compatibility with the Atari 2600 is made possible by including many of the same chips used in the Atari 2600. When operating in “2600” mode to play Atari 2600 titles, the 7800 uses a Television Interface Adapter (TIA) chip to generate graphics and sound. The processor is slowed to 1.19 MHz, enabling the 7800 to mirror the performance of the 2600's stripped-down 6507 processor. RAM is limited to 128 bytes found in the RIOT and game data is accessed in 4K blocks.</p>
<p>When in “7800” mode (signified by the appearance of the full-screen Atari logo), the graphics are generated entirely by the MARIA graphics processing unit, all system RAM is available and game data is accessed in larger 48K blocks. The system’s SALLY 6502 runs at its normal 1.79 MHz instead of the reduced speed of 2600 mode. The 2600 chips are used in 7800 mode to generate sound as well as switch and controller interfaces.</p>
<p>The Atari 7800 does not support backward compatibility for Atari 5200 games or accessories.</p>

<p>The Atari 7800 came bundled with the Atari Proline Joystick, a two button controller with a joystick for movement. In response to criticism over ergonomic issues in the 7800’s Pro-Line controllers, Atari later released joypad controllers with European 7800s, which were similar in style to controllers found on Nintendo and Sega Systems. The Joypad was not available in the United States.</p>
<p>Unlike the NES or Sega Master System, there were few add-on peripherals for the 7800, though its backwards compatibility feature allowed it to be compatible with most Atari 2600 peripherals.</p>
<p>The most notable exception was the XG-1 lightgun, which came bundled with the Atari XE Game System. The XG-1 was fully compatible with the 7800 and was sold separately for other Atari systems. Atari released four 7800 light gun games: <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_Brigade" title="Alien Brigade">Alien Brigade</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crossbow_(video_game)" title="Crossbow (video game)">Crossbow</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meltdown_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meltdown (video game) (page does not exist)">Meltdown</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Barnyard_Blaster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Barnyard Blaster (page does not exist)">Barnyard Blaster</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canceled_peripherals">Canceled peripherals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canceled peripherals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the acquisition of the Atari Consumer Division by Jack Tramiel in 1984, a number of planned peripherals for the system were canceled.</p>
<ul>
<li>The High Score Cartridge was designed to save player high scores for up to 65 separate games. The cartridge was intended as a pass-through device (similar to the later <a href="/wiki/Game_Genie" title="Game Genie">Game Genie</a>). Nine games were programmed with the feature but the cartridge was canceled before it was released. In 1999, a limited run of cartridges were produced by Atari historian Curt Vendel using ROM code from Gary Rubio (the former Atari liaison to GCC on the Atari 7800 project). This feature has been included in many homebrew releases in recent years.</li>
<li>The 7800 included an expansion port which would have allowed for the addition of a planned computer keyboard, connection to laserdisc players and other peripherals. The expansion port was removed in the second and third revisions of the 7800.</li>
<li>A dual joystick holder was designed for games like <i><a href="/wiki/Robotron:_2084" title="Robotron: 2084">Robotron: 2084</a></i> and future games like <i><a href="/wiki/Battlezone_(1980_video_game)" title="Battlezone (1980 video game)">Battlezone</a></i> and others, but not produced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_library">Software library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Software library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>While the 7800 can actually play hundreds of titles due to its compatibility with the Atari 2600, there was limited third party support for the 7800 and fewer than 100 titles were specifically designed for it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unreleased_games">Unreleased games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Unreleased games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with most game consoles, there were many more games in development for the 7800 than were actually released. However, very few prototypes have been located, due to Tramiel Atari’s reluctance to make them in the first place. Atari 7800 prototypes tend to be highly coveted by collectors, often fetching hundreds of dollars when sold. Some collectors are unwilling to share the rare items publicly as doing so is assumed to decrease the value of their prototype.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, some unreleased Atari 7800 games, as well as early versions of released games have been released to the public. A few have been manufactured and sold.</p>
<p>These include</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Klax_(video_game)" title="Klax (video game)">Klax</a></i> (Programmed by Blue Sky Software). The game was nearly completed when canceled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> For years, it was traded privately until a ROM was made available to the general public. Later, the developer appeared to create additional levels for the community.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Gato_(video_game)" title="Gato (video game)">Gato</a></i> (Programmed by Ibid Inc Software). Released on the XE Game System, an Atari 7800 version was started but not completed beyond a simple demo.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Missing_in_Action_(video_game)" title="Missing in Action (video game)">Missing in Action</a></i> (Programmed by Sculptured Software for TNT Games). A side scrolling adventure that was about 85% complete when cancelled. The game is mostly playable with some later collision detection issues requiring cheats to get past.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pit-Fighter" title="Pit-Fighter">Pit-Fighter</a></i> (Programmed by Imagitec Design). An early demo of the game has been found, with crude graphics, no sound and poor collision detection.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Rescue_on_Fractalus!" title="Rescue on Fractalus!">Rescue on Fractalus!</a></i> (Programmed by GCC). A conversion of the popular computer game was released by GCC in 2004. The game was partially complete with a running engine but no enemies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>Other 7800 games remain lost, despite indications that development occurred. The most notable of these are <i><a href="/wiki/Skyfox" title="Skyfox">Skyfox</a></i> (shown on the back of the original system box) and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Electrocop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Electrocop (page does not exist)">Electrocop</a></i> (artwork has since been uncovered).</li>
</ul>
<p>Engineering Notes list <i><a href="/wiki/Tempest_(video_game)" title="Tempest (video game)">Tempest</a></i> as a game that was between 15–20% completed for the Atari 7800; no code to date has been found. The Atari Museum located and posted unreleased box art and notes for a 7800 version of <i><a href="/wiki/Crystal_Castles_(video_game)" title="Crystal Castles (video game)">Crystal Castles</a></i>, but no code to date has been found for that game, either. Atari's earlier 7800 games listing showed <i><a href="/wiki/Millipede_(video_game)" title="Millipede (video game)">Millipede</a></i> as one of the games in the line up; however, it does not appear that it was ever started or worked on.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_code_release">Source code release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Source code release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The source code for 13 games, as well as the OS and development tools (for the <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a> computer system) were discovered in a dumpster behind the Atari building in Sunnyvale, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Commented <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly language</a> source code was made available for <i><a href="/wiki/Centipede_(video_game)" title="Centipede (video game)">Centipede</a></i>, <i>Commando</i>, <i>Crossbow</i>, <i>Desert Falcon</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dig_Dug" title="Dig Dug">Dig Dug</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Food_Fight_(video_game)" title="Food Fight (video game)">Food Fight</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Galaga" title="Galaga">Galaga</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hat_Trick_(video_game)" title="Hat Trick (video game)">Hat Trick</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Joust_(video_game)" title="Joust (video game)">Joust</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ms._Pac-Man" title="Ms. Pac-Man">Ms. Pac-Man</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Super_Stunt_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Super Stunt Cycle (page does not exist)">Super Stunt Cycle</a></i>, <i>Robotron: 2084</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Xevious" title="Xevious">Xevious</a></i> game titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emulation_and_homebrew">Emulation and homebrew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Emulation and homebrew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When emulators of 1980s video game consoles began to appear on home computers in the late 1990s, the Atari 7800 was one of the last to be emulated. The lack of awareness of the system, the lack of understanding of the hardware, and fears about the digital signature lockout initially caused concerns. Since that time, however, the 7800 has been emulated successfully and is now common on emulation sites. One such program is ProSystem, written in C/C++ for the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> operating system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It uses the Windows API and DirectX to display what it emulates in both <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">PAL</a> and <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">NTSC</a>.</p>
<p>The digital signature long prevented homebrew games from being developed until the original encryption generating software was discovered. When the original digital signature generating software was turned over to the Atari community, development of new Atari 7800 titles began. In addition, the Atari community has slowly uncovered the original 7800 development tools and released them into the public domain. New tools, documentation, source code and utilities for development have since been created which has sponsored additional homebrew development. Several new commercial Atari 7800 titles such as <i>Beef Drop</i>, <i>B*nQ</i>, <i>Pac-Man Collection</i>, <i>Combat 1990</i>, <i>Santa Simon</i>, and <i>Space War</i> have been created and released.</p>
<p>System compatible hardware has also been produced for the system. Among these was the Cuttle Cart II, a device that allowed the Atari 7800 to read MMC cards containing binary files of Atari 7800 programs. The Cuttle Cart II has enabled more people to play the entire 2600 and 7800 library on an original system as well as binaries of unreleased games and new homebrew titles. The Cuttle Cart II was a success by homebrew standards, selling out both production runs and commanding high prices on eBay.</p>
<p>A more recent development is the Atari 7800 expansion module developed by Legacy Engineering with a high scores save feature (with compatible games), additional RAM capabilities, as well as vastly improved sound capabilities using the <a href="/wiki/POKEY" title="POKEY">POKEY</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_YM2151" title="Yamaha YM2151">YM2151</a> sound chips. The Expansion module is designed as a pass-through device that sits on top of the console and requires no system modding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atari_Flashback">Atari Flashback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atari_7800&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Atari Flashback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004, Atari (now owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infogrames_Entertainment,_SA" title="Infogrames Entertainment, SA">Infogrames</a>) released the first <a href="/wiki/Atari_Flashback" title="Atari Flashback">Atari Flashback</a> console. This system resembled a miniature Atari 7800 and joysticks and had 20 built in games (five 7800 and fifteen 2600 titles). While the unit sold well, it was controversial among Atari fans. Atari had given the engineering firm, Legacy Engineering, extremely limited development timelines. The firm was forced to build the Flashback using <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System_hardware_clone" title="Nintendo Entertainment System hardware clone">NES-On-A-Chip</a> hardware instead of recreating the Atari 7800 hardware. As a result, the Flashback has been criticized for failing to properly replicate the actual Atari gaming experience.</p>
<p>Legacy Engineering was later commissioned to create another 7800 project that was subsequently cancelled after prototypes were made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
