<p><b>The Ataris</b> are an American <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band from <a href="/wiki/Anderson,_Indiana" title="Anderson, Indiana">Anderson, Indiana</a>. Formed in 1995, they have released five studio albums, with <i>So Long, Astoria</i> certified gold. In 2009, an album was announced to be entitled <i>The Graveyard of the Atlantic</i> although the album's status has been on indefinite ambiguity, with just two EPs released in 2010 and 2012 both with the same titles as the awaited album. They are best known for their hit cover song, originally recorded by <a href="/wiki/Don_Henley" title="Don Henley">Don Henley</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Boys_of_Summer_(song)" title="The Boys of Summer (song)">"The Boys of Summer"</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ataris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kung_Fu_Records_.281995.E2.80.932002.29">Kung Fu Records (1995–2002)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ataris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kung Fu Records (1995–2002)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Formed in 1995, the band originally consisted of singer, songwriter, guitarist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kristopher_Roe" title="Kristopher Roe">Kristopher Roe</a> and guitarist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jasin_Thomason" title="Jasin Thomason">Jasin Thomason</a>. Using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-track_(multitrack)" title="4-track (multitrack)">4-track</a>, Roe wrote and recorded demos in his bedroom, using a drum machine while he searched for a full-time drummer. The band's first big break came in 1996 when Roe attended a show at the club Bogart's in Cincinnati, where Jasin passed one of the band's demo tapes to a roadie from the band. The roadie gave the tape to <a href="/wiki/Joe_Escalante" title="Joe Escalante">Joe Escalante</a>, bassist from the band <a href="/wiki/The_Vandals" title="The Vandals">The Vandals</a> who owned their own label, <a href="/wiki/Kung_Fu_Records" title="Kung Fu Records">Kung Fu Records</a>. A few weeks later, Roe received a call from Kung Fu Records, who told him they were interested in putting out their record, even though he was really only searching for a drummer. The Ataris signed to Kung Fu and the label passed the tape along to various drummers. Eventually, Roe decided upon ex-<a href="/wiki/Lagwagon" title="Lagwagon">Lagwagon</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Derrick_Plourde" title="Derrick Plourde">Derrick Plourde</a>. The band then proceeded to record their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Anywhere_but_Here_(The_Ataris_album)" title="Anywhere but Here (The Ataris album)">Anywhere but Here</a></i>, tracking the whole thing in less than a week. The album was released on April 29, 1997 and the band held a release show party at Missing Link Records in Indianapolis on May 2.</p>
<p>In June 1997, Roe moved from <a href="/wiki/Anderson,_Indiana" title="Anderson, Indiana">Anderson</a>, Indiana to <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a>, California. Shortly afterward, Marko Desantis joined the band on bass for a short time. Thomason decided to leave the band to stay in Indiana, and the group toured as a three-piece for a short while. After a brief tour in October 1997, this lineup disbanded. Roe, out of money and living in a van, contemplated moving back to Indiana. But the band still had an upcoming tour booked with <a href="/wiki/Dance_Hall_Crashers" title="Dance Hall Crashers">Dance Hall Crashers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unwritten_Law" title="Unwritten Law">Unwritten Law</a>, so Roe decided to give the band one more shot. He got his friend from Santa Barbara, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Davenport" title="Mike Davenport">Mike Davenport</a>, to play bass. Davenport shared a small rehearsal space on East Haley Street with his friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marco_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Marco Peña">Marco Peña</a>, who was in a different band. One day at the rehearsal space, Roe and Davenport heard the drummer of Peña's band, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Knapp_(musician)" title="Chris Knapp (musician)">Chris Knapp</a>, playing and immediately asked him to join The Ataris. While Roe and Davenport joked that Peña would be upset over them "stealing" his band's drummer, Peña surprised them by showing up at their practice the next day, strapping on his guitar, and playing along. It turned out Peña had learned all the songs from <i>Anywhere But Here</i>. He joined the band as a second guitarist and the foursome went on their scheduled tour. However, Peña soon left the band due to personal reasons and was replaced by Patrick Riley.</p>
<p>From there, the band gradually increased in popularity in the underground rock scene, a lot due to their consistent touring and personal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DIY" title="DIY">DIY</a> approach to the band. They then found more success with their 1998 EP <i><a href="/wiki/Look_Forward_to_Failure" title="Look Forward to Failure">Look Forward to Failure</a></i>, released on San Francisco punk label <a href="/wiki/Fat_Wreck_Chords" title="Fat Wreck Chords">Fat Wreck Chords</a>. However, it wasn't until the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Skies,_Broken_Hearts...Next_12_Exits" title="Blue Skies, Broken Hearts...Next 12 Exits">Blue Skies, Broken Hearts...Next 12 Exits</a></i> in the year 1999 that they started to gain widespread acclaim. The album's name comes from the name of a mobile home park along Highway 101 in Santa Barbara. Also, "...next 12 exits" refers to a nearby sign on Highway 101 North that reads "Santa Barbara, Next 12 Exits". The album contained personal, storytelling lyrics and heartfelt tales of relationships, discovery and things once left behind. After this album, Riley left the band to go back to school, and Marco Peña re-joined the band.</p>
<p>The band's third full-length studio album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_is_Forever" title="End is Forever">End is Forever</a></i>, was released in 2001. This album painted a more intensely personal side of the band's storytelling and personal relationships. Due to their persistent touring schedule, a lot of the arrangements and lyrics were worked out at the last minute while in the studio and then the vocals recorded while Roe had a bad cold. Roe maintains that he was not completely happy with half of the songs, although he went on to state that in spite of this, several of the songs such as "Fast Times at Dropout High" and "Road Signs and Rock Songs" were still to date some of the band's strongest of their earlier career.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="So_Long.2C_Astoria_.282003.E2.80.9305.29"><i>So Long, Astoria</i> (2003–05)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ataris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: So Long, Astoria (2003–05)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, the lineup changed again, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Collura" title="John Collura">John Collura</a> replacing Peña on guitar. Prior to this, Collura had played in his own band Beefcake who'd toured with The Ataris. He'd also done time on tour as a guitar tech for The Ataris just prior to Peña's departure.</p>
<p>During this same period, the Ataris' contract with Kung Fu Records expired, and the band chose to sign with <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>. Later that year, they began recording their fourth full-length album and major label debut, <i><a href="/wiki/So_Long,_Astoria" title="So Long, Astoria">So Long, Astoria</a></i>, released on March 4, 2003.</p>
<p>Produced by <a href="/wiki/Lou_Giordano" title="Lou Giordano">Lou Giordano</a>, the album's production served as a stark contrast to any of the band's past releases. Whereas previous efforts would be recorded in short time spans between tours.</p>
<p><i>So Long, Astoria</i> was widely credited for introducing The Ataris to a larger mainstream audience and generating several successful singles, including "In This Diary" and "The Saddest Song." Musically, the album showcased very personal, encrypted and slightly more optimistic songwriting with a more refined, straightforward rock sound, not unlike <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Eat_World" title="Jimmy Eat World">Jimmy Eat World</a>. This album also included their hit cover song, originally recorded by Don Henley "<a href="/wiki/The_Boys_of_Summer_(song)" title="The Boys of Summer (song)">The Boys of Summer</a>", which much to the dismay of the band became their "accidental" second single after a radio station, KROQ in Los Angeles started playing it, even though the band had already chosen the song "My Reply" as their second single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The single is their highest charting single to date, reaching No. 20 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.</p>
<p>The band toured behind this record for most of 2003 and into early 2004. That year also saw the release of a low-key live album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_At_The_Metro" title="Live At The Metro">Live At The Metro</a></i>, along with a track on the <i><a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_2" title="Spider-Man 2">Spider-Man 2</a></i> soundtrack. <i><a href="/wiki/So_Long,_Astoria" title="So Long, Astoria">So Long, Astoria</a></i> sold well in excess of 700,000 copies and was certified gold. After a number of personal, financial and artistic differences, this line-up decided to respectfully and creatively go its separate ways over the summer and fall of 2004.</p>
<p>Davenport, interested in playing heavier music, became a founding member of the band Versus the World, while Knapp stopped playing music altogether and stayed in Santa Barbara. Details regarding the departures of Knapp and Davenport have been kept quiet for sometime, and neither camp has appeared interested in fueling any public debate or ill-will. Looking for some time off, Roe headed back to Indiana while Collura moved back to New York; though they would later regroup to begin the recordings of the band's next album.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welcome_the_Night_.282005.E2.80.9307.29"><i>Welcome the Night</i> (2005–07)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ataris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Welcome the Night (2005–07)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After some time off, Roe and Collura later moved forward and began writing songs that would become part of the follow-up to <i>So Long, Astoria</i> titled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welcome_The_Night" title="Welcome The Night">Welcome The Night</a></i>. They held some informal practices with some friends from New York, who played in the band Park Ranger. These sessions led to three members of Park Ranger joining The Ataris: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sean_Hansen" title="Sean Hansen">Sean Hansen</a> on bass, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shane_Chickeles" title="Shane Chickeles">Shane Chickeles</a> on drums, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Carabello" title="Paul Carabello">Paul Carabello</a> on third guitar. To round out the lineup they added longtime friends <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hoag" title="Bob Hoag">Bob Hoag</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Pollen_(band)" title="Pollen (band)">Pollen</a> and The Go Reflex (which was managed by Roe), on piano and keyboards, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angus_Cooke" title="Angus Cooke">Angus Cooke</a> on cello. Cooke played cello on past Ataris records, and helped with production as well. Starting in 2005, the band began recording <i>Welcome the Night</i> at Seedy Underbelly in California, with producer Nick Launay. Writing and recording eventually took the better part of two years and was extended to multiple studios. The album was routinely delayed by Columbia.</p>
<p>On June 10, 2006, the band announced that it had left <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> due to the label's internal disintegration. In November 2006, The Ataris started their own imprint, Isola Recordings, through <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_Records" title="Sanctuary Records">Sanctuary Records</a> and RED Distribution, and simultaneously announced the official release date of their fifth album <i>Welcome the Night</i> as February 20, 2007. <i>Welcome the Night</i> debuted at number 85 on the <i>Billboard</i> charts with over 12,000 copies sold. Following the album's release, the band embarked on a tour of the United States and Europe.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Graveyard_of_the_Atlantic.2C_So_Long.2C_Astoria_Reunion_Tour.2C_and_October_in_This_Railroad_Earth_.282010-present.29"><i>The Graveyard of the Atlantic</i>, <i>So Long, Astoria</i> Reunion Tour, and <i>October in This Railroad Earth</i> (2010-present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ataris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Graveyard of the Atlantic, So Long, Astoria Reunion Tour, and October in This Railroad Earth (2010-present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, a two track EP, <i>All Souls' Day &amp; the Graveyard of the Atlantic</i> containing just its eponymous tracks was released under the <a href="/wiki/Paper_%2B_Plastick" title="Paper + Plastick">Paper + Plastick</a> label. In 2012, the band released a four track EP entitled <i>The Graveyard of the Atlantic</i>. From 2013, The Ataris began a North American tour with Kris Roe as singer and the former members John Collura, Mike Davenport and Chris Knapp to celebrate the 10 years of their most successful album <i>So Long, Astoria</i>. In 2016, the band released a six-song EP entitled <i>October in This Railroad Earth</i> through <a href="/wiki/Bandcamp" title="Bandcamp">Bandcamp</a>. A second EP, along with a limited vinyl pressing of <i>October in This Railroad Earth</i>, will follow at a later date.</p>
