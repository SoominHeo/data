<p>The <b>Attalid dynasty</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Δυναστεία των Ατταλιδών</span>) was a <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic</a> dynasty that ruled the city of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> after the death of <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">Lysimachus</a>, a general of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>. The Attalid kingdom was the <a href="/wiki/Rump_state" title="Rump state">rump state</a> left after the collapse of the Lysimachian Empire. One of Lysimachus' officers, <a href="/wiki/Philetaerus" title="Philetaerus">Philetaerus</a>, took control of the city in 282 BC. The later Attalids were descended from his father and they expanded the city into a kingdom. <a href="/wiki/Attalus_I" title="Attalus I">Attalus I</a> proclaimed himself King in the 230s BC, following his victories over the <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Galatians</a>. The Attalids ruled Pergamon until <a href="/wiki/Attalus_III" title="Attalus III">Attalus III</a> bequeathed the kingdom to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> in 133 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> to avoid a likely succession crisis. A war with <a href="/wiki/Eumenes_III" title="Eumenes III">Eumenes III</a> resulted in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Asia_(Roman_province)" title="Asia (Roman province)">Roman province of Asia</a> over much of the territory.</p>
<p>On the interior of the <a href="/wiki/Pergamon_Altar" title="Pergamon Altar">Pergamon Altar</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a> depicting the life of <a href="/wiki/Telephus" title="Telephus">Telephus</a>, son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herakles" title="Herakles">Herakles</a>, whom the ruling Attalid dynasty associated with its city and utilized to claim descent from the Olympians. Pergamon, having entered the Greek world much later than its counterparts to the west, could not boast the same divine heritage as older city-states and retroactively had to cultivate its place in Greek mythology.</p>
