<p><b>Art Bears</b> were an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avant-rock" title="Avant-rock">avant-rock</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">group</a> formed during the disassembly of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cow" title="Henry Cow">Henry Cow</a> in 1978 by three of its members, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Cutler" title="Chris Cutler">Chris Cutler</a> (percussion, texts), <a href="/wiki/Fred_Frith" title="Fred Frith">Fred Frith</a> (guitar, bass guitar, violin, keyboards) and <a href="/wiki/Dagmar_Krause" title="Dagmar Krause">Dagmar Krause</a> (vocals; previously of <a href="/wiki/Slapp_Happy" title="Slapp Happy">Slapp Happy</a>). The group released three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio albums</a> between 1978 and 1981, and toured <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in 1979.</p>
<p>In 2008 Cutler, Frith and others formed <b>Art Bears Songbook</b>, an Art Bears "review" project that performed at the <a href="/wiki/Festival_International_de_Musique_Actuelle_de_Victoriaville" title="Festival International de Musique Actuelle de Victoriaville">Festival International de Musique Actuelle de Victoriaville</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, Canada.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Art_Bears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Art Bears were formed during the recording of Henry Cow's last album after disagreements arose over the album's content. Frith and Cutler favoured song-oriented material, while others in the band preferred instrumental compositions. As a compromise, Frith, Cutler and Krause agreed, early in 1978, to release the songs already created on their own album, <i><a href="/wiki/Hopes_and_Fears_(Art_Bears_album)" title="Hopes and Fears (Art Bears album)">Hopes and Fears</a></i>, under the name Art Bears, with the rest of Henry Cow credited as guests. The instrumental material appeared later on the final Henry Cow album, <i><a href="/wiki/Western_Culture_(album)" title="Western Culture (album)">Western Culture</a></i> (1979).</p>
<p><i>Hopes and Fears</i> (1978) thus consisted of Henry Cow songs with the addition of new Art Bears material recorded later by Frith, Cutler and Krause. Towards the end of 1978, Art Bears returned to the studio to record their first "true" album, <i><a href="/wiki/Winter_Songs_(Art_Bears_album)" title="Winter Songs (Art Bears album)">Winter Songs</a></i> (1979). It comprised fourteen short songs composed by Frith around texts by Cutler inspired by carvings on the <a href="/wiki/Stylobate" title="Stylobate">stylobate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Amiens_Cathedral" title="Amiens Cathedral">Amiens Cathedral</a>.</p>
<p>In December 1978, Art Bears joined <a href="/wiki/Rock_in_Opposition" title="Rock in Opposition">Rock in Opposition</a> (RIO), and toured Europe in April and May 1979. For the tour, they were joined by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blegvad" title="Peter Blegvad">Peter Blegvad</a> (ex-<a href="/wiki/Slapp_Happy" title="Slapp Happy">Slapp Happy</a>, guitar, bass guitar, voice) and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Hollander" title="Marc Hollander">Marc Hollander</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aksak_Maboul" title="Aksak Maboul">Aksak Maboul</a>, keyboards, clarinet). They rehearsed at the Cold Storage Recording Studios in <a href="/wiki/Brixton" title="Brixton">Brixton</a>, before leaving for Italy in late April. They performed in Italy, France, Belgium and Czechoslovakia, including an RIO festival on 1 May in Milan. Some of the songs recorded during the tour were later added to the CD release of <i>Hopes and Fears</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Art_Box" title="The Art Box">The Art Box</a></i> (2004), a <a href="/wiki/Box_set" title="Box set">box set</a> of Art Bears material.</p>
<p>The band returned to the studio in 1980 to make one final album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_as_It_Is_Today" title="The World as It Is Today">The World as It Is Today</a></i> (1981), before splitting up. In October 1983 Frith, Cutler and Krause reunited again when they joined <a href="/wiki/Duck_and_Cover_(German_band)" title="Duck and Cover (German band)">Duck and Cover</a>, a commission from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Jazz_Festival" title="Berlin Jazz Festival">Berlin Jazz Festival</a>, for a performance of the "<a href="/wiki/Duck_and_Cover_(German_band)#.22Berlin_Programme.22" title="Duck and Cover (German band)">Berlin Programme</a>" in <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a>, followed by another in February 1984 in <a href="/wiki/East_Berlin" title="East Berlin">East Berlin</a>, and one at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Contemporary_Arts" title="Institute of Contemporary Arts">ICA</a> in London the following August. The "Berlin Programme" included fragments of three Art Bears songs.</p>
<p>In 1993 Frith, Cutler and Krause worked together again on a song project, <i>Domestic Stories</i> (1993) by Chris Cutler and <a href="/wiki/Lutz_Glandien" title="Lutz Glandien">Lutz Glandien</a>, with saxophonist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Harth" title="Alfred Harth">Alfred Harth</a>. While similar to Art Bears, the addition of Glandien's electronic music made <i>Domestic Stories</i> a distinctly different album.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_Bears_Songbook">Art Bears Songbook</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Art_Bears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Art Bears Songbook">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An Art Bears "review" took place in May 2008 at the world premiere of the Art Bears Songbook at the 25th <a href="/wiki/Festival_International_de_Musique_Actuelle_de_Victoriaville" title="Festival International de Musique Actuelle de Victoriaville">Festival International de Musique Actuelle de Victoriaville</a> in Quebec. It was performed by Cutler (drums), Frith (guitar, bass guitar, violin, piano), <a href="/wiki/Jewlia_Eisenberg" title="Jewlia Eisenberg">Jewlia Eisenberg</a> (voice), <a href="/wiki/Carla_Kihlstedt" title="Carla Kihlstedt">Carla Kihlstedt</a> (violin, voice), <a href="/wiki/Zeena_Parkins" title="Zeena Parkins">Zeena Parkins</a> (keyboards, accordion), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kristin_Slipp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kristin Slipp (page does not exist)">Kristin Slipp</a> (voice) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Norman_Conquest_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Norman Conquest (musician) (page does not exist)">The Norman Conquest</a> (sound manipulation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAJ_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAJ-2">[2]</a></sup> Krause was unable to participate, so Frith and Cutler decided to rework the trio's repertoire for an expanded group, with the voices of Eisenberg, Slipp and Kihlstedt replacing Krause's "eccentric and idiomatic delivery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAJ_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AAJ-2">[2]</a></sup> The project was so-named because Frith and Cutler did not want it to be seen as an Art Bears reunion. According to <i><a href="/wiki/All_About_Jazz" title="All About Jazz">All About Jazz</a></i> the Art Bears Songbook was "not just a highlight, but the highlight of the [five-day] festival."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAJ_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AAJ-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another performance of the Art Bears Songbook took place at the third edition of the French <a href="/wiki/Rock_in_Opposition" title="Rock in Opposition">Rock in Opposition</a> event in <a href="/wiki/Carmaux" title="Carmaux">Carmaux</a> in September 2010. The line-up was the same as before, except that Krause, who had agreed to come out of retirement, replaced Eisenberg, who was ill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Art_Bears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Art Bears's music was often deeply political in content (reflecting the band's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> leanings) and frequently <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde_music" title="Avant-garde music">avant-garde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Experimental_music" title="Experimental music">experimental</a>. They were more "song oriented" than Henry Cow, although much of the material on their debut album was actually written with the intention of being performed by the latter band.</p>
<p>Their music was "very dark in concept and in atmosphere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Reviewing <i>The Art Box</i>, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Music" title="BBC Music">BBC Music</a> described it as: "Carefully wrought dissonances, angular folk tunes, sudden shifts in dynamics, dense layers of spectral drones, slabs of noise, topped off with Dagmar's strange, elastic <a href="/wiki/Sprechgesang" title="Sprechgesang">Sprechstimme</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Krause's voice contributed significantly to the mood and character of the songs. Cutler said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I don't write simple or obvious words, [...] they are not easy to sing. Dagmar had the amazing ability to make them make sense, to make them sound obvious. She sings from the inside and her accent helps to lift words out of their slots and give them a slightly resonant displacement. No one else could have done what Dagmar did on those LPs. I'm still amazed by her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In "<a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive</a>" circles, the Art Bears were generally well received. <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> wrote: "Their life was fleeting, but the Art Bears wrote and recorded bold, challenging, idiosyncratic music that, despite its occasional difficulty, is ultimately very rewarding."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Art_Bears&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Art Bears took their name from a sentence in <a href="/wiki/Jane_Ellen_Harrison" title="Jane Ellen Harrison">Jane Ellen Harrison</a>'s book <i>Ancient Art and Ritual</i> (1913): "Even to-day, when individualism is rampant, art bears traces of its collective, social origin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Chris Cutler explains that it was a deliberate out-of-context quote, but that "not too much should be read into this; it just sounds intriguing, has an animal in it, plays with ambiguity and is mildly ridiculous".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
