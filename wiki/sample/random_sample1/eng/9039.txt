<p><b>Attis</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἄττις</span> or <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἄττης</span>) was the consort of <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a> in <a href="/wiki/Phrygians" title="Phrygians">Phrygian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> His priests were <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">eunuchs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Galli" title="Galli">Galli</a>, as explained by <a href="/wiki/Origin_myth" title="Origin myth">origin myths</a> pertaining to Attis and <a href="/wiki/Castration" title="Castration">castration</a>. Attis was also a Phrygian <a href="/wiki/Vegetation_deity" title="Vegetation deity">god of vegetation</a>, and in his self-mutilation, death, and resurrection he represents the fruits of the earth, which die in winter only to rise again in the spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 19th-century identification with the name <i>Atys</i> encountered in <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> (i.34–45) as the historical name of the son of <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">Croesus</a> ("Atys the sun god, slain by the boar's tusk of winter")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is mistaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2600" data-file-width="1400" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg/150px-Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg/225px-Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg/300px-Attis_thymiaterion_Louvre_Tarse61.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Terracotta <a href="/wiki/Thymiaterion" title="Thymiaterion">thymiaterion</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarsus_(city)" title="Tarsus (city)">Tarsus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>)</div>

<p>An Attis cult began around 1250 BCE in <a href="/wiki/Dindymon" title="Dindymon">Dindymon</a> (today's Murat Dağı of <a href="/wiki/Gediz,_K%C3%BCtahya" title="Gediz, Kütahya">Gediz, Kütahya</a>, Turkey). He was originally a local semi-deity of <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>, associated with the great Phrygian trading city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pessinos" title="Pessinos">Pessinos</a>, which lay under the lee of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Agdistis" title="Mount Agdistis">Mount Agdistis</a>. The mountain was personified as a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daemon_(mythology)" title="Daemon (mythology)">daemon</a></i>, whom foreigners associated with the Great Mother Cybele.</p>
<p>In the late 4th century BC, a cult of Attis became a feature of the Greek world. The story of his origins at <a href="/wiki/Agdistis" title="Agdistis">Agdistis</a>, recorded by the traveler <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, have some distinctly non-Greek elements: Pausanias was told that the <i>daemon</i> Agdistis initially bore both male and female attributes. But the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympian_gods" title="Olympian gods">Olympian gods</a>, fearing Agdistis, cut off the male organ and cast it away. There grew up from it an almond-tree, and when its fruit was ripe, <a href="/wiki/Nana_(Greek_mythology)" title="Nana (Greek mythology)">Nana</a>, who was a daughter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_deity" title="Water deity">river-god</a> <a href="/wiki/Sangarius_(mythology)" title="Sangarius (mythology)">Sangarius</a>, picked an almond and laid it in her bosom. The almond disappeared, and she became pregnant. Nana abandoned the baby (Attis). The infant was tended by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/He-goat" title="He-goat">he-goat</a>. As Attis grew, his long-haired beauty was godlike, and Agdistis as Cybele then fell in love with him. But the foster parents of Attis sent him to Pessinos, where he was to wed the king's daughter. According to some versions the King of Pessinos was <a href="/wiki/Midas" title="Midas">Midas</a>. Just as the marriage-song was being sung, Agdistis/Cybele appeared in her transcendent power, and Attis went mad and cut off his genitals. Attis' father-in-law-to-be, the king who was giving his daughter in marriage, followed suit, prefiguring the self-castrating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corybantes" title="Corybantes">corybantes</a> who devoted themselves to Cybele. But Agdistis repented and saw to it that the body of Attis should neither rot at all nor decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the temple of Cybele in Pessinus, the mother of the gods was still called Agdistis, the geographer <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> recounted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>As neighboring <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a> came to control Phrygia, the cult of Attis was given a Lydian context too. Attis is said to have introduced to Lydia the cult of the Mother Goddess Cybele, incurring the jealousy of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, who sent a boar to destroy the Lydian crops. Then certain Lydians, with Attis himself, were killed by the boar. Pausanias adds, to corroborate this story, that the Gauls who inhabited Pessinos abstained from pork. This myth element may have been invented solely to explain the unusual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_law" title="Dietary law">dietary laws</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Galatia" title="Galatia">Lydian Gauls</a>. In Rome, the eunuch followers of Cybele were known as <i><a href="/wiki/Galli" title="Galli">Galli</a></i>.</p>
<p>Julian the Apostate gives an account of the spread of the orgiastic cult of <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a> in his <i>Oratio</i> 5. It spread from Anatolia to Greece and eventually to Rome in Republican times, and the cult of Attis, her reborn eunuch consort, accompanied her.</p>
<p>The first literary reference to Attis is the subject of one of the most famous poems by <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but it appears that Attis was not worshipped at Rome until the early Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a> mentions Attis' self-mutilation in his poem "The Sphinx": "And Atys with his blood-stained knife were better than the thing I am."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_finds">Archaeological finds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeological finds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_great_mother_of_the_gods_(1901)_(14594571307).jpg"><img alt="The great mother of the gods (1901) (14594571307).jpg" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3636" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/The_great_mother_of_the_gods_%281901%29_%2814594571307%29.jpg/300px-The_great_mother_of_the_gods_%281901%29_%2814594571307%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/The_great_mother_of_the_gods_%281901%29_%2814594571307%29.jpg/450px-The_great_mother_of_the_gods_%281901%29_%2814594571307%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/The_great_mother_of_the_gods_%281901%29_%2814594571307%29.jpg/600px-The_great_mother_of_the_gods_%281901%29_%2814594571307%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p>The most important representation of Attis is the life size statue discovered at <a href="/wiki/Ostia_Antica" title="Ostia Antica">Ostia</a>. The statue is of a reclining Attis, after the emasculation. In his left hand is a <a href="/wiki/Shepherd%27s_crook" title="Shepherd's crook">shepherd's crook</a>, in his right hand a <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a>. His head is crowned with a <a href="/wiki/Wreath_(attire)" title="Wreath (attire)">pine garland with fruits</a>, bronze rays of the sun, and on his Phrygian cap is a crescent moon. It was discovered in 1867 at the Campus of the Magna Mater together with other statues. The objects seem to have been hidden there in late antiquity. A plaster cast is in the apse of the Sanctuary of Attis at the Campus of the Magna Mater, the original is in the Vatican Museums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A marble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bas-relief" title="Bas-relief">bas-relief</a> depicting Cybele in her chariot and Attis, from <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a>, is in the archaeological museum in Venice. The pair also feature prominently on the silver <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parabiago_plate" title="Parabiago plate">Parabiago plate</a>.</p>
<p>A finely executed silvery brass Attis that had been ritually consigned to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moselle_(river)" title="Moselle (river)">Mosel</a> was recovered during construction in 1963 and is kept at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rheinisches_Landesmuseum_of_Trier" title="Rheinisches Landesmuseum of Trier">Rheinisches Landesmuseum of Trier</a>. It shows the typically Anatolian costume of the god: trousers fastened together down the front of the legs with toggles and the <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_cap" title="Phrygian cap">Phrygian cap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, in the ruins of Herculaneum a wooden throne was discovered adorned with a relief of Attis beneath a sacred pine tree, gathering cones. Various finds suggest that the cult of Attis was popular in Herculaneum at the time of the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
