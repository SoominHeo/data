<p>The family <b>Apodanthaceae</b> comprises about 10 species<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christenhusz-Byng2016_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christenhusz-Byng2016-1">[1]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">endoparasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbs</a>. They live in the branches or stems of their hosts (as filaments similar to a fungal <a href="/wiki/Mycelium" title="Mycelium">mycelium</a>), emerging only to flower and fruit. The plants produce no green parts and do not carry out any photosynthesis (that is, they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holoparasitism" title="Holoparasitism">holoparasitic</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There are two genera: <i><a href="/wiki/Pilostyles" title="Pilostyles">Pilostyles</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Apodanthes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Apodanthes (page does not exist)">Apodanthes</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blarer2004_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-blarer2004-3">[3]</a></sup> A third genus, <i>Berlinianche</i>, was never validly published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bellot2013_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bellot2013-4">[4]</a></sup> Mitochondrial and nuclear DNA sequences confidently place the Apodanthaceae in the Cucurbitales, where they also fit well in terms of their flower morphology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filipowicz_and_Renner.2C_2010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filipowicz_and_Renner.2C_2010-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
