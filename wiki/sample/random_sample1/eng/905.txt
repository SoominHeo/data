<p><b>Do it yourself</b> (<b>DIY</b>) is the method of building, modifying, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Repair" title="Repair">repairing</a> things without the direct aid of experts or professionals. Academic research describes DIY as behaviors where "individuals engage raw and semi-raw materials and component parts to produce, transform, or reconstruct material possessions, including those drawn from the natural environment (e.g. landscaping)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> DIY behavior can be triggered by various motivations previously categorized as <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">marketplace</a> motivations (economic benefits, lack of product availability, lack of product quality, need for customization), and <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">identity</a> enhancement (craftsmanship, empowerment, community seeking, uniqueness).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "do-it-yourself" has been associated with consumers since at least 1912 primarily in the domain of home improvement and maintenance activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The phrase "do it yourself" had come into common usage (in standard English) by the 1950s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constructing_Contemporary:_Common_sense_approaches_to_.27going_modern_in_the_1950s_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Constructing_Contemporary:_Common_sense_approaches_to_.27going_modern_in_the_1950s-4">[4]</a></sup> in reference to the emergence of a trend of people undertaking <a href="/wiki/Home_improvement" title="Home improvement">home improvement</a> and various other small craft and construction projects as both a creative-recreational and cost-saving activity.</p>
<p>Subsequently, the term DIY has taken on a broader meaning that covers a wide range of skill sets. DIY is associated with the international <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a> music scenes; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indymedia" title="Indymedia">indymedia</a> networks, <a href="/wiki/Pirate_radio" title="Pirate radio">pirate radio</a> stations, and the <a href="/wiki/Zine" title="Zine">zine</a> community. In this context, DIY is related to the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a>, in that it offers an alternative to modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_culture" title="Consumer culture">consumer culture</a>'s emphasis on relying on others to satisfy needs. The abbreviation DIY is also widely used in the military as a way to teach commanders or other types of units to take responsibility, so that they'd be able to do things themselves just as a preparation for their own future.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_it_yourself&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Italian archaeologists unearthed the ruins of a 6th-century BC <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> structure in southern Italy that came with detailed assembly instructions and is being called an "ancient <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a> building". The structure was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_temple" title="Greek temple">temple-like</a> building discovered at <a href="/wiki/Tito,_Basilicata" title="Tito, Basilicata">Torre Satriano</a>, near the southern city of <a href="/wiki/Potenza" title="Potenza">Potenza</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">Basilicata</a>, a region where local people mingled with <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> who settled along the southern coast known as <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> from the 8th century BC onwards. Professor Christopher Smith, director of the <a href="/wiki/British_School_at_Rome" title="British School at Rome">British School at Rome</a>, said that the discovery was "the clearest example yet found of mason's marks of the time. It looks as if someone was instructing others how to mass-produce components and put them together in this way". Much like the instruction booklets, various sections of the luxury building were inscribed with coded symbols showing how the pieces slotted together. The characteristics of these inscriptions indicate they date back to around the 6th century BC, which tallies with the architectural evidence suggested by the decoration. The building was built by Greek artisans coming from the <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> colony of <a href="/wiki/Taranto" title="Taranto">Taranto</a> in <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_improvement">Home improvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_it_yourself&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Home improvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Home_improvement" title="Home improvement">Home improvement</a></div>

<p>The DIY movement is a re-introduction (often to urban and suburban dwellers) of the old pattern of personal involvement and use of skills in upkeep of a house or apartment, making clothes; maintenance of cars, computers, websites; or any material aspect of living. The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Alan_Watts" title="Alan Watts">Alan Watts</a> (from the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houseboat_Summit" title="Houseboat Summit">Houseboat Summit</a>" panel discussion in a 1967 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Oracle" title="San Francisco Oracle">San Francisco Oracle</a></i>) reflected a growing sentiment:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Our educational system, in its entirety, does nothing to give us any kind of material competence. In other words, we don't learn how to cook, how to make clothes, how to build houses, how to make love, or to do any of the absolutely fundamental things of life. The whole education that we get for our children in school is entirely in terms of abstractions. It trains you to be an insurance salesman or a bureaucrat, or some kind of cerebral character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the 1970s, DIY spread through the North American population of college- and recent-college-graduate age groups. In part, this movement involved the renovation of affordable, rundown older homes. But it also related to various projects expressing the social and environmental vision of the 1960s and early 1970s. The young visionary <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Brand" title="Stewart Brand">Stewart Brand</a>, working with friends and family, and initially using the most basic of typesetting and page-layout tools, published the first edition of <i>The <a href="/wiki/Whole_Earth_Catalog" title="Whole Earth Catalog">Whole Earth Catalog</a></i> (subtitled <i>Access to Tools</i>) in late 1968.</p>

<p>The first <i>Catalog</i>, and its successors, used a broad definition of the term "tools". There were informational tools, such as books (often technical in nature), professional journals, courses, classes, and the like. There were specialized, designed items, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenters" title="Carpenters">carpenters</a>' and <a href="/wiki/Stonemasonry" title="Stonemasonry">masons</a>' tools, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garden_tools" title="Garden tools">garden tools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> equipment, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chainsaws" title="Chainsaws">chainsaws</a>, fiberglass materials and so on; even early personal computers. The designer <a href="/wiki/J._Baldwin" title="J. Baldwin">J. Baldwin</a> acted as editor to include such items, writing many of the reviews. The <i>Catalog'</i>s publication both emerged from and spurred the great wave of experimentalism, convention-breaking, and do-it-yourself attitude of the late 1960s. Often copied, the <i>Catalog</i> appealed to a wide cross-section of people in North America and had a broad influence.</p>
<p>For decades, magazines such as <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">Popular Mechanics</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mechanix_Illustrated" title="Mechanix Illustrated">Mechanix Illustrated</a></i> offered a way for readers to keep current on useful practical skills and techniques. DIY home improvement books began to flourish in the 1970s, first created as collections of magazine articles. An early, extensive line of DIY how-to books was created by <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Books" title="Sunset Books">Sunset Books</a>, based upon previously published articles from their magazine, <i><a href="/wiki/Sunset_(magazine)" title="Sunset (magazine)">Sunset</a></i>, based in California. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-Life" title="Time-Life">Time-Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Better_Homes_and_Gardens_(magazine)" title="Better Homes and Gardens (magazine)">Better Homes and Gardens</a>, and other publishers soon followed suit.</p>

<p>In the mid-1990s, DIY <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Home-improvement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Home-improvement (page does not exist)">home-improvement</a> content began to find its way onto the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. HouseNet was the earliest bulletin-board style site where users could share information. HomeTips.com, established in early 1995, was among the first Web-based sites to deliver free extensive DIY home-improvement content created by expert authors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Since the late 1990s, DIY has exploded on the Web through thousands of sites.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, when home video (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VCR" title="VCR">VCRs</a>) came along, DIY instructors quickly grasped its potential for demonstrating processes by audio-visual means. In 1979, the <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/This_Old_House" title="This Old House">This Old House</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Bob_Vila" title="Bob Vila">Bob Vila</a>, premiered and this spurred a DIY television revolution. The show was immensely popular, educating people on how to improve their living conditions (and the value of their house) without the expense of paying someone else to do (as much of) the work. In 1994, the <a href="/wiki/HGTV" title="HGTV">HGTV</a> Network cable television channel was launched in the United States and Canada, followed in 1999 by the <a href="/wiki/DIY_Network" title="DIY Network">DIY Network</a> cable television channel. Both were launched to appeal to the growing percentage of North Americans interested in DIY topics, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_renovation" title="Home renovation">home improvement</a> to <a href="/wiki/Knitting" title="Knitting">knitting</a>. Such channels have multiple shows showing how to stretch one's budget to achieve professional-looking results (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Design_Cents&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Design Cents (page does not exist)">Design Cents</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Design_on_a_Dime" title="Design on a Dime">Design on a Dime</a></i>, etc.) while doing the work yourself. <i><a href="/wiki/Toolbelt_Diva" title="Toolbelt Diva">Toolbelt Diva</a></i> specifically caters to female DIYers.</p>
<p>Beyond magazines and television, the scope of home improvement DIY continues to grow online where most mainstream media outlets now have extensive DIY-focused informational websites such as <i><a href="/wiki/This_Old_House" title="This Old House">This Old House</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Martha_Stewart" title="Martha Stewart">Martha Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hometalk" title="Hometalk">Hometalk</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/DIY_Network" title="DIY Network">DIY Network</a>. These are often extensions of their magazine or television brand. The growth of independent online DIY resources is also spiking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The number of homeowners who <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> about their experiences continues to grow, along with DIY websites from smaller organizations.</p>

<p>DIY amongst the <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a> community is popular. With ideas being shared on social media such as <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> about clothing, jewellery and hair styles. Techniques include <a href="/wiki/Distressing" title="Distressing">distressing</a> jeans, bleaching jeans, redesigning an old shirt, and studding <a href="/wiki/Denim" title="Denim">denim</a>. <li>Skateparks built by skateboarders without paid professional assistance</li>

<p>DIY as a subculture could be said to have begun with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punk_movement" title="Punk movement">punk movement</a> of the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Instead of traditional means of bands reaching their audiences through large music labels, bands began recording, manufacturing albums and merchandise, booking their own tours, and creating opportunities for smaller bands to get wider recognition and gain cult status through repetitive low-cost DIY touring. The burgeoning <a href="/wiki/Zine" title="Zine">zine</a> movement took up coverage of and promotion of the <a href="/wiki/Underground_music" title="Underground music">underground</a> punk scenes, and significantly altered the way fans interacted with musicians. Zines quickly branched off from being hand-made music magazines to become more personal; they quickly became one of the youth culture's gateways to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DIY_culture" title="DIY culture">DIY culture</a>. This led to tutorial zines showing others how to make their own shirts, posters, zines, books, food, etc.</p>

<p>Publications and websites that focus on DIY and/or crafting content include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bazaar_Bizarre" title="Bazaar Bizarre">Bazaar Bizarre</a> - An informal craft show and a book with the same name emphasizing subcultural aesthetics</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Craft_(magazine)" title="Craft (magazine)"><i>Craft</i></a> - A magazine publication focusing on projects using cheap materials</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Craftster" title="Craftster">Craftster</a> - An online crafting community making use of traditional crafting techniques</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CrimethInc." title="CrimethInc.">CrimethInc.</a> - An anarchist collective encouraging self-publishing</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destination_DIY" title="Destination DIY">Destination DIY</a> - An Oregon radio show discussing DIY projects</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hometalk" title="Hometalk">Hometalk</a> - A social networking site focusing home improvement</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Instructables" title="Instructables">Instructables</a> - A website featuring user-published DIY projects of a wide range of topics</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lifehacker" title="Lifehacker">Lifehacker</a> - A website revolving around life tips</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Make_(magazine)" title="Make (magazine)"><i>Make</i></a> - A magazine publication covering a wide array of complex projects</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maker_Faire" title="Maker Faire">Maker Faire</a> - Regular conferences and exhibitions / events in various places, sometimes for a very large attendance. Sponsored by Make Magazine</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Microcosm_Publishing" title="Microcosm Publishing">Microcosm Publishing</a> - A magazine encouraging self-dependence</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">Popular Mechanics</a> - A magazine featuring popular technology</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ReadyMade_Magazine" title="ReadyMade Magazine"><i>ReadyMade</i> (magazine)</a> - A publication focusing on sustainable design and independent music</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TechShop" title="TechShop">TechShop</a> - A chain of workshops enabling people of various skill level to use industrial equipment</li>
</ul>
