<p><i><b>Apollo 13</b></i> is a 1995 American <a href="/wiki/Docudrama" title="Docudrama">docudrama</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_about_outer_space" title="List of films about outer space">space</a> <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Bacon" title="Kevin Bacon">Kevin Bacon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Paxton" title="Bill Paxton">Bill Paxton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Sinise" title="Gary Sinise">Gary Sinise</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ed_Harris" title="Ed Harris">Ed Harris</a>. The screenplay by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Broyles,_Jr." title="William Broyles, Jr.">William Broyles, Jr.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Al_Reinert" title="Al Reinert">Al Reinert</a>, that dramatizes the aborted 1970 <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a> lunar mission, is an adaptation of the book <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Moon" title="Lost Moon">Lost Moon: The Perilous Voyage of Apollo 13</a></i> by astronaut <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">Jim Lovell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Kluger" title="Jeffrey Kluger">Jeffrey Kluger</a>.</p>
<p>The film depicts astronauts Lovell, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Swigert" title="Jack Swigert">Jack Swigert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Haise" title="Fred Haise">Fred Haise</a> aboard <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a> for America's third Moon landing mission. En route, an on-board explosion deprives their spacecraft of most of its oxygen supply and electric power, forcing <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s flight controllers to abort the Moon landing, and turning the mission into a struggle to get the three men home safely.</p>
<p>Howard went to great lengths to create a technically accurate movie, employing <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s technical assistance in astronaut and flight controller training for his cast, and even obtaining permission to film scenes aboard a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduced_gravity_aircraft" title="Reduced gravity aircraft">reduced gravity aircraft</a> for realistic depiction of the "weightlessness" experienced by the astronauts in space.</p>
<p>Released to cinemas in the United States on June 30, 1995,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Apollo 13</i> was nominated for nine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a> (winning for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Best Film Editing">Best Film Editing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Sound" title="Academy Award for Best Sound">Best Sound</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscar_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscar-4">[4]</a></sup> In total, the film grossed over $355 million worldwide during its theatrical releases. The film was very positively received by critics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Astronaut Jim Lovell hosts a <a href="/wiki/House_party" title="House party">house party</a> where guests watch <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>'s televised first human steps on the Moon. Afterwards Lovell, who had orbited the Moon on <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a>, tells his wife Marilyn that he intends to return to the Moon to walk on its surface.</p>
<p>Three months later, as Lovell conducts a VIP tour of NASA's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_Assembly_Building" title="Vertical Assembly Building">Vertical Assembly Building</a>, his boss <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a> informs him that his crew will fly Apollo 13 instead of 14. Lovell, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Mattingly" title="Ken Mattingly">Ken Mattingly</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_W._Haise,_Jr." title="Fred W. Haise, Jr.">Fred Haise</a> train for their new mission. A few days before launch, Mattingly is exposed to <a href="/wiki/Rubella" title="Rubella">the measles</a>, and the flight surgeon demands his replacement with Mattingly's backup, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Swigert" title="Jack Swigert">Jack Swigert</a>. Lovell resists breaking up his team, but relents when Slayton threatens to bump his crew to a later mission. As the launch date approaches, Marilyn has a nightmare about her husband's safety, but goes to the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> the night before launch to see him off.</p>
<p>On April 11, 1970, Flight Director <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_F._Kranz" title="Eugene F. Kranz">Gene Kranz</a> gives the go-ahead from Houston's <a href="/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr._Mission_Control_Center" title="Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center">Mission Control Center</a> for the Apollo 13 launch. As the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket climbs through the atmosphere, a second stage engine cuts off prematurely, but the craft reaches its Earth parking orbit. After the third stage fires to send Apollo 13 to the Moon, Swigert performs the <a href="/wiki/Transposition,_docking,_and_extraction" title="Transposition, docking, and extraction">maneuver</a> to connect the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Command/Service Module</a> <i>Odyssey</i> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Module" title="Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a> <i>Aquarius</i> and pull it away from the spent rocket.</p>
<p>Three days into the mission, the crew makes a television transmission, which the networks decline to broadcast live. After Swigert turns on the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> tank stirring fans as requested, one of the tanks explodes, emptying its contents into space and sending the craft tumbling. The other tank is soon found to be leaking. Mission Control aborts the Moon landing, and Lovell and Haise must hurriedly power up <i>Aquarius</i> to use as a "lifeboat" for the return home, as Swigert shuts down <i>Odyssey</i> before its battery power runs out. In Houston, Kranz rallies his team to come up with a plan to bring the astronauts home safely, declaring "failure is not an option". Controller <a href="/wiki/John_Aaron" title="John Aaron">John Aaron</a> recruits Mattingly to help him invent a procedure to restart <i>Odyssey</i> for the landing on Earth.</p>
<p>As Swigert and Haise watch the Moon pass beneath them, Lovell laments his lost chance of walking on its surface, then turns their attention to the business of getting home. With <i>Aquarius</i> running on minimal electrical power, the crew suffers freezing conditions, and Haise contracts a urinary infection and a fever. Swigert suspects Mission Control is withholding their inability to get them home; Haise angrily blames Swigert's inexperience for the accident; and Lovell quickly squelches the argument. When <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> approaches dangerous levels, ground control must quickly invent a way to make the Command Module's square filters work in the Lunar Module's round receptacles. With the guidance systems on <i>Aquarius</i> shut down, the crew must make a difficult but vital course correction by manually igniting the Lunar Module's engine.</p>
<p>Mattingly and Aaron struggle to find a way to turn on the Command Module systems without drawing too much power, and finally transmit the procedure to Swigert, who restarts <i>Odyssey</i> by transferring extra power from <i>Aquarius</i>. When the crew jettisons the Service Module, they are surprised to see the extent of the damage. As they release <i>Aquarius</i> and re-enter the Earth's atmosphere, no one is sure that <i>Odyssey</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablative_heat_shield" title="Ablative heat shield">heat shield</a> is intact. The tense period of radio silence due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionization_blackout" title="Ionization blackout">ionization blackout</a> is longer than normal, but the astronauts report all is well and splash down in the Pacific Ocean.</p>
<p>As helicopters bring the three men aboard the recovery ship <a href="/wiki/USS_Iwo_Jima_(LPH-2)" title="USS Iwo Jima (LPH-2)">USS <i>Iwo Jima</i></a> for a hero's welcome, Lovell's voice-over describes the subsequent investigation into the explosion, and the careers of Haise, Swigert, Mattingly, and Kranz. He wonders if and when mankind will return to the Moon.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preproduction_and_props">Preproduction and props</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preproduction and props">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While planning the film, director Ron Howard decided that every shot of the film would be original and that no mission footage would be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDProductionNotes_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVDProductionNotes-12">[12]</a></sup> The spacecraft interiors were constructed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansas_Cosmosphere_and_Space_Center" title="Kansas Cosmosphere and Space Center">Kansas Cosmosphere and Space Center</a>'s Space Works, which also restored the Apollo 13 Command Module. Two individual Lunar Modules and two Command Modules were constructed for filming. While each was a replica, composed of some of the original Apollo materials, they were built so that different sections were removable, which enabled filming to take place inside the capsules. Space Works also built modified Command and Lunar Modules for filming inside a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_KC-135_Stratotanker" title="Boeing KC-135 Stratotanker">Boeing KC-135</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduced_gravity_aircraft" title="Reduced gravity aircraft">reduced-gravity aircraft</a>, and the pressure suits worn by the actors, which are exact reproductions of those worn by the Apollo astronauts, right down to the detail of being airtight. When the actors put the suits on with their helmets locked in place, air was pumped into the suits to cool them down and allow them to breathe, exactly as in launch preparations for the real Apollo missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProductionNotes2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ProductionNotes2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The real <a href="/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr._Mission_Control_Center" title="Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center">Mission Control Center</a> consisted of two control rooms located on the second and third floors of Building 30 at the <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> offered the use of the control room for filming, but Howard declined, opting instead to make his own replica from scratch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDProductionNotes_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-DVDProductionNotes-12">[12]</a></sup> Production designer Michael Corenblith and set decorator Merideth Boswell were in charge of the construction of the Mission Control set at Universal Studios. The set was equipped with giant rear-screen projection capabilities and a complex set of computers with individual video feeds to all the flight controller stations. The actors playing the flight controllers were able to communicate with each other on a private audio loop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProductionNotes2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ProductionNotes2-13">[13]</a></sup> The Mission Control room built for the film was on the ground floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDProductionNotes_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-DVDProductionNotes-12">[12]</a></sup> One NASA employee, who was a consultant for the film, said that the set was so realistic that he would leave at the end of the day and look for the elevator before remembering he was not in Mission Control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-6">[6]</a></sup> By the time the film was made, the USS <i>Iwo Jima</i> had been scrapped, so her sister ship, the <a href="/wiki/USS_New_Orleans_(LPH-11)" title="USS New Orleans (LPH-11)">USS <i>New Orleans</i></a>, was used as the recovery ship, instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDProductionNotes_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-DVDProductionNotes-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:35%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"For actors, being able to actually shoot in zero gravity as opposed to being in incredibly painful and uncomfortable harnesses for special effects shots was all the difference between what would have been a horrible moviemaking experience as opposed to the completely glorious one that it actually was."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">—Tom Hanks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProductionNotes2_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-ProductionNotes2-13">[13]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Howard anticipated difficulty in portraying <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">weightlessness</a> in a realistic manner. He discussed this with <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>, who suggested using a KC-135 airplane, which can be flown in such a way as to create about 23 seconds of weightlessness, a method NASA has always used to train its astronauts for space flight. Howard obtained NASA's permission and assistance in filming in the realistic conditions aboard multiple KC-135 flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Findustrycentral.net.2Fdirector_interviews.2FRH01.HTM_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Findustrycentral.net.2Fdirector_interviews.2FRH01.HTM-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast_training_and_filming">Cast training and filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cast training and filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To prepare for their roles in the film, Hanks, Paxton, and Bacon all attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Space_Camp" title="U.S. Space Camp">U.S. Space Camp</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>. While there, astronauts Jim Lovell and <a href="/wiki/David_Scott" title="David Scott">David Scott</a>, commander of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a>, did actual training exercises with the actors inside a simulated Command Module and Lunar Module. The actors were also taught about each of the 500 buttons, toggles, and switches used to operate the spacecraft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The actors then traveled to <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a> in Houston, where they flew in NASA's KC-135 reduced-gravity aircraft to simulate weightlessness in outer space. While in the KC-135, filming took place in bursts of 25 seconds, the length of each period of weightlessness that the plane could produce. The filmmakers eventually flew 612 <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabolas</a> which added up to a total of three hours and 54 minutes of weightlessness. Parts of the Command Module, Lunar Module, and the tunnel that connected them were built by production designer Michael Corenblith, art directors David J. Bomba and Bruce Alan Miller, and their crew to fit inside the KC-135. Filming in such an environment, while never done before for a film, was a tremendous time saver. In the KC-135, the actors moved wherever they wanted, surrounded by floating props; the camera and cameraman were weightless, so filming could take place on any axis from which a shot could be set up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In Los Angeles, Ed Harris and all the actors portraying <a href="/wiki/Flight_controller" title="Flight controller">flight controllers</a> enrolled in a Flight Controller School led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerry_Griffin" title="Gerry Griffin">Gerry Griffin</a>, an Apollo 13 flight director, and flight controller Jerry Bostick. The actors studied audiotapes from the mission, reviewed hundreds of pages of NASA transcripts, and attended a crash course in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDProductionNotes_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-DVDProductionNotes-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProductionNotes2_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-ProductionNotes2-13">[13]</a></sup> Astronaut Dave Scott was impressed with their efforts, stating that each actor was determined to make every scene technically correct, word for word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lost_moon_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-lost_moon-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The score to <i>Apollo 13</i> was composed and conducted by <a href="/wiki/James_Horner" title="James Horner">James Horner</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">soundtrack</a> was released in 1995 by <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA Records</a> and has seven tracks of score, eight period songs used in the film, and seven tracks of dialogue by the actors at a running time of nearly seventy-eight minutes. The music also features solos by vocalist <a href="/wiki/Annie_Lennox" title="Annie Lennox">Annie Lennox</a> and Tim Morrison on the trumpet. The score was a critical success and garnered Horner an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Best Original Score</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>All music composed by <a href="/wiki/James_Horner" title="James Horner">James Horner</a>, except where noted.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was released on 30 June 1995 in North America and on 22 September 1995 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a>.</p>
<p>In September 2002 the film was re-released in <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a>. It was the first film to be digitally remastered using <a href="/wiki/IMAX#IMAX_DMR_.28Digital_Media_Remastering.29" title="IMAX">IMAX DMR</a> technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reports that the film has an overall approval rating of 95%, based on 85 reviews, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_average" title="Weighted average">weighted average</a> rating of 8.2/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "In recreating the troubled space mission, <i>Apollo 13</i> pulls no punches: it's a masterfully told drama from director Ron Howard, bolstered by an ensemble of solid performances."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating to reviews from mainstream critics, gave the film an average score of 77 out of 100, based on 22 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> praised the film in his review saying: "A powerful story, one of the year's best films, told with great clarity and remarkable technical detail, and acted without pumped-up histrionics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Corliss" title="Richard Corliss">Richard Corliss</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> highly praised the film, saying: "From lift-off to splashdown, Apollo 13 gives one hell of a ride."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Edward Guthmann of <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> gave a mixed review and wrote: "I just wish that <i>Apollo 13</i> worked better as a movie, and that Howard's threshold for corn, mush and twinkly sentiment weren't so darn wide."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_(magazine)" title="Rolling Stone (magazine)">Rolling Stone</a></i> praised the film and wrote: "Howard lays off the manipulation to tell the true story of the near-fatal 1970 Apollo 13 mission in painstaking and lively detail. It's easily Howard's best film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Movie Room Reviews said "This film is arguably one of the most dramatic and horrendous spaceflight stories ever told."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Janet_Maslin" title="Janet Maslin">Janet Maslin</a> made the film an <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">NYT</a></i> Critics' Pick, calling it an "absolutely thrilling" film that "unfolds with perfect immediacy, drawing viewers into the nail-biting suspense of a spellbinding true story." According to Maslin, "like <i><a href="/wiki/Quiz_Show_(film)" title="Quiz Show (film)">Quiz Show</a></i>, <i>Apollo 13</i> beautifully evokes recent history in ways that resonate strongly today. Cleverly nostalgic in its visual style (<a href="/wiki/Rita_Ryack" title="Rita Ryack">Rita Ryack</a>'s costumes are especially right), it harks back to movie making without phony heroics and to the strong spirit of community that enveloped the astronauts and their families. Amazingly, this film manages to seem refreshingly honest while still conforming to the three-act dramatic format of a standard Hollywood hit. It is far and away the best thing Mr. Howard has done (and <i><a href="/wiki/Far_and_Away" title="Far and Away">Far and Away</a></i> was one of the other kind)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The academic critic Raymond Malewitz focuses on the DIY aspects of the "mailbox" filtration system to illustrate the emergence of an unlikely hero in late 20th-century American culture—"the creative, improvisational, but restrained thinker—who replaces the older prodigal cowboy heroes of American mythology and provides the country a better, more frugal example of an appropriate 'husband'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ron Howard stated that, after the first test preview of the film, one of the comment cards indicated "total disdain"; the audience member had written that it was a "typical Hollywood" ending and that the crew would never have survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Marilyn Lovell praised Quinlan's portrayal of her, stating she felt she could feel what Quinlan's character was going through, and remembered how she felt in her mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lost_moon_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-lost_moon-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 10th-anniversary <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> of the film was released in 2005; it included both the theatrical version and the IMAX version, along with several extras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDInfo_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVDInfo-31">[31]</a></sup> The IMAX version has a 1.66:1 <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(image)" title="Aspect ratio (image)">aspect ratio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, <i>Apollo 13</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">HD DVD</a> and on 13 April 2010 it was released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_disc" title="Blu-ray disc">Blu-ray disc</a> as the 15th-anniversary edition on the 40th anniversary of the Apollo 13 accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDInfo_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-DVDInfo-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_and_historical_accuracy">Technical and historical accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_13_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Technical and historical accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg/220px-Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg/330px-Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg/440px-Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Universal_Studios_Hollywood_2012_58.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Apollo 13 space capsule prop from the film</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The film depicts the crew hearing a bang quickly after Swigert followed directions from mission control to stir the oxygen and hydrogen tanks. In reality, the crew heard the bang 93 seconds later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film portrays the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> launch vehicle being rolled out to the launch pad two days before launch. In reality, the launch vehicle was rolled out on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_launch_platform" title="Mobile launch platform">mobile launch platform</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Crawler-transporter" title="Crawler-transporter">crawler-transporter</a> weeks before the launch date.</p>
<p>The movie depicts Swigert and Haise arguing about who was at fault. The show <i>The Real Story: Apollo 13</i> broadcast on the Smithsonian Channel includes Haise stating that no such argument took place and that there was no way anyone could have foreseen that stirring the tank would cause problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dialogue between ground control and the astronauts was taken nearly verbatim from transcripts and recordings, with the exception of one of the taglines of the film, "Houston, we have a problem." (This quote was voted #50 on the list "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Movie_Quotes" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Movie Quotes">AFI's 100 Years... 100 Movie Quotes</a>".) According to the mission transcript, the actual words uttered by Jack Swigert were "Hey, we've got a problem here" (talking over Haise, who had started "Okay, Houston"). Ground control responded by saying "This is Houston, say again please." Jim Lovell then repeated, "Houston, we've had a problem."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>One other incorrect dialogue is after the re-entry blackout. In the movie, Tom Hanks (as Lovell) says "Hello Houston... this is <i>Odyssey</i>... it's good to see you again." In the actual re-entry, the Command Module's transmission was finally acquired by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikorsky_SH-3D_Sea_King" title="Sikorsky SH-3D Sea King">Sikorsky SH-3D Sea King</a> recovery aircraft which then relayed communications to Mission Control. CAPCOM and fellow astronaut Joe Kerwin (not Mattingly, who serves as CAPCOM in this scene in the movie) then made a call to the spacecraft "Odyssey, Houston standing by. Over." Jack Swigert, not Lovell, replied "Okay, Joe," and unlike in the movie, this was well before the parachutes deployed; the celebrations depicted at Mission Control were triggered by visual confirmation of their deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tagline "Failure is not an option", stated in the film by Gene Kranz, also became very popular, but was not taken from the historical transcripts. The following story relates the origin of the phrase, from an e-mail by Apollo 13 <a href="/wiki/Flight_controller#Flight_Dynamics_Officer_.28FDO_or_FIDO.29" title="Flight controller">Flight Dynamics Officer</a> Jerry Bostick:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"As far as the expression 'Failure is not an option,' you are correct that Kranz never used that term. In preparation for the movie, the script writers, Al Reinart and Bill Broyles, came down to Clear Lake to interview me on 'What are the people in Mission Control really like?' One of their questions was 'Weren't there times when everybody, or at least a few people, just panicked?' My answer was 'No, when bad things happened, we just calmly laid out all the options, and failure was not one of them. We never panicked, and we never gave up on finding a solution.' I immediately sensed that Bill Broyles wanted to leave and assumed that he was bored with the interview. Only months later did I learn that when they got in their car to leave, he started screaming, 'That's it! That's the tag line for the whole movie, Failure is not an option. Now we just have to figure out who to have say it.' Of course, they gave it to the Kranz character, and the rest is history."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_commentary_(DVD)" title="Audio commentary (DVD)">DVD commentary track</a>, recorded by Jim and Marilyn Lovell and included with both DVD versions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDInfo_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-DVDInfo-31">[31]</a></sup> mentions several inaccuracies included in the film, all done for reasons of <a href="/wiki/Artistic_license" title="Artistic license">artistic license</a>:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:45%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"We were working and watching the controls during that time. Because we came in shallow, it took us longer coming through the atmosphere where we had ionization. And the other thing was that we were just slow in answering."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">—Jim Lovell, on the real reason for the delay in replying after Apollo 13's four-minute re-entry into Earth's atmosphere<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytlovell_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytlovell-40">[40]</a></sup></cite></div>
<ul>
<li>In the film, Mattingly plays a key role in solving a power consumption problem that Apollo 13 was faced with as it approached re-entry. Lovell points out in his commentary that Mattingly was a composite of several astronauts and engineers—including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Duke" title="Charles Duke">Charles Duke</a> (whose rubella led to Mattingly's grounding)—all of whom played a role in solving that problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>When Jack Swigert is getting ready to dock with the LM, a concerned NASA technician says: "If Swigert can't dock this thing, we don't have a mission." Lovell and Haise also seem worried. In his DVD commentary, the real Jim Lovell says that if Swigert had been unable to dock with the LM, he or Haise could have done it. He also says that Swigert was a well-trained Command Module Pilot and that no one was really worried about whether he was up to the job,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytlovell_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytlovell-40">[40]</a></sup> but he admitted that it made a nice subplot for the film. What the astronauts were really worried about, Lovell says, was the expected rendezvous between the Lunar Module and the Command Module after Lovell and Haise left the surface of the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>A scene set the night before the launch, showing the astronauts' family members saying their goodbyes while separated by a road, to reduce the possibility of any last-minute <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(medicine)" title="Transmission (medicine)">transmission</a> of disease, depicted a tradition not begun until the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The film depicts Marilyn Lovell dropping her wedding ring down a shower drain. According to Jim Lovell, this did occur,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytlovell_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytlovell-40">[40]</a></sup> but the drain trap caught the ring and his wife was able to retrieve it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-6">[6]</a></sup> Lovell has also confirmed that the scene in which his wife had a nightmare about him being "sucked through an open door of a spacecraft into outer space" also occurred, though he believes the nightmare was prompted by her seeing a scene in <i><a href="/wiki/Marooned_(film)" title="Marooned (film)">Marooned</a></i>, a 1969 film they saw three months before Apollo 13 blasted off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytlovell_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-nytlovell-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
