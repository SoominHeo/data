<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Apollonian gasket</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, an <b>Apollonian gasket</b> or <b>Apollonian net</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a> generated starting from a triple of circles, each tangent to the other two, and successively filling in more circles, each <a href="/wiki/Tangent" title="Tangent">tangent</a> to another three. It is named after <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga" title="Apollonius of Perga">Apollonius of Perga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollonian_gasket.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Apollonian_gasket.svg/220px-Apollonian_gasket.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Apollonian_gasket.svg/330px-Apollonian_gasket.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Apollonian_gasket.svg/440px-Apollonian_gasket.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollonian_gasket.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of an Apollonian gasket</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An Apollonian gasket can be constructed as follows. Start with three circles <i>C</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>C</i><sub>2</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>3</sub>, each one of which is tangent to the other two (in the general construction, these three circles can be any size, as long as they have common tangents). Apollonius discovered that there are two other non-intersecting circles, <i>C</i><sub>4</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>5</sub>, which have the property that they are tangent to all three of the original circles – these are called <i>Apollonian circles</i> (see <a href="/wiki/Descartes%27_theorem" title="Descartes' theorem">Descartes' theorem</a>). Adding the two Apollonian circles to the original three, we now have five circles.</p>
<p>Take one of the two Apollonian circles – say <i>C</i><sub>4</sub>. It is tangent to <i>C</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>2</sub>, so the triplet of circles <i>C</i><sub>4</sub>, <i>C</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>2</sub> has its own two Apollonian circles. We already know one of these – it is <i>C</i><sub>3</sub> – but the other is a new circle <i>C</i><sub>6</sub>.</p>
<p>In a similar way we can construct another new circle <i>C</i><sub>7</sub> that is tangent to <i>C</i><sub>4</sub>, <i>C</i><sub>2</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>3</sub>, and another circle <i>C</i><sub>8</sub> from <i>C</i><sub>4</sub>, <i>C</i><sub>3</sub> and <i>C</i><sub>1</sub>. This gives us 3 new circles. We can construct another three new circles from <i>C</i><sub>5</sub>, giving six new circles altogether. Together with the circles <i>C</i><sub>1</sub> to <i>C</i><sub>5</sub>, this gives a total of 11 circles.</p>
<p>Continuing the construction stage by stage in this way, we can add 2·3<sup><i>n</i></sup> new circles at stage <i>n</i>, giving a total of 3<sup><i>n</i>+1</sup> + 2 circles after <i>n</i> stages. In the limit, this set of circles is an Apollonian gasket.</p>
<p>The Apollonian gasket has a <a href="/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension" title="Hausdorff dimension">Hausdorff dimension</a> of about 1.3057.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Curvature">Curvature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Curvature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The curvature of a circle (bend) is defined to be the inverse of its radius.</p>
<ul>
<li>Negative curvature indicates that all other circles are internally tangent to that circle. This is bounding circle.</li>
<li>Zero curvature gives a line (circle with infinite radius).</li>
<li>Positive curvature indicates that all other circles are externally tangent to that circle. This circle is in the interior of circle with negative curvature.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollonian_spheres.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="492" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Apollonian_spheres.jpg/220px-Apollonian_spheres.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Apollonian_spheres.jpg/330px-Apollonian_spheres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Apollonian_spheres.jpg/440px-Apollonian_spheres.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollonian_spheres.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Apollonian sphere packing</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An Apollonian gasket can also be constructed by replacing one of the generating circles by a straight line, which can be regarded as a circle passing through the point at infinity.</p>
<p>Alternatively, two of the generating circles may be replaced by parallel straight lines, which can be regarded as being tangent to one another at infinity. In this construction, the circles that are tangent to one of the two straight lines form a family of <a href="/wiki/Ford_circle" title="Ford circle">Ford circles</a>.</p>
<p>The three-dimensional equivalent of the Apollonian gasket is the <a href="/wiki/Apollonian_sphere_packing" title="Apollonian sphere packing">Apollonian sphere packing</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symmetries">Symmetries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Symmetries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If two of the original generating circles have the same radius and the third circle has a radius that is two-thirds of this, then the Apollonian gasket has two lines of reflective symmetry; one line is the line joining the centres of the equal circles; the other is their mutual tangent, which passes through the centre of the third circle. These lines are perpendicular to one another, so the Apollonian gasket also has rotational symmetry of degree 2; the symmetry group of this gasket is <i>D</i><sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>If all three of the original generating circles have the same radius then the Apollonian gasket has three lines of reflective symmetry; these lines are the mutual tangents of each pair of circles. Each mutual tangent also passes through the centre of the third circle and the common centre of the first two Apollonian circles. These lines of symmetry are at angles of 60 degrees to one another, so the Apollonian gasket also has rotational symmetry of degree 3; the symmetry group of this gasket is <i>D</i><sub>3</sub>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Links_with_hyperbolic_geometry">Links with hyperbolic geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Links with hyperbolic geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three generating circles, and hence the entire construction, are determined by the location of the three points where they are tangent to one another. Since there is a <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation" title="Möbius transformation">Möbius transformation</a> which maps any three given points in the plane to any other three points, and since Möbius transformations preserve circles, then there is a Möbius transformation which maps any two Apollonian gaskets to one another.</p>
<p>Möbius transformations are also isometries of the <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry" title="Hyperbolic geometry">hyperbolic plane</a>, so in hyperbolic geometry all Apollonian gaskets are congruent. In a sense, there is therefore only one Apollonian gasket, up to (hyperbolic) isometry.</p>
<p>The Apollonian gasket is the limit set of a group of Möbius transformations known as a <a href="/wiki/Kleinian_group" title="Kleinian group">Kleinian group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symmetry_of_integral_Apollonian_circle_packings">Symmetry of integral Apollonian circle packings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Symmetry of integral Apollonian circle packings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="No_symmetry">No symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: No symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>If none of the curvatures are repeated within the first five, the gasket contains no symmetry, which is represented by symmetry group <i>C</i><sub>1</sub>; the gasket described by curvatures (−10, 18, 23, 27) is an example.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="D1_symmetry"><i>D</i><sub>1</sub> symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: D1 symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Whenever two of the largest five circles in the gasket have the same curvature, that gasket will have <i>D</i><sub>1</sub> symmetry, which corresponds to a reflection along a diameter of the bounding circle, with no rotational symmetry.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="D2_symmetry"><i>D</i><sub>2</sub> symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: D2 symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>If two different curvatures are repeated within the first five, the gasket will have D<sub>2</sub> symmetry; such a symmetry consists of two reflections (perpendicular to each other) along diameters of the bounding circle, with a two-fold rotational symmetry of 180°. The gasket described by curvatures (−1, 2, 2, 3) is the only Apollonian gasket (up to a scaling factor) to possess D<sub>2</sub> symmetry.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="D3_symmetry"><i>D</i><sub>3</sub> symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: D3 symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are no integer gaskets with <i>D</i><sub>3</sub> symmetry.</p>
<p>If the three circles with smallest positive curvature have the same curvature, the gasket will have <i>D</i><sub>3</sub> symmetry, which corresponds to three reflections along diameters of the bounding circle (spaced 120° apart), along with three-fold rotational symmetry of 120°. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequential_curvatures">Sequential curvatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonian_gasket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sequential curvatures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="360" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg/220px-ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg/330px-ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg/440px-ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ApollianGasketNested_2-20.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Nested Apollonian gaskets</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For any integer <i>n</i> &gt; 0, there exists an Apollonian gasket defined by the following curvatures:<br/>
(−<i>n</i>, <i>n</i> + 1, <i>n</i>(<i>n</i> + 1), <i>n</i>(<i>n</i> + 1) + 1).<br/>
For example, the gaskets defined by (−2, 3, 6, 7), (−3, 4, 12, 13), (−8, 9, 72, 73), and (−9, 10, 90, 91) all follow this pattern. Because every interior circle that is defined by <i>n</i> + 1 can become the bounding circle (defined by −<i>n</i>) in another gasket, these gaskets can be <a href="/wiki/Hereditarily_finite_set" title="Hereditarily finite set">nested</a>. This is demonstrated in the figure at right, which contains these sequential gaskets with <i>n</i> running from 2 through 20.</p>
