<p>The <b>Afghan rupee</b> was the currency of <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> until 1925. Before 1891, silver rupees circulated with copper <i>falus</i> and gold <i>mohur</i>. The three metals had no fixed <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a> between them, with different regions issuing their own coins.</p>
<p>In 1891, a new currency was introduced, based on the <a href="/wiki/Kabuli_rupee" title="Kabuli rupee">Kabuli rupee</a>. The rupee was subdivided into 60 <i>paisa</i>, each of 10 <i>dinar</i>. Other denominations issued included the <i>shahi</i> of 5 paisa, the <i>sanar</i> of 10 paisa, the <i>abbasi</i> of 20 paisa, the <i>qiran</i> of 30 paisa and the <i>tilla</i> and later the <i>amani</i>, both of 10 rupee. The rupee was replaced in 1925 by the <a href="/wiki/Afghan_afghani" title="Afghan afghani">Afghani</a>, which is the currency today.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rupee" title="Rupee">rupee</a> itself was first issued by Pashtun monarch <a href="/wiki/Sher_Shah_Suri" title="Sher Shah Suri">Sher Shah Suri</a> during his rule of northern India in the sixteenth century; <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> still uses its own variant of the rupee (along with <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> - <i>see</i> <a href="/wiki/Pakistani_rupee" title="Pakistani rupee">Pakistani rupee</a> - since its creation in 1947), whereas Afghanistan does not.</p>

<p>In 1919, Treasury notes were introduced in denominations of 1, 5, 10, 50 and 100 rupees.</p>
