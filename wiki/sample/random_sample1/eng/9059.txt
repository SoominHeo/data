<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Afrasiab painting</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Afrasiab painting</b>, also called the <b>Ambassadors' Painting</b>, is a rare example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdiana" title="Sogdiana">Sogdian</a> art. It was discovered in 1965 when the local authorities decided the construction of a road in the middle of <a href="/wiki/Afrasiyab_(Samarkand)" title="Afrasiyab (Samarkand)">Afrāsiāb</a> mound, the old site of pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol_empire" title="Mongol empire">Mongol</a> <a href="/wiki/Samarkand" title="Samarkand">Samarkand</a>. It is now preserved in a special museum on the Afrāsiāb mound.</p>
<p>The painting dates back to the middle of the 7th century CE. On the four walls of the room of a private house, three or four different countries neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> are depicted. On the northern wall <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (a Chinese festival, with the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">Empress</a> on a boat, and the Emperor hunting), on the Southern Wall Samarkand (i.e.; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Persia" title="Ancient Persia">Iranian</a> world: a religious funerary procession in honor of the ancestors during the <a href="/wiki/Nowruz" title="Nowruz">Nowruz</a> festival), on the eastern wall <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (as the land of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrologer" title="Astrologer">astrologers</a> and of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pygmies" title="Pygmies">pygmies</a>, but the painting is much destroyed there).</p>
<p>The topic on the main wall, the western wall facing the entrance is debated between specialists. <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkish</a> soldiers are escorting ambassadors coming from various countries of the world (<a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, China, Iranian principalities etc.). There are three main hypotheses. The leading expert on Sogdian painting, the excavator of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panjikent" title="Panjikent">Panjikent</a>, B. Marshak points out that Sogdian painting, gods are always depicted on the top of the main wall. However, as the Turks are guiding the embassies but are not themselves ambassadors, it has been suggested also that the Turkish <a href="/wiki/Khagan" title="Khagan">Qaghan</a>, then lord of <a href="/wiki/Inner_Asia" title="Inner Asia">inner</a> and central Asia, might be depicted there. A Chinese text is indeed saying that the idea of the "Four Lords of the World", here China, India, Iran and Turks, is depicted on the walls of palaces near Samarkand precisely during this period, and this would perfectly fit the four walls of this room. The last hypothesis makes use of an inscription mentioning the king of Samarkand to propose the idea that the ambassadors are presenting their gifts to him.</p>
<p>In early 2014, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> declared that it would finance the restoration of the Afrasiab painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
