In Christian or Islamic communities, religious beliefs are also sometimes characterized with <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretism</a> with the beliefs and practices of traditional religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpanafrican1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpanafrican1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comparative_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comparative-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="African_Traditional_Religion">African Traditional Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: African Traditional Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Traditional_Religion" title="African Traditional Religion">African Traditional Religion</a></div>

<p>Africa encompasses a wide variety of traditional beliefs. Although religious customs are sometimes shared by many local societies, they are usually unique to specific populations or geographic regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Dr J Omosade Awolalu, The "traditional" in this context means indigenous, that which is foundational, handed down from generation to generation, meant as to be upheld and practised today and forevermore. A heritage from the past, yet not treated as a thing of the past but that which connects the past with the present and the present with eternity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comparative_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Comparative-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Often spoken of in the terms of a singularity, deliberate; yet conscious of the fact that Africa is a large continent with multitudes of nations who have complex cultures, innumerable languages and myriad dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comparative_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Comparative-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The essence of this school of thought is based mainly on oral transmission; that which is written in people's hearts, minds, oral history, customs, temples and religious functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It has no founders or leaders like Gautama the Buddha, Jesus, Krishna, Ashoka, or Muhammed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has no missionaries or the intent to propagate or to proselytise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Some of the African traditional religions are those of the <a href="/wiki/Serer_religion" title="Serer religion">Serer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_religion" title="Yoruba religion">Yoruba</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igbo_religion" title="Igbo religion">Igbo</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Akan_religion" title="Akan religion">Akan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>. The religion of the <a href="/wiki/Gbe_languages" title="Gbe languages">Gbe peoples</a> (mostly the <a href="/wiki/Ewe_people" title="Ewe people">Ewe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fon_people" title="Fon people">Fon</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a> and Ghana is called <a href="/wiki/West_African_Vodun" title="West African Vodun">Vodun</a> and is the main source for similarly named religions in the <a href="/wiki/African_diaspora" title="African diaspora">diaspora</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Voodoo" title="Louisiana Voodoo">Louisiana Voodoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">Haitian Vodou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Vod%C3%BA" title="Cuban Vodú">Cuban Vodú</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Vud%C3%BA" title="Dominican Vudú">Dominican Vudú</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_Vodum" title="Brazilian Vodum">Brazilian Vodum</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abrahamic_religions">Abrahamic religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Abrahamic religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The majority of Africans are adherents of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. African people often combine the practice of their traditional belief with the practice of Abrahamic religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googlemix_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-googlemix-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-googlemix_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-googlemix-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Abrahamic religions are widespread throughout Africa. They have both spread and replaced indigenous African religions, but are often adapted to African cultural contexts and belief systems. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_World_Book_Encyclopedia" title="The World Book Encyclopedia">The World Book Encyclopedia</a> has estimated that in 2002 Christians formed 40% of the continent's population, with Muslims forming 45%. It was also estimated in 2002 that Christians form 45% of Africa's population, with Muslims forming 40.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD_Faith">Bahá'í Faith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bahá'í Faith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_in_Africa" title="Bahá'í Faith in Africa">Bahá'í Faith in Africa</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a> in Africa has a diverse history. It is the 3rd most widespread organized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religion" title="Abrahamic religion">Abrahamic religion</a> in Africa after <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calabar_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Calabar-14">[14]</a></sup> after its wide-scale growth in the 1950s and extend in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UofC_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-UofC-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Religion_Data_Archives" title="Association of Religion Data Archives">Association of Religion Data Archives</a> (relying on <a href="/wiki/World_Christian_Encyclopedia" title="World Christian Encyclopedia">World Christian Encyclopedia</a>) lists many large and smaller populations in Africa<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCE-05_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-WCE-05-16">[16]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> among the top ten numerical populations of Bahá'ís in the world in 2005 (each with over 200,000 adherents), and <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> in terms of percentage of the national population.</p>
<p>All three individual heads of the religion, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a>, <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="`Abdu'l-Bahá">`Abdu'l-Bahá</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shoghi_Effendi" title="Shoghi Effendi">Shoghi Effendi</a>, were in Africa at various times. More recently the roughly 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egypt-today_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-egypt-today-17">[17]</a></sup> Bahá'ís of Egypt have been embroiled in the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_identification_card_controversy" title="Egyptian identification card controversy">Egyptian identification card controversy</a> from 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eipr2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-eipr2-18">[18]</a></sup> through 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vict_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-vict-19">[19]</a></sup> Since then there have been homes burned down and families driven out of towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-out_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-out-20">[20]</a></sup> On the other hand, Sub-Saharan Bahá'ís were able to mobilize for nine regional conferences called for by the <a href="/wiki/Universal_House_of_Justice" title="Universal House of Justice">Universal House of Justice</a> 20 October 2008 to celebrate recent achievements in grassroots community-building and to plan their next steps in organizing in their home areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Christianity is now one of the most widely practiced religions in Africa along with Islam and is the largest religion in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>. Most adherents outside <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a> are Roman Catholic or Protestant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syncretistic" title="Syncretistic">syncretistic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messianism" title="Messianism">messianic</a> sects have formed throughout much of the continent, including the <a href="/wiki/Nazareth_Baptist_Church" title="Nazareth Baptist Church">Nazareth Baptist Church</a> in South Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Aladura" title="Aladura">Aladura</a> churches in Nigeria.There is also fairly widespread populations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventists" title="Seventh-day Adventists">Seventh-day Adventists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>. The oldest Christian denominations in Africa are the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic church in Egypt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eritrean_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a>, all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox" title="Oriental Orthodox">Oriental Orthodox</a>, which rose to prominence in the fourth century AD after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ezana" title="Ezana">King Ezana the Great</a> made Ethiopia one of the first <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christian nations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first few centuries of Christianity, Africa produced many figures who had a major influence outside the continent, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Augustine_of_Hippo" title="St Augustine of Hippo">St Augustine of Hippo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Maurice" title="St Maurice">St Maurice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">Origen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a>, and three Roman Catholic popes (<a href="/wiki/Pope_Victor_I" title="Pope Victor I">Victor I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Miltiades" title="Pope Miltiades">Miltiades</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gelasius_I" title="Gelasius I">Gelasius I</a>), as well as the Biblical characters <a href="/wiki/Simon_of_Cyrene" title="Simon of Cyrene">Simon of Cyrene</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_eunuch" title="Ethiopian eunuch">Ethiopian eunuch</a> baptised by <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Evangelist" title="Philip the Evangelist">Philip the Evangelist</a>. Christianity existed in Ethiopia before the rule of King Ezana the Great of the Kingdom of Axum, but the religion took a strong foothold when it was declared a state religion in 330 AD, becoming one of the first Christian nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The earliest and best known reference to the introduction of Christianity to Africa is mentioned in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Bible" title="Christian Bible">Christian Bible</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a>, and pertains to the evangelist Phillip's conversion of an <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopian</a> traveler in the 1st century AD. Although the Bible refers to them as Ethiopians, scholars have argued that Ethiopia was a common term encompassing the area South-Southeast of Egypt.</p>
<p>Other traditions have the convert as a Jew who was a steward in the Queen’s court.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> All accounts do agree on the fact that the traveler was a member of the royal court who successfully succeeded in converting the Queen, which in turn caused a church to be built. <a href="/wiki/Tyrannius_Rufinus" title="Tyrannius Rufinus">Tyrannius Rufinus</a>, a noted church historian, also recorded a personal account as do other church historians such as Socrates and Sozemius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Some experts predict the shift of Christianity's center from the European industrialized nations to Africa and Asia in modern times. <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Lamin_Sanneh" title="Lamin Sanneh">Lamin Sanneh</a> stated, that "African Christianity was not just an exotic, curious phenomenon in an obscure part of the world, but that African Christianity might be the shape of things to come."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The statistics from the World Christian Encyclopedia (David Barrett) illustrate the emerging trend of dramatic Christian growth on the continent and supposes, that in 2025 there will be 633 million Christians in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> is the other major religion in Africa alongside Christianity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclbrit_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclbrit-28">[28]</a></sup> with 47% of the population being Muslim, accounting for 1/4 of the world's Muslim population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The faith's historic roots on the continent stem from the time of the Prophet <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>, whose early disciples <a href="/wiki/Migration_to_Abyssinia" title="Migration to Abyssinia">migrated to Abyssinia</a> (hijira) in fear of persecution from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagan" title="Pagan">pagan</a> Arabs.</p>
<p>The spread of Islam in North Africa came with the <a href="/wiki/Military_conquests_of_Umar%27s_era" title="Military conquests of Umar's era">expansion of Arab empire</a> under Caliph <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Umar</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai Peninsula</a>. Spread of Islam in West Africa was through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> traders and sailors.</p>
<p>Islam is the dominant religion in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a>. It has also become the predominant religion on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swahili_Coast" title="Swahili Coast">Swahili Coast</a> as well as the West African seaboard and parts of the interior. There have been several Muslim empires in Western Africa which exerted considerable influence, notably the <a href="/wiki/Mali_Empire" title="Mali Empire">Mali Empire</a>, which flourished for several centuries and the <a href="/wiki/Songhai_Empire" title="Songhai Empire">Songhai Empire</a>, under the leadership of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mansa_Musa" title="Mansa Musa">Mansa Musa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonni_Ali" title="Sonni Ali">Sonni Ali</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Askia_Mohammed" title="Askia Mohammed">Askia Mohammed</a>.</p>
<p>The vast majority of <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> in Africa are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a>, belonging to either <a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shafi" title="Shafi">Shafi</a> schools of <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/Hanafi" title="Hanafi">Hanafi</a> school of <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a> is also represented, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> There are also sizeable minorities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahmadiyya" title="Ahmadiyya">Ahmadis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibadi" title="Ibadi">Ibadi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sufis" title="Sufis">Sufis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judaism_in_Africa" title="Judaism in Africa">Judaism in Africa</a></div>
<p>Adherents of Judaism can be found scattered in a number of countries across Africa; including <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Buddhism_in_Africa" title="Buddhism in Africa">Buddhism in Africa</a></div>
<p>Buddhism is a tiny religion in Africa with around 250,000 practicing adherents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and up to nearly 400,000 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> if combined with <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Folk_Religion" title="Chinese Folk Religion">Chinese Folk Religion</a> as a common traditional religion of mostly new <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Chinese migrants</a> (significant minority in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>). About half of African Buddhists are now living in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> has the highest Buddhist percentage in the continent, between 1.5%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> to 2%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> of the total population.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_religions">Other religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other faiths are practiced in Africa, including <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrianism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rastafarianism" title="Rastafarianism">Rastafarianism</a> among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreligion">Irreligion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Irreligion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irreligion_in_Africa" title="Irreligion in Africa">Irreligion in Africa</a></div>
<p>A Gallup poll shows that the irreligious comprise 20% in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, 16% in <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>, 13% in <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, 13% in <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a>, 12% in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire" title="Côte d'Ivoire">Côte d'Ivoire</a>, 10% in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, 9% in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, 8% in <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a> and 7% in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syncretism">Syncretism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Syncretism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">Syncretism</a> is the combining of different (often contradictory) beliefs, often while melding practices of various schools of thought. In the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">commonwealth of Africa</a> syncretism with indigenous beliefs is practiced throughout the region. It is believed by some to explain religious tolerance between different groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Kwesi Yankah and <a href="/wiki/John_Mbiti" title="John Mbiti">John Mbiti</a> argue that many African peoples today have a 'mixed' religious heritage to try to reconcile traditional religions with Abrahamic faiths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Mugambi" title="Jesse Mugambi">Jesse Mugambi</a> claims that the Christianity taught to Africans by missionaries had a fear of syncretism, which was carried on by current African Christian leadership in an attempt to keep Christianity "pure."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Syncretism in Africa is said by others to be overstated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and due to a misunderstanding of the abilities of local populations to form their own orthodoxies and also confusion over what is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_culture" title="African culture">culture</a> and what is religion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Others state that the term syncretism is a vague one,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> since it can be applied to refer to substitution or modification of the central elements of Christianity or Islam with beliefs or practices from somewhere else. The consequences under this definition, according to <a href="/wiki/Missiology" title="Missiology">missiologist</a> Keith Ferdinando, are a fatal compromise of the religion's integrity. However, communities in Africa (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic" title="Afro-Asiatic">Afro-Asiatic</a>) have many common practices which are also found in Abrahamic faiths, and thus these traditions do not fall under the category of some definitions of syncretism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-Encyclbrit-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Encyclbrit_28-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Encyclopædia Britannica. Britannica Book of the Year 2003. Encyclopædia Britannica, (2003) <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780852299562">ISBN 978-0-85229-956-2</a> p.306<br/>
According to the Encyclopædia Britannica, as of mid-2002, there were 480,453,000 Christians, 479,869,000 Muslims and 98,734,000 people who practiced traditional religions in Africa. <a class="external text" href="http://www.greenwoodsvillage.com/gor/islam.htm" rel="nofollow">Ian S. Markham, (A World Religions Reader. Cambridge, MA: Blackwell Publishers, 1996.)</a> is cited by Morehouse University as giving the mid-1990s figure of 278,250,800 Muslims in Africa, but still as 40.8% of the total. These numbers are estimates, and remain a matter of conjecture (see Amadu Jacky Kaba). The spread of Christianity and Islam in Africa: a survey and analysis of the numbers and percentages of Christians, Muslims and those who practice indigenous religions. The Western Journal of Black Studies, Vol 29, Number 2, June 2005. Discusses the estimations of various almanacs and encyclopedium, placing Britannica's estimate as the most agreed figure. Notes the figure presented at the <a class="external text" href="http://www.afrikaworld.net/afrel/Statistics.htm" rel="nofollow">World Christian Encyclopedia, summarized here</a>, as being an outlier. On rates of growth, Islam and Pentecostal Christianity are highest, see: <a class="external text" href="http://www.foreignpolicy.com/story/cms.php?story_id=3835" rel="nofollow">The List: The World’s Fastest-Growing Religions</a>, Foreign Policy, May 2007.</span></li>
