<p>The <b>African lungfishes</b> are the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Protopterus</b></i> and constitute the four <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lungfish" title="Lungfish">lungfish</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. <i>Protopterus</i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">sole genus</a> in the family <b>Protopteridae</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protopterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>African lungfishes are elongated, <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eel</a>-like fishes, with thread-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a>. They have soft scales, and the <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_anatomy#Types_of_fin" title="Fish anatomy">tail fins</a> are fused into a single structure. They can either swim like eels, or crawl along the bottom, using their pectoral and pelvic fins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-1">[1]</a></sup> The largest species reach about 200 cm (6.6 ft) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FB_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FB-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>African lungfishes generally inhabit shallow waters, such as swamps and marshes; however, they are also found in larger lakes such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a>. They can live out of water for many months in burrows of hardened mud beneath a dried-up stream bed. They are carnivorous, eating <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, aquatic insect larvae, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protopterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The African lungfish is an example of how the evolutionary transition from breathing water to breathing air can happen. Lungfish are periodically exposed to water with low oxygen content or situations in which their aquatic environment dries up. Their adaptation for dealing with these conditions is an outpocketing of the gut, related to the <a href="/wiki/Swim_bladder" title="Swim bladder">swim bladder</a> of other fishes, that serves as a lung.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-1">[1]</a></sup> The lung contains many thin-walled blood vessels, so blood flowing through those vessels can pick up oxygen from air gulped into the lung.</p>
<p>The African lungfishes are obligate air breathers, with reduced gills in the adults. They have two anterior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gill_arch" title="Gill arch">gill arches</a> that retain gills, though they are too small to function as the sole respiratory apparatus. The lungfish heart has adaptations that partially separate the flow of blood into its pulmonary and systemic circuits. The <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(heart)" title="Atrium (heart)">atrium</a> is partially divided, so that the left side receives oxygenated blood and the right side receives deoxygenated blood from the other tissues. These two blood streams remain mostly separate as they flow through the <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricle</a> leading to the gill arches. As a result, oxygenated blood mostly goes to the anterior gill arches and the deoxygenated blood mostly goes to the posterior arches.</p>
<p>African lungfishes breed at the beginning of the rainy season. They construct nests or burrows in the mud to hold their eggs, which they then guard against predators. When they hatch, the young resemble <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a>, with <a href="/wiki/External_gills" title="External gills">external gills</a>, and only later develop lungs and begin to breathe air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protopterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Native Africans have been found to dig up lungfishes, burrow and all, and store them for later use when they want fresh fish to eat. These fish have also been carried in their mud burrows for exhibition in the United States. They have a strong taste. The taste is such that "it is locally either highly appreciated or strongly disliked".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As technology advancements such as <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longlines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a> have been increasingly applied over the past 50 years, the lungfish populations there are believed to be decreasing. In Uganda, women do not eat the lungfish because they consider it a "sister fish", and therefore it is associated with men and manhood.</p>
