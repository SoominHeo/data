<p><b>Afriqiyah Airways</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">الخطوط الجوية الأفريقية</span>‎‎ <i>Al-Khuṭūṭ al-Jawwiyyah al-Afrīqiyyah</i>) is a state-owned <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Before the 17th of February 2011 revolution it operated domestic services between Tripoli and <a href="/wiki/Benghazi" title="Benghazi">Benghazi</a>, and international scheduled services to over 25 countries in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>; since the end of the war, it has been rebuilding its business. Afriqiyah Airways' main base is <a href="/wiki/Tripoli_International_Airport" title="Tripoli International Airport">Tripoli International Airport</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI-2">[2]</a></sup> and the airline is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Air_Carriers_Organization" title="Arab Air Carriers Organization">Arab Air Carriers Organization</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association" title="International Air Transport Association">International Air Transport Association</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment_and_growth:_2001-2011">Establishment and growth: 2001-2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment and growth: 2001-2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Afriqiyah Airways was established in April 2001 and commenced scheduled services on 1 December 2001. The name <i>Afriqiyah</i> comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> word for <i>African</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNNWireArrive_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNNWireArrive-3">[3]</a></sup> It is wholly owned by the Libyan government and has 287 employees (at March 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FI-2">[2]</a></sup> The airline started with <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737-400</a> aircraft, but in 2003, an all-<a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> fleet was introduced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Italian airline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Panorama" title="Blue Panorama">Blue Panorama</a> jointly set up the airline with the Libyan government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The airline generated US$120 million in revenue in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Afriqiyah Airways signed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorandum_of_Understanding" title="Memorandum of Understanding">Memorandum of Understanding</a> for the purchase of six <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A320s</a> and three <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A319s</a> plus an option on five, as well as for three <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330-200s</a>, with an option for three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The first A319 was delivered on September 8, 2008.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The new A320s and A319s entered service on Afriqiyah’s growing international network, covering routes from its base at Tripoli to seventeen destinations in North, West, and Central Africa and the Middle East, as well as to European destinations such as <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>. Afriqiyah’s A319s carry 124 passengers in a two-class configuration,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> while the A320 seats 144 in two class configurations (J16/Y128). The A330s serve the long-distance operations on routes to Southern Africa, Asia and Europe, and have a two-class configuration with 230 seats (J30/Y200). As of 2015 the airline no longer flies to some of these destinations anymore.</p>
<p>On 20 August 2009 an Air Afriqiyah aircraft (registration 5A-IAY) - the private aircraft of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonel_Gaddafi" title="Colonel Gaddafi">Colonel Gaddafi</a> - flew to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Airport" title="Glasgow Airport">Glasgow Airport</a> to collect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abelbasset_al-Megrahi" title="Abelbasset al-Megrahi">Abelbasset al-Megrahi</a> (who had been convicted of the 1988 Lockerbie bombing and released on compassionate grounds by the Cabinet Secretary for Justice in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>). He was flown directly from Glasgow to Tripoli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">A330s</a> that were delivered in 2009 were used to inaugurate new routes to <a href="/wiki/Dhaka" title="Dhaka">Dhaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAAHC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAAHC-9">[9]</a></sup> In the winter 2010, two new routes were added to the airline's network - <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nouakchott" title="Nouakchott">Nouakchott</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In mid-October 2010, Afriqiyah Airways and <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Airlines" title="Libyan Airlines">Libyan Airlines</a> (Libya's other state <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a>) were expected to merge into one airline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and, although postponed, the merger is still planned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suspended_operations:_2011">Suspended operations: 2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Suspended operations: 2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a consequence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Libyan_Civil_War" title="2011 Libyan Civil War">Libyan Civil War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya" title="2011 military intervention in Libya">resulting no-fly zone</a> over the country enforced by <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1973" title="United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973">United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973</a>, all flight operations by Afriqiyah Airways were terminated on 17 March 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Point 17 of the United Nations resolution specifically banned flights into members of the United Nations by aircraft registered in Libya. This was to have been rescinded when Afriqiyah Airways was officially 'unsanctioned' on 22 September 2011, when Libyan-registered aircraft should have been again permitted to enter EU airspace. This did not happen and up to 5 March 2013 however no such easing had been announced and Libyan-registered aircraft are still banned from Europe, even overflying through the airspace. The Tripoli - Istanbul route has to route further east, via over Alexandria, which adds an hour each way to the sector time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Afriqiyah Airways announced that they expected to resume flights between Tripoli and London by the end of the year, subject to the issue of the correct air transport and security permits, using A320 equipment. However, flights did not resume until 3 July 2012. In order to get round the EU ban, Afriqiyah has wet-leased an A320 (ER-AXP) from Air Moldova, which complies with the EU requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebuilding_post-war_services:_2012_onwards">Rebuilding post-war services: 2012 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rebuilding post-war services: 2012 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="484" data-file-width="874" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg/220px-Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg/330px-Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg/440px-Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Afriqiyah_airways_airplane.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Afriqiyah Airways <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A320-200" title="Airbus A320-200">Airbus A320-200</a> wearing the current livery</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After suffering badly during the war, Afriqiyah Airways expressed its renewed optimism for the future on 12 November 2012 when it increased its order for A350 aircraft, announcing a new firm order for four A350-900s, and converting its original order for six A350-800s into six of the larger A350-900 model, taking the total number of A350s on order to 10 A350-900s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Libya_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Libya-17">[17]</a></sup> Deliveries are scheduled to start in 2020, and the airline plans to deploy the aircraft on new routes to the United States, the Middle East and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Libya_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Libya-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 December 2012 the airline unveiled its new livery, which features a white fuselage and black tailfin adorned with three blue stripes, representing the neck markings of the Turtle Dove. This design will replace the former livery with the 9.9.99 logo on its tailfin.</p>
<p>The airline's hub, Tripoli International Airport, has been closed since 13 July 2014 and is still shut down as of October 2016. Afriqiyah Airways currently operates a small route network out of <a href="/wiki/Mitiga_International_Airport" title="Mitiga International Airport">Mitiga International Airport</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Afriqiyah Airways is a subsidiary of the Libyan African Aviation Holding Company (LAAHC), which itself is owned by the Libyan National Social Fund, the Libyan National Investment Company, the Libya-Africa Investment Fund and the Libyan Foreign Investment Company; the airline is ultimately owned by the Libyan government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Libya_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Libya-17">[17]</a></sup> LAAHC is also the holding company for <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Airlines" title="Libyan Airlines">Libyan Airlines</a>; although they currently have separate operations, a merger of the two carriers is progressing slowly, though earlier expectations of a union in the first half of 2013 appear to have repeatedly been put back,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Libya_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Libya-17">[17]</a></sup> and in June 2014 it was reported that the merger was "not currently being worked on".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branding">Branding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Branding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Afriqiyah_Logo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="101" data-file-width="381" height="58" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/Afriqiyah_Logo.png/220px-Afriqiyah_Logo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/Afriqiyah_Logo.png/330px-Afriqiyah_Logo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/b3/Afriqiyah_Logo.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Afriqiyah_Logo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The original logo is a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Sirte_Declaration" title="Sirte Declaration">Sirte Declaration</a></div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi" title="History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi">Gaddafi-era</a> 9.9.99 logo on the side of Afriqiyah's aircraft refers to the date of the <a href="/wiki/Sirte_Declaration" title="Sirte Declaration">Sirte Declaration</a>, signed on 9 September 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNNWireArrive_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNNWireArrive-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The declaration marked the formation of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>. On <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>'s orders, the date was placed on the fuselage of all of the aircraft when the airline was founded. Tom Little of the <i><a href="/wiki/Libya_Herald" title="Libya Herald">Libya Herald</a></i> said "Gaddafi saw the declaration as one of his proudest achievements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Littlenewlogo_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Littlenewlogo-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 the airline decided to use a new branding to replace the previous one with its Gaddafi overtones. Saeed Al-Barouni, the in-flight services and catering manager, created a new logo that was selected from sixty other candidates. Al-Barouni's new logo is based on the markings of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turtle_dove" title="Turtle dove">turtle dove</a>. The new branding was unveiled on 19 December 2012 at the Rixos Al Nasr hotel in <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Littlenewlogo_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Littlenewlogo-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destinations">Destinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Destinations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Afriqiyah_Airways_destinations" title="Afriqiyah Airways destinations">Afriqiyah Airways destinations</a></div>
<p>As of August 2016, Afriqiyah Airways operates a small network to destinations in Northern Africa as well as <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. Until the outbreak of the civil war, it used to serve several more destinations in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.</p>

<p>An Airbus A300, registration 5A-IAY was painted in the airline colours and operated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_jet" title="Private jet">private jet</a> for Libyan leader <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a> from 2003 to 2011 fitted with a luxurious VIP interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It was completely burned to the ground during the battle at Tripoli International Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afriqiyah_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="801" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg/220px-Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg/330px-Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg/440px-Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Afriqiyah_Airways_Airbus_A330.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
5A-ONG, the aircraft involved in the accident of Flight 771</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>On 12 May 2010, at 04:10 UTC (06:10 Tripoli time) <a href="/wiki/Afriqiyah_Airways_Flight_771" title="Afriqiyah Airways Flight 771">Flight 771</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A330-202" title="Airbus A330-202">Airbus A330-202</a>, flying from <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a> crashed on approach to Tripoli airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC8676758_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC8676758-32">[32]</a></sup> 11 crew members and 93 passengers were killed. The sole survivor was a nine-year-old Dutch boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The aircraft (serial number 1024) was delivered on 8 September 2009, thus being some eight months old at the time of the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The aircraft had logged approximately 1600 hours on 420 flights.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The weather at the time of the crash was officially recorded as cloudy, but with good visibility, and no fog or thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> However, the aircraft was making an approach to the easterly runway, which has no blind landing aids.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The sun was just on the horizon and a sandstorm had just passed, making the visibility into the sun decidedly murky. The westerly runway, away from the sun, had excellent visibility, however, and it is unclear why the pilot was directed to use the easterly runway in such marginal conditions following a long overnight flight.</li>
<li>On 25 August 2011, during crossfire in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tripoli_(2011)" title="Battle of Tripoli (2011)">Battle of Tripoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">Airbus A300B4-620</a> 5A-IAY was destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN250811_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASN250811-37">[37]</a></sup> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a>, 5A-ONK, sustained damage to the starboard wing root from a rocket-propelled grenade while it was parked on the ramp on <a href="/wiki/Tripoli_International_Airport" title="Tripoli International Airport">Tripoli International Airport</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 20 July 2014, two <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330</a> aircraft with registrations 5A-ONF and 5A-ONP were hit by rocket fired by one of the rival militias (Misratah and Zintan) fighting for control of the facility at <a href="/wiki/Tripoli_International_Airport" title="Tripoli International Airport">Tripoli International Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> 5A-ONF was damaged beyond repair and was written off; however, 5A-ONP suffered minor to moderate damage, and was stored for repair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 23 December 2016, <a href="/wiki/Afriqiyah_Airways_Flight_209" title="Afriqiyah Airways Flight 209">Flight 209</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebha_Airport" title="Sebha Airport">Sebha Airport</a> to Mitiga International Airport, operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a> 5A-ONB was hijacked and diverted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luqa_Airport" title="Luqa Airport">Luqa Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN231216_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASN231216-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
