<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Appiani family</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Appiani</b> (also <b>Appiano</b> or <b>d'Appiano</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_nobility" title="Italian nobility">Italian noble</a> family, originally from Al Piano or Appiano, a now disappeared <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toponym" title="Toponym">toponym</a> identified with the modern La Pieve in the <i>comune</i> of <a href="/wiki/Ponsacco" title="Ponsacco">Ponsacco</a>. They held the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Piombino" title="Principality of Piombino">principality of Piombino</a> from the early 15th century until 1628.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appiani_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known member is one Guarnito d'Appiano, a notary who lived between 1200 and 1255; his sono Jacopo (flourished at <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> c. 1230-1290) was also a notary, as well as his grandson Benvenuto, who became chief of the Pisane Corporation of Notaries. His grand-grandson Vanni, also a notary, becamen an Anziano ("Ancient", meaning consul) of Pisa and then Chancellor of the Senate of <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> in 1347, before he was beheahed at Pisa in May 1355. The latter's son Jacopo (c. 1322 - 1398) becanem Chancellor of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Pisa" title="Republic of Pisa">Republic of Pisa</a> and head of the political party of the Raspanti, associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Della_Gherardesca" title="Della Gherardesca">Della Gherardesca</a> family. In 1392, after assassinating Pietro Gambacorti and his sons, he became effective lord of Pisa for some a decade.</p>
<p>He was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Gherardo_Appiani" title="Gherardo Appiani">Gherardo</a> (c. 1370-1405) in 1398, who, one year later, ceded the seigniory of Pisa to <a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Visconti" title="Gian Galeazzo Visconti">Gian Galeazzo Visconti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">lord of Milan</a>; when the latter died, Pisa was sold to <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriele_Maria_Visconti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabriele Maria Visconti (page does not exist)">Gabriele Maria Visconti</a>, while the Appiani retained the lordship of <a href="/wiki/Piombino" title="Piombino">Piombino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suvereto" title="Suvereto">Suvereto</a>, Buriano, <a href="/wiki/Scarlino" title="Scarlino">Scarlino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vignale" title="Vignale">Vignale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Populonia" title="Populonia">Populonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pianosa" title="Pianosa">Pianosa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montecristo" title="Montecristo">Montecristo</a>. Gherardo was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Iacopo_II_Appiani" title="Iacopo II Appiani">Iacopo II</a> in 1404; at his death (1441), her mother and regent <a href="/wiki/Paola_Colonna" title="Paola Colonna">Paola Colonna</a> clashed against <a href="/wiki/Emanuele_Appiani" title="Emanuele Appiani">Emanuele Appiani</a> for Piombino. Paola Colonna died in 1445, and the rule went to <a href="/wiki/Caterina_Appiani" title="Caterina Appiani">Caterina</a>, Iacopo II's sister, although the effective rule was held by her husband <a href="/wiki/Rinaldo_Orsini" title="Rinaldo Orsini">Rinaldo Orsini</a>. Rinaldo died of plague in 1450 and Caterina in 1451, after which Emanuele was elected lord of Piombino by the population.</p>
<p>In 1509 <a href="/wiki/Iacopo_IV_Appiani" title="Iacopo IV Appiani">Iacopo IV</a> obtained the title of Prince of Piombino by <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">emperor Maximilian</a> of Habsburg. His son <a href="/wiki/Iacopo_V_Appiani" title="Iacopo V Appiani">Iacopo V</a> married two sisters of the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici_family" title="Medici family">Medici</a> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">popes Leo X</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_VII" title="Clement VII">Clement VII</a> and, after they had both died, a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo de' Medici</a>. However, his son <a href="/wiki/Iacopo_VI_Appiani" title="Iacopo VI Appiani">Iacopo VI</a> was ousted from Piombino in 1548 by emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>, who assigned his lands to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosimo_I_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo I de' Medici">Cosimo I, Duke of Tuscany</a>. Iacopo was restored in 1559, though three years later a popular revolt forced him to fight in the Tuscan navy leaving his son <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Appiani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alessandro Appiani (page does not exist)">Alessandro</a> as governor. Alessandro was able to obtain the imperial legitimation, but was killed by the populace after four years of reign.</p>

<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Iacopo_VII_Appiani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Iacopo VII Appiani (page does not exist)">Iacopo VII</a> succeeded him, obtaining from emperor <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolf II</a> the title of prince. After Iacopo died heirless, Piombino went shortly to a cousin, but the principality was actually held by <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Appiani" title="Isabella Appiani">Isabella Appiani</a> until 1628, when she was ousted; finally, in 1634 emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand II</a> assigned Piombino to <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Ludovisi" title="Niccolò Ludovisi">Niccolò Ludovisi</a>, who married a daughter of Isabella.</p>
