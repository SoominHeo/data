<p><b>Apiashal</b> had been an early <a href="/wiki/List_of_Assyrian_kings" title="List of Assyrian kings">monarch</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a></i> <i>c.</i> 2205 BCE — <i>c.</i> 2192 BCE) of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Period_of_Assyria" title="Early Period of Assyria">Early Period of Aššūrāyu</a></i> (<i>Assyria</i>) according to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assyrian_King_List" title="Assyrian King List">Assyrian King List</a></i> (<i>AKL</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glassner_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glassner-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruno_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruno-2">[2]</a></sup> He is listed within the section of the <i>AKL</i> as the last of whom, "<i>altogether seventeen kings, tent dwellers</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glassner_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glassner-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruno_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bruno-2">[2]</a></sup> This section shows marked similarities to the ancestors of the <i><a href="/wiki/First_Babylonian_Dynasty" title="First Babylonian Dynasty">First Babylonian Dynasty</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruno_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bruno-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>AKL</i> also states that <i>Apiashal</i> had been preceded by his father <i><a href="/wiki/Ushpia" title="Ushpia">Ushpia</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a></i> <i>c.</i> 2218 BCE — <i>c.</i> 2205 BCE.) Additionally, the <i>AKL</i> states that <i>Apiashal</i> had been succeeded by his son <i><a href="/wiki/Hale_(Assyrian_king)" title="Hale (Assyrian king)">Hale</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a></i> <i>c.</i> 2192 BCE — <i>c.</i> 2179 BCE.)</p>
<p><i>Apiashal</i> is also listed within a section of the <i>AKL</i> as the first out of the ten, "<i>kings whose fathers are known</i>.” This section (which in contrast to the rest of the list) had been written in reverse order—beginning with <a href="/wiki/Aminu_(Assyrian_king)" title="Aminu (Assyrian king)">Aminu</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a></i> <i>c.</i> 2088 BCE — <i>c.</i> 2075 BCE.) and ending with Apiashal, "<i>altogether ten kings who are ancestors</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glassner_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Glassner-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruno_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bruno-2">[2]</a></sup>—has often been interpreted as the list of ancestors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorite" title="Amorite">Amorite</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Shamshi-Adad_I" title="Shamshi-Adad I">Šamši-Adad I</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruno_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bruno-2">[2]</a></sup> (<i><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a></i> <i>c.</i> 1754 BCE — <i>c.</i> 1721 BCE) who had conquered the city-state of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C5%A1%C5%A1ur" title="Aššur">Aššur</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mieroop_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mieroop-3">[3]</a></sup> In keeping with this assumption, scholars have inferred that the original form of the Assyrian King List had been written (among other things) as an, “<i>attempt to justify that Šamši-Adad I was a legitimate ruler of the city-state Aššur and to obscure his non-Assyrian antecedents by incorporating his ancestors into a native Assyrian genealogy</i>.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruno_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bruno-2">[2]</a></sup> However, this interpretation has not been accepted universally; the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_Ancient_History" title="Cambridge Ancient History">Cambridge Ancient History</a></i> rejected this interpretation and instead interpreted the section as being that of the ancestors of <i><a href="/wiki/Sulili" title="Sulili">Sulili</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a></i> <i>c.</i> 2075 BCE — <i>c.</i> 2062 BCE.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hildegard_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hildegard-4">[4]</a></sup> Very little is otherwise known of Apiashal's reign.</p>
