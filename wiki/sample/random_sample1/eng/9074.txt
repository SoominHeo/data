<p><i><b>The Devil Wears Prada</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/2003_in_literature" title="2003 in literature">2003</a>) is a best-selling <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Weisberger" title="Lauren Weisberger">Lauren Weisberger</a> about a young woman who is hired as a <a href="/wiki/Personal_assistant" title="Personal assistant">personal assistant</a> to a powerful <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a> <a href="/wiki/Magazine" title="Magazine">magazine</a> <a href="/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editor</a>, a job that becomes nightmarish as she struggles to keep up with her boss's grueling schedule and demeaning demands. It spent six months on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> bestseller list</a> and became the basis for the <a href="/wiki/The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(film)" title="The Devil Wears Prada (film)">2006 film of the same name</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Hathaway" title="Anne Hathaway">Anne Hathaway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emily_Blunt" title="Emily Blunt">Emily Blunt</a>. The novel is considered by many to be an example of the "<a href="/wiki/Chick_lit" title="Chick lit">chick lit</a>" genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_chick_lit_article_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_chick_lit_article-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick_lit_book_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chick_lit_book-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book's sequel, titled <i>Revenge Wears Prada: The Devil Returns</i>, was published on June 4, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andrea Sachs, a recent graduate of <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> with a degree in <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English</a>, moves to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> with her best friend, Lily, a graduate student at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia</a>. Andrea hopes to find a career in publishing and blankets the city with her <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9sum%C3%A9" title="Résumé">résumé</a>. She believes she'll be closer to her dream of working for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> if she can get a job in the magazine industry. She gets a surprise interview at the Elias-Clark Group and is hired as junior assistant for Miranda Priestly, editor-in-chief of the fashion magazine <i>Runway</i>. Although she knows little of the fashion world, everyone tells her that "a million girls would die for [her] job". If she manages to work for Miranda for a year, people tell her, she can have her choice of jobs within the magazine industry.</p>
<p>At a celebrity party, Andrea meets Christian Collinsworth, a charismatic <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> graduate who is considered one of the hot, new up-and-coming writers of their generation. They are attracted to each other, which complicates her relationship with her boyfriend, Alex.</p>
<p>Andrea's relationships become entangled because of her new job. Lily increasingly turns to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcohol</a> and picks up dubious men to relieve the pressure of graduate school. Alex, struggling with his own demanding job as an inner-city schoolteacher, grows frustrated with Andrea's long hours and constant stress. Andrea's relationship with her family also suffers. Matters finally come to a head when her co-worker, Emily, gets <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mononucleosis" title="Mononucleosis">mononucleosis</a> and Andrea must travel to Paris with Miranda in her stead. In Paris, she has a surprise encounter with Christian. Later that night, Miranda finally lets down her guard and asks Andrea what she has learned, and where she wants to work afterwards. She promises to place phone calls to people she knows at the <i>New Yorker</i> on Andrea's behalf once her year is up and suggests she take on some small writing assignments at <i>Runway</i>.</p>
<p>Back at the hotel, Andrea gets urgent calls from Alex and her parents asking her to call them. She does and learns that Lily is <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">comatose</a> after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drunk_driving_(USA)" title="Drunk driving (USA)">driving drunk</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_accident" title="Car accident">wrecking a car</a>. Though her family and Alex pressure her to return home, she tells Miranda she will honor her commitment to <i>Runway</i>. Miranda is pleased, and says her future in magazine publishing is bright, but phones with another impossible demand at <a href="/wiki/Christian_Dior" title="Christian Dior">Christian Dior</a>'s Paris fashion show. Andrea decides that her family and friends are more important than her job, and realizes to her horror that she is becoming more and more like Miranda. She refuses to comply with Miranda's latest outrageous request, and when Miranda scolds her publicly, Andrea replies, "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Fuck_you" title="wikt:Fuck you">Fuck you</a>, Miranda. <i>Fuck you</i>." She is fired on the spot, and returns home to reconnect with friends and family. Her romantic relationship with Alex is beyond repair, but they remain friends. Lily recovers and is lucky to receive only <a href="/wiki/Community_service" title="Community service">community service</a> for her DUI charge.</p>
<p>In the last chapter Andrea learns her dispute with Miranda made her a minor <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a> when the incident made "<i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post#Highlights" title="New York Post">Page Six</a></i>". Afraid she has been blacklisted from publishing for good, she moves back with her parents. She works on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_fiction" title="Short fiction">short fiction</a> and finances her unemployment with profits made from reselling the designer clothing she was provided for her Paris trip. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventeen_(magazine)" title="Seventeen (magazine)">Seventeen</a></i> buys one of her stories. At the novel's end, she returns to the Elias-Clark building to discuss a position at one of the company's other magazines and sees Miranda's new junior assistant, who looks as harried and put-upon as she once did.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Andrea "Andy" Sachs</b>, a recent <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown</a> graduate hired as junior personal assistant to a powerful and tyrannical fashion magazine editor.</li>
<li><b>Miranda Priestly</b>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a>-born (as Miriam Princhek) editor-in-chief of <i>Runway</i>, an influential fashion magazine published by the Elias-Clark company. She is known for wearing a white <a href="/wiki/Herm%C3%A8s" title="Hermès">Hermès</a> <a href="/wiki/Scarf" title="Scarf">scarf</a> somewhere on her person every day, and treats her subordinates in a manner that borders on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emotional_abuse" title="Emotional abuse">emotional</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_abuse" title="Psychological abuse">psychological abuse</a>.</li>
<li><b>Emily Charlton</b>, Miranda's former junior assistant, now her senior assistant. She and Andrea have a conflicted relationship.</li>
<li><b>Alex Fineman</b>, Andrea's boyfriend, who teaches at an elementary school in the South <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronx" title="Bronx">Bronx</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teach_for_America" title="Teach for America">Teach for America</a>.</li>
<li><b>Lily Goodwin</b>, a free-spirited graduate student in <a href="/wiki/Russian_literature" title="Russian literature">Russian literature</a> at Columbia with curly black hair. She is Andrea's roommate.</li>
<li><b>Nigel</b>, a very tall <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a> British man who serves as <i>Runway's</i> creative director. He often appears on television as a fashion consultant and is one of the few stars of the magazine Andrea knows before she works there. He is a loud speaker with an outrageous sense of style, and the only person who can get away with critiquing Miranda's personal wardrobe choices.</li>
<li><b>James</b>, another gay man at <i>Runway</i> who works at the beauty department. He befriends Andrea, and jokes about "calling in fat" on days when he feels unattractive.</li>
<li><b>Jeffy</b>, who oversees <i>Runway's</i> famous "Closet." The Closet is stocked with clothing on loan from fashion designers for use in shoots, but is rarely returned and often "borrowed" by magazine staff. He is responsible for transforming Andrea's wardrobe so she can fit in among the fashionable hallways of <i>Runway</i> offices.</li>
<li><b>Hunter Tomlinson</b>, a prominent New York tax attorney who is Miranda's current husband (she is divorced from the father of her two daughters, a well-known British rock star). As nice to Andrea and Emily as his wife is cruel, he is referred to by other close associates of Miranda's as "B-DAD" behind his back, for Blind Deaf and Dumb—the only way they could imagine anyone being able to live with her.</li>
<li><b>Eduardo</b>, a security guard at the Elias-Clark building, who playfully makes Andrea or anyone else unfortunate enough to work as one of Miranda's personal assistants sing or put on some sort of act before he lets them enter the building.</li>
<li><b>Christian Collinsworth</b>, a handsome young writer whom Andrea meets at a party. They develop a mutual attraction.</li>
<li><b>Caroline</b> and <b>Cassidy</b>, the twin daughters Miranda dotes on.</li>
<li><b>Cara</b>, the girls' <a href="/wiki/Nanny" title="Nanny">nanny</a>, who saves Andrea's skin more than once but is eventually fired by Miranda after she gives the twins a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_time-out" title="Child time-out">timeout</a> in their bedroom for a bad attitude.</li>
<li><b>Jill</b>, Andrea's older sister, who is married and lives in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a>, where she has begun to affect a <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern accent</a>, much to Andrea's displeasure.</li>
<li>The <b>Clackers</b>, the magazine's many female editorial staffers, mainly <b>Allison</b> (former senior assistant, now beauty editor), <b>Lucia</b> (fashion department), <b>Jocelyn</b> (editorial), and <b>Stef</b> (accessories). Andrea gives them their nickname for the sound their <a href="/wiki/Stiletto_heel" title="Stiletto heel">stiletto heel</a> shoes make on the marble floors of the Elias-Clark building.</li>
<li><b>Benjamin</b>, referred to as Benji. He is Lily's ex-boyfriend, but they have stayed in touch despite their breakup. He was involved in the car accident with Lily.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception">Conception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Weisberger stated in publicity materials that Priestly's demands are partial fiction and a composite of actual experiences she and her friends had in their first jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some reviewers state that <a href="/wiki/Anna_Wintour" title="Anna Wintour">Anna Wintour</a>, editor-in-chief of <i><a href="/wiki/Vogue_(magazine)" title="Vogue (magazine)">Vogue</a></i>, was the inspiration for Priestly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postreview_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Postreview-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_and_critical_reception">Commercial and critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial and critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kate_Betts" title="Kate Betts">Kate Betts</a>, a former editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Bazaar" title="Harper's Bazaar">Harper's Bazaar</a></i> who also worked for Wintour at one point in her career, spared no barb in the <i>Times <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Book_Review" title="New York Times Book Review">Book Review</a></i>, stressing the main character's ingratitude at the unique opportunity of working at <i>Vogue</i>: "[I]f Andrea doesn't ever realize why she should care about Miranda Priestly, why should we care about Andrea, or prize the text for anything more than the cheap frisson of the context?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Betts_DWP_review_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Betts_DWP_review-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Janet_Maslin" title="Janet Maslin">Janet Maslin</a>, in the daily paper, joined in: "a mean-spirited <i>Gotcha!</i> of a book, one that offers little indication that the author could interestingly sustain a gossip-free narrative ...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maslin avoided naming either the magazine where Weisberger actually worked or the woman she allegedly modeled her main character on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <i>Times</i> continued this practice when the film was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critics who favored the book admitted it had problems, as any first novel might, but praised it as a "fun, frivolous read".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>No <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cond%C3%A9_Nast_Publications" title="Condé Nast Publications">Condé Nast Publications</a> reviewed or otherwise mentioned <i>The Devil Wears Prada</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptation">Film adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(film)" title="The Devil Wears Prada (film)">The Devil Wears Prada (film)</a></div>
<p>The film version was released on June 30, 2006 by <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>. It was produced by <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Finerman" title="Wendy Finerman">Wendy Finerman</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Forrest_Gump" title="Forrest Gump">Forrest Gump</a></i>), freely adapted for the screen by <a href="/wiki/Aline_Brosh_McKenna" title="Aline Brosh McKenna">Aline Brosh McKenna</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/David_Frankel" title="David Frankel">David Frankel</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anne_Hathaway" title="Anne Hathaway">Anne Hathaway</a> played Andrea, <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a> earned critical praise, a win for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a> nomination as Miranda, and <a href="/wiki/Emily_Blunt" title="Emily Blunt">Emily Blunt</a> played Emily.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_production" title="Film production">Production</a> took place during fall 2005, on location in New York and Paris. Weisberger herself made a very brief non-speaking <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearance</a> as the twins' <a href="/wiki/Nanny" title="Nanny">nanny</a>.</p>
<p>It was very successful, taking in over $300 million worldwide, making it the highest-grossing film for both lead actresses up to that date. In September, Weisberger and Frankel jointly accepted the first-ever <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quill_Awards" title="Quill Awards">Quill</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> Blockbuster Book to Film Award.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel">Sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book's sequel, <i>Revenge Wears Prada: The Devil Returns</i>, is set a decade after the events of the first novel. In it, Andy is the editor for a new bridal magazine. But as she plans her own wedding, she remains haunted by her experience with Miranda until the two meet again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-March_2013_EW_story_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-March_2013_EW_story-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kate Erbland from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MSN.com" title="MSN.com">MSN.com</a> called the story disconcerting, saying, "Readers who have warm and fuzzy memories of indefatigable Andy might not be so inclined to pick it up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSN_sequel_story_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSN_sequel_story-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical">Musical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil_Wears_Prada_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Musical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2015, it was reported that <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_producer" title="Theatrical producer">producer</a> <a href="/wiki/Kevin_McCollum" title="Kevin McCollum">Kevin McCollum</a> (who has previously produced the musicals <a href="/wiki/Rent_(musical)" title="Rent (musical)">Rent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avenue_Q" title="Avenue Q">Avenue Q</a> amongst others) had signed a deal two years earlier with Fox to develop some of the movies from its back catalog into <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musicals</a> for the stage. He expressed particular interest in <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs._Doubtfire" title="Mrs. Doubtfire">Mrs. Doubtfire</a></i> and <i>The Devil Wears Prada</i>. On 26th January 2017, it was officially announced by McCollum that in partnership with Fox Stage Productions and Rocket Entertainment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> a musical version of The Devil Wears Prada (influenced by both the movie and the book) would be produced. <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Sir Elton John</a> (who also wrote the scores for <a href="/wiki/Billy_Elliot_the_Musical" title="Billy Elliot the Musical">Billy Elliot</a> and the <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_King_(musical)" title="The Lion King (musical)">The Lion King</a> amongst others) was announced as the composer for the project and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rudnick" title="Paul Rudnick">Paul Rudnick</a> (most notable for writing the movie <a href="/wiki/Sister_Act" title="Sister Act">Sister Act</a>) would write the lyrics and story. The casting and production schedule is still to be announced but it is aimed to play on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
