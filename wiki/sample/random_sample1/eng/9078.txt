<p><i><b>Action française</b></i> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[aksjɔ̃ fʁɑ̃sɛz]</a></span>, <b>AF</b>; English: French Action) is a French far right political movement. The name was also given to a journal associated with the movement.</p>
<p>The movement and the journal were founded by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Pujo" title="Maurice Pujo">Maurice Pujo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Vaugeois" title="Henri Vaugeois">Henri Vaugeois</a> in 1899, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalist" title="Nationalist">nationalist</a> reaction against the intervention of left-wing intellectuals on the behalf of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Dreyfus" title="Alfred Dreyfus">Alfred Dreyfus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurras" title="Charles Maurras">Charles Maurras</a> quickly joined <i>Action française</i> and became its principal ideologist. Under the influence of Maurras, <i>Action française</i> became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarchist" title="Monarchist">monarchist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counter-revolutionary" title="Counter-revolutionary">counter-revolutionary</a> (objecting to the legacy of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>) and anti-democratic, and supported <a href="/wiki/Integralism" title="Integralism">Integralism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>.</p>
<p>Shortly after it was created, <i>Action française</i> tried to influence public opinion by turning its journal to a daily newspaper and by setting up various organizations. By 1914, it had become the best structured and the most vital nationalist movement in France. In the inter-war period, the movement enjoyed prestige and influence, but its popularity gradually declined as a result of the rise of <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a> and of a rupture in the relations with the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, <i>Action française</i> supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vichy_Regime" title="Vichy Regime">Vichy Regime</a> and Marshal <a href="/wiki/Philippe_P%C3%A9tain" title="Philippe Pétain">Philippe Pétain</a>. After the fall of Vichy, its newspaper was banned and Maurras was sentenced to life imprisonment. The movement nevertheless continued to exist due to new publications and political movements. Although <i>Action française</i> is not a major force in the right as it used to be, its ideas have remained influential.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology">Ideology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ideology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ideology of <i>Action française</i> was dominated by the thought of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurras" title="Charles Maurras">Charles Maurras</a>, following his adherence and his conversion of the movement's founders to <a href="/wiki/Monarchism" title="Monarchism">monarchism</a>. The movement supported a restoration of the monarchy and, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1905_French_law_on_the_separation_of_Church_and_State" title="1905 French law on the separation of Church and State">1905 law on the separation of Church and State</a>, the restoration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholicism</a> as the state religion, even though Maurras was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agnostic" title="Agnostic">agnostic</a> himself. It should not be considered that the movement intended to restore real power to the king, merely to set him up as a rallying point in distinction to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Republic_of_France" title="Third Republic of France">Third Republic of France</a> which was considered corrupt and unworkable by many of its opponents, whom they hoped to come to their banner.</p>
<p>The movement advocated decentralization (a "<a href="/wiki/Federal_monarchy" title="Federal monarchy">federal monarchy</a>"), with the restoration of pre-<a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">Revolutionary</a> liberties to the ancient provinces of France (replaced during the Revolution by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partement_in_France" title="Département in France">departmental system</a>). It aimed to achieve a restoration by means of a <i><a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a></i>, probably involving a transitional authoritarian government.</p>
<p><i>Action française</i> was not focused on denouncing one social or political group as the conspiratorial source of ills befalling France. Different groups of the French far right had especial animus against either the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a> (French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestants</a>), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freemasons" title="Freemasons">Freemasons</a>. To these Maurras added unspecific foreigners residing in France, who had been outside French law under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancien_regime" title="Ancien regime">ancien regime</a>, and to whom he invented a slur name derived from ancient Greek history: <i><a href="/wiki/Metic" title="Metic">métèques</a></i>. These four groups of "internal foreigners" Maurras called <i>les quatre états confédérés</i> and were all considered to be part of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-French_sentiment" title="Anti-French sentiment">Anti-France</a>". Of course he was also opposed to <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>, and, after the 1917 <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a>, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communists" title="Communists">communists</a>, but antagonism against them did not have to be constructed or marshalled (although the Protestants and the Freemasons were traditional supporters of the Republic, pejoratively called <i>la gueuse</i> (the slut) by the AF, and were thus in general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_rise_.281898.E2.80.931914.29">Founding and rise (1898–1914)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding and rise (1898–1914)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1899, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Pujo" title="Maurice Pujo">Maurice Pujo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Vaugeois" title="Henri Vaugeois">Henri Vaugeois</a> left the nationalist mouvement <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligue_de_la_Patrie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Ligue de la Patrie française">Ligue de la Patrie française</a></i> and established a new movement, called <i>Action française</i>, and a new journal, <i>Revue de l'Action française</i>. This was their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalist" title="Nationalist">nationalist</a> reaction against the intervention of left-wing intellectuals on the behalf of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Dreyfus" title="Alfred Dreyfus">Alfred Dreyfus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurras" title="Charles Maurras">Charles Maurras</a> quickly joined <i>Action française</i> and became its principal ideologist. Under the influence of Maurras, <i>Action française</i> became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarchist" title="Monarchist">monarchist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counter-revolutionary" title="Counter-revolutionary">counter-revolutionary</a> (objecting to the legacy of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>) and anti-democratic, and supported <a href="/wiki/Integralism" title="Integralism">integralism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dreyfus_affair" title="Dreyfus affair">Dreyfus affair</a> gave some Catholics the impression that Catholicism is not compatible with democracy. Therefore, they regarded <i>Action française</i> as rampart of religion and the most fitting expression of the church doctrine regarding society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its early years, <i>Action française</i> tried to influence public opinion and to spread its ideas. For example, it created related organizations, such as student groups. <i>Ligue d'Action française</i> was created in 1905 to raise money for conferences, and <i>L'Institut d'Action française</i> was created in 1906 as an alternative institute for higher education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judaken_2005_1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Judaken_2005_1-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1908 the movement's periodical was turned to a daily newspaper, called simply <i>Action française</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Camelots_du_Roi" title="Camelots du Roi">Camelots du Roi</a>, the movement's youth wing, was created in the same year to sell the newspaper in the streets. Its members also served as a paramilitary wing, providing security for meetings and engaging in street violence with political opponents. The newspaper's literary quality and polemical vigor attracted readers and made Maurras and the movement significant figures in French politics. By 1914, <i>Action française</i> was the best structured and the most vital nationalist movement in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judaken_2005_1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Judaken_2005_1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_World_War_and_aftermath_.281914.E2.80.931926.29">First World War and aftermath (1914–1926)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First World War and aftermath (1914–1926)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, <i>Action française</i> supported the prime minister <a href="/wiki/Georges_Clemenceau" title="Georges Clemenceau">Georges Clemenceau</a> and the will to defeat the Germans. France's victory in the war, and the movement's anti-German intransigence on the peace terms resulted in a peak of success, prestige and influence in the inter-war period. For example, in 1917 it moved into new spacious offices on the rue Caumartin, near St. Lazare train station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judaken_2005_1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Judaken_2005_1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Action française</i> exploited the disquiet aroused on the right by the victory of the left-wing coalition in 1924 (<a href="/wiki/Cartel_des_Gauches" title="Cartel des Gauches">Cartel des gauches</a>) and the horror of communism, sending about thirty candidates to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_parliament" title="French parliament">French parliament</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judaken_2005_1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Judaken_2005_1-2">[2]</a></sup> Well-known writers endorsed the movement, which advertised itself as the thinking man's party. Literary reviews, especially <i>Revue universelle</i>, spread the message of <i>Action française</i>. The polemics of the review, its personal attacks on leaders, and its systematic exploitation of scandals and crises helped detach some of the intellectuals from their allegiance to the republic and democracy. This agitation culminated in the <a href="/wiki/6_February_1934_crisis" title="6 February 1934 crisis">6 February 1934 crisis</a>.</p>
<p>The successes shaped the ideology of <i>Action française</i>. It became more integrated into mainstream <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservatism</a>, stressing patriotism and Catholicism as opposed to monarchism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Papal_condemnation_and_decline">Papal condemnation and decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Papal condemnation and decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In spite of the movement's support for Roman Catholicism as a state religion and the fact that the vast majority of its members were practising Catholics (indeed, they included significant numbers of clergy), some Catholics regarded it with distrust.</p>
<p>Much of this was due to the influence of Maurras, an agnostic whose advocacy of Catholicism was due to his belief that it was a factor of social cohesion and stability and to its importance in French history. This rather utilitarian view of religion disturbed people who were often in agreement with many of his ideas. Its influence on young Catholics was also considered problematic. Thus, on 29 December 1926, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">Pope Pius XI</a> condemned <i>Action française</i>.</p>
<p>Several of Maurras's writings, including the newspaper were placed on the <i><a href="/wiki/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum" title="Index Librorum Prohibitorum">Index Librorum Prohibitorum</a></i> at the same time, on 9 January 1927, with <i>Action française</i> being the first newspaper ever placed on the Roman Catholic Church's list of banned books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This was a devastating blow to the movement. On 8 March 1927 AF members were prohibited from receiving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacraments" title="Sacraments">sacraments</a>. Many of its members left (two Catholics who were forced to look for a different path in politics and life were writers <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mauriac" title="François Mauriac">François Mauriac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bernanos" title="Georges Bernanos">Georges Bernanos</a>); and it entered a period of decline.</p>
<p>In 1939, following the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> and a revival of <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-communism</a> in the Catholic Church, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> decided to end the condemnation. Thereafter, <i>Action française</i> claimed that the condemnation had been declared for political purposes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interwar_revival">Interwar revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interwar revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the 1926 Papal condemnation, <i>Action française</i> remained popular during the interwar period, being one of the most important far right leagues, along with the <i><a href="/wiki/Croix-de-Feu" title="Croix-de-Feu">Croix-de-Feu</a></i> and others. As increasing numbers of people in France (as in Europe as a whole) turned to authoritarian political movements, many turned to <i>Action française</i>. It thus continued to recruit members from the new generations, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Brasillach" title="Robert Brasillach">Robert Brasillach</a> (who would become an infamous <a href="/wiki/Collaborationism" title="Collaborationism">collaborationist</a>), <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Maulnier" title="Thierry Maulnier">Thierry Maulnier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Rebatet" title="Lucien Rebatet">Lucien Rebatet</a>, etc. It was marginally represented for a time in the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies" title="Chamber of Deputies">Chamber of Deputies</a>, notably by Léon Daudet, elected in the right-wing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chambre_bleue_horizon" title="Chambre bleue horizon">Chambre bleue horizon</a></i> (1919–1924).</p>
<p>However, with the rise of fascism and the creation of seemingly fascist leagues, added to the 1926 Papal condemnation, the royalist movement was weakened by various dissidents: <a href="/wiki/Georges_Valois" title="Georges Valois">Georges Valois</a> would create the fascist <i><a href="/wiki/Faisceau" title="Faisceau">Faisceau</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Dimier" title="Louis Dimier">Louis Dimier</a> would break away, while other members (<a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Deloncle" title="Eugène Deloncle">Eugène Deloncle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Jeantet" title="Gabriel Jeantet">Gabriel Jeantet</a>, etc.) created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist" title="Terrorist">terrorist</a> <i><a href="/wiki/La_Cagoule" title="La Cagoule">La Cagoule</a></i> group.</p>
<p><i>Action française</i> spearheaded the <a href="/wiki/6_February_1934_crisis" title="6 February 1934 crisis">6 February 1934 crisis</a>, which led to the fall of the second <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartel_des_gauches" title="Cartel des gauches">Cartel des gauches</a></i> and to the replacement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical-Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Radical-Socialist Party (France)">Radical</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Daladier" title="Édouard Daladier">Édouard Daladier</a> by conservative <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Doumergue" title="Gaston Doumergue">Gaston Doumergue</a>. In foreign policy, Maurras and Bainville supported <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Laval" title="Pierre Laval">Pierre Laval</a>'s double alliance with <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Fascist Italy</a> and with the United Kingdom in the <a href="/wiki/Stresa_Front" title="Stresa Front">Stresa Front</a> (1935) on one side, and with the Soviet Union on the other side, against the common enemy <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>. The <i>Action française</i> greeted <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a>'s appearance with delight, and supported the self-proclaimed <i><a href="/wiki/Caudillo" title="Caudillo">Caudillo</a></i> during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> (1936–39). But the extra-parliamentary agitation brought by the various <a href="/wiki/Far-right_leagues" title="Far-right leagues">far-right leagues</a>, including the AF, led <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Laval" title="Pierre Laval">Pierre Laval</a>'s government to outlaw militias and paramilitary leagues, leading to the dissolution of the AF on 13 February 1936<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decour_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Decour-4">[4]</a></sup> — the others leagues were dissolved only in June 1936 by the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_(France)" title="Popular Front (France)">Popular Front</a>.</p>
<p>Marshal <a href="/wiki/Philippe_P%C3%A9tain" title="Philippe Pétain">Philippe Pétain</a>'s proclamation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vichy_regime" title="Vichy regime">Vichy regime</a> and of the <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9volution_nationale" title="Révolution nationale">Révolution nationale</a></i> after the failure of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">Battle of France</a> was acclaimed by Maurras as a "divine surprise", and he rallied the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collaborationist" title="Collaborationist">collaborationist</a> regime. Royalist members hoped that Pétain would restore the monarchy, and the headquarters of the movement were moved from Paris to <a href="/wiki/Vichy" title="Vichy">Vichy</a>. However, the AF members were split between supporting the counter-revolutionary regime and their <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>: after 1942, and in particular in 1943, some members, such as <a href="/wiki/Henri_d%27Astier_de_la_Vigerie" title="Henri d'Astier de la Vigerie">Henri d'Astier de la Vigerie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_B%C3%A9nouville" title="Pierre de Bénouville">Pierre Guillain de Bénouville</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_d%27Estienne_d%27Orves" title="Honoré d'Estienne d'Orves">Honoré d'Estienne d'Orves</a> joined the <a href="/wiki/French_Resistance" title="French Resistance">Resistance</a> or escaped to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_French_Forces" title="Free French Forces">Free French Forces</a>. Others actively collaborated, while Maurras supported the Vichy government, but theoretically opposed Pétain's collaboration with the Germans. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Liberation" title="French Liberation">Liberation</a>, he was condemned to life imprisonment in 1944, though he was reprieved in 1952. <i>Action française</i> was dissolved in 1944.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_World_War_II">Since World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Since World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Action française</i> reformed itself in 1947, under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Pujo" title="Maurice Pujo">Maurice Pujo</a>, who created the newspaper <i><span lang="fr">Aspects de la France</span></i> (AF) and the counter-revolutionary movement, "<span lang="fr">la Restauration Nationale</span>" ("National Restoration"). After the death of Maurras in 1952, two rival newspapers, <i><span lang="fr">Aspects de la France</span></i> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boutang" title="Pierre Boutang">Pierre Boutang</a>'s <i><span lang="fr">La Nation française</span></i> revived the Maurrassian legacy, until the demise of <i><span lang="fr">La Nation française</span></i> in 1967.</p>
<p>In 1971, a breakaway movement, the "<span lang="fr">Nouvelle Action française</span>" was formed by <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Renouvin" title="Bertrand Renouvin">Bertrand Renouvin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges-Paul_Wagner" title="Georges-Paul Wagner">Georges-Paul Wagner</a> and others. It subsequently became the <i><a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_Action_Royaliste" title="Nouvelle Action Royaliste">Nouvelle Action Royaliste</a></i> (NAR), which supported the Orleanist heir (although in his 1968 reprinting of his study on the three French right-wing families, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_R%C3%A9mond" title="René Rémond">René Rémond</a> still classified it in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legitimist" title="Legitimist">legitimist</a> movement because of its counter-revolutionary ideology). The movement called for the support of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a> in the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_1981" title="French presidential election, 1981">1981 presidential election</a>, instead of supporting <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">Jacques Chirac</a>'s "neo-Gaullism" movement (the <a href="/wiki/Gaullism" title="Gaullism">Gaullists</a> are classed by René Rémond as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonapartist" title="Bonapartist">Bonapartists</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Val%C3%A9ry_Giscard_d%27Estaing" title="Valéry Giscard d'Estaing">Valéry Giscard d'Estaing</a>'s "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orleanist" title="Orleanist">Orleanist</a>" movement (because of his support of <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a>).</p>
<p>In the beginning of the 1980s, various AF figures, such as <a href="/wiki/Georges-Paul_Wagner" title="Georges-Paul Wagner">Georges-Paul Wagner</a> or <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippe_Colombani_(journaliste)" title="fr:Philippe Colombani (journaliste)">Philippe Colombani (in French)</a> joined the ranks of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marie_Le_Pen" title="Jean-Marie Le Pen">Jean-Marie Le Pen</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(France)" title="National Front (France)">National Front</a> (FN). Until the 1999 breakaway of the <a href="/wiki/National_Republican_Movement" title="National Republican Movement">National Republican Movement</a> (MNR) led by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_M%C3%A9gret" title="Bruno Mégret">Bruno Mégret</a>, Jean-Marie Le Pen's success was partly explained by his unification of the various far right families (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditionalist_Catholics" title="Traditionalist Catholics">Traditionalist Catholics</a>, royalists, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neofascists" title="Neofascists">neofascists</a>, etc.) which share few ideals apart from a distrust of <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a> and a staunch <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-communism</a>.</p>
<p>The AF movement still exists as the monarchist and anti-<a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> "<span lang="fr">Centre royaliste d'Action française</span>" and publishes a magazine called <i><span lang="fr">Action française 2000</span></i>. Its leader was <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Pujo" title="Pierre Pujo">Pierre Pujo</a> (Maurice Pujo's son), who died in Paris on 10 November 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The student movement, called <i><span lang="fr">Action française étudiante</span></i>, has approximately 15 local delegations (in places such as Paris, Normandy, Rennes, Bordeaux, and <a href="/wiki/Forez" title="Forez">Forez</a>) and a newspaper, <i><span lang="fr">Insurrection</span></i>. Its President is Oliver Perceval.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judgment_of_political_scientists">Judgment of political scientists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Judgment of political scientists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_as_fascist">Classification as fascist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classification as fascist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1965, the German historian <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Nolte" title="Ernst Nolte">Ernst Nolte</a> claimed that <i>Action française</i> was fascist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He considered <i>Action française</i> to be the first fascist party.</p>
<p>Present-day scholars regard Nolte's view as extreme and archaic. For example, in 1999, the British historian Richard Thurlow<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> claimed that "his [Nolte's] linking of <i>Action française</i> to the fascist tradition was misleading".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Later, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_R%C3%A9mond" title="René Rémond">René Rémond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_G._Payne" title="Stanley G. Payne">Stanley G. Payne</a> described the differences between <i>Action française</i> and Italian fascism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.A9mond_2006_8-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Dr. Jonathan Judaken<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> classified <i>Action française</i> as an extreme right movement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Judaken_2005_1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Judaken_2005_1-2">[2]</a></sup> and did not refer to it as fascist.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_national_syndicalism_and_fascism">Influence on national syndicalism and fascism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influence on national syndicalism and fascism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the books <i>Neither Right Nor Left</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <i>The Birth of Fascist Ideology</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zeev_Sternhell" title="Zeev Sternhell">Zeev Sternhell</a> claimed that <i>Action française</i> influenced <a href="/wiki/National_syndicalism" title="National syndicalism">national syndicalism</a> and, consequently, <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a>. According to Sternhell, national syndicalism was formed by the combination between the integral nationalism of <i>Action française</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Syndicalism" title="Syndicalism">revolutionary syndicalism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Georges_Sorel" title="Georges Sorel">Georges Sorel</a>. National syndicalism spread to Italy, and was later a part of the doctrine of Italian fascist movement. In France, national syndicalism influenced the <a href="/wiki/Non-conformists_of_the_1930s" title="Non-conformists of the 1930s">non-conformists of the 1930s</a>. Based on the views of the non-conformists themselves, Sternhell argued that the non-conformists were actually a French form of fascism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ren.C3.A9_R.C3.A9mond.27s_classification">René Rémond's classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Fran%C3%A7aise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: René Rémond's classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although it supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orleanist" title="Orleanist">Orleanist</a> branch, according to historian <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_R%C3%A9mond" title="René Rémond">René Rémond</a>'s categorization of French <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">right-wing</a> families, it would be closer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legitimist" title="Legitimist">legitimist</a> branch, characterized by a complete rejection of all changes to France since the 1789 <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. According to Rémond supporters of the Orleanist branch tended to favour <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a>.</p>
