<p><b>Exophthalmos</b> (also called <b>exophthalmus</b>, <b>exophthalmia</b>, <b>proptosis</b>, or <b>exorbitism</b>) is a bulging of the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">eye</a> anteriorly out of the <a href="/wiki/Orbit_(anatomy)" title="Orbit (anatomy)">orbit</a>. Exophthalmos can be either bilateral (as is often seen in <a href="/wiki/Graves%27_disease" title="Graves' disease">Graves' disease</a>) or unilateral (as is often seen in an orbital tumor). Complete or partial dislocation from the orbit is also possible from trauma or swelling of surrounding tissue resulting from trauma.</p>
<p>In the case of Graves' disease, the displacement of the eye is due to abnormal connective tissue deposition in the orbit and <a href="/wiki/Extraocular_muscles" title="Extraocular muscles">extraocular muscles</a> which can be visualized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_axial_tomography" title="Computed axial tomography">CT</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">MRI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>If left untreated, exophthalmos can cause the eyelids to fail to close during sleep leading to <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">corneal</a> dryness and damage. Another possible complication would be a form of redness or irritation called "<a href="/wiki/Superior_limbic_keratoconjunctivitis" title="Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis">Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis</a>", where the area above the cornea becomes inflamed as a result of increased friction when blinking. The process that is causing the displacement of the eye may also compress the <a href="/wiki/Optic_nerve" title="Optic nerve">optic nerve</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmic_artery" title="Ophthalmic artery">ophthalmic artery</a>, leading to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exophthalmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Measurement of the degree of exophthalmos is performed using an <a href="/wiki/Exophthalmometer" title="Exophthalmometer">exophthalmometer</a>.</p>
<p>Most sources define exophthalmos/proptosis as a protrusion of the globe greater than 18 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term exophthalmos is often used when describing proptosis associated with <a href="/wiki/Graves%27_disease" title="Graves' disease">Graves' disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy">Anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exophthalmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proptosis is the anterior displacement of the eye from the orbit. Since the orbit is closed off posteriorly, medially and laterally, any enlargement of structures located within will cause the anterior displacement of the eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robbins_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robbins-3">[3]</a></sup> Swelling or enlargement of the lacrimal gland causes inferior medial and anterior dislocation of the eye. This is because the lacrimal glands are located superiorly and laterally in the orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robbins_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robbins-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exophthalmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Inflammatory/Infection:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Graves%27_ophthalmopathy" title="Graves' ophthalmopathy">Graves' ophthalmopathy</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Graves%27_disease" title="Graves' disease">Graves' disease</a>, usually causes bilateral proptosis.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Orbital_cellulitis" title="Orbital cellulitis">Orbital cellulitis</a> - often with unilateral proptosis, severe redness, and moderate to severe pain, <a href="/wiki/Sinusitis" title="Sinusitis">sinusitis</a> and an elevated white blood cell count.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecil_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cecil-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dacryoadenitis" title="Dacryoadenitis">Dacryoadenitis</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erdheim-Chester_Disease" title="Erdheim-Chester Disease">Erdheim-Chester Disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mucormycosis" title="Mucormycosis">Mucormycosis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Idiopathic_orbital_inflammatory_disease" title="Idiopathic orbital inflammatory disease">Orbital pseudotumor</a> - presents with acute, usually unilateral proptosis with severe pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cecil_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cecil-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_cerebral_edema" title="High altitude cerebral edema">High altitude cerebral edema</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Granulomatosis_with_polyangiitis" title="Granulomatosis with polyangiitis">Granulomatosis with polyangiitis</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Neoplastic:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">Leukemias</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Meningioma" title="Meningioma">Meningioma</a>, (of sphenoid wing)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nasopharyngeal_angiofibroma" title="Nasopharyngeal angiofibroma">Nasopharyngeal angiofibroma</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hand%E2%80%93Sch%C3%BCller%E2%80%93Christian_disease" title="Hand–Schüller–Christian disease">Hand–Schüller–Christian disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hemangioma" title="Hemangioma">Hemangioma</a>, cavernous</li>
</ul>
<p>Cystic:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dermoid_cyst" title="Dermoid cyst">Dermoid cyst</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Vascular:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carotid-cavernous_fistula" title="Carotid-cavernous fistula">Carotid-cavernous fistula</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aortic_insufficiency" title="Aortic insufficiency">Aortic insufficiency</a>: manifests as a pulsatile pseudoproptosis, described by British cardiothoracic surgeon, Hutan Ashrafian in 2006</li>
</ul>
<p>Others:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_fracture" title="Orbital fracture">Orbital fracture</a>: apex, floor, medial wall, zygomatic</li>
<li>Retrobulbar hemorrhage: trauma to the orbit can lead to bleeding behind the eye. The hemorrhage has nowhere to escape and the increased pressure pushes the eye out of the socket, leading to proptosis and can also cause blindness if not treated promptly.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cushing%27s_syndrome" title="Cushing's syndrome">Cushing's syndrome</a> (due to fat in the orbital cave)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pfeiffer_syndrome" title="Pfeiffer syndrome">Pfeiffer syndrome</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exophthalmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG/220px-Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG/330px-Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG/440px-Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Exophthalmos_in_pug.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Exophthalmos in a Pug</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Exophthalmos is commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>. It is seen in <a href="/wiki/Cephalic_index" title="Cephalic index">brachycephalic</a> (short-nosed) dog breeds because of the shallow <a href="/wiki/Orbit_(anatomy)" title="Orbit (anatomy)">orbit</a>. However, it can lead to <a href="/wiki/Keratitis" title="Keratitis">keratitis</a> secondary to exposure of the <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a>. Exophthalmos is commonly seen in the <a href="/wiki/Pug" title="Pug">Pug</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Terrier" title="Boston Terrier">Boston Terrier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pekingese" title="Pekingese">Pekingese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shih_Tzu" title="Shih Tzu">Shih Tzu</a>.</p>
<p>It is a common result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_trauma" title="Head trauma">head trauma</a> and pressure exerted on the front of the neck too hard in dogs. Most commonly it occurs in brachycephalic (short nosed) breeds. In cats, eye proptosis is uncommon and is often accompanied by facial fractures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 40% of proptosed eyes retain vision after being replaced in the <a href="/wiki/Orbit_(anatomy)" title="Orbit (anatomy)">orbit</a>, but in cats very few retain vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Replacement of the eye requires general <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a>. The eyelids are pulled outward, and the eye is gently pushed back into place. The eyelids are sewn together in a procedure known as <a href="/wiki/Tarsorrhaphy" title="Tarsorrhaphy">tarsorrhaphy</a> for about five days to keep the eye in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gelatt_1999_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gelatt_1999-7">[7]</a></sup> Replaced eyes have a higher rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keratoconjunctivitis_sicca" title="Keratoconjunctivitis sicca">keratoconjunctivitis sicca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keratitis" title="Keratitis">keratitis</a> and often require lifelong treatment. If the damage is severe, the eye is removed in a relatively simple <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Enucleation_of_the_eye" title="Enucleation of the eye">enucleation of the eye</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Prognosis" title="Prognosis">prognosis</a> for a replaced eye is determined by the extent of damage to the <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sclera" title="Sclera">sclera</a>, the presence or absence of a <a href="/wiki/Pupillary_light_reflex" title="Pupillary light reflex">pupillary light reflex</a>, and the presence of ruptured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscles_of_orbit" title="Muscles of orbit">rectus muscles</a>. The rectus muscles normally help hold the eye in place and direct eye movement. Rupture of more than two rectus muscles usually requires the eye to be removed, because significant blood vessel and nerve damage also usually occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gelatt_1999_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gelatt_1999-7">[7]</a></sup> Compared to brachycephalic breeds, dochilocephalic (long-nosed) breeds usually have more trauma to the eye and its surrounding structures, so the prognosis is worse .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
