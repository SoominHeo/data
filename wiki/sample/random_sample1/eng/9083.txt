<p>She was born in <a href="/wiki/Flamschen" title="Flamschen">Flamschen</a>, a farming community at <a href="/wiki/Coesfeld" title="Coesfeld">Coesfeld</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocese_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="Diocese of Münster">Diocese of Münster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a>, Germany, and died at age 49 in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BClmen" title="Dülmen">Dülmen</a>, where she had been a nun, and later became bedridden. Emmerich is notable for her visions on the life and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_of_Jesus_Christ" title="Passion of Jesus Christ">passion of Jesus Christ</a>, reputed to be revealed to her by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Blessed Virgin Mary</a> under <a href="/wiki/Religious_ecstasy" title="Religious ecstasy">religious ecstasy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During her bedridden years, a number of well-known figures were inspired to visit her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup> The poet <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Brentano" title="Clemens Brentano">Clemens Brentano</a> interviewed her at length and wrote two books based on his notes of her visions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahl_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stahl-3">[3]</a></sup> The authenticity of Brentano's writings has been questioned and critics have characterized the books as "conscious elaborations by a poet" and a "well-intentioned fraud" by Brentano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-America_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-America-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anvil_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anvil-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Emmerich was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beatified" title="Beatified">beatified</a> on 3 October 2004, by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup> However, the Vatican focused on her own personal piety rather than the religious writings associated to her by Clemens Brentano. Her documents of postulation towards canonization is handled by the <a href="/wiki/Priestly_Fraternity_of_St._Peter" title="Priestly Fraternity of St. Peter">Priestly Fraternity of St. Peter</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was born as <i>Anna Katharina</i> into a family of poor farmers and had nine brothers and sisters. From an early age, she had to help with the house and farm work. Her schooling was rather brief, but all those who knew her noticed that she felt drawn to prayer from an early age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup> At twelve, she started to work at a large farm in the vicinity for three years and later learned to be a seamstress and worked as such for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CathEnc_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CathEnc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>She applied for admission to various convents, but she was rejected because she could not afford a <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a>. Eventually, the <a href="/wiki/Poor_Clares" title="Poor Clares">Poor Clares</a> in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a> agreed to accept her, provided she would learn to play the organ. She went to the organist Söntgen in <a href="/wiki/Coesfeld" title="Coesfeld">Coesfeld</a> to study music and learn to play the organ, but she never got around to it because the poverty of the Söntgen family prompted her to work there to help them, and she sacrificed her small savings for that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CathEnc_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CathEnc-6">[6]</a></sup> Later, one of the Söntgen daughters entered the convent with her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_life">Religious life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Religious life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1802, at the age of 28, Anne Catherine and her friend Klara Söntgen finally managed to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustinian_nun" title="Augustinian nun">Augustinian nuns</a> at the convent of Agnetenberg in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BClmen" title="Dülmen">Dülmen</a>. The following year, Anne Catherine took her <a href="/wiki/Vow#Divine_vows" title="Vow">religious vows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup> In the convent, she became known for her strict observance of the order's rule; but, from the beginning to 1811, she was often quite ill and had to endure great pain. At times, her zeal and strict adherence to rules disturbed some of the more tepid sisters, who were puzzled by her weak health and religious ecstasies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CathEnc_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-CathEnc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bonaparte" title="Jérôme Bonaparte">Jérôme Bonaparte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">King of Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious_intolerance" title="Religious intolerance">suppressed the convent</a> in 1812, she found refuge in a widow's house.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stigmata">Stigmata</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stigmata">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emmerickhaus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1536" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Emmerickhaus.jpg/250px-Emmerickhaus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Emmerickhaus.jpg/375px-Emmerickhaus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Emmerickhaus.jpg/500px-Emmerickhaus.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emmerickhaus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Birthplace of Anne Catherine Emmerich in Coesfeld-Flamschen</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In early 1813, marks of the <a href="/wiki/Stigmata" title="Stigmata">stigmata</a> were reported on Emmerich's body. The parish priest called in two doctors to examine her. When word of the phenomenon spread three months later, he notified the vicar general. With the news causing considerable talk in the town, the ecclesiastical authorities conducted a lengthy investigation. Many doctors wished to examine the case, and although efforts were made to discourage the curious, there were visitors whose rank or status gained them entry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thurston_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-7">[7]</a></sup> During this time, the poet and romanticist Clement Bretano first visited.</p>
<p>At the end of 1818, the periodic bleeding of Emmerich's hands and feet had stopped and the wounds had closed. While many in the community viewed the stigmata as real, others considered Emmerich an impostor conspiring with her associates to perpetrate a fraud. In August 1819, the civil authorities intervened and moved Emmerich to a different house, where she was kept under observation for three weeks. The members of the commission could find no evidence of fraud and were divided in their opinions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thurston_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the cross on her breastbone had the unusual shape of a "Y", similar to a cross in the local church of Coesfeld, English priest <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Thurston" title="Herbert Thurston">Herbert Thurston</a> surmised that "the subjective impressions of the stigmatic exercise a preponderating influence upon the manifestations which appear exteriorly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thurston_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visions_and_inspirations">Visions and inspirations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Visions and inspirations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anne Catherine Emmerich said that as a child she had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vision_(religion)" title="Vision (religion)">visions</a> in which she talked with Jesus, saw the souls in <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">Purgatory</a> and witnessed the core of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" title="Holy Trinity">Holy Trinity</a> in the form of three concentric interpenetrating full spheres. The largest but dimmest of the spheres represented the Father core, the medium sphere the Son core and the smallest and brightest sphere the Holy Spirit core. Each sphere of omnipresent God is extended toward infinity beyond God's core placed in <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a>. The Brentano compilation tells that during an illness in Emmerich's childhood, she was visited by a child (suggested as being Jesus), who told her of plants she should ingest in order to heal; among these was <a href="/wiki/Morning_glory" title="Morning glory">morning glory</a>, a plant containing LSA, a weaker analog of LSD, which can induce visions of <a href="/wiki/Phosphene" title="Phosphene">phosphenes</a> and cause delusions or hallucinations.</p>
<p>Based on Emmerich's growing reputation, a number of figures who were influential in the renewal movement of the church early in the 19th century came to visit her, among them <a href="/wiki/Clemens_August_von_Droste-Vischering" title="Clemens August von Droste-Vischering">Clemens von Vischering</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Cologne" title="Archbishop of Cologne">Archbishop of Cologne</a>; <a href="/wiki/Johann_Michael_Sailer" title="Johann Michael Sailer">Johann Michael Sailer</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ratisbon" title="Bishop of Ratisbon">Bishop of Ratisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Heinrich_Overberg" title="Bernhard Heinrich Overberg">Bernhard Overberg</a> and authors <a href="/wiki/Luise_Hensel" title="Luise Hensel">Luise Hensel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Leopold_zu_Stolberg-Stolberg" title="Friedrich Leopold zu Stolberg-Stolberg">Friedrich Stolberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup> Clemens von Vischering, who was the vicar‑general at that time, called Emmerich "a special friend of God" in a letter he wrote to Stolberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clemens_Brentano.27s_visits">Clemens Brentano's visits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clemens Brentano's visits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg/220px-D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg/330px-D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg/440px-D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick_Sterbezimmer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The reconstruction of Emmerich's room with the original furniture, at the Holy Cross church in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BClmen" title="Dülmen">Dülmen</a>, Germany</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the time of Emmerich's second examination in 1819, Brentano visited her and she immediately recognized him. He claimed that she told him he was sent to help her fulfill God's command, to express in writing the revelations made to her. Brentano became one of Emmerich's many supporters at the time, believing her to be a "chosen Bride of Christ". Professor Andrew Weeks claims that Brentano's own <a href="/wiki/Complex_(psychology)" title="Complex (psychology)">personal complexes</a> were a factor in substituting Emmerich as a maternal figure in his own life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahl_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stahl-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1819 until Emmerich's death in 1824, Brentano filled many notebooks with accounts of her visions involving scenes from the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> and the life of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>. Because Emmerich only spoke the <a href="/wiki/Westphalian_language" title="Westphalian language">Westphalian dialect</a>, Brentano could not transcribe her words directly, and often could not even take notes in her presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corl_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corl-8">[8]</a></sup> Brentano would quickly write a set of notes based on what he remembered of the conversations he had with Emmerich in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">standard German</a> when he returned to his own apartment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corl_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Corl-8">[8]</a></sup> Brentano edited the notes later, years after the death of Emmerich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corl_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Corl-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>About ten years after Emmerich had recounted her visions, Brentano completed editing his records for publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corl_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Corl-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1833, he published his first volume, <i>The Dolorous Passion of Our Lord Jesus Christ According to the Meditations of Anne Catherine Emmerich</i>. Brentano then prepared <i>The Life of the Blessed Virgin Mary from the Visions of Anna Catherine Emmerich</i> for publication, but he died in 1842. The book was published posthumously in 1852 in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.</p>
<p>Catholic priest Father Karl Schmoger edited Brentano's manuscripts and from 1858 to 1880 published the three volumes of <i>The Life of Our Lord</i>. In 1881, a large illustrated edition followed. Schmoger also penned a biography of Anne Catherine Emmerich in two volumes that has been republished in English language editions.</p>
<p>The Vatican does not endorse the authenticity of the books written by Brentano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSFeb2004_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNSFeb2004-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSOct2004_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNSOct2004-10">[10]</a></sup> However, it views their general message as "an outstanding proclamation of the gospel in service to salvation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Other critics have been less sympathetic and have characterized the books Brentano produced from his notes as "conscious elaborations of an overwrought romantic poet".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stahl_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stahl-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brentano wrote that Emmerich said she believed that <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>'s son <a href="/wiki/Ham_(son_of_Noah)" title="Ham (son of Noah)">Ham</a> was the progenitor of "the black, idolatrous, stupid nations" of the world. The "Dolorous Passion" is claimed to reveal a "clear antisemitic strain throughout",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> with Brentano writing that Emmerich believed that "Jews ... strangled Christian children and <a href="/wiki/Blood_libel" title="Blood libel">used their blood</a> for all sorts of suspicious and diabolical practices"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_partial_fabrication_by_Brentano">Allegations of partial fabrication by Brentano</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Allegations of partial fabrication by Brentano">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg/220px-D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg/330px-D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg/440px-D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BClmen_Hl._Kreuz_Grab_Anna-Katharina_Emmerick.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The tomb of Anne Catherine at the Holy Cross church in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BClmen" title="Dülmen">Dülmen</a>, Germany</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the case for Anne Catherine's beatification was submitted to the Vatican in 1892, a number of experts in Germany began to compare and analyze Brentano's original notes from his personal library with the books he had written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-America_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-America-4">[4]</a></sup> The analysis revealed various apocryphal biblical sources, maps and travel guides among his papers, which could have been used to enhance Emmerich's narrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-America_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-America-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his 1923 theological thesis, German priest Winfried Hümpfner, who had compared Brentano's original notes to the published books, wrote that Brentano had fabricated much of the material he had attributed to Emmerich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anvil_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anvil-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winfrid_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-winfrid-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1928, the experts had come to the conclusion that only a small portion of Brentano's books could be safely attributed to Emmerich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-America_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-America-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anvil_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Anvil-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of the Emmerich's beatification in 2004, the Vatican position on the authenticity of the Brentano books was elucidated by Father Peter Gumpel, who was involved in the study of the issues for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Causes_of_the_Saints" title="Congregation for the Causes of the Saints">Congregation for the Causes of the Saints</a>: "It is absolutely not certain that she ever wrote this. There is a serious problem of authenticity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anvil_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Anvil-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSFeb2004_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNSFeb2004-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSOct2004_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNSOct2004-10">[10]</a></sup> According to Gumpel, the writings attributed to Emmerich were "absolutely discarded" by the Vatican as part of her beatification process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-America_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-America-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_burial">Death and burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death and burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anne Catherine began to grow ever weaker during the summer of 1823. She died on 9 February 1824 in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BClmen" title="Dülmen">Dülmen</a> and was buried in the graveyard outside the town, with a large number of people attending her funeral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup> Her grave was reopened twice in the weeks following the funeral, due to a rumor that her body had been stolen, but the coffin and the body were found to be intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vatican_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Vatican-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CathEnc_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-CathEnc-6">[6]</a></sup> In February 1975, Emmerich's remains were moved to the Holy Cross Church in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BClmen" title="Dülmen">Dülmen</a>, where they rest today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_the_Virgin_Mary">House of the Virgin Mary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: House of the Virgin Mary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg/220px-House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg/330px-House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg/440px-House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:House_of_the_Virgin_Mary.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Virgin_Mary" title="House of the Virgin Mary">House of the Virgin Mary</a></i> now a chapel in <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, Turkey</div>
</div>

<p>Neither Brentano nor Emmerich had ever been to <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, and indeed the city had not yet been excavated; but visions contained in <i>The Life of The Blessed Virgin Mary</i> were used during the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Virgin_Mary" title="House of the Virgin Mary">House of the Virgin Mary</a>, the Blessed Virgin's supposed home before her <a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption</a>, located on a hill near <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">Ephesus</a>, as described in the book <i>Mary's House</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1881, a French priest, the Abbé <a href="/wiki/Julien_Gouyet" title="Julien Gouyet">Julien Gouyet</a> used Emmerich's book to search for the house in Ephesus and found it based on the descriptions. He was not taken seriously at first, but sister <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_Mandat-Grancey" title="Marie de Mandat-Grancey">Marie de Mandat-Grancey</a> persisted until two other priests followed the same path and confirmed the finding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> has taken no official position on the authenticity of the location yet, but in 1896 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Pope Leo XIII</a> visited it and in 1951 <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> initially declared the house a Holy Place. <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a> later made the declaration permanent. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a> in 1967, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> in 1979 and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> in 2006 visited the house and treated it as a shrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beatification">Beatification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Beatification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The process of Anne Catherine's <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">beatification</a> was started in 1892 by the Bishop of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a>. However, in 1928 the Vatican suspended the process when it was suspected that Clemens Brentano had fabricated some of the material that appeared in the books he wrote, and had attributed to Ann Catherine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWEM_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-EWEM-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1973 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Causes_of_the_Saints" title="Congregation for the Causes of the Saints">Congregation for the Causes of the Saints</a> allowed the case for her beatification to be re-opened, provided it only focused on the issue of her life, without any reference to the possibly doctored material produced by Clemens Brentano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWEM_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-EWEM-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2003 the Congregation for the Causes of the Saints promulgated a decree of a miracle attributed to her, and that paved the way for her beatification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWEM_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-EWEM-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 October 2004 Anne Catherine Emmerich was beatified by Pope John Paul II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> However, the books produced by Brentano were set aside, and her cause adjudicated solely on the basis of her own personal sanctity and virtue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anvil_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Anvil-5">[5]</a></sup> Father Peter Gumpel who was involved in the analysis of the matter at the Vatican told Catholic News Service: "Since it was impossible to distinguish what derives from Sister Emmerich and what is embroidery or additions, we could not take these writings as a criteria. Therefore, they were simply discarded completely from all the work for the cause".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSFeb2004_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNSFeb2004-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNSOct2004_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNSOct2004-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinematic_portrayals">Cinematic portrayals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Catherine_Emmerich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cinematic portrayals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003 actor <a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a> brought Anne Catherine Emmerich's vision to prominence as he used her book <i>The Dolorous Passion</i> as a key source for his movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Passion_of_the_Christ" title="The Passion of the Christ">The Passion of the Christ</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corl_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Corl-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garcia_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Gibson stated that Scripture and "accepted visions" were the only sources he drew on, and a careful reading of Emmerich's book shows the film's high level of dependence on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corl_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Corl-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garcia_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia-22">[22]</a></sup> In his review of the movie in the Catholic publication <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/America_(Jesuit_magazine)" title="America (Jesuit magazine)">America</a></i>, Jesuit priest John O' Malley used the terms "devout fiction" and "well-intentioned fraud" to refer to the writings of Clemens Brentano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-America_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-America-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anvil_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Anvil-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 German director Dominik Graf made the movie <i>The Pledge</i> as a dramatization of the encounters between Anne Catherine (portrayed by actress <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanja_Schleiff" title="de:Tanja Schleiff">Tanja Schleiff</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Brentano" title="Clemens Brentano">Clemens Brentano</a>, based on a novel by Kai Meyer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
