<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anna Magnani</h1>

<p><b>Anna Magnani</b> (<small>Italian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[ˈanna maɲˈɲaːni]</a></span>; 7 March 1908 – 26 September 1973) was an Italian stage and film actress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WVobit_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WVobit-1">[1]</a></sup> She won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Best Actress</a>, along with four other international awards, for her portrayal of a Sicilian widow in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rose_Tattoo_(film)" title="The Rose Tattoo (film)">The Rose Tattoo</a></i>.</p>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-2">[2]</a></sup> she worked her way through Rome's Academy of Dramatic Art by singing at night clubs. During her career, her only child was stricken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">polio</a> when he was 18 months old and remained crippled.</p>
<p>She was referred to as "La Lupa," the "perennial toast of Rome" and a "living she-wolf symbol" of the cinema. <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine described her personality as "fiery", and drama critic <a href="/wiki/Harold_Clurman" title="Harold Clurman">Harold Clurman</a> said her acting was "volcanic". In the realm of Italian cinema she was "passionate, fearless, and exciting," an actress that film historian Barry Monush calls "the volcanic earth mother of all Italian cinema."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monush_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monush-3">[3]</a></sup> Director <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a> called her "the greatest acting genius since <a href="/wiki/Eleonora_Duse" title="Eleonora Duse">Eleonora Duse</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-2">[2]</a></sup> Playwright <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a> became an admirer of her acting and wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rose_Tattoo" title="The Rose Tattoo">The Rose Tattoo</a></i> specifically for her to star in, a role for which she received an Oscar in 1955.</p>
<p>After meeting director <a href="/wiki/Goffredo_Alessandrini" title="Goffredo Alessandrini">Goffredo Alessandrini</a> she received her first screen role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blind_Woman_of_Sorrento_(1934_film)" title="The Blind Woman of Sorrento (1934 film)">La cieca di Sorrento</a></i> (<i>The Blind Woman of Sorrento</i>) (1934) and later achieved international fame in Rossellini's <i><a href="/wiki/Rome,_Open_City" title="Rome, Open City">Rome, Open City</a></i> (1945), considered the first significant movie to launch the <a href="/wiki/Italian_neorealism" title="Italian neorealism">Italian neorealism</a> movement in cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monush_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Monush-3">[3]</a></sup> As an actress she became recognized for her dynamic and forceful portrayals of "earthy lower-class women"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in such films as <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Amore_(film)" title="L'Amore (film)">L'Amore</a></i> (1948), <i><a href="/wiki/Bellissima_(film)" title="Bellissima (film)">Bellissima</a></i> (1951), <i>The Rose Tattoo</i> (1955), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fugitive_Kind" title="The Fugitive Kind">The Fugitive Kind</a></i> (1959) and <i><a href="/wiki/Mamma_Roma" title="Mamma Roma">Mamma Roma</a></i> (1962). As early as 1950 <i><a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life</a></i> magazine had already stated that Magnani was "one of the most impressive actresses since <a href="/wiki/Greta_Garbo" title="Greta Garbo">Garbo</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AnnChristie39stage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="439" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e3/AnnChristie39stage.jpg/220px-AnnChristie39stage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e3/AnnChristie39stage.jpg/330px-AnnChristie39stage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e3/AnnChristie39stage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AnnChristie39stage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Acting on stage as <i>Anna Christie</i>, 1939<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Magnani's parentage and birthplace are uncertain. Some sources suggest she was born in Rome, other in Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Actors_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Actors-7">[7]</a></sup> Her mother was Marina Magnani.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-2">[2]</a></sup> The film director, <a href="/wiki/Franco_Zeffirelli" title="Franco Zeffirelli">Franco Zeffirelli</a>, who claimed to know Magnani well, states in his autobiography that she was born in Alexandria, Egypt, to an Italian-Jewish mother and Egyptian father, and that "only later did she become Roman when her grandmother brought her over and raised her in one of the Roman slum districts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeffirelli_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zeffirelli-8">[8]</a></sup> Magnani herself stated that her mother was married in Egypt but returned to Rome before giving birth to her at <a href="/wiki/Porta_Pia" title="Porta Pia">Porta Pia</a>; and did not know how the rumour of her Egyptian birth got started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> She was enrolled in a French convent school in Rome where she learned to speak French and play the piano. She also developed a passion for acting from watching the nuns stage their Christmas plays. This period of formal education lasted until the age of fourteen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was a "plain, frail child with a forlornness of spirit". Her grandparents compensated by pampering her with food and clothes. Yet while growing up, she is said to have felt more at ease around "more earthly" companions, often befriending the "toughest kid on the block".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> This trait carried over into her adult life when she proclaimed, "I hate respectability. Give me the life of the streets, of common people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>At age 17, she went on to study at the <a href="/wiki/Eleonora_Duse" title="Eleonora Duse">Eleonora Duse</a> Royal Academy of Dramatic Art in Rome for two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> To support herself, Magnani sang in <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">cabarets</a>; leading to her being dubbed "the Italian <a href="/wiki/%C3%89dith_Piaf" title="Édith Piaf">Édith Piaf</a>". However, an actor friend Micky Knox, writes that she "never studied acting formally" and started her career in Italian music halls singing traditional Roman Folk songs. "She was instinctive" he writes. "She had the ability to call up emotions at will, to move an audience, to convince them that life on the stage was as real and natural as life in their own kitchen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Stage</dt>
</dl>
<p>She was considered an "outstanding theatre actress" in <i><a href="/wiki/Anna_Christie" title="Anna Christie">Anna Christie</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Petrified_Forest" title="The Petrified Forest">The Petrified Forest</a></i> and had a successful career in variety shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Early film roles</dt>
</dl>
<p>In 1933 she was acting in experimental plays in Rome when she was discovered by Italian filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Goffredo_Alessandrini" title="Goffredo Alessandrini">Goffredo Alessandrini</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> He had been one of the first Italian filmmakers to make use of sound. The two married the same year, and he subsequently directed her in her first major film role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blind_Woman_of_Sorrento_(1934_film)" title="The Blind Woman of Sorrento (1934 film)">The Blind Woman of Sorrento</a></i> (<i>La Cieca di Sorrento</i>) in 1934. In 1941, Magnani starred in <i>Teresa Venerdì</i> (<i>Friday Theresa</i>) with the writer and director <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_De_Sica" title="Vittorio De Sica">Vittorio De Sica</a>. He called this Magnani's "first true film". In it she plays Loletta Prima, the girlfriend of De Sica’s character, Pietro Vignali. De Sica described Magnani's laugh as "loud, overwhelming, and tragic".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During her career Magnani worked alongside a number of the most pre-eminent directors and screenwriters; including <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pier_Paolo_Pasolini" title="Pier Paolo Pasolini">Pier Paolo Pasolini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luchino_Visconti" title="Luchino Visconti">Luchino Visconti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Renoir" title="Jean Renoir">Jean Renoir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lumet" title="Sidney Lumet">Sidney Lumet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rome.2C_Open_City_.281945.29"><i>Rome, Open City</i> (1945)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rome, Open City (1945)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Her film career had spread over almost 20 years before she gained international renown as Pina in <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a>'s neorealist milestone <i>Roma, città aperta</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Rome,_Open_City" title="Rome, Open City">Rome, Open City</a></i>, 1945). Her harrowing death scene remains one of cinema's most devastating moments. The film was about Italy's final days under German occupation during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> where Magnani gave a "brilliant performance" as a woman who dies fighting to protect her husband, an underground fighter against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">Nazis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Italy (and gradually elsewhere) she soon became established as a star, although she lacked the conventional beauty and glamour often associated with the term. Slightly plump and rather short in stature with a face framed by unkempt raven hair and eyes encircled by deep, dark shadows, she smouldered with seething earthiness and volcanic temperament. Rossellini, whom she called "this forceful, secure courageous man", was her lover at the time, and she collaborated with him on other films.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="L.27Amore:_The_Human_Voice_and_The_Miracle_.281948.29"><i>L'Amore: The Human Voice and The Miracle</i> (1948)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: L'Amore: The Human Voice and The Miracle (1948)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other collaborations with Rossellini include <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Amore_(film)" title="L'Amore (film)">L'Amore</a></i>, a two part film from 1948 which includes "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miracle_Decision" title="Miracle Decision">The Miracle</a>" and "The Human Voice" ("Il miracolo", and "Una voce umana"). In the former, Magnani, playing a peasant outcast who believes the baby she's carrying is Christ, plumbs both the sorrow and the righteousness of being alone in the world. The latter film, based on <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a>'s play about a woman desperately trying to salvage a relationship over the telephone, is remarkable for the ways in which Magnani's powerful moments of silence segue into cries of despair.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcano_.281950.29"><i>Volcano</i> (1950)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Volcano (1950)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After "The Miracle", Rossellini promised to direct Magnani in a film he was preparing which he told her would be "the crowning vehicle of her career". However, when the screenplay was completed, he instead gave the role for <i><a href="/wiki/Stromboli_(film)" title="Stromboli (film)">Stromboli</a></i> to the Swedish actress, <a href="/wiki/Ingrid_Bergman" title="Ingrid Bergman">Ingrid Bergman</a>. The two subsequently began a love affair; and this resulted in Magnani's permanent breakup with Rossellini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result, Magnani took on the starring role of <i><a href="/wiki/Volcano_(1950_film)" title="Volcano (1950 film)">Volcano</a></i>, which was said to have been deliberately produced to invite comparison:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:125</sup> both films were shot in similar locales of <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_Islands" title="Aeolian Islands">Aeolian Islands</a> only 40 kilometres apart; both actresses played independent-minded roles in a neorealist fashion; and both films were shot simultaneously. <i>Life</i> magazine wrote, "... in an atmosphere crackling with rivalry... Reporters were accredited, like war correspondents, to one or the other of the embattled camps.... Partisanship infected the <a href="/wiki/Via_Veneto" title="Via Veneto">Via Veneto</a> (boulevard in Rome), where Magnaniacs and Bergmaniacs clashed frequently." However, Magnani still considered Rossellini the "greatest director she ever acted for".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bellissima_.281951.29"><i>Bellissima</i> (1951)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bellissima (1951)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WithVisconti51.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="524" data-file-width="409" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e8/WithVisconti51.jpg/220px-WithVisconti51.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e8/WithVisconti51.jpg/330px-WithVisconti51.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e8/WithVisconti51.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WithVisconti51.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
With director Luchino Visconti on the terrace of <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Altieri" title="Palazzo Altieri">Palazzo Altieri</a> where Magnani lived in the fifties.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Luchino_Visconti" title="Luchino Visconti">Luchino Visconti</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bellissima_(1951_film)" title="Bellissima (1951 film)">Bellissima</a></i> (1951) she plays Maddalena, a blustery, obstinate stage mother who drags her daughter to <a href="/wiki/Cinecitt%C3%A0" title="Cinecittà">Cinecittà</a> for the 'Prettiest Girl in Rome' contest, with dreams that her plain daughter will be a star. Her emotions in the film went from those of rage and humiliation to maternal love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buford_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buford-14">[14]</a></sup> The film was made during the "grim period" of Italy's post-World War II recovery.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Golden_Coach_.281953.29"><i>The Golden Coach</i> (1953)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Golden Coach (1953)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magnani then went on to star as Camille (stage name: Columbine) in <a href="/wiki/Jean_Renoir" title="Jean Renoir">Jean Renoir</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Coach" title="The Golden Coach">Le Carrosse d'or</a></i> (also known as <i>The Golden Coach</i>) in 1953. Here she played a woman torn between three men - a soldier, a bullfighter, and a viceroy. Renoir called her "the greatest actress I have ever worked with".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Rose_Tattoo_.281955.29"><i>The Rose Tattoo (1955)</i></span><i><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Rose Tattoo (1955)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></i></h3>
<p>She played the widowed mother of a teenage daughter in <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Mann" title="Daniel Mann">Daniel Mann</a>'s 1955 film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rose_Tattoo_(film)" title="The Rose Tattoo (film)">The Rose Tattoo</a></i>, based on the play by <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>. It co-starred <a href="/wiki/Burt_Lancaster" title="Burt Lancaster">Burt Lancaster</a>, and was Magnani's first English speaking role in a mainstream Hollywood movie, winning her the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for Best Actress. Lancaster, who played the role of a "lusty truck driver", said that "if she had not found acting as an outlet for her enormous vitality, she would have become 'a great criminal'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buford_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buford-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Film historian John DiLeo has written that Magnani's acting in the film "displays why she is inarguably one of the half dozen greatest screen actresses of all time", and added:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Whenever Magnani laughs or cries (which is often), it's as if you've never seen anyone laugh or cry before: has laughter ever been so burstingly joyful or tears so shatteringly sad?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiLeo_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiLeo-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:275</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Tennessee Williams wrote the screenplay and based the character of Serafina on Magnani, as Williams was a great admirer of her acting abilities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monush_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Monush-3">[3]</a></sup> and he even stipulated that the movie "must star what <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_magazine" title="Time magazine">Time</a></i> described as 'the most explosive emotional actress of her generation, Anna Magnani."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buford_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Buford-14">[14]</a></sup> In his <i>Memoirs</i>, Williams described why he insisted on Magnani playing this role:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Anna Magnani was magnificent as Serafina in the movie version of <i>Tattoo</i>.... She was as unconventional a woman as I have known in or out of my professional world, and if you understand me at all, you must know that in this statement I am making my personal estimate of her honesty, which I feel was complete. She never exhibited any lack of self-assurance, any timidity in her relations with that society outside of whose conventions she quite publicly existed.... [s]he looked absolutely straight into the eyes of whomever she confronted and during that golden time in which we were dear friends, I never heard a false word from her mouth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It was originally staged on Broadway with <a href="/wiki/Maureen_Stapleton" title="Maureen Stapleton">Maureen Stapleton</a> as Magnani's English was too limited at the time for her to star. Magnani won other Best Actress awards for her role, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTA</a> Film Award, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globes" title="Golden Globes">Golden Globes</a> Award, <a href="/wiki/National_Board_of_Review" title="National Board of Review">National Board of Review</a>, USA, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Film_Critics_Circle_Awards" title="New York Film Critics Circle Awards">New York Film Critics Circle Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> When her name was announced as the Oscar winner, an American journalist called her in Rome to tell her the news; his challenge was convincing her he wasn't joking.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Fugitive_Kind_.281959.29"><i>The Fugitive Kind</i> (1959)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Fugitive Kind (1959)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magnani worked with <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a> again for the 1959 film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fugitive_Kind" title="The Fugitive Kind">The Fugitive Kind</a></i> (originally titled, <i>Orpheus Descending</i>) directed by <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lumet" title="Sidney Lumet">Sidney Lumet</a>, in which she played Lady Torrance and starred with <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a>. The original screenplay <i>Orpheus Descending</i> was another play inspired by Magnani, although she similarly did not feature in the Broadway play. In the film she played a woman "hardened by life's cruelties and a grief that will not fade."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiLeo_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-DiLeo-15">[15]</a></sup> It also co-starred a young <a href="/wiki/Joanne_Woodward" title="Joanne Woodward">Joanne Woodward</a> in one of her early roles. In an article he wrote for <i>Life</i> magazine Williams discussed why he chose her for the part:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Anna and I had both cherished the dream that her appearance in the part I created for her in <i>The Fugitive Kind</i> would be her greatest triumph to date... She is simply a rare being who seems to have about her a little lightning-shot cloud all her own.... In a crowded room she can sit perfectly motionless and silent and still you feel the atmospheric tension of her presence, its quiver and hum in the air like a live wire exposed, and a mood of Anna's is like the presence of royalty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><i>The Wild, Wild Women</i> (Nella Citta' L'Inferno, 1958) paired Magnani, as an unrepentant streetwalker, with <a href="/wiki/Giulietta_Masina" title="Giulietta Masina">Giulietta Masina</a> in a women-in-prison film.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mamma_Roma_.281962.29"><i>Mamma Roma</i> (1962)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mamma Roma (1962)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pier_Paolo_Pasolini" title="Pier Paolo Pasolini">Pier Paolo Pasolini</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mamma_Roma" title="Mamma Roma">Mamma Roma</a></i> (1962), Magnani is both the mother and the whore, playing an irrepressible prostitute determined to give her teenage son a respectable middle-class life. <i>Mamma Roma</i>, while one of Magnani's critically acclaimed films, was not released in the United States until 1995, deemed too controversial thirty years earlier. By now she was frustrated at being typecast in the roles of poor women. Magnani in 1963 commented "I’m bored stiff with these everlasting parts as a hysterical, loud, working-class woman".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Secret_of_Santa_Vittoria_.281969.29"><i>The Secret of Santa Vittoria</i> (1969)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Secret of Santa Vittoria (1969)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In one of her last film roles, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_Santa_Vittoria" title="The Secret of Santa Vittoria">The Secret of Santa Vittoria</a></i> (1969), she co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Quinn" title="Anthony Quinn">Anthony Quinn</a>, and they played husband and wife in what <i>Life</i> magazine called "perhaps the most memorable fight since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jimmy_Cagney" title="Jimmy Cagney">Jimmy Cagney</a> smashed <a href="/wiki/Mae_Clarke" title="Mae Clarke">Mae Clarke</a> in the face with a half a grapefruit." Magnani and Quinn did feud in private outside view of the cameras, however, and their animosity spilled over into their scenes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"By the time the movie makers were ready to shoot the fight scene, the stars were ready too. Magnani not only went for Quinn with the pasta and with a rolling pin, but with her foot; she kicked so hard she broke a bone in her right foot. She also bit him in the neck. 'That's not in the script', Quinn protested. Magnani snarled, 'I'm supposed to win this fight, remember?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fellini.27s_Roma_.281972.29"><i>Fellini's Roma</i> (1972)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fellini's Roma (1972)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>She later played herself (within a dramatic context) in <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Roma_(1972_film)" title="Roma (1972 film)">Roma</a></i> (1972). Towards the end of her career, Magnani was quoted as having said, "The day has gone when I deluded myself that making movies was art. Movies today are made up of…intellectuals who always make out that they’re teaching something".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_style">Acting style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Acting style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to film critic <a href="/wiki/Robin_Wood_(critic)" title="Robin Wood (critic)">Robin Wood</a> Magnani's "persona as a great actress is built, not on transformation, but on emotional authenticity... [she] doesn't portray characters but expresses 'genuine' emotions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Actors_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Actors-7">[7]</a></sup> Her style is notable by not displaying the more obvious attributes of the female star, with neither her face or physical makeup being considered "beautiful". However, she possesses a "remarkably expressive face," and for American audiences, at least, she represents "what Hollywood had consistently failed to produce: 'reality'". She was the atypical star, the "nonglamorous human being", as her genuine style of acting became a "rejection of glamour".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Actors_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Actors-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her most distinguished work in Hollywood is in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_is_the_Wind" title="Wild is the Wind">Wild is the Wind</a></i>, according to Wood. Directed by <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">George Cukor</a>, "the American cinema's greatest director of actresses," he was able to draw out the "individual essence" of Magnani's "sensitive and inward performance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Actors_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Actors-7">[7]</a></sup> Her other well-known Hollywood films were <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rose_Tattoo_(film)" title="The Rose Tattoo (film)">The Rose Tattoo</a></i>, which she won the best actress Oscar for in 1955, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fugitive_Kind" title="The Fugitive Kind">The Fugitive Kind</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s rule, Magnani was known to make rude jokes about the <a href="/wiki/National_Fascist_Party" title="National Fascist Party">Italian Fascist Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeffirelli_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zeffirelli-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WithSon_Life-50.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="289" data-file-width="277" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bb/WithSon_Life-50.jpg/220px-WithSon_Life-50.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/bb/WithSon_Life-50.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WithSon_Life-50.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<center>Visiting her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">polio</a>-stricken son at a sanatorium, circa 1947</center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>She married her first film director, <a href="/wiki/Goffredo_Alessandrini" title="Goffredo Alessandrini">Goffredo Alessandrini</a>, in 1935, two years after he discovered her on stage. After they married, she retired from full-time acting to "devote herself exclusively to her husband", although she continued to play smaller film parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> They separated in 1942.</p>
<p>Magnani had a love affair with the actor <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Serato" title="Massimo Serato">Massimo Serato</a>, by whom she had her only child, a son named Luca,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeffirelli_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zeffirelli-8">[8]</a></sup> who was born on October 29, 1942 in Rome, after her separation from Alessandrini. Magnani's life was struck by tragedy when Luca came down with crippling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">polio</a> at only 18 months of age. He never regained use of his legs. As a result, she spent most of her early earnings for specialists and hospitals. After once seeing a legless war veteran drag himself along the sidewalk, she said, "I realize now that it's worse when they grow up", and resolved to earn enough to "shield him forever from want".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1945 she fell in love with director <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a> while working on <i>Roma, Città Aperta</i> (AKA <i><a href="/wiki/Rome,_Open_City" title="Rome, Open City">Rome, Open City</a></i> (1945). "I thought at last I had found the ideal man... [He] had lost a son of his own and I felt we understood each other. Above all, we had the same artistic conceptions." Rossellini had become violent, volatile and possessive, and they argued constantly about films or out of jealousy. "In fits of rage they threw crockery at each other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> As artists, however, they complemented each other well while working on neorealist films. The two finally split apart when Rossellini fell in love with and married, <a href="/wiki/Ingrid_Bergman" title="Ingrid Bergman">Ingrid Bergman</a>.</p>
<p>Magnani was mystically inclined and consulted astrologers, as well as believing in numerology. She also claimed to be clairvoyant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> She ate and drank very little and could subsist for long periods on nothing more than black coffee and cigarettes. However, these habits often affected her sleep: "My nights are appalling," she said. "I wake up in a state of nerves and it takes me hours to get back in touch with reality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life50_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-Life50-5">[5]</a></sup> Perhaps her strangest quirk was her love of defleaing street kittens with her thumbnails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeffirelli_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zeffirelli-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Magnani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnani died at the age of 65 in Rome from <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic cancer</a> in 1973. Huge crowds gathered for the funeral. She was provisionally laid to rest in the family <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum" title="Mausoleum">mausoleum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a>; but then subsequently interred in the Cimitero Comunale of <a href="/wiki/San_Felice_Circeo" title="San Felice Circeo">San Felice Circeo</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Lazio" title="Lazio">Lazio</a>.</p>
