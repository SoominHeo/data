<p><i><b>The Fault in Our Stars</b></i> is a 2014 American <a href="/wiki/Romance_film" title="Romance film">romantic drama film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Josh_Boone_(director)" title="Josh Boone (director)">Josh Boone</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/The_Fault_in_Our_Stars" title="The Fault in Our Stars">novel of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_Green_(author)" title="John Green (author)">John Green</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Shailene_Woodley" title="Shailene Woodley">Shailene Woodley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Elgort" title="Ansel Elgort">Ansel Elgort</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nat_Wolff" title="Nat Wolff">Nat Wolff</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Laura_Dern" title="Laura Dern">Laura Dern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Trammell" title="Sam Trammell">Sam Trammell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Willem_Dafoe" title="Willem Dafoe">Willem Dafoe</a> playing supporting roles. Woodley plays Hazel Grace Lancaster, a sixteen-year-old cancer patient who is forced by her parents to attend a support group, where she meets and subsequently falls in love with Augustus Waters, another cancer patient, played by Elgort.</p>
<p>Development of <i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> began in January 2012 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_2000" title="Fox 2000">Fox 2000</a>, a division of <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>, optioned the rights to adapt the novel into a feature film. Principal photography began on August 26, 2013, in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, with a few additional days in Amsterdam, The Netherlands, before concluding on October 16, 2013. Pittsburgh doubled for all of the scenes set in Indianapolis, Indiana, the novel's setting, as well as for some interior scenes set in Amsterdam.</p>
<p><i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> was released in the United States on June 6, 2014, to positive critical reception, with praise for Woodley's performance as well as the script. The film was also a blockbuster, becoming number one at the box office during its opening weekend, and grossing over <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$307 million</span> worldwide against a budget of $12 million. It was released on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> on September 16, 2014, and grossed over <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$42 million</span> in total domestic video sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Numbers_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Numbers-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hazel Grace Lancaster is an intelligent and witty teenager living in Indianapolis, who has terminal <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_cancer" title="Thyroid cancer">thyroid cancer</a> that has spread to her lungs. Believing she is depressed, her mother Frannie urges her to attend a weekly cancer patient <a href="/wiki/Support_group" title="Support group">support group</a> to help her make friends with individuals who are going through the same thing. There Hazel meets Augustus Waters, a charming teenager who lost a leg from <a href="/wiki/Osteosarcoma" title="Osteosarcoma">bone cancer</a> but has since been cancer-free. He invites Hazel to his house where they bond over their hobbies and agree to read each other's favorite book. Hazel recommends <i>An Imperial Affliction</i>, a novel about a cancer-stricken girl named Anna that parallels her experience, and Augustus gives Hazel <i>Counter Insurgence</i>. They keep in touch via text over the weeks that follow and grow closer. After Augustus finishes the book, he expresses frustration with its abrupt ending (it ends in the middle of a sentence). Hazel explains that the novel's mysterious author, Peter Van Houten (<a href="/wiki/Willem_Dafoe" title="Willem Dafoe">Willem Dafoe</a>), retreated to Amsterdam following the novel's publication and has not been heard from since.</p>
<p>Weeks later, Augustus tells Hazel he has traced Van Houten's assistant, Lidewij, and has corresponded with Van Houten by email. She writes to him to find out more about the novel's ambiguous ending. Van Houten replies that he is only willing to answer her questions in person. Hazel asks her mother if she can travel to Amsterdam to visit him, but Frannie refuses because of financial and medical constraints. Augustus suggests that she use the "cancer wish" she received from <a href="/wiki/Make-A-Wish_Foundation" title="Make-A-Wish Foundation">Make-A-Wish Foundation</a> but Hazel explains that she has already used hers to visit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney_World" title="Disney World">Disney World</a>. Augustus and Hazel go on a picnic date and begin to fall in love. Augustus surprises Hazel with tickets to Amsterdam. After a medical setback, Hazel's doctors eventually agree to allow the trip, since they expect that she will soon become incapable of doing anything at all.</p>
<p>Hazel and Augustus arrive in Amsterdam and are presented with reservations at an expensive restaurant, pre-paid by Van Houten. During the meal, Augustus confesses his love for Hazel. The following afternoon, they go to Van Houten's house, but are shocked to find he is a mean-spirited alcoholic. Lidewij arranged the meeting and their dinner without Van Houten knowing anything about it. Angered by his assistant's actions, he taunts Hazel for seeking serious answers to a piece of fiction and belittles her medical condition. She leaves, utterly distraught. Lidewij invites them to go sightseeing to make up for their ruined experience. The three visit the <a href="/wiki/Anne_Frank_House" title="Anne Frank House">Anne Frank House</a>, where Hazel struggles to climb the house's many stairs. They spend that night together in their hotel and have sex for the first time. The next day Augustus tells Hazel that his cancer has returned and spread throughout his body and is terminal. Hazel is heartbroken, expressing how unfair life can be.</p>
<p>After their return to Indianapolis, Augustus' health worsens. He is taken to the ICU and realizes he is close to death. Augustus invites his blind best friend Isaac and Hazel to his pre-funeral, where they deliver eulogies that they have both prepared. Hazel tells him she would not trade their short time together for anything, since he "gave me a forever within the numbered days." Augustus dies eight days later and Hazel is astonished to find Van Houten at the funeral. He tells her that Augustus had demanded he attend his funeral to make up for the spoiled trip. Van Houten tells her that the novel is based on the experiences of his daughter Anna, who died from leukemia at a young age. He gives Hazel a piece of paper which she crumples up asking him to leave. Later, talking with Isaac, Hazel learns that Augustus had asked Van Houten to help him write a eulogy for her. She retrieves the crumpled paper and reads his words accepting his death and about his love for her. She lies on her back on her lawn looking up at the stars, smiling as she remembers Augustus and says: "Okay."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="An_Imperial_Affliction"><i>An Imperial Affliction</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: An Imperial Affliction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The book <i>An Imperial Affliction</i>, which features within both the original novel and the film, is fictitious, as is its author Peter Van Houten; both are used to enhance the themes and story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_the_novel_and_film">Differences between the novel and film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Differences between the novel and film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Lindsey Weber of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulture.com" title="Vulture.com">Vulture.com</a></i>, the differences between the book and the film include Hazel mentioning the band the <i>Hectic Glow</i> many times in the book, but not in the film. Gus is 17 in the book but 18 in the film. In the book, Gus has an ex-girlfriend, Caroline, who died from cancer which is not mentioned in the film. Hazel's former best friend Kaitlyn does not appear in the film. In the book, Hazel and Gus meet when she turns around to find him staring at her, while in the film they meet on the way to their support group. Gus dies more quickly in the film than in the book. In the book, Hazel searches for the letter, while in the film, Van Houten gives it to her. In the book, Hazel shows forgiveness towards van Houten after Gus's funeral, while in the film she demands that he leave. In the book, Hazel is a vegetarian but this is not referenced in the film. A scene in the film in which Gus comes in a limousine to pick up Hazel and her mother when they are leaving for Amsterdam, does not appear in the book. However, in the book, Gus is late when Hazel and her mother arrive at his house in a taxi because he appears to be fighting with his parents over his cancer relapse and the trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulture-Reasons_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-vulture-Reasons-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-production">Pre-production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pre-production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 31, 2012, it was announced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_2000" title="Fox 2000">Fox 2000</a>, a division of <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>, had optioned the rights to adapt <a href="/wiki/John_Green_(author)" title="John Green (author)">John Green</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fault_in_Our_Stars" title="The Fault in Our Stars">The Fault in Our Stars</a></i> for a feature film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deahl_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-deahl-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wyck_Godfrey" title="Wyck Godfrey">Wyck Godfrey</a> and Marty Bowen were due to produce the film with their production company, <a href="/wiki/Temple_Hill_Entertainment" title="Temple Hill Entertainment">Temple Hill Entertainment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Chbosky" title="Stephen Chbosky">Stephen Chbosky</a>, who directed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Perks_of_Being_a_Wallflower_(film)" title="The Perks of Being a Wallflower (film)">The Perks of Being a Wallflower</a></i> (also filmed in Pittsburgh), was in talks to direct the film but turned it down because of its similarity to <i>Perks</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On February 19, 2013, <a href="/wiki/Josh_Boone_(director)" title="Josh Boone (director)">Josh Boone</a> was hired as director;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Director_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Director-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Scott_Neustadter" title="Scott Neustadter">Scott Neustadter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_H._Weber" title="Michael H. Weber">Michael H. Weber</a> were hired to adapt the novel into a screenplay—their second adaptation for Fox, following <i><a href="/wiki/Rosaline" title="Rosaline">Rosaline</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Director_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Director-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 19, 2013, <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> announced that Shailene Woodley (who had recently been cast in, and was filming, <i><a href="/wiki/Divergent_(film)" title="Divergent (film)">Divergent</a></i>) would play Hazel Grace Lancaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casting1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-casting1-18">[18]</a></sup> Director Josh Boone said: "We read close to 150 actresses for the role, and I saw about 50 of those. Within ten or fifteen seconds of Shailene's audition, I knew she was Hazel. She held up her script pages and just her eyes were peeking over them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On May 10, 2013, <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Elgort" title="Ansel Elgort">Ansel Elgort</a> was cast as Hazel's love interest, Augustus Waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casting2_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-casting2-8">[8]</a></sup> On July 23, <a href="/wiki/Laura_Dern" title="Laura Dern">Laura Dern</a> joined the cast as Hazel's mother Frannie Lancaster, and <a href="/wiki/Nat_Wolff" title="Nat Wolff">Nat Wolff</a> as Issac, Augustus' best friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casting3_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-casting3-9">[9]</a></sup> Wolff told <a href="/wiki/HuffPost_Live" title="HuffPost Live">HuffPost Live</a>: "It's exciting, I feel really lucky. The fact that I get to work with these super talented people—I mean, that's part of the reason why, I'm good in the movie, that's the reason."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On August 14, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Trammell" title="Sam Trammell">Sam Trammell</a> was cast as Hazel's father Michael Lancaster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casting4_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-casting4-10">[10]</a></sup> and on August 28, author John Green announced that <a href="/wiki/Mike_Birbiglia" title="Mike Birbiglia">Mike Birbiglia</a> would be playing Patrick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casting5_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-casting5-12">[12]</a></sup> On September 6, he tweeted that <a href="/wiki/Willem_Dafoe" title="Willem Dafoe">Willem Dafoe</a> would play Peter van Houten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casting6_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-casting6-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to these announcements, author John Green had tweeted that he would be happy if <a href="/wiki/Mae_Whitman" title="Mae Whitman">Mae Whitman</a> played Hazel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and in February 2013, there had been speculation that Shailene Woodley and <a href="/wiki/Hailee_Steinfeld" title="Hailee Steinfeld">Hailee Steinfeld</a> were among those being considered for the lead role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Director_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Director-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>John Green filmed a <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo appearance</a>; his scene was cut from the film but is included in the deleted scenes section of the film's DVD and Blu-ray releases. In the cut scene, Green plays the father of a young girl who asks about Hazel's <a href="/wiki/Cannula" title="Cannula">cannula</a> while at the airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Green said: "They cut [the scene] because it was totally unnecessary to the movie-slash-I was terrible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulture-interview_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-vulture-interview-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> began on August 26, 2013, in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Film locations included Oakmont, Pennsylvania,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Hospital_of_Pittsburgh_of_UPMC" title="Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh of UPMC">Pittsburgh Children's Hospital</a>, and the historic The Mansions on Fifth hotel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The church scenes were filmed at St. Paul's Episcopal Church in the Pittsburgh suburb of Mt. Lebanon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Filming in Pennsylvania continued until October 10,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> then the production moved to Amsterdam, where filming began on October 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Filming was officially completed on October 16, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Amsterdam, three days of filming took place. Woodley and Elgort were filmed on a canal-side public bench. On July 2, 2014, <i>The Guardian</i> reported that the bench had gone missing, and city officials said they did not know where it was. Amsterdam city spokesman, Stephan van der Hoek said: "It's a bit embarrassing, because we do keep good track of them, but it's gone all right". He promised to install a new bench within weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Just a week later, <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> said the Amsterdam film office had tweeted a photograph taken during the re-installation of the stolen bench; Amsterdam film commissioner Simon Brester said it was the same bench, not a replacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Filming in Pittsburgh included the interior scenes at the Anne Frank House, which was recreated on a soundstage at Pittsburgh Studios located in Churchill Borough, Pennsylvania in the east suburbs of Pittsburgh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Production designer Molly Hughes and art director Greg Weimerskirch built three different studio sets in Pittsburgh, one for each floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Also, the <a href="/wiki/Funky_Bones" title="Funky Bones">Funky Bones</a> art monument in Indianapolis was recreated in Pittsburgh with the help of the sculpture's creator, Dutch artist Joep Van Lieshout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author John Green, though an Indianapolis native, was pleased with the selection of Pittsburgh for filming. He commented that</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Pittsburgh has an amazing community of really talented film professionals that’s been built up over the last 20 years. I’ve been blown away by their professionalism, their confidence, their dedication. There aren’t a lot of cities like that. You kind of have to find the city that can be Indianapolis. And Pittsburgh, even though it has a lot of hills, it can be Indianapolis. We just have to find a couple of flat streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_premiere" title="Film premiere">premiered</a> to 300 guests at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ziegfeld_Theater" title="Ziegfeld Theater">Ziegfeld Theater</a> in New York City on June 2, 2014. At the premiere, Green said: "I didn't want to sell it, because Hollywood sucks at making unsentimental movies about illness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The film was released on June 6, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> After the New York premiere, Evangeline Earl, a student, announced that Green's book, from which the film was adapted, had been inspired by her sister Esther Earl, who had also been an author. Esther suffered from thyroid cancer and died in 2010. Evangeline said Esther had met Green at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LeakyCon" title="LeakyCon">LeakyCon</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> convention held in Boston in 2009. Green said, "I could never have written <i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> without knowing Esther. Every word on that book depends on her."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Green said he used the word "okay" in the book and the film because Hazel and Augustus used it to express their love for each other;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> it becomes a special word between them. The original novel was inspired by the late author <a href="/wiki/Esther_Earl" title="Esther Earl">Esther Earl</a>, who used the word "okay" with her sister Abigail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Trailer_(promotion)" title="Trailer (promotion)">trailer</a> was released on January 29, 2014. The trailer had over three million views in less than 24 hours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and more than 15 million views in its first seven days. A video clip was shown before the show at the <a href="/wiki/2014_MTV_Movie_Awards" title="2014 MTV Movie Awards">2014 MTV Movie Awards</a> on April 13, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> An extended trailer was released on April 28, 2014, and Fox released more clips via <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> as part of the film's pre-release promotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On April 2, 2014, the studio announced the launch of a promotional tour program named Demand Our Stars. Green, Woodley, Elgort, and Wolff would visit the states that got most votes from fans re-blogging their states' map outlines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Four states won: the tour program started in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a>, on May 6; went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, on May 7; then went to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>, on May 8; and ended in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas, Texas</a>, on May 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> on September 16, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The <i>The Fault in Our Stars:Little Infinities</i> Blu-ray edition features the theatrical 126-minute and extended 133-minute versions of the film, a number of <a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">deleted scenes</a> including the John Green cameo, and featurettes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The home video release grossed over $42.6 million in total domestic video sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Numbers_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Numbers-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Big movies have their embedded lessons in heroism, but it's nice to have tales of human-scale fortitude."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Lisa Kennedy, writing for <i>Denver Post</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p><i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> received generally positive reviews, with Woodley's performance and Neustadter and Weber's screenplay receiving praise. It received a "Certified Fresh" rating from <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a> <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, which reported an 80% approval rating from critics with an average score of 6.9/10, based on 199 reviews. The site's consensus says: "Wise, funny, and heartbreaking without resorting to exploitation, <i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> does right by its bestselling source material."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomatoes_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomatoes-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gave the film a 69 out of 100, based on reviews from 45 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacritic_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CinemaScore" title="CinemaScore">CinemaScore</a> gave the film an "A" on an A+ to F scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steven_Rea" title="Steven Rea">Steven Rea</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="The Philadelphia Inquirer">The Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i> gave the film a score of 3 out of 4; he said: "Woodley ... balances grace with gravity, wit with heart."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">A. O. Scott</a> writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> said: "The film sets out to make you weep—not just sniffle or choke up a little, but sob until your nose runs and your face turns blotchy. It succeeds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a>'s</i> <a href="/wiki/Ty_Burr" title="Ty Burr">Ty Burr</a> gave the film a rating of 2.5 out 4 and said: "If Elgort's Gus is glibly charming and ultimately affecting, "The Fault in Our Stars" belongs to Woodley, a performer who always seems to be backing warily into her own movies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quickflix" title="Quickflix">Quickflix</a>'s Simon Miraudo rated the film 3/5, and said: "<i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> undeniably sets us up to knock us down."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Roeper" title="Richard Roeper">Richard Roeper</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> gave the film 4 stars. He said Woodley's performance as Hazel was "transcendent, pure and authentic", stating that, "she's that memorable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Scott Mendelson of <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine said the film is "exceptionally high-quality mainstream entertainment" and called it "a genuine work of art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Anna Smith of <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i> said it was a, "touching romance and Shailene Woodley's best performance yet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Emma Dibdin of <a href="/wiki/Digital_Spy" title="Digital Spy">Digital Spy</a> wrote "<i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> is a sharp and emotionally-sophisticated weepie that imbues its teenage characters with rare intelligence, and tackles its bleak subject matter with acerbic wit and tenderness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Andy Lea of the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Star_(British_newspaper)" title="Daily Star (British newspaper)">Daily Star</a></i> said: "as teen romances go, this is powerful stuff."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Wilson Morales at <i>BlackFilm.com</i> praised Woodley and Elgort's performances, saying they: "are so captivating and genuine in their performances, they manage to make this intensely poignant film very moving, romantic and highly entertaining."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"It's a fresh, lively love story, brimming with humor and heartbreak, and lifted to the heights by Shailene Woodley, a sublime actress with a résumé that pretty much proves she's incapable of making a false move on camera."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Peter Travers of <i>Rolling Stone</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's Richard Corliss wrote: "Hazel and Augustus will live in film lore because of the young actors who play them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Chris Vongar of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas_Morning_News" title="Dallas Morning News">Dallas Morning News</a></i> gave the film a B+ as "the movie is witty and alive and only very occasionally maudlin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today_(magazine)" title="USA Today (magazine)">USA Today</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Claudia Puig called the film "well-written, well-acted, acerbic, funny and wisely observed", giving it 3.5 out of 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Chris Nashawaty of <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> called the film a "funny, sweet, three-hankie tearjerker" and gave the B grade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Pevere" title="Geoff Pevere">Geoff Pevere</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">The Globe and Mail</a></i> said, "While it may not conform to one's real-life expectations it certainly hews tightly to teen-flick conventions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Connie Ogle of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Miami_Herald" title="The Miami Herald">The Miami Herald</a></i> called it a, "sweet, romantic film full of sudden warmth and humor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> A.A. Dowd of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> gave the film a "B" saying that it is, "blessed with sparks of wit and buoyed by the talents of a charismatic cast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> gave the film a negative review, likening it to "being mugged by a professional whose skills in mixed martial arts you can't help but notice and appreciate, even as you are savagely beaten, then dragged upright, bruised and bleeding, and forced to watch as your assailant gives fully 45% of your money to charity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christy_Lemire" title="Christy Lemire">Christy Lemire</a> writing for <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>'s website gave it two stars out of four and criticized the film for being "emotionally inert, despite its many moments that are meant to put a lump in our throats." Lemire criticized Elgort's performance and praised Woodley's "abiding, disarming naturalism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robbie_Collin" title="Robbie Collin">Robbie Collin</a> writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> said: "Gus is something of a manic pixie dreamboat: the lesser-spotted male version of the kooky, adoring girlfriend-type sometimes played by <a href="/wiki/Kirsten_Dunst" title="Kirsten Dunst">Kirsten Dunst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Portman" title="Natalie Portman">Natalie Portman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zooey_Deschanel" title="Zooey Deschanel">Zooey Deschanel</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Edelstein" title="David Edelstein">David Edelstein</a> of <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I know people who cried at the trailer of the romantic teen cancer movie <i>The Fault in Our Stars</i>—at the movie they'll need a life preserver to keep from drowning in a flood of tears. Me, I didn't cry, though at times my tear ducts tingled; I was on the verge. The film is a little slick for my taste, too engineered. But it's gently directed by Josh Boone and beautifully acted. Whatever the faults, it's not in the stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Shalini Langer of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Indian_Express" title="The Indian Express">The Indian Express</a></i> gave the film two and a half stars and said: "The film is faithful to a fault from the dialogues to the clothes, the setting and the food, even while skipping over some of the unpleasant details."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dana_Stevens_(critic)" title="Dana Stevens (critic)">Dana Stevens</a> writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)">Slate Magazine</a></i> said: "What in the name of God is wrong with me that I didn't cry once—I, who just the day before wept through the entirety of my child's thoroughly upbeat school play."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Two Christian reviewers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> and some theologians<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> have mentioned the presence of Christian themes in the film. Writing for <a href="/wiki/Catholic_News_Agency" title="Catholic News Agency">Catholic News Agency</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Barron_(priest)" title="Robert Barron (priest)">Robert Barron</a> said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I don’t think it is the least bit accidental that Waters (Gus’s last name) and Grace (Hazel’s middle name) met in the sacred heart of Christ and thereby, despite their shared suffering, managed to give life to one another ... [and so] Hazel effectively repudiates her nihilism and materialism as she responds across the barrier of death to Gus’s 'okay'<span class="nowrap"> </span>." He also noted: "[Is] this film a satisfying presentation of Christianity? Hardly. But for those who are struggling to find their way to meaning and faith, it's not an entirely bad place to start."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catholicnewsagency_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-catholicnewsagency-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The film's studio, 20th Century Fox, mounted an unsuccessful campaign to have Shailene Woodley nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a>, as well as the film's adapted screenplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> earned <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$124,872,350</span> in North America and $181,645,551 in other countries for a worldwide total of $306,517,901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In North America, the film's income received a boost from "The Night Before Our Stars," a premium-priced event for which tickets sold for up to $25. The event included a screening of the film and a simulcast question-and-answer session with cast and crew, including Woodley, Elgort, Wolff, and Green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMJune05_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOMJune05-85">[85]</a></sup> The film earned $8.2 million from Thursday-night showings and $26.1 million on its opening day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMJune07_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOMJune07-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> During its opening weekend, the film earned $48,002,573 from 3,173 theaters at an average of $15,128 per theater, making it number one in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>At locations outside North America, <i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> earned $16.59 million from 2,892 screens in 17 markets in its opening weekend, placing at number four behind <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge_of_Tomorrow_(film)" title="Edge of Tomorrow (film)">Edge of Tomorrow</a></i> ($81 million), <i><a href="/wiki/Maleficent" title="Maleficent">Maleficent</a></i> ($61.7 million), and <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_Days_of_Future_Past" title="X-Men: Days of Future Past">X-Men: Days of Future Past</a></i> ($41.1 million). Brazil, Mexico, and Australia generated the highest income with $5.8 million, $3.8 million, and $3.7 million respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> The following week the film earned $16.1 million from 40 markets, remaining at number 4 again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hindi_remake">Hindi remake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fault_in_Our_Stars_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Hindi remake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 6, 2014, India's <a href="/wiki/Fox_Star_Studios" title="Fox Star Studios">Fox Star Studios</a> announced its intention to produce a remake of <i>The Fault in Our Stars</i> in <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Hindi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The studio's CEO, Vijay Singh, said the English version of the film, which was released in India in July 2014, had earned over $1 million there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> On September 19, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Homi_Adajania" title="Homi Adajania">Homi Adajania</a>, the director of <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Fanny" title="Finding Fanny">Finding Fanny</a></i> (2014), and producer <a href="/wiki/Dinesh_Vijan" title="Dinesh Vijan">Dinesh Vijan</a> would be working with <a href="/wiki/Fox_Star_Studios" title="Fox Star Studios">Fox Star Studios</a> to develop the film, which will be co-produced by Vijan's company Maddock Films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adajania_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adajania-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
