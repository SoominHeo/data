<p>The <b>Andaman Islands</b> form an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> between <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, to the west, and <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, to the north and east. Most are part of the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands" title="Andaman and Nicobar Islands">Andaman and Nicobar Islands</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Territory" title="Union Territory">Union Territory</a> of India, while a small number in the north of the archipelago, including the <a href="/wiki/Coco_Islands" title="Coco Islands">Coco Islands</a>, belong to Myanmar.</p>
<p>The Andaman Islands are home to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentinelese_people" title="Sentinelese people">Sentinelese people</a>, who have had no contact with any other people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name of the Andaman Islands is ancient.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. A theory that became prevalent in the late 19th century is that it derives from Andoman, a form of <a href="/wiki/Hanuman" title="Hanuman">Hanuman</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a> name of the <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Indian</a> God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunter1908_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hunter1908-3">[3]</a></sup> Another Italian traveller, <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_de%27_Conti" title="Niccolò de' Conti">Niccolò de' Conti</a> (c. 1440), mentioned the islands and said that the name means "Island of Gold".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_inhabitants">Early inhabitants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early inhabitants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Andaman islands have been inhabited for several thousand years, at the very least. The earliest <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> evidence yet documented goes back some 2,200 years; however, the indications from <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a> and isolation studies suggests that the islands may have been inhabited as early as the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Paleolithic" title="Middle Paleolithic">Middle Paleolithic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The indigenous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andamanese_people" title="Andamanese people">Andamanese people</a> appear to have lived on the islands in substantial isolation from that time until the 18th century CE.</p>
<p>The Andamans are theorised to be a key stepping stone in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Coastal_Migration" title="Great Coastal Migration">great coastal migration</a> of humans from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a>, along the coastal regions of the Indian mainland and towards <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wells2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wells2002-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chola_empire">Chola empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chola empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 800 to 1200 CE, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamil_people" title="Tamil people">Tamil</a> <a href="/wiki/Chola_dynasty" title="Chola dynasty">Chola dynasty</a> created an empire that eventually extended from southeastern peninsular India to parts of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bingham1964_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bingham1964-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rajendra_Chola_I" title="Rajendra Chola I">Rajendra Chola I</a> (1014 to 1042 CE) took over the Andaman and Nicobar Islands and maintained them as a strategic naval base to launch a naval expedition against the <a href="/wiki/Srivijaya" title="Srivijaya">Srivijaya empire</a> (a Hindu-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malay_People" title="Malay People">Malay</a> empire based on the island of Sumatra, Indonesia).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_colonisation_and_penal_colony">British colonisation and penal colony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: British colonisation and penal colony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1789, <a href="/wiki/Bengal_Presidency" title="Bengal Presidency">Bengal Presidency</a> established a naval base and <a href="/wiki/Penal_colony" title="Penal colony">penal colony</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chatham_Island_(Andaman)" title="Chatham Island (Andaman)">Chatham Island</a> in the southeast bay of <a href="/wiki/South_Andaman_Island" title="South Andaman Island">Great Andaman</a>. The settlement is now known as <a href="/wiki/Port_Blair" title="Port Blair">Port Blair</a> (after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombay_Marine" title="Bombay Marine">Bombay Marine</a> lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Blair" title="Archibald Blair">Archibald Blair</a> who founded it). After two years, the colony was moved to the northeast part of <a href="/wiki/North_Andaman_Island" title="North Andaman Island">Great Andaman</a> and was named Port Cornwallis after Admiral <a href="/wiki/William_Cornwallis" title="William Cornwallis">William Cornwallis</a>. However, there was much disease and death in the penal colony and the government ceased operating it in May 1796.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olivierblaise_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-olivierblaise-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1824, Port Cornwallis was the rendezvous of the fleet carrying the army to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Burmese_War" title="First Burmese War">First Burmese War</a>. In the 1830s and 1840s, shipwrecked crews who landed on the Andamans were often attacked and killed by the natives and the islands had a reputation for cannibalism. The loss of the <i>Runnymede</i> and the <i>Briton</i> in 1844 during the same storm, while transporting goods and passengers between India and Australia, and the continuous attacks launched by the natives, which the survivors fought off, alarmed the British government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1855, the government proposed another settlement on the islands, including a <a href="/wiki/Cellular_Jail" title="Cellular Jail">convict establishment</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">Indian Rebellion of 1857</a> forced a delay in its construction. However, because the rebellion gave the British so many prisoners, it made the new Andaman settlement and prison urgently necessary. Construction began in November 1857 at Port Blair using inmates' labour, avoiding the vicinity of a salt swamp that seemed to have been the source of many of the earlier problems at Port Cornwallis.</p>
<p>17 May 1859 was another major day for Andaman. The "Battle of Aberdeen" was fought between the Great Andamanese Tribe and the British. Today, a memorial stands in Andaman Water sports complex as a tribute to the people who lost their life. Fearing foreign invasion and with help from an escaped convict from Cellular Jail, the great Andamanese tribe stormed the British post, but they were outnumbered and soon suffered heavy loss of life. Later, it was identified that an escaped convict named Doodnath had changed sides and informed the British about the tribe's plans. Today, the tribe has been reduced to some 50 people, with less than 50% of them adults. The government of Andaman Islands is making efforts to increase the headcount of this tribe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rapidiq_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rapidiq-10">[10]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-One_India_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-One_India-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andaman_Sheekha_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andaman_Sheekha-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1867, the ship <i>Nineveh</i> wrecked on the reef of <a href="/wiki/North_Sentinel_Island" title="North Sentinel Island">North Sentinel Island</a>. The 86 survivors reached the beach in the ship's boats. On the third day, they were attacked with iron-tipped spears by naked islanders. One person from the ship escaped in a boat and the others were later rescued by a British Royal Navy ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodheart_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodheart-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>For some time, sickness and mortality were high, but <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">swamp reclamation</a> and extensive forest clearance continued. The Andaman colony became notorious with the murder of the Viceroy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Southwell_Bourke,_6th_Earl_of_Mayo" title="Richard Southwell Bourke, 6th Earl of Mayo">Richard Southwell Bourke, 6th Earl of Mayo</a>, on a visit to the settlement (8 February 1872), by a Muslim convict, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashtun_people" title="Pashtun people">Pathan</a> from <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, Sher Ali. In the same year, the two island groups Andaman and <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_Islands" title="Nicobar Islands">Nicobar</a>, were united under a chief commissioner residing at Port Blair.</p>

<p>From the time of its development in 1858 under the direction of James Pattison Walker, and in response to the mutiny and rebellion of the previous year, the settlement was first and foremost a repository for <a href="/wiki/Political_prisoner" title="Political prisoner">political prisoners</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cellular_Jail" title="Cellular Jail">Cellular Jail</a> at Port Blair when completed in 1910 included 698 cells designed for solitary confinement; each cell measured 4.5 by 2.7 m (15 by 9 ft) with a single ventilation window 3 metres (10 ft) above the floor. A notable prisoner there was <a href="/wiki/Vinayak_Damodar_Savarkar" title="Vinayak Damodar Savarkar">Vinayak Damodar Savarkar</a>.</p>
<p>The Indians imprisoned here referred to the Island and its prison as <i>Kala Pani</i> ("black water");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> a 1996 film set on the island took that term as its title <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaalapani_(1996_film)" title="Kaalapani (1996 film)">Kaalapani</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The number of prisoners who died in this camp is estimated to be in the thousands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Many more died of harsh treatment and the harsh living and working conditions in this camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Viper Chain Gang Jail on <a href="/wiki/Viper_Island" title="Viper Island">Viper Island</a> was reserved for troublemakers, and was also the site of hangings. In the 20th century, it became a convenient place to house prominent members of India's independence movement.</p>

<p>The Andaman and Nicobar islands were <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_the_Andaman_Islands" title="Japanese occupation of the Andaman Islands">occupied by Japan</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The islands were nominally put under the authority of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arzi_Hukumat-e-Azad_Hind" title="Arzi Hukumat-e-Azad Hind">Arzi Hukumat-e-Azad Hind</a> (Provisional Government of Free India) headed by <a href="/wiki/Subhas_Chandra_Bose" title="Subhas Chandra Bose">Subhas Chandra Bose</a>, who visited the islands during the war, and renamed them as <a href="/wiki/Shahid" title="Shahid">Shaheed</a> (Martyr) &amp; Swaraj (Self-rule). On 30 December 1943, during the Japanese occupation, Bose, who was allied with the Japanese, first raised the flag of Indian independence. General <a href="/wiki/A._D._Loganathan" title="A. D. Loganathan">Loganathan</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Army" title="Indian National Army">Indian National Army</a>, was Governor of the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, which had been annexed to the Provisional Government. According to Werner Gruhl: "Before leaving the islands, the Japanese rounded up and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_war_crimes" title="Japanese war crimes">executed 750 innocents</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> After the end of the war the islands returned to British control before becoming part of the newly independent state of India.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At the close of World War II, the British government announced its intention to abolish the penal settlement. The government proposed to employ former inmates in an initiative to develop the island's fisheries, timber, and agricultural resources. In exchange, inmates would be granted return passage to the Indian mainland, or the right to settle on the islands. The penal colony was eventually closed on 15 August 1947 when India gained independence. It has since served as a museum to the independence movement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 1998, American photographer John S Callahan organised the first surfing project in the Andamans, starting from <a href="/wiki/Phuket_Province" title="Phuket Province">Phuket</a> in Thailand with the assistance of Southeast Asia Liveaboards (SEAL), a UK owned dive charter company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With a crew of international professional surfers, they crossed the Andaman Sea on the yacht <i>Crescent</i> and cleared formalities in Port Blair. The group proceeded to Little Andaman Island, where they spent ten days surfing several spots for the first time, including Jarawa Point near Hut Bay and the long right reef point at the southwest tip of the island, named Kumari Point. The resulting article in <i>Surfer Magazine</i>, "Quest for Fire" by journalist Sam George, put the Andaman Islands on the surfing map for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Footage of the waves of the Andaman Islands also appeared in the film <i>Thicker than Water</i>, shot by <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary filmmaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Jack_Johnson_(musician)" title="Jack Johnson (musician)">Jack Johnson</a>, who later achieved worldwide fame as a popular musician.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Callahan went on to make several more surfing projects in the Andamans, including a trip to the Nicobar Islands in 1999.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On 26 December 2004, the coast of the Andaman Islands was devastated by a 10-metre (33 ft) high tsunami following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">2004 Indian Ocean earthquake</a>, which is the longest recorded earthquake, lasting for between 500 and 600 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Strong oral traditions in the area warned of the importance of moving inland after a quake and is credited with saving many lives. In the aftermath, more than 2,000 people were confirmed dead and more than 4,000 children were orphaned or had lost one parent. At least 40,000 residents were rendered homeless and were moved to relief camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On 11 August 2009, a magnitude 7 earthquake struck near the Andaman Islands, causing a tsunami warning to go into effect. On 30 March 2010, a magnitude 6.9 earthquake struck near the Andaman Islands.</p>

<p>The Andaman Archipelago is an oceanic continuation of the Burmese <a href="/wiki/Chin_Hills" title="Chin Hills">Arakan Yoma range</a> in the North and of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Indonesia" title="List of islands of Indonesia">Indonesian Archipelago</a> in the South. It has 325 islands which cover an area of 6,408 km<sup>2</sup> (2,474 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planning_Commission_Report_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planning_Commission_Report-23">[23]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Sea" title="Andaman Sea">Andaman Sea</a> to the east between the islands and the coast of Burma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olivierblaise_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-olivierblaise-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/North_Andaman_Island" title="North Andaman Island">North Andaman Island</a> is 285 kilometres (177 mi) south of Burma, although a few smaller Burmese islands are closer, including the three Coco Islands.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ten_Degree_Channel" title="Ten Degree Channel">Ten Degree Channel</a> separates the Andamans from the <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_Islands" title="Nicobar Islands">Nicobar Islands</a> to the south. The highest point is located in North Andaman Island (<a href="/wiki/Saddle_Peak_(Andaman_Islands)" title="Saddle Peak (Andaman Islands)">Saddle Peak</a> at 732 m (2,402 ft)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planning_Commission_Report_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Planning_Commission_Report-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup></p>
<p>The subsoil of the Andaman islands consists essentially of <a href="/wiki/Late_Jurassic" title="Late Jurassic">Late Jurassic</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Eocene" title="Early Eocene">Early Eocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">ophiolites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Argillaceous_limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Argillaceous limestone (page does not exist)">argillaceous</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Algal_limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Algal limestone (page does not exist)">algal limestones</a>), deformed by numerous deep <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faults</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrusts</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> igneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion_(geology)" title="Intrusion (geology)">intrusions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsu_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-tsu-24">[24]</a></sup> There are at least 11 <a href="/wiki/Mud_volcano" title="Mud volcano">mud volcanoes</a> on the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsu_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-tsu-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The climate is typical of tropical islands of similar latitude. It is always warm, but with sea-breezes. Rainfall is irregular, usually dry during the north-east, and very wet during the south-west, monsoons.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Middle Andamans harbour mostly moist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deciduous_forests" title="Deciduous forests">deciduous forests</a>. North Andamans is characterised by the wet evergreen type, with plenty of woody climbers.</p>
<p>The natural vegetation of the Andamans is tropical forest, with <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a> on the coast. The rainforests are similar in composition to those of the west coast of Burma. Most of the forests are evergreen, but there are areas of deciduous forest on North Andaman, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Andaman_Island" title="Middle Andaman Island">Middle Andaman</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baratang" title="Baratang">Baratang</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Andaman_Island" title="South Andaman Island">South Andaman Island</a>. The South Andaman forests have a profuse growth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiphytic" title="Epiphytic">epiphytic</a> vegetation, mostly ferns and orchids.</p>
<p>The Andaman forests are largely unspoiled, despite logging and the demands of the fast-growing population driven by immigration from the Indian mainland. There are protected areas on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Andaman_Island" title="Little Andaman Island">Little Andaman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narcondam_Island" title="Narcondam Island">Narcondam</a>, North Andaman and South Andaman, but these are mainly aimed at preserving the coast and the marine wildlife rather than the rainforests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Threats to wildlife come from introduced species including rats, dogs, cats and the elephants of <a href="/wiki/Interview_Island" title="Interview Island">Interview Island</a> and North Andaman.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timber">Timber</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Timber">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Andaman forests contain 200 or more timber producing species of trees, out of which about 30 varieties are considered to be commercial. Major commercial timber species are Gurjan (<i><a href="/wiki/Dipterocarpus" title="Dipterocarpus">Dipterocarpus</a></i> spp.) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Padauk" title="Padauk">Padauk</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Pterocarpus_dalbergioides" title="Pterocarpus dalbergioides">Pterocarpus dalbergioides</a></i>). The following ornamental woods are noted for their pronounced grain formation:</p>
<ul>
<li>Marble Wood (<i><a href="/wiki/Diospyros" title="Diospyros">Diospyros marmorata</a></i>)</li>
<li>Padauk (<i><a href="/wiki/Pterocarpus_dalbergioides" title="Pterocarpus dalbergioides">Pterocarpus dalbergioides</a></i>)</li>
<li>Silver Grey (a special formation of wood in white <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Utkarsh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Utkarsh (page does not exist)">utkarsh</a>)</li>
<li>Chooi (<i><a href="/wiki/Sageraea" title="Sageraea">Sageraea elliptica</a></i>)</li>
<li>Kokko (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albizzia_lebbeck" title="Albizzia lebbeck">Albizzia lebbeck</a></i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Padauk wood is sturdier than teak and is widely used for furniture making.</p>
<p>There are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burr_wood" title="Burr wood">burr wood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buttress_root" title="Buttress root">buttress root</a> formations in Andaman Padauk. The largest piece of buttress known from Andaman was a dining table of 13 ft × 7 ft (4.0 m × 2.1 m). The largest piece of burr wood was again a dining table for eight.</p>
<p>The holy <a href="/wiki/Rudraksha" title="Rudraksha">Rudraksha</a> (<i>Elaeocarps sphaericus</i>) and aromatic Dhoop resin trees also are found here.</p>

<p>The Andaman Islands are home to a number of animals, many of them endemic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammals">Mammals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The island's endemic mammals include</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andaman_spiny_shrew" title="Andaman spiny shrew">Andaman spiny shrew</a> (<i>Crocidura hispida</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andaman_shrew" title="Andaman shrew">Andaman shrew</a> (<i>Crocidura andamanensis</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jenkins%27_shrew" title="Jenkins' shrew">Jenkins' shrew</a> (<i>Crocidura jenkinsi</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andaman_horseshoe_bat" title="Andaman horseshoe bat">Andaman horseshoe bat</a> (<i>Rhinolophus cognatus</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andaman_rat" title="Andaman rat">Andaman rat</a> (<i>Rattus stoicus</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Banded_pig" title="Banded pig">banded pig</a> (<i>Sus scrofa vittatus</i>), also known as the Andaman wild boar and once thought to be an endemic subspecies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SrinivasuluSrinivasulu2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-SrinivasuluSrinivasulu2012-26">[26]</a></sup> is protected by the Wildlife Protection Act 1972 (Sch I). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spotted_deer" title="Spotted deer">spotted deer</a> (<i>Axis axis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Indian_muntjac" title="Indian muntjac">Indian muntjac</a> (<i>Muntiacus muntjak</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Sambar_deer" title="Sambar deer">sambar</a> (<i>Rusa unicolor</i>) were all introduced to the Andaman islands, though the sambar did not survive.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Interview_Island" title="Interview Island">Interview Island</a> (the largest wildlife sanctuary in the territory) in Middle Andaman holds a population of feral <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>, which were brought in for forest work by a timber company and released when the company went bankrupt. This population has been subject to research studies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds">Birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Endemic or near endemic birds include</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spilornis_elgini" title="Spilornis elgini">Spilornis elgini</a></i>, a serpent-eagle</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rallina_canningi" title="Rallina canningi">Rallina canningi</a></i>, a crake (endemic; data-deficient per IUCN 2000)</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columba_palumboides" title="Columba palumboides">Columba palumboides</a></i>, a wood-pigeon</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macropygia_rufipennis" title="Macropygia rufipennis">Macropygia rufipennis</a></i>, a cuckoo dove</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centropus_andamanensis" title="Centropus andamanensis">Centropus andamanensis</a></i>, a subspecies of brown coucal (endemic)</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otus_balli" title="Otus balli">Otus balli</a></i>, the scops owl</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninox_affinis" title="Ninox affinis">Ninox affinis</a></i>, a hawk-owl</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aceros_narcondami" title="Aceros narcondami">Aceros narcondami</a></i>, the Narcondam hornbill</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dryocopus_hodgei" title="Dryocopus hodgei">Dryocopus hodgei</a></i>, a woodpecker</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dicrurus_andamanensis" title="Dicrurus andamanensis">Dicrurus andamanensis</a></i>, a drongo</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendrocitta_bayleyii" title="Dendrocitta bayleyii">Dendrocitta bayleyii</a></i>, a treepie</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sturnus_erythropygius" title="Sturnus erythropygius">Sturnus erythropygius</a></i>, the white-headed starling</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collocalia_esculenta" title="Collocalia esculenta">Collocalia esculenta</a></i>, the glossy swiftlet</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodramus_fuciphagus" title="Aerodramus fuciphagus">Aerodramus fuciphagus</a></i>, the edible-nest swiftlet</li>
</ul>
<p>The islands' many <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>, such as those at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chalis_Ek_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chalis Ek Caves (page does not exist)">Chalis Ek</a> are nesting grounds for the edible-nest <a href="/wiki/Swiftlet" title="Swiftlet">swiftlet</a>, whose nests are prized in China for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird%27s_nest_soup" title="Bird's nest soup">bird's nest soup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soup_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-soup-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reptiles_and_amphibians">Reptiles and amphibians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reptiles and amphibians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The islands also have a number of endemic <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toad" title="Toad">toads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, such as the South Andaman krait (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bungarus_andamanensis" title="Bungarus andamanensis">Bungarus andamanensis</a></i>) and Andaman water monitor (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Varanus_salvator_andamanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Varanus salvator andamanensis (page does not exist)">Varanus salvator andamanensis</a></i>).</p>
<p>There is a sanctuary 45 miles (72 km) from <a href="/wiki/Havelock_Island" title="Havelock Island">Havelock Island</a> for <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_crocodile" title="Saltwater crocodile">saltwater crocodiles</a>. Over the past 25 years there have been 24 crocodile attacks with four fatalities, including the death of American tourist Lauren Failla. The government has been criticised for failing to inform tourists of the crocodile sanctuary and danger, while simultaneously promoting tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Crocodiles are not only found within the sanctuary, but throughout the island chain in varying densities. They are habitat restricted, so the population is stable but not large. Populations occur throughout available mangrove habitat on all major islands, including a few creeks on Havelock. The species uses the ocean as a means of travel between different rivers and estuaries, thus they are not as commonly observed in open ocean. It is best to avoid swimming near mangrove areas or the mouths of creeks; swimming in the open ocean should be safe, but it is best to have a spotter around.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the tribal people in Andaman and Nicobar Islands believe in a religion that can be described as a form of <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">monotheistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">Animism</a>. The tribal people of these islands believe that Paluga is the only deity and is responsible for everything happening on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The faith of the Andamanese teaches that Paluga resides on the Andaman and Nicobar Islands' <a href="/wiki/Saddle_Peak_(Andaman_Islands)" title="Saddle Peak (Andaman Islands)">Saddle Peak (Andaman Islands)</a>. People try to avoid any action that might displease Paluga. People belonging to this religion believe in the presence of souls, ghosts, and spirits. Interestingly, people of this religion put a lot of emphasis on dreams. They let dreams decide different courses of action in their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other religions practiced in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands are, in terms of size, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the population of the Andaman was 343,125,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> having grown from 50,000 in 1960. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_Andamanese">Indigenous Andamanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Indigenous Andamanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andamanese_people" title="Andamanese people">Andamanese people</a></div>
<p>Of the people who live in the Andaman Islands, a small minority of about 1,000 are the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andamanese_people" title="Andamanese people">Andamanese</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">aboriginal inhabitants</a> (<a href="/wiki/Adivasi" title="Adivasi">adivasi</a>) of the islands. By the 1850s when they first came into sustained contact by outside groups, there were estimated 7,000 Adamanese, divided into the following major groups:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Great_Andamanese" title="Great Andamanese">Great Andamanese</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jarawa_(Andaman_Islands)" title="Jarawa (Andaman Islands)">Jarawa</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jangil" title="Jangil">Jangil</a> (or <i>Rutland Jarawa</i>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onge_people" title="Onge people">Onge</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentinelese_people" title="Sentinelese people">Sentinelese</a></li>
</ul>
<p>As the numbers of settlers from the mainland increased (at first mostly prisoners and involuntary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_labour" title="Indentured labour">indentured labourers</a>, later purposely recruited farmers), these indigenous people lost territory and numbers in the face of punitive expeditions by British troops, land encroachment and various epidemic diseases. Presently, there remain only approximately 400–450 indigenous Andamanese. The Jangil were soon extinct. The Great Andamanese were originally 10 distinct tribes with 5,000 people in total; most of the tribes are extinct, and the survivors, now just 52, speak mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The Onge are reduced to less than 100 people. Only the Jarawa and Sentinelese still maintain a steadfast independence and refuse most attempts at contact; their numbers are uncertain but estimated to be in the low hundreds.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Port_Blair" title="Port Blair">Port Blair</a> is the chief community on the islands, and the administrative centre of the Union Territory. The Andaman Islands form a single administrative district within the Union Territory, the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_district" title="Andaman district">Andaman district</a> (the Nicobar Islands were separated and established as the new <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_district" title="Nicobar district">Nicobar district</a> in 1974).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The islands are prominently featured in <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> mystery, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sign_of_the_Four" title="The Sign of the Four">The Sign of the Four</a>,</i> as well as in <a href="/wiki/M._M._Kaye" title="M. M. Kaye">M. M. Kaye</a>'s <i>Death in the Andamans</i>. The magistrate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lady_Gregory" title="Lady Gregory">Lady Gregory</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Spreading_the_News" title="Spreading the News">Spreading the News</a></i> had formerly served in the islands. <a href="/wiki/Marianne_Wiggins" title="Marianne Wiggins">Marianne Wiggins</a>' novel, John Dollar (1989), is set on one of the islands; the characters begin an expedition from Burma to celebrate King George's birthday and after an earthquake and tsunami it becomes a grim survival story. A principal character in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Six_Suspects_(novel)" title="Six Suspects (novel)">Six Suspects</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Vikas_Swarup" title="Vikas Swarup">Vikas Swarup</a> is from the Andaman Islands. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaalapani_(1996_film)" title="Kaalapani (1996 film)">Kaalapani</a></i> (Malayalam) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sirai_Chaalai" title="Sirai Chaalai">Sirai Chaalai</a></i> (Tamil), a 1996 Indian film by <a href="/wiki/Priyadarshan" title="Priyadarshan">Priyadarshan</a>, depicts the Indian freedom struggle and the lives of prisoners in the <a href="/wiki/Cellular_Jail" title="Cellular Jail">Cellular Jail</a> in Port Blair. <i>Island's End</i> is a 2011 novel by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Padma_Venkatraman" title="Padma Venkatraman">Padma Venkatraman</a> about the training of an indigenous shaman.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andaman_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The only commercial airport in the islands is <a href="/wiki/Veer_Savarkar_International_Airport" title="Veer Savarkar International Airport">Veer Savarkar International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Port_Blair" title="Port Blair">Port Blair</a>, which has scheduled services to <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengaluru" title="Bengaluru">Bengaluru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visakhapatnam" title="Visakhapatnam">Visakhapatnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhubaneswar" title="Bhubaneswar">Bhubaneswar</a>. The airport is under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a>. Previously, only daylight operations were allowed, but since the beginning of 2016 night flights have also operated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A small airstrip of approximately 1000 metres is located near the Eastern shore of North Andaman near <a href="/wiki/Diglipur" title="Diglipur">Diglipur</a>.</p>
<p>Due to the length of the routes and the small number of airlines flying to the islands, fares have traditionally been relatively expensive, although cheaper for locals than visitors. Fares are high during the peak seasons of spring and winter, although fares have decreased over time due to the expansion of the civil aviation industry in India.</p>
