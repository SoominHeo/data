<p><b>Andalusian Arabic</b>, also known more usually as <b>Andalusi Arabic</b> or <b>Spanish Arabic</b> or <b>Hispano-Arabic</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Arabic" title="Varieties of Arabic">variety</a> or were varieties of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a>, the regions of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>) under Muslim rule. It became an <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">extinct language</a> in Iberia after the <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_the_Moriscos" title="Expulsion of the Moriscos">expulsion of the Moriscos</a>, which took place over a century after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Granada" title="Conquest of Granada">Conquest of Granada</a> by Christian Spain. Andalusi Arabic is still used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusi_music" title="Andalusi music">Andalusi music</a> and has significantly influenced the dialects of such towns as <a href="/wiki/Sfax" title="Sfax">Sfax</a> in Tunisia, <a href="/wiki/Fez,_Morocco" title="Fez, Morocco">Fez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabat" title="Rabat">Rabat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nedroma" title="Nedroma">Nedroma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tlemcen" title="Tlemcen">Tlemcen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blida" title="Blida">Blida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherchell" title="Cherchell">Cherchell</a>,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is still used<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> by communities of the descendants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moriscos" title="Moriscos">Moriscos</a> (Andalusi Muslims) in cities like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangiers" title="Tangiers">Tangiers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetouan" title="Tetouan">Tetouan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Testour" title="Testour">Testour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghar_al_Milh" title="Ghar al Milh">Ghar al Milh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sfax" title="Sfax">Sfax</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> which welcomed Moriscos refugees. It also exerted some influence on <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">Mozarabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">Andalusian</a>), <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">Ladino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">Berber</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_Arabic" title="Moroccan Arabic">Moroccan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisian_Arabic" title="Tunisian Arabic">Tunisian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hassaniya_Arabic" title="Hassaniya Arabic">Hassani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algerian_Arabic" title="Algerian Arabic">Algerian Arabic</a> dialects.</p>
<p>Andalusian Arabic appears to have spread rapidly and been in general oral use in most parts of Al-Andalus between the ninth and fifteenth centuries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The number of speakers is estimated to have peaked at around 5–7 million speakers around the eleventh and twelfth centuries before dwindling as a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>, the gradual but relentless takeover by the Christians. In 1502, the Muslims of Granada were forced to choose between conversion and exile; those who converted became known as the Moriscos. In 1526, this requirement was extended to the Muslims elsewhere in Spain (<a href="/wiki/Mud%C3%A9jar" title="Mudéjar">Mudéjars</a>). In 1567, <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a> issued a royal decree in Spain forbidding Moriscos from the <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">use of Arabic on all occasions</a>, formal and informal, speaking and writing. Using Arabic in any sense of the word would be regarded as a crime. They were given three years to learn a "Christian" language, after which they would have to get rid of all Arabic written material. This triggered one of the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morisco_Revolt" title="Morisco Revolt">Morisco Revolts</a>. Still, Andalusian Arabic remained in use in certain areas of Spain until the final <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_the_Moriscos" title="Expulsion of the Moriscos">expulsion of the Moriscos</a> at the beginning of the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As in every other Arabic-speaking land, native speakers of Andalusian Arabic were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diglossic" title="Diglossic">diglossic</a>, that is, they spoke their local dialect in all low-register situations, but only Classical Arabic was resorted to when a high register was required and for written purposes as well.</p>
<p>Andalusian Arabic belongs to Early Western Neo-Arabic, which does not allow for any separation between Bedouin, urban, or rural dialects, nor does it show any detectable difference between communal dialects, such as <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a>.</p>
<p>The oldest evidence of Andalusian Arabic utterances can be dated from the 10th and 11th century, in isolated quotes, both in prose and stanzaic Classical Andalusi poems (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muwashahat" title="Muwashahat">muwashahat</a></i>), and then, from the 11th century on, in stanzaic dialectal poems (<i>zajal</i>) and dialectal proverb collections, while its last documents are a few business records and one letter written at the beginning of the 17th century in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia,_Spain" title="Valencia, Spain">Valencia</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_BRILL"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andalusian_Arabic#endnote_BRILL">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features_of_Andalusian_Arabic">Features of Andalusian Arabic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Arabic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features of Andalusian Arabic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many features of Andalusian Arabic have been reconstructed by Arabists using Hispano-Arabic texts (such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Zajal" title="Zajal">azjâl</a></i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Quzm%C3%A2n" title="Ibn Quzmân">Ibn Quzmân</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_al-Shushtari" title="Abu al-Hasan al-Shushtari">Shushtari</a> and others) composed in Arabic with varying degrees of deviation from classical norms, augmented by further information from the manner in which the Arabic script was used to transliterate Romance words. Such features include the following.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Arabic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The phoneme represented by the letter ق in texts is a point of contention. The letter, which in Classical Arabic represented either a voiceless pharyngealized velar stop or a voiceless uvular stop, most likely represented some kind of post-alveolar affricate or velar plosive in Andalusian Arabic.</p>
<p>The vowel system was subject to a heavy amount of fronting and raising, a phenomenon known as <i><a href="/wiki/Im%C4%81la" title="Imāla">imāla</a></i>, causing <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a(ː)/</span> to be raised, probably to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid front unrounded vowel">ɛ</a>]</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">e</a>]</span> and, particularly with short vowels, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front unrounded vowel">ɪ</a>]</span> in certain circumstances, particularly when i-mutation was possible.</p>
<p>Contact with native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance speakers</a> led to the introduction of the phonemes <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_stop" title="Voiceless bilabial stop">p</a>/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_stop" title="Voiced velar stop">ɡ</a>/</span> and, possibly, the affricate <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate">tʃ</a>/</span> from borrowed words.</p>
<p>Monophthongization led to the disappearance of certain diphthongs such as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aw/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aj/</span> which were leveled to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">oː</a>/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">eː</a>/</span>, respectively, though Colin hypothesizes that these diphthongs remained in the more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesolect" title="Mesolect">mesolectal</a> registers influenced by the Classical language.</p>
<p>There was a fair amount of compensatory lengthening involved where a loss of consonantal gemination lengthened the preceding vowel, whence the transformation of <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">عشّ</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʕuʃ(ʃ)/</span> ("nest") into <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">عوش</span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʕuːʃ/</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax_and_morphology">Syntax and morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Arabic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Syntax and morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">-an</span></i> which, in Classical Arabic, marked a noun as indefinite accusative (see <a href="/wiki/Nunation" title="Nunation">nunation</a>), became an indeclinable conjunctive particle, as in Ibn Quzmân's expression <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">rajul-an 'ashîq</span></i>.</p>
<p>The unconjugated prepositive negative particle <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">lis</span></i> developed out of the classical verb <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">lays-a</span></i>.</p>
<p>The derivational morphology of the verbal system was substantially altered. Whence the initial <i>n-</i> on verbs in the first person singular, a feature shared by many <a href="/wiki/Maghrebi_Arabic" title="Maghrebi Arabic">Maghrebi dialects</a>. Likewise the form V pattern of <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">tafaʻʻal-a</span></i> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">تَفَعَّلَ</span>) was altered by epenthesis to <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">atfa``al</span></i> (<span lang="xaa" xml:lang="xaa">أتْفَعَّل</span>).</p>
<p>Andalusian Arabic developed a contingent/subjunctive tense (after a protasis with the conditional particle <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">law</span></i><span style="margin-left:1px">)</span> consisting of the imperfect (prefix) form of a verb, preceded by either <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">kân</span></i> or <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">kîn</span></i> (depending on the register of the speech in question), of which the final <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">-n</span></i> was normally assimilated by preformatives <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">y-</span></i> and <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Andalusian Arabic transliteration">t-</span></i>. An example drawn from Ibn Quzmân will illustrate this:</p>
