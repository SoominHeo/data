<p><a href="/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra">Andorra</a> has a monetary agreement with the EU allowing it to make the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> its official currency, and permitting it to issue <a href="/wiki/Euro_coins" title="Euro coins">euro coins</a> as early as 1 July 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MA-2">[1]</a></sup> They planned to issue their first coins by March or April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marchapril_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-marchapril-3">[2]</a></sup> On 23 December coins were delivered for pre-booked customers at the Government Administration Building, while actual circulation started on 15 January 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andorra_and_the_euro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andorra did not have an official currency prior to adopting the euro, and unlike its two larger neighbours, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, which surround it, it is not a member of the EU. However it <i>de facto</i> used the EU's <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> (the currency of the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">Eurozone</a> states) as it had historically used the <a href="/wiki/French_franc" title="French franc">French franc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_peseta" title="Spanish peseta">Spanish peseta</a>. When those two currencies were replaced by the euro in 2002, the euro replaced the franc and peseta as the sole circulating currency in Andorra.</p>
<p>Unlike the three other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_microstates" title="European microstates">European microstates</a> outside of the EU, <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">San Marino</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>, which adopted the euro when it was introduced, Andorra did not conclude a monetary agreement with the EU but rather used it unilaterally. These agreements gave the three microstates the right to issue their own <a href="/wiki/Euro_coins" title="Euro coins">euro coins</a>, which have a common design on one side, and a national-specific side on the other. As with coins minted in other eurozone states, the microstate coins are valid across the eurozone; however they do not gain representation on the euro's governing bodies, the <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">European Central Bank</a> (ECB) and the <a href="/wiki/Eurogroup" title="Eurogroup">Eurogroup</a>. In 2003 Andorra requested that the EU conclude a monetary agreement with it which would give it the right to mint its own coins.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Currency_agreements">Currency agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andorra_and_the_euro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Currency agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004 the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Council of the European Union">Council of the European Union</a> adopted its negotiating position with Andorra. Following Andorra's agreement to abide by Council Directive 2003/48/EC on taxation of savings income in the form of interest payments, the Commission recommended opening negotiations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Negotiations were expected to be concluded by 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> but were repeatedly stalled due in part been to poor relations stemming from Andorra's <a href="/wiki/Tax_haven" title="Tax haven">tax haven</a> status. A monetary agreement was eventually agreed to by Andorra and the EU in February 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> and the agreement was signed on 30 June 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MA-2">[1]</a></sup> After the agreement came into force on 1 April 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> the euro became Andorra's official currency. Andorra would have been permitted to issue up to 2.4 million euro coins from 1 July 2013 onwards provided that it complied with the agreement's terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2012, Jordi Cinca, Andorra's Minister of Finance, stated that 1 January 2014 was a more likely date to start issuing euros due to delays in adopting the legislation required by the monetary agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DELAY2014_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-DELAY2014-12">[11]</a></sup> In February 2013, the Director of the Mint of Andorra Jordi Puigdemasa confirmed that Andorra would not begin issuing euros until 1 January 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-design_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-design-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amisdeleuro_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-amisdeleuro-14">[13]</a></sup> However, the EU did not approve the minting of the coins until December 2013, thus their release was delayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Q1-15">[14]</a></sup> Minister of Culture Stephen Albert was optimistic that the coins would be circulating by March or April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marchapril_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-marchapril-3">[2]</a></sup> By May, with still no euros issued, Cinca said that they had again been delayed but would be in circulation by the end of 2014. He cited complications from having the minting of the coins being split between the French and Spanish mints, and efforts to ensure that the coins made it into circulation, rather than to collectors, for the delay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> In July, the French mint said that the coins had been minted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> On 15 January 2015 the 10, 20 and 50 cent coins went into circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andorra_and_the_euro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A design competition for the national side of the euro coins was launched on 19 March 2013, with a deadline of 16 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-design_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-design-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> The winning designs were announced on 16 May and depict a <a href="/wiki/Pyrenean_chamois" title="Pyrenean chamois">pyrenean chamois</a> on the 1, 2 and 5 cent pieces, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_Santa_Coloma" title="Church of Santa Coloma">Church of Santa Coloma</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Christ_Pantocrator" title="Christ Pantocrator">depiction of Christ</a> from the church <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sant_Mart%C3%AD_de_la_Cortinada" title="Sant Martí de la Cortinada">Sant Martí de la Cortinada</a> on the 10, 20, and 50 cent pieces, and <a href="/wiki/Casa_de_la_Vall" title="Casa de la Vall">Casa de la Vall</a> on the 1 euro piece. The government had previously decided that the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Andorra" title="Coat of arms of Andorra">Coat of arms of Andorra</a> would be featured on the 2 euro piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-win_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-win-21">[20]</a></sup> Final approval of the coins is expected in late June, at which point they will be forwarded to the EU for their consent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-win_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-win-21">[20]</a></sup> In August, a spokesperson for Cinca confirmed that the design of the 10, 20 and 50 cent pieces had been modified to remove the depiction of Christ due to objections from the European Commission on the grounds of religious neutrality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2 euro, fifty, twenty and ten euro cent coins were produced by <a href="/wiki/Monnaie_de_Paris" title="Monnaie de Paris">Monnaie de Paris</a>, while the 1 euro, five, two and one euro cent coins were manufactured at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mint_(Spain)" title="Royal Mint (Spain)">Royal Mint of Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Q1-15">[14]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-status-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-status_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> is the subject of a territorial dispute between the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Republic of Kosovo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Republic of Serbia</a>. The Republic of Kosovo <a href="/wiki/2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence" title="2008 Kosovo declaration of independence">unilaterally declared independence</a> on 17 February 2008, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbia%27s_reaction_to_the_2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence" title="Serbia's reaction to the 2008 Kosovo declaration of independence">Serbia continues to claim</a> it as part of its <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Province_of_Kosovo_and_Metohija" title="Autonomous Province of Kosovo and Metohija">own sovereign territory</a>. The two governments <a href="/wiki/Belgrade%E2%80%93Pristina_negotiations" title="Belgrade–Pristina negotiations">began to normalise relations</a> in 2013, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Agreement_(2013)" title="Brussels Agreement (2013)">Brussels Agreement</a>. Kosovo has received recognition as an independent state from <a href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_Kosovo" title="International recognition of Kosovo">110</a> out of 193 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">member states</a>.</span></li>
</ol>
