<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Andromeda–Milky Way collision</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Andromeda–Milky Way collision</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Interacting_galaxy" title="Interacting galaxy">galactic collision</a> predicted to occur in about 4 billion years between the two largest <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> (which contains the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sohn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muir_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-muir-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> although the stars involved are sufficiently far apart that it is improbable that any of them will individually collide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">[5]</a></sup> However some stars will be ejected from the resulting galaxy, nicknamed <i>Milkomeda</i> or <i>Milkdromeda</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_collisions">Stellar collisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stellar collisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the Andromeda Galaxy contains about 1 trillion (10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">12</span></sup>) <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> and the Milky Way contains about 300 billion (3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">11</span></sup>), the chance of even two stars colliding is negligible because of the huge distances between the stars. For example, the nearest star to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">Proxima Centauri</a>, about 4.2 light-years (4.0<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>13</sup> km; 2.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>13</sup> mi) or 30 million (3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">7</span></sup>) solar diameters away. If the Sun were a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ping-pong_ball" title="Ping-pong ball">ping-pong ball</a>, Proxima Centauri would be a pea about 1,100 km (680 mi) away, and the Milky Way would be about 30 million km (19 million mi) wide. Although stars are more common near the centres of each galaxy, the average distance between stars is still 160 billion (1.6<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">11</span></sup>) km (100 billion mi). That is analogous to one ping-pong ball every 3.2 km (2.0 mi). Thus, it is extremely unlikely that any two stars from the merging galaxies would collide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_hole_collisions">Black hole collisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Black hole collisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Milky Way and Andromeda galaxies each contain a central <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a>, these being <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_A*" title="Sagittarius A*">Sagittarius A*</a> (ca. <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7036715878000000000♠</span>3.6<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>6</sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass"><i>M</i><sub>☉</sub></a></span>) and an object within the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy#Nucleus" title="Andromeda Galaxy">P2 concentration of Andromeda's nucleus</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7038397709999999999♠</span>1–2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>8</sup> <i>M</i><sub>☉</sub></span>). These black holes will converge near the center of the newly formed galaxy, transferring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_energy" title="Orbital energy">orbital energy</a> to stars that will be moved to higher orbits by gravitationally interacting with them, in a process that may take millions of years. When they come within one light year of one another, they will emit <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">gravitational waves</a> that will radiate further orbital energy until they merge completely. Gas taken up by the combined black hole could create a luminous <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasar</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nucleus</a>. As of 2006, simulations indicated that the Sun might be brought near the center of the combined galaxy, potentially coming near one of the black holes before being ejected entirely out of the galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Certainty">Certainty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Certainty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1280" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg/300px-Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg/450px-Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg/600px-Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andromeda_Collides_Milky_Way.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Based on data from the Hubble Space Telescope, Milky Way galaxy and Andromeda galaxy are predicted to distort each other with tidal pull in 3.75 billion years, as shown in this illustration.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Andromeda Galaxy is approaching the Milky Way at about 110 kilometres per second (68 mi/s)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature1-2">[2]</a></sup> as indicated by <a href="/wiki/Blueshift" title="Blueshift">blueshift</a>. However, the lateral velocity is very difficult to measure with a precision to draw reasonable conclusions: a lateral speed of only 7.7 km/s would mean that the Andromeda Galaxy is moving toward a point 177800 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> to the side of the Milky Way ((7.7 km/s) / (110 km/s) × (2 540 000 ly)), and such a speed over an 8-year time frame amounts to only 1/3000 of a <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> pixel (Hubble's resolution≈0.05 arcsec: (7.7 km/s)/(300 000 km/s)×(8 y)/(2 540 000 ly)×180°/π×3600 = 0.000 017 arcsec). Until 2012, it was not known whether the possible collision was definitely going to happen or not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanderMarel2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanderMarel2012-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2012, researchers concluded that the collision is sure using Hubble to track the motion of stars in Andromeda between 2002 and 2010 with sub-pixel accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sohn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sohn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nature1-2">[2]</a></sup> Andromeda's tangential or sideways velocity with respect to the Milky Way was found to be much smaller than the speed of approach and therefore it is expected that it will directly collide with the Milky Way in around 4 billion years.</p>
<p>Such collisions are relatively common. Andromeda, for example, is believed to have collided with at least one other galaxy in the past,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and several <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy" title="Dwarf galaxy">dwarf galaxies</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_Dwarf_Spheroidal_Galaxy" title="Sagittarius Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxy">Sgr dSph</a> are currently colliding with the Milky Way and being merged into it.</p>
<p>These studies also suggest that M33, the <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy" title="Triangulum Galaxy">Triangulum Galaxy</a>—the third largest and brightest galaxy of the Local Group—will participate in this event too. Its most likely fate is to end up orbiting the merger remnant of the Milky Way and Andromeda galaxies and finally to merge with it in an even further future, but a collision with the Milky Way before it collides with the Andromeda Galaxy or being ejected from the Local Group cannot be ruled out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanderMarel2012_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-vanderMarel2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate_of_the_Solar_System">Fate of the Solar System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fate of the Solar System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System#Galactic_collision_and_planetary_disruption" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">Formation and evolution of the Solar System § Galactic collision and planetary disruption</a></div>
<p>Two scientists with the <a href="/wiki/Harvard%E2%80%93Smithsonian_Center_for_Astrophysics" title="Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics">Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics</a> stated that when, and even whether, the two galaxies collide will depend on Andromeda's transverse velocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muir_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-muir-3">[3]</a></sup> Based on current calculations they predict a 50% chance that in a merged galaxy, the Solar System will be swept out three times farther from the galactic core than its current distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muir_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-muir-3">[3]</a></sup> They also predict a 12% chance that the Solar System will be ejected from the new galaxy sometime during the collision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cain_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cain-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cox2008-10">[10]</a></sup> Such an event would have no adverse effect on the system and the chances of any sort of disturbance to the Sun or planets themselves may be remote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cain_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cain-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox2008_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cox2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Excluding <a href="/wiki/Planetary_engineering" title="Planetary engineering">planetary engineering</a>, by the time the two galaxies collide the surface of the Earth will have already become far too hot for liquid water to exist, ending all terrestrial life; that is currently estimated to occur in about 3.75 billion years due to gradually increasing <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">luminosity of the Sun</a> (it will have risen by 35–40% above the current luminosity).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schroeder_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_triggered_stellar_events">Possible triggered stellar events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Possible triggered stellar events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When two spiral galaxies collide, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> present on their disks is compressed producing <a href="/wiki/Starburst_galaxy" title="Starburst galaxy">strong star formation</a> as can be seen on interacting systems like the <a href="/wiki/Antennae_Galaxies" title="Antennae Galaxies">Antennae Galaxies</a>. In the case of the Andromeda–Milky Way collision, it is believed that there will be little gas remaining in the disks of both galaxies, so the mentioned starburst will be relatively weak, though it still may be enough to form a quasar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox2008_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cox2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_remnant">Merger remnant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Merger remnant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The galaxy product of the collision has been nicknamed <i>Milkomeda</i> or <i>Milkdromeda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to simulations, this object will look like a giant <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxy</a>, but with a center showing less stellar density than current elliptical galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox2008_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cox2008-10">[10]</a></sup> It is however possible the resulting object will be a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disk_galaxy" title="Disk galaxy">disk galaxy</a>, depending on the amount of remaining gas in the Milky Way and Andromeda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ueda2014_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ueda2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_far_future" title="Timeline of the far future">In the far future</a>, roughly 150 billion years from now, the remaining galaxies of the Local Group will coalesce into this object, that being the next evolutionary stage of the local group of galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
