<p><i><b>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</b></i> (retitled <i><b>Blade Runner: Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</b></i> in some later printings) is a <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> novel by American writer <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a>. First published in 1968, the novel is set in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-apocalyptic" title="Post-apocalyptic">post-apocalyptic</a> San Francisco, where Earth's life has been greatly damaged by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_war" title="Nuclear war">nuclear</a> <a href="/wiki/World_War_III" title="World War III">global war</a>. Most animal species are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">endangered</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> from extreme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">radiation poisoning</a>, so that owning an animal is now a sign of <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">status</a> and empathy, an attitude encouraged towards animals. The book served as the primary basis for the 1982 film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>.</p>
<p>The main plot follows <a href="/wiki/Rick_Deckard" title="Rick Deckard">Rick Deckard</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bounty_hunter" title="Bounty hunter">bounty hunter</a> who is faced with killing ("retiring") six escaped Nexus-6 model <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">androids</a>, while a secondary plot follows John Isidore, a man of sub-par IQ who aids the fugitive androids. In connection with Deckard's mission, the novel explores the issue of what it is to be human. Unlike humans, the androids are claimed to possess no sense of empathy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-apocalyptic" title="Post-apocalyptic">post-apocalyptic</a> 1992 (or, in later editions, 2021),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> after "World War Terminus," the Earth's dust-irradiated atmosphere leads the United Nations to encourage mass emigrations to <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">off-world colonies</a> to preserve humanity's genetic integrity, with the incentive of free personal <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">androids</a>: robot servants identical to humans. On Earth, owning real live animals is a <a href="/wiki/Status_symbol" title="Status symbol">status symbol</a>, because of mass extinctions and the accompanying cultural push for greater empathy that has motivated a technology-based religion called Mercerism. Poor people can only afford realistic-looking electric animals, including Deckard, who owns a robotic <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Blackface" title="Scottish Blackface">black-faced sheep</a>. Mercerism uses "empathy boxes" to simultaneously link users to a collective <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> of communal suffering, centered on a martyr-like character, Wilbur Mercer, who eternally climbs up a hill while being hit with crashing stones.</p>
<p>The story also contains passing mention to "Penfield mood organs", which fill the role that mind-altering drugs take in other Dick stories. The mood organ can induce any desired mood in the people nearby, such as "an optimistic business-like attitude" or "the desire to watch television, no matter what is on". A slightly ironic passage in the opening chapter has Deckard and his wife, Iran, discussing what settings to use to start the day. She announces that she has scheduled six hours of "existential despair" for later in order to deal with their loneliness in an almost-deserted apartment building.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bounty hunter Rick Deckard signs on to a new police mission to earn enough money to buy a live animal to replace his electric sheep, seeking greater existential fulfillment for himself and his depressed wife, Iran. The mission involves hunting down ("retiring") a group of six Nexus-6 androids that violently went rogue and fled from Mars to Earth after their creation by the Rosen Association. Deckard visits Rosen headquarters in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle,_Washington" title="Seattle, Washington">Seattle</a> to confirm the validity of a question-and-answer empathy test: a method for identifying any androids posing as humans. Deckard is greeted by Rachael Rosen, who quickly fails his test. Rachael attempts to bribe Deckard into silence, but he verifies that she is indeed a Nexus-6 android used by Rosen to try to discredit the test.</p>
<p>Deckard soon meets a Soviet police contact, who turns out to be one of the disguised Nexus-6 renegades. Deckard retires the android, then flies off to retire his next target: Luba Luft, an opera-singing Nexus-6. This android, however, has him arrested by a police officer he has never met and detained at a police department he has never known. At this strange police station, Deckard's worldview is shaken when an official named Garland accuses Deckard of being an android. After a series of mysterious revelations at the police station, Deckard ponders the <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_artificial_intelligence" title="Ethics of artificial intelligence">ethical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_artificial_intelligence" title="Philosophy of artificial intelligence">philosophical questions</a> his line of work raises regarding android intelligence, empathy, and what it really means to be human. Phil Resch, the station's bounty hunter, finally gets testing equipment to determine if his coworkers as well as Deckard are androids or humans. Finally, Garland reveals that the entire station is a sham, completely staffed by androids, including Garland himself. Resch shoots Garland in the head and escapes with Deckard; together, they find and arrest the android opera singer, which Resch suddenly, brutally retires in cold blood. Although Resch and Deckard are now collaborating, each still worries that the other, or himself, might be an android. Deckard administers the empathy test to himself and Resch, which confirms that Resch is a particularly ruthless human being, and that Deckard is also human, but with empathy for androids.</p>
<p><span id="Isidore"></span>Only three of the Nexus-6 android fugitives remain, and one, Pris Stratton, moves in to an apartment building whose only other inhabitant is John R. Isidore, a radioactively damaged, intellectually slow human classified as a "special." The lonely Isidore attempts to befriend her. Roy and Irmgard Baty, the final two rogue androids, visit the building, and they all together plan how to survive. Meanwhile, Deckard with his reward money buys Iran an authentic <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Nubian_goat" title="Anglo-Nubian goat">Nubian goat</a>. After quitting work, Deckard is pulled back in after being notified of a new lead and experiencing a vision of the prophet-like Mercer confusingly telling him to proceed though his mission is immoral. Deckard calls back upon Rachael Rosen, since her own <a href="/wiki/Insider" title="Insider">insider</a> knowledge as an android will aid his investigation. Rachael reveals that she and Pris are the same exact model, meaning that he will have to shoot down an android that looks just like her. Rachael coaxes Deckard to have sex, after which they mutually confess their love, but she reveals she has slept with multiple other bounty hunters in order to dissuade them from their missions. After threatening to kill her, Deckard abruptly deserts her.</p>
<p>Isidore develops friendships with the three android fugitives, and they all watch a television program giving definitive evidence that Mercerism is a hoax. Roy Baty tells Isidore that the show was produced by androids to discredit Mercerism and blur the distinction with humans. Suddenly Deckard enters the building, with supernatural warning visions of Mercer appearing to both him and Isidore. Deckard becomes legally justified in shooting down all three androids without testing them, since they attack him first, and he successfully does so. Isidore is devastated, and Deckard is soon rewarded for a record number of Nexus-6 kills in one day. When Deckard returns home, he finds Iran grieving because Rachael Rosen recently showed up and murdered their goat.</p>
<p>Deckard goes to an uninhabited, obliterated region of Oregon to reflect. He climbs a hill when he is hit by falling rocks and realizes this is an experience similar to Mercer's. Rushing back to his car, he stumbles abruptly upon a <a href="/wiki/Toad" title="Toad">toad</a>, an animal previously thought to be extinct, and one of the animals sacred to Mercer himself. With newfound joy, Deckard brings the toad home, where Iran quickly discovers that it is just a robot. While Deckard is unhappy, he decides that he at least prefers to know the truth: whether the toad is real or artificial, remarking with an exhausted reflection upon the events that have taken place that "The electrical things have their lives too, paltry as those lives are".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></div>
<p>In 1982, Hampton Fancher and David Peoples' loose cinematic adaptation became the film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>, which was directed by <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>. Following the international success of the film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the title <i>Blade Runner</i> was also used for some later editions of the novel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As part of their <i>Dangerous Visions</i> dystopia series, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> broadcast a two-part adaptation of the novel. It was produced and directed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sasha_Yevtushenko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sasha Yevtushenko (page does not exist)">Sasha Yevtushenko</a> from an adaption by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Holloway_(playwright)" title="Jonathan Holloway (playwright)">Jonathan Holloway</a>. It stars <a href="/wiki/James_Purefoy" title="James Purefoy">James Purefoy</a> as Rick Deckard and <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Raine" title="Jessica Raine">Jessica Raine</a> as Rachael Rosen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The episodes were originally broadcast on Sunday 15 June and 22 June 2014.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audiobook">Audiobook</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Audiobook">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The novel has been released in <a href="/wiki/Audiobook" title="Audiobook">audiobook</a> form at least twice. A version was released in 1994 that featured <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Modine" title="Matthew Modine">Matthew Modine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calista_Flockhart" title="Calista Flockhart">Calista Flockhart</a>.</p>
<p>A new audiobook version was released in 2007 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_House_Audio" title="Random House Audio">Random House Audio</a> to coincide with the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner#Versions" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner: The Final Cut</a></i>. This version, read by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Brick" title="Scott Brick">Scott Brick</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Abridgement" title="Abridgement">unabridged</a> and runs approximately 9.5 hours over eight CDs. This version is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_tie-in_(book)" title="Movie tie-in (book)">tie-in</a>, using the <i>Blade Runner: The Final Cut</i> film poster and <i>Blade Runner</i> title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theater">Theater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Theater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A stage adaptation of the book, written by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Einhorn" title="Edward Einhorn">Edward Einhorn</a>, ran from November 18 to December 10, 2010 at the 3LD Art &amp; Technology Center in New York<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and made its West Coast Premiere on September 13, playing until October 10, 2013 at the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Fools_Theater_Company" title="Sacred Fools Theater Company">Sacred Fools Theater Company</a> in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_book">Comic book</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comic book">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BOOM!_Studios" title="BOOM! Studios">BOOM! Studios</a> published a 24-issue <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic book</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limited_series" title="Limited series">limited series</a> based on <i>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</i> containing the full text of the novel illustrated by artist Tony Parker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comics_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-comics-8">[8]</a></sup> The comic garnered a nomination for "Best New Series" from the 2010 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eisner_Awards" title="Eisner Awards">Eisner Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prequel_Comic_book">Prequel Comic book</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prequel Comic book">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In May 2010 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BOOM!_Studios" title="BOOM! Studios">BOOM! Studios</a> began serializing an eight issue prequel subtitled <i>Dust To Dust</i> and written by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Roberson_(author)" title="Chris Roberson (author)">Chris Roberson</a> and drawn by Robert Adler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The story took place in the days immediately after <i>World War Terminus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequels">Sequels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sequels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three novels intended to serve as sequels to both <i>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</i> and <i>Blade Runner</i> have been published:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_2:_The_Edge_of_Human" title="Blade Runner 2: The Edge of Human">Blade Runner 2: The Edge of Human</a></i> (1995),</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_3:_Replicant_Night" title="Blade Runner 3: Replicant Night">Blade Runner 3: Replicant Night</a></i> (1996),</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner_4:_Eye_and_Talon" title="Blade Runner 4: Eye and Talon">Blade Runner 4: Eye and Talon</a></i> (2000).</li>
</ul>
<p>These official and authorized sequels were written by Dick's friend <a href="/wiki/K._W._Jeter" title="K. W. Jeter">K. W. Jeter</a>. They continue the story of Rick Deckard and attempt to reconcile many of the differences between the novel and the film.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Do_Androids_Dream_of_Electric_Sheep%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critical reception of <i>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</i> has been overshadowed by its 1982 Ridley Scott film adaptation, <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>. Of those critics who focus on the novel, several nest it predominantly in the history of <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a>'s body of work. In particular, Dick's 1972 speech "The Human and the Android" is cited in this connection. Jill Galvan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> calls attention to the correspondence between Dick's portrayal of the narrative's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dystopian" title="Dystopian">dystopian</a>, polluted, man-made setting and the description Dick gives in his speech of the increasingly artificial and potentially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentient" title="Sentient">sentient</a> or "quasi-alive" environment of his present. The essential point in Dick's speech, for Galvan, is that "[o]nly by recognizing how [technology] has encroached upon our understanding of 'life' can we come to full terms with the technologies we have produced" (414). As a "<a href="/wiki/Bildungsroman" title="Bildungsroman">bildungsroman</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cybernetic" title="Cybernetic">cybernetic</a> age," Galvan maintains, <i>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</i> follows one person's gradual acceptance of the new reality. Christopher Palmer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> emphasizes Dick's speech to bring to attention the increasingly dangerous risk of humans becoming "mechanical".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_259_on_20160812013804_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_259_on_20160812013804-14">[14]</a></sup> "Androids threaten reduction of what makes life valuable, yet promise expansion or redefinition of it, and so do aliens and gods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_259_on_20160812013804_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_259_on_20160812013804-14">[14]</a></sup> Gregg Rickman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> cites another, earlier and lesser known Dick novel that also deals with androids, <i><a href="/wiki/We_Can_Build_You" title="We Can Build You">We Can Build You</a></i>, asserting that <i>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</i> can be read as a sequel.</p>
<p>In a departure from the tendency among most critics to examine the novel in relation to other texts by Dick, Klaus Benesch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> examined <i>Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?</i> primarily in connection with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacan" title="Lacan">Lacan's</a> essay on the mirror stage. There, Lacan claims that the formation and reassurance of the self depends on the construction of an Other through imagery, beginning with a double as seen in the mirror. The androids, Benesch argues, perform a doubling function similar to the mirror image of the self, but they do this on a social, not individual, scale. Therefore, human anxiety about androids expresses uncertainty about human identity and society. Benesch draws on Kathleen Woodward's<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> emphasis on the body to illustrate the shape of human anxiety about an android <a href="/wiki/Other" title="Other">Other</a>. The debate over distinctions between human and machine, Woodward asserts, usually fail to acknowledge the presence of the body. "If machines are invariably contrived as technological prostheses that are designed to amplify the physical faculties of the body, they are also built, according to this logic, to outdo, to surpass the human in the sphere of physicality altogether".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
