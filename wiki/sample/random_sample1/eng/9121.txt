<p><b>Andry Rajoelina</b> (Malagasy: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Malagasy" title="Help:IPA for Malagasy">[ˈjanɖʐʲ nʲˈrin<sup>ə</sup> radzoˈel]</a></span>) (born 30 May 1974) was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Madagascar" title="List of Presidents of Madagascar">President</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Transitional_Authority_(Madagascar)" title="High Transitional Authority (Madagascar)">High Transitional Authority</a> of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. He became president on 21 March 2009 during a <a href="/wiki/2009_Malagasy_political_crisis" title="2009 Malagasy political crisis">political crisis</a>, having held the office of Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Antananarivo" title="Antananarivo">Antananarivo</a> for one year prior, and stepped down on 25 January 2014 following internationally recognized <a href="/wiki/Malagasy_general_election,_2013" title="Malagasy general election, 2013">general elections</a> held in 2013. Before entering the political arena, Rajoelina launched several successful enterprises, including a printing and advertising company called Injet in 1999 and the Viva radio and television networks in 2007. He began his career as an entrepreneur in his teenage years, first as a disc jockey at local clubs and parties, and later by organizing and promoting musical events in the capital.</p>
<p>Upon rising to power, Rajoelina dissolved the Senate and National Assembly and transferred their powers to a variety of new governance structures he made responsible for overseeing the transition toward a new constitutional authority. These administrative structures repeatedly conflicted with the internationally mediated process to establish a transitional government of consensus. Voters approved a new constitution in a <a href="/wiki/Malagasy_constitutional_referendum,_2010" title="Malagasy constitutional referendum, 2010">national referendum</a> unilaterally organized by the Rajoelina administration in November 2010, ushering in the Fourth Republic and putting in place the conditions enabling Rajoelina to stand in the next <a href="/wiki/Malagasy_general_election,_2013" title="Malagasy general election, 2013">general election</a>. In January 2013 he announced his decision to abstain from running in the 2013 general election, but in May 2013 he reversed this decision and submitted his candidature. A special electoral court ruled in August 2013 that his candidature was invalid and that Rajoelina would not be permitted to run in the 2013 election. He has declared an interest in presenting himself as a presidential candidate in a future election.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andry Rajoelina was born on 30 May 1974 to a relatively wealthy family in Antananarivo. His father, now-retired Colonel Roger Yves Rajoelina, held dual nationality and fought for the French army in the <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">Algerian War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-3">[3]</a></sup> At the age of 13, Rajoelina became the 1987 junior-class national karate champion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MadTribuneBio_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MadTribuneBio-4">[4]</a></sup> While still in high school, Rajoelina began working as a disc jockey at parties and clubs in Antananarivo to earn pocket money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-repression_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-repression-5">[5]</a></sup> Although Rajoelina's family could afford a college education for their son, he opted to discontinue his studies after completing his <i>baccalaureat</i> to launch a career as an entrepreneur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, Rajoelina met his future spouse <a href="/wiki/Mialy_Rajoelina" title="Mialy Rajoelina">Mialy Razakandisa</a>, who was then completing her senior year at a high school in Antananarivo. The couple courted long-distance for six years while Mialy completed her undergraduate and masters studies in finance and accounting in Paris; they were reunited in Madagascar in 2000 and wed the same year. Their marriage produced two boys, Arena (born 2002) and Ilonstoa (born 2005), and a daughter born in 2007 that the couple named Andrialy, a contraction of their own names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mialy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mialy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_entrepreneur">Media entrepreneur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Media entrepreneur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1993, at the age of 19, Rajoelina established his first enterprise: a small event production company called <i>Show Business.</i> The connections and influence of his father, then a prominent military officer, in combination with Rajoelina's own knowledge of Antananarivo nightlife, were factors in the company's success. By the following year, he had firmly established his reputation among the youth elite of the capital for organizing an annual concert called <i>Live</i> that brought together foreign and <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Madagascar" title="Music of Madagascar">Malagasy musical artists</a>. The event continued to grow in popularity with each passing year, attracting 50,000 participants on its tenth anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-repression_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-repression-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1999, he launched Injet, an advertising and digital printing company that quickly grew in prominence with its expansion of billboard advertising throughout the capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-repression_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-repression-5">[5]</a></sup> The business was the first to make digital printing technology available on the island. Local magazine <i>Echo Australe</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MadTribuneBio_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MadTribuneBio-4">[4]</a></sup> which named then-mayor of Antananarivo <a href="/wiki/Marc_Ravalomanana" title="Marc Ravalomanana">Marc Ravalomanana</a> their Entrepreneur of the Year in 1999, bestowed the same honor on Rajoelina in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-3">[3]</a></sup> Following Rajoelina's marriage in 2000, his wife's wealthy parents invested in Injet and allowed Rajoelina in 2001 to acquire Domapub, a competing Antananarivo-based billboard advertising business that they owned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intelligence_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-intelligence-7">[7]</a></sup> The couple worked together to manage the family businesses, with Rajoelina responsible for Injet and his wife handling the affairs of Domapub.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mialy_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mialy-6">[6]</a></sup> A competition organized by French bank BNI Crédit Lyonnais awarded first prize for best young entrepreneur in Madagascar to Rajoelina in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rajoelina's business affairs gradually drew him into the political arena. Shortly after Ravalomanana won the presidential elections in 2001, Rajoelina befriended the new president's daughter and continued to expand his relationship with the capital's political elite over the course of the Ravalomanana presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-3">[3]</a></sup> He assumed an increasingly political role after the policies of Ravalomanana's <a href="/wiki/Tiako_I_Madagasikara" title="Tiako I Madagasikara">Tiako I Madagasikara</a> (TIM)-dominated government increasingly obstructed the expansion of his business activities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intelligence_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-intelligence-7">[7]</a></sup> such as requiring the removal of Antananarivo's first LED advertising panels, which Rajoelina had installed at a major roundabout in the capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turbo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-turbo-9">[9]</a></sup> In May 2007 he purchased the <i>Ravinala</i> television and radio stations, which he renamed Viva TV and Viva FM. The official launch of these newly acquired media channels on 27 May was attended by many of the capital's political and cultural elite, including Antananarivo mayor Hery Rafalimanana and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Sylla" title="Jacques Sylla">Jacques Sylla</a>, president of the TIM political party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-repression_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-repression-5">[5]</a></sup> Rajoelina used these media channels as outlets for voicing criticism of the Ravalomanana administration, quickly transforming his public image into that of an opposition leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intelligence_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-intelligence-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayor_of_Antananarivo">Mayor of Antananarivo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mayor of Antananarivo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007 Rajoelina created and led the political association <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Malagasies_Determined" title="Young Malagasies Determined">Tanora malaGasy Vonona</a></i> (TGV), meaning "determined Malagasy youth". Shortly afterward he announced his candidacy for the position of Mayor of Antananarivo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intelligence_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-intelligence-7">[7]</a></sup> Rajoelina was elected on 12 December 2007 with 63.3% of the vote and a 75% voter turnout,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> beating TIM party incumbent Hery Rafalimanana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-repression_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-repression-5">[5]</a></sup> Although Rafalimanana enjoyed strong popularity during his tenure as mayor, having been nationally and internationally credited with effectively managing the capital and achieving significant transformation of the urban landscape in the city, his campaign suffered from inadequate TIM promotional support and the increasingly unpopular image of the TIM party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rajoelina faced significant challenges in his tenure as mayor. Upon taking office, he was confronted with redressing the city's treasury, which had accumulated 8.2 billion Malagasy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ariary" title="Ariary">Ariary</a> (approximately 4.6 million U.S. dollars) in debts under previous mayors, including Ravalomanana himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Beginning 4 January 2008, Ravalomanana ordered water cutoffs at public pumps and <a href="/wiki/Brownout_(electricity)" title="Brownout (electricity)">brownouts</a> of Antananarivo's street lights run by the state utilities company <a href="/wiki/Jirama" title="Jirama">Jirama</a>, due to 3.3 million ariary of unpaid debts to Jirama by the City of Antananarivo. Rajoelina responded by condemning the move as political and proceeded to undertake an audit that identified and addressed long-standing procedural irregularities and issues of corruption within the city's administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Following a series of crimes against members of Rajoelina's inner circle in 2008 that comprised the burgling of his cabinet director's car, the kidnapping and ransoming of one of his special advisers, and the death of an assistant under mysterious circumstances, rumors spread that the Rajoelina administration was being deliberately intimidated by supporters of the president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confrontation_with_Ravalomanana">Confrontation with Ravalomanana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Confrontation with Ravalomanana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2009_Malagasy_political_crisis" title="2009 Malagasy political crisis">2009 Malagasy political crisis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg/220px-Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg/330px-Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg/440px-Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antananarivo_-_P1090823.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pro-Rajoelina protesters in Antananarivo, 2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 13 December 2008, the Government closed Andry Rajoelina's Viva TV, stating that a Viva interview with exiled former head of state <a href="/wiki/Didier_Ratsiraka" title="Didier Ratsiraka">Didier Ratsiraka</a> was "likely to disturb peace and security".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossroadsMarcus_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CrossroadsMarcus-15">[15]</a></sup> This move catalyzed the political opposition and a public already dissatisfied with other recent actions undertaken by Ravalomanana,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup1-16">[16]</a></sup> including the July 2008 deal with <a href="/wiki/Daewoo" title="Daewoo">Daewoo Logistics</a> to lease half the island's arable land for South Korean cultivation of corn and palm oil,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the November 2008 purchase of a second presidential jet at a cost of 60 million U.S. dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup1-16">[16]</a></sup> Within a week Rajoelina met with twenty of Madagascar's most prominent opposition leaders, referred to in the press as the "Club of 20", to develop a joint statement demanding that the Ravalomanana administration improve its adherence to democratic principles. The demand was broadcast at a public press conference where Rajoelina also promised to dedicate a politically open public space in the capital which he would call <i>Place de la democratie</i> ("Democracy Place").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in January 2009, Andry Rajoelina led a series of political rallies in downtown Antananarivo where he gave voice to the frustration that Ravalomanana's policies had triggered, particularly among the economically marginalized and members of the political opposition. On 17 January he gathered 30,000 supporters at a public park and declared it renamed <i>Place de la democratie</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossroadsMarcus_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-CrossroadsMarcus-15">[15]</a></sup> At a rally on 31 January 2009 Rajoelina announced that he was in charge of the entire Malagasy Republic, declaring: "Since the president and the government have not assumed their responsibilities, I therefore proclaim that I will run all national affairs as of today." He added that a request for President Ravalomanana to formally resign would shortly be filed with the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Madagascar" title="Parliament of Madagascar">Parliament of Madagascar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This self-declaration of power discredited Rajoelina in the eyes of many of the supporters who had rallied around his original pro-democracy message, and the number of attendees at subsequent rallies declined, averaging around 3,000 to 5,000 participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossroadsMarcus_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-CrossroadsMarcus-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 February, Ravalomanana dismissed Rajoelina as mayor of Antananarivo and appointed a <a href="/wiki/Special_delegation_(France)" title="Special delegation (France)">special delegation</a> headed by Guy Randrianarisoa to manage the affairs of the capital. Rajoelina denounced the decision and warned that it would not be accepted by the populace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The following day Rajoelina instead designated Michele Ratsivalaka to succeed him as mayor. Rajoelina incited demonstrators on 7 February to occupy the presidential palace, prompting the presidential guard to open fire on the advancing crowd, killing 31 and wounding more than 200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup1_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup1-16">[16]</a></sup> Popular disapproval of Ravalomanana intensified and polarized some in favor of his resignation, although perceptions of Rajoelina as an alternative remained mixed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossroadsMarcus_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-CrossroadsMarcus-15">[15]</a></sup> Conflicts between pro-Rajoelina demonstrators and security forces continued over the following weeks, resulting in several additional deaths. The security forces unsuccessfully attempted to arrest Rajoelina at his compound on 5 March; they also raided the offices of his Viva media network. Initially they surrounded Viva, and after thirty minutes the staff attempted to remove Viva property, at which point the security forces stormed the building and confiscated the equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-27_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-27-21">[21]</a></sup> For the next several days Rajoelina took refuge in the home of the French ambassador, reporting to the press that he feared for his safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refuge_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-refuge-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 11 March, following a declaration of neutrality by army leadership, pro-opposition soldiers from the CAPSAT (Army Corps of Personnel and Administrative and Technical Services) stormed the army headquarters and <a href="/wiki/2009_Camp_Capsat_mutiny" title="2009 Camp Capsat mutiny">forced the army chief of staff to resign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-32_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-32-23">[23]</a></sup> Over the next several days the army deployed forces to enable the opposition to occupy key ministries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-35_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-35-24">[24]</a></sup> the chief of military police transferred his loyalty to Rajoelina,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-37_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-37-25">[25]</a></sup> and the army sent tanks against the president's <a href="/wiki/Iavoloha_Palace" title="Iavoloha Palace">Iavoloha Palace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-38_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-38-26">[26]</a></sup> Rajoelina rejected Ravalomanana's offer on 15 March to hold a national referendum to determine whether the president should resign, and called on security forces to arrest the president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-43_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-43-27">[27]</a></sup> The following day, the army stormed the president's <a href="/wiki/Ambohitsorohitra_Palace" title="Ambohitsorohitra Palace">Ambohitsorohitra Palace</a> and captured the Central Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-44_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-44-28">[28]</a></sup> Hours later Ravalomanana transferred his power to group of senior army personnel, an act described by the opposition as a voluntary resignation, although Ravalomanana later declared he had been forced at gunpoint to relinquish power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-46_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-46-29">[29]</a></sup> The military council would have been charged with organizing elections within 24 months and re-writing the constitution for the "Fourth Republic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lough1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-lough1-30">[30]</a></sup> However, Vice Admiral Hyppolite Ramaroson announced on 18 March that it would transfer power directly to Rajoelina, making him president of the opposition-dominated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Transitional_Authority_(Madagascar)" title="High Transitional Authority (Madagascar)">High Transitional Authority</a> (HAT) that he had appointed weeks earlier. With the military's backing, the authority was charged with taking up the task previously accorded to Ravalomanana's proposed military directorate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc-2009-03-18_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-2009-03-18-31">[31]</a></sup> Madagascar's constitutional court deemed the transfer of power, from Ravalomanana to the military board and then to Rajoelina, to be legal;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aj-2009-03-19_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-aj-2009-03-19-32">[32]</a></sup> the court's statement did not include a justification for its decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc-2009-03-21_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-2009-03-21-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon the army's transfer of power to Rajoelina, Malagasy navy troops called for his resignation by 25 March, threatening to use force otherwise to protect the constitution of Madagascar and denouncing Rajoelina for the "civil war occurring in Madagascar". The navy troops claimed there was "irrefutable" evidence that Rajoelina had paid the army corps hundreds of millions of <i><a href="/wiki/Malagasy_ariary" title="Malagasy ariary">ariary</a></i> and that they should face trial in accordance with military law, but called for other nations not to get involved in what they considered a purely domestic affair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdo-2009-30-25_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdo-2009-30-25-34">[34]</a></sup> Rajoelina was sworn in as President on 21 March at <a href="/wiki/Mahamasina_Municipal_Stadium" title="Mahamasina Municipal Stadium">Mahamasina stadium</a> before a crowd of 40,000 supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gn-2009-03-21_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-gn-2009-03-21-35">[35]</a></sup> He was 35 years of age when sworn in, making him the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_heads_of_state_of_Madagascar" title="List of heads of state of Madagascar">youngest president in the country's history</a> and the youngest head of government in the world at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Southern_African_Development_Community" title="Southern African Development Community">Southern African Development Community</a>, a bloc of 15 nations including Madagascar, announced on 19 March that it would not recognize Rajoelina's presidency since the takeover was unconstitutional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VOA_News_2009-03-19_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-VOA_News_2009-03-19-37">[37]</a></sup> His ascension to the presidency was also condemned by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_2009-03-20_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_2009-03-20-38">[38]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> suspended Madagascar and threatened sanctions if the constitutional government had not been restored in six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Econ1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Econ1-39">[39]</a></sup> No foreign diplomats attended Rajoelina's investiture;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc-2009-03-21_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-2009-03-21-33">[33]</a></sup> the HAT foreign minister said none had been invited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AutoRI-50_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-AutoRI-50-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="President_of_the_High_Transitional_Authority">President of the High Transitional Authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: President of the High Transitional Authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Transitional_Authority_(Madagascar)" title="High Transitional Authority (Madagascar)">High Transitional Authority (Madagascar)</a></div>
<p>Rajoelina took power in an atmosphere of national tension and international pressure to re-establish constitutional authority. The new head of state announced on 17 March that a new constitution would be presented for voter approval and general elections would be held within 24 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_1-41">[41]</a></sup> Immediately after taking office, Rajoelina began naming opposition leaders to positions within the HAT. He dissolved the Senate and Parliament and transferred their powers to his cabinet, the officials of the HAT, and the newly established Council for social and economic strengthening, through which his policies were issued as decrees. Legislative authority rested in practice with Rajoelina and his cabinet, composed of his closest advisers. A military committee established in April increased HAT control over security and defense policy. The following month, after the suspension of the country's 22 regional governors, Rajoelina strengthened his influence over local government by naming replacements despite the fact that regional governorships were normally elected positions. The National Inquiry Commission (CNME) was established shortly thereafter; although its official purpose was to strengthen HAT effectiveness in addressing judicial and legal matters, the new entity carried out investigations and arrests of TIM supporters and other opponents of the TGV party. In addition, the new administration launched a strong crackdown on demonstrations by the new opposition, composed largely of Ravalomanana supporters; at two demonstrations in April 2009 security forces opened fire on unarmed civilians protesting the HAT's closure of three media networks formerly controlled by Ravalomanana, killing four and wounding sixty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup1_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup1-16">[16]</a></sup> Political analysts have criticized Rajoelina for using his position primarily to consolidate power and protect his political position and business interests and those of his supporters, observing that his initially broad range of supporters within the opposition movement has significantly diminished over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossroadsMarcus_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-CrossroadsMarcus-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>While in power, Rajoelina periodically engaged in ongoing negotiations to establish conditions under which free and fair elections could be held to restore government constitutionality and legitimacy in the eyes of the international community. On 4 August 2009 Rajoelina met with former Madagascar presidents Ravalomanana, <a href="/wiki/Didier_Ratsiraka" title="Didier Ratsiraka">Ratsiraka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Zafy" title="Albert Zafy">Zafy</a> along with former Mozambican President <a href="/wiki/Joaquim_Chissano" title="Joaquim Chissano">Joaquim Chissano</a> acting as mediator at the four-day <a href="/wiki/2009_Malagasy_political_crisis#Mediation_crisis_talks" title="2009 Malagasy political crisis">mediation crisis talks</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Maputo" title="Maputo">Maputo</a>, Mozambique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The Maputo Accords signed by the four leaders in August and further accords signed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Addis-Abeba" title="Addis-Abeba">Addis-Abeba</a> in November provided guidelines for a period of consensual political transition. In the months that followed, however, Rajoelina obstructed the implementation of the accords, making unilateral political decisions and filling all important government posts with TGV party representatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup1_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After being pushed back repeatedly,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nov_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nov-43">[43]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Malagasy_constitutional_referendum,_2010" title="Malagasy constitutional referendum, 2010">constitutional referendum</a> was held on 17 November 2010 that resulted in adoption of the state's fourth constitution with 73% in favor and a voter turnout of 52.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peoplesd_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-peoplesd-44">[44]</a></sup> The independent Malagasy political watchdog group KMF-CNOE,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verite_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verite-45">[45]</a></sup> as well as the political opposition and the international community, cited numerous irregularities in the process, which was carried out unilaterally by the HAT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup2_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup2-46">[46]</a></sup> The constitution in effect at the time Rajoelina took office required that presidential candidates attain a minimum of 40 years of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2009-03-19_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2009-03-19-47">[47]</a></sup> One substantive change made by the new constitution was to lower the minimum age for presidential candidates from 40 to 35, making Rajoelina eligible to stand in presidential elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In addition, the new constitution mandated the leader of the High Transitional Authority – the position held by Rajoelina – be kept as interim president until an election could take place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Economist_2010-11-18_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Economist_2010-11-18-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2010-11-21_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2010-11-21-50">[50]</a></sup> The new constitution also contained a clause that required presidential candidates to have lived in Madagascar for at least six months prior to the elections, effectively barring Ravalomanana and other opposition leaders living in exile from running in the next election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2010-11-21_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2010-11-21-50">[50]</a></sup> After being repeatedly postponed, the general election was held on 25 October 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-October_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-October-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies_and_governance">Policies and governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policies and governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The HAT under Rajoelina's leadership affected highly visible, concrete activities that appealed to popular concerns. Rajoelina occasionally organized events to distribute basic items to groups of poor recipients, including medicines, clothing, house maintenance materials and school supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Rajoelina's administration also spent billions of ariary to subsidize basic needs like electricity, petrol,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and food staples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Public works construction projects featured prominently among Rajoelina's activities. In 2010, two years after Rajoelina launched the project as mayor of Antananarivo, the HAT completed the reconstruction of the <i>Hotel de Ville</i> (town hall) of Antananarivo which had been destroyed by arson during the <i><a href="/wiki/Rotaka" title="Rotaka">rotaka</a></i> political protests of 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Also in 2010, Rajoelina oversaw the construction of a hospital built to international standards in Toamasina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toamasina_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-toamasina-56">[56]</a></sup> Through its <i>trano mora</i> ("cheap house") initiative, the HAT built several subsidized housing developments intended for young middle class couples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Numerous other construction projects were planned or completed, including the restoration of historic staircases in Antananarivo built in the 19th century during the reign of Queen <a href="/wiki/Ranavalona_I" title="Ranavalona I">Ranavalona I</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> renovation of Toamasina's two main markets and its principal boulevard, repaving of the heavily traveled road between Toamasina and Foulpointe, and the construction of a 15,000-capacity municipal stadium and new town hall in Toamasina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toamasina_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-toamasina-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the national level, sanctions and suspension of donor aid amounting to 50 percent of the national budget and 70 percent of public investments obstructed the Rajoelina administration's management of state affairs and its ability to systematically combat poverty on a wider scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> A United Nations envoy concluded in 2011 that Rajoelina's controversial accession to the presidency had reduced food security, with Madagascar ranking among the worst in the world for child nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faim_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-faim-60">[60]</a></sup> The frequent non-payment of civil servant and military salaries resulted in regular school and hospital closures, general strikes and widespread increases in corruption. Crime and insecurity grew dramatically, with a sharp upswing in violent crime and theft in Antananarivo and increasingly frequent and deadly incidences of armed cattle rustling in the central southern region of the island. Key development indicators steeply declined between 2009 and 2013, including maternal and child mortality, education enrollment and completion, per capita income and employment rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chatham_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chatham-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-putch_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-putch-62">[62]</a></sup> In 2010 the United Nations ranked Madagascar among the ten poorest countries in the world, noting that economic gains made under Ravalomanana had been lost following Rajoelina's unconstitutional rise to power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> magazine ranked Madagascar as the worst economy in the world, faulting its political instability and poor governance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Environmental conditions on the island also worsened under Rajoelina. The illegal exportation of endangered rosewood from protected national forests abruptly increased under the HAT, with several business leaders and politicians linked to the HAT having been implicated in trafficking the valuable wood to China; NGOs and the US government accused the Rajoelina administration of selling the wood to supplement the national budget and finance its public works projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chatham_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chatham-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> A large, unexplained stash of rosewood logs was discovered at the presidential palace after the end of Rajoelina's term in office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July2014_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-July2014-66">[66]</a></sup> Due to accelerating deforestation and a spike in bush meat consumption linked with deepening poverty, the island's <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemurs</a> moved closer toward extinction under Rajoelina's tenure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> A July 2012 assessment found that 90 percent of lemur species were found to be threatened with extinction, an increase from 38 percent in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lemurextinction_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-lemurextinction-68">[68]</a></sup> Lack of funds for pesticides and systematic spraying resulted in the <a href="/wiki/2013_Madagascar_locust_infestation" title="2013 Madagascar locust infestation">March 2013 outbreak</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Migratory_locust" title="Migratory locust">locust</a> infestation that was anticipated to persist for five to ten years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-69">[69]</a></sup> and require $41 million U.S. dollars to bring under control, deepening the risk of nationwide famine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBT_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBT-70">[70]</a></sup> and destruction of habitat for endangered species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>After coming to power, Rajoelina's HAT pursued action against Ravalomanana and his business interests. On 2 June 2009, Ravalomanana was fined 70 million US dollars (42 million British pounds) and sentenced to four years in prison for alleged abuse of office which, according to HAT Justice Minister Christine Razanamahasoa, included the December 2008 purchase of a second presidential jet ("Air Force II") worth $60 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madagascar_sentences_ex-president_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madagascar_sentences_ex-president-72">[72]</a></sup> Rajoelina sold the controversial jet in 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> and pursued legal action against Ravalomanana's company Tiko to reclaim 35 million US dollars in back taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TikoPressure_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-TikoPressure-74">[74]</a></sup> Additionally, on 28 August 2010, the HAT sentenced Ravalomanana <a href="/wiki/Trial_in_absentia" title="Trial in absentia"><i>in absentia</i></a> to hard labor for life and issued an arrest warrant for his role in the protests and ensuing deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Rajoelina likewise sought to distinguish himself from his predecessor in policy choices. One of Rajoelina's first measures as president was to cancel Ravalomanana's unpopular deal with Daewoo Logistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> He also rejected Ravalomanana's medium term development strategy, termed the Madagascar Action Plan, and abandoned education reforms initiated by his predecessor that adopted Malagasy and English as languages of instruction, instead returning to the traditional use of French.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chatham_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chatham-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomatic_relations">Diplomatic relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diplomatic relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The international community maintained that Rajoelina's legitimacy was conditional to free and fair elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> A number of key international organizations in which Madagascar had been a member, including the <a href="/wiki/Southern_African_Development_Community" title="Southern African Development Community">Southern African Development Community</a> (SADC) and the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>, withheld recognition of Rajoelina's legitimacy throughout his tenure. The United States, Madagascar's largest bilateral donor and provider of humanitarian aid, also refused to acknowledge the Rajoelina administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VOA_News_2009-03-19_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-VOA_News_2009-03-19-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_2009-03-20_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_2009-03-20-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Econ1_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Econ1-39">[39]</a></sup> The United Nations responded to the power transfer by freezing 600 million euros in planned aid. The United States suspended Madagascar from the list of beneficiaries of the <a href="/wiki/African_Growth_and_Opportunity_Act" title="African Growth and Opportunity Act">African Growth and Opportunity Act</a> (AGOA), which had ensured preferential tariffs for the import of Malagasy textiles. This sanction resulted in the loss of over 50,000 jobs and dealt a severe blow to the textile sector, which had accounted for half of Madagascar's exports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faim_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-faim-60">[60]</a></sup> Rajoelina initially sought to persuade former donors to recommence their support; when these efforts failed to yield results, he explored the possibility of foregoing the support of traditional partners through new or strengthened relations with such alternatives as Libya, China and Saudi Arabia but was again unable to secure the desired partnerships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crisisgroup2_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crisisgroup2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rajoelina's relations with France were characterized by an initial period of gradually increasing diplomatic engagement that weakened after 2012. On 13 May 2011, Andry Rajoelina met with <a href="/wiki/Alain_Jupp%C3%A9" title="Alain Juppé">Alain Juppé</a>, the French Minister of Foreign Affairs, and was received by the French President <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sarkozy" title="Nicolas Sarkozy">Nicolas Sarkozy</a> on 7 December 2011. According to the French presidency, "The interview was devoted to the situation in Madagascar and the completion of the political transition initiated in 2009 through the implementation of the roadmap for the return of constitutional order, validated by the international community".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Following Rajoelina's announcement to run in the 2013 election, however, France responded by suspending the visas issued to Rajoelina and his family, and refusing to issue new visas requested by his ministers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Rajoelina administration was gradually afforded increasing entry to the United Nations. From 9 to 13 May 2011, Rajoelina was invited to participate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_developed_country#Fourth_UN_Conference_on_LDCs_.28UN_LDC-IV.29" title="Least developed country">Fourth United Nations Conference on the Least Developed Countries</a>, held in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, Turkey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Rajoelina also spoke during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/66th_Session_of_the_United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="66th Session of the United Nations General Assembly">66th Session of the United Nations General Assembly</a> which took place on 23 September 2011 in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_presidential_candidacy">2013 presidential candidacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2013 presidential candidacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 15 January 2013, Rajoelina officially renounced his candidature in the next <a href="/wiki/Malagasy_general_election,_2013" title="Malagasy general election, 2013">presidential election</a>, then planned for May 2013. This decision followed a similar declaration on the part of former president Ravalomanana. Exclusion of both political figures from the next general election was a key precondition to international recognition of the elections, as defined by the <a href="/wiki/Southern_African_Development_Community" title="Southern African Development Community">Southern African Development Community</a> in its roadmap toward ending the political crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Rajoelina expressed support for Edgar Razafindravahy, interim <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Antananarivo" title="Mayor of Antananarivo">mayor of Antananarivo</a> and the official candidate of the TGV party among several other party candidates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> and declared his intention to run in the 2018 presidential election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, on 3 May 2013, Rajoelina made last-minute arrangements to stand in the 2013 presidential elections, declaring the roadmap agreement had been breached when former president Ravalomanana's wife, <a href="/wiki/Lalao_Ravalomanana" title="Lalao Ravalomanana">Lalao</a>, decided to stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The candidacy of former president Didier Ratsiraka was also permitted; the candidature of all three prominent politicians was rejected by the international community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-electiondate_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-electiondate-86">[86]</a></sup> At the time that Rajoelina submitted his candidacy, he would have run against forty other candidates in total, of whom seven were affiliated with his own political movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>The African Union urged Rajoelina, Ravalomanana and Ratsiraka to withdraw their controversial candidacies, and the European Union responded by suspended funding to the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-electiondate_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-electiondate-86">[86]</a></sup> The French government sanctioned all three candidates by revoking their French visas, effectively barring them from French territory, and considered freezing their financial and real estate assets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Following the suspension of election funding, the HAT again pushed back the election date, citing inadequate financial resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-electiondate_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-electiondate-86">[86]</a></sup> A special electoral court ruled in August 2013 that the candidatures of Rajoelina, Ravalomanana and Ratsiraka were invalid and that Rajoelina and his two chief rivals would not be permitted to run in the 2013 election. Rajoelina then announced his endorsement of presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Hery_Rajaonarimampianina" title="Hery Rajaonarimampianina">Hery Rajaonarimampianina</a>, who successfully progressed from the first round voting held 25 October 2013 alongside the candidate backed by Ravalomanana, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Louis_Robinson" title="Jean Louis Robinson">Jean Louis Robinson</a>. The run-off between the two candidates was held on 20 December 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-October_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-October-51">[51]</a></sup> Rajaonarimampianina was declared the winner of the election, and Rajoelina officially stepped down on 25 January 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July2014_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-July2014-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-presidency">Post-presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andry_Rajoelina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following Rajaonarimampianina's election, the international community offered "conditional recognition" of the new government. This tentative validation of the results was motivated by the concern that the new president was a puppet for Rajoelina, as well as the allegations by Robinson of massive electoral fraud after losing the runoff despite a wide lead over his opponent in the first round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Rajaonarimampianina set up the MAPAR committee to organize the selection of his cabinet, a process that extended over several months. During this time, Rajoelina sought to be nominated for the position of <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Madagascar" title="Prime Minister of Madagascar">Prime Minister of Madagascar</a> and claimed the MAPAR had promised he would be nominated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trahison_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-trahison-90">[90]</a></sup> In April 2014, Rajaonarimampianina instead announced the selection of <a href="/wiki/Roger_Kolo" title="Roger Kolo">Roger Kolo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> following a consultation round which showed he had support from a majority in the parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> On 18 April, a cabinet was announced that comprised 31 members with varied political affiliations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Rajoelina qualified Rajaonarimampianina's decision to exclude him and form alliances with the political opposition, including Ravalomanana's supporters, as "hypocrisy and betrayal", and has declared a refusal to seek reconciliation with Rajaonarimampianina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trahison_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-trahison-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the end of Rajoelina's term, the French government lifted its sanctions against him and 108 other HAT members. He and his family have since maintained a discreet profile, traveling to France, Dubai, South Africa and other foreign destinations. He has also made occasional visits to Madagascar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Rajoelina has declared an interest in presenting himself as a presidential candidate in the 2018 presidential election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-October_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-October-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
