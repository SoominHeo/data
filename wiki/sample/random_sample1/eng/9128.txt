<p><b>Euthanasia</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">εὐθανασία</span>; "good death": εὖ, <i>eu</i>; "well" or "good" – θάνατος, <i>thanatos</i>; "death") is the practice of intentionally ending a life in order to relieve <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffering" title="Suffering">suffering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are different <a href="/wiki/Legality_of_euthanasia" title="Legality of euthanasia">euthanasia laws in each country</a>. The British <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Select_Committee_(Westminster_System)" title="Select Committee (Westminster System)">Select Committee</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_Ethics" title="Medical Ethics">Medical Ethics</a> defines euthanasia as "a deliberate intervention undertaken with the express intention of ending a life, to relieve intractable suffering".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris-2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-2001-2">[2]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, euthanasia is understood as "termination of life by a doctor at the request of a patient".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Euthanasia is categorized in different ways, which include voluntary, non-voluntary, or involuntary. <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_euthanasia" title="Voluntary euthanasia">Voluntary euthanasia</a> is legal in some countries. <a href="/wiki/Non-voluntary_euthanasia" title="Non-voluntary euthanasia">Non-voluntary euthanasia</a> (patient's consent unavailable) is illegal in all countries. <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_euthanasia" title="Involuntary euthanasia">Involuntary euthanasia</a> (without asking consent or against the patient's will) is also illegal in all countries and is usually considered <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2006, euthanasia is the most active area of research in contemporary <a href="/wiki/Bioethics" title="Bioethics">bioethics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some countries there is a divisive public controversy over the moral, ethical, and legal issues of euthanasia. Those who are against euthanasia may argue for the sanctity of life, while proponents of euthanasia rights emphasize alleviating suffering, and preserving <a href="/wiki/Bodily_integrity" title="Bodily integrity">bodily integrity</a>, self-determination, and personal autonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Jurisdictions where euthanasia is legal include the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<p>Like other terms borrowed from history, "euthanasia" has had different meanings depending on usage. The first apparent usage of the term "euthanasia" belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a>, who described how the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, "dying quickly and without suffering in the arms of his wife, Livia, experienced the 'euthanasia' he had wished for."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The word "euthanasia" was first used in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical" title="Medical">medical</a> context by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> in the 17th century, to refer to an easy, painless, happy death, during which it was a "physician's responsibility to alleviate the 'physical sufferings' of the body." Bacon referred to an "outward euthanasia"—the term "outward" he used to distinguish from a spiritual concept—the euthanasia "which regards the preparation of the soul."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In current usage, euthanasia has been defined as the "painless inducement of a quick death".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, it is argued that this approach fails to properly define euthanasia, as it leaves open a number of possible actions which would meet the requirements of the definition, but would not be seen as euthanasia. In particular, these include situations where a person kills another, painlessly, but for no reason beyond that of personal gain; or accidental deaths that are quick and painless, but not intentional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draper1998_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Draper1998-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another approach incorporates the notion of <a href="/wiki/Suffering" title="Suffering">suffering</a> into the definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979-11">[11]</a></sup> The definition offered by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> incorporates suffering as a necessary condition, with "the painless killing of a patient suffering from an incurable and painful disease or in an irreversible coma",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This approach is included in Marvin Khol and Paul Kurtz's definition of it as "a mode or act of inducing or permitting death painlessly as a relief from suffering".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Counterexamples can be given: such definitions may encompass killing a person suffering from an incurable disease for personal gain (such as to claim an inheritance), and commentators such as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Beauchamp" title="Tom Beauchamp">Tom Beauchamp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Davidson" title="Arnold Davidson">Arnold Davidson</a> have argued that doing so would constitute "murder simpliciter" rather than euthanasia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The third element incorporated into many definitions is that of intentionality – the death must be intended, rather than being accidental, and the intent of the action must be a "merciful death".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979-11">[11]</a></sup> Michael Wreen argued that "the principal thing that distinguishes euthanasia from intentional killing simpliciter is the agent's motive: it must be a good motive insofar as the good of the person killed is concerned."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wreen1988p639_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wreen1988p639-15">[15]</a></sup> Similarly, Heather Draper speaks to the importance of motive, arguing that "the motive forms a crucial part of arguments for euthanasia, because it must be in the best interests of the person on the receiving end."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draper1998_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Draper1998-12">[12]</a></sup> Definitions such as that offered by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Select_Committee_(Westminster_System)" title="Select Committee (Westminster System)">Select Committee</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_Ethics" title="Medical Ethics">Medical Ethics</a> take this path, where euthanasia is defined as "a deliberate intervention undertaken with the express intention of ending a life, to relieve intractable suffering."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris-2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Beauchamp and Davidson also highlight <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Brody" title="Baruch Brody">Baruch Brody's</a> "an act of euthanasia is one in which one person ... (A) kills another person (B) for the benefit of the second person, who actually does benefit from being killed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Draper argued that any definition of euthanasia must incorporate four elements: an agent and a subject; an intention; a causal proximity, such that the actions of the agent lead to the outcome; and an outcome. Based on this, she offered a definition incorporating those elements, stating that euthanasia "must be defined as death that results from the intention of one person to kill another person, using the most gentle and painless means possible, that is motivated solely by the best interests of the person who dies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draper1998p176_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Draper1998p176-17">[17]</a></sup> Prior to Draper, Beauchamp and Davidson had also offered a definition that includes these elements. Their definition specifically discounts <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetuses</a> in order to distinguish between abortions and euthanasia:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979p303_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979p303-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"In summary, we have argued ... that the death of a human being, A, is an instance of euthanasia if and only if (1) A's death is intended by at least one other human being, B, where B is either the cause of death or a causally relevant feature of the event resulting in death (whether by action or by omission); (2) there is either sufficient current evidence for B to believe that A is acutely suffering or irreversibly comatose, or there is sufficient current evidence related to A's present condition such that one or more known causal laws supports B's belief that A will be in a condition of acute suffering or irreversible comatoseness; (3) (a) B's primary reason for intending A's death is cessation of A's (actual or predicted future) suffering or irreversible comatoseness, where B does not intend A's death for a different primary reason, though there may be other relevant reasons, and (b) there is sufficient current evidence for either A or B that causal means to A's death will not produce any more suffering than would be produced for A if B were not to intervene; (4) the causal means to the event of A's death are chosen by A or B to be as painless as possible, unless either A or B has an overriding reason for a more painful causal means, where the reason for choosing the latter causal means does not conflict with the evidence in 3b; (5) A is a nonfetal organism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979p304_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979p304-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Wreen, in part responding to Beauchamp and Davidson, offered a six-part definition:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Person A committed an act of euthanasia if and only if (1) A killed B or let her die; (2) A intended to kill B; (3) the intention specified in (2) was at least partial cause of the action specified in (1); (4) the causal journey from the intention specified in (2) to the action specified in (1) is more or less in accordance with A's plan of action; (5) A's killing of B is a voluntary action; (6) the motive for the action specified in (1), the motive standing behind the intention specified in (2), is the good of the person killed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wreen1988pp637-640_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wreen1988pp637-640-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Wreen also considered a seventh requirement: "(7) The good specified in (6) is, or at least includes, the avoidance of evil", although as Wreen noted in the paper, he was not convinced that the restriction was required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wreen1988p645_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wreen1988p645-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In discussing his definition, Wreen noted the difficulty of justifying euthanasia when faced with the notion of the subject's "<a href="/wiki/Right_to_life" title="Right to life">right to life</a>". In response, Wreen argued that euthanasia has to be voluntary, and that "involuntary euthanasia is, as such, a great wrong".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wreen1988p645_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wreen1988p645-21">[21]</a></sup> Other commentators incorporate consent more directly into their definitions. For example, in a discussion of euthanasia presented in 2003 by the European Association of Palliative Care (EPAC) Ethics Task Force, the authors offered: "Medicalized killing of a person without the person's consent, whether nonvoluntary (where the person in unable to consent) or involuntary (against the person's will) is not euthanasia: it is murder. Hence, euthanasia can be voluntary only."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Materstvedt2003_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Materstvedt2003-22">[22]</a></sup> Although the EPAC Ethics Task Force argued that both <a href="/wiki/Non-voluntary_euthanasia" title="Non-voluntary euthanasia">non-voluntary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_euthanasia" title="Involuntary euthanasia">involuntary euthanasia</a> could not be included in the definition of euthanasia, there is discussion in the literature about excluding one but not the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wreen1988p645_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wreen1988p645-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_euthanasia">Classification of euthanasia</span></h2>
<p>Euthanasia may be classified according to whether a person gives <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">informed consent</a> into three types: voluntary, non-voluntary and involuntary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a debate within the medical and bioethics literature about whether or not the non-voluntary (and by extension, involuntary) killing of patients can be regarded as euthanasia, irrespective of intent or the patient's circumstances. In the definitions offered by Beauchamp and Davidson and, later, by Wreen, consent on the part of the patient was not considered as one of their criteria, although it may have been required to justify euthanasia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeauchampDavidson1979_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-BeauchampDavidson1979-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wreen1988_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wreen1988-25">[25]</a></sup> However, others see consent as essential.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_euthanasia">Voluntary euthanasia</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_euthanasia" title="Voluntary euthanasia">Voluntary euthanasia</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Right_to_die" title="Right to die">Right to die</a></div>
<p>Euthanasia conducted with the consent of the patient is termed <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_euthanasia" title="Voluntary euthanasia">voluntary euthanasia</a>. Active voluntary euthanasia is legal in Belgium, Luxembourg and the Netherlands. Passive voluntary euthanasia is legal throughout the U.S. per <i><a href="/wiki/Cruzan_v._Director,_Missouri_Department_of_Health" title="Cruzan v. Director, Missouri Department of Health">Cruzan v. Director, Missouri Department of Health</a></i>. When the patient brings about his or her own death with the assistance of a physician, the term <a href="/wiki/Assisted_suicide" title="Assisted suicide">assisted suicide</a> is often used instead. Assisted suicide is legal in Switzerland and the U.S. states of California, Oregon, Washington, Montana and Vermont.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-voluntary_euthanasia">Non-voluntary euthanasia</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-voluntary_euthanasia" title="Non-voluntary euthanasia">Non-voluntary euthanasia</a></div>
<p>Euthanasia conducted when the consent of the patient is unavailable is termed <a href="/wiki/Non-voluntary_euthanasia" title="Non-voluntary euthanasia">non-voluntary euthanasia</a>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Child_euthanasia" title="Child euthanasia">child euthanasia</a>, which is illegal worldwide but decriminalised under certain specific circumstances in the Netherlands under the <a href="/wiki/Groningen_Protocol" title="Groningen Protocol">Groningen Protocol</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Involuntary_euthanasia">Involuntary euthanasia</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_euthanasia" title="Involuntary euthanasia">Involuntary euthanasia</a></div>
<p>Euthanasia conducted against the will of the patient is termed <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_euthanasia" title="Involuntary euthanasia">involuntary euthanasia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_and_active_euthanasia">Passive and active euthanasia</span></h3>
<p>Voluntary, non-voluntary and involuntary euthanasia can all be further divided into passive or active variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Passive euthanasia entails the withholding of common treatments, such as antibiotics, necessary for the continuance of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris-2001_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Active euthanasia entails the use of lethal substances or forces, such as administering a <a href="/wiki/Lethal_injection" title="Lethal injection">lethal injection</a>, to kill and is the most controversial means. While some authors consider these terms to be misleading and unhelpful, they are nonetheless used in the literature, and so should be clarified and understood. Active euthanasia involves taking deliberate steps to end a patient's life. As an example, an administration of a lethal compound that might induce a cardiac arrest, a practice that is illegal in most jurisdictions. Passive euthanasia occur when treatments necessary for the continuance of life are withheld. In some cases, such as the administration of increasingly necessary, but toxic doses of painkillers, there is a debate whether or not to regard the practice as active or passive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris-2001_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-2001-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>According to the historian N. D. A. Kemp, the origin of the contemporary debate on euthanasia started in 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemp_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-27">[27]</a></sup> Euthanasia is known to have been debated and practiced long before that date. Euthanasia was practiced in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>: for example, <a href="/wiki/Conium" title="Conium">hemlock</a> was employed as a means of hastening death on the island of <a href="/wiki/Kea_(island)" title="Kea (island)">Kea</a>, a technique also employed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marseilles" title="Marseilles">Marseilles</a>. Euthanasia, in the sense of the deliberate hastening of a person's death, was supported by Socrates, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Elder" title="Seneca the Elder">Seneca the Elder</a> in the ancient world, although <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> appears to have <a href="/wiki/Hippocratic_Oath" title="Hippocratic Oath">spoken against the practice</a>, writing "I will not prescribe a deadly drug to please someone, nor give advice that may cause his death" (noting there is some debate in the literature about whether or not this was intended to encompass euthanasia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stolberg_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-stolberg-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span></h3>
<p>The term "euthanasia" in the earlier sense of supporting someone as they died was used for the first time by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> (1561–1626). In his work, <i>Euthanasia medica</i>, he chose this ancient Greek word and, in doing so, distinguished between <i>euthanasia interior</i>, the preparation of the soul for death, and <i>euthanasia exterior</i>, which was intended to make the end of life easier and painless, in exceptional circumstances by shortening life. That the ancient meaning of an easy death came to the fore again in the early modern period can be seen from its definition in the 18th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grosses_vollst%C3%A4ndiges_Universal-Lexicon_Aller_Wissenschafften_und_K%C3%BCnste" title="Grosses vollständiges Universal-Lexicon Aller Wissenschafften und Künste"><i>Zedlers Universallexikon</i></a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Euthanasia: a very gentle and quiet death, which happens without painful convulsions. The word comes from ευ,</i> bene<i>, well, and θανατος,</i> mors<i>, death</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The concept of euthanasia in the sense of alleviating the process of death goes back to the medical historian, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Heinrich_Marx" title="Karl Friedrich Heinrich Marx">Karl Friedrich Heinrich Marx</a>, who drew on Bacon's philosophical ideas. According to Marx, a doctor had a moral duty to ease the suffering of death through encouragement, support and mitigation using medication. Such an "alleviation of death" reflected the contemporary <i><a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist" title="Zeitgeist">Zeitgeist</a></i>, but was brought into the medical canon of responsibility for the first time by Marx. Marx also stressed the distinction between the theological care of the soul of sick people from the physical care and medical treatment by doctors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Euthanasia in its modern sense has always been strongly opposed in the <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> tradition. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> opposed both and argued that the practice of euthanasia contradicted our natural human instincts of survival,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> as did Francois Ranchin (1565–1641), a French physician and professor of medicine, and Michael Boudewijns (1601–1681), a physician and teacher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stolberg_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-stolberg-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:208</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gesundheit2006p623_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gesundheit2006p623-35">[35]</a></sup> Other voices argued for euthanasia, such as <a href="/wiki/John_Donne" title="John Donne">John Donne</a> in 1624,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and euthanasia continued to be practised. In 1678, the publication of Caspar Questel's <i>De pulvinari morientibus non subtrahend</i>, ("<i>On the pillow of which the dying should not be deprived</i>"), initiated debate on the topic. Questel described various customs which were employed at the time to hasten the death of the dying, (including the sudden removal of a pillow, which was believed to accelerate death), and argued against their use, as doing so was "against the laws of God and Nature".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stolberg_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-stolberg-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:209–211</sup> This view was shared by many who followed, including Philipp Jakob Spener, Veit Riedlin and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Kr%C3%BCnitz" title="Johann Georg Krünitz">Johann Georg Krünitz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stolberg_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-stolberg-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:211</sup> Despite opposition, euthanasia continued to be practised, involving techniques such as bleeding, suffocation, and removing people from their beds to be placed on the cold ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stolberg_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-stolberg-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:211–214</sup></p>
<p>Suicide and euthanasia became more accepted during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gesundheit2006p623_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gesundheit2006p623-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">Thomas More</a> wrote of euthanasia in <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia_(book)" title="Utopia (book)">Utopia</a></i>, although it is not clear if More was intending to endorse the practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stolberg_29-5"><a href="#cite_note-stolberg-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:208–209</sup> Other cultures have taken different approaches: for example, in Japan suicide has not traditionally been viewed as a sin, as it is used in cases of honor, and accordingly, the perceptions of euthanasia are different from those in other parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_of_the_contemporary_euthanasia_debate">Beginnings of the contemporary euthanasia debate</span></h3>
<p>In the mid-1800s, the use of <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> to treat "the pains of death" emerged, with <a href="/wiki/John_Collins_Warren" title="John Collins Warren">John Warren</a> recommending its use in 1848. A similar use of <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> was revealed by Joseph Bullar in 1866. However, in neither case was it recommended that the use should be to hasten death. In 1870 Samuel Williams, a schoolteacher, initiated the contemporary euthanasia debate through a speech given at the Birmingham Speculative Club in England, which was subsequently published in a one-off publication entitled <i>Essays of the Birmingham Speculative Club</i>, the collected works of a number of members of an amateur philosophical society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel1994_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel1994-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:794</sup> Williams' proposal was to use chloroform to deliberately hasten the death of terminally ill patients:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That in all cases of hopeless and painful illness, it should be the recognized duty of the medical attendant, whenever so desired by the patient, to administer choloroform or such other anaesthetic as may by-and-bye supersede chloroform – so as to destroy consciousness at once, and put the sufferer to a quick and painless death; all needful precautions being adopted to prevent any possible abuse of such duty; and means being taken to establish, beyond the possibility of doubt or question, that the remedy was applied at the express wish of the patient.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Samuel Williams (1872), <i>Euthanasia</i> Williams and Northgate: London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel1994_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel1994-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:794</sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The essay was favourably reviewed in <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Review_(London)" title="Saturday Review (London)">The Saturday Review</a></i>, but an editorial against the essay appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemp_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-27">[27]</a></sup> From there it proved to be influential, and other writers came out in support of such views: Lionel Tollemache wrote in favour of euthanasia, as did <a href="/wiki/Annie_Besant" title="Annie Besant">Annie Besant</a>, the essayist and reformer who later became involved with the <a href="/wiki/National_Secular_Society" title="National Secular Society">National Secular Society</a>, considering it a duty to society to "die voluntarily and painlessly" when one reaches the point of becoming a 'burden'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemp_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin2007_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin2007-39">[39]</a></sup> <i>Popular Science</i> analyzed the issue in May 1873, assessing both sides of the argument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Kemp notes that at the time, medical doctors did not participate in the discussion; it was "essentially a philosophical enterprise ... tied inextricably to a number of objections to the Christian doctrine of the sanctity of human life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemp_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_euthanasia_movement_in_the_United_States">Early euthanasia movement in the United States</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia_in_the_United_States" title="Euthanasia in the United States">Euthanasia in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2876" data-file-width="1980" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg/170px-Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg/255px-Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg/340px-Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Felix-Adler-Hine.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Felix Adler, circa 1913, the first prominent American to argue for permitting suicide in cases of chronic illness</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The rise of the euthanasia movement in the United States coincided with the so-called <a href="/wiki/Gilded_Age" title="Gilded Age">Gilded Age</a>, a time of social and technological change that encompassed an "individualistic conservatism that praised laissez-faire economics, <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalism</a>", along with major <a href="/wiki/Depression_(economics)" title="Depression (economics)">depressions</a>, industrialisation and conflict between corporations and labour unions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel1994_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel1994-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:794</sup> It was also the period in which the modern hospital system was developed, which has been seen as a factor in the emergence of the euthanasia debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_G._Ingersoll" title="Robert G. Ingersoll">Robert Ingersoll</a> argued for euthanasia, stating in 1894 that where someone is suffering from a terminal illness, such as terminal cancer, they should have a right to end their pain through suicide. Felix Adler offered a similar approach, although, unlike Ingersoll, Adler did not reject religion. In fact, he argued from an <a href="/wiki/Ethical_movement" title="Ethical movement">Ethical Culture</a> framework. In 1891, Alder argued that those suffering from overwhelming pain should have the right to commit suicide, and, furthermore, that it should be permissible for a doctor to assist – thus making Adler the first "prominent American" to argue for suicide in cases where people were suffering from chronic illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin-42">[42]</a></sup> Both Ingersoll and Adler argued for voluntary euthanasia of adults suffering from terminal ailments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin-42">[42]</a></sup> Dowbiggin argues that by breaking down prior moral objections to euthanasia and suicide, Ingersoll and Adler enabled others to stretch the definition of euthanasia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin2003p13_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin2003p13-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first attempt to legalise euthanasia took place in the United States, when <a href="/wiki/Henry_Thomas_Hunt" title="Henry Thomas Hunt">Henry Hunt</a> introduced legislation into the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_General_Assembly" title="Ohio General Assembly">General Assembly</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> in 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appel2004_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Appel2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:614</sup> Hunt did so at the behest of <a href="/wiki/Anna_Hall" title="Anna Hall">Anna Hall</a>, a wealthy heiress who was a major figure in the euthanasia movement during the early 20th century in the United States. Hall had watched her mother die after an extended battle with <a href="/wiki/Liver_cancer" title="Liver cancer">liver cancer</a>, and had dedicated herself to ensuring that others would not have to endure the same suffering. Towards this end she engaged in an extensive letter writing campaign, recruited <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lurana_Sheldon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lurana Sheldon (page does not exist)">Lurana Sheldon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maud_Ballington_Booth" title="Maud Ballington Booth">Maud Ballington Booth</a>, and organised a debate on euthanasia at the annual meeting of the <a href="/wiki/American_Humane_Association" title="American Humane Association">American Humane Association</a> in 1905 – described by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_M._Appel" title="Jacob M. Appel">Jacob Appel</a> as the first significant public debate on the topic in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appel2004_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Appel2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:614–616</sup></p>
<p>Hunt's bill called for the administration of an <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> to bring about a patient's death, so long as the person is of lawful age and sound mind, and was suffering from a fatal injury, an irrevocable illness, or great physical pain. It also required that the case be heard by a physician, required informed consent in front of three witnesses, and required the attendance of three physicians who had to agree that the patient's recovery was impossible. A motion to reject the bill outright was voted down, but the bill failed to pass, 79 to 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel1994_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel1994-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:796</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appel2004_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Appel2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:618–619</sup></p>
<p>Along with the Ohio euthanasia proposal, in 1906 Assemblyman Ross Gregory introduced a proposal to permit euthanasia to the <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a> legislature. However, the Iowa legislation was broader in scope than that offered in Ohio. It allowed for the death of any person of at least ten years of age who suffered from an ailment that would prove fatal and cause extreme pain, should they be of sound mind and express a desire to artificially hasten their death. In addition, it allowed for infants to be euthanised if they were sufficiently deformed, and permitted guardians to request euthanasia on behalf of their wards. The proposed legislation also imposed penalties on physicians who refused to perform euthanasia when requested: a 6–12 month prison term and a fine of between $200 and $1000. The proposal proved to be controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appel2004_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Appel2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:619–621</sup> It engendered considerable debate and failed to pass, having been withdrawn from consideration after being passed to the Committee on Public Health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appel2004_44-4"><a href="#cite_note-Appel2004-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:623</sup></p>
<p>After 1906 the euthanasia debate reduced in intensity, resurfacing periodically, but not returning to the same level of debate until the 1930s in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel1994_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel1994-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:796</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s_in_Britain">1930s in Britain</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voluntary_Euthanasia_Legalisation_Society" title="Voluntary Euthanasia Legalisation Society">Voluntary Euthanasia Legalisation Society</a> was founded in 1935 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Killick_Millard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles Killick Millard (page does not exist)">Charles Killick Millard</a> (now called <a href="/wiki/Dignity_in_Dying" title="Dignity in Dying">Dignity in Dying</a>). The movement campaigned for the legalisation of euthanasia in Great Britain.</p>
<p>In January 1936, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a> was given a fatal dose of morphine and <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> in order to hasten his death. At the time he was suffering from cardio-respiratory failure, and the decision to end his life was made by his physician, <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Dawson,_1st_Viscount_Dawson_of_Penn" title="Bertrand Dawson, 1st Viscount Dawson of Penn">Lord Dawson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Although this event was kept a secret for over 50 years, the death of George V coincided with proposed legislation in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> to legalise euthanasia. The legislation came through the British Volunteer Euthanasia Legalisation Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Euthanasia opponent <a href="/wiki/Ian_Dowbiggin" title="Ian Dowbiggin">Ian Dowbiggin</a> argues that the early membership of the Euthanasia Society of America (ESA) reflected how many perceived euthanasia at the time, often seeing it as a eugenics matter rather than an issue concerning individual rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin-42">[42]</a></sup> Dowbiggin argues that not every eugenist joined the ESA "solely for eugenic reasons", but he postulates that there were clear ideological connections between the eugenics and euthanasia movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazi_Euthanasia_Program_.28Action_T4.29">Nazi Euthanasia Program (Action T4)</span></h3>

<p>A 24 July 1939 killing of a severely disabled infant in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> was described in a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> "Genocide Under the Nazis Timeline" as the first "state-sponsored euthanasia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July24_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-July24-47">[47]</a></sup> Parties that consented to the killing included Hitler's office, the parents, and the Reich Committee for the Scientific Registration of Serious and Congenitally Based Illnesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July24_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-July24-47">[47]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Telegraph</a></i> noted that the killing of the disabled infant—whose name was <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Kretschmar" title="Gerhard Kretschmar">Gerhard Kretschmar</a>, born blind, with missing limbs, subject to convulsions, and reportedly "an idiot"— provided "the rationale for a secret Nazi decree that led to 'mercy killings' of almost 300,000 mentally and physically handicapped people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casek1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-casek1-48">[48]</a></sup> While Kretchmar's killing received parental consent, most of the 5,000 to 8,000 children killed afterwards were forcibly taken from their parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July24_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-July24-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-casek1_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-casek1-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "euthanasia campaign" of mass murder gathered momentum on 14 January 1940 when the "handicapped" were killed with gas vans and killing centres, eventually leading to the deaths of 70,000 adult Germans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-January14_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-January14-49">[49]</a></sup> Professor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Jay_Lifton" title="Robert Jay Lifton">Robert Jay Lifton</a>, author of <i>The Nazi Doctors</i> and a leading authority on the T4 program, contrasts this program with what he considers to be a genuine euthanasia. He explains that the Nazi version of "euthanasia" was based on the work of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adolf_Jost&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adolf Jost (page does not exist)">Adolf Jost</a>, who published <i>The Right to Death</i> (Das Recht auf den Tod) in 1895. Lifton writes: "Jost argued that control over the death of the individual must ultimately belong to the social organism, the state. This concept is in direct opposition to the Anglo-American concept of euthanasia, which emphasizes the <i>individual's</i> 'right to die' or 'right to death' or 'right to his or her own death,' as the ultimate human claim. In contrast, Jost was pointing to the state's right to kill. ... Ultimately the argument was biological: 'The rights to death [are] the key to the fitness of life.' The state must own death—must kill—in order to keep the social organism alive and healthy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In modern terms, the use of "euthanasia" in the context of Action T4 is seen to be a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/euphemism" title="wikt:euphemism">euphemism</a> to disguise a program of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a>, in which people were killed on the grounds of "disabilities, religious beliefs, and discordant individual values".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michalsen2006_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michalsen2006-51">[51]</a></sup> Compared to the discussions of euthanasia that emerged post-war, the Nazi program may have been worded in terms that appear similar to the modern use of "euthanasia", but there was no "mercy" and the patients were not necessarily terminally ill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michalsen2006_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Michalsen2006-51">[51]</a></sup> Despite these differences, historian and euthanasia opponent <a href="/wiki/Ian_Dowbiggin" title="Ian Dowbiggin">Ian Dowbiggin</a> writes that "the origins of Nazi euthanasia, like those of the American euthanasia movement, predate the Third Reich and were intertwined with the history of <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">Social Darwinism</a>, and with efforts to discredit traditional morality and ethics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin_42-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:65</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1949_New_York_State_Petition_for_Euthanasia_and_Catholic_Opposition">The 1949 New York State Petition for Euthanasia and Catholic Opposition</span></h3>
<p>On January 6, 1949, the Euthanasia Society of America presented to the New York State Legislature a petition to legalize euthanasia, signed by 379 leading Protestant and Jewish ministers, the largest group of religious leaders ever to have taken this stance. A similar petition had been sent to the New York State Legislature in 1947, signed by approximately 1,000 New York physicians. Catholic religious leaders criticized the petition, saying that such a bill would "legalize a suicide-murder pact" and a "rationalization of the fifth commandment of God, 'Though Shalt Not Kill.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mercy_Killing_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mercy_Killing-52">[52]</a></sup> The Right Reverend Robert E. McCormick stated that</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"The ultimate object of the Euthanasia Society is based on the Totalitarian principle that the state is supreme and that the individual does not have the right to live if his continuance in life is a burden or hindrance to the state. The Nazis followed this principle and compulsory Euthanasia was practiced as a part of their program during the recent war. We American citizens of New York State must ask ourselves this question: 'Are we going to finish Hitler's job?'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mercy_Killing_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mercy_Killing-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The petition brought tensions between the American Euthanasia Society and the Catholic Church to a head that contributed to a climate of anti-Catholic sentiment generally regarding issues such as birth control, eugenics, and population control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowbiggin_42-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dowbiggin-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The petition did not lead to a law.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Euthanasia_debate">Euthanasia debate</span></h2>
<p>Historically, the euthanasia debate has tended to focus on a number of key concerns. According to euthanasia opponent <a href="/wiki/Ezekiel_Emanuel" title="Ezekiel Emanuel">Ezekiel Emanuel</a>, proponents of euthanasia have presented four main arguments: a) that people have a right to <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a>, and thus should be allowed to choose their own fate; b) assisting a subject to die might be a better choice than requiring that they continue to suffer; c) the distinction between passive euthanasia, which is often permitted, and active euthanasia, which is not substantive (or that the underlying principle–the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_double_effect" title="Doctrine of double effect">doctrine of double effect</a>–is unreasonable or unsound); and d) permitting euthanasia will not necessarily lead to unacceptable consequences. Pro-euthanasia activists often point to countries like the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, and states like <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, where euthanasia has been legalized, to argue that it is mostly unproblematic.</p>
<p>Similarly, Emanuel argues that there are four major arguments presented by opponents of euthanasia: a) not all deaths are painful; b) alternatives, such as cessation of active treatment, combined with the use of effective pain relief, are available; c) the distinction between active and passive euthanasia is morally significant; and d) legalising euthanasia will place society on a <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia_and_the_slippery_slope" title="Euthanasia and the slippery slope">slippery slope</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> which will lead to unacceptable consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel1994_38-5"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel1994-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:797–8</sup> In fact, in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, in 2013, pain wasn't one of the top five reasons people sought euthanasia. Top reasons were a loss of dignity, and a fear of burdening others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa151020_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-usa151020-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States in 2013, 47% nationwide supported doctor-assisted suicide. This included 32% of <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">Latinos</a>, 29% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-Americans</a>, and almost nobody with disabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa151020_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-usa151020-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Legality_of_euthanasia" title="Legality of euthanasia">Legality of euthanasia</a></div>
<p>West's <i>Encyclopedia of American Law</i> states that "a 'mercy killing' or euthanasia is generally considered to be a criminal homicide"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and is normally used as a synonym of homicide committed at a request made by the patient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomas_Valiente_1997_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tomas_Valiente_1997-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The judicial sense of the term "<a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">homicide</a>" includes any intervention undertaken with the express intention of ending a life, even to relieve intractable suffering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomas_Valiente_1997_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tomas_Valiente_1997-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohanty_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mohanty-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legal-dictionary.thefreedictionary.com_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-legal-dictionary.thefreedictionary.com-58">[58]</a></sup> Not all homicide is unlawful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-homicide_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-homicide-59">[59]</a></sup> Two designations of homicide that carry no criminal punishment are justifiable and excusable homicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-homicide_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-homicide-59">[59]</a></sup> In most countries this is not the status of euthanasia. The term "euthanasia" is usually confined to the active variety; the University of Washington website states that "euthanasia generally means that the physician would act directly, for instance by giving a lethal injection, to end the patient's life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_of_Washington_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-University_of_Washington-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Assisted_suicide" title="Assisted suicide">Physician-assisted suicide</a> is thus not classified as euthanasia by <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">the US State of Oregon</a>, where it is legal under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oregon_Death_with_Dignity_Act" title="Oregon Death with Dignity Act">Oregon Death with Dignity Act</a>, and despite its name, it is not legally classified as suicide either.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Unlike physician-assisted suicide, withholding or withdrawing life-sustaining treatments with patient consent (voluntary) is almost unanimously considered, at least in the United States, to be legal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The use of pain medication in order to relieve suffering, even if it hastens death, has been held as legal in several court decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_of_Washington_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-University_of_Washington-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some governments around the world have legalized voluntary euthanasia but most commonly it is still considered to be criminal homicide. In the Netherlands and Belgium, where euthanasia has been legalized, it still remains homicide although it is not prosecuted and not punishable if the perpetrator (the doctor) meets certain legal conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physician_sentiment">Physician sentiment</span></h2>
<p>A survey in the United States of more than 10,000 physicians came to the result that approximately 16% of physicians would ever consider halting life-sustaining therapy because the family demands it, even if they believed that it was premature. Approximately 55% would not, and for the remaining 29%, it would depend on circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kane_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-kane-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>This study also stated that approximately 46% of physicians agree that physician-assisted suicide should be allowed in some cases; 41% do not, and the remaining 14% think it depends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kane_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-kane-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the pro-assisted dying group <a href="/wiki/Dignity_in_Dying" title="Dignity in Dying">Dignity in Dying</a> cite conflicting research on attitudes by doctors to assisted dying: with a 2009 <i>Palliative Medicine</i>-published survey showing 64% support (to 34% oppose) for assisted dying in cases where a patient has an incurable and painful disease, while 49% of doctors in a study published in <i>BMC Medical Ethics</i> oppose changing the law on assisted dying to 39% in favour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
