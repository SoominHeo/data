<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Laying on of hands</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>laying on of hands</b> is a ritual that is a part of <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious practices</a> found in various cultures throughout the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian churches</a>, this practice is used as both a symbolic and formal method of invoking the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> primarily during <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptisms</a> and confirmations, <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">healing services</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessings" title="Blessings">blessings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priests</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_religion" title="Minister of religion">ministers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elder_(religious)" title="Elder (religious)">elders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacons</a>, and other church officers, along with a variety of other church <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">sacraments</a> and holy ceremonies.</p>
<p>The practice of laying on of hands is used in <a href="/wiki/Navajo" title="Navajo">Navajo</a> religious ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_tradition">Jewish tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laying_on_of_hands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Jewish tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The laying on of hands was an action referred to on numerous occasions in the <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">Tanakh</a> to accompany the conferring of a blessing or authority. For example, <a href="/wiki/Isaac" title="Isaac">Isaac</a> blessed his son <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a> by laying on of hands (<a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0127.htm#27" rel="nofollow">Genesis 27:27</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aaron" title="Aaron">Aaron</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kohen_Gadol" title="Kohen Gadol">High Priests</a> who succeeded him symbolically transferred the sins of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children_of_Israel" title="Children of Israel">Children of Israel</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Scapegoat" title="Scapegoat">sacrificial goat</a> by the laying on of hands: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leviticus" title="Leviticus">Leviticus</a><a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0316.htm#21" rel="nofollow">16:21</a>. <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> ordained <a href="/wiki/Joshua" title="Joshua">Joshua</a> through <i><a href="/wiki/Semikhah" title="Semikhah">semikhah</a></i>—i.e. by the laying on of hands: <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0427.htm#15" rel="nofollow">Num 27:15–23</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0534.htm#9" rel="nofollow">Deut 34:9</a>. The Bible adds that Joshua was thereby "filled with the spirit of wisdom". Moses also ordained the 70 elders (<a class="external text" href="http://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0411.htm#16" rel="nofollow">Num 11:16–25</a>). The elders later ordained their successors in this way. Their successors in turn ordained others. This chain of hands-on <i>semikhah</i> continued through the time of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Temple" title="Second Temple">Second Temple</a>, to an undetermined time. The exact date that the original <i>semikhah</i> succession ended is not certain. Many medieval authorities believed that this occurred during the reign of Hillel II, circa AD 360.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, it seems to have continued at least until AD 425 when <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_II" title="Theodosius II">Theodosius II</a> executed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamaliel_VI" title="Gamaliel VI">Gamaliel VI</a> and suppressed the <a href="/wiki/Nasi_(Hebrew_title)" title="Nasi (Hebrew title)">Patriarchate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin" title="Sanhedrin">Sanhedrin</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_traditions">Christian traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laying_on_of_hands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Christian traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christian_laying_on_of_hands" title="Christian laying on of hands">Christian laying on of hands</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG/220px-Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG/330px-Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG/440px-Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laying_on_of_hands_Finnish_Lutheran_ordination_in_Oulu.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Laying on of hands Finnish Lutheran ordination in Oulu</div>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> the laying on of hands was associated with the receiving of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#8:14" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">8:14–19</a>). Initially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" title="Twelve Apostles">Apostles</a> laid hands on new believers as well as believers (see <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#6:5" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">Acts 6:5–6</a>). In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_church" title="Early church">early church</a>, the practice continued and is still used in a wide variety of church ceremonies, such as during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confirmation_(sacrament)" title="Confirmation (sacrament)">confirmation</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> also associates the laying on of hands with the conferral of authority or designation of a person to a position of responsibility. (See <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#6:6" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">6:6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#13:3" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">13:3</a>; and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/1_Timothy#4:14" title="s:Bible (King James)/1 Timothy">1 Timothy 4:14</a>. Also possibly <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#14:23" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">14:23</a>, where "ordained"—Greek: χειροτονήσαντες—may be translated "extended the hand".) The use of the laying on of hands for the <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordination</a> of church officers has continued in many branches of Christianity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State_use">State use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laying_on_of_hands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: State use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The laying on of hands, known as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_touch" title="Royal touch">royal touch</a>, was performed by <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">kings</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and was believed to cure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrofula" title="Scrofula">scrofula</a> (also called "King's Evil" at the time), a name given to a number of skin diseases. The rite of the king's touch began in France with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_II_the_Pious" title="Robert II the Pious">Robert II the Pious</a>, but legend later attributed the practice to <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> founder of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman</a> kingdom, and <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> in England. The belief continued to be common throughout the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> but began to die out with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Enlightenment" title="The Enlightenment">the Enlightenment</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> was the last British monarch to claim to possess this divine ability, though the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> <a href="/wiki/Pretender" title="Pretender">pretenders</a> also claimed to do so. The French monarchy maintained the practice up until the 19th century. The act was usually performed at large ceremonies, often at <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> or other holy days.</p>
