<p><b>Andrzej Witold Wajda</b> (<small>Polish: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[ˈandʐɛj ˈvajda]</a></span>; 6 March 1926 – 9 October 2016) was a Polish film and theatre director. Recipient of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Oscar" title="Honorary Oscar">Honorary Oscar</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> he was a prominent member of the "<a href="/wiki/Polish_Film_School" title="Polish Film School">Polish Film School</a>". He was known especially for his trilogy of war films consisting of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Generation" title="A Generation">A Generation</a></i> (1954), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kana%C5%82_(film)" title="Kanał (film)">Kanał</a></i> (1956) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ashes_and_Diamonds_(film)" title="Ashes and Diamonds (film)">Ashes and Diamonds</a></i> (1958).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Four of his films have been nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Promised_Land_(1975_film)" title="The Promised Land (1975 film)">The Promised Land</a></i> (1975),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscars1976_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscars1976-4">[4]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maids_of_Wilko" title="The Maids of Wilko">The Maids of Wilko</a></i> (1979),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscars1980_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscars1980-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Man_of_Iron" title="Man of Iron">Man of Iron</a></i> (1981) and <i><a href="/wiki/Katy%C5%84_(film)" title="Katyń (film)">Katyń</a></i> (2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrzej_Wajda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wajda was born in <a href="/wiki/Suwa%C5%82ki" title="Suwałki">Suwałki</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Poland, the son of Aniela (née Białowąs), a school teacher, and Jakub Wajda, an army officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Wajda's father was murdered by the Soviets in 1940 in what came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Katyn_massacre" title="Katyn massacre">Katyn massacre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1942 he joined the Polish resistance and served in the <a href="/wiki/Home_Army" title="Home Army">Home Army</a>. After the war, he studied to be a painter at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Fine_Arts,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Academy of Fine Arts, Kraków">Kraków's Academy of Fine Arts</a> before entering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%81%C3%B3d%C5%BA_Film_School" title="Łódź Film School">Łódź Film School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrzej_Wajda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Wajda's apprenticeship to director <a href="/wiki/Aleksander_Ford" title="Aleksander Ford">Aleksander Ford</a>, Wajda was given the opportunity to direct his own film. <i><a href="/wiki/A_Generation" title="A Generation">A Generation</a></i> (1955) was his first major film. At the same time Wajda began his work as a director in theatre, including such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_V._Gazzo" title="Michael V. Gazzo">Michael V. Gazzo</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Hatful_of_Rain" title="A Hatful of Rain">A Hatful of Rain</a></i> (1959), <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i> (1960), and <i><a href="/wiki/Two_for_the_Seesaw" title="Two for the Seesaw">Two for the Seesaw</a></i> (1963) by <a href="/wiki/William_Gibson_(playwright)" title="William Gibson (playwright)">William Gibson</a>. Wajda made two more increasingly accomplished films, which developed further the anti-war theme of <i>A Generation</i>: <i><a href="/wiki/Kana%C5%82" title="Kanał">Kanał</a></i> (1956) (<a href="/wiki/Jury_Prize_(Cannes_Film_Festival)#Prize_winners" title="Jury Prize (Cannes Film Festival)">Special Jury Prize</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> in 1957, shared with Bergman's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seventh_Seal" title="The Seventh Seal">The Seventh Seal</a></i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ashes_and_Diamonds_(film)" title="Ashes and Diamonds (film)">Ashes and Diamonds</a></i> (1958) with <a href="/wiki/Zbigniew_Cybulski" title="Zbigniew Cybulski">Zbigniew Cybulski</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While capable of turning out mainstream commercial fare (often dismissed as "trivial" by critics), Wajda was more interested in works of <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and certain symbols (such as setting fire to a glass of liquor, representing the flame of youthful idealism that was extinguished by the war) recur often in his films. <i><a href="/wiki/Lotna" title="Lotna">Lotna</a></i> (1959) is full of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrealistic" title="Surrealistic">surrealistic</a> and symbolic scenes and shots, but he managed to explore other styles, making <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">new wave</a> style <i><a href="/wiki/Innocent_Sorcerers" title="Innocent Sorcerers">Innocent Sorcerers</a></i> (1960) with music by <a href="/wiki/Krzysztof_Komeda" title="Krzysztof Komeda">Krzysztof Komeda</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Roman_Polanski" title="Roman Polanski">Roman Polanski</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerzy_Skolimowski" title="Jerzy Skolimowski">Jerzy Skolimowski</a> (who was also a co-script writer) in the episodes. Then Wajda directed <i><a href="/wiki/Samson_(1961_Polish_film)" title="Samson (1961 Polish film)">Samson</a></i> (1961), the story of Jacob, a Jewish boy, who wants to survive during the Nazi occupation of Poland. In the mid-1960s Wajda made <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ashes_(film)" title="The Ashes (film)">The Ashes</a></i> (1965) based on the novel by Polish writer <a href="/wiki/Stefan_%C5%BBeromski" title="Stefan Żeromski">Stefan Żeromski</a> and directed several films abroad: <i><a href="/wiki/Love_at_Twenty" title="Love at Twenty">Love at Twenty</a></i> (1962), <i><a href="/wiki/Siberian_Lady_Macbeth" title="Siberian Lady Macbeth">Siberian Lady Macbeth</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> (1962) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gates_To_Paradise" title="Gates To Paradise">Gates To Paradise</a></i> (1968).</p>
<p>In 1967, Cybulski was killed in a train accident, whereupon the director articulated his grief with <i><a href="/wiki/Everything_for_Sale" title="Everything for Sale">Everything for Sale</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (1968), considered one of his most personal films, using the technique of a film-within-a-film to tell the story of a film maker's life and work.The following year he directed an ironic satire <i><a href="/wiki/Hunting_Flies_(1969_film)" title="Hunting Flies (1969 film)">Hunting Flies</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> with the script written by <a href="/wiki/Janusz_G%C5%82owacki" title="Janusz Głowacki">Janusz Głowacki</a> and a short television film called <i><a href="/wiki/Przek%C5%82adaniec" title="Przekładaniec">Przekładaniec</a></i> based on a screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lem" title="Stanisław Lem">Stanisław Lem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistic_recognition">Artistic recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrzej_Wajda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Artistic recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The 1970s were the most lucrative artistic period for Wajda, who made over ten films: <i><a href="/wiki/Landscape_After_the_Battle" title="Landscape After the Battle">Landscape After the Battle</a></i> (1970), <i><a href="/wiki/Pilate_and_Others" title="Pilate and Others">Pilate and Others</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wedding_(1972_film)" title="The Wedding (1972 film)">The Wedding</a></i> (1972) – the film version of the famous Polish poetic drama by <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Wyspia%C5%84ski" title="Stanisław Wyspiański">Stanisław Wyspiański</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Promised_Land_(1975_film)" title="The Promised Land (1975 film)">The Promised Land</a></i> (1974), <i><a href="/wiki/Man_of_Marble" title="Man of Marble">Man of Marble</a></i> (1976) – the film takes place in two time periods, the first film showing the episodes of <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinism</a> in Poland, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shadow_Line" title="The Shadow Line">The Shadow Line</a></i> (1976), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rough_Treatment" title="Rough Treatment">Rough Treatment</a></i> (the other title: <i><a href="/wiki/Without_Anesthesia" title="Without Anesthesia">Without Anesthesia</a>)</i> (1978), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Orchestra_Conductor" title="The Orchestra Conductor">The Orchestra Conductor</a></i> (1980), starring <a href="/wiki/John_Gielgud" title="John Gielgud">John Gielgud</a>; and two psychological and existential films based upon novels by <a href="/wiki/Jaros%C5%82aw_Iwaszkiewicz" title="Jarosław Iwaszkiewicz">Jarosław Iwaszkiewicz</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birch_Wood" title="The Birch Wood">The Birch Wood</a></i> (1970) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maids_of_Wilko" title="The Maids of Wilko">The Maids of Wilko</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> (1979). <i>The Birch Wood</i> was entered into the <a href="/wiki/7th_Moscow_International_Film_Festival" title="7th Moscow International Film Festival">7th Moscow International Film Festival</a> where Wajda won the Golden Prize for Direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscow1971_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscow1971-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wajda continued to work in theatre, including <i><a href="/wiki/Play_Strindberg" title="Play Strindberg">Play Strindberg</a></i>, Dostoyevsky's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Possessed_(play)" title="The Possessed (play)">The Possessed</a></i> and <i>Nastasja Filippovna</i> – Wajda's version of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Idiot_(novel)" title="The Idiot (novel)">The Idiot</a></i>, <i>November Night</i> by Wyspiański, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Immigrants" title="The Immigrants">The Immigrants</a></i> by Sławomir Mrożek, <i>The Danton Affair</i> or <i>The Dreams of Reason</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Wajda's later commitment to Poland's burgeoning <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity</a> movement was manifested in <i><a href="/wiki/Man_of_Iron" title="Man of Iron">Man of Iron</a></i> (1981), a thematic sequel to <i>The Man of Marble</i>, with Solidarity leader <a href="/wiki/Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa" title="Lech Wałęsa">Lech Wałęsa</a> appearing as himself in the latter film. The film sequence is loosely based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Anna_Walentynowicz" title="Anna Walentynowicz">Anna Walentynowicz</a>, a hero of socialist labor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stakhanovite" title="Stakhanovite">Stakhanovite</a> turned dissident and alludes to events from real life, such as the firing of Walentynowicz from the shipyard and the underground wedding of <a href="/wiki/Bogdan_Borusewicz" title="Bogdan Borusewicz">Bogdan Borusewicz</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alina_Pienkowska" title="Alina Pienkowska">Alina Pienkowska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The director's involvement in this movement would prompt the Polish government to force Wajda's production company out of business. For the film, Wajda won the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>In 1983, he directed <i><a href="/wiki/Danton_(1983_film)" title="Danton (1983 film)">Danton</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Depardieu" title="Gérard Depardieu">Gérard Depardieu</a> in the title role, a film set in 1794 (Year Two) dealing with the <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Post-Revolutionary Terror</a>. Made against the backdrop of the <a href="/wiki/Martial_law_in_Poland" title="Martial law in Poland">martial law in Poland</a>, Wajda showed how easily revolution can change into terror and starts to "eat its own children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> For this film Wajda was honoured with the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Delluc_Prize" title="Louis Delluc Prize">Louis Delluc Prize</a> and a <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award_for_Best_Director" title="César Award for Best Director">César Award for Best Director</a>. In the 1980s he also made <i><a href="/wiki/A_Love_in_Germany" title="A Love in Germany">A Love in Germany</a></i> (1983) featuring <a href="/wiki/Hanna_Schygulla" title="Hanna Schygulla">Hanna Schygulla</a>, <i>The Chronicle of Amorous Incidents</i> (1986) an adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Konwicki" title="Tadeusz Konwicki">Tadeusz Konwicki</a>'s novel and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Possessed_(play)" title="The Possessed (play)">The Possessed</a></i> (1988) based on <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">Dostoyevsky</a>'s novel. In theatre he prepared an interpretation of Dostoyevsky's <i><a href="/wiki/Crime_and_Punishment" title="Crime and Punishment">Crime and Punishment</a></i> (1984) and other unique spectacles such as <i>Antygone</i>, his sequential <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i> versions or an old Jewish play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dybbuk" title="The Dybbuk">The Dybbuk</a></i>. In 1989, he was the President of the Jury at the <a href="/wiki/16th_Moscow_International_Film_Festival" title="16th Moscow International Film Festival">16th Moscow International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscow1989_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscow1989-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_after_1990">Career after 1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrzej_Wajda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Career after 1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1990, Andrzej Wajda was honoured by the <a href="/wiki/European_Film_Awards" title="European Film Awards">European Film Awards</a> for his lifetime achievement, only the third director to be so honoured, after <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">Ingmar Bergman</a>. In the early 1990s, he was elected a senator and also appointed artistic director of Warsaw's Teatr Powszechny. He continued to make films set during World War II, including <i><a href="/wiki/Korczak_(film)" title="Korczak (film)">Korczak</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> (1990), a story about a Jewish-Polish doctor who takes care of orphan children, in <i>The Crowned-Eagle Ring</i> (1993) and <i><a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_(film)" title="Holy Week (film)">Holy Week</a></i> (1995) specifically on Jewish-Polish relations. In 1994 Wajda presented his own film version of <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">Dostoyevsky</a>'s novel <a href="/wiki/The_Idiot" title="The Idiot">The Idiot</a> in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Nastasja" title="Nastasja">Nastasja</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> starring Japanese actor Tamasoburo Bando in the double role of Prince Mishkin and Nastasja. The film was photographed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pawel_Edelman" title="Pawel Edelman">Pawel Edelman</a>, who subsequently became one of Wajda's great co-workers. In 1996 the director went in a different direction with <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Nobody_(1996_film)" title="Miss Nobody (1996 film)">Miss Nobody</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> a coming-of-age drama that explored the darker and more spiritual aspects of a relationship between three high-school girls. In 1999 Wajda released the epic film <i><a href="/wiki/Pan_Tadeusz" title="Pan Tadeusz">Pan Tadeusz</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> based on the epic poem of the Polish 19th-century romantic poet <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz" title="Adam Mickiewicz">Adam Mickiewicz</a>.</p>
<p>A year later, at the <a href="/wiki/72nd_Academy_Awards" title="72nd Academy Awards">2000 Academy Awards</a>, Wajda was presented with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Oscar" title="Honorary Oscar">honorary Oscar</a> for his contribution to world cinema;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storoznyski.2C_Alex_.28March_2000.29_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storoznyski.2C_Alex_.28March_2000.29-29">[29]</a></sup> he subsequently donated the award to Kraków's Jagiellonian University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Vancouver_Sun_.28April_2000.29_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Vancouver_Sun_.28April_2000.29-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2002, Wajda directed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Revenge_(film)" title="The Revenge (film)">The Revenge</a></i>, a film version of his 1980s comedy theatre production, with <a href="/wiki/Roman_Polanski" title="Roman Polanski">Roman Polanski</a> in one of the main roles. In February 2006, Wajda received an honorary <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bear" title="Golden Bear">Golden Bear</a> for lifetime achievement at the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berlinale2006_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-berlinale2006-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/Katy%C5%84_(film)" title="Katyń (film)">Katyń</a></i> was released, a well-received film about the <a href="/wiki/Katyn_massacre" title="Katyn massacre">Katyn massacre</a>, in which Wajda's father was murdered; the director also shows the dramatic situation of those who await their relatives (mothers, wives and children). The film was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Best Foreign Language Film</a> Oscar in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wajda followed it with <i><a href="/wiki/Tatarak" title="Tatarak">Sweet Rush</a></i> (2009) with <a href="/wiki/Krystyna_Janda" title="Krystyna Janda">Krystyna Janda</a> as a main character. It is partly based upon a short <a href="/wiki/Jaros%C5%82aw_Iwaszkiewicz" title="Jarosław Iwaszkiewicz">Jarosław Iwaszkiewicz</a> novel. The film is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Edward_K%C5%82osi%C5%84ski" title="Edward Kłosiński">Edward Kłosiński</a>, Janda's husband, a cinematographer and a long-time Wajda friend and co-worker who died of cancer the same year. For this film Wajda was awarded by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Bauer_Prize" title="Alfred Bauer Prize">Alfred Bauer Prize</a> at the 2009 Berlin International Film Festival. He received the Prix FIPRESCI during the 2009 European Film Awards. <i><a href="/wiki/Walesa._Man_of_Hope" title="Walesa. Man of Hope">Walesa. Man of Hope</a></i> (<i>Wałęsa. Człowiek z nadziei</i>), Wajda's biography of <a href="/wiki/Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa" title="Lech Wałęsa">Lech Wałęsa</a>, based on a script by <a href="/wiki/Janusz_G%C5%82owacki" title="Janusz Głowacki">Janusz Głowacki</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wi%C4%99ckiewicz" title="Robert Więckiewicz">Robert Więckiewicz</a> in the title role, had its world premiere at the 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="Venice International Film Festival">Venice International Film Festival</a>. His last film was the 2016 <i><a href="/wiki/Afterimage_(film)" title="Afterimage (film)">Afterimage</a></i> (<i>Powidoki</i>), starring <a href="/wiki/Bogus%C5%82aw_Linda" title="Bogusław Linda">Bogusław Linda</a> as Polish avant-garde painter <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Strzemi%C5%84ski" title="Władysław Strzemiński">Władysław Strzemiński</a>.</p>
<p>Andrzej Wajda founded The Japanese Centre of Art and Technology in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a> in 1994. In 2002 he founded and led his own film school with Polish filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Wojciech_Marczewski" title="Wojciech Marczewski">Wojciech Marczewski</a>. Students of Wajda School take part in different film courses led by famous European film makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrzej_Wajda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wajda was married four times. His third wife was actress <a href="/wiki/Beata_Tyszkiewicz" title="Beata Tyszkiewicz">Beata Tyszkiewicz</a> with whom he had a daughter, Karolina (born 1967). His fourth wife was the theatre costume designer and actress <a href="/wiki/Krystyna_Zachwatowicz" title="Krystyna Zachwatowicz">Krystyna Zachwatowicz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Wajda died in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> on 9 October 2016 at the age of 90 from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_failure" title="Pulmonary failure">pulmonary failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-3">[3]</a></sup> He was buried at <a href="/wiki/Salwator_Cemetery" title="Salwator Cemetery">Salwator Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrzej_Wajda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Man_of_Iron" title="Man of Iron">Man of Iron</a></i> won the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1981_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1981 Cannes Film Festival">1981 Cannes Film Festival</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Promised_Land_(1975_film)" title="The Promised Land (1975 film)">The Promised Land</a></i> won the Golden Prize at the <a href="/wiki/9th_Moscow_International_Film_Festival" title="9th Moscow International Film Festival">9th Moscow International Film Festival</a> in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscow1975_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscow1975-36">[36]</a></sup> Four of Wajda's works (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Promised_Land_(1975_film)" title="The Promised Land (1975 film)">The Promised Land</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maids_of_Wilko" title="The Maids of Wilko">The Maids of Wilko</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Man_of_Iron" title="Man of Iron">Man of Iron</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Katy%C5%84_(film)" title="Katyń (film)">Katyń</a>)</i> have been nominated for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for Best Foreign Language Film. In 2000, Wajda received an honorary Oscar from the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences</a>.</p>
<p>In 1988, his film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Possessed_(1988_film)" title="The Possessed (1988 film)">Les Possédés</a></i> was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bear" title="Golden Bear">Golden Bear</a> at the <a href="/wiki/38th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="38th Berlin International Film Festival">38th Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale_1988_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale_1988-37">[37]</a></sup> In 1996, his film <i><a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_(film)" title="Holy Week (film)">Wielki tydzień</a></i> won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Bear" title="Silver Bear">Silver Bear for an outstanding artistic contribution</a> at the <a href="/wiki/46th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="46th Berlin International Film Festival">46th Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale_1996_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale_1996-38">[38]</a></sup> The following year, his film <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Nobody_(1996_film)" title="Miss Nobody (1996 film)">Miss Nobody</a></i> won an Honourable Mention at the <a href="/wiki/47th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="47th Berlin International Film Festival">47th Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale_1997_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale_1997-39">[39]</a></sup> Wajda received an honorary Golden Bear for lifetime achievement at the <a href="/wiki/56th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="56th Berlin International Film Festival">56th Berlin International Film Festival</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berlinale2006_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-berlinale2006-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
