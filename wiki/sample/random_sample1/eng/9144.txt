<p><b>Alpha Phoenicis</b> (<b>α Phoenicis</b>, abbreviated <b>Alpha Phe</b> or <b>α Phe</b>), also named <b>Ankaa</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-CSN_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAU-CSN-8">[8]</a></sup> is the brightest star in the constellation of <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(constellation)" title="Phoenix (constellation)">Phoenix</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Phoenicis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Alpha Phoenicis</i> is the star's <a href="/wiki/Bayer_designation" title="Bayer designation">Bayer designation</a>. It also bore the traditional name <b>Ankaa</b> sometime after 1800, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> العنقاء <i>al-ʿanqā’</i> "the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(mythology)" title="Phoenix (mythology)">phoenix</a>" for the name of the constellation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Medieval Arab astronomers formed the constellation of the <a href="/wiki/Dhow" title="Dhow">dhow</a> (where Phoenix is), so another popular name for the star is <i>Nair al Zaurak</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen1899_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-allen1899-10">[10]</a></sup> from نائر الزوق <i>na’ir az-zawraq</i> "the bright (star) of the skiff". The Latin translation is <i>Cymbae,</i> from <i>lūcida cumbae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen1899_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-allen1899-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2016, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> organized a <a href="/wiki/IAU_Working_Group_on_Star_Names" title="IAU Working Group on Star Names">Working Group on Star Names</a> (WGSN)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGSN_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGSN-11">[11]</a></sup> to catalog and standardize proper names for stars. The WGSN's first bulletin of July 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGSN1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGSN1-12">[12]</a></sup> included a table of the first two batches of names approved by the WGSN; which included <i>Ankaa</i> for this star.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Phoenicis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary" title="Spectroscopic binary">spectroscopic binary</a> star system with components that orbit each other every 3848.8 days (10.5 years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa442_1_365_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-aaa442_1_365-5">[5]</a></sup> The combined <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classification</a> of the system is K0.5 IIIb,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aj132_1_161_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-aj132_1_161-3">[3]</a></sup> which matches the <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> of a lower luminosity <a href="/wiki/Giant_star" title="Giant star">giant star</a>. It has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_visual_magnitude" title="Apparent visual magnitude">apparent visual magnitude</a> of 2.4,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sps1966_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-sps1966-2">[2]</a></sup> so it is somewhat outshone by its first magnitude neighbors <a href="/wiki/Achernar" title="Achernar">Achernar</a> (α Eridani) and <a href="/wiki/Fomalhaut" title="Fomalhaut">Fomalhaut</a> (α Piscis Australis). Based upon <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> measurements, this system is at a distance of about 85 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (26 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a>) from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa474_2_653_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-aaa474_2_653-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">interferometry</a>-measured <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">angular diameter</a> of the primary component, after correcting for <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">limb darkening</a>, is <span class="nowrap">5.25 ± 0.06 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliarcseconds" title="Milliarcseconds">mas</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa431_773_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa431_773-13">[13]</a></sup> which, at its estimated distance, equates to a physical radius of about 15 times the <a href="/wiki/Solar_radius" title="Solar radius">radius of the Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lang2006_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-lang2006-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ankaa is similar to many of the visible stars of the night sky, being an orange giant of relatively average stellar size. It is currently thought to be in the midst of a short but stable <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> burning phase of its <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a>, although it probably will not be long in astronomical terms before it sheds its outer layers as a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> and ends its life quietly as a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a>. Ankaa has a small stellar companion, about which little is known.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
