<p><b>Antonie (Anton) Pannekoek</b> (2 January 1873 – 28 April 1960) was a Dutch <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist_theory" title="Marxist theory">Marxist theorist</a>, and social revolutionary. He was one of the main theorists of <a href="/wiki/Council_communism" title="Council communism">council communism</a> (Dutch: <i>radencommunisme</i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonie_Pannekoek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pannekoek studied <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">Leiden</a> from 1891. Even before he went to college he was interested in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and studied the variability of <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">Polaris</a>. He published his first article, <i>On the Necessity of Further Researches on the Milky Way</i>, as a student. Some years after he had finished his study he started work at the <a href="/wiki/Leiden_Observatory" title="Leiden Observatory">Leiden Observatory</a> (<i>Leidse Sterrewacht</i>), where he wrote his thesis.</p>
<p>After reading <a href="/wiki/Edward_Bellamy" title="Edward Bellamy">Edward Bellamy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Equality_(novel)" title="Equality (novel)">Equality</a></i>, Pannekoek became a convinced <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a> and started studying <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>'s theories. Soon Pannekoek became a well-known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> writer, writing for both Dutch and German magazines. Threatened with dismissal from his job at the observatory for leading a strike support committee and dissatisfied with their oudated methodologies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerber2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerber2-1">[1]</a></sup> he decided to move to Berlin, where he became a lecturer at the school funded by the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a>. His radical opinions soon got him in trouble with both the German government and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_union" title="Labor union">labor unions</a>.</p>
<p>He was on holiday in the Netherlands when the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> broke out. Prevented from returning to Germany, he started work as a <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and science teacher. Though the Leidse Sterrewacht wanted him back, government opposition because of his Marxist sympathies made this fall through. Instead, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amsterdam_city_council" title="Amsterdam city council">Amsterdam city council</a> got him an appointment at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Amsterdam" title="University of Amsterdam">University of Amsterdam</a> in 1925, first as a part-time professor, and in 1932 as a full professor.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonie_Pannekoek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AntonPannekoek1908.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1044" data-file-width="750" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/AntonPannekoek1908.jpg/220px-AntonPannekoek1908.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/AntonPannekoek1908.jpg/330px-AntonPannekoek1908.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/AntonPannekoek1908.jpg/440px-AntonPannekoek1908.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AntonPannekoek1908.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anton Pannekoek</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In his scientific work, Pannekoek started studying the distribution of stars through the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, as well as the structure of our galaxy. Later he became interested in the nature and evolution of stars. Because of these studies, he is considered to be the founder of <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a> as a separate discipline in the Netherlands. He became member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from his theoretical work, he also went on several foreign expeditions to observe <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipses</a> and take spectra of stars. In 1926 he undertook an expedition to <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> in order to chart the Southern Constellations. He was also interested in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_astronomy" title="History of astronomy">history of astronomy</a> and his book, <i>A History of Astronomy</i>, is considered a standard reference on the subject.</p>
<p>His work in galactic structure, astrophysics and the history of astronomy was of international renown and won him an honorary degree from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1936, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Medal_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society">Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society</a> in 1951. The crater <a href="/wiki/Pannekoek_(crater)" title="Pannekoek (crater)">Pannekoek</a> on the Moon and the asteroid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2378_Pannekoek" title="2378 Pannekoek">2378 Pannekoek</a> are named after him.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Amsterdam#Anton_Pannekoek_Astronomical_Institute" title="University of Amsterdam">Anton Pannekoek Astronomical Institute</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Amsterdam" title="University of Amsterdam">University of Amsterdam</a>, of which he had been a director, still carries his name.</p>

<p>As a recognized Marxist theorist, Pannekoek was one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Council_communism" title="Council communism">council communism</a> and a main figure in the <a href="/wiki/Radicalism_(historical)" title="Radicalism (historical)">radical left</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. He was active in the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Netherlands" title="Communist Party of the Netherlands">Communist Party of the Netherlands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Workers%27_Party_of_the_Netherlands" title="Communist Workers' Party of the Netherlands">Communist Workers' Party of the Netherlands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Workers%27_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Workers' Party of Germany">Communist Workers' Party of Germany</a>.</p>
<p>Pannekoek was best known for his writing on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workers%27_councils" title="Workers' councils">workers' councils</a>. He regarded these as a new form of organisation capable of overcoming the limitations of the old institutions of the labour movement, the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a> parties. Basing his theory on what he regarded as the practical lessons of the 1917 <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>, Pannekoek argued that the workers' revolution and the transition from <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> had to be achieved by the workers themselves, democratically organised in workers' councils.</p>
<p>He was a sharp critic of <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a>, social democracy and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leninism" title="Leninism">Leninism</a>. During the early years of the Russian revolution, Pannekoek gave critical support to the <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a>, a position shared by fellow council communist <a href="/wiki/Herman_Gorter" title="Herman Gorter">Herman Gorter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg" title="Rosa Luxemburg">Rosa Luxemburg</a>. He expressed misgivings about the authoritarian tendencies of Leninism, fearing for the socialist character of the Russian Revolution unless it should find a rectifying support in a proletarian revolution in the West. His later analysis of the failure of the Russian revolution was that after Lenin and the Bolsheviks came to power, they crippled the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_(council)" title="Soviet (council)">soviets</a>. Instead of workers' councils, the Bolsheviks had instituted the rule of their party, which in Pannekoek's view is what led to the institution of the Bolsheviks as a new ruling class. He put his views forward in his 1938 book <i>Lenin als filosoof : een kritische beschouwing over de filosofische grondslagen van het Leninisme</i>, originally published under the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> J. Harper, translated in English as <i>Lenin as philosopher - a critical examination of the philosophical basis of Leninism</i> (1948).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism_and_Darwinism">Marxism and Darwinism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonie_Pannekoek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marxism and Darwinism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a pamphlet Pannekoek strongly attacked and rebutted the arguments of <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">Social Darwinists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a>, whom Pannekoek dubbed "Bourgeois Darwinists".</p>
<p>On the basis of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Darwin</a>'s own writings—in particular on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Descent_of_Man,_and_Selection_in_Relation_to_Sex" title="The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex">The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex</a></i> (1871)—Pannekoek stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[The Bourgeois Darwinists] claimed that only the extermination of all the weak is in accordance with nature and that it is necessary to prevent the deterioration of the race, while protection of the weak is unnatural and leads to degeneration. But what do we see? In nature itself, in the animal world, we find that the weak are protected, that they don't need to persist by their individual strength, and that they are not exterminated due to their individual weakness. And this arrangement does not weaken a group in which it is the rule, but strengthens it. The animal groups in which mutual aid is best developed maintain themselves best in the struggle for existence.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Anton Pannekoek, <i><a class="external text" href="https://www.marxists.org/archive/pannekoe/1912/marxism-darwinism.htm" rel="nofollow">Darwinisme en Marxisme</a></i>, 1909</cite></div>
</blockquote>
