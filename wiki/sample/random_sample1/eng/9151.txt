<p><b>Antonio de Leyva, Duke of Terranova, Prince of Ascoli</b> (1480–1536) was a <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spanish</a> general during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_War_of_1521" title="Italian War of 1521">Italian War of 1521</a>, he commanded <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a> during the siege of the city by <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a>, and took part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia" title="Battle of Pavia">Battle of Pavia</a> in 1525. After the death of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Avalos" title="Fernando de Avalos">Fernando de Avalos</a>, he held further commands in Italy during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cognac" title="War of the League of Cognac">War of the League of Cognac</a> and afterwards, finally dying shortly after attempting an invasion of <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_de_Leyva,_Duke_of_Terranova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Belonging to a family from Navarre he made his military debut in the <a href="/wiki/Alpujarras" title="Alpujarras">Alpujarras</a> (1502) during the struggle against the revolting <a href="/wiki/Mud%C3%A9jar" title="Mudéjar">Mudéjares</a> from <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a> and later served in Italy under the <i><a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">Gran Capitán</a></i> (1503–1504). He had a prominent part in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> and was wounded at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ravenna_(1512)" title="Battle of Ravenna (1512)">battle of Ravenna</a> (1512). Later on, under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Avalos" title="Fernando de Avalos">Marquis of Pescara</a>, he fought near Milan and in the unlucky campaign of <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> in 1524.</p>
<p>After this failure he had the post of commander of the garrison of <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a> and here sustained the long siege from the French army led by <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">King Francis I</a> (October 1524 – February 1525) which gave time to the Spanish and Imperial forces to reorganize and to win the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia_(1525)" title="Battle of Pavia (1525)">battle of Pavia</a>.</p>
<p>In 1525 he succeeded to the Marquis of Pescara as commander-in-chief of the Imperial army in the Duchy of Milan. When the last Sforza Duke of Milan <a href="/wiki/Francesco_II_Sforza" title="Francesco II Sforza">Francesco II</a> died in 1535, he was appointed Governor of Milan by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles V</a>.</p>
<p>During a review in honor of de Leyva, Emperor Charles V appeared dressed as a common pikeman and asked to be recorded in the rolls of the Tercio as 'Carlos de Gante, soldier of the gallant Tercio of Antonio de Leyva'.</p>
<p>He died of <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a> during the campaign of Provence in 1536 but was buried in Milan in the now destroyed church of San Dionigi.</p>
<p>His descendants lived in Milan where they became one of the most prominent families. One of his descendants was the infamous <a href="/wiki/The_Nun_of_Monza" title="The Nun of Monza">Nun of Monza</a>.</p>
