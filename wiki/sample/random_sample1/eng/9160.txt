<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <b>Antigone</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>an-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">TI</span></span></b>-gə-nee</i></a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἀντιγόνη</span>) is the daughter/sister of <a href="/wiki/Oedipus" title="Oedipus">Oedipus</a> and his mother, <a href="/wiki/Jocasta" title="Jocasta">Jocasta</a>. The meaning of the name is, as in the case of the masculine equivalent <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Antigonus_(disambiguation)" title="Antigonus (disambiguation)">Antigonus</a>, "worthy of one's parents" or "in place of one's parents".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_depictions">Classical depictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antigone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classical depictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Oedipus" title="Oedipus">Oedipus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_(%C3%96dipus).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="710" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_%28%C3%96dipus%29.svg/220px-Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_%28%C3%96dipus%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_%28%C3%96dipus%29.svg/330px-Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_%28%C3%96dipus%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_%28%C3%96dipus%29.svg/440px-Genealogical_tree_of_Antigone_%28%C3%96dipus%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

Genealogy of Antigone</div>

<p>Antigone is the subject of a story in which she attempts to secure a respectable burial for her brother <a href="/wiki/Polynices" title="Polynices">Polynices</a>. Oedipus's sons, <a href="/wiki/Eteocles" title="Eteocles">Eteocles</a> and Polynices, had shared the rule jointly until they quarrelled, and Eteocles expelled his brother. In Sophocles' account, the two brothers agreed to alternate rule each year, but Eteocles decided not to share power with his brother after his tenure expired. Polynices left the kingdom, gathered an army and attacked the city of Thebes in a conflict called the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Against_Thebes" title="Seven Against Thebes">Seven Against Thebes</a>. Both brothers were killed in the battle.</p>
<p>King <a href="/wiki/Creon" title="Creon">Creon</a>, who has ascended to the throne of Thebes after the death of the brothers, decrees that Polynices is not to be buried or even mourned, on pain of death by stoning. Antigone, Polynices' sister, defies the order, but is caught.</p>
<p>In the oldest version of the story, the burial of Polynices takes place during Oedipus' reign in Thebes, before Oedipus marries his mother, Jocasta. However, in other versions such as <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>' <a href="/wiki/Greek_tragedy" title="Greek tragedy">tragedies</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Oedipus_at_Colonus" title="Oedipus at Colonus">Oedipus at Colonus</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigone_(Sophocles)" title="Antigone (Sophocles)">Antigone</a></i>, it occurs in the years after the banishment and death of Oedipus and Antigone's struggles against Creon.</p>
<p>Antigone is brought before Creon, and states that she knew Creon's law regarding no mourning for Polynices but chose to break it, expounding upon the superiority of 'divine law' to that made by man. She puts the will of the gods ahead of man-made laws, responding to the decision of not granting Polynices a burial with courage, passion and determination.</p>
<p>Sophocles' <i>Antigone</i> ends in disaster, with Antigone being locked in a tomb on Creon's orders. Although Creon has a change of heart and heads to the tomb to release Antigone, Creon's son <a href="/wiki/Haemon" title="Haemon">Hæmon</a> (who was engaged to Antigone) stabs himself after seeing that Antigone has hanged herself in the tomb. Queen <a href="/wiki/Eurydice_of_Thebes" title="Eurydice of Thebes">Eurydice</a>, wife of King Creon, also kills herself following her son's death. She has been forced to weave throughout the entire story and her death alludes to <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai">The Fates</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euripides.27_lost_story">Euripides' lost story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antigone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Euripides' lost story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The dramatist <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a> also wrote a play called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigone_(Euripides)" title="Antigone (Euripides)">Antigone</a></i>, which is lost, but some of the text was preserved by later writers and in passages in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenissae" title="Phoenissae">Phoenissae</a></i>. In Euripides, the calamity is averted by the intercession of <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a> and is followed by the marriage of Antigone and Hæmon. Antigone also plays a role in Euripides' extant play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phoenician_Women" title="The Phoenician Women">The Phoenician Women</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_elsewhere">Appearance elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antigone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearance elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Different elements of the legend appear in other places. A description of an ancient painting by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philostratus_III" title="Philostratus III">Philostratus</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Imagines_(work_by_Philostratus)" title="Imagines (work by Philostratus)">Imagines</a></i> ii. 29) refers to Antigone placing the body of Polynices on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funeral_pyre" title="Funeral pyre">funeral pyre</a>, and this is also depicted on a <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Doria_Pamphili" title="Villa Doria Pamphili">Villa Doria Pamphili</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. And in <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Hyginus" title="Gaius Julius Hyginus">Hyginus'</a> version of the legend, founded apparently on a tragedy by some follower of Euripides, Antigone, on being handed over by <a href="/wiki/Creon" title="Creon">Creon</a> to her lover Hæmon to be slain, is secretly carried off by him and concealed in a shepherd's hut, where she bears him a son, <a href="/wiki/Maeon" title="Maeon">Maeon</a>. When the boy grows up, he attends some funeral games at Thebes, and is recognized by the mark of a dragon on his body. This leads to the discovery that Antigone is still alive. The demi-god <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> then intercedes and pleads with Creon to forgive Hæmon, but in vain. Hæmon then kills Antigone and himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The intercession by Heracles is also represented on a painted vase (circa 380–300 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heydermann_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heydermann-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings_on">Writings on</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antigone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writings on">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegel" title="Hegel">Hegel</a>, in particular in his discussion of <a href="/wiki/Sittlichkeit" title="Sittlichkeit">Sittlichkeit</a> in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenomenology_of_Spirit" title="Phenomenology of Spirit">Phenomenology of Spirit</a></i> and his <i><a href="/wiki/Elements_of_the_Philosophy_of_Right" title="Elements of the Philosophy of Right">Elements of the Philosophy of Right</a></i>, Antigone is figured as exposing a tragic rift between the so-called feminine "Divine Law," which Antigone represents, and the "Human Law," represented by Creon. The psychoanalyst <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Jacques Lacan</a> writes about the ethical dimension of Antigone in his Seminar VII, <i>The Ethics of Psychoanalysis</i>. Others who have written on Antigone include theorist <a href="/wiki/Judith_Butler" title="Judith Butler">Judith Butler</a>, in her book <i>Antigone's Claim</i>, as well as philosopher <a href="/wiki/Slavoj_%C5%BDi%C5%BEek" title="Slavoj Žižek">Slavoj Žižek</a>, in various works, including <i>Interrogating the Real</i> (Bloomsbury: London, 2005) and <i>The Metastases of Enjoyment</i> (Verso: London, 1994).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_productions">Contemporary productions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antigone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contemporary productions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>A new translation of <i>Antigone</i> into English by the Canadian poet <a href="/wiki/Anne_Carson" title="Anne Carson">Anne Carson</a> has been used in a production of the play produced by BAM (Brooklyn Academy of Music) and put on at the BAM Harvey Theatre in Brooklyn, New York. The performance features Juliette Binoche as Antigone, with Ivo van Hove as the director. The play ran from September 24 to October 4, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
