<p>The <b>Algarve</b> (<small>Portuguese pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[aɫˈɡaɾv(ɨ)]</a></span>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">الغرب</span>‎‎ <i>al-Gharb</i> "the West") is the southernmost region of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Portugal" title="Continental Portugal">continental Portugal</a>. It has an area of 4,997 square kilometres (1,929 sq mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dgt_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dgt-3">[3]</a></sup> with 451,006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ine_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ine-4">[4]</a></sup> permanent inhabitants, and incorporates 16 municipalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-law75_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-law75-5">[5]</a></sup> The region has as its administrative centre in the city of <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a>, where both the region's international <a href="/wiki/Faro_Airport" title="Faro Airport">airport</a> (FAO) and <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public university</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Algarve" title="University of Algarve">University of Algarve</a>, are located. Tourism and related activities are extensive and make up the bulk of the Algarve's summer economy. Production of food, which includes fish and other seafood, fruit, oranges, <a href="/wiki/Ceratonia_siliqua" title="Ceratonia siliqua">carob beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_fig" title="Common fig">figs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>, is also economically important in the region. The Algarve is the most popular tourist destination in Portugal, and one of the most popular in Europe. Its population triples to nearly 1.5 million people in the peak holiday season thanks to seasonal residents, and receives an average of 7 million foreign tourists each year. In total, including national visitors, almost 10 million people visit the Algarve annually.</p>
<p>The Algarve is currently the third richest region in Portugal, after <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a> 86% of the European Union average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Human presence in southern Portugal dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> periods. The presence of <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> stones in the area of <a href="/wiki/Vila_do_Bispo" title="Vila do Bispo">Vila do Bispo</a> attests to this presence.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cynetes" title="Cynetes">Cynetes</a>, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Tartessos" title="Tartessos">Tartessos</a>, were established by the sixth century BC in the region of the Algarve. They would be strongly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Celtici" title="Celtici">Celtici</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a> had established trading ports along the coast circa 1000 BC. The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Carthaginians</a> founded Portus Hanibalis—known today as <a href="/wiki/Portim%C3%A3o" title="Portimão">Portimão</a>—in about 550 BC. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> spread through the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> in the second century BC and many Roman ruins can still be seen in the region, notably in <a href="/wiki/Lagos,_Portugal" title="Lagos, Portugal">Lagos</a>. Roman bath complexes and fish salting tanks have been found near the shore in several locations, for example the ones near <a href="/wiki/Vilamoura" title="Vilamoura">Vilamoura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Praia_da_Luz" title="Praia da Luz">Praia da Luz</a>.</p>
<p>In the 5th century the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> took control of the Algarve until the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">Umayyad conquest of Hispania</a> in 711. When the Moors conquered Lagos in 716, it was named Zawaia. Faro, which the Christian residents had called Santa Maria, was renamed Faraon, which means "settlement of the knights." Due to the conquest of the Iberian Peninsula, the region was called <a href="/wiki/Gharb_Al-Andalus" title="Gharb Al-Andalus">Gharb Al-Andalus</a>: <i>Gharb</i> means "the west", while <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">al-Andalus</a> is the Arabic name for the Iberian Peninsula. For several years, the town of <a href="/wiki/Silves,_Portugal" title="Silves, Portugal">Silves</a> was the capital of the region.</p>
<p>In the mid-13th century, during the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Kingdom of Portugal</a> conquered the region in a series of successful military campaigns against the Moors. <i>Al-Gharb</i> became the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Algarve" title="Kingdom of the Algarve">Kingdom of the Algarve</a>, but battles with Muslim forces persisted. It was not until the early 14th century that the Portuguese finally secured the region against the subsequent Muslim attempts to recapture the area. King <a href="/wiki/Afonso_III_of_Portugal" title="Afonso III of Portugal">Afonso III of Portugal</a> started calling himself King of Portugal and the Algarve. After 1471, with the conquest of several territories in the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a> - the area considered an extension of the Algarve - <a href="/wiki/Afonso_V_of_Portugal" title="Afonso V of Portugal">Afonso V of Portugal</a> began fashioning himself "King of Portugal and the Algarve", referring to the European and African possessions.</p>
<p>Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Independence_of_Brazil" title="Independence of Brazil">independence of Brazil</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Portugal,_Brazil_and_the_Algarves" title="United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves">United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves</a> (1815–1822) was an official designation for Portugal which also alluded to the Algarve. Portuguese monarchs continued used this title until the proclamation of the <a href="/wiki/First_Portuguese_Republic" title="First Portuguese Republic">First Portuguese Republic</a> in 1910. Between 1595 and 1808, the Algarve was a semi-autonomous area of Portugal with its own governor, as well as a separate taxation system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (February 2008)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>In the 15th century, Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_the_Navigator" title="Henry the Navigator">Henry the Navigator</a> based himself near <a href="/wiki/Lagos,_Portugal" title="Lagos, Portugal">Lagos</a> and conducted various maritime expeditions which established the colonies that comprised the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a>. It was also from Lagos that <a href="/wiki/Gil_Eanes" title="Gil Eanes">Gil Eanes</a> set sail in 1434 to become the first seafarer to round <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bojador" title="Cape Bojador">Cape Bojador</a> in West Africa. The voyages of discovery brought Lagos fame and fortune. Trade flourished and Lagos became the capital of the historical province of Algarve in 1577 and remained so until the year of the fabled <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">1755 Lisbon earthquake</a>. The earthquake damaged many areas in the Algarve and an accompanying <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> destroyed or damaged coastal fortresses, while coastal towns and villages were heavily damaged except Faro, which was protected by the sandy banks of <a href="/wiki/Ria_Formosa" title="Ria Formosa">Ria Formosa</a> lagoon. In Lagos, the waves reached the top of the city walls. For many Portuguese coastal regions, including the Algarve, the destructive effects of the tsunami were more disastrous than those of the earthquake proper.</p>
<p>In 1807, while <a href="/wiki/Jean-Andoche_Junot" title="Jean-Andoche Junot">Jean-Andoche Junot</a> led the first <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleonic</a> invasion in the north of Portugal, the Algarve was occupied by Spanish troops under <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Godoy" title="Manuel Godoy">Manuel Godoy</a>. Beginning in 1808, and after subsequent battles in various towns and villages, the region was the first to drive out the Spanish occupiers.</p>
<p>The establishment First Portuguese Republic in 1910 marked the end of the Kingdom of Portugal and the Algarve.</p>

<p>The Algarve covers 4997 square km,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dgt_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dgt-3">[3]</a></sup> extending just south of the Tagus valley to the southern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. Its highest point is <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B3ia_(mountain)" title="Fóia (mountain)">Fóia</a>, 902 metres (2,959 ft), in the <a href="/wiki/Serra_de_Monchique" title="Serra de Monchique">mountain range of Monchique</a>. It also includes some islands and islets. The region is also the home of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ria_Formosa_lagoon" title="Ria Formosa lagoon">Ria Formosa lagoon</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a> of over 170 square kilometres and a stopping place for hundreds of different birds. The length of the south-facing coastline is approximately 155 kilometres. Beyond the westernmost point of <a href="/wiki/Cape_St._Vincent" title="Cape St. Vincent">Cape St. Vincent</a> it stretches a further 50 kilometres to the north. The coastline is notable for picturesque <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> caves and grottoes, particularly around Lagos, which are accessible by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powerboat" title="Powerboat">powerboat</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The maximum recorded temperatures in the Algarve fluctuate between 25 °C (77 °F) in winter and 48 °C (118 °F) in summer, with the temperature rarely falling below zero in the winter months. The winter of 2008–09 was exceptionally cold and wet. Temperatures below 0 °C (32 °F) were recorded in coastal areas for the first time in many years.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_geography">Human geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Human geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are about 450,000 permanent inhabitants (90 residents per square kilometre) in the area, although this figure increases to over a million people at the height of summer, due to an influx of tourists. The Algarve has several cities, towns and villages: the region's capital is the city of <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a>, while other cities include <a href="/wiki/Albufeira" title="Albufeira">Albufeira</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagoa_Municipality_(Algarve)" title="Lagoa Municipality (Algarve)">Lagoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagos,_Portugal" title="Lagos, Portugal">Lagos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loul%C3%A9" title="Loulé">Loulé</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olh%C3%A3o_Municipality" title="Olhão Municipality">Olhão</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portim%C3%A3o" title="Portimão">Portimão</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quarteira" title="Quarteira">Quarteira</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silves_Municipality,_Portugal" title="Silves Municipality, Portugal">Silves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tavira_Municipality" title="Tavira Municipality">Tavira</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vila_Real_de_Santo_Ant%C3%B3nio_Municipality" title="Vila Real de Santo António Municipality">Vila Real de Santo António</a>, in addition to various summer retreats such as <a href="/wiki/Vilamoura" title="Vilamoura">Vilamoura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Praia_da_Rocha" title="Praia da Rocha">Praia da Rocha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arma%C3%A7%C3%A3o_de_P%C3%AAra" title="Armação de Pêra">Armação de Pêra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alvor_(Portim%C3%A3o)" title="Alvor (Portimão)">Alvor</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monte_Gordo,_Algarve" title="Monte Gordo, Algarve">Monte Gordo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tavira_Municipality" title="Tavira Municipality">Tavira</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sagres,_Portugal" title="Sagres, Portugal">Sagres</a>.</p>
<p>Before 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Faro_District" title="Faro District">Faro District</a> was the administrative unit governing the Algarve. In 2004 the <i>Greater Metropolitan Area of the Algarve</i> was formed, which was converted into an <a href="/wiki/Intermunicipal_communities_of_Portugal" title="Intermunicipal communities of Portugal">intermunicipal community</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Algarve is also a <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">NUTS</a> II and NUTS III statistical region. The intermunicipal community of Algarve is subdivided into 16 municipalities:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-law75_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-law75-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Agricultural products of the region include <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">fig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a>, orange, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carob_bean" title="Carob bean">carob bean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arbutus_unedo" title="Arbutus unedo">strawberry tree</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cork_oak" title="Cork oak">cork oak</a>. Horticulture is important and the region's landscape was known for the large areas of land covered with plastic <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> which are used for that end. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_in_Portugal" title="Fishing in Portugal">Fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> are important activities in the coastal area of Algarve, with <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sole_(fish)" title="Sole (fish)">soles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyprinids" title="Cyprinids">cyprinids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gilt-head_bream" title="Gilt-head bream">gilt-head bream</a> and various seafood, including the <a href="/wiki/Grooved_carpet_shell" title="Grooved carpet shell">grooved carpet shell</a>, being the major productions. Algarve's wines are also renowned. There are four wines in the region which have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_Designation_of_Origin" title="Protected Designation of Origin">Protected Designation of Origin</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Denomina%C3%A7%C3%A3o_de_Origem_Controlada" title="Denominação de Origem Controlada">Denominação de Origem Controlada</a></i> – DOC): <a href="/wiki/Lagoa_DOC" title="Lagoa DOC">Lagoa DOC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagos_DOC" title="Lagos DOC">Lagos DOC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portim%C3%A3o_DOC" title="Portimão DOC">Portimão DOC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tavira_DOC" title="Tavira DOC">Tavira DOC</a>. <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">Food processing</a>, cement and construction, are the main industries. Tourism related activities are extensive and make the bulk of Algarve's economy during summer time. The Algarve's economy has always been closely linked to the sea, and fishing has been an important activity since ancient times. Only since the 1960s, has the region embraced tourism, which has become its most important economic activity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the 1960s, Algarve has been experiencing a large development due to the need to accommodate its visitors. The region started the construction of better <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrastructures" title="Infrastructures">infrastructures</a>, mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roads" title="Roads">roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_grids" title="Power grids">power grids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hospitals" title="Hospitals">hospitals</a> and better <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">housing</a>. Due to the austerity measures introduced in 2011, tolls have been introduced on the main motorway that crosses the region which is hoped will offset the expense of its construction. It also started the construction of a huge variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotels" title="Hotels">hotels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resorts" title="Resorts">resorts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golf_courses" title="Golf courses">golf courses</a> (which are considered to be some of the best in Europe) and <a href="/wiki/Villa" title="Villa">villas</a>. All this led to a huge <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">development</a> in the region, especially for locals who had previously lived in poorer circumstances. Today, Algarve is amongst the regions in Portugal with best <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> and <a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">safety</a>. And it's one of the many reasons why millions of tourists, mostly European, choose this sunny part of Portugal as their holiday destination.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1960s the Algarve became a very popular destination for tourists, mainly from the United Kingdom. It has since become a common destination for people from Germany, the Netherlands and Ireland. Many of these tourists own their own property in the region. There are Algarve-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publications" title="Publications">publications</a> and newspapers written in English specifically for this community.</p>
<p>Tourist attractions in the region include its beaches, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_of_Portugal" title="Climate of Portugal">Mediterranean climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crime_in_Portugal" title="Crime in Portugal">safety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_cuisine" title="Portuguese cuisine">cuisine</a> and relatively low costs. Well-known beaches in the Algarve range from <a href="/wiki/Praia_da_Marinha" title="Praia da Marinha">Marinha Beach</a> to <a href="/wiki/Arma%C3%A7%C3%A3o_de_P%C3%AAra" title="Armação de Pêra">Armação de Pêra</a>. A well-known <a href="/wiki/Spa_town" title="Spa town">spa town</a> is <a href="/wiki/Caldas_de_Monchique" title="Caldas de Monchique">Caldas de Monchique</a>. In addition to its natural features and beaches, the Algarve has invested in the creation of a network of <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf courses</a>.</p>
<p>The Algarve is also popular for religious tourism, notably pilgrimages to the Sanctuary of Our Lady of Piety (best known as the Sovereign Mother), a Marian shrine dedicated to the patron saint of <a href="/wiki/Loul%C3%A9" title="Loulé">Loulé</a>, that attract thousands of pilgrims of the Catholic faithful to the city, or with the international pilgrimages to the apparitions site of Our Lady Mother of Goodness occurred near <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Marcos_da_Serra" title="São Marcos da Serra">São Marcos da Serra</a>.</p>

<p>Algarve's mild climate has attracted interest from Northern Europeans wishing to have a holiday home or residence in the region. Being a region of Portugal, and therefore in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_citizen" title="EU citizen">EU citizen</a> has the right to freely buy property and reside with little formality in the Algarve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> British expatriates, followed by German, Dutch and Scandinavians, are among the largest groups wishing to own a home in this sunny region of Portugal.</p>
<p>Tourism plays an important role in the economy of the Algarve. A large number of seasonal job opportunities are tourism-related and are fulfilled by thousands of immigrant workers from other countries. In March 2007, the Portuguese economic minister, Manuel Pinho, announced the creation of the "Allgarve" brand, as a part of a strategical promotion of the Algarve as a tourism destination for foreign citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> According to World Travel Awards, Algarve is the Europe's Leading Golf Destination 2013 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> There are over 25 top-class courses in the Algarve, most of which were designed by legendary names such as Nick Faldo, Seve Ballesteros, Jack Nicklaus and Christy O'Connor Jnr.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Accommodation">Accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Accommodation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Accommodation in the Algarve ranges from high rise resorts in places like Albufeira to traditional guesthouses, located in the small towns and villages surrounding the Algarve coast. Over the past 50 years the Algarve has seen an increase in development, particularly from non-Portuguese developers. Over the past few years many tourists visiting the Algarve have moved away from the resorts, and have chosen the comfort of a traditional Algarve guesthouse, many of these run by ex-pats from England, the Netherlands, and Germany who have escaped to the Algarve for a higher <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Algarve" title="University of Algarve">University of Algarve</a>, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a> with an extension in <a href="/wiki/Portim%C3%A3o" title="Portimão">Portimão</a>, is a public university which awards all academic degrees in fields ranging from <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biology</a> to economics to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_engineering" title="Environmental engineering">environmental engineering</a>. There are also several higher education private institutions, state-run and private secondary education schools, including a number of <a href="/wiki/International_school" title="International school">international schools</a>, and a wide network of <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">kindergartens</a> and primary schools.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Algarve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EstadioAlgarve.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="394" data-file-width="525" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/EstadioAlgarve.JPG/200px-EstadioAlgarve.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/EstadioAlgarve.JPG/300px-EstadioAlgarve.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/EstadioAlgarve.JPG/400px-EstadioAlgarve.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

The 30,000-seater Algarve Stadium (<i><a href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_Algarve" title="Estádio Algarve">Estádio Algarve</a></i>) was built as a venue for <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2004" title="UEFA Euro 2004">UEFA Euro 2004</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Algarve has many sports clubs, including football teams (<a href="/wiki/S.C._Olhanense" title="S.C. Olhanense">S.C. Olhanense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portimonense_S.C." title="Portimonense S.C.">Portimonense S.C.</a>) which play in the first, second and third lay tiers of professional football. <a href="/wiki/S.C._Farense" title="S.C. Farense">S.C. Farense</a> is the most successful football club in the Algarve and play in the <a href="/wiki/Campeonato_de_Portugal_(league)" title="Campeonato de Portugal (league)">Campeonato de Portugal</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_Tavira" title="Team Tavira">Team Tavira</a> is a noted Portuguese cycling team. The major stadium in the Algarve is the <i><a href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_Algarve" title="Estádio Algarve">Estádio Algarve</a></i>, where SC Farense and <a href="/wiki/Louletano_D.C." title="Louletano D.C.">Louletano D.C.</a> play their home matches.</p>
<p>The region is also host to the annual <a href="/wiki/Algarve_Cup" title="Algarve Cup">Algarve Cup</a>, an invitational tournament for national teams in <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_association_football" title="Women's association football">women's association football</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Algarve_International_Circuit" title="Algarve International Circuit">Algarve International Circuit</a>, a 4.692 kilometres (2.915 mi) race circuit, is located in <a href="/wiki/Portim%C3%A3o" title="Portimão">Portimão</a>.</p>

<p>The Algarve is famous for its pottery and ceramics, particularly hand-painted pottery and <i><a href="/wiki/Azulejo" title="Azulejo">azulejos</a></i>, which are painted, <a href="/wiki/Tin-glazing" title="Tin-glazing">tin-glazed</a> ceramic <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tiles</a>. There are numerous ceramics and pottery outlets throughout the Algarve. For working potteries and ceramics workshops the main, or best-known, pottery centers are located in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Almancil" title="Almancil">Almancil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porches_(Lagoa)" title="Porches (Lagoa)">Porches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loul%C3%A9" title="Loulé">Loulé</a>, but there are many other potteries and workshops in the Algarve region.</p>
