<p>A <b>granular material</b> is a conglomeration of discrete <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopic</a> <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particles</a> characterized by a loss of energy whenever the particles interact (the most common example would be <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> when grains collide).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The constituents that compose granular material must be large enough such that they are not subject to thermal motion fluctuations. Thus, the lower size limit for grains in granular material is about 1 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a>. On the upper size limit, the physics of granular materials may be applied to ice floes where the individual grains are <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> with individual grains being <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>.</p>
<p>Some examples of granular materials are <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_flakes" title="Corn flakes">corn flakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ball_bearing" title="Ball bearing">ball bearings</a>. <a href="/wiki/Powder_(substance)" title="Powder (substance)">Powders</a> are a special class of granular material due to their small particle size, which makes them more <a href="/wiki/Cohesion_(chemistry)" title="Cohesion (chemistry)">cohesive</a> and more easily <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspended</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>. Granular materials are commercially important in applications as diverse as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> industry, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_production" title="Energy production">energy production</a>. Research into granular materials is thus directly applicable and goes back at least to <a href="/wiki/Charles-Augustin_de_Coulomb" title="Charles-Augustin de Coulomb">Charles-Augustin de Coulomb</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">law of friction</a> was originally stated for granular materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldier</a>/<a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> Brigadier <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Alger_Bagnold" title="Ralph Alger Bagnold">Ralph Alger Bagnold</a> was an early pioneer of the physics of granular matter and whose book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Physics_of_Blown_Sand_and_Desert_Dunes" title="The Physics of Blown Sand and Desert Dunes">The Physics of Blown Sand and Desert Dunes</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> remains an important reference to this day.</p>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_science" title="Material science">material scientist</a> Patrick Richard, "Granular materials are ubiquitous in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> and are the second-most manipulated material in industry (the first one is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some sense, granular materials do not constitute a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phases_of_matter" title="Phases of matter">phase of matter</a> but have characteristics reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> depending on the average energy per grain. However, in each of these states granular materials also exhibit properties which are unique.</p>
<p>Granular materials also exhibit a wide range of pattern forming behaviors when excited (e.g. vibrated or allowed to flow). As such granular materials under excitation can be thought of as an example of a <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex system</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Granular_solids">Granular solids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granular_material&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Granular solids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Force_chain" title="Force chain">Force chain</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stress_transmision.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="735" data-file-width="735" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Stress_transmision.svg/150px-Stress_transmision.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Stress_transmision.svg/225px-Stress_transmision.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Stress_transmision.svg/300px-Stress_transmision.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stress_transmision.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Chain of transmission of stress forces in a granular media</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the average energy of the individual grains is low and the grains are fairly stationary relative to each other, the granular material acts like a solid. In general, stress in a granular solid is not distributed uniformly but is conducted away along so-called <a href="/wiki/Force_chain" title="Force chain">force chains</a> which are networks of grains resting on one another. Between these chains are regions of low stress whose grains are shielded for the effects of the grains above by <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaulting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arching</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Granular_gases">Granular gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granular_material&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Granular gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If the granular material is driven harder such that contacts between the grains become highly infrequent, the material enters a gaseous state. Correspondingly, one can define a granular temperature equal to the root mean square of grain velocity fluctuations that is analogous to <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">thermodynamic temperature</a>. Unlike conventional gases, granular materials will tend to cluster and clump due to the [dissipative] nature of the collisions between grains. This clustering has some interesting consequences. For example, if a partially partitioned box of granular materials is vigorously shaken then grains will over time tend to collect in one of the partitions rather than spread evenly into both partitions as would happen in a conventional gas. This effect, known as the granular <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_demon" title="Maxwell's demon">Maxwell's demon</a>, does not violate any thermodynamics principles since energy is constantly being lost from the system in the process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamming_transition">Jamming transition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granular_material&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jamming transition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Granular_jamming.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="972" data-file-width="739" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Granular_jamming.svg/150px-Granular_jamming.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Granular_jamming.svg/225px-Granular_jamming.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Granular_jamming.svg/300px-Granular_jamming.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Granular_jamming.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jamming during discharge of granular material is due to arch formation (red spheres)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Granular systems are known to exhibit <a href="/wiki/Jamming_(physics)" title="Jamming (physics)">jamming</a> and undergo a jamming transition which is thought of as a thermodynamic phase transition to a jammed state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lubachevsky-Stillinger_algorithm" title="Lubachevsky-Stillinger algorithm">Lubachevsky-Stillinger algorithm</a> of jamming allows one to produce simulated jammed granular configurations. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pattern_formation">Pattern formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granular_material&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pattern formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Excited granular matter is a rich pattern-forming system. Some of the pattern-forming behaviours seen in granular materials are:</p>
<ul>
<li>The un-mixing or segregation of unlike grains under vibration and flow. An example of this is the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazil_nut_effect" title="Brazil nut effect">Brazil nut effect</a> where Brazil nuts rise to the top of a packet of mixed nuts when shaken.The cause of this effect is that when shaken, granular (and some other) materials move in a circular pattern. some larger materials (Brazil nuts) get stuck while going down the circle and therefore stay on the top.</li>
<li>The formation of surface patterns in vibrated granular layers. These patterns include but are not limited to stripes, squares and hexagons. These patterns are thought to be composed of fundamental excitations of the surface known as <a href="/wiki/Oscillon" title="Oscillon">oscillons</a>.</li>
<li>The formation of sand <a href="/wiki/Ripple_marks" title="Ripple marks">ripples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandsheet" title="Sandsheet">sandsheets</a></li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_(2007-05-226).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_%282007-05-226%29.jpg/150px-Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_%282007-05-226%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_%282007-05-226%29.jpg/225px-Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_%282007-05-226%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_%282007-05-226%29.jpg/300px-Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_%282007-05-226%29.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Argenberg_-_Sand_dunes_(2007-05-226).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sand dunes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some of the pattern-forming behaviours have been possible to reproduce in computer simulations. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> There are two main computational approaches to such simulations, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Time-stepped&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Time-stepped (page does not exist)">time-stepped</a> and <a href="/wiki/Event-driven_programming" title="Event-driven programming">event-driven</a>, the former being the most efficient for a higher density of the material and the motions of a lower intensity, and the latter for a lower density of the material and the motions of a higher intensity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustic_effects">Acoustic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granular_material&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acoustic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some beach sands, such as those of the aptly named <a href="/wiki/Tidal_River_(Victoria)" title="Tidal River (Victoria)">Squeaky Beach</a>, exhibit squeaking when walked upon. Some desert dunes are known to exhibit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booming_dunes" title="Booming dunes">booming</a> during avalanching or when their surface is otherwise disturbed. Granular materials discharged from silos produce loud acoustic emissions in a process known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Silo_honking&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Silo honking (page does not exist)">silo honking</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Granulation">Granulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granular_material&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Granulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Granulation_(process)" title="Granulation (process)">Granulation (process)</a></div>
<p><b>Granulation</b> is the act or process in which primary <a href="/wiki/Powder_(substance)" title="Powder (substance)">powder</a> <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particles</a> are made to adhere to form larger, multiparticle entities called <i>granules.</i></p>
