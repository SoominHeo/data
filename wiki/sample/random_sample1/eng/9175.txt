<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Al-Nahda</h1>

<p><b>Al-Nahda</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">النهضة</span>‎‎ / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALA-LC" title="ALA-LC">ALA-LC</a>: <i>an-Nahḍah</i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> for "awakening" or "<a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a>") was a cultural renaissance that began in the late 19th and early 20th centuries in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, then later moving to <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a>-ruled Arabic-speaking regions including <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and others. It is often regarded as a period of intellectual modernization and reform.</p>
<p>In traditional scholarship, the Nahda is seen as connected to the cultural shock brought on by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/French_campaign_in_Egypt_and_Syria" title="French campaign in Egypt and Syria">invasion</a> of Egypt in 1798, and the reformist drive of subsequent rulers such as <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali</a>. However, recent scholarship has shown that the Middle Eastern and North African Renaissance was a cultural reform program that was as "autogenetic" as it was Western inspired, linked to the Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a> and internal changes in political economy and communal reformations in Egypt and Syro-Lebanon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheehi2004_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheehi2004-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Egyptian <i>nahda</i> was articulated in purely Egyptian terms, and its participants were mostly <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> was the geographical center of the movement. But al-Nahda was also felt in neighboring Arab capitals, notably <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>. The shared language of Arabic-speaking nations ensured that the accomplishments of the movement could be quickly picked up by intellectuals in Arab countries.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a>-ruled Arabic regions, major influence and motive were the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">tanzimat</a> reforms of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, which brought a constitutional order to Ottoman politics and engendered a new political class and later the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turk_Revolution" title="Young Turk Revolution">Young Turk Revolution</a>, which allowed proliferation of press and other publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rifa.27a_Rafi.27_El-Tahtawi">Rifa'a Rafi' El-Tahtawi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rifa'a Rafi' El-Tahtawi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rifa%27a_el-Tahtawi" title="Rifa'a el-Tahtawi">Rifa'a el-Tahtawi</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tahtawi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="216" data-file-width="150" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f7/Tahtawi.jpg" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tahtawi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rifa'a el-Tahtawy, 1801–1873.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Egyptian scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rifa%27a_el-Tahtawi" title="Rifa'a el-Tahtawi">Rifa'a el-Tahtawi</a> (1801–1873) is widely seen as the pioneering figure of the Nahda. He was sent to Paris in 1826 by Muhammad Ali's government to study Western sciences and educational methods, although originally to serve as <a href="/wiki/Imam" title="Imam">Imam</a> for the Egyptian cadets training at the Paris military academy. He came to hold a very positive view of French society, although not without criticisms. Learning French, he began translating important scientific and cultural works into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Classical Arabic</a>. He also witnessed the <a href="/wiki/July_Revolution" title="July Revolution">July Revolution</a> of 1830, against <a href="/wiki/Charles_X_of_France" title="Charles X of France">Charles X</a>, but, perhaps understandably, was careful in commenting on the matter in his reports to Muhammad Ali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His political views, originally influenced by the conservative Islamic teachings at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Azhar" title="Al-Azhar">El Azhar</a> university, changed on a number of matters, and he came to advocate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentarism" title="Parliamentarism">parliamentarism</a> and women's education.</p>
<p>After five years in France, he then returned to Egypt to implement the philosophy of reform he had developed there, summarizing his views in the book <i>Takhlis al-Ibriz fi Talkhis Bariz</i> (sometimes translated as <i>The Quintessence of Paris</i>), published in 1834. It is written in <a href="/wiki/Rhymed_prose" title="Rhymed prose">rhymed prose</a>, and describes France and Europe from an Egyptian Muslim viewpoint. Tahtawi's suggestion was that the Egypt and the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Muslim world</a> had much to learn from Europe, and generally embraced Western society, but also held that reforms should be adapted to the values of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_culture" title="Islamic culture">Islamic culture</a>. This brand of self-confident but open-minded modernism came to be the defining creed of al-Nahda.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Butrus_Al-Bustani">Butrus Al-Bustani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Butrus Al-Bustani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Butrus_al-Bustani" title="Butrus al-Bustani">Butrus al-Bustani</a> (1819–1893) was born to a <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_people" title="Lebanese people">Lebanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maronite_Christian" title="Maronite Christian">Maronite Christian</a> family in the village of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dibbiye" title="Dibbiye">Dibbiye</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chouf" title="Chouf">Chouf</a> region, in January 1819. A polyglot, educator, and activist, al-Bustani was a tour de force in the nahda centered in mid-nineteenth century Beirut. After becoming involved with the American missionaries, he converted to Protestantism becoming a leader in the native Protestant church. Inititally, he taught in the schools of the Protestant missionaries at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%27Abey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="'Abey (page does not exist)">'Abey</a> and was a central figure in the missionaries' translation of the Bible into Arabic. Despite his close ties with the Americans, al-Bustani increasingly became independent eventually breaking away.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Butrus_bustani.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="183" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Butrus_bustani.jpg/150px-Butrus_bustani.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Butrus_bustani.jpg 1.5x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Butrus_bustani.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Butrus al Bustani, 1860.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the bloody <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1860_Druze%E2%80%93Maronite_conflict" title="1860 Druze–Maronite conflict">1860 Druze–Maronite conflict</a> and the increasing entrenchment of <a href="/wiki/Confessionalism_(religion)" title="Confessionalism (religion)">confessionalism</a>, al-Bustani founded the National School or al-madrasa al-wataniyya in 1863 on secular principles. This school employed the leading nahda "pioneers" of Beirut and graduated a generation of nahda thinkers. At the same time, he compiled and published several school textbooks and dictionaries to become known famously as the Master of the Arabic Renaissance.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Elite"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Nahda#endnote_Elite">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the social, national and political spheres, al-Bustani founded associations with a view to forming a national elite and launched a series of appeals for unity in his magazine Nafir Suriya.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Elite"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Nahda#endnote_Elite">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the cultural/scientific fields, he published a fortnightly review and two daily newspapers. In addition, he began work, together with Drs <a href="/wiki/Eli_Smith" title="Eli Smith">Eli Smith</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornelius_Van_Dyck" title="Cornelius Van Dyck">Cornelius Van Dyck</a> of the American Mission, on a translation of the Bible into Arabic known as the Smith-Van Dyke translation.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Translation"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Nahda#endnote_Translation">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>His prolific output and groundbreaking work led the creation of modern Arabic expository prose. While educated by westerners and a strong advocate of western technology, he was a fierce secularist, playing a decisive role in formulating the principles of <a href="/wiki/Syrian_nationalism" title="Syrian nationalism">Syrian nationalism</a> (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Arab_nationalism" title="Arab nationalism">Arab nationalism</a>).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_Sheehi" title="Stephen Sheehi">Stephen Sheehi</a> states that al-Bustani's "importance does not lay in his prognosis of Arab culture or his national pride. Nor is his advocacy of discriminately adopting Western knowledge and technology to "awaken" the Arabs' inherent ability for cultural success (najah) unique among his generation. Rather, his contribution lies in the act of elocution. That is, his writing articulates a specific formula for native progress that expresses a synthetic vision of the matrix of modernity within Ottoman Syria."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheehi2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheehi2011-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hayreddin_Pasha">Hayreddin Pasha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hayreddin Pasha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Pasha" title="Hayreddin Pasha">Hayreddin Pasha al-Tunsi</a> (1820-1890) had made his way to Ottoman Tunisia as a slave, where he rose through the ranks of the government of <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_I_ibn_Mustafa" title="Ahmad I ibn Mustafa">Ahmad Bey</a>, the modernizing ruler of Tunisia. He soon was made responsible for diplomatic missions to the Ottoman Empire and the countries of Europe, bringing him into contact with the Western ideals, as well as with the <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">Tanzimat</a> reforms of the Ottoman Empire. He served as Prime Minister of Tunisia from 1859 until 1882. In this period, he was a major force of modernization in Tunisia.</p>
<p>In numerous writing, he vision of a seamless blending of Islamic tradition with Western modernization. Basing his beliefs on European Enlightenment writings and Arabic political thought, his main concern was with preserving the autonomy of the Tunisian people in particular, and Muslim peoples in general. In this quest, he ended up bringing forth what amounted to the earliest example of Muslim constitutionalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His modernizing theories have had an enormous influence on Tunisian and Ottoman thought.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_influence_of_al-Nahda">The influence of al-Nahda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The influence of al-Nahda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="743" data-file-width="520" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg/180px-Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg/270px-Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg/360px-Sayyid_Dscham%C4%81l_ad-D%C4%ABn_al-Afgh%C4%81n%C4%AB.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>

<p>In the religious field, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamal_al-Din_al-Afghani" title="Jamal al-Din al-Afghani">Jamal al-Din al-Afghani</a> (1839–1897) gave <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> a modernist reinterpretation and fused adherence to the faith with an anti-colonial doctrine that preached <a href="/wiki/Pan-Islamism" title="Pan-Islamism">Pan-Islamic</a> solidarity in the face of European pressures. He also favored the replacement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authoritarian" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a> monarchies with representative rule, and denounced what he perceived as the dogmatism, stagnation and corruption of the Islam of his age. He claimed that tradition (<i><a href="/wiki/Taqlid" title="Taqlid">taqlid</a></i>, تقليد) had stifled Islamic debate and repressed the correct practices of the faith. Al-Afghani's case for a redefinition of old interpretations of Islam, and his bold attacks on traditional religion, would become vastly influential with the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphate</a> in 1924. This created a void in the religious doctrine and social structure of Islamic communities which had been only temporarily reinstated by <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Abdul Hamid II</a> in an effort to bolster universal Muslim support, suddenly vanished. It forced Muslims to look for new interpretations of the faith, and to re-examine widely held dogma; exactly what al-Afghani had urged them to do decades earlier.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muhammad_Abduh.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="590" data-file-width="414" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Muhammad_Abduh.jpg/180px-Muhammad_Abduh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Muhammad_Abduh.jpg/270px-Muhammad_Abduh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Muhammad_Abduh.jpg/360px-Muhammad_Abduh.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muhammad_Abduh.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Muhammad Abduh</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Al-Afghani influenced many, but greatest among his followers is undoubtedly his student <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Abduh" title="Muhammad Abduh">Muhammad Abduh</a> (1849–1905), whose teachings would play a similarly important role in the reform of the practice of Islam. Like al-Afghani, Abduh accused traditionalist Islamic authorities of moral and intellectual corruption, and of imposing a doctrinaire form of Islam on the <a href="/wiki/Ummah" title="Ummah">ummah</a>, that had hindered correct applications of the faith. He therefore advocated that Muslims should return to the "true" Islam practiced by the ancient Caliphs, which he held had been both rational and divinely inspired. Applying the original message of the <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">Prophet</a> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> with no interference of tradition or the faulty interpretations of his followers, would automatically create the just society ordained by God in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>, and so empower the Muslim world to stand against colonization and injustices.</p>
<p>Among the students of Abduh were Syrian Islamic scholar and reformer <a href="/wiki/Rashid_Rida" title="Rashid Rida">Rashid Rida</a> (1865–1935), who continued his legacy, and expanded on the concept of just Islamic government. His theses on how an <a href="/wiki/Islamic_state" title="Islamic state">Islamic state</a> should be organized remain influential among modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist" title="Islamist">Islamists</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_Brotherhood" title="Muslim Brotherhood">Muslim Brotherhood</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shia_Islam">Shia Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shia Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shi%27a" title="Shi'a">Shi'a</a> scholars contributed to the renaissance movement, such as the linguist shaykh <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Rida" title="Ahmad Rida">Ahmad Rida</a>, the historian <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Jaber_Al_Safa" title="Muhammad Jaber Al Safa">Muhammad Jaber Al Safa</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suleiman_Daher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suleiman Daher (page does not exist)">Suleiman Daher</a>. Important political reforms took place simultaneously also in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and Shi'a religious beliefs saw important developments with the systematization of a religious <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>. A wave of political reform followed, with the constitutional movement in Iran, to some extent paralleling the Egyptian Nahda reforms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_literature" title="Egyptian literature">Egyptian literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_literature" title="Arabic literature">Arabic literature</a></div>
<p>Through the 19th century and early 20th centuries, a number of new developments in Arabic literature started to emerge, initially sticking closely to the classical forms, but addressing modern themes and the challenges faced by the Arab world in the modern era.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="404" data-file-width="285" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg/180px-Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg/270px-Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/73/Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Qestaki_al-Homsi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Qustaki al-Himsi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1865, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syrian" title="Syrian">Syrian</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Francis_Marrash" title="Francis Marrash">Francis Marrash</a> published <i>Ghabat al-haqq</i>, an allegory which deals with ideas of peace, freedom and equality. Aleppine writer <a href="/wiki/Qustaki_al-Himsi" title="Qustaki al-Himsi">Qustaki al-Himsi</a> is considered to have founded modern <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">literary criticism</a>, with one of his works, <i>The researcher's source in the science of criticism</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maryana_Marrash" title="Maryana Marrash">Maryana Marrash</a> was the first Arab woman in the nineteenth century to revive the tradition of the literary <a href="/wiki/Salon_(gathering)" title="Salon (gathering)">salon</a> in the Arab world, with the salon she ran in her family home in <a href="/wiki/Aleppo" title="Aleppo">Aleppo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1914, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Husayn_Haykal" title="Muhammad Husayn Haykal">Muhammad Husayn Haykal</a> (1888–1956) published <i><a href="/wiki/Zaynab_(novel)" title="Zaynab (novel)">Zaynab</a></i>, often considered the first modern Egyptian novel. Prose writing rapidly developed from this date.</p>
<p>A group of young writers formed <i>The New School</i>, and in 1925 began publishing the weekly literary journal <i>al-Fajr</i> (<i>The Dawn</i>), which would have a great impact on Arabic literature. The group was especially influenced by 19th-century Russian writers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dostoyevsky" title="Dostoyevsky">Dostoyevsky</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolstoy" title="Tolstoy">Tolstoy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gogol" title="Gogol">Gogol</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG/200px-Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG/300px-Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG/400px-Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Villa_Borghese_-_monumento_Ahmed_Shawky_01215.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monument to Shawqi in <a href="/wiki/Villa_Borghese" title="Villa Borghese">Villa Borghese</a>, Rome</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In poetry, the Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shawqi" title="Ahmad Shawqi">Ahmad Shawqi</a>, among others, began to explore the limits of the classical qasida, although he remained a clearly neo-classical poet. After him, others, including <a href="/wiki/Hafez_Ibrahim" title="Hafez Ibrahim">Hafez Ibrahim</a> began to use poetry to explore themes of <a href="/wiki/Anti-imperialism" title="Anti-imperialism">anticolonialism</a> as well as the classical concepts. The <a href="/wiki/Mahjar" title="Mahjar">Mahjar poets</a>, of whom the most famous is the Lebanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khalil_Gibran" title="Khalil Gibran">Khalil Gibran</a> (1883–1931), but which included other writers, in South America as well as the USA, further contributed to the development of the forms available to Arab poets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Prophet_(book)" title="The Prophet (book)">The Prophet</a></i>, published in 1923 by the <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>-based Gibran, is perhaps the best known work of the era in the West, but was actually first written in English (Read <i>The Prophet</i> online here <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.columbia.edu/~gm84/gibtable.html" rel="nofollow">[5]</a>). Gibran's associate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab-American" title="Arab-American">Arab-American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Pen_League" title="New York Pen League">League of the Pen</a> (<i>al-Rabita al-Qalamiyya</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mikha%27il_Na%27ima" title="Mikha'il Na'ima">Mikha'il Na'ima</a> (1898–1989) would later return to Lebanon and contribute to the development of the novel there.</p>
<p>One of the main literary innovators in the later stages of al-Nahda was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prof." title="Prof.">Prof.</a> <a href="/wiki/Taha_Hussein" title="Taha Hussein">Taha Hussein</a> (1889–1973), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blind</a> child of an Egyptian peasant family who is today widely considered an intellectual giant of Egypt, and apart from his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'anic</a> education at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Azhar" title="Al-Azhar">al-Azhar</a> held triple doctorates from <a href="/wiki/Cairo_University" title="Cairo University">Cairo University</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Sorbonne" title="University of Sorbonne">University of Sorbonne</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a>. He served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Education" title="Minister of Education">Minister of Education</a> in Egypt in the 1950s, and was responsible for creating free and mandatory schooling. His best known book is the autobiographical <i>el-Ayyam</i> (<i>The Days</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Middle_Eastern_newspapers" title="History of Middle Eastern newspapers">History of Middle Eastern newspapers</a></div>
<p>The first printing press in the Middle East was in the monastery of St Anthony of Kozhaya in Lebanon and dates back to 1610. It printed Arabic books using the Syriac letters. The first printing press with Arabic letters, was built in St John's monastery in Khinshara, Lebanon by "Al-Shamas Abdullah Zakher" in 1734. The printing press operated from 1734 till 1899.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1821, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali of Egypt</a> brought the first <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> to Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Modern printing techniques spread rapidly and gave birth to a modern Egyptian press, which brought the reformist trends of the al-Nahda into contact with the emerging Egyptian middle class of clerks and tradesmen. The Egyptian newspaper <a href="/wiki/Al-Ahram" title="Al-Ahram">al-Ahram</a> dates from 1875, and between 1870 and 1900, Beirut alone saw the founding of about 40 new periodicals and 15 newspapers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The efforts at translating European and American literature led to the modernization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a> [citation]. Many scientific and academic terms, as well as words for modern inventions, were incorporated in modern Arabic vocabulary, and new words were coined in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triliteral" title="Triliteral">Arabic root system</a> to cover for others. The development of a modern press (see above) ensured that <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">classical Arabic</a> spread through society in its updated form, <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">Modern Standard Arabic</a>, which is used still today all over the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a>. The Lebanese scholar <a href="/wiki/Butrus_al-Bustani" title="Butrus al-Bustani">Butrus al-Bustani</a> (1819–83) created, in the late 19th century, the first modern Arabic encyclopedia, drawing both on medieval Arab scholars and Western methods of <a href="/wiki/Lexicography" title="Lexicography">lexicography</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmad_Reda" title="Ahmad Reda">Ahmad Reda</a> created the first modern dictionary of Arabic, "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Matn_al-Lugha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Matn al-Lugha (page does not exist)">Matn al-Lugha</a>" ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisan_al-Arab" title="Lisan al-Arab">Lisan al-Arab</a>" was written in the 13th century).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Nahda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1876, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> promulgated a constitution, as the crowning accomplishment of the <a href="/wiki/Tanzimat" title="Tanzimat">tanzimat</a> reforms (1839–76) and inaugurating the Empire's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Constitutional_Era_(Ottoman_Empire)" title="First Constitutional Era (Ottoman Empire)">First Constitutional Era</a>. It was inspired by European methods of government and designed to bring the Empire back on level with the Western powers. The constitution was opposed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultan_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Sultan of the Ottoman Empire">Sultan</a>, whose powers it checked, but had vast symbolic and political importance.</p>
<p>The introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentarism" title="Parliamentarism">parliamentarism</a> also created a political class in the Ottoman-controlled provinces, from which later emerged a liberal nationalist elite that would spearhead the several nationalist movements, in particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_identity_of_Egyptians" title="National identity of Egyptians">Egyptian nationalism</a>. Egyptian nationalism was non-Arab, emphasising ethnic Egyptian identity and history in response to European colonialism and the Turkish occupation of Egypt. This was paralleled by the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turks" title="Young Turks">Young Turks</a> in the central Ottoman provinces and administration. The resentment towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Turkish</a> rule fused with protests against the Sultan's autocracy, and the largely secular concepts of <a href="/wiki/Arab_nationalism" title="Arab nationalism">Arab nationalism</a> rose as a cultural response to the Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphates</a> claims of religious legitimacy. Various Arab nationalist secret societies rose in the years prior to World War One, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-fatat" title="Al-fatat">Al-fatat</a> and the military based al-Ahd.</p>
<p>This was complemented by the rise of other national movements, including <a href="/wiki/Syrian_nationalism" title="Syrian nationalism">Syrian nationalism</a>, which like Egyptian nationalism was in some of its manifestations essentially non-Arabist and connected to the concept of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Syria" title="Greater Syria">Greater Syria</a>. The main other example of the late al-Nahda era is the emerging <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_nationalism" title="Palestinian nationalism">Palestinian nationalism</a>, which was set apart from Syrian nationalism by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> immigration to <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a> and the resulting sense of Palestinian particularism.</p>
