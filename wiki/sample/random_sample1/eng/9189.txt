<p><b>Alençon</b> (<small>French pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[a.lɑ̃.sɔ̃]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Fr-Alen%C3%A7on.ogg" title="File:Fr-Alençon.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Fr-Alen%C3%A7on.ogg" title="Fr-Alençon.ogg">listen</a>)</small>) is a <a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">commune</a> in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, France, capital of the <a href="/wiki/Orne" title="Orne">Orne</a> <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a>. It is situated 173 kilometres (107 mi) west of Paris. Alençon belongs to the intercommunality of Alençon (with 52,000 people).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alen%C3%A7on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of Alençon was probably founded in the fourth century by <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alan</a> soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The name is first seen in a document dated in the seventh century. During the tenth century, Alençon was a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_state" title="Buffer state">buffer state</a> between <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maine_(province_of_France)" title="Maine (province of France)">Maine</a> regions. In 1049-1051, William Duke of Normandy, later known as <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> and king of England, laid <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> to the town, which had risen in support of the <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_II,_Count_of_Anjou" title="Geoffrey II, Count of Anjou">Count</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_of_Anjou" title="County of Anjou">Anjou</a> along with two other towns of the Bellême estates, Domfront (then in Maine) and Bellême (held directly from King Henry I of France). According to Duke William's chaplain and panegyrist, William of Poitiers, the citizens insulted William by hanging animal skins from the walls, in reference to his ancestry as the illegitimate son of Duke Robert and a tanner's daughter. On capturing the town, William had a number of the citizens' hands and feet cut off in revenge. Alençon was occupied by the English during the Anglo-Norman wars of 1113 to 1203.</p>
<p>The city became the seat of a <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">dukedom</a> in 1415, belonging to the sons of the King of France until the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, and some of them played important roles in French history: see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Alen%C3%A7on" title="Duke of Alençon">Duke of Alençon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> caused relatively little disorder in this area although there were some royalist uprisings nearby.</p>
<p>A long-standing local fabric industry gave birth to the town's famous <a href="/wiki/Alen%C3%A7on_lace" title="Alençon lace">point d’Alençon</a> lace in the 18th century. The economic development of the nineteenth century was based on iron foundries and mills in the surrounding region. In the first half of the twentieth century the city developed a flourishing <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> industry.</p>
<p>Alençon was home to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie-Az%C3%A9lie_Gu%C3%A9rin_Martin" title="Marie-Azélie Guérin Martin">Marie-Azélie Guérin Martin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-louiszeliemartin-alencon.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-louiszeliemartin-alencon.com-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Martin_(blessed)" title="Louis Martin (blessed)">Louis Martin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup> the parents of St. <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_of_Lisieux" title="Thérèse of Lisieux">Thérèse of Lisieux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-4">[4]</a></sup> They were the first spouses in the history of the Catholic Church to be proposed for sainthood as a couple, in 2008. Zélie and Louis were married at the Basilica<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> of Notre-Dame in Alençon on 13 July 1858 and spent their whole married life in Alençon, where Thérèse was born<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in January 1873 and spent her early childhood until the death of her mother in 1877. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.thereseoflisieux.org/cause/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<p>On 17 June 1940 the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">German Army</a> took occupation of Alençon. On 12 August 1944 Alençon was the first French city to be liberated by the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Hauteclocque" title="Philippe de Hauteclocque">General Leclerc</a>, after minor bomb damage.</p>
<p>After the war the population sharply increased and new industries settled. Many of these were related to plastics and the town is now a major plastics educational centre.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alen%C3%A7on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the seventeenth century Alençon was chiefly noted for its <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">lace</a> called <a href="/wiki/Alen%C3%A7on_lace" title="Alençon lace">point d'Alençon</a>.</p>
<p>Today, Alençon is home to a prosperous <a href="/wiki/Plastics_industry" title="Plastics industry">plastics industry</a>, and, since 1993, to a plastics engineering school.</p>
<p>MPO Fenêtres is a local PVC windows company established in Alençon since 1970, is one of the first company in Alençon with around 170 employees (2009) and a turnover of 28 million euros in 2008. It is also the oldest French PVC windows company still in activity.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alen%C3%A7on&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alençon is linked by the A28 <a href="/wiki/Autoroutes_of_France" title="Autoroutes of France">autoroute</a> (motorway/freeway) with the nearby cities of <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">Le Mans</a> to the south (<a href="/wiki/Sarthe" title="Sarthe">Sarthe</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">Rouen</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haute-Normandie" title="Haute-Normandie">Haute-Normandie</a>) to the north.</p>
<p>The A88 autoroute links the A28 just north of Alençon to the coastal port of <a href="/wiki/Caen" title="Caen">Caen</a>.</p>
<p>A comprehensive town bus system operates from 7:00 to 19:00.</p>
<p>There is a comprehensive network of cycle paths.</p>
