<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">DTS-HD Master Audio</h1>

<p><b>DTS-HD Master Audio</b> is a combined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" title="Lossless data compression">lossless</a>/<a href="/wiki/Lossy_compression" title="Lossy compression">lossy</a> audio <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codec</a> created by <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)" title="DTS (sound system)">DTS</a> (formerly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Theater_Systems" title="Digital Theater Systems">Digital Theater Systems</a>), commonly used for surround-sound movie soundtracks on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DTS-HD_Master_Audio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DTS-HD Master Audio is an extension of DTS' previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DTS_Coherent_Acoustics" title="DTS Coherent Acoustics">DTS Coherent Acoustics</a> codec. Prior to 2004, it had been known as <i>DTS++</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Though it is an optional audio format for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> format, by 2010, it had become the dominant Blu-ray lossless audio format over its competitor <a href="/wiki/Dolby_TrueHD" title="Dolby TrueHD">Dolby TrueHD</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> DTS-HD Master Audio is also the carrier for home delivery of DTS:X.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DTS-HD_Master_Audio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DTS-HD Master Audio is a lossless compression codec containing a lossy DTS Digital core, thus allowing for bit-to-bit representation of the original movie's master soundtrack. DTS-HD Master Audio supports variable bit rates up to 24.5 Mbit/s. The format supports a maximum of 192 kHz sampling frequency and 24-bit depth samples from 2 to 5.1 channels, and 96 kHz/24bit resolution up to 7.1 channels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> DTS-HD is capable of virtually any number of discrete channels but is limited by storage media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a 3D audio delivery format, a DTS:X encoded DTS-HD Master Audio stream is able to contain up to 7.1 channels as well as nine objects and its associated metadata at 96 kHz/24 bit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_lossless.2Flossy_compression">Combined lossless/lossy compression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DTS-HD_Master_Audio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Combined lossless/lossy compression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When played back on devices which do not support the Master Audio or <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)#DTS-HD_High_Resolution_Audio" title="DTS (sound system)">High Resolution</a> extension, it degrades to a "core" track which is <a href="/wiki/Lossy_compression" title="Lossy compression">lossy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the DTS-HD White Paper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitepaper_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitepaper-7">[7]</a></sup> DTS-HD Master Audio contains 2 data streams: the original DTS core stream and the additional "residual" stream which contains the "difference" between the original signal and the lossy compression DTS core stream. The audio signal is split into two paths at the input to the encoder. One path goes to the core encoder for backwards compatibility and is then decoded. The other path compares the original audio to the decoded core signal and generates residuals, which are data over and above what the core contains that is needed to restore the original audio as bit-for-bit identical to the original. The residual data is then encoded by a lossless encoder and packed together with the core. The decoding process is simply the reverse.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="AV_transport">AV transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DTS-HD_Master_Audio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: AV transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DTS-HD Master Audio may be transported to AV receivers in <a href="/wiki/5.1_surround_sound" title="5.1 surround sound">5.1</a>, 6.1 or <a href="/wiki/7.1_surround_sound" title="7.1 surround sound">7.1</a> channels, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_audio#Lossless_audio_compression" title="Lossless audio">lossless</a> quality, in one of three ways depending on player and/or receiver support:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitepaper_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitepaper-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Over 6, 7 or 8 <a href="/wiki/RCA_connector" title="RCA connector">RCA connectors</a> as analog audio (not lossless), using the player's internal decoder and <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">digital-to-analog converter</a> (DAC).</li>
<li>Over <a href="/wiki/HDMI" title="HDMI">HDMI</a> 1.1 (or higher) connections as 6-, 7- or 8-channel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_pulse_code_modulation" title="Linear pulse code modulation">linear PCM</a>, using the player's decoder and the AV receiver's DAC.</li>
<li>Over HDMI 1.3 (or higher) connections as the original DTS-HD Master Audio bitstream, with decoding and DAC both done by the AV receiver.</li>
</ul>
