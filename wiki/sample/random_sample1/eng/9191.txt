<p>The <b>Alenia Aermacchi M-346 Master</b> is a military <a href="/wiki/Twinjet" title="Twinjet">twin-engine</a> <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">transonic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trainer_aircraft" title="Trainer aircraft">trainer aircraft</a>. Originally co-developed with <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev" title="Yakovlev">Yakovlev</a> as the <b>Yak/AEM-130</b>, the partnership was dissolved in 2000 and Alenia Aermacchi (merged into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonardo-Finmeccanica" title="Leonardo-Finmeccanica">Leonardo-Finmeccanica</a>, since 2016)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> proceeded to separately develop the M-346 Master, while Yakolev continued work on the <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev_Yak-130" title="Yakovlev Yak-130">Yakovlev Yak-130</a>. The first flight of the M-346 was performed in 2004. The type is currently operated by the air forces of Italy, Israel, Singapore, and Poland.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alenia_Aermacchi_M-346_Master&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1992_in_aviation" title="1992 in aviation">1992</a>, Aermacchi signed a cooperation agreement with Yakovlev to provide financial and technical support for the new trainer that the firm had been developing since 1991 for the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Air_Force" title="Russian Air Force">Russian Air Force</a> in competition with the <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan_MiG-AT" title="Mikoyan MiG-AT">Mikoyan MiG-AT</a>; Aermacchi also gained the right to modify and market the aircraft for the Western market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The resulting aircraft first flew in <a href="/wiki/1996_in_aviation" title="1996 in aviation">1996</a> and was brought to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> the following year to replace the aging <a href="/wiki/Aermacchi_MB-339" title="Aermacchi MB-339">MB-339</a>. By this point, the aircraft was being marketed as the <i>Yak/AEM-130</i>. In February 1996, Russia provided initial funding for the Yak/AEM-130 and pledged to purchase up to 200 aircraft for the Russian Air Force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 1998, it was reported that the venture was increasingly becoming an Italian-led effort due to a lack of financial support on the part of Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By July 2000, Aermacchi held a 50% stake in the development programme, Yakovlev and Sokol had a 25% share each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engine_2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-engine_2000-6">[6]</a></sup> In mid-2000, it was announced that differences in priorities between the two firms, and a lack of financial backing from the programme's Russian participants, had brought about an end to the partnership, and that each firm would pursue development of the aircraft independently; Yakovlev received US$77 million for technical documents of the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBT_2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBT_2002-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russ_replace_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-russ_replace-8">[8]</a></sup> Yakovlev would be able to sell the Yak-130 to countries such those in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">Commonwealth of Independent States</a>, India, Slovakia and Algeria; while Aeromacchi would be able to sell the M-346 to NATO countries, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBT_2002_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-IBT_2002-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M-346 is a highly modified version of the aircraft that was being developed under the joint venture. It uses equipment exclusively from Western manufacturers, such as the digital flight control system being developed by a collaboration between Teleavio, <a href="/wiki/Marconi_Company" title="Marconi Company">Marconi Italiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russ_replace_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-russ_replace-8">[8]</a></sup> In July 2000, Aermacchi selected the <a href="/wiki/Honeywell/ITEC_F124" title="Honeywell/ITEC F124">Honeywell F124</a> <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engine to power the type in place of the originally intended <a href="/wiki/Lotarev_DV-2" title="Lotarev DV-2">Povazske Strojarne DV-2S</a> powerplant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engine_2000_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-engine_2000-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On 7 June 2003, the first M-346 prototype rolled out, this prototype conducted its maiden flight on 15 July 2004. In 2004, a contract for the development of a full-mission simulator for the M-346 was awarded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAE_(company)" title="CAE (company)">CAE</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Further production contracts for CAE's full-mission simulator have since been issued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aermacchi_M-346-002(w).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Aermacchi_M-346-002%28w%29.jpg/220px-Aermacchi_M-346-002%28w%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Aermacchi_M-346-002%28w%29.jpg/330px-Aermacchi_M-346-002%28w%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Aermacchi_M-346-002%28w%29.jpg/440px-Aermacchi_M-346-002%28w%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In January <a href="/wiki/2005_in_aviation" title="2005 in aviation">2005</a>, the Greek Ministry of Defence signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) to become a partner in the programme and, in <a href="/wiki/2006_in_aviation" title="2006 in aviation">2006</a>, Aermacchi signed an industrial cooperation agreement with <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_Aerospace_Industry" title="Hellenic Aerospace Industry">Hellenic Aerospace Industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In March <a href="/wiki/2008_in_aviation" title="2008 in aviation">2008</a> the <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> <a href="/wiki/ENAER" title="ENAER">ENAER</a> signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with Alenia Aermacchi at the FIDAE air show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 10 April 2008, a further prototype, produced in the final configuration (new <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_brake_(aircraft)" title="Air brake (aircraft)">air brake</a>, more composite parts), was rolled out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aer_ne_2008_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-aer_ne_2008-14">[14]</a></sup> On 18 December 2008, Aermacchi announced that the M-346 had attained a maximum speed of Mach 1.15 (1,255 km/h, 678 knots, 780 mph), claiming the occasion to be the first in which an all-Italian built aircraft had broken the sound barrier in 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAPR1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAPR1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2008, <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> signed a Memorandum of Understanding to cooperate on the marketing, sales, training and support of two Aermacchi trainer aircraft, the M-346 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alenia_Aermacchi_M-311" title="Alenia Aermacchi M-311">M-311</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 20 June 2011, a <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">Military Type Certification</a> was granted to Alenia Aermacchi for the M-346 Master by the General Directorate for Aeronautical Armaments of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(Italy)" title="Ministry of Defence (Italy)">Italian Ministry of Defence</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. Throughout the certification process, the M-346 development aircraft made 180 test flights, totalling 200 flights across the course of the previous five months, during which over 3,300 test points were completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFM282_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFM282-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the trainer jet role, the M-346 is unarmed; however, in November 2015, it was reported that Alenia Aermacchi was close to finalising a combat-capable dual-role variant of the airplane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dualRole_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-dualRole-18">[18]</a></sup> In late 2014, a series of armed tests involving the <a href="/wiki/IRIS-T" title="IRIS-T">IRIS-T</a> missiles took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRISTtests_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-IRISTtests-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2015, an armed variant, designated as the M-346 LCA (Light Combat Aircraft), was offered to Poland; this reportedly included a capability of operating the <a href="/wiki/Brimstone_(missile)" title="Brimstone (missile)">Brimstone</a> air-to-ground missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dualRole_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-dualRole-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCAforPoland_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCAforPoland-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2016, the newly created, consolidated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonardo-Finmeccanica" title="Leonardo-Finmeccanica">Leonardo-Finmeccanica</a> company promoted the Aermacchi M346 in two new roles; companion training and dissimilar air combat training. In order to better replicate the flight performance and behavior of various enemy aircraft, both the <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">g-force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a> can be independently selected in the flight control system; reportedly, existing customers have stated the type to be well suited to the aggressor role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alenia_Aermacchi_M-346_Master&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aermacchi_M-346.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="582" data-file-width="867" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Aermacchi_M-346.jpg/220px-Aermacchi_M-346.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Aermacchi_M-346.jpg/330px-Aermacchi_M-346.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Aermacchi_M-346.jpg/440px-Aermacchi_M-346.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aermacchi_M-346.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An M-346 in flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The M-346 is designed for the principal role of lead-in fighter trainer, in which capacity it is used to deliver pilot training for the latest generation of combat fighter aircraft. Powered by a pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honeywell_F124" title="Honeywell F124">Honeywell F124</a> <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines, it is capable of transonic flight without using an <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a>, which is designed to reduce acquisition and operating costs; Alenia Aermacchi has claimed that the M-346's flight performance to be "second only to afterburner-equipped aircraft".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup> During the design process, the twin concepts of "design-to-cost" and "design-to-maintain" were adhered to, reducing acquisition and operational costs;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup> the per flying hour costs of the M346 are reportedly one-tenth of those of the <a href="/wiki/Eurofighter_Typhoon" title="Eurofighter Typhoon">Eurofighter Typhoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup> Outside of the training role, the M-346 was designed from the onset to accommodate additional operational capabilities, including combat missions such as <a href="/wiki/Close_air_support" title="Close air support">close air support</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerial_warfare" title="Aerial warfare">air police</a> duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M-346 incorporates a full-authority quadruplex digital <a href="/wiki/Fly-by-wire" title="Fly-by-wire">fly-by-wire</a> flight control system which, in combination with the optimized aerodynamic configuration of the aircraft, provides for full manoeuvrability and controllability at very high angle of attack (in excess of 30° degrees).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup> The flight control system, incorporating a <a href="/wiki/HOTAS" title="HOTAS">HOTAS</a> design philosophy, is equipped with adjustable angle of attack and g-force limitations; when combined with its wide performance envelope, this allows the M-346 to effectively mimic the flight performance of various fighter aircraft operated by trainee pilots or to progressively increase difficulty levels, thus raising the training's effectiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup> A pilot activated recovery system is present which, when pressed, conducts an automatic recovery by returning the aircraft to a steady and level flight path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>A digital avionics system, modelled on its counterparts on board the latest generation of military aircraft such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saab_JAS-39_Gripen" title="Saab JAS-39 Gripen">Saab JAS-39 Gripen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor</a> and the Eurofighter Typhoon, is incorporated, making it suitable for all stages of advanced flight training and thus reducing the use of combat aircraft for training purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modular" title="Modular">modular</a> avionics architecture is employed, allowing for new equipment and systems to be incorporated and increasing the type's growth potential. The M-346's <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpit</a> is representative of the latest generation cockpit and is compatible with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night_vision_goggles" title="Night vision goggles">night vision goggles</a>; it has three color <a href="/wiki/Liquid-crystal_display" title="Liquid-crystal display">LCD</a> multifunctional displays, a <a href="/wiki/Head-up_display" title="Head-up display">head-up display</a>, and an optional <a href="/wiki/Helmet-mounted_display" title="Helmet-mounted display">helmet-mounted display</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup> A voice command system is also present, which is integrated with functions such as the navigation system. The communication systems include <a href="/wiki/Very_high_frequency" title="Very high frequency">VHF</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> transceivers, <a href="/wiki/Identification_friend_or_foe" title="Identification friend or foe">IFF</a> transponder, and <a href="/wiki/Traffic_collision_avoidance_system" title="Traffic collision avoidance system">traffic collision avoidance system</a> (TCAS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>A key feature of the M-346 is the embedded tactical training system (ETTS). The ETTS is capable of emulating various equipment, such as radar, targeting pods, weapons, and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare" title="Electronic warfare">electronic warfare</a> systems; additionally, the ETTS can interface with various munitions and other equipment actually being carried on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup> The system can act in a standalone mode, in which simulated data and scenario information is loaded prior to takeoff, or in a network, during which data is received and acted upon in real time from ground monitoring stations via the aircraft's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datalink" title="Datalink">datalink</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup> For post-mission evaluation and analysis purposes, accumulated data, such as video from the optional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helmet_mounted_display" title="Helmet mounted display">helmet mounted display</a>, can be extracted and reviewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup> Alenia Aermacchi also offer an Integrated Training System (ITS), combining the M-346 with a ground-based training system as part of a wider syllabus towards qualifying pilots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>The M-346 is equipped with a total of nine <a href="/wiki/Hardpoint" title="Hardpoint">hardpoints</a>, capable of carrying external loads up to 3,000 kg while maintaining a high thrust-to-weight ratio; stores management data can be presented upon any of the multifunction displays in the cockpit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross-section</a> of the M346 in a standard configuration is reportedly 20 square meters; this can be reduced to a single square meter by installing a low-observability kit which has been developed for the type. Other self-protection systems that can be fitted include a <a href="/wiki/Radar_warning_receiver" title="Radar warning receiver">radar warning receiver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flare" title="Flare">flares</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chaff" title="Chaff">chaff</a> dispensers, and active electronic warfare systems according to Alenia Aermacchi the kit provides "excellent levels of survivability and effectiveness when operating in hostile theatres".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-6"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup> Other equipment that is optionally installable upon the M-346 includes targeting pods, a tactical datalink, and multi-mode <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_radar" title="Fire-control radar">fire-control radar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alenia_2014_22-7"><a href="#cite_note-alenia_2014-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Combat missions can also be performed by the M-346, having been designed to be rapidly reconfigured in the field into a combat aircraft capable of performing ground attack, <a href="/wiki/Anti-surface_warfare" title="Anti-surface warfare">anti-shipping</a>, and aerial combat missions, in a "home defense" capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup> Various munitions can be carried, including a 12.7mm gun pod, <a href="/wiki/IRIS-T" title="IRIS-T">IRIS-T</a> or <a href="/wiki/AIM-9_Sidewinder" title="AIM-9 Sidewinder">AIM-9 Sidewinder</a> air-to-air missiles, various air-to-surface missiles, anti-ship missiles, free-fall and laser-guided bombs and rockets, reconnaissance pods, and electronic warfare pods; weapon aiming is performed using the helmet mounted display and the multifunction displays. All main systems are duplicated, and the flight system reconfigurable, to increase survivability and functionality in the event of battle damage being sustained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-7"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup> The aircraft has a maximum range of 1,470 nautical miles when outfitted with a maximum of three external fuel tanks, this can be extended via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-flight_refuelling" title="In-flight refuelling">in-flight refuelling</a> via a removable refuelling probe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-8"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alenia_Aermacchi_M-346_Master&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

M-346 at RIAT 2009</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Italian_Air_Force" title="Italian Air Force">Italian Air Force</a> intends to acquire a first batch of 15 low rate production M-346 aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On 18 June 2009, Alenia Aermacchi announced they had received an order for the first six with an option for nine more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In September 2015, the Italian Air Force conducted their first training course using the M-346 trainer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The M-346 was named the winner of a competition by the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> at the <a href="/wiki/International_Defence_Exhibition" title="International Defence Exhibition">IDEX</a> 2009 defense show in <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a> on 25 February 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The official said the order involved delivery of 48 aircraft to be used for pilot training and light attack duties. A final request for proposals in 2010 had set the requirement at 20 trainers, 20 aircraft for combat duties, and the remainder would go toward the creation of a formation flying team. However, by January 2010, negotiations to sign a contract had reportedly stalled over specifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2010, the M-346 was selected by <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Singapore_Air_Force" title="Republic of Singapore Air Force">Republic of Singapore Air Force</a> (RSAF) to replace the <a href="/wiki/ST_Aerospace_A-4SU_Super_Skyhawk" title="ST Aerospace A-4SU Super Skyhawk">ST Aerospace A-4SU Super Skyhawks</a> in the Advanced Jet Training (AJT) role, based at <a href="/wiki/French_Air_Force#Southern_Region" title="French Air Force">BA 120 Cazaux Air Base</a> in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In a press release by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(Singapore)" title="Ministry of Defence (Singapore)">Singaporean Ministry of Defence</a> on 28 September 2010, <a href="/wiki/ST_Aerospace" title="ST Aerospace">ST Aerospace</a> was awarded the contract to acquire twelve M-346 and a ground-based training system on behalf of RSAF. As stipulated in the contract, ST Aerospace acts as the main contractor in the maintenance of the aircraft after delivery by Alenia Aermacchi while <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Defense,_Space_%26_Security" title="Boeing Defense, Space &amp; Security">Boeing</a> supplies the training system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNA_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNA-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The RSAF holds the distinction of being the first export customer for the type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sing16_AON_21-8"><a href="#cite_note-sing16_AON-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Advance European Pilot Training (AEPTJ) program – also unofficially called <i>Eurotraining</i> – a consortium of 12 European nations to give advance &amp; lead-in fighter training with a common core course and training provided by a common aircraft – has contacted Alenia Aermacchi through the European Defence agency in 2010, for further information on the M-346.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In May 2013, Aleni Aermacchi announced that the AEPTJ held a low priority for the firm and that "...progress has been slow."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 November 2011 the prototype, which had been on display at the Dubai Air Show crashed after departing Dubai on return to Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 16 February 2012, the M-346 was selected by the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Air_Force" title="Israeli Air Force">Israeli Air Force</a> (IAF) in an exchange deal, in which Israel will build a <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">reconnaissance satellite</a> and AWACs systems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> for Italy in return for the planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesofisrael.com_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-timesofisrael.com-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It will operate as the IAF's main training jet to replace the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_A-4_Skyhawk#Israel" title="Douglas A-4 Skyhawk">McDonnell Douglas A-4H/N Skyhawk</a>, which has served the IAF for over 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPost_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-JPost-41">[41]</a></sup> On 19 July 2012, a contract was signed between Alenia Aermacchi and the Israeli Ministry of Defence to supply 30 M-346 advanced jet trainers, with the first delivery expected in the middle of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA1-42">[42]</a></sup> The Israeli Air Force announced on 2 July 2013 that in Israeli service the M-346 would be named the <i>Lavi</i>, reusing the name given to the cancelled <a href="/wiki/IAI_Lavi" title="IAI Lavi">IAI Lavi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The IAF's first M-346 was rolled out in a ceremony at Alenia Aermacchi’s factory in <a href="/wiki/Venegono_Superiore" title="Venegono Superiore">Venegono Superiore</a> on March 20, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 11 May 2013, an Alenia-operated M346 crashed near the village of <a href="/wiki/Piana_Crixia" title="Piana Crixia">Piana Crixia</a>, in Val Bormida, between <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cuneo" title="Province of Cuneo">Cuneo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Savona" title="Province of Savona">Savona</a>'s provinces, Italy, during a test flight. The pilot was able to eject successfully and survived the crash, but received serious injuries after jumping from the tree where his parachute had been entangled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The type was grounded for more than three months while the cause of the crash was investigated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 23 December 2013 it was announced that Poland had selected the M-346 to meet a requirement for an advanced jet trainer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The deal for eight aircraft was signed on 27 February 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> First two delivered in November 2016. Both aircraft have not been received until January 2017 due to technical reasons (non-compliance with contract specifications).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, Alenia Aermacchi is to submit the M-346 for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>'s <a href="/wiki/T-X_program" title="T-X program">T-X program</a> to replace the aging <a href="/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon" title="Northrop T-38 Talon">Northrop T-38 Talon</a>, rebranding the aircraft as the <i>T-100 Integrated Training System</i>. Alenia originally intended to be the prime contractor, anticipating moving the final assembly location from Italy to the United States if the bid succeeds. About 350 aircraft are expected to be ordered, further purchases could lead to over 1,000 aircraft being purchased overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t38_repla_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-t38_repla-50">[50]</a></sup> In January 2013, Alenia Aermacchi signed a letter of intent with General Dynamics C4 Systems, who intended to serve as the prime contractor for the T-X bid; however, General Dynamics announced their withdrawal in March 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> On January 1, 2016, Alenia Aermacchi was absorbed into a new consolidated corporation titled: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonardo-Finmeccanica" title="Leonardo-Finmeccanica">Leonardo-Finmeccanica</a>. In February 2016, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a>, who shall serve as the prime contractor, had teamed up with Leonardo-Finmeccanica to offer an advanced variant of the M-346 for the T-X program called the T-100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2016 the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Air_Force" title="Argentine Air Force">Argentine Air Force</a> also evaluated the M-346 as a potential combat fighter to replace the <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_III" title="Dassault Mirage III">Dassault Mirage III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_5" title="Dassault Mirage 5">Mirage 5</a> aircraft it had retired in 2015, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_A-4_Skyhawk#Variants" title="Douglas A-4 Skyhawk">Douglas A-4R</a> aircraft that remain in service with only limited capability. Argentina is speculated to be interested in 10 to 12 aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<dl>
<dt>M-346 </dt>
<dd>Designation for the basic type</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>T-346A </dt>
<dd>Italian military designation from 2012 for the M-346.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dgaa_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-dgaa-55">[55]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>M-346LCA (Light Combat Aircraft)</dt>
<dd>Armed variant offered to Poland as a replacement for aging <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-17#Poland" title="Sukhoi Su-17">Su-22</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCAforPoland_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-LCAforPoland-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>M-346FT (Fighter Trainer)</dt>
<dd>Multirole variant capable switching between training and combat operations. New features include a new tactical datalink system and different armament capability, but do not include physical changes to the hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>T-100 </dt>
<dd>Designation used for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>'s <a href="/wiki/T-X_program" title="T-X program">T-X program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-t38_repla_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-t38_repla-50">[50]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i> Alenia Aermacchi<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlenAer_M-346_broch_23-9"><a href="#cite_note-AlenAer_M-346_broch-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> two, student and instructor</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 11.49 m (37.70 ft)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 9.72 m (31.89 ft)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 4.76 m (16.11 ft)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 23.52 m² (253.2 ft²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 4,610 kg (10,165 lb)</li>
<li><b>Loaded weight:</b> 6,700 kg (14,770 lb)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 9,500 kg (20,945 lb)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 2 × <a href="/wiki/Honeywell/ITEC_F124" title="Honeywell/ITEC F124">Honeywell F124-GA-200</a> , 28 kN (6,250 lb<sub>f</sub>) each</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Vne" title="V speeds">Never exceed speed</a>:</b> <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 1.2 (1,470 km/h, 793 knots)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 1,059 km/h (572 knots)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Stall_(fluid_mechanics)#Stall_speed" title="Stall (fluid mechanics)">Stall speed</a>:</b> 176 km/h (95 knots)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 1,981 km (1,070 nautical miles)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Ferry range</a>:</b> 2,722 km (1,470 nmi) ; with 3 external drop tanks</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Endurance_(aeronautics)" title="Endurance (aeronautics)">Endurance</a>:</b> 2.75 hours (4 hours with external drop tanks)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 13,716 m (45,000 ft)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 6,705 m/min (22,000 ft/min)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 285 kg/m² (58.3 lb/ft²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">Thrust/weight</a>:</b> 0.84</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hardpoint" title="Hardpoint">Hardpoints</a>:</b> Provisions for a total of 9 pylon stations (2× wingtip, 1× under-fuselage plus 6× underwing), capable of mounting up to 3,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilograms</a> (6,600 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a>) of external payload and up to 3× 630 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litres</a> (140 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_gallon" title="Imperial gallon">imp gal</a>; 170 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_gallon" title="US gallon">US gal</a>) external <a href="/wiki/Drop_tank" title="Drop tank">drop tanks</a> (only pylon stations 4, 5, 6 are wet-plumbed)</li>
</ul>
