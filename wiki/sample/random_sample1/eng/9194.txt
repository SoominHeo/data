<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alessandro Farnese (cardinal)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Alessandro Farnese</b> (5 October 1520<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> – 2 March 1589), an Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diplomat" title="Diplomat">diplomat</a> and a great collector and <a href="/wiki/Patronage#Arts" title="Patronage">patron of the arts</a>, was the grandson of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_III" title="Pope Paul III">Pope Paul III</a> (who also bore the name <i>Alessandro Farnese</i>), and the son of <a href="/wiki/Pier_Luigi_Farnese,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Pier Luigi Farnese, Duke of Parma">Pier Luigi Farnese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Parma" title="Duke of Parma">Duke of Parma</a>, who was murdered in 1547. He should not be confused with his nephew <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Farnese,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Alexander Farnese, Duke of Parma">Alessandro Farnese</a>, Governor of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a>, grandson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Charles_V" title="Emperor Charles V">Emperor Charles V</a> and great-grandson of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_III" title="Pope Paul III">Pope Paul III</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born at the family castle at <a href="/wiki/Valentano" title="Valentano">Valentano</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> (current <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Viterbo" title="Province of Viterbo">province of Viterbo</a>), the son of Pierluigi Farnese, who was the son of Cardinal Alessandro Farnese (Pope Paul III); and Girolama Orsini, daughter of Ludovico Orsini, seventh Conte di Pitigliano, and Giulia Conti. They were married in Rome on August 6, 1519. Young Alessandro studied at <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> along with his cousin, Guido Ascanio Sforza di Santa Fiora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was a member of the Collegio Ancarano, which had been founded in the XV century by Petrus de Ancarano de nobilibus de Farnesino provinciae Patrimoniae B. Petri in Tuscia, for students specializing in <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">legal studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 December 1534, at the age of 14, he was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Deacon" title="Cardinal Deacon">Cardinal Deacon</a> of the Title of Sant'Angelo in Pescheria<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> by his grandfather <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_III" title="Paul III">Paul III</a>, who had been elected to the papacy two months previously. On August 11, 1535, he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">Abbot</a> Commendatory of the Abbey of Tre Fontane on the Via Ostiense, a position he held until 1544.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1535, he was also appointed Abbot commendatory of S. Étienne de Caën.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Offices">Offices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Offices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Young Cardinal Farnese received many other offices and benefices, becoming Vice-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Holy_Roman_Church" title="Chancellor of the Holy Roman Church">Chancellor of the Holy Roman Church</a> (August 13, 1535 – March 2, 1589),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also became Governor of Tivoli (1535-1550),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Archpriest" title="Archpriest">Archpriest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Maria_Maggiore" title="Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore">St. Mary Major Basilica</a> (1537-1543),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Archpriest of <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> (1543-1589).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On August 27, 1539, at the age of 18, Alessandro Farnese was named titular Latin Patriarch of Jerusalem; he vacated the office on the appointment of a new Patriarch on February 28, 1550. The office was highly lucrative, the duties were nominal and did not necessarily involve episcopal functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1538, he was appointed Pope Paul III's principal Secretary, and, with the assistance of <a href="/wiki/Monsignor" title="Monsignor">Monsignor</a> (Msgr.) Marcello Cervino, he managed most of the papal business until 1549.</p>
<p>In 1541, Cardinal Farnese was named Protector of the Holy Roman Empire before the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> and Protector of Spain before the Holy See.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> These offices made him the most prominent expediter of all Imperial and Spanish business in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Curia" title="Roman Curia">Roman Curia</a>; his opinion was always consulted and, since he was the Pope's nephew, it was often followed. At the same time, he was appointed <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9gats_pontificaux_%C3%A0_Avignon" title="fr:Légats pontificaux à Avignon">Papal Legate in Avignon</a> (1541-1565). Royal consent was required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1564 to 1565, he was Bishop of Sabina, and it is <a href="/wiki/Conjecture" title="Conjecture">conjectured</a> (in the absence of positive evidence) that it was in 1564 that Farnese finally was consecrated a bishop. He was certainly a bishop when he was the principal Consecrator of Cardinal Giulio della Rovere on April 15, 1566.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> From 1565 to 1578, he was Bishop of Tusculum (Frascati). He was the Bishop of Porto from July 9, 1578 to December 5, 1580. He was then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal-Bishop_of_Ostia_and_Velletri" title="Cardinal-Bishop of Ostia and Velletri">Cardinal-Bishop of Ostia and Velletri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dean_of_the_College_of_Cardinals" title="Dean of the College of Cardinals">Dean of the College of Cardinals</a> from December 5, 1580 until his death on March 2, 1589.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefices_and_Income">Benefices and Income</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefices and Income">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tizian_068.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2942" data-file-width="2536" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Tizian_068.jpg/220px-Tizian_068.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Tizian_068.jpg/330px-Tizian_068.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Tizian_068.jpg/440px-Tizian_068.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tizian_068.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>'s triple portrait, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_III_and_his_Grandsons" title="Pope Paul III and his Grandsons">Pope Paul III and his Grandsons</a></i>, depicts Alessandro at left.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>His grandfather Pope Paul III immediately named Alessandro Farnese Administrator of the Diocese of Parma (November 1, 1534), allowing him to collect the episcopal income during the interregnum. Alessandro resigned on August 13, 1535, when Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Guido_Ascanio_Sforza_di_Santa_Fiora" title="Guido Ascanio Sforza di Santa Fiora">Guido Ascanio Sforza di Santa Fiora</a>, another grandson of Paul III and only two years older than Alessandro, was named the new Administrator (enjoying his Parmesan income until he resigned in 1560).</p>
<p>Farnese was appointed Administrator of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Ja%C3%A9n" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Jaén">Jaen</a>, Spain, from July 30, 1535 until another Administrator, Cardinal Alessandro Cesarini, was appointed on July 6, 1537.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He was Administrator of the Archdiocese of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Avignon" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Avignon">Avignon</a> from 1535 to 1551.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and of the Diocese of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Vizeu" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Vizeu">Vizeu</a>, Portugal (1547-1552).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 9, 1536, the Emperor Charles V named Cardinal Farnese as the new Archbishop of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Monreale" title="Bishop of Monreale">Monreale</a> in Sicily; his appointment was confirmed on May 15, 1536, by Pope Paul III.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> But Monreale was not a happy place, since the monks of the Cathedral of Monreale and the clergy of the diocese were in one squabble after another with each other. On July 26, 1549, the Pope was forced to intervene, in a letter in which he referred to the Cardinal under the title of <i>perpetuus Administrator</i>. A <a href="/wiki/Vicar" title="Vicar">Vicar</a> was appointed for the underage and absentee Archbishop, Msgr. Giovanni Antonio Fassari, titular Bishop of Christopolis in Greece; he was succeeded in 1546 by Pompeo Zambecari. In 1557, he was followed by Msgr. Giovanni Pietro Fortiguerra, Bishop of Cyrene in Libya.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Cardinal allowed the Jesuits to found a college in Monreale, and allowed the priests of the Society to work in the diocese. In 1568, Cardinal Farnese visited his diocese and held a <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> He was accompanied by his librarian, the famous antiquarian and papal biographer, <a href="/wiki/Onofrio_Panvinio" title="Onofrio Panvinio">Onofrio Panvinio</a>, who, unhappily, died at Palermo on March 16 (?), 1568.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Cardinal continued to enjoy the income of the diocese until 1573, when he resigned the bishopric.</p>
<p>On June 17, 1537, Farnese was appointed Administrator of the Diocese of Bitonto in the Kingdom of Naples on the resignation of Bishop Lopez de Alarcon; his administration ceased upon the appointment of a new bishop on January 8, 1538.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He became Administrator of the Diocese of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Massa" title="Bishop of Massa">Massa</a> Marittima on November 15, 1538, on the resignation of Bishop Hieronymus de Glanderonibus; since he was only eighteen, he was not canonically eligible to be the bishop, though he could—and did—collect the income of the bishop until a successor was appointed in April 1547.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On July 16, 1540, Farnese was named Administrator of the Diocese of <a href="/wiki/Cavaillon" title="Cavaillon">Cavaillon</a> in Provence, which he resigned one year later on July 20, 1541.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1549, his grandfather Pope Paul III died.</p>
<p>He was Administrator of the Archdiocese of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Tours" title="Archbishop of Tours">Tours</a> from April 28, 1553, until Pope Julius III ordered the issue of bulls for Archbishop Simon de Mailly on June 25, 1554.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In the case of Tours, the right of nomination belonged to the King of France, Henri II, whose daughter Diane had married Farnese's youngest brother Orazio in 1552.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On June 25, 1554, the same day that his administration of Tours ceased, Farnese was appointed Administrator of the Diocese of Viviers, which lasted until the Pope approved King Henri's nomination of a new bishop on November 12, 1554.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He was likewise nominated by the King of France to be Administrator of the Diocese of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Cahors" title="Bishop of Cahors">Cahors</a>, the appointment being approved by Pope Julius III on November 12, 1554; a new bishop was approved by Pope Paul IV on May 7, 1557, ending his appointment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> There had been two <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave" title="Papal conclave">Conclaves</a> in the interim, accounting probably for the length of his Administration of Cahors. In 1555, Cardinal Farnese was named Administrator of the Diocese of Spoleto, a post he held until a new bishop was appointed on December 16, 1562.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Cardinal Alessandro Farnese also served as Administrator of the Archdiocese of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Benevento" title="Archbishop of Benevento">Benevento</a> from November 22, 1556, until a new Archbishop was approved by Paul IV on January 14, 1558.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> All of these appointments should be considered as opportunities for financial enrichment, not opportunities for service in vineyards of the Lord far from Rome. The various administrations were carried out by authorized agents.</p>
<p>In 1564, Alessandro Farnese succeeded his brother <a href="/wiki/Ranuccio_Farnese_(cardinal)" title="Ranuccio Farnese (cardinal)">Ranuccio</a> as Abbot Commendatory of the Monastery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territorial_Abbey_of_Farfa" title="Territorial Abbey of Farfa">Farfa</a>, which he held until his death in 1589.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It was he who introduced the Benedictine monks of the Congregation of Monte Cassino into the monastery in 1567.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> He also built, or rebuilt, the monastery's water supply.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomatic_Activity">Diplomatic Activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diplomatic Activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He also became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Legate" title="Papal Legate">Papal Legate</a>, arranging peace between the perpetually warring <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a>. In the Consistory of 24 November 1539 he was sent as Apostolic <i>Legatus a latere</i> to attempt to arrange a peace between the two feuding monarchs. He left Rome on 29 November and made his solemn entry into Paris on 31 December. He was in Rouen on 14 January 1540, and had a solemn meeting with the King on 14 February. He then left for Flanders on 17 February for a meeting with the Emperor; he stayed for three months, and returned to Paris on 14 May. He had a meeting with King Francis on the 17th at Saint-Germain-en-Laye. He returned to Rome on 5 June 1540.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cardinal Farnese was named <i>Legatus a latere</i> again, and for the same purpose, at the Consistory of 21 November 1543. He arrived at Fontainebleau on 29 December 1543 and remained until 6 January 1544, when he departed for Bruxelles to visit the Emperor. He arrived on 14 January, and was back in France by 4 February. He returned to Rome on 1 March 1544.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1546, he accompanied the troops sent by the pope to the aid of Charles V against the <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_League" title="Schmalkaldic League">Schmalkaldic League</a>.</p>
<p>In 1548, Cardinal Alessandro Farnese took into his service as his private secretary <a href="/wiki/Annibale_Caro" title="Annibale Caro">Annibale Caro</a>, the noted poet and prose stylist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Caro had previously been in the service of Cardinal Alessandro's father, <a href="/wiki/Pier_Luigi_Farnese,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Pier Luigi Farnese, Duke of Parma">Pierluigi Farnese</a>, and after his murder on September 10, 1547, to Duke Ottavio Farnese,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and then to Cardinal Ranuccio Farnese, his own younger brother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1551, Cardinal Alessandro was sent by Pope Julius III to convince his brother Ottavio, the Duke of Parma and Piacenza, to surrender those territories, which, the Pope pointed out, were <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a> of the Church. Julius was being incredibly arrogant, insensitive and stupid. The Farnese had spent more than fifteen years developing their dukedom, and Cardinal Alessandro's father had been assassinated in the struggle with the Gonzaga and the Emperor Charles over it. The Farnese were being protected by the French Crown, which considered Parma its entrée into northern Italy, where it challenged the Emperor to the possession of the Duchy of Milan and the Lombard plain. Julius was toying with explosive material. Naturally Ottavio refused, and Alessandro supported him. The Farnese underwent a complete breach in relations with the Pope, and Alessandro was immediately unwelcome in Rome. Pope Julius sequestrated his diocese of Monreale, and confiscated all of the moveables in the Palazzo Farnese, said to have been worth 30,000 scudi. Duke Ottavio's duchy was sequestered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> He withdrew in April, first to a visit with his sister Vittoria, the Duchess of Urbino, then to Florence, and finally to Avignon.</p>
<p>After peace was made between the Pope and France, Orazio Farnese had the <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">Duchy</a> of Castro restored to him (1552) and, to protect the Farnese's French connection, Orazio married Diane de France, the illegitimate daughter of Henri II. Unfortunately, Cardinal Farnese's brother Orazio was killed in battle on July 18, 1553, leaving no heirs. The dukedoms went to their younger brother Ottavio Farnese.</p>
<p>Farnese had been in France, when on June 6, 1554, he was appointed by Henri II to go to Rome and take charge of French affairs in the absence of Cardinal d'Este, who was in Parma—over the objections of Cardinal du Bellay, who did not appreciate being supplanted. Cardinal d'Este was ruling Parma on behalf of the French king, who had acquired the duchy from Paul III as the price of an alliance. In November, Henri named a new Ambassador to the Holy See, and Cardinal Farnese was free to return to France. He took up residence in Avignon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclaves_of_1555">Conclaves of 1555</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conclaves of 1555">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cardinal Farnese did not participate in the first conclave of 1555, April 5--April 9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> which followed the death of Julius III. He had been in Avignon, serving as Legate and avoiding the unwelcome attention of the Pope. But, on the news of the death of Pope Julius, he took the road for Rome. He was carrying letters from Henri II of France to the College of Cardinals and to individual cardinals, in favor of Cardinal Reginald Pole. He did not arrive, however, until after the middle of the month of April. Cardinal Louis de Guise-Lorraine, also travelling from France, arrived on the 21st. In fact, only two French cardinals were in Rome, thereby giving the Imperial faction a great advantage. This was a matter of annoyance for the French, for King Henri had extracted from Pope Julius III, through negotiations carried on by Cardinals Georges d' Armagnac, Alessandro Farnese, and Jean du Bellay, a <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">bull</a> which allowed an extra 15 days before a Conclave began, in order to allow cardinals who had to travel a long distance (the French) to reach Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The bull was completely ignored by the Cardinals already in Rome, and only the <i>Novendiales</i> were observed.</p>
<p>In accordance with older instructions direct from Henri II, the French faction was supporting Cardinal d'Este, then the Cardinal de Tournon (who was not present at the Conclave), and then Cardinal du Bellay. The Emperor, as in the Conclave of 1550, had a preference for Cardinal Reginald Pole, the Papal Legate in England. Pole, however, was strongly opposed by the French (in ignorance of the letters which Farnese was carrying), and by Cardinal Gian Pietro Carafa, the Dean of the Sacred College of Cardinals and principal Inquisitor of the Roman Inquisition, who regarded Pole (and a number of other cardinals) as Protestant heretics. D'Este was not electable (Sir John Masone, the English agent wrote: "Marry, we hear of no quality to set him forward but that he is rich."), though the six votes he received in the first ballot caused considerable consternation, and the beginning of a "Stop D'Este" movement. Some of the cleverer cardinals, led by Carafa and Madruzzi of Trent, realized that, when all the opinions were factored in, there were very few electable cardinals, the best of whom was Cardinal Marcello Cervini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He was a reformer, he was strict, and he was uncorrupted; he was opposed to <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a>. Unfortunately, Cervini was disliked by the Emperor. But the genuine reformers, who wanted the resumption of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a>, worked with Carafa and Madruzzi to produce a two-thirds majority. Among them were Ranuccio Farnese, Farnese's brother, and Guido Ascanio Sforza, his first-cousin. When Farnese finally arrived from Avignon in mid-April, he was no doubt delighted to find his grandfather's secretary, Cardinal Marcello Cervini, on the throne of Peter. Unfortunately, on the morning of the 18th, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Marcellus_II" title="Pope Marcellus II">Pope Marcellus II</a> showed symptoms of a fever and in the night between April 30 and May 1, he died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> But even before Marcellus was dead, Cardinal Farnese wrote directly to Henri II, urging him to send the French cardinals to Rome immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> A second Conclave was necessary.</p>
<p>The second Conclave of 1555 opened on May 15, with the same cardinals as in April, but with the addition of eight late arrivals. The leading candidate seemed to be Cardinal Carafa, the Inquisitor, but he was told to his face by the Imperial Ambassador that the Emperor Charles V did not want him as Pope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The Imperial faction was favoring Cardinals Carpi, Morone, and Pole. Pole also seemed to have French support, but there was an influential group, led by Carafa, Carpi, and Alvarez (all professional Inquisitors), who openly questioned the orthodoxy of Pole and of Morone. The French Ambassador, Jean d' Avanson, informed King Henri that his favorite candidate, Cardinal d'Este, was being opposed vigorously by the Imperial faction, and that he could not win, thanks to a "virtual veto" (that is, the withholding of votes for a candidate by more than one-third of the voters); the Emperor even expressed fears that d'Este might try to bribe himself into the papacy. D'Avanson also had to break the news that Cardinal du Bellay, out of personal ambition, had broken ranks and would support Cardinal Carafa. In the voting, the Imperial candidate, Cardinal Carpi, seemed to be moving forward, until the French faction and the cardinals created by Julius III (of which there were fifteen at the Conclave) combined to put him out of the running. Once it was clear that nobody in the French faction was going to succeed, Cardinal Farnese and Cardinal d'Este decided to throw their support to Cardinal Carafa. The Imperial faction was so frightened at what Pope Carafa might do in trying to get Naples out of the hands of the Emperor that they sent Cardinals Corner and Ricci to Alessandro Farnese to beg him to abandon Carafa and accept their votes <i>for himself</i>. But Farnese was not so foolish as to believe that he could be successful without the endorsement either of the Emperor or of the King of France—and he had neither. He did not respond to the offer. The supporters of Carafa finally numbered more than the two-thirds needed for election, but the Imperialists (who were caucusing in the Hall of the Secret Consistory) refused to come to the Chapel and carry out the electoral process. It was Farnese who, using both blandishments and threats, managed to get the Imperialists to give in and assemble with the rest of the cardinals in the Cappella Paolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On the afternoon of May 23, the <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Ascension" title="Feast of the Ascension">Feast of the Ascension</a>, the seventy-eight year old Cardinal Gian Pietro Carafa of Naples was elected pope by <a href="/wiki/Acclamation" title="Acclamation">acclamation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> He chose the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_IV" title="Paul IV">Paul IV</a>.</p>
<p>He had been the co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/Theatines" title="Theatines">Theatine Order</a>, and was a promoter of reform in the Church. He was no <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist</a>, however, and preferred the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>. He was also a vigorous opponent of <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, and anything that favored it. He saw <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretics</a> everywhere, even inside the College of Cardinals, and as an Inquisitor he showed no scruple or mercy for those who were tainted. Showing all of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Prejudice" title="Prejudice">prejudices</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, and especially against converted Jews, he issued a bull on July 14, 1555, <i>Cum nimis absurdum</i>, creating the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_quarter_(diaspora)" title="Jewish quarter (diaspora)">Jewish Ghetto</a> of Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> He refused to recognize the election of Ferdinand I as Holy Roman Emperor, and he refused to recognize the retirement of Charles V. He ruled until his death on August 18, 1559, or rather his nephew, Cardinal Carlo Carafa, did. It was only three months before his death that Paul IV was fully informed of the misdeeds of his nephews, Cardinal Carafa, Giovanni Caraffa Duke of Paliano, and Antonio Carafa Marchese of Montebello. When the Florentine Ambassador, Bongiano Gianfiliazzi, had attempted to have an audience with Paul IV to enlighten him about his corrupt nephews, the door was slammed in his face by Cardinal Carafa. Obviously, there was no room in the Curia for the advice of Cardinal Farnese. There was considerable danger to Farnese properties because of Paul IV's anti-imperial policy.</p>
<p>It was in 1556 that Cardinal Farnese commissioned Giacomo Barozzi da Vignola to take in hand the half-completed <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortress</a> at Caprarola and turn it into a country villa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Actual construction began in 1559 and was completed in 1573. Even when half completed, in 1561, Cardinal Alessandro commissioned <a href="/wiki/Taddeo_Zuccari" title="Taddeo Zuccari">Taddeo Zuccari</a> and his workshop to decorate the rooms on the lower floor. The Stanza della solitudine was decorated by the same artists between 1563 and 1565, in accordance with a design created by Onufrio Panvinio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cardinal Farnese retired to Parma in the meantime (1557), where he stayed with his brother, Duke Ottavio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> It was there and at that time that they were approached with peace feelers by Cardinal Carafa, who was even willing to marry one of the Carafas to young Duke <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Farnese,_Duke_of_Parma" title="Alexander Farnese, Duke of Parma">Alessandro</a>, the son of Ottavio and Margaret of Parma. That alliance did not take place. Nonetheless, Cardinal Alessandro's influence in Rome was considerable. He was named Cardinal Protector of the Empire (September 14, 1541), Sicily, of Aragon (December 13, 1565),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> of Portugal, of Poland, of the Republic of Genoa, and of Ragusa<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Morone_Affair">The Morone Affair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Morone Affair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carafa was elected, and began dealing with real or imagined enemies.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
<p>First Ascanio della Corgna came under suspicion. He was general of the papal cavalry, and was actually suspected of being loyal to the Emperor. Paul IV had him sent to the Castel S. Angelo, along with his brother, Cardinal della Corgna (July 27, 1556). Both were nephews of Pope Julius III. The cardinal had been Administrator of the Diocese of Spoleto on the appointment of Julius III, but Paul IV immediately replaced him with Cardinal Alessandro Farnese. The charge against the cardinal that brought him to the Castel S. Angelo was that he had attempted to open communications with Philip II of Spain. The pressure of Spanish victories in Lombardy and Tuscany, however, forced their release.</p>
<p>Then it was the turn of Cardinal Morone, a man of tested prudence and familiar with Germany. He had been chosen as the <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">Papal Legate</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diet</a> in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, on the insistence of both Charles V and his brother Ferdinand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> He received the Legatine cross on February 13, 1555, and was in Augsburg on March 23, the day Pope Julius III died. He therefore arrived in Rome too late for the April Conclave. In the second Conclave, a month later, both Pole and Morone were Imperial candidates, but Cardinal Carafa (a candidate himself) loudly voiced his suspicions that both were heretics.</p>
<p>Suddenly, on May 31, 1557, Morone was arrested by the Pope's nephew and Secretary of State personally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Pietro Carnesecchi, a former adherent of Morone, wrote: "Why Morone is imprisoned, no one knows; many say that the Cardinals have brought it about, in order that he may be out of their way at the next election of a Pope, when he would get the greatest number of votes. The Pope intends summoning all the Cardinals to Rome, that they as a college may judge Morone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> There was an immediate outcry. The cardinals wanted a Congregation be held at which explanations would be demanded. Paul IV took the initiative and held a Congregation at which he presided, telling the cardinals that it was not politics but the honor of God that was involved. The process against Morone would be carried out by the members of the Inquisition. Twenty-one charges were levelled at him. On June 12, 1557, Morone was interrogated in the Castel S. Angelo by the committee: Cardinals Innocenzo del Monte, Jean Suau, Scipione Rebiba, Spoletano [Alessandro Farnese], and Michele Ghislieri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Having examined him and heard his extensive rejoinders, the committee reported in favor of Morone but Paul IV was not satisfied. Morone remained in the Castel S. Angelo until the death of Paul IV, when the College of Cardinals ordered his release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Cardinal Rebiba was sent as Nuncio to France, to discuss the matter of an alliance with Henri II.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France_or_Spain">France or Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: France or Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For two decades, the Farnese had been trying to maintain friendships both with King Henri II of France and the Emperor Charles V of the Holy Roman Empire. This became more difficult when Cardinal Carafa, in the name of Paul IV, concluded a treaty with Henri II on July 23, 1556, committing them to a war against the Emperor for the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 1557, the efforts collapsed. On October 23, 1557, Henri struck against Cardinal Alessandro Farnese, issuing letters patent by which he confiscated all of the benefices of the Cardinal which he still enjoyed in France. The Abbeys of St. Étienne de Caën, Beauport in Brittany, and Granselve, as well as the Administratorship of the Diocese of Viviers, were also included. In the document, Henri complained of the Cardinal and his brothers taking the part of the King of Spain. The total loss for the Cardinal alone amounted to more than 30.000 <a href="/wiki/Franc" title="Franc">francs</a>. The benefices were all given to Cardinal Ippolito II d'Este, Farnese's enemy, who had spent his youth at the French Court and was a personal friend of Henri and the Royal Family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>These losses were in addition to those inflicted by Pope Julius III, whose election had been opposed by Cardinal Farnese in the interest of Pope Paul III's secretary, Marcello Cervini. One of those was the Governorship of Tivoli, which also went to Cardinal d'Este. Another was the Patriarchate of Jerusalem, which was granted away only twenty days after the end of the Conclave.</p>
<p>But Julius III (Monte) had died in 1555, as had Marcellus II (Cervini). The Emperor Charles V had retired in 1556 and died in 1558 and both Paul IV and Henri II died in 1559, within a month of each other. A new and brighter day dawned.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclave_of_1559">Conclave of 1559</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conclave of 1559">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pope Paul IV died on August 18, 1559. Riots overwhelmed Rome. The Pope's statue on the Capitol was pulled down, and its head used as a football for three days. The Palace of the Inquisition was destroyed and its prisoners released, under the eyes of 2000 Romans. S. Maria sopra Minerva, the Dominican headquarters, was attacked. Cardinal Carafa fled the City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> For the approaching Conclave, King Philip II let it be known that his choices for pope were Cardinals Rodolfo Pio de Carpi, Giacomo Puteo, Giovanni Angelo de' Medici, and Clemente d' Olera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Medici had been one of the cardinals who had been on bad terms with Paul IV, and therefore he was living quietly at <a href="/wiki/Bagni_di_Lucca" title="Bagni di Lucca">Bagni di Lucca</a>. François II and Marie de Medicis were promoting Cardinals Ippolito d'Este, François de Tournon, and Ercole Gonzaga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Cardinal Farnese, who was the senior Cardinal Deacon in 1559, hated Cardinal d'Este and had no love for his cousin Cardinal Gonzaga, and therefore decided to do everything he could to elect Cardinal Carpi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Conclave of 1559 began on September 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Cardinal Farnese, still only 38 years of age, was active in the politicking, but, since he controlled only four or five votes, he had to work in alliance with some other group. He chose neither the French nor the Imperial faction, but allied himself with the Cardinals who had been created by Paul IV and were being led by his nephew, Cardinal Carlo Carafa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> By September 27, d'Este and Tournon were out of the running, and Gonzaga was the French candidate. The French attempted a coup-de-theatre by trying to have Gonzaga elected by acclamation. They failed miserably. Cardinal Jean Suau, who had been informed of the French king's wishes, ought to have worked for Gonzaga, but he was indebted to Cardinal Carafa, and therefore he worked with Carafa and Farnese to defeat the plan for Gonzaga. The maneuvering continued for months until it became apparent that the only candidate who could win support from both sides was Giovanni de' Medici. Suddenly, on the afternoon of Christmas Day, what turned out to be final negotiations began, and by 8:00 p.m. he was elected by acclamation. The next morning, a formal vote was taken, and it was Cardinal Farnese's privilege to announce to the people the election of Cardinal de' Medici as <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IV" title="Pope Pius IV">Pope Pius IV</a>. He was crowned by Cardinal Farnese on January 6, 1560.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The new Cardinal Nephews were <a href="/wiki/Charles_Borromeo" title="Charles Borromeo">Carlo Borromeo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Sittich_von_Hohenems_Altemps" title="Mark Sittich von Hohenems Altemps">Markus Sittich von Hohenems Altemps</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclave_of_1566">Conclave of 1566</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conclave of 1566">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the reign of Pope Pius IV, Cardinal Farnese enjoyed good fortune. The Pope was a friend of his, and he was able to avoid the unpleasantries of international affairs, as he built a circle of friends in the College of Cardinals and in the Roman Curia. On April 14, 1564, Cardinal Farnese was promoted to the title of Cardinal Priest of S. Lorenzo in Damaso, a post he held for less than a month. On May 12, he was promoted to the Order of Cardinal Bishops with the diocese of Sabina. He distanced himself from the horrors surrounding the fall of the Carafa nephews in 1561. Pius was not a healthy man, and his anticipated demise gave Farnese and others time to plan. Pope Pius IV (Medici) died on December 4, 1565.</p>
<p>The French, at this time Catherine de' Medicis, having learned nothing from 1555 or 1559, still offered their support to Cardinal Ippolito d'Este. King Philip II favored d' Olera, Ghislieri, Ricci, Morone, and Pacecho.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> When the Spanish Ambassador, Don Luis Requesens de Zuniga, arrived in Rome on December 21, 1565, however, he carried instructions to support Ghislieri and Morone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> He was suspicious of the orthodoxy of Cardinal Morone though, and he feared that Cardinal Farnese might pursue a vendetta against Spain for the murder of his father if he were to become pope. The Emperor Maximilian II was informed by his agent in Rome, Nosti Camiani, that the most favored cardinals were: d' Olera, Boncompagni, Suau, Sirleto, Simonetta, Gianbernardino Scotti, and Michele Ghislieri. He wrote directly to Cosimo III of Florence, asking for his assistance in the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Cosimo replied that he was no longer in the business of influencing papal elections, but agreed to serve the Emperor's wishes. He was in fact very active behind the scenes. He wanted a pope who would make him <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_Tuscany" title="List of rulers of Tuscany">Duke of Tuscany</a>, and would validate his control over Siena, and he had already picked out Cardinal Ghislieri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The Duchess of Ferrara, a daughter of the Emperor, was recommending Cardinal Francesco Gonzaga, the 27 year old nephew of Cardinal Ercole Gonzaga (who had died in 1563), for whom she was soliciting the Emperor, the Duke of Savoy and the King of France, Francois II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The Duchess of Savoy was pushing for Cardinal Ferrero, who was only 28 years old. The nephews of Pius IV, Cardinal Borromeo and Cardinal Altemps, had another candidate in mind, the Nuncio in Spain, Cardinal Ugo Boncompagni, and they sent a swift galley to fetch him. He did not arrive in time though. And then there was Cardinal Alessandro Farnese, who was campaigning for himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were fifty-two cardinals in attendance on December 20, 1565. Seventeen cardinals were under the age of forty, seven of whom were under the age of thirty. Cardinal Farnese, nephew of Pope Paul III, had in his faction Cardinals Corregio, Gambara, Savelli, Paleotti and Orsini. Farnese was personally beloved by the Roman populace as a patron of the poor and a Maecenas of the arts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The cardinals who had been created by Paul IV were also supporting Farnese. They included: Vitelli (the <a href="/wiki/Camerlengo_of_the_Holy_Roman_Church" title="Camerlengo of the Holy Roman Church">Camerlengo</a>), <a href="/wiki/Gianantonio_Capizucchi" title="Gianantonio Capizucchi">Capizucchi</a>, Reuman Suau, Rebiba, Ghislieri, and D' Olera. The cardinals created by Pius IV numbered nine, and under certain circumstances might draw four more. The Gonzaga faction had six members. The cardinals created by Julius III numbered five. The Florentines had between four and six. The Venetians had three. Six French cardinals did not come to the Conclave at all. The large number of factions would present a major difficulty for anyone trying to put together a two-thirds majority. Nationality was not the only way of dividing up the cardinals either. There were the seniors and the juniors, and the eager reformers and the complacent majority.</p>

<p>As soon as the Conclave opened, Cardinal Borromeo, who, as the nephew of the late pope, considered himself a leader, if not a pope-maker, approached Cardinal Farnese, who had the largest number of commitments. He indicated that he thought that Cardinal Morone should be pope. This was in accordance with the wishes of the Emperor, and Morone had been one of the Presidents of the Council of Trent. Farnese seemed friendly and willing to please, which Borromeo (wrongly) took as an agreement to supply votes. There were those who would never vote for a friend of the Emperor, and there were those who remembered Morone's troubles with the Inquisition, and the bull of Paul IV which had stated that no one who was arrested, imprisoned, or convicted by the Inquisition could ever vote in a papal election, let alone be elected pope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In addition, there were those who did not like Morone personally. It is alleged that both Alessandro Farnese and Ippolito d'Este had grudges against Morone due to decisions which he had made while Legate in Bologna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> His enemies could always muster more than one-third of the College to block his election. When it came to a vote, Morone got twenty-nine votes. Farnese must have realized that he was in somewhat the same situation himself. He could muster between twenty-eight and thirty votes, but not the thirty-five needed to elect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his straightforward way, Borromeo then went on to his second candidate, Cardinal Sirleto, but he too failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Borromeo went to Farnese, and stated flatly that he was not going to support Farnese in the current Conclave, and he asked Farnese to help him in choosing a worthy pope. Farnese suggested the names of four cardinals whom he would support: Gianbernardino Scotti (Trani), D' Olera (Aracoeli), Ghislieri (Alessandrino) and Ricci (Montepulciano). Borromeo was delighted with the naming of Cardinal Ghislieri:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Having known the Cardinal of Alessandria [Ghislieri] for a considerable time, and conceived a high esteem for him on account of his singular holiness and zeal, I judged that no more fitting Pontiff than he could be found to rule the Christian commonwealth wisely and well. I therefore took up his cause with all my might; and with little delay he was elected Pope to the great satisfaction of all. Nothing could be so great a consolation to me in my grief for my uncle, as the certainty that he is succeeded by one who possesses all the qualities that your Eminence sympathizes with me in lamenting, and who with equal courage and strength of soul will know how to maintain and uphold the authority of religion</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Within two hours, they had sufficient votes to elect Ghislieri. It was January 7, 1566. That afternoon, the Cardinals assembled and took a vote; two cardinals voted from their sickbeds. The votes were not by secret ballot, but out loud. Ghislieri was elected unanimously and took the name <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_V" title="Pope Pius V">Pius V</a>. Borromeo and Farnese had made a pope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1569, the Cardinal was Legatus perpetuus (Permanent legate) of the Province of the Patrimony of St. Peter, resident in <a href="/wiki/Viterbo" title="Viterbo">Viterbo</a>. This is recorded on the inscription of a new town gate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1569, Cardinal Alessandro Farnese made his journey to Sicily, to inspect his Archdiocese of Monreale. Transportation was provided by four galleys lent by the Knights of St. John of Jerusalem. At Monreale, he conducted a <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_Synod" title="Diocesan Synod">diocesan synod</a>. He resigned the diocese on December 9, 1573.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 7, 1571, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto" title="Battle of Lepanto">Battle of Lepanto</a> took place in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Corinth" title="Gulf of Corinth">Gulf of Corinth</a>. The Christian fleet, commanded by King Philip II's half-brother, Don John of Austria, inflicted a decisive defeat on the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Turks</a>, destroying all but thirty of its ships. Cardinal Farnese was on the docks at <a href="/wiki/Civitavecchia" title="Civitavecchia">Civitavecchia</a> to welcome home the hero, his brother's brother-in-law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclave_of_1572">Conclave of 1572</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conclave of 1572">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the determining factors in the minds of the electors was the recent <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto" title="Battle of Lepanto">Battle of Lepanto</a> (October 7, 1571). It had been a stunning defeat for the Ottoman Turks, the first naval defeat in more than a century, and nearly their entire navy was destroyed. But they did not lose control over the Eastern Mediterranean, and they were already rebuilding their navy at lightning speed. In some people's minds, what was needed was a pope who could hold the various forces together: a Crusading pope who would also be generous with Church money to finance the war. The new pope must also be a strong and strict defender of the Faith in the face of unorthodoxy and one who would enforce the decrees of the Council of Trent (1545-1547; 1551-1552; 1562-1563).</p>

<p>There were other problems that a pope would have to face. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth</a> of England had been excommunicated by Pius V, and she had obtained possession of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a>. Mary's relatives, the Guises, were the leaders of the devout hyper-Catholic party in France, who were eager for a show-down with the <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a>. <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de' Medici">Catherine de Medicis</a> was attempting to avoid a civil war in France, and she believed that the marriage of her daughter Margot with Henri of Navarre, a Protestant, might avert disaster but dispensations would be required of the pope. She was also trying to tempt Queen Elizabeth into marriage with her son, Henri, and that would require papal cooperation as well. Her choices were the Cardinal of Ferrara, <a href="/wiki/Ippolito_II_d%27Este" title="Ippolito II d'Este">Ippolito d'Este</a>, who was as disliked in 1572 as he had been in 1549. His collection of enemies had grown to include Cardinals Bonelli, Borromeo, Farnese, Medici, and Morone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Catherine, however, was in contact with her cousin, <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_I_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cosimo I, Grand Duke of Tuscany</a>, who, behind the scenes, was promoting Cardinal Boncompagni. Cardinal de' Medici was to inform the leaders of the Faction of Pius IV, Borromeo and Altemps, that the Medici were interested only in Sirleto and Boncompagni. With the French and Florentine votes, in fact, Cardinal d'Este had the resources to block any candidate he pleased (the 'virtual veto').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>D'Este, however, was not electable. The cardinals of Pius V (some twelve or thirteen votes) were joined with many of those of Pius IV to prevent the election of d'Este, but also of Farnese, Ricci, and Burali d' Arezzo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Cardinal Giovanni Morone was also a candidate again, but Cardinal Bonelli (Alessandrino, the nephew of Pius V) was prepared use his votes to exclude him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> His friends nonetheless made an effort to have him elected by acclamation on the opening day of the Conclave, May 12, but the attempt failed.</p>
<p>Cardinal Farnese believed that this was his conclave, and he was making every effort to win supporters. He knew, however, that the Spanish were against him. On the night of the opening of the Conclave, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Perrenot_de_Granvelle" title="Antoine Perrenot de Granvelle">Granvelle</a> arrived from Naples. He had been sent to Italy by <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> in 1571 to prepare the fleet which eventually met the Turks at Lepanto; he was kept on as Viceroy of Naples. Shortly after his entry into the Conclave, he produced an unopened letter which (he said) had reached him while he was on the highway from Naples. It was from Philip II. Granvelle opened the letter in Cardinal Farnese's presence and read the contents, which ordered Granvelle to advise Farnese that he was not to attempt to become pope "this time"—it should be remembered that, experienced as he was, Cardinal Farnese was only fifty-one. Farnese's chances ended on the first day of the Conclave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>Boncompagni was the obvious candidate. He was acceptable to Cardinal Borromeo and the reformers. He was a successful Nuncio in Spain, and was acceptable to the Spanish faction, which included Naples. The Conclave turned out to be a very short one. On May 14, Ugo Boncompagni was elected Pope, and took the name <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII" title="Pope Gregory XIII">Gregory XIII</a>. He was crowned by Cardinal Ippolito d'Este, the senior Cardinal Deacon, on May 20, 1572. Cardinal d'Este, one of Alessandro Farnese's favorite enemies, died on December 2, 1572.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclave_of_1585">Conclave of 1585</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Conclave of 1585">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Spring of 1585, an embassy from Japan was making its way to Rome. They arrived by ship at <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">Livorno</a> on March 1, and proceeded by land through Tuscany. They were received in Florence by the Grand Duke Francesco de' Medici. They proceeded on to Rome, accompanied by Cardinal Francesco Gambara, and were received with a grand show of hospitality at Caprarola by Cardinal Alessandro Farnese. Accompanied by Gambara and Farnese, the embassy reached Rome on March 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>

<p>Pope Gregory XIII died after fifteen years on the Throne of Peter on April 10, 1585. There were sixty-two living cardinals, but only thirty-two managed to make it to Rome in time for the opening ceremonies of the Conclave on Easter Sunday, April 21, 1585. Cardinal Farnese, though he was only sixty-four years old, was the senior cardinal present. He was both Dean of the Sacred <a href="/wiki/College_of_Cardinals" title="College of Cardinals">College of Cardinals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suburbicarian_Diocese_of_Ostia" title="Suburbicarian Diocese of Ostia">Cardinal Bishop of Ostia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> But he was no faction leader. Thirty-nine cardinals, many of them his friends and supporters, had died during the reign of Gregory XIII. There was only one other cardinal present who had been created by his grandfather, Paul III. The Imperial-Spanish faction was headed by Cardinal Ferdinando de' Medici, brother of the Grand Duke of Tuscany, and soon to become the Grand Duke himself. The actual Spanish leader was Cardinal Ludovico Madruzzo but he did not arrive in Rome until the evening of April 23. The French faction was headed by Ludovico d'Este, grandson of Louis XII and Protector of France before the Holy See.</p>
<p>After the first vote, on April 23, it was obvious that neither a strong Spanish candidate nor a strong French candidate could be elected. Medici and d'Este met, and Medici proposed two possible compromise candidates to d'Este: Cardinal Albani and Cardinal Felice Peretti Montalto. Madruzzo's chosen candidate was Cardinal Sirleto, but in a meeting with d'Este shortly after he entered the Conclave on the 24th, it was made clear to him that Sirleto was being excluded by the votes of the French. Madruzzo declared that he would not accept Albani. D'Este met with Cardinal Farnese, hoping to stop any effort that Farnese might begin to exclude Montalto. He had already been trying to organize some of Gregory XIII's cardinals into a faction of his own. Farnese already believed that Montalto had little real support, and d'Este encouraged that misapprehension. But, when all the likely votes were tallied, it seemed that the supporters of Montalto lacked four votes, which would have to come from Farnese. When the Cardinals assembled to begin the balloting, d'Este suddenly intervened and announced that it was not necessary to proceed to a ballot since they already had a pope—Cardinal Montalto. The cardinals immediately proceeded to 'adore' Montalto—which was a legitimate method for electing a pope. There had to be no opposition, and there was none. Farnese had been silenced and coerced into cooperation.  </p>
<p>In the Spring of 1586, Cardinal Farnese's young nephew Ranuccio, aged 17, came to Rome to swear allegiance for his domains of which the Church was the feudal souverain. He made the mistake of appearing before <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_V" title="Pope Sixtus V">Pope Sixtus V</a> in armor carrying weapons, and for that horrible crime he was imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/Castel_Sant%27Angelo" title="Castel Sant'Angelo">Castel S. Angelo</a>. His uncle the Cardinal pleaded twice with the Pope to have him released, and finally contrived to have Ranuccio escape. The Spanish Ambassador, Olivares, invited the Castellan of the Castel S. Angelo to dinner, while the Cardinal tricked the guards into releasing Ranuccio into his custody. The Pope was livid at having been circumvented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collector_and_Patron">Collector and Patron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Collector and Patron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The buildings that Cardinal Farnese built or restored include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Gesu" title="Church of the Gesu">Church of the Gesù</a> in Rome, the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Farnese" title="Villa Farnese">Villa Farnese</a> at Caprarola, and the Farnese palace near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Bracciano" title="Lake Bracciano">Lake Bracciano</a>, and the monastery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tre_Fontane" title="Tre Fontane">Tre Fontane</a>.</p>
<p>Alessandro Farnese is remembered for gathering the greatest collection of Roman sculpture assembled in private hands since Antiquity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> now mostly in Naples, after passing by inheritance to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbon-Parma" title="Bourbon-Parma">Bourbon-Parma</a> kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> His generosity towards artists made a virtual <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">academy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> at the house he built at <a href="/wiki/Caprarola" title="Caprarola">Caprarola</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> and in his lodgings at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palazzo_della_Cancellaria" title="Palazzo della Cancellaria">Palazzo della Cancellaria</a> and, after his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Ranuccio_Farnese" title="Cardinal Ranuccio Farnese">Cardinal Ranuccio Farnese</a> died in 1565, at the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Farnese" title="Palazzo Farnese">Palazzo Farnese</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Farnese" title="Palazzo Farnese">Palazzo Farnese</a> the best sculptors worked under his eye, to restore fragments of antiquities as complete sculptures, with great scholarly care. He was also a great patron of living artists including, most notably, <a href="/wiki/El_Greco" title="El Greco">El Greco</a>. Under the direction of his <a href="/wiki/Curator" title="Curator">curator</a> and librarian, the <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquarian</a> iconographer <a href="/wiki/Fulvio_Orsini" title="Fulvio Orsini">Fulvio Orsini</a>, the Farnese collections were enlarged and systematized. Farnese collected ancient coins and commissioned modern medals. He had paintings by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, and an important collection of drawings. He commissioned the masterpiece of <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Clovio" title="Giulio Clovio">Giulio Clovio</a>, arguably the last major <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscript</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Farnese_Hours" title="Farnese Hours">Farnese Hours</a>,</i> which was completed in 1546 after being nine years in the making (now the property of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgan_Library" title="Morgan Library">Morgan Library</a>, New York). The <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(room)" title="Cabinet (room)">studiolo</a> built to house this collection appears to be the one re-erected at the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27%C3%89couen" title="Château d'Écouen">Musée de la Renaissance</a>, Écouen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1550, Farnese acquired a northern portion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palatine_hill" title="Palatine hill">Palatine hill</a> in Rome and had Roman ruins from the palace built by the Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> (A. D. 14–37) at the northwest end filled in, and converted to a summer home and formal gardens. The <a href="/wiki/Farnese_Gardens" title="Farnese Gardens">Farnese Gardens</a> became one of the first <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical gardens</a> in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> From these gardens are derived the names of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acacia_farnesiana" title="Acacia farnesiana">Acacia farnesiana</a> and from its floral essence, the important biochemical <a href="/wiki/Farnesol" title="Farnesol">farnesol</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants">Descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cardinal Alessandro Farnese had a daughter <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clelia_Farnese" title="it:Clelia Farnese">Clelia</a>, with Claude de Beaune de Semblançay, dame de Châteaubrun, a <a href="/wiki/Lady-in-waiting" title="Lady-in-waiting">lady-in-waiting</a> and confidante of Catherine de Medicis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> born in 1556. Claude de Beaune's brother Martin became Bishop of Puy (1557-1561) and her other brother, Renaud, Bishop of Mende (1571-1581) and then <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Bourges" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Bourges">Archbishop of Bourges</a>. In 1570, Clelia married <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovan_Giorgio_Cesarini" title="it:Giovan Giorgio Cesarini">Giangiorgio Cesarini</a>, Marchese of Civitanova, <a href="/wiki/Gonfaloniere" title="Gonfaloniere">Gonfaloniere</a> of the City of Rome (1565-1585). In September 1572, they had a son, <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuliano_Cesarini_(1572-1613)" title="it:Giuliano Cesarini (1572-1613)">Giuliano</a>. Clelia's husband died in 1585. On August 2, 1587, at the Farnese palace in Caprarola, she married her second husband, <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marco_Pio_di_Savoia" title="it:Marco Pio di Savoia">Marco Pio di Savoia</a>, Lord of Sassuolo; the marriage was without issue. Clelia died in 1613. The absence of scandal concerning her birth suggests that Cardinal Farnese was not yet in Holy Orders ca. 1555-1556.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_Monument:_The_Ges.C3.B9">Death and Monument: The Gesù</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Death and Monument: The Gesù">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cardinal Alessandro Farnese wrote a <a href="/wiki/Will_and_testament" title="Will and testament">will</a> in 1580, and added <a href="/wiki/Codicil_(will)" title="Codicil (will)">codicils</a> in 1585, but these were revoked and replaced by a new will, written on June 22, 1588, with codicils added in July and August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 7, 1588, the physician of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Urbino" title="Duchy of Urbino">Duke of Urbino</a>, Vincenzo Remosetti, was summoned to a consultation at the Farnese Palace at Caprarola, "per uno grande accidente di epilepsia," accompanied by severe <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory</a> problems. The Cardinal was gravely ill. He was subjected to the usual <a href="/wiki/Quackery" title="Quackery">quackery</a>, cautery and bleeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> He was attended by Msgr. Camillo Caetani, the brother of Cardinal Enrico Caetani, who kept his brother informed of Farnese's condition. On August 13, the doctor had to return to Rome; he left the Cardinal restless and weak, and suffering from "gotta" in his left arm. On February 28, 1589, he suffered an attack of some sort, perhaps a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> ("ictus"). Cardinal Farnese died quietly of the effects of <a href="/wiki/Apoplexy" title="Apoplexy">apoplexy</a> in Rome on March 2, 1589, at the age of seventy. He was buried before the high altar in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Ges%C3%B9" title="Church of the Gesù">Church of the Gesù</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Forty-two cardinals participated in the funeral ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Above the main door of that church, on the interior side, is the famous inscription:</p>
<center>ALEXANDER . FARNESIVS<br/>
<p>CARD . S . R . E . VICECANCELL<br/>
PAVLI . III. PONT . MAX . NEPOS<br/>
CVIVS . AVCTORITATE . SOCIETAS<br/>
IESV . RECEPTA . PRIMVM, FVIT<br/>
ET . DECRETIS . AMPLISS . ORNATA<br/>
TEMPLVM . HOC . SUAE. MONVMENTVM<br/>
RELIGIONIS . ET. PERPETVAE<br/>
IN . EVM . ORDINEM . VOLVNTATIS<br/>
A . FVNDAMENTIS . EXTRVXIT<br/>
ANNO . IVBILEI . M . D . LXXV</p>
</center>
<p>commemorating the establishment of the Jesuit Order by Paul III in 1540, and the building of the Church of the Gesù for them through the generosity of Cardinal Farnese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> The church, the work of <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Barozzi_da_Vignola" title="Giacomo Barozzi da Vignola">Giacomo Vignola</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_della_Porta" title="Giacomo della Porta">Giacomo della Porta</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> (1568-1575), is one of the great monuments of <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> religious architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
