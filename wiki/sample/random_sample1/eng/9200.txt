<p><b>Alejandro Jodorowsky Prullansky</b> (<small>Spanish: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[aleˈxandɾo xoðoˈɾofski]</a></span>; born 17 February 1929)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chilean_people" title="Chilean people">Chilean</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>-French film and theatre director, screenwriter, playwright, actor, author, poet, producer, composer, musician, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comics_writer" title="Comics writer">comics writer</a>, and spiritual guru. Best known for his <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> films, he has been "venerated by <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">cult cinema</a> enthusiasts" for his work which "is filled with violently <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surreal</a> images and a hybrid blend of mysticism and religious provocation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Born to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish-Ukrainian" title="Jewish-Ukrainian">Jewish-Ukrainian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a> parents in Chile, Jodorowsky experienced an unhappy and alienated childhood, and so immersed himself in reading and writing poetry. Dropping out of college, he became involved in theater and in particular mime, working as a clown before founding his own theater troupe, the <i>Teatro Mimico</i>, in 1947. Moving to Paris in the early 1950s, Jodorowsky studied mime under <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Decroux" title="Étienne Decroux">Étienne Decroux</a> before turning to cinema, directing the short film <i><a href="/wiki/Les_t%C3%AAtes_interverties" title="Les têtes interverties">Les têtes interverties</a></i> in 1957. From 1960 he divided his time between Paris and Mexico City, in the former becoming a founding member of the anarchistic avant-garde <a href="/wiki/Panic_Movement" title="Panic Movement">Panic Movement</a> of performance artists. In 1966 he created his first comic strip, <i>Anibal 5</i>, while in 1967 he directed his first feature film, the surrealist <i><a href="/wiki/Fando_y_Lis" title="Fando y Lis">Fando y Lis</a></i>, which caused a huge scandal in Mexico, eventually being banned.</p>
<p>His next film, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_western" title="Acid western">acid western</a> <i><a href="/wiki/El_Topo" title="El Topo">El Topo</a></i> (1970), became a hit on the <a href="/wiki/Midnight_movie" title="Midnight movie">midnight movie</a> circuit in the United States, considered as the first-ever midnight cult film, garnering high praise from <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, which led to Jodorowsky being provided with $1 million to finance his next film. The result was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Mountain_(1973_film)" title="The Holy Mountain (1973 film)">The Holy Mountain</a></i> (1973), a surrealist exploration of <a href="/wiki/Western_esotericism" title="Western esotericism">western esotericism</a>. Disagreements with the film's distributor <a href="/wiki/Allen_Klein" title="Allen Klein">Allen Klein</a>, however, led to both <i>The Holy Mountain</i> and <i>El Topo</i> failing to gain widespread distribution, although both became classics on the underground film circuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dune_(Jodorowsky_film)" title="Dune (Jodorowsky film)">an aborted attempt</a> at filming <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>'s 1965 science fiction novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_(novel)" title="Dune (novel)">Dune</a></i>, Jodorowsky produced three more films, the family film <i><a href="/wiki/Tusk_(1980_film)" title="Tusk (1980 film)">Tusk</a></i> (1980), the surrealist horror <i><a href="/wiki/Santa_Sangre" title="Santa Sangre">Santa Sangre</a></i> (1989), and the failed blockbuster <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow_Thief" title="The Rainbow Thief">The Rainbow Thief</a></i> (1990). Meanwhile, he has simultaneously written a series of science fiction comic books, most notably <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incal" title="The Incal">The Incal</a></i> (1980–1989), which has been described as having a claim to be "the best comic book" ever written,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and also <i><a href="/wiki/The_Technopriests" title="The Technopriests">The Technopriests</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Metabarons" title="Metabarons">Metabarons</a></i>. Accompanying this, he has also written books and regularly lectures on his own spiritual system, which he calls "psychomagic" and "psychoshamanism" and which borrows from his interests in <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tarot" title="Tarot">tarot</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" title="Zen Buddhism">Zen Buddhism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shamanism" title="Shamanism">shamanism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005xi_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005xi-8">[8]</a></sup> His son Cristóbal has followed his teachings on psychoshamanism; this work is captured in the feature documentary <i>Quantum Men</i>, directed by Carlos Serrano Azcona.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_.281929.E2.80.931952.29">Early years (1929–1952)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years (1929–1952)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jodorowsky was born in 1929 in the coastal town of <a href="/wiki/Tocopilla" title="Tocopilla">Tocopilla</a>, Chile, to parents who were Jewish immigrants from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yekaterinoslav" title="Yekaterinoslav">Yekaterinoslav</a> (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dnipro_(city)" title="Dnipro (city)">Dnipro</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elisavetgrad" title="Elisavetgrad">Elisavetgrad</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Kropyvnytskyi" title="Kropyvnytskyi">Kropyvnytskyi</a>) and other cities of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>. His father, Jaime Jodorowsky Groismann, was a merchant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005ix_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005ix-10">[10]</a></sup> who was largely abusive to his wife Sara Felicidad Prullansky Arcavi, and at one time accused her of flirting with a customer. Angered, he subsequently beat and raped her, getting her pregnant, which led to the birth of Alejandro. Because of this brutal conception, Sara both hated her husband and disliked her son, telling him that "I cannot love you" and rarely showing him tenderness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky200539.E2.80.9340_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky200539.E2.80.9340-11">[11]</a></sup> Alejandro also had an elder sister, Raquel Jodorowsky, but disliked her for he believed that she was selfish, doing "everything to expel me from the family so that she could be the centre of attention."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005140_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005140-12">[12]</a></sup> Alongside his dislike for his family, he also held contempt for many of the local people, who viewed him as an outsider because of his status as the son of immigrants, and also for the American mining industrialists who worked locally and treated the Chilean people badly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup> It was this treatment at the hands of Americans that led to his later condemnation of American <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-colonialism" title="Neo-colonialism">neo-colonialism</a> in Latin America in several of his films. Nonetheless he liked his local area, and was greatly unhappy when he was forced to leave it aged nine years old, something for which he blamed his father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005115_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005115-13">[13]</a></sup> His family subsequently moved to the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago, Chile</a>.</p>
<p>He immersed himself in reading, and also began writing poetry, having his first poem published when he was sixteen years old, alongside associating with such Chilean poets as <a href="/wiki/Nicanor_Parra" title="Nicanor Parra">Nicanor Parra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Lihn" title="Enrique Lihn">Enrique Lihn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire-oct-09_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-empire-oct-09-14">[14]</a></sup> Becoming interested in the political ideology of <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a>, he began attending college, studying <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and philosophy, but stayed for only two years. After dropping out, and having an interest in theatre and particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mime" title="Mime">mime</a>, he took up employment as a <a href="/wiki/Clown" title="Clown">clown</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circus</a> and began a career as a theatre director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup> Meanwhile, in 1947 he founded his own theatrical troupe, the Teatro Mimico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire-oct-09_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-empire-oct-09-14">[14]</a></sup> which by 1952 had fifty members, and the following year he wrote his first play, <i>El Minotaura</i> (<i>The Minotaur</i>). Nonetheless, Jodorowsky felt that there was little for him left in Chile, and so that year he moved to Paris, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France.2C_Mexico.2C_and_Fando_y_Lis_.281953.E2.80.931969.29">France, Mexico, and <i>Fando y Lis</i> (1953–1969)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: France, Mexico, and Fando y Lis (1953–1969)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was while in Paris that Jodorowsky began studying mime with <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Decroux" title="Étienne Decroux">Étienne Decroux</a> and joined the troupe of one of Decroux's students, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Marceau" title="Marcel Marceau">Marcel Marceau</a>. It was with Marceau's troupe that he went on a world tour, and wrote several routines for the group, including "The Cage" and "The Mask Maker". After this, he returned to theatre directing, working on the music hall comeback of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Chevalier" title="Maurice Chevalier">Maurice Chevalier</a> in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1957, Jodorowsky turned his hand to filmmaking, creating <i><a href="/wiki/Les_t%C3%AAtes_interverties" title="Les têtes interverties">Les têtes interverties</a></i> (<i>The Severed Heads</i>), a 20-minute adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a>'s novella. It consisted almost entirely of mime, and told the surreal story of a head-swapping merchant who helps a young man find courtship success. Jodorowsky played the lead role. The director <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a> admired the film, and wrote an introduction for it. It was considered lost until a print of the film was discovered in 2006.</p>
<p>In 1960, Jodorowsky moved to Mexico, where he settled down in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>. Nonetheless, he continued to return occasionally to France, on one occasion visiting the <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealist</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="André Breton">André Breton</a>, but he was disillusioned in that he felt Breton had become somewhat conservative in his old age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup> Continuing his interest in surrealism, in 1962 he founded the <a href="/wiki/Panic_Movement" title="Panic Movement">Panic Movement</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Arrabal" title="Fernando Arrabal">Fernando Arrabal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roland_Topor" title="Roland Topor">Roland Topor</a>. The movement aimed to go beyond the conventional surrealist ideas by embracing absurdism. Its members refused to take themselves seriously, while laughing at those critics who did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Church_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Church-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1966 he produced his first comic strip, <i>Anibal 5</i>, which was related to the Panic Movement. The following year he created a new feature film, <i><a href="/wiki/Fando_y_Lis" title="Fando y Lis">Fando y Lis</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire-oct-09_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-empire-oct-09-14">[14]</a></sup> loosely based on a play written by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Arrabal" title="Fernando Arrabal">Fernando Arrabal</a>, who was working with Jodorowsky on <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a> at the time. <i>Fando y Lis</i> premiered at the 1968 <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a> Film Festival, where it instigated a riot amongst those objecting to the film's content,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenbaum92_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenbaum92-15">[15]</a></sup> and subsequently it was banned in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenbaum93_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenbaum93-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was in Mexico City that he encountered Ejo Takata (1928–1997), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" title="Zen Buddhism">Zen Buddhist</a> monk who had studied at the Horyuji and Shofukuji monasteries in Japan before traveling to Mexico via the United States in 1967 to spread Zen. Jodorowsky became a disciple of Takata and offered his own house to be turned into a <a href="/wiki/Zendo" title="Zendo">zendo</a>. Subsequently, Takata attracted other disciples around him, who spent their time in <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a> and the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koan" title="Koan">koans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky20052.E2.80.934_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky20052.E2.80.934-17">[17]</a></sup> Eventually, Takata instructed Jodorowsky that he had to learn more about his feminine side, and so he went and befriended the English surrealist <a href="/wiki/Leonora_Carrington" title="Leonora Carrington">Leonora Carrington</a>, who recently had moved to Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky200524_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky200524-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="El_Topo_and_The_Holy_Mountain_.281970.E2.80.931974.29"><i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Holy Mountain</i> (1970–1974)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: El Topo and The Holy Mountain (1970–1974)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1970, Jodorowsky released the film <i><a href="/wiki/El_Topo" title="El Topo">El Topo</a></i>, which sometimes is known in English as <i>The Mole</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empire-oct-09_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-empire-oct-09-14">[14]</a></sup> which he had both directed and starred in. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_western" title="Acid western">acid western</a>, <i>El Topo</i> tells the story of a wandering Mexican bandit and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunslinger" title="Gunslinger">gunslinger</a>, El Topo (played by Jodorowsky), who is on a search for spiritual enlightenment, taking his young son along with him. Along the way, he violently confronts a number of other individuals, before finally being killed and being resurrected to live within a community of deformed people who are trapped inside a mountain cave. Describing the work, he stated that "I ask of film what most North Americans ask of psychedelic drugs. The difference being that when one creates a psychedelic film, he need not create a film that shows the visions of a person who has taken a pill; rather, he needs to manufacture the pill."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Knowing how <i>Fando y Lis</i> had caused such a scandal in Mexico, Jodorowsky decided not to release <i>El Topo</i> there,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenbaum93_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenbaum93-16">[16]</a></sup> instead focusing on its release in other countries across the world, including Mexico's northern neighbour, the United States. It was in New York City where the film would play as a "<a href="/wiki/Midnight_movie" title="Midnight movie">midnight movie</a>" for several months at <a href="/wiki/Ben_Barenholtz" title="Ben Barenholtz">Ben Barenholtz</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Elgin_Theater" title="Elgin Theater">Elgin Theater</a>. It attracted the attention of rock musician and countercultural figure <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, who thought very highly of it, and convinced the president of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' company <a href="/wiki/Apple_Corps" title="Apple Corps">Apple Corps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Klein" title="Allen Klein">Allen Klein</a>, to distribute it in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005237_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005237-20">[20]</a></sup> In regard to the making of <i><a href="/wiki/El_Topo" title="El Topo">El Topo</a></i>, Jodorowsky stated that, "When I wanted to do the rape scene, I explained to [Mara Lorenzio] that I was going to hit her and rape her. There was no emotional relationship between us, because I had put a clause in all the women's contracts stating that they would not make love with the director. We had never talked to each other. I knew nothing about her. We went to the desert with two other people: the photographer and a technician. No one else. I said, 'I'm not going to rehearse. There will be only one take because it will be impossible to repeat. Roll the cameras only when I signal you to.' Then I told her, 'Pain does not hurt. Hit me.' And she hit me. I said, 'Harder.' And she started to hit me very hard, hard enough to break a rib...I ached for a week. After she had hit me long enough and hard enough to tire her, I said, 'Now it's my turn. Roll the cameras.' And I really...I really...I really raped her. And she screamed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crouse2010_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crouse2010-21">[21]</a></sup> He went on to state, "Then she told me that she had been raped before. You see, for me the character is frigid until El Topo rapes her. And she has an orgasm. That's why I show a stone phallus in that scene . . . which spouts water. She has an orgasm. She accepts the male sex. And that's what happened to Mara in reality. She really had that problem. Fantastic scene. A very, very strong scene."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crouse2010_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crouse2010-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Klein agreed to give Jodorowsky $1 million to go toward creating his next film. The result was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Mountain_(1973_film)" title="The Holy Mountain (1973 film)">The Holy Mountain</a></i>, released in 1973. It has been suggested that <i>The Holy Mountain</i> may have been inspired by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Daumal" title="René Daumal">René Daumal</a>'s Surrealist novel <i><a href="/wiki/Mount_Analogue" title="Mount Analogue">Mount Analogue</a></i>. <i>The Holy Mountain</i> was another complex, multi-part story that featured a man credited as "The Thief" and equated with <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus Christ</a>, a mystical <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemist</a> played by Jodorowsky, seven powerful business people representing seven of the planets (Venus and the six planets from Mars to Pluto), a religious training regimen of spiritual rebirth, and a quest to the top of a holy mountain for the secret of <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortality</a>. During the completion of <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, Jodorowsky received spiritual training from <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Ichazo" title="Oscar Ichazo">Oscar Ichazo</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arica_School" title="Arica School">Arica School</a>, who encouraged him to take <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid_diethylamide" title="Lysergic acid diethylamide">LSD</a> and guided him through the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">psychedelic</a> experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Around the same time (2 November 1973), Jodorowsky participated in an <a href="/wiki/Isolation_tank" title="Isolation tank">isolation tank</a> experiment conducted by <a href="/wiki/John_C._Lilly" title="John C. Lilly">John Lilly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly thereafter, Allen Klein demanded that Jodorowsky create a film adaptation of Pauline Réage's classic novel of female <a href="/wiki/Sadomasochism" title="Sadomasochism">masochism</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Story_of_O" title="Story of O">Story of O</a></i>. Klein had promised this adaptation to various investors. Jodorowsky, who had discovered <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> during the filming of <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, refused to make the film, going so far as to leave the country to escape directing duties. In retaliation, Allen Klein made <i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, to which he held the rights, completely unavailable to the public for more than 30 years. Jodorowsky frequently decried Klein's actions in interviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after the release of <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, Jodorowsky gave a talk at the Teatro <a href="/wiki/Julio_Castillo" title="Julio Castillo">Julio Castillo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Mexico" title="University of Mexico">University of Mexico</a> on the subject of koans (despite the fact that he initially had been booked on the condition that his talk would be about cinematography), at which Ejo Takata appeared. After the talk, Takata gave Jodorowsky his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyosaku" title="Kyosaku">kyosaku</a>, believing that his former student had mastered the art of understanding koans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dune_and_Tusk_.281975.E2.80.931980.29"><i>Dune</i> and <i>Tusk</i> (1975–1980)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dune and Tusk (1975–1980)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 1974, a French consortium led by Jean-Paul Gibon purchased the <a href="/wiki/Film_rights" title="Film rights">film rights</a> to <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>'s epic 1965 science fiction novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_(novel)" title="Dune (novel)">Dune</a></i> and asked Jodorowsky to direct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dune_(Jodorowsky_film)" title="Dune (Jodorowsky film)">a film version</a>. In the role of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Shaddam_IV" title="Shaddam IV">Shaddam IV</a>, Jodorowsky planned to cast the Surrealist artist <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a>, who agreed when Jodorowsky offered to pay him a fee of $100,000 per minute of screen time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dune_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dune-27">[27]</a></sup> He also planned to cast <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> as Baron <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Harkonnen" title="Vladimir Harkonnen">Vladimir Harkonnen</a>; Welles only agreed when Jodorowsky offered to get his favourite gourmet chef to prepare his meals for him throughout the filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005227.E2.80.93230_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005227.E2.80.93230-28">[28]</a></sup> The book's protagonist, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Atreides" title="Paul Atreides">Paul Atreides</a>, was to be played by Jodorowsky's son, <a href="/wiki/Brontis_Jodorowsky" title="Brontis Jodorowsky">Brontis Jodorowsky</a>. The music would be composed by <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magma_(band)" title="Magma (band)">Magma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dune_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dune-27">[27]</a></sup> Jodorowsky set up a pre-production unit in Paris consisting of <a href="/wiki/Chris_Foss" title="Chris Foss">Chris Foss</a>, a British artist who designed covers for science fiction publications, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Giraud" title="Jean Giraud">Jean Giraud (Moebius)</a>, a French illustrator who created and also wrote and drew for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_Hurlant" title="Metal Hurlant">Metal Hurlant</a></i> magazine, and <a href="/wiki/H._R._Giger" title="H. R. Giger">H. R. Giger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dune_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dune-27">[27]</a></sup> Frank Herbert travelled to Europe in 1976 to find that $2 million of the $9.5 million budget had already been spent in pre-production, and that Jodorowsky's script would result in a 14-hour movie ("It was the size of a phonebook", Herbert later recalled).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Jodorowsky took creative liberties with the source material, but Herbert said that he and Jodorowsky had an amicable relationship. The production for the film collapsed when no film studio could be found willing to fund the movie to Jodorowsky's terms. The aborted production was chronicled in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Jodorowsky%27s_Dune" title="Jodorowsky's Dune">Jodorowsky's Dune</a></i>. Subsequently, the rights for filming were sold to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dino_de_Laurentiis" title="Dino de Laurentiis">Dino de Laurentiis</a>, who employed the American filmmaker <a href="/wiki/David_Lynch" title="David Lynch">David Lynch</a> to direct, creating the film <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_(film)" title="Dune (film)">Dune</a></i> in 1984.</p>
<p>After the collapse of the <i>Dune</i> project, Jodorowsky completely changed course and, in 1980, premiered his children's fable <i><a href="/wiki/Tusk_(1980_film)" title="Tusk (1980 film)">Tusk</a></i>, shot in India. Taken from <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Campbell" title="Reginald Campbell">Reginald Campbell</a>'s novel <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Poo_Lorn_of_the_Elephants&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Poo Lorn of the Elephants (page does not exist)">Poo Lorn of the Elephants</a></i>, the film explores the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soul-mate" title="Soul-mate">soul-mate</a> relationship between a young British woman living in India and a highly prized elephant. The film exhibited little of the director's outlandish visual style and was never given wide release.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Santa_Sangre_and_The_Rainbow_Thief_.281981.E2.80.931990.29"><i>Santa Sangre</i> and <i>The Rainbow Thief</i> (1981–1990)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Santa Sangre and The Rainbow Thief (1981–1990)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982 Jodorowsky divorced his wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005235_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005235-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, Jodorowsky completed the Mexican-Italian production <i><a href="/wiki/Santa_Sangre" title="Santa Sangre">Santa Sangre</a></i> (<i>Holy Blood</i>). The film received limited theatrical distribution, putting Jodorowsky back on the cultural map despite its mixed critical reviews. <i>Santa Sangre</i> was a surrealistic <a href="/wiki/Slasher_film" title="Slasher film">slasher film</a> with a plot like a mix of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Psycho_(1960_film)" title="Psycho (1960 film)">Psycho</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wiene" title="Robert Wiene">Robert Wiene</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Hands_of_Orlac_(1924_film)" title="The Hands of Orlac (1924 film)">The Hands of Orlac</a>". It featured a protagonist who, as a child, saw his mother lose both her arms, and as an adult let his own arms act as hers, and so was forced to commit murders at her whim. Several of Jodorowsky's sons were recruited as actors.</p>
<p>He followed in 1990 with a very different film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow_Thief" title="The Rainbow Thief">The Rainbow Thief</a></i>. Though it gave Jodorowsky a chance to work with the "movie stars" <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O'Toole">Peter O'Toole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omar_Sharif" title="Omar Sharif">Omar Sharif</a>, the executive producer, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Salkind" title="Alexander Salkind">Alexander Salkind</a>, effectively curtailed most of Jodorowsky's artistic inclinations, threatening to fire him on the spot if anything in the script was changed (Salkind's wife, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Berta_Dom%C3%ADnguez_D.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Berta Domínguez D. (page does not exist)">Berta Domínguez D.</a>, wrote the screenplay).</p>
<p>That same year (1990), Jodorowsky and his family returned to live in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005216_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005216-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, Alejandro's son, Teo, died in an accident while his father was busy preparing for a trip to Mexico City to promote his new book. Upon arriving in Mexico City, he gave a lecture at the <a href="/wiki/Julio_Castillo" title="Julio Castillo">Julio Castillo</a> Theatre where once again he met Ejo Takata, who at this time had moved into a poor suburb of the city where he had continued to teach meditation and Zen. Takata would die two years later, and Jodorowsky would never get to see his old friend again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005216.E2.80.93218_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005216.E2.80.93218-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempts_to_return_to_filmmaking_.281990.E2.80.932011.29">Attempts to return to filmmaking (1990–2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Attempts to return to filmmaking (1990–2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Jodorowsky won the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Smith_Lifetime_Achievement_Award&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jack Smith Lifetime Achievement Award (page does not exist)">Jack Smith Lifetime Achievement Award</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Underground_Film_Festival" title="Chicago Underground Film Festival">Chicago Underground Film Festival</a> (CUFF). Jodorowsky attended the festival and his films were shown, including <i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, which at the time had grey legal status. According to festival director <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bryan_Wendorf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bryan Wendorf (page does not exist)">Bryan Wendorf</a>, it was an open question of whether CUFF would be allowed to show both films, or whether the police would show up and shut the festival down.</p>
<p>Until 2007, <i>Fando y Lis</i> and <i>Santa Sangre</i> were the only Jodorowsky works available on DVD. Neither <i>El Topo</i> nor <i>The Holy Mountain</i> were available on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Videocassette" title="Videocassette">videocassette</a> or DVD in the United States or the United Kingdom, due to ownership disputes with distributor <a href="/wiki/Allen_Klein" title="Allen Klein">Allen Klein</a>. After settlement of the dispute in 2004, however, plans to re-release Jodorowsky's films were announced by ABKCO Films. On 19 January 2007, the website,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> announced that on 1 May 2007, <a href="/wiki/Anchor_Bay_Entertainment" title="Anchor Bay Entertainment">Anchor Bay</a> released a box set including <i>El Topo</i>, <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, and <i>Fando y Lis</i>. A limited edition of the set includes both the <i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Holy Mountain</i> soundtracks. And, in early February 2007, Tartan Video announced its 14 May 2007, release date for the UK <i>PAL DVD</i> editions of <i>El Topo</i>, <i>The Holy Mountain</i>, and the six-disc box set which, alongside the aforementioned feature films, includes the two soundtrack CDs, as well as separate DVD editions of Jodorowsky's 1968 debut feature <i>Fando y Lis</i> (with his 1957 short <i>La cravate</i> a.k.a. <i>Les têtes interverties</i>, included as an extra) and the 1994 feature-length documentary <i>La constellation Jodorowsky</i>. Notably, <i>Fando y Lis</i> and <i>La cravate</i> were digitally restored extensively and remastered in London during late 2006, thus providing the perfect complement to the quality restoration work undertaken on <i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Holy Mountain</i> in the States by Abkco, and ensuring that the presentation of <i>Fando y Lis</i> is a significant improvement over the 2001 Fantoma DVD edition. Prior to the availability of these legitimate releases, only inferior quality, optically censored, <a href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording" title="Bootleg recording">bootleg</a> copies of both <i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Holy Mountain</i> have been circulated on the Internet and on DVD.</p>

<p>In the 1990s and early 2000s, Jodorowsky attempted to make <a href="/wiki/El_Topo#Sequel" title="El Topo">a sequel to <i>El Topo</i></a>, called at different times <i>The Sons of El Topo</i> and <i>Abel Cain</i>, but could not find investors for the project.</p>
<p>In an interview with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premiere_Magazine" title="Premiere Magazine">Premiere Magazine</a></i>, Jodorowsky said he intended his next project to be a gangster film called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=King_Shot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="King Shot (page does not exist)">King Shot</a></i>. In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> newspaper in November 2009, however, Jodorowsky revealed that he was unable to find the funds to make <i>King Shot</i>, and instead would be entering preparations on <i><a href="/wiki/Abel_Cain" title="Abel Cain">Sons of El Topo</a></i>, for which he claimed to have signed a contract with "some Russian producers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, the Museum of Arts and Design in New York City staged the first American cinema retrospective of Alejandro Jodorowsky entitled <i>Blood into Gold: The Cinematic Alchemy of Alejandro Jodorowsky</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Jodorowsky would attend the retrospective and hold a master class on art as a way of transformation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> This retrospective would inspire the museum MOMA PS1 to present the exhibition <i>Alejandro Jodorowsky: The Holy Mountain</i> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dance_of_Reality_and_Poes.C3.ADa_Sin_Fin_.282011.E2.80.93present.29"><i>The Dance of Reality</i> and <i>Poesía Sin Fin</i> (2011–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Dance of Reality and Poesía Sin Fin (2011–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2011, Alejandro arrived in a town in Chile where he grew up, also the setting of his autobiography <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dance_of_Reality" title="The Dance of Reality">The Dance of Reality</a></i>, to promote an autobiographical film based upon his book.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> honored Jodorowsky on Halloween night, 31 October 2011, by showing <i>The Holy Mountain</i>. He attended, and spoke about his work and life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TarotNYT_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-TarotNYT-39">[39]</a></sup> The next evening he presented <i>El Topo</i> at the Walter Reade Theatre at Lincoln Center.</p>
<p>Alejandro has stated that after finishing <i>The Dance of Reality</i> he was preparing to shoot his long-gestating <i><a href="/wiki/El_Topo" title="El Topo">El Topo</a></i> sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Abel_Cain" title="Abel Cain">Abel Cain</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> By January 2013, Alejandro finished filming on <i>The Dance of Reality</i> and entered into post-production. Alejandro's son and co-star in the film, Brontis, claimed the film was to be finished by March 2013, and that the film was "very different than the other films he made".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miaminewtimes1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-miaminewtimes1-42">[42]</a></sup> On 23 April, it was announced that the film would have its world premiere at the Filmfestival in Cannes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elsa_Keslassy_.40elsakeslassy_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elsa_Keslassy_.40elsakeslassy-43">[43]</a></sup> coinciding with <i>The Dance of Reality</i> premiered alongside the documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Jodorowsky%27s_Dune" title="Jodorowsky's Dune">Jodorowsky's Dune</a></i>, which premiered in May 2013 at the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>, creating a "Jodorowsky double bill".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Jodorowsky began a new film entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poes%C3%ADa_Sin_Fin" title="Poesía Sin Fin">Poesía Sin Fin</a></i>, the sequel to his last "auto-biopic", <i>The Dance of Reality</i>. His Paris-based production company, Satori Films, launched two successful crowdfunding campaigns to finance the film. The Indiegogo campaign has been left open indefinitely, receiving donations from fans and movie-goers in support of the independent production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The Film was shot between June and August 2015, in the streets of Matucana in Santiago, Chile, where Jodorowsky lived for a period in his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kickstarter.com_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-kickstarter.com-47">[47]</a></sup> The film portrays his young adulthood in Santiago, years during which he became a core member of the Chilean poetic avant-garde alongside artists such as like Hugo Marín, Gustavo Becerra, Enrique Lihn, Stella Díaz Varín, Nicanor Parra and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Jodorowsky's son, <a href="/wiki/Adan_Jodorowsky" title="Adan Jodorowsky">Adan Jodorowsky</a>, plays him as an adult. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jeremias_Herskovitz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jeremias Herskovitz (page does not exist)">Jeremias Herskovitz</a>, from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dance_of_Reality" title="The Dance of Reality">The Dance of Reality</a></i>, portrays Jodorowsky as a teenager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kickstarter.com_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-kickstarter.com-47">[47]</a></sup> The film premiered in the <a href="/wiki/Directors%27_Fortnight" title="Directors' Fortnight">Directors' Fortnight</a> section of the <a href="/wiki/2016_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2016 Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> on 14 May 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a>'s review was overwhelmingly positive, calling it "...the most accessible movie he has ever made, and it may also be the best."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>During an interview at the Cannes Film Festival in 2016, Jodorowsky announced his plans to finally make <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Son_of_El_Topo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Son of El Topo (page does not exist)">The Son of El Topo</a></i> as soon as financial backing is obtained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jodorowsky is a weekly contributor of "good news" to the nightly "author newsreport" of his friend, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_S%C3%A1nchez-Drag%C3%B3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fernando Sánchez-Dragó (page does not exist)">Fernando Sánchez-Dragó</a> in <a href="/wiki/Telemadrid" title="Telemadrid">Telemadrid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drag.C3.B3_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drag.C3.B3-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also released a 12" vinyl with the Original Soundtrack of Zarathustra (Discos Tizoc, Mexico, 1970)</p>
<p>He has cited the filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a> as his primary cinematic influence;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005232_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005232-54">[54]</a></sup> other artistic influences included <a href="/wiki/George_Gurdjieff" title="George Gurdjieff">George Gurdjieff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonin_Artaud" title="Antonin Artaud">Antonin Artaud</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> He has been described as an influence on such figures as <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Manson" title="Marilyn Manson">Marilyn Manson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005236.E2.80.93237_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJodorowsky2005236.E2.80.93237-57">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Lynch" title="David Lynch">David Lynch</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Winding_Refn" title="Nicolas Winding Refn">Nicolas Winding Refn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Kounen" title="Jan Kounen">Jan Kounen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Hopper" title="Dennis Hopper">Dennis Hopper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comics">Comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4272" data-file-width="2848" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg/200px-Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg/300px-Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg/400px-Alejandro_Jodorowsky_20080706_Japan_Expo_04.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Jodorowsky started his comic career in Mexico with the creation of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anibal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anibal 5 (page does not exist)">Anibal 5</a></i> series in mid-1966 with illustrations by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_Moro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manuel Moro (page does not exist)">Manuel Moro</a>. He also drew his own comic strip in the weekly series <i>Fabulas pánicas</i> that appeared in the Mexican newspaper, <i><a href="/wiki/El_Heraldo_de_M%C3%A9xico" title="El Heraldo de México">El Heraldo de México</a></i>. He also wrote original stories for at least two or three other comic books in Mexico during those days: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Los_insoportables_Borbolla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Los insoportables Borbolla (page does not exist)">Los insoportables Borbolla</a></i> was one of them. After his fourth film, <i><a href="/wiki/Tusk_(1980_film)" title="Tusk (1980 film)">Tusk</a></i>, he started <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incal" title="The Incal">The Incal</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Jean_Giraud" title="Jean Giraud">Jean Giraud</a> (Mœbius). This <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> has its roots deep in the tarot and its symbols, e.g., the protagonist of <i>The Incal</i>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Difool&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Difool (page does not exist)">John Difool</a>, is linked to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fool_(Zero)" title="The Fool (Zero)">Fool card</a>. <i>The Incal</i> (which would branch off into a prequel and sequel) forms the first in a sequence of several science fiction comic book series, all set in the same <a href="/wiki/Space_opera" title="Space opera">space opera</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jodoverse" title="Jodoverse">Jodoverse</a> (or "Metabarons Universe") published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanoids_Publishing" title="Humanoids Publishing">Humanoids Publishing</a>.</p>
<p>Comic books set in this milieu are Incal (trilogy: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Before_the_Incal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Before the Incal (page does not exist)">Before the Incal</a>/ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incal" title="Incal">Incal</a>/ <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Final_Incal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Final Incal (page does not exist)">Final Incal</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Metabarons" title="Metabarons">Metabarons</a> (trilogy: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Castaka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Castaka (page does not exist)">Castaka</a>/ <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Caste_of_the_Metabarons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Caste of the Metabarons (page does not exist)">The Caste of the Metabarons</a>/ <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Weapons_of_the_Metabaron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Weapons of the Metabaron (page does not exist)">Weapons of the Metabaron</a></i>), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technopriests" title="Technopriests">The Technopriests</a></i> and also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabletop_roleplaying_game" title="Tabletop roleplaying game">RPG adaptation</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabarons_Roleplaying_Game" title="Metabarons Roleplaying Game">The Metabarons Roleplaying Game</a></i>. Many ideas and concepts derived from Jodorowsky's planned adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Dune_(novel)" title="Dune (novel)">Dune</a></i> (which he would have only loosely based upon <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>'s original novel) are featured in this universe.</p>
<p>Mœbius and Jodorowsky sued <a href="/wiki/Luc_Besson" title="Luc Besson">Luc Besson</a>, director of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifth_Element" title="The Fifth Element">The Fifth Element</a></i>, claiming that the 1997 film borrowed graphic and story elements from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incal" title="The Incal">The Incal</a></i>, but they lost their case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The suit was plagued by ambiguity since Mœbius had willingly participated in the creation of the film, having been hired by Besson as a contributing artist, but had done so without gaining the approval of <i>Incal</i> co-creator Jodorowsky, whose services Besson did not call upon. For more than a decade, Jodorowsky pressured his publisher <a href="/wiki/Les_Humano%C3%AFdes_Associ%C3%A9s" title="Les Humanoïdes Associés">Les Humanoïdes Associés</a> to sue Luc Besson for <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarism</a>, but the publisher refused, fearing the inevitability of the final outcome. In a 2002 interview with the Danish comic book magazine <i>Strip!</i>, Jodorowsky stated that he considered it an honour that somebody stole his ideas.</p>
<p>Other action comics by Jodorowsky outside the genre of science fiction include the historically-based <i>Bouncer</i> illustrated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francois_Boucq" title="Francois Boucq">Francois Boucq</a>, <i>Juan Solo</i> (<i>Son of the Gun</i>), and <i>Le Lama blanc</i> (<i>The White Lama</i>), the later were illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bess" title="Georges Bess">Georges Bess</a>.</p>
<p><i>Le Cœur couronné</i> (<i>The Crowned Heart</i>, translated into English as <i>The Madwoman of the Sacred Heart</i>), a racy satire on religion set in contemporary times, won Jodorowsky and his collaborator, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Giraud" title="Jean Giraud">Jean Giraud</a>, the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Haxtur_Award" title="Haxtur Award">Haxtur Award</a> for Best Long Strip. He is currently working on a new graphic novel for the U.S. market.</p>
<p>Jodorowsky's comic book work also appears in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taboo_(comic)" title="Taboo (comic)">Taboo</a></i> volume 4 (ed. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_R._Bissette" title="Stephen R. Bissette">Stephen R. Bissette</a>), which features an interview with the director, designs for his version of Frank Herbert's <i>Dune</i>, comic storyboards for <i>El Topo</i>, and a collaboration with Moebius with the illustrated <i>Eyes of the Cat</i>.</p>
<p>He collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Milo_Manara" title="Milo Manara">Milo Manara</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borgia" title="Borgia">Borgia</a></i> (2006), a graphic novel about the history of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Borgia" title="House of Borgia">House of Borgia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychomagic">Psychomagic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Psychomagic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jodorowsky spent almost a decade reconstructing the original form of the <a href="/wiki/Tarot_of_Marseilles" title="Tarot of Marseilles">Tarot de Marseille</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TarotNYT_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-TarotNYT-39">[39]</a></sup> From this work he moved into more therapeutic work in three areas: <a href="/wiki/Psychomagic_(psychotherapy)" title="Psychomagic (psychotherapy)">psychomagic</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Psychogenealogy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Psychogenealogy (page does not exist)">psychogenealogy</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Initiatic_massage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Initiatic massage (page does not exist)">initiatic massage</a>. Psychomagic aims to heal psychological wounds suffered in life. This therapy is based on the belief that the performance of certain acts can directly act upon the unconscious mind, releasing it from a series of traumas, some of which practitioners of the therapy believe are passed down from generation to generation. Psychogenealogy includes the studying of the patient's personality and family tree in order to best address their specific sources. It is similar, in its phenomenological approach to genealogy, to the <a href="/wiki/Family_Constellations" title="Family Constellations">Constellations</a> pioneered by Bert Hellinger.</p>
<p>Jodorowsky has several books on his therapeutic methods, including <i>Psicomagia: La trampa sagrada</i> (<i>Psychomagic: The Sacred Trap</i>) and his autobiography, <i>La danza de la realidad</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Dance_of_Reality" title="The Dance of Reality">The Dance of Reality</a></i>), which he was filming as a feature-length film in March 2012. To date he has published more than 23 novels and philosophical treatises, along with dozens of articles and interviews. His books are widely read in Spanish and French, but are for the most part unknown to English-speaking audiences.</p>
<p>Throughout his career, Jodorowsky has gained a reputation as a philosopher and scholar who presents the teachings of religion, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, and spiritual masters, by molding them into imaginative endeavors. All of his enterprises integrate an artistic approach. Currently, Jodorowsky dedicates much of his time to lecturing about his work.</p>
<p>For a quarter of a century, Jodorowsky held classes and lectures for free, in cafés and universities all over the city of Paris. Typically, such courses or talks would begin on Wednesday evenings as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarot_divination" title="Tarot divination">tarot divination</a> lessons, and would culminate in an hour long conference, also free, where at times hundreds of attendees would be treated to live demonstrations of a psychological "arbre généalogique" ("tree of genealogy") involving volunteers from the audience. In these conferences, Jodorowsky would pave the way to building a strong base of students of his philosophy, which deals with understanding the <a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">unconscious</a> as the "over-self", composed of many generations of family relatives, living or deceased, acting on the <a href="/wiki/Psyche_(psychology)" title="Psyche (psychology)">psyche</a>, well into adult lives, and causing <a href="/wiki/Compulsive_behavior" title="Compulsive behavior">compulsions</a>. Of all his work, Jodorowsky considers these activities to be the most important of his life. Though such activities only take place in the insular world of <a href="/wiki/Parisian_caf%C3%A9" title="Parisian café">Parisian cafés</a>, he has devoted thousands of hours of his life to teaching and helping people "become more conscious," as he puts it.</p>
<p>Since 2011 these talks have dwindled to once a month and take place at the "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Librairie_Les_Cent_Ciels&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Librairie Les Cent Ciels (page does not exist)">Librairie Les Cent Ciels</a>" in Paris.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alejandro_Jodorowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jodorowsky's first wife was the actress Valérie Trumblay. He is currently married to the artist and costume designer Pascale Montandon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has four sons: <a href="/wiki/Brontis_Jodorowsky" title="Brontis Jodorowsky">Brontis Jodorowsky</a>, an actor (he worked with his father in <i>El Topo</i> and <i>The Dance of Reality</i>); Teo (died of overdose in 1995, at the age of 24; he played a role in <i>Santa Sangre</i>);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Cristobal, a psychoshaman and an actor (he was in <i>Santa Sangre</i> and the main character in the psychomagic documentary <i>Quantum Men</i>); and <a href="/wiki/Adan_Jodorowsky" title="Adan Jodorowsky">Adan Jodorowsky</a>, the youngest, a musician known by his stage name of Adanowsky. He also has a daughter, Eugenia.</p>
<p>On his religious views, Jodorowsky called himself an "atheist mystic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>He does not drink or smoke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and has stated that he does not eat red meat and poultry because he "does not like corpses", basing his diet on vegetables, fruits, cereals and occasionally marine products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
