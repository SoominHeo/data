<p><b>Alexa Internet, Inc.</b> is a California-based company that provides commercial <a href="/wiki/Web_traffic" title="Web traffic">web traffic</a> data and analytics. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholly_owned_subsidiary" title="Wholly owned subsidiary">wholly owned subsidiary</a> of <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>.</p>
<p>Founded as an independent company in 1996, Alexa was acquired by Amazon in 1999. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexa_Toolbar" title="Alexa Toolbar">toolbar</a> collects data on browsing behavior and transmits them to the Alexa website, where they are stored and analyzed. This is the basis for the company's web traffic reporting. According to its website, Alexa provides traffic data, global rankings, and other information on 30 million websites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2015, its website has been visited by over 6.5 million people monthly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexa_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-alexa-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_and_history">Operations and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operations and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1996.E2.80.931999">1996–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1996–1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexa Internet was founded in April 1996 by American web entrepreneurs <a href="/wiki/Brewster_Kahle" title="Brewster Kahle">Brewster Kahle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Gilliat" title="Bruce Gilliat">Bruce Gilliat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The company's name was chosen in homage to the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Alexandria" title="Library of Alexandria">Library of Alexandria</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic Egypt</a>, drawing a parallel between the largest repository of knowledge in the ancient world and the potential of the Internet to become a similar store of knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gift_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-gift-6">[6]</a></sup> Alexa initially offered a toolbar that gave Internet users suggestions on where to go next, based on the traffic patterns of its user community. The company also offered context for each site visited: to whom it was registered, how many pages it had, how many other sites pointed to it, and how frequently it was updated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Alexa's operations grew to include archiving of web pages as they are <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">crawled</a>. This database served as the basis for the creation of the Internet Archive accessible through the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1998, the company donated a copy of the archive, two <a href="/wiki/Terabyte" title="Terabyte">terabytes</a> in size, to the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gift_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-gift-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexa continues to supply the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Archive" title="Internet Archive">Internet Archive</a> with Web crawls. In 1999, as the company moved away from its original vision of providing an "intelligent" search engine, Alexa was acquired by Amazon.com for approximately US$250 million in Amazon <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000.E2.80.932017">2000–2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000–2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexa began a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> in early 2002, and with the web directory <a href="/wiki/DMOZ" title="DMOZ">DMOZ</a> in January 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup> In May 2006, Amazon replaced Google with <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> (at the time known as <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)#Windows_Live_Search" title="Bing (search engine)">Windows Live Search</a>) as a provider of search results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, Amazon released Alexa Image Search. Built in-house, it was the first major application built on the company's Web platform. In December 2005, Alexa opened its extensive search index and Web-crawling facilities to third-party programs through a comprehensive set of <a href="/wiki/Web_service" title="Web service">Web services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">APIs</a>. These could be used, for instance, to construct <a href="/wiki/Vertical_search" title="Vertical search">vertical search</a> engines that could run on Alexa's own servers or elsewhere. In May 2007, Alexa changed their API to limit comparisons to three websites, reduce the size of embedded graphs in <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a>, and add mandatory embedded BritePic advertisements.</p>
<p>In April 2007, the company filed a lawsuit, <i>Alexa v. Hornbaker,</i> to stop <a href="/wiki/Trademark_infringement" title="Trademark infringement">trademark infringement</a> by the Statsaholic service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the lawsuit, Alexa alleged that Ron Hornbaker was stealing traffic graphs for profit, and that the primary purpose of his site was to display graphs that were generated by Alexa's servers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Hornbaker removed the term Alexa from his service name on March 19, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On November 27, 2008, Amazon announced that Alexa Web Search was no longer accepting new customers, and that the service would be deprecated or discontinued for existing customers on January 26, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Thereafter, Alexa became a purely analytics-focused company.</p>
<p>On March 31, 2009, Alexa launched a major website redesign. The redesigned site provided new web traffic metrics—including average page views per individual user, bounce rate, and user time on site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the following weeks, Alexa added more features, including visitor demographics, clickstream and search traffic statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Alexa introduced these new features to compete with other web analytics services.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracking">Tracking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tracking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toolbar">Toolbar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Toolbar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexa ranks sites based primarily on tracking a sample set of Internet traffic—users of its toolbar for the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a> web browsers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-davis-18">[18]</a></sup> The Alexa Toolbar includes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popup_blocker" title="Popup blocker">popup blocker</a>, a search box, links to <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> and the Alexa homepage, and the Alexa ranking of the site that the user is visiting. It also allows the user to rate the site and view links to external, relevant sites. In early 2005, Alexa stated that there had been 10 million downloads of the toolbar, though the company did not provide statistics about active usage. Originally, web pages were only ranked amongst users who had the Alexa Toolbar installed, and could be biased if a specific audience subgroup was reluctant to take part in the rankings. This caused some controversies over how representative Alexa's user base was of typical Internet behavior,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> especially for less-visited sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-davis-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Arrington" title="Michael Arrington">Michael Arrington</a> provided examples of Alexa rankings known to contradict data from the <a href="/wiki/ComScore" title="ComScore">comScore</a> web analytics service, including ranking <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> ahead of Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 2007, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third-party_developer" title="Third-party developer">third-party</a>-supplied plugin for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firefox_browser" title="Firefox browser">Firefox browser</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searchstatus_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-searchstatus-21">[21]</a></sup> served as the only option for Firefox users after Amazon abandoned its A9 toolbar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a9_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-a9-22">[22]</a></sup> On July 16, 2007, Alexa released an official toolbar for Firefox called Sparky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparky_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sparky-23">[23]</a></sup> On 16 April 2008, many users reported drastic shifts in their Alexa rankings. Alexa confirmed this later in the day with an announcement that they had released an updated ranking system, claiming that they would now take into account more sources of data "beyond Alexa Toolbar users".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certified_statistics">Certified statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Certified statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using the Alexa Pro service, website owners can sign up for "certified statistics", which allows Alexa more access to a site's traffic data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Site owners input <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> code on each page of their website that, if permitted by the user's security and privacy settings, runs and sends traffic data to Alexa, allowing Alexa to display—or not display, depending on the owner's preference—more accurate statistics such as total pageviews and unique pageviews.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_and_malware_assessments">Privacy and malware assessments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexa_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Privacy and malware assessments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antivirus" title="Antivirus">antivirus</a> companies have assessed Alexa's toolbar. The toolbar for <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer_7" title="Internet Explorer 7">Internet Explorer 7</a> was at one point flagged as malware by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Defender" title="Microsoft Defender">Microsoft Defender</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Symantec_Operations_Readiness_Tools" title="Symantec Operations Readiness Tools">Symantec</a> classifies the toolbar as "trackware",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McAfee" title="McAfee">McAfee</a> classifies it as <a href="/wiki/Adware" title="Adware">adware</a>, deeming it a "potentially unwanted program."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McAfee_Site_Advisor" title="McAfee Site Advisor">McAfee Site Advisor</a> rates the Alexa site as "green", finding "no significant problems" but warning of a "small fraction of downloads ... that some people consider adware or other potentially unwanted programs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Though it is possible to delete a paid subscription within an Alexa account, it is not possible to delete an account that is created at Alexa through any web interface, though any user may contact the company via its support webpage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
