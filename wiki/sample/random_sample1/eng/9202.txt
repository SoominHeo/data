<p><b>Alexander Grothendieck</b> (<small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈɡroːtn̩diːk]</a></span>; <small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ɡʁɔtɛndik]</a></span>; 28 March 1928 – 13 November 2014) was a German-born French <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> who became the leading figure in the creation of modern <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jackson2-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytob_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytob-7">[7]</a></sup> His research extended the scope of the field and added elements of <a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">commutative algebra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheaf_theory" title="Sheaf theory">sheaf theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a> to its foundations, while his so-called <a href="/wiki/Grothendieck%27s_relative_point_of_view" title="Grothendieck's relative point of view">"relative" perspective</a> led to revolutionary advances in many areas of <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-jackson2-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MumfordTate2015_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MumfordTate2015-8">[8]</a></sup> He is considered by many to be the greatest mathematician of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Born in Germany, Grothendieck was raised and lived primarily in France. For much of his working life, however, he was, in effect, <a href="/wiki/Statelessness" title="Statelessness">stateless</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004p._10.2C_footnote_12_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004p._10.2C_footnote_12-1">[1]</a></sup> As he consistently spelled his first name "Alexander" rather than "Alexandre"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004p._9_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004p._9-10">[10]</a></sup> and his surname, taken from his mother, was the Dutch-like <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> "Grothendieck," he was sometimes mistakenly believed to be of Dutch origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2001p._391.2C_footnote_3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2001p._391.2C_footnote_3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grothendieck began his productive and public career as a mathematician in 1949. In 1958, he was appointed a research professor at the <a href="/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes_Scientifiques" title="Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques">Institut des hautes études scientifiques</a> (IHÉS) and remained there until 1970, when, driven by personal and political convictions, he left following a dispute over military funding. Although he later became a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montpellier" title="University of Montpellier">University of Montpellier</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup> and produced some private mathematical work, he otherwise withdrew from the mathematical community and devoted himself to political causes. Soon after his formal retirement in 1988, he moved to the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, where he lived in isolation until his death in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERuelle200740_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERuelle200740-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_childhood">Family and childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family and childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck was born in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> parents. His father, <a href="/wiki/Sascha_Schapiro" title="Sascha Schapiro">Alexander "Sascha" Schapiro</a> (also known as Alexander Tanaroff), had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hassidic" title="Hassidic">Hassidic</a> roots and had been imprisoned in Russia before moving to Germany in 1922, while his mother, Johanna "Hanka" Grothendieck, came from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> family in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> and worked as a journalist. Both had broken away from their early backgrounds in their teens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siam.org_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-siam.org-13">[13]</a></sup> At the time of his birth, Grothendieck's mother was married to the journalist Johannes Raddatz and his birthname was initially recorded as "Alexander Raddatz." The marriage was dissolved in 1929 and Schapiro/Tanaroff acknowledged his paternity, but never married Hanka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siam.org_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-siam.org-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grothendieck lived with his parents in Berlin until the end of 1933, when his father moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> to evade <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>, followed soon thereafter by his mother. They left Grothendieck in the care of Wilhelm Heydorn, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a> and teacher<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. During this time, his parents took part in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, according to <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winfried_Scharlau_(Mathematiker)" title="de:Winfried Scharlau (Mathematiker)">Winfried Scharlau</a>, as non-combatant auxiliaries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> though others state that Sascha fought in the anarchist militia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 1939, Grothendieck was put on a train in Hamburg for France. Shortly afterwards his father was interned in <a href="/wiki/Camp_Vernet" title="Camp Vernet">Le Vernet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aczel_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aczel-17">[17]</a></sup> He and his mother were then interned in various camps from 1940 to 1942 as "undesirable dangerous foreigners".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The first was the <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camp_de_Rieucros" title="fr:Camp de Rieucros">Camp de Rieucros</a>, where his mother contracted the tuberculosis which eventually caused her death and where Alexander managed to attend the local school, at <a href="/wiki/Mende,_Loz%C3%A8re" title="Mende, Lozère">Mende</a>. Once Alexander managed to escape from the camp, intending to assassinate Hitler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aczel_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aczel-17">[17]</a></sup> Later, his mother Hanka was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Gurs_internment_camp" title="Gurs internment camp">Gurs internment camp</a> for the remainder of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aczel_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Aczel-17">[17]</a></sup> Alexander was permitted to live, separated from his mother,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viale_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Viale-19">[19]</a></sup> in the village of <a href="/wiki/Le_Chambon-sur-Lignon" title="Le Chambon-sur-Lignon">Le Chambon-sur-Lignon</a>, sheltered and hidden in local boarding houses or <a href="/wiki/Pension_(lodging)" title="Pension (lodging)">pensions</a>, though he occasionally had to seek refuge in the woods during Nazis raids, surviving at times without food or water for several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aczel_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Aczel-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viale_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Viale-19">[19]</a></sup> His father was arrested under the <a href="/wiki/Vichy_anti-Jewish_legislation" title="Vichy anti-Jewish legislation">Vichy anti-Jewish legislation</a>, and sent to the <a href="/wiki/Drancy_internment_camp" title="Drancy internment camp">Drancy</a>, and then handed over by the <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">French Vichy government</a> to the Germans to be sent to be murdered at the <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a> in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytob_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytob-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In Chambon, Grothendieck attended the Collège Cévenol (now known as the <a href="/wiki/Le_Coll%C3%A8ge-Lyc%C3%A9e_C%C3%A9venol_International" title="Le Collège-Lycée Cévenol International">Le Collège-Lycée Cévenol International</a>), a unique secondary school founded in 1938 by local Protestant pacifists and anti-war activists. Many of the refugee children hidden in Chambon attended Cévenol, and it was at this school that Grothendieck apparently first became fascinated with mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liberation-Douroux-Obit-2014-11-13_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liberation-Douroux-Obit-2014-11-13-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_and_contact_with_research_mathematics">Studies and contact with research mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Studies and contact with research mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the war, the young Grothendieck studied mathematics in France, initially at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montpellier" title="University of Montpellier">University of Montpellier</a> where he did not initially perform well, failing such classes as astronomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Working on his own, he rediscovered the <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">Lebesgue measure</a>. After three years of increasingly independent studies there, he went to continue his studies in Paris in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, Grothendieck attended <a href="/wiki/Henri_Cartan" title="Henri Cartan">Henri Cartan</a>'s Seminar at <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Normale_Sup%C3%A9rieure" title="École Normale Supérieure">École Normale Supérieure</a>, but he lacked the necessary background to follow the high-powered seminar. On the advice of Cartan and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil" title="André Weil">André Weil</a>, he moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nancy" title="University of Nancy">University of Nancy</a> where he wrote his dissertation under <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Schwartz" title="Laurent Schwartz">Laurent Schwartz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dieudonn%C3%A9" title="Jean Dieudonné">Jean Dieudonné</a> on <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>, from 1950 to 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Festschrift_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Festschrift-24">[24]</a></sup> At this time he was a leading expert in the theory of <a href="/wiki/Topological_vector_space" title="Topological vector space">topological vector spaces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> By 1957, he set this subject aside in order to work in algebraic geometry and <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Festschrift_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Festschrift-24">[24]</a></sup> The same year he was invited to visit Harvard by <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Zariski" title="Oscar Zariski">Oscar Zariski</a>, but the offer fell through when he refused to sign a pledge promising not to work to overthrow the United States government, a position that, he was warned, might have landed him in prison. The prospect did not worry him, as long as he could have access to books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Comparing Grothendieck during his Nancy years to the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Normale_Sup%C3%A9rieure" title="École Normale Supérieure">École Normale Supérieure</a> trained students at that time: <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Samuel" title="Pierre Samuel">Pierre Samuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Godement" title="Roger Godement">Roger Godement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Thom" title="René Thom">René Thom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Dixmier" title="Jacques Dixmier">Jacques Dixmier</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Cerf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Cerf (page does not exist)">Jean Cerf</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yvonne_Bruhat" title="Yvonne Bruhat">Yvonne Bruhat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre" title="Jean-Pierre Serre">Jean-Pierre Serre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Malgrange" title="Bernard Malgrange">Bernard Malgrange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leila_Schneps" title="Leila Schneps">Leila Schneps</a> says:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He was so completely unknown to this group and to their professors, came from such a deprived and chaotic background, and was, compared to them, so ignorant at the start of his research career, that his fulgurating ascent to sudden stardom is all the more incredible; quite unique in the history of mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IH.C3.89S_years">IHÉS years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: IHÉS years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1958 Grothendieck was installed at the <a href="/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes_Scientifiques" title="Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques">Institut des hautes études scientifiques</a> (IHÉS), a new privately funded research institute that, in effect, had been created for <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dieudonn%C3%A9" title="Jean Dieudonné">Jean Dieudonné</a> and Grothendieck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004-28">[28]</a></sup> Grothendieck attracted attention by an intense and highly productive activity of seminars there (<i>de facto</i> working groups drafting into foundational work some of the ablest French and other mathematicians of the younger generation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup> Grothendieck himself practically ceased publication of papers through the conventional, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Learned_journal" title="Learned journal">learned journal</a> route. He was, however, able to play a dominant role in mathematics for around a decade, gathering a strong school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artist_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-artist-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this time, he had officially as students <a href="/wiki/Michel_Demazure" title="Michel Demazure">Michel Demazure</a> (who worked on SGA3, on <a href="/wiki/Group_scheme" title="Group scheme">group schemes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Luc_Illusie" title="Luc Illusie">Luc Illusie</a> (cotangent complex), <a href="/wiki/Michel_Raynaud" title="Michel Raynaud">Michel Raynaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Verdier" title="Jean-Louis Verdier">Jean-Louis Verdier</a> (cofounder of the <a href="/wiki/Derived_category" title="Derived category">derived category</a> theory) and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Deligne" title="Pierre Deligne">Pierre Deligne</a>. Collaborators on the SGA projects also included <a href="/wiki/Michael_Artin" title="Michael Artin">Michael Artin</a> (<a href="/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology" title="Étale cohomology">étale cohomology</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Katz" title="Nick Katz">Nick Katz</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monodromy_theory" title="Monodromy theory">monodromy theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lefschetz_pencil" title="Lefschetz pencil">Lefschetz pencils</a>). <a href="/wiki/Jean_Giraud_(mathematician)" title="Jean Giraud (mathematician)">Jean Giraud</a> worked out <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torsor" title="Torsor">torsor</a> theory extensions of <a href="/wiki/Nonabelian_cohomology" title="Nonabelian cohomology">nonabelian cohomology</a>. Many others were involved.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Golden_Age.22">"Golden Age"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "Golden Age"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexander Grothendieck's work during the "Golden Age" period at the IHÉS established several unifying themes in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Complex_analysis" title="Complex analysis">complex analysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Festschrift_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Festschrift-24">[24]</a></sup> His first (pre-IHÉS) discovery in algebraic geometry was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grothendieck%E2%80%93Hirzebruch%E2%80%93Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Grothendieck–Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch theorem">Grothendieck–Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch theorem</a>, a generalisation of the <a href="/wiki/Hirzebruch%E2%80%93Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch theorem">Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch theorem</a> proved algebraically; in this context he also introduced <a href="/wiki/K-theory" title="K-theory">K-theory</a>. Then, following the programme he outlined in his talk at the 1958 <a href="/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians" title="International Congress of Mathematicians">International Congress of Mathematicians</a>, he introduced the theory of <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics)" title="Scheme (mathematics)">schemes</a>, developing it in detail in his <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89l%C3%A9ments_de_g%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie_alg%C3%A9brique" title="Éléments de géométrie algébrique">Éléments de géométrie algébrique</a></i> (<i>EGA</i>) and providing the new more flexible and general foundations for algebraic geometry that has been adopted in the field since that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup> He went on to introduce the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology" title="Étale cohomology">étale cohomology</a> theory of schemes, providing the key tools for proving the <a href="/wiki/Weil_conjectures" title="Weil conjectures">Weil conjectures</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_cohomology" title="Crystalline cohomology">crystalline cohomology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algebraic_de_Rham_cohomology" title="Algebraic de Rham cohomology">algebraic de Rham cohomology</a> to complement it. Closely linked to these cohomology theories, he originated <a href="/wiki/Topos" title="Topos">topos</a> theory as a generalisation of topology (relevant also in <a href="/wiki/Categorical_logic" title="Categorical logic">categorical logic</a>). He also provided an algebraic definition of <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_group" title="Fundamental group">fundamental groups</a> of schemes and more generally the main structures of a categorical <a href="/wiki/Galois_theory" title="Galois theory">Galois theory</a>. As a framework for his <a href="/wiki/Coherent_duality" title="Coherent duality">coherent duality</a> theory he also introduced <a href="/wiki/Derived_category" title="Derived category">derived categories</a>, which were further developed by Verdier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The results of work on these and other topics were published in the <i>EGA</i> and in less polished form in the notes of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A9minaire_de_g%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie_alg%C3%A9brique" title="Séminaire de géométrie algébrique">Séminaire de géométrie algébrique</a></i> (<i>SGA</i>) that he directed at the IHÉS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_activism">Political activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Political activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck's political views were <a href="/wiki/Political_radicalism" title="Political radicalism">radical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacifist" title="Pacifist">pacifist</a>, and he strongly opposed both United States <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">intervention in Vietnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Empire" title="Soviet Empire">Soviet military expansionism</a>. He gave lectures on <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a> in the forests surrounding <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> while the city was being bombed, to protest against the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He retired from scientific life around 1970, after having discovered that the IHÉS was partly funded by the military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He returned to academia a few years later as a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montpellier" title="University of Montpellier">University of Montpellier</a>.</p>
<p>While the issue of military funding was perhaps the most obvious explanation for Grothendieck's departure from the IHÉS, those who knew him say that the causes of the rupture ran deeper. <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Cartier_(mathematician)" title="Pierre Cartier (mathematician)">Pierre Cartier</a>, a <i>visiteur de longue durée</i> ("long-term guest") at the IHÉS, wrote a piece about Grothendieck for a special volume published on the occasion of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ams.org/notices/199903/ihes-changes.pdf" rel="nofollow">IHÉS's fortieth anniversary</a>. The <i>Grothendieck Festschrift</i>, published in 1990, was a three-volume collection of research papers to mark his sixtieth birthday in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In it, Cartier notes that as the son of an antimilitary anarchist and one who grew up among the disenfranchised, Grothendieck always had a deep compassion for the poor and the downtrodden. As Cartier puts it, Grothendieck came to find <a href="/wiki/Bures-sur-Yvette" title="Bures-sur-Yvette">Bures-sur-Yvette</a> "<i>une cage dorée</i>" ("a golden cage"). While Grothendieck was at the IHÉS, opposition to the Vietnam War was heating up, and Cartier suggests that this also reinforced Grothendieck's distaste at having become a mandarin of the scientific world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004-28">[28]</a></sup> In addition, after several years at the IHÉS, Grothendieck seemed to cast about for new intellectual interests. By the late 1960s, he had started to become interested in scientific areas outside mathematics. <a href="/wiki/David_Ruelle" title="David Ruelle">David Ruelle</a>, a physicist who joined the IHÉS faculty in 1964, said that Grothendieck came to talk to him a few times about <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[n 1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">Biology</a> interested Grothendieck much more than physics, and he organized some seminars on biological topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1970, Grothendieck, with two other mathematicians, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chevalley" title="Claude Chevalley">Claude Chevalley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Samuel" title="Pierre Samuel">Pierre Samuel</a>, created a political group called <i>Survivre</i>—the name later changed to <i>Survivre et vivre</i>. The group published a bulletin and was dedicated to antimilitary and ecological issues, and also developed strong criticism of the indiscriminate use of science and technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> Grothendieck devoted the next three years to this group and served as the main editor of its bulletin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After leaving the IHÉS, Grothendieck became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temporary_professor" title="Temporary professor">temporary professor</a> at <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_France" title="Collège de France">Collège de France</a> for two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> He then became a professor at the University of Montpellier, where he became increasingly estranged from the mathematical community. His mathematical career, for the most part, ended when he left the IHÉS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytob_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytob-7">[7]</a></sup> He formally retired in 1988, a few years after having accepted a research position at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_National_de_la_Recherche_Scientifique" title="Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique">CNRS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscripts_written_in_the_1980s">Manuscripts written in the 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manuscripts written in the 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While not publishing mathematical research in conventional ways during the 1980s, he produced several influential manuscripts with limited distribution, with both mathematical and biographical content.</p>
<p>Produced during 1980 and 1981, <i>La Longue Marche à travers la théorie de Galois</i> (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Long_March_Through_Galois_Theory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Long March Through Galois Theory (page does not exist)">The Long March Through Galois Theory</a></i>) is a 1600-page handwritten manuscript containing many of the ideas that led to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esquisse_d%27un_programme" title="Esquisse d'un programme">Esquisse d'un programme</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esquissefr_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-esquissefr-37">[36]</a></sup> It also includes a study of <a href="/wiki/Teichm%C3%BCller_space" title="Teichmüller space">Teichmüller theory</a>.</p>
<p>In 1983, stimulated by correspondence with <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Brown_(mathematician)" title="Ronald Brown (mathematician)">Ronald Brown</a> and Tim Porter at the <a href="/wiki/Bangor_University" title="Bangor University">Bangor University</a>, Grothendieck wrote a 600-page manuscript titled <i><a href="/wiki/Pursuing_Stacks" title="Pursuing Stacks">Pursuing Stacks</a></i>, starting with a letter addressed to <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Quillen" title="Daniel Quillen">Daniel Quillen</a>. This letter and successive parts were distributed from Bangor (see <a href="#External_links">External links</a> below). Within these, in an informal, diary-like manner, Grothendieck explained and developed his ideas on the relationship between <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_homotopy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Algebraic homotopy theory (page does not exist)">algebraic homotopy theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a> and prospects for a noncommutative theory of <a href="/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)" title="Stack (mathematics)">stacks</a>. The manuscript, which is being edited for publication by G. Maltsiniotis, later led to another of his monumental works, <i>Les Dérivateurs</i>. Written in 1991, this latter opus of about 2000 pages further developed the homotopical ideas begun in <i>Pursuing Stacks</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson2_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-jackson2-6">[6]</a></sup> Much of this work anticipated the subsequent development of the motivic homotopy theory of <a href="/wiki/Fabien_Morel" title="Fabien Morel">Fabien Morel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V._Voevodsky" title="V. Voevodsky">V. Voevodsky</a> in the mid-1990s.</p>
<p>In 1984, Grothendieck wrote the proposal <i><a href="/wiki/Esquisse_d%27un_Programme" title="Esquisse d'un Programme">Esquisse d'un Programme</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esquissefr_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-esquissefr-37">[36]</a></sup> ("Sketch of a Programme") for a position at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_National_de_la_Recherche_Scientifique" title="Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique">Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique</a> (CNRS). It describes new ideas for studying the <a href="/wiki/Moduli_space" title="Moduli space">moduli space</a> of complex curves. Although Grothendieck himself never published his work in this area, the proposal inspired other mathematicians' work by becoming the source of <a href="/wiki/Dessin_d%27enfant" title="Dessin d'enfant">dessin d'enfant</a> theory and <a href="/wiki/Anabelian_geometry" title="Anabelian geometry">Anabelian geometry</a>. It was later published in the two-volume <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Hlf1j2XmXkcC&amp;dq=Geometric+Galois+Actions&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;source=bl&amp;ots=q9iN4QMkYj&amp;sig=fAsvVlPOhN9K9LfjGFJC5zm_IA4&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=KW-3ScSkHYHIMuDukOEK&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ct=result" rel="nofollow"><i>Geometric Galois Actions</i></a> (Cambridge University Press, 1997).</p>
<p>During this period, Grothendieck also gave his consent to publishing some of his drafts for EGA on <a href="/wiki/Theorem_of_Bertini" title="Theorem of Bertini">Bertini-type theorems</a> (<i>EGA</i> V, published in Ulam Quarterly in 1992-1993 and later made available on the <a class="external text" href="http://www.math.jussieu.fr/~leila/grothendieckcircle/index.php" rel="nofollow">Grothendieck Circle</a> web site in 2004).</p>
<p>In the 1,000-page autobiographical manuscript <i>Récoltes et semailles</i> (1986) Grothendieck describes his approach to mathematics and his experiences in the mathematical community, a community that initially accepted him in an open and welcoming manner but which he progressively perceived to be governed by competition and status. He complains about what he saw as the "burial" of his work and betrayal by his former students and colleagues after he had left the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Récoltes et semailles</i> work is now available on the internet in the French original,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grothen_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-grothen-38">[37]</a></sup> and an English translation is underway. Parts of <i>Récoltes et semailles</i> have been translated into Spanish<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> and into Russian and published in Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1988 Grothendieck declined the <a href="/wiki/Crafoord_Prize" title="Crafoord Prize">Crafoord Prize</a> with an open letter to the media. He wrote that established mathematicians like himself had no need for additional financial support and criticized what he saw as the declining ethics of the scientific community, characterized by outright scientific theft that, according to him, had become commonplace and tolerated. The letter also expressed his belief that totally unforeseen events before the end of the century would lead to an unprecedented collapse of civilization. Grothendieck added however that his views are "in no way meant as a criticism of the Royal Academy's aims in the administration of its funds" and added "I regret the inconvenience that my refusal to accept the Crafoord prize may have caused you and the Royal Academy" and that he "apologized" for the inconvenience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-columbia_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-columbia-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>La Clef des Songes</i>, a 315-page manuscript written in 1987, is Grothendieck's account of how his consideration of the source of dreams led him to conclude that <a href="/wiki/Existence_of_God" title="Existence of God">God exists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> As part of the notes to this manuscript, Grothendieck described the life and word of 18 "mutants," people whom he admired as visionaries far ahead of their time and heralding a new age. The only mathematician on his list was <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann" title="Bernhard Riemann">Bernhard Riemann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> Influenced by the Catholic mystic <a href="/wiki/Marthe_Robin" title="Marthe Robin">Marthe Robin</a> who was claimed to survive on the Holy Eucharist alone, Grothendieck almost starved himself to death in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup> His growing preoccupation with spiritual matters was also evident in a letter entitled <i>Lettre de la Bonne Nouvelle</i> sent to 250 friends in January 1990. In it, he described his encounters with a deity and announced that a "New Age" would commence on 14 October 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson2_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-jackson2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over 20,000 pages of Grothendieck's mathematical and other writings, held at the University of Montpellier, remain unpublished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> They are now being digitized for preservation and will probably be made available to the mathematical community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_into_reclusion_and_death">Retirement into reclusion and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retirement into reclusion and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, Grothendieck moved to a new address which he did not provide to his previous contacts in the mathematical community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup> Very few people visited him afterward. Local villagers helped sustain him with a more varied diet after he tried to live on a staple of <a href="/wiki/Taraxacum" title="Taraxacum">dandelion soup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> After his death, it was revealed that he lived alone in a house in <a href="/wiki/Lasserre,_Ari%C3%A8ge" title="Lasserre, Ariège">Lasserre, Ariège</a>, a small village at the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2010, Grothendieck wrote the letter "Déclaration d'intention de non-publication" to <a href="/wiki/Luc_Illusie" title="Luc Illusie">Luc Illusie</a>, claiming that all materials published in his absence have been published without his permission. He asks that none of his work be reproduced in whole or in part and that copies of this work be removed from libraries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> A website devoted to his work was called "an abomination."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> This order may have been reversed later in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 November 2014, aged 86, Grothendieck died in the hospital of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Girons,_Ari%C3%A8ge" title="Saint-Girons, Ariège">Saint-Girons, Ariège</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liberation-Douroux-Obit-2014-11-13_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Liberation-Douroux-Obit-2014-11-13-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citizenship">Citizenship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Citizenship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck was born in <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Germany</a>. In 1938, aged ten, he moved to France as a refugee. Records of his nationality were destroyed in the fall of Germany in 1945 and he did not apply for French citizenship after the war. He thus became a stateless person for at least the majority of his working life, traveling on a <a href="/wiki/Nansen_passport" title="Nansen passport">Nansen passport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004p._10.2C_footnote_12_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004p._10.2C_footnote_12-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleinert2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleinert2007-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDouroux2012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDouroux2012-3">[3]</a></sup> Part of this reluctance to hold French nationality is attributed to not wishing to serve in the French military, particularly due to the <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">Algerian War</a> (1954–62).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2001_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2001-52">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKleinert2007_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKleinert2007-2">[2]</a></sup> He eventually applied for French citizenship in the early 1980s, well past the age that exempted him from military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck was very close to his mother to whom he dedicated his dissertation. She died in 1957 from the <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> that she contracted in camps for displaced persons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> He had five children: a son with his landlady during his time in Nancy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECartier2004_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECartier2004-28">[28]</a></sup> three children, Johanna (1959), Alexander (1961) and Mathieu (1965) with his wife Mireille Dufour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup> and one child with Justine Skalba, with whom he lived in a commune in the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScharlau2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_work">Mathematical work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mathematical work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grothendieck's early mathematical work was in <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">functional analysis</a>. Between 1949 and 1953 he worked on his doctoral thesis in this subject at <a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a>, supervised by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dieudonn%C3%A9" title="Jean Dieudonné">Jean Dieudonné</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Schwartz" title="Laurent Schwartz">Laurent Schwartz</a>. His key contributions include <a href="/wiki/Topological_tensor_product" title="Topological tensor product">topological tensor products</a> of <a href="/wiki/Topological_vector_space" title="Topological vector space">topological vector spaces</a>, the theory of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_space" title="Nuclear space">nuclear spaces</a> as foundational for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schwartz_distribution" title="Schwartz distribution">Schwartz distributions</a>, and the application of <a href="/wiki/Lp_space" title="Lp space">L<sup>p</sup> spaces</a> in studying linear maps between topological vector spaces. In a few years, he had turned himself into a leading authority on this area of functional analysis—to the extent that Dieudonné compares his impact in this field to that of <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Banach" title="Stefan Banach">Banach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is, however, in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" title="Algebraic geometry">algebraic geometry</a> and related fields where Grothendieck did his most important and influential work. From about 1955 he started to work on <a href="/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics)" title="Sheaf (mathematics)">sheaf</a> theory and <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>, producing the influential "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%B4hoku_paper" title="Tôhoku paper">Tôhoku paper</a>" (<i>Sur quelques points d'algèbre homologique</i>, published in the <a href="/wiki/Tohoku_Mathematical_Journal" title="Tohoku Mathematical Journal">Tohoku Mathematical Journal</a> in 1957) where he introduced <a href="/wiki/Abelian_category" title="Abelian category">abelian categories</a> and applied their theory to show that <a href="/wiki/Sheaf_cohomology" title="Sheaf cohomology">sheaf cohomology</a> can be defined as certain <a href="/wiki/Derived_functor" title="Derived functor">derived functors</a> in this context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Homological methods and sheaf theory had already been introduced in algebraic geometry by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre" title="Jean-Pierre Serre">Jean-Pierre Serre</a> and others, after sheaves had been defined by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Leray" title="Jean Leray">Jean Leray</a>. Grothendieck took them to a higher level of abstraction and turned them into a key organising principle of his theory. He shifted attention from the study of individual varieties to the <i><a href="/wiki/Grothendieck%27s_relative_point_of_view" title="Grothendieck's relative point of view">relative point of view</a></i> (pairs of varieties related by a <a href="/wiki/Morphism" title="Morphism">morphism</a>), allowing a broad generalization of many classical theorems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> The first major application was the relative version of Serre's theorem showing that the cohomology of a <a href="/wiki/Coherent_sheaf" title="Coherent sheaf">coherent sheaf</a> on a complete variety is finite-dimensional; Grothendieck's theorem shows that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_direct_image" title="Higher direct image">higher direct images</a> of coherent sheaves under a proper map are coherent; this reduces to Serre's theorem over a one-point space.</p>
<p>In 1956, he applied the same thinking to the <a href="/wiki/Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Riemann–Roch theorem">Riemann–Roch theorem</a>, which had already recently been generalized to any dimension by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hirzebruch" title="Friedrich Hirzebruch">Hirzebruch</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Grothendieck%E2%80%93Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch theorem">Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch theorem</a> was announced by Grothendieck at the initial <a href="/wiki/Mathematische_Arbeitstagung" title="Mathematische Arbeitstagung">Mathematische Arbeitstagung</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> It appeared in print in a paper written by <a href="/wiki/Armand_Borel" title="Armand Borel">Armand Borel</a> with Serre. This result was his first work in algebraic geometry. He went on to plan and execute a programme for rebuilding the foundations of algebraic geometry, which were then in a state of flux and under discussion in <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chevalley" title="Claude Chevalley">Claude Chevalley</a>'s seminar; he outlined his programme in his talk at the 1958 <a href="/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians" title="International Congress of Mathematicians">International Congress of Mathematicians</a>.</p>
<p>His foundational work on algebraic geometry is at a higher level of abstraction than all prior versions. He adapted the use of non-closed <a href="/wiki/Generic_point" title="Generic point">generic points</a>, which led to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics)" title="Scheme (mathematics)">schemes</a>. He also pioneered the systematic use of <a href="/wiki/Nilpotent" title="Nilpotent">nilpotents</a>. As 'functions' these can take only the value 0, but they carry <a href="/wiki/Infinitesimal" title="Infinitesimal">infinitesimal</a> information, in purely algebraic settings. His <i>theory of schemes</i> has become established as the best universal foundation for this field, because of its expressiveness as well as technical depth. In that setting one can use <a href="/wiki/Birational_geometry" title="Birational geometry">birational geometry</a>, techniques from <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galois_theory" title="Galois theory">Galois theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commutative_algebra" title="Commutative algebra">commutative algebra</a>, and close analogues of the methods of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a>, all in an integrated way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mclarty_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mclarty-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is also noted for his mastery of abstract approaches to mathematics and his perfectionism in matters of formulation and presentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artist_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-artist-29">[29]</a></sup> Relatively little of his work after 1960 was published by the conventional route of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Learned_journal" title="Learned journal">learned journal</a>, circulating initially in duplicated volumes of seminar notes; his influence was to a considerable extent personal. His influence spilled over into many other branches of mathematics, for example the contemporary theory of <a href="/wiki/D-module" title="D-module">D-modules</a>. (It also provoked adverse reactions, with many mathematicians seeking out more concrete areas and problems.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scienceline_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-scienceline-57">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectator_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-spectator-58">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EGA.2C_SGA.2C_FGA"><i>EGA</i>, <i>SGA</i>, <i>FGA</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: EGA, SGA, FGA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bulk of Grothendieck's published work is collected in the monumental, yet incomplete, <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89l%C3%A9ments_de_g%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie_alg%C3%A9brique" title="Éléments de géométrie algébrique">Éléments de géométrie algébrique</a></i> (<i>EGA</i>) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A9minaire_de_g%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie_alg%C3%A9brique" title="Séminaire de géométrie algébrique">Séminaire de géométrie algébrique</a></i> (<i>SGA</i>). The collection <i><a href="/wiki/Fondements_de_la_G%C3%A9ometrie_Alg%C3%A9brique" title="Fondements de la Géometrie Algébrique">Fondements de la Géometrie Algébrique</a></i> (<i>FGA</i>), which gathers together talks given in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A9minaire_Bourbaki" title="Séminaire Bourbaki">Séminaire Bourbaki</a>, also contains important material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grothendieck's work includes the invention of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology" title="Étale cohomology">étale</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-adic_cohomology" title="L-adic cohomology">l-adic cohomology</a> theories, which explain an observation of <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil" title="André Weil">André Weil</a>'s that there is a connection between the topological characteristics of a variety and its diophantine (number theoretic) properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> For example, the number of solutions of an equation over a <a href="/wiki/Finite_field" title="Finite field">finite field</a> reflects the topological nature of its solutions over the <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex numbers</a>. Weil realized that to prove such a connection one needed a new cohomology theory, but neither he nor any other expert saw how to do this until such a theory was found by Grothendieck.</p>
<p>This program culminated in the proofs of the <a href="/wiki/Weil_conjectures" title="Weil conjectures">Weil conjectures</a>, the last of which was settled by Grothendieck's student <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Deligne" title="Pierre Deligne">Pierre Deligne</a> in the early 1970s after Grothendieck had largely withdrawn from mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson1_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-jackson1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_mathematical_topics_.28from_R.C3.A9coltes_et_Semailles.29">Major mathematical topics (from <i>Récoltes et Semailles</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Major mathematical topics (from Récoltes et Semailles)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck wrote a retrospective assessment of his mathematical work (see the external link <i>La Vision</i> below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup> As his main mathematical achievements ("maître-thèmes"), he chose this collection of 12 topics (his chronological order):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005_36-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPragacz2005-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Topological_tensor_product" title="Topological tensor product">Topological tensor products</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_space" title="Nuclear space">nuclear spaces</a></li>
<li>"Continuous" and "discrete" <a href="/wiki/Duality_(mathematics)" title="Duality (mathematics)">duality</a> (<a href="/wiki/Derived_category" title="Derived category">derived categories</a> and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_operations_(mathematics)" title="Six operations (mathematics)">six operations</a>")</li>
<li><i>Yoga</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Grothendieck%E2%80%93Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch theorem">Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch theorem</a> (<a href="/wiki/K-theory" title="K-theory">K-theory</a>, relation with <a href="/wiki/Intersection_theory" title="Intersection theory">intersection theory</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics)" title="Scheme (mathematics)">Schemes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Topos" title="Topos">Topoi</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology" title="Étale cohomology">Étale cohomology</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-adic_cohomology" title="L-adic cohomology">l-adic cohomology</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motive_(mathematics)" title="Motive (mathematics)">Motives</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motivic_Galois_group" title="Motivic Galois group">motivic Galois group</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Grothendieck_category" title="Grothendieck category">Grothendieck categories</a>)</li>
<li>Crystals and <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_cohomology" title="Crystalline cohomology">crystalline cohomology</a>, <i>yoga</i> of De Rham and Hodge coefficients</li>
<li>Topological algebra, infinity-stacks, <a href="/wiki/Derivator" title="Derivator">'dérivateurs'</a>, cohomological formalism of toposes as an inspiration for a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homotopic_algebra" title="Homotopic algebra">homotopic algebra</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tame_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tame topology (page does not exist)">Tame topology</a></li>
<li><i>Yoga</i> of <a href="/wiki/Anabelian_geometry" title="Anabelian geometry">anabelian geometry</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galois%E2%80%93Teichm%C3%BCller_theory" title="Galois–Teichmüller theory">Galois–Teichmüller theory</a></li>
<li>Schematic point of view, or "arithmetics" for <a href="/wiki/Regular_polyhedron" title="Regular polyhedron">regular polyhedra</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Regular_configurations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Regular configurations (page does not exist)">regular configurations</a> of all sorts.</li>
</ol>
<p>He wrote that the central theme of the topics above is that of topos theory, while the first and last were of the least importance to him.</p>
<p>Here the term <i>yoga</i> denotes a kind of "meta-theory" that can be used heuristically; <a href="/wiki/Michel_Raynaud" title="Michel Raynaud">Michel Raynaud</a> writes the other terms "Ariadne's thread" and "philosophy" as effective equivalents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grothendieck is considered by many to be the greatest mathematician of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-obit-9">[9]</a></sup> In an obituary <a href="/wiki/David_Mumford" title="David Mumford">David Mumford</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Tate" title="John Tate">John Tate</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Although mathematics became more and more abstract and general throughout the 20th century, it was Alexander Grothendieck who was the greatest master of this trend. His unique skill was to eliminate all unnecessary hypotheses and burrow into an area so deeply that its inner patterns on the most abstract level revealed themselves–and then, like a magician, show how the solution of old problems fell out in straightforward ways now that their real nature had been revealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-obit-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>By the 1970s, Grothendieck's work was seen as influential not only in algebraic geometry, and the allied fields of sheaf theory and homological algebra,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> but influenced logic, in the field of categorical logic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geometry">Geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck approached algebraic geometry by clarifying the foundations of the field, and by developing mathematical tools intended to prove a number of notable conjectures. Algebraic geometry has traditionally meant the understanding of geometric objects, such as <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_curve" title="Algebraic curve">algebraic curves</a> and surfaces, through the study of the algebraic equations for those objects. Properties of algebraic equations are in turn studied using the techniques of <a href="/wiki/Ring_theory" title="Ring theory">ring theory</a>. In this approach, the properties of a geometric object are related to the properties of an associated ring. The space (e.g., real, complex, or projective) in which the object is defined is extrinsic to the object, while the ring is intrinsic.</p>
<p>Grothendieck laid a new foundation for algebraic geometry by making intrinsic spaces ("spectra") and associated rings the primary objects of study. To that end he developed the theory of <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics)" title="Scheme (mathematics)">schemes</a>, which can be informally thought of as <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological spaces</a> on which a <a href="/wiki/Commutative_ring" title="Commutative ring">commutative ring</a> is associated to every open subset of the space. Schemes have become the basic objects of study for practitioners of modern algebraic geometry. Their use as a foundation allowed geometry to absorb technical advances from other fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grothendieck%E2%80%93Hirzebruch%E2%80%93Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem" title="Grothendieck–Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch theorem">generalization</a> of the classical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riemann-Roch_theorem" title="Riemann-Roch theorem">Riemann-Roch theorem</a> related topological properties of complex <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_curve" title="Algebraic curve">algebraic curves</a> to their algebraic structure. The tools he developed to prove this theorem started the study of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_K-theory" title="Algebraic K-theory">algebraic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Topological_K-theory" title="Topological K-theory">topological K-theory</a>, which study the topological properties of objects by associating them with rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartshorne_AG_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hartshorne_AG-63">[62]</a></sup> Topological K-theory was founded by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Atiyah" title="Michael Atiyah">Michael Atiyah</a>, after direct contact with Grothendieck's ideas at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonn_Arbeitstagung" title="Bonn Arbeitstagung">Bonn Arbeitstagung</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atiyah2014_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atiyah2014-64">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cohomology_theories">Cohomology theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cohomology theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck's construction of new <a href="/wiki/Cohomology" title="Cohomology">cohomology</a> theories, which use algebraic techniques to study topological objects, has influenced the development of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory" title="Algebraic number theory">algebraic number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Representation_theory" title="Representation theory">representation theory</a>. As part of this project, his creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topos_theory" title="Topos theory">topos theory</a>, a category-theoretic generalization of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point-set_topology" title="Point-set topology">point-set topology</a>, has influenced the fields of <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_logic" title="Mathematical logic">mathematical logic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Weil_conjectures" title="Weil conjectures">Weil conjectures</a> were formulated in the later 1940s as a set of mathematical problems in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry" title="Arithmetic geometry">arithmetic geometry</a>. They describe properties of analytic invariants, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_zeta_function" title="Local zeta function">local zeta functions</a>, of the number of points on an algebraic curve or variety of higher dimension. Grothendieck's discovery of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology" title="Étale cohomology">ℓ-adic étale cohomology</a>, the first example of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weil_cohomology" title="Weil cohomology">Weil cohomology</a> theory, opened the way for a proof of the Weil conjectures, ultimately completed in the 1970s by his student <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Deligne" title="Pierre Deligne">Pierre Deligne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartshorne_AG_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hartshorne_AG-63">[62]</a></sup> Grothendieck's large-scale approach has been called a "visionary program."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> The ℓ-adic cohomology then became a fundamental tool for number theorists, with applications to the <a href="/wiki/Langlands_program" title="Langlands program">Langlands program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grothendieck's conjectural theory of <a href="/wiki/Motive_(algebraic_geometry)" title="Motive (algebraic geometry)">motives</a> was intended to be the "ℓ-adic" theory but without the choice of "ℓ", a prime number. It did not provide the intended route to the Weil conjectures, but has been behind modern developments in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_K-theory" title="Algebraic K-theory">algebraic K-theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motivic_cohomology" title="Motivic cohomology">motivic homotopy theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Motivic_integration" title="Motivic integration">motivic integration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup> This theory, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Quillen" title="Daniel Quillen">Daniel Quillen</a>'s work, and Grothendieck's theory of <a href="/wiki/Chern_class" title="Chern class">Chern classes</a>, are considered the background to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_cobordism" title="Algebraic cobordism">algebraic cobordism</a>, another algebraic analogue of topological ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Category_theory">Category theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Grothendieck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Category theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grothendieck's emphasis on the role of <a href="/wiki/Universal_property" title="Universal property">universal properties</a> across varied mathematical structures brought <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theory</a> into the mainstream as an organizing principle for mathematics in general. Among its uses, category theory creates a common language for describing similar structures and techniques seen in many different mathematical systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup> His notion of <a href="/wiki/Abelian_category" title="Abelian category">abelian category</a> is now the basic object of study in <a href="/wiki/Homological_algebra" title="Homological algebra">homological algebra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup> The emergence of a separate mathematical discipline of category theory has been attributed to Grothendieck's influence, though unintentional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup></p>
