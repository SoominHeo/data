<p><b>Alexander Hill Key</b> (September 21, 1904 – July 25, 1979) was an American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> writer, most of whose books were aimed at a juvenile audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merrimous_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-merrimous-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_the_Art_Institute" title="School of the Art Institute">School of the Art Institute</a> in Chicago, Illinois between 1921-1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merrimous_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-merrimous-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He became a nationally known illustrator before he became an author. After he began writing novels for young people, he moved his family to the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> mountains, and most of his books include that wild and rugged landscape.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_work">Literary work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literary work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His novel <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_to_Witch_Mountain" title="Escape to Witch Mountain">Escape to Witch Mountain</a></i> was made into a popular film in 1975, 1995, and again in 2009. The sequel was made into another popular film in 1978. His novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Incredible_Tide" title="The Incredible Tide">The Incredible Tide</a></i> became a popular <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> series called <i><a href="/wiki/Future_Boy_Conan" title="Future Boy Conan">Future Boy Conan</a></i> in 1978.</p>
<p>He is known for his portrayals of alien but human-looking people who have tremendously strong <a href="/wiki/Psychic" title="Psychic">psychic</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psionic" title="Psionic">psionic</a> abilities and a close communion with nature, and who can telepathically speak with animals. In <i>The Strange White Doves</i>, he professed his belief that animals are conscious, thinking, feeling, perceiving, independent, and self-aware intelligent beings, and have subtle ways of communicating, perhaps via <a href="/wiki/Empathy" title="Empathy">empathy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Telepathy" title="Telepathy">telepathy</a>. The protagonists of Key's books are often ostracized, feared, or persecuted because of their astonishing abilities or extraterrestrial origins, and Key uses this as a clear metaphor for racism and other prejudice.</p>
<p>In several of his novels (most notably <i>The Case of the Vanishing Boy</i>) Key portrays some sort of communal withdrawal from society by a group of like-minded individuals. Key sometimes depicted government-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Social_services" title="Social services">social services</a> for children as inefficient or even counterproductive in its efforts: In <i>The Forgotten Door,</i> social services is presented as a clearly undesirable alternative for the protagonist Little Jon, and in <i>Escape to Witch Mountain</i>, Tony and Tia actively flee the system. In both cases, however, it's for a very logical reason: the characters are "not from around here." All they want to do is go home and, happily, a few of us locals have the decency to help them do so (Key's <i>The Forgotten Door</i> predates <a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a> by over a decade).</p>
<p>The plot of Key's <i>The Magic Meadow</i> is even more poignant for any reader who has ever been bedridden in hospital. Its ending in particular is phenomenally optimistic. That was another Alexander Key theme: that good and decent people deserve to escape <i>to</i> a place worthy of them.</p>
