<p><b>Alexander Joseph</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Knight_Grand_Cross_of_the_Order_of_the_Bath" title="Honorary Knight Grand Cross of the Order of the Bath">GCB</a></span> (<a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg" xml:lang="bg">Александър I Батенберг</span>; 5 April 1857 – 23 October 1893), known as <b>Alexander of Battenberg</b>, was the first prince (<i><a href="/wiki/Knyaz" title="Knyaz">knyaz</a></i>) of <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bulgaria" title="Principality of Bulgaria">modern Bulgaria</a> from 1879 until his abdication in 1886.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_of_Battenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alexander was the second son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Alexander_of_Hesse_and_by_Rhine" title="Prince Alexander of Hesse and by Rhine">Prince Alexander of Hesse and by Rhine</a> by the latter's <a href="/wiki/Morganatic_marriage" title="Morganatic marriage">morganatic marriage</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julia_von_Hauke" title="Julia von Hauke">Countess Julia von Hauke</a>. The Countess and her descendants gained the title of Princess of <a href="/wiki/Battenberg_family" title="Battenberg family">Battenberg</a> (derived from an old residence of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Grand Dukes of Hesse</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Style_(manner_of_address)" title="Style (manner of address)">style</a> <i>Durchlaucht</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Serene_Highness" title="Serene Highness">Serene Highness</a>") in 1858. Prince Alexander was a nephew of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsar_Alexander_II" title="Tsar Alexander II">Tsar Alexander II</a>, who had married a sister of Prince Alexander of Hesse; his mother, a daughter of Count Moritz von Hauke, had been <a href="/wiki/Lady-in-waiting" title="Lady-in-waiting">lady-in-waiting</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsaritsa" title="Tsaritsa">Tsaritsa</a>. Alexander was known to his family, and many later biographers, as "Sandro" and "Drino".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_relatives">Notable relatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_of_Battenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable relatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexander's brother, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Louis_of_Battenberg" title="Prince Louis of Battenberg">Prince Louis of Battenberg</a>, married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Victoria_of_Hesse_and_the_Rhine" title="Princess Victoria of Hesse and the Rhine">Princess Victoria of Hesse and the Rhine</a>, a granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>. Their children included <a href="/wiki/Louise_Mountbatten" title="Louise Mountbatten">Queen Louise of Sweden</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Mountbatten" title="Louis Mountbatten">Earl Mountbatten of Burma</a> and Princess Alice, the mother of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a>, husband of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>.</p>
<p>Alexander's other brother, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Henry_of_Battenberg" title="Prince Henry of Battenberg">Prince Henry of Battenberg</a>, married Queen Victoria's youngest daughter <a href="/wiki/Princess_Beatrice_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Princess Beatrice of the United Kingdom">Princess Beatrice</a>. Among their children was <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Eugenie_of_Battenberg" title="Victoria Eugenie of Battenberg">Queen Victoria Eugenia of Spain</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prince_of_Bulgaria">Prince of Bulgaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_of_Battenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prince of Bulgaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his boyhood and early youth Alexander frequently visited <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, and he accompanied his uncle, the Tsar, who was much attached to him, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War,_1877-78" title="Russo-Turkish War, 1877-78">Bulgarian campaign</a> of 1877. When, under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Berlin,_1878" title="Treaty of Berlin, 1878">Treaty of Berlin</a> (1878), Bulgaria became an autonomous <a href="/wiki/Principality" title="Principality">principality</a> under the suzerainty of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, the Tsar recommended his nephew to the Bulgarians as a candidate for the newly created throne, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Bulgaria" title="National Assembly of Bulgaria">Grand National Assembly</a> unanimously elected Prince Alexander as Prince of Bulgaria (29 April 1879). At that time he held a commission as a lieutenant in the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> life-guards at <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam</a>. Before proceeding to Bulgaria, Prince Alexander paid visits to the Tsar at <a href="/wiki/Livadia_Palace" title="Livadia Palace">Livadia</a>, to the courts of the great powers and to the <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultan</a>; a Russian warship then conveyed him to <a href="/wiki/Varna" title="Varna">Varna</a>, and after taking the <a href="/wiki/Oath" title="Oath">oath</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Tarnovo_Constitution" title="Tarnovo Constitution">new constitution</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turnovo" title="Turnovo">Turnovo</a> (8 July 1879) he went to <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>. The people everywhere <i>en route</i> greeted him with immense enthusiasm. (For the political history of Prince Alexander's reign, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bulgaria_(1878%E2%80%931946)" title="History of Bulgaria (1878–1946)">History of Bulgaria</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new ruling prince had not had any previous training in governing, and a range of problems confronted him. He found himself caught between the official representatives of Russia, who wanted him to behave as a <i><a href="/wiki/Roi_fain%C3%A9ant" title="Roi fainéant">roi fainéant</a></i>, and the Bulgarian politicians, who actively pursued their own quarrels with a violence that threatened the stability of Bulgaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1881, a marriage began to be talked of between Alexander and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Viktoria_of_Prussia" title="Princess Viktoria of Prussia">Princess Viktoria of Prussia</a>. While this plan was supported by the <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Princess_Royal" title="Victoria, Princess Royal">Crown Princess</a> and her mother, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, and Princess Viktoria had fallen in love with him, it was strongly opposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_I" title="Kaiser Wilhelm I">Kaiser Wilhelm I</a>, his grandson, later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_II" title="Kaiser Wilhelm II">Kaiser Wilhelm II</a> and the German Chancellor, <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a>, who feared that a marriage would offend the Russians, most notably, Prince Alexander's cousin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsar_Alexander_III" title="Tsar Alexander III">Tsar Alexander III</a>, who recently ascended the throne, and who, unlike his father, was far from kindly disposed to the prince.</p>
<p>After attempting to govern under these conditions for nearly two years, the prince, with the consent of the Russian tsar, Alexander assumed absolute power, having suspended the Constitution (9 May 1881). A specially convened assembly voted (13 July 1881) for suspension of the ultra-democratic constitution for a period of seven years. The experiment, however, proved unsuccessful; the monarchical coup infuriated Bulgarian Liberal and Radical politicians, and the real <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">power</a> passed to two Russian generals, <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Sobolev" title="Leonid Sobolev">Sobolev</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Kaulbars" title="Aleksandr Kaulbars">Kaulbars</a>, specially despatched from Saint Petersburg. The prince, after vainly endeavouring to obtain the recall of the generals, restored the constitution with the concurrence of all the Bulgarian political parties (19 September 1883). A serious breach with <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> followed, and the part which the prince subsequently played in encouraging the national aspirations of the Bulgarians widened that breach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The revolution of <a href="/wiki/Plovdiv" title="Plovdiv">Plovdiv</a> (18 September 1885), which brought about the union of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Rumelia" title="Eastern Rumelia">Eastern Rumelia</a> with Bulgaria, took place with Alexander's consent, and he at once assumed the government of the province. In the year which followed, the prince gave evidence of considerable military and diplomatic ability. He rallied the Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">army</a>, now deprived of its Russian officers, to resist the <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Bulgarian_War" title="Serbo-Bulgarian War">Serbian invasion</a>, and after a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Slivnitsa" title="Battle of Slivnitsa">victory at Slivnitsa</a> (19 November),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup> which Alexander had little to do with, having arrived in Slivnitsa after the battle (incidentally initiated by a volunteer of the rank of private) was already over,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> pursued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milan_Obrenovic_IV" title="Milan Obrenovic IV">King Milan of Serbia</a> into Serbian territory as far as <a href="/wiki/Pirot" title="Pirot">Pirot</a>, which he captured (27 November). Although the intervention of Austria protected <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> from the consequences of defeat, Prince Alexander's success sealed the union with Eastern Rumelia, and after long negotiations the <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultan</a> <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Abdul Hamid II</a> nominated the Prince of Bulgaria as governor-general of that province for five years (5 April 1886).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_throne">Loss of throne</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_of_Battenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Loss of throne">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>This arrangement, however, cost Alexander much of his popularity in Bulgaria, while discontent prevailed among a number of his officers, who considered themselves slighted in the distribution of rewards at the close of the campaign. A <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_of_1886" title="Bulgarian coup d'état of 1886">military plot</a> formed, and on the night of 20 August 1886 the conspirators seized the prince in the palace at <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> and compelled him to sign his abdication; they then hurried him to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">Danube</a> at Rakhovo, transported him on his yacht to <a href="/wiki/Reni,_Ukraine" title="Reni, Ukraine">Reni</a>, and handed him over to the Russian authorities, who allowed him to proceed to <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">Lemberg</a>. However, he soon returned to Bulgaria as a result of the success of the counter-revolution led by <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Stambolov" title="Stefan Stambolov">Stefan Stambolov</a>, which overthrew the provisional government set up by the Russian party at Sofia. His position, however, had become untenable, partly as a result of an ill-considered telegram which he addressed to Tsar Alexander III on his return. The attitude of Bismarck, who, in conjunction with the Russian and Austrian governments, forbade him to punish the leaders of the military conspiracy, also undermined Alexander's position. He therefore issued a manifesto resigning the throne, and left Bulgaria on 8 September 1886.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his abdication from the Bulgarian throne, Alexander I claimed the title <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Tarnovo" title="Prince of Tarnovo">Prince of Tarnovo</a> and used it until his death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_years">Last years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_of_Battenberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Last years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Alexander then retired into private life. A few years later he married <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Loisinger" title="Johanna Loisinger">Johanna Loisinger</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actress" title="Actress">actress</a>, and assumed the style of Count von <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hartenau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hartenau (page does not exist)">Hartenau</a> (6 February 1889). There were a son and a daughter from this marriage. The last years of his life he spent principally at <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, where he held a local command in the Austrian army, and where he died on 23 October 1893. His remains, brought to Sofia, received a public funeral there, and were buried in a <a href="/wiki/Battenberg_Mausoleum" title="Battenberg Mausoleum">mausoleum erected to his memory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourchier1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prince Alexander possessed much charm and amiability of manner; he was tall, dignified and strikingly handsome. Competent authorities have generally recognised his capabilities as a soldier. As a ruler he committed some errors, but his youth and inexperience and the extreme difficulty of his position account for much. He had some aptitude for diplomacy, and his intuitive insight and perception of character sometimes enabled him to outwit the crafty politicians who surrounded him. His principal fault remained a want of tenacity and resolution; his tendency to unguarded language undoubtedly increased the number of his enemies.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Battenberg_Hill" title="Battenberg Hill">Battenberg Hill</a> on <a href="/wiki/Livingston_Island" title="Livingston Island">Livingston Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is named after Prince Alexander Battenberg of Bulgaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">excerpts from <i>A History of Volunteers in the Serbo-Bulgarian War</i> by colonel Yordan Benedikov page 73: "This commenced the battle initiated by private Shurbanov." page 676: "Knyaz Alexander arrived in Slivnitsa after the battle had ended on all fronts. The prince found out from Paprikov that Benderev had commenced the battle on his own initiative on the right flank. The prince was said to have reacted extremely negatively to this unilateral action stating the Benderev deserved to be shot. But on his way back from Slivnitsa he was said to have overcame his indignation saying in front of his cohorts "victors are never judged." /справка стр 73 от <i>историята на доброволците на сръбско-българската война</i> от полк. Йордан Венедиков: "Така се почна битката по инициативата на ю. Шурбанов." "И така главнокомандуващите и от двете страни решиха на 5-и ноемврий да не предприемат никакво нападение; да се отбраняват докато пристигнат очакваните подкрепления. Обаче сутринта на 5-и северните им флангове бяха така доближени, че една нищо и никаква случайност можеше да предизвика боя. Гъстата мъгла, която покриваше бойното поле, държеше на щрек всички и увеличаваше вероятността да се предизвика боят случайно, особено поради това, че българите очакваха без друго сърбите да атакуват. Командирът на сръбската конна бригада поради погрешно турените в картата имена бе завел бригадата си вместо в Голямо Малово в Мало Малово. Поради това вместо на фланга, той се оказа пред десния български фланг. При това той не бе получил и заповедта за отлагането атаката от 5-и за 6-и ноемврий, и сутринта на 5-и той се опита да обходи десния български фланг.; обаче българите от върха Леща - 3-а дружина от 1-и Софийски полк и 3-и ескадрон от 2-и конен полк - посрещнаха конната бригада с огън. Докато се водеше престрелката, бригадата получи заповедта за отлагане атаката. Тя се оттегли, обаче българската дружина на Леща счете оттеглянето на сърбите за свой успех и настъпи. Трябва да се отбележи, че инициаторът на това първо настъпление против сърбите на Сливница и въобще на Южния театър беше доброволецът юнкер Шурбанов. Той поведе полуротата си без да дочака заповедта на дружинния си командир. Скоро по заповед на командира си, капитан Бахчеванов, настъпи и цялата дружина. Така се почна голямото тридневно сражение на Сливница. Цитат, стр 676 “Княз Александър пристигна в Сливница, когато сражението бе свършено по всички линии. От Паприков Княза научи, че Бендерев почнал по своя инициатива боят на десния фланг. Князът се произнесъл твърде остро за това самоволно действие. За такава постъпка Бендерев заслужава да бъде разстрелян. Но на връщане от Сливница той съвладял своето негодувание и казал пред своята свита “победителите не съдят.”</span></li>
