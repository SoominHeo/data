<p><b>Alexander Dubček</b> (<small>Slovak pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Slovak" title="Help:IPA for Slovak">[ˈaleksander ˈdupt͡ʃek]</a></span>; 27 November 1921 – 7 November 1992) was a <a href="/wiki/Slovaks" title="Slovaks">Slovak</a> politician and, briefly, leader of <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> (1968–1969). He attempted to reform the <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Socialist_Republic" title="Czechoslovak Socialist Republic">communist regime</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a> but he was forced to resign following the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia">Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia</a>.</p>
<p>Later, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velvet_revolution" title="Velvet revolution">overthrow of the government in 1989</a>, he was Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Assembly_(Czechoslovakia)" title="Federal Assembly (Czechoslovakia)">federal Czechoslovak parliament</a>. Also in 1989, the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> awarded Dubček the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakharov_Prize_for_Freedom_of_Thought" title="Sakharov Prize for Freedom of Thought">Sakharov Prize for Freedom of Thought</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk98_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dk98-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dubček was born in <a href="/wiki/Uhrovec" title="Uhrovec">Uhrovec</a>, Czechoslovakia, on 27 November 1921<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and raised in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyrgyz_SSR" title="Kyrgyz SSR">Kyrgyz SSR</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>) as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Esperanto_culture" title="Esperanto culture">Esperantist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ido_(language)" title="Ido (language)">Idist</a> industrial cooperative <a href="/wiki/Interhelpo" title="Interhelpo">Interhelpo</a>. Alexander Dubček was conceived in Chicago, but born after the family relocated to Czechoslovakia. When Alexander Dubček was three, the family moved to the Soviet Union, in part to help build socialism and in part because jobs were scarce in Czechoslovakia. In 1938, the family returned to Czechoslovakia.</p>
<p>During the Second World War, Alexander Dubček joined the underground resistance against the wartime pro-German <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Republic_(1939%E2%80%931945)" title="Slovak Republic (1939–1945)">Slovak state</a> headed by <a href="/wiki/Jozef_Tiso" title="Jozef Tiso">Jozef Tiso</a>. In August 1944, Dubček fought in the <a href="/wiki/Slovak_National_Uprising" title="Slovak National Uprising">Slovak National Uprising</a> and was wounded. His brother, Július, was killed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the war, Alexander Dubček joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Slovakia_(1939)" title="Communist Party of Slovakia (1939)">Communist Party of Slovakia</a> (KSS), which had been created after the formation of the Slovak state and in 1948 was transformed into the Slovak branch of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">Communist Party of Czechoslovakia</a> (KSČ).</p>
<p>After the war, he steadily rose through the ranks in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Czechoslovakia_(1948%E2%80%931989)" title="History of Czechoslovakia (1948–1989)">Communist Czechoslovakia</a>. From 1951 to 1955 he was a member of the National Assembly, the parliament of Czechoslovakia. In 1953, he was sent to the Moscow Political College, where he graduated in 1958. In 1955 he joined the Central Committee of the Slovak branch and in 1962 became a member of the presidium. In 1958 he also joined the Central Committee of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">Communist Party of Czechoslovakia</a>, which he served as a secretary from 1960 to 1962 and as a member of the presidium after 1962. From 1960 to 1968 he once more was a member of the federal parliament.</p>
<p>In 1963, a power struggle in the leadership of the Slovak branch unseated Karol Bacílek and Pavol David, hard-line allies of <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Novotn%C3%BD" title="Antonín Novotný">Antonín Novotný</a>, First Secretary of the KSČ and President of Czechoslovakia. In their place, a new generation of Slovak Communists took control of party and state organs in Slovakia, led by Alexander Dubček, who became First Secretary of the Slovak branch of the party.</p>
<p>Under Dubček's leadership, Slovakia began to evolve toward political liberalization. Because Novotný and his <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinist</a> predecessors had denigrated Slovak "bourgeois nationalists", most notably <a href="/wiki/Gust%C3%A1v_Hus%C3%A1k" title="Gustáv Husák">Gustáv Husák</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladim%C3%ADr_Clementis" title="Vladimír Clementis">Vladimír Clementis</a>, in the 1950s, the Slovak branch worked to promote Slovak identity. This mainly took the form of celebrations and commemorations, such as the 150th birthdays of 19th century leaders of the Slovak National Revival <a href="/wiki/%C4%BDudov%C3%ADt_%C5%A0t%C3%BAr" title="Ľudovít Štúr">Ľudovít Štúr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jozef_Miloslav_Hurban" title="Jozef Miloslav Hurban">Jozef Miloslav Hurban</a>, the centenary of the <a href="/wiki/Matica_slovensk%C3%A1" title="Matica slovenská">Matica slovenská</a> in 1963, and the twentieth anniversary of the Slovak National Uprising. At the same time, the political and intellectual climate in Slovakia became freer than that in the Czech Lands. This was exemplified by the rising readership of <i>Kultúrny život</i>, the weekly newspaper of the Union of Slovak Writers, which published frank discussions of liberalization, federalization and democratization, written by the most progressive or controversial writers – both Slovak and Czech. <i>Kultúrny život</i> consequently became the first Slovak publication to gain a wide following among Czechs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prague_Spring">Prague Spring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prague Spring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Czechoslovak planned economy in the 1960s was in serious decline and the imposition of central control from <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> disappointed local Communists while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destalinization" title="Destalinization">destalinization</a> program caused further disquiet. In October 1967, a number of reformers, most notably <a href="/wiki/Ota_%C5%A0ik" title="Ota Šik">Ota Šik</a> and Alexander Dubček, took action: they challenged First Secretary <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Novotn%C3%BD" title="Antonín Novotný">Antonín Novotný</a> at a Central Committee meeting. Novotný faced a mutiny in the Central Committee, so he secretly invited <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a>, the Soviet leader, to make a whirlwind visit to Prague in December 1967 in order to shore up his own position.</p>
<p>When Brezhnev arrived in Prague and met with the Central Committee members, he was stunned to learn of the extent of the opposition to Novotný, leading Brezhnev to withhold support and paving the way for the Central Committee to force Novotný's resignation. Dubček became the new First Secretary of the Communist Party of Czechoslovakia on 5 January 1968.</p>
<p>The period following Novotný's downfall became known as the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a>. During this time, Dubček and other reformers sought to liberalize the Communist government—creating "socialism with a human face". Though this loosened the party's influence on the country, Dubček remained a devoted Communist and intended to preserve the party's rule. However, during the Prague Spring, he and other reform-minded Communists sought to win popular support for the Communist government by eliminating its worst, most repressive features, allowing greater freedom of expression and tolerating political and social organizations not under Communist control.</p>
<p>"Dubček! <a href="/wiki/Ludv%C3%ADk_Svoboda" title="Ludvík Svoboda">Svoboda</a>!" became the popular refrain of student demonstrations during this period. Yet Dubček found himself in an increasingly untenable position. The program of reform gained momentum, leading to pressures for further liberalization and democratization. At the same time, hard-line Communists in Czechoslovakia and the leaders of other <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> countries pressured Dubček to rein in the Prague Spring. Though Dubček wanted to oversee the reform movement, he refused to resort to any draconian measures to do so.</p>
<p>The Soviet leadership tried to slow down or stop the changes in Czechoslovakia through a series of negotiations. The Soviet Union agreed to bilateral talks with Czechoslovakia in July at Čierna nad Tisou, near the Slovak-Soviet border. At the meeting, Dubček tried to reassure the Soviets and the Warsaw Pact leaders that he was still friendly to Moscow, arguing that the reforms were an internal matter. He thought he had learned an important lesson from the failing of the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1956" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1956">Hungarian Revolution of 1956</a>, in which the leaders had gone as far as withdrawing from the Warsaw Pact. Dubček believed that the Kremlin would allow him a free hand in pursuing domestic reform as long as Czechoslovakia remained a faithful member of the Soviet bloc. Despite Dubček's continuing efforts to stress these commitments, Brezhnev and other Warsaw Pact leaders remained wary.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downfall">Downfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Downfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the night of 20–21 August 1968, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> forces entered Czechoslovakia. The occupying armies quickly seized control of Prague and the Central Committee's building, taking Dubček and other reformers into Soviet custody. But, before they were arrested, Dubček urged the people not to resist militarily. Later in the day, Dubček and the others were taken to Moscow on a Soviet military transport aircraft (reportedly one of the aircraft used in the Soviet invasion).</p>
<p>Despite the inspired non-violent resistance of the Czech and Slovak population, which delayed full loss of control to the Warsaw Pact forces for a full eight months (in contrast to the Soviet military's estimate of four days) and became a prime example of <a href="/wiki/Civilian-based_defense" title="Civilian-based defense">civilian-based defense</a>, the reformers ultimately were forced to accede to Soviet demands, signing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_protocols" title="Moscow protocols">Moscow protocols</a>. (Only <a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_Kriegel" title="František Kriegel">František Kriegel</a> refused to sign.)</p>
<p>Dubček and most of the reformers were returned to Prague on 27 August and Dubček retained his post as the party's first secretary for a while. Indeed, the achievements of the Prague Spring were not reversed overnight, but over a period of several months.</p>
<p>In January 1969, Dubček was hospitalized in Bratislava complaining of a cold and had to cancel a speech. Rumours sprang up that his illness was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_sickness" title="Radiation sickness">radiation sickness</a> and that it was caused by radioactive <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> being placed in his soup during his stay in Moscow in an attempt to kill him. However, a U.S. intelligence report discounted this for lack of evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dubček was forced to resign as First Secretary in April 1969, following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Hockey_Riots_(1969)" title="Czechoslovak Hockey Riots (1969)">Czechoslovak Hockey Riots</a>. He was re-elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Assembly_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Federal Assembly of Czechoslovakia">Federal Assembly</a> (as the federal parliament was now called) and became its Speaker. He was later sent as ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> (1969–70), allegedly in the hope that he would defect to the West, which however did not occur. In 1970, he was expelled from the Communist party and lost his seats in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Council_of_the_Slovak_Republic" title="National Council of the Slovak Republic">Slovak parliament</a> (which he had held continuously since 1964) and the Federal Assembly.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_citizen">Private citizen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Private citizen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After his expulsion from the party, Dubček worked in the Forestry Service in Slovakia. He remained a popular figure among the Slovaks and Czechs he encountered on the job, using this reverence to procure scarce and hard-to-find materials for his workplace. Dubček and his wife, Anna, continued to live in a comfortable villa in a nice neighbourhood in <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>. In 1988, Dubček was allowed to travel to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> to accept an honorary doctorate from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bologna_University" title="Bologna University">Bologna University</a>, and, while there, he gave an interview with Italian newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Unit%C3%A0" title="L'Unità">L'Unità</a></i>, his first public remarks to the press since 1970. Dubček's appearance and interview helped to return him to international prominence.</p>
<p>In 1989, he was awarded the annual <a href="/wiki/Sakharov_Prize" title="Sakharov Prize">Sakharov Prize</a> in its second year of existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Velvet_Revolution">Velvet Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Velvet Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1373" data-file-width="2006" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG/260px-Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG/390px-Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG/520px-Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek_1921-1992.JPG 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a> of 1989, he supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_against_Violence" title="Public against Violence">Public against Violence</a> (VPN) and the <a href="/wiki/Civic_Forum" title="Civic Forum">Civic Forum</a>. On the night of 24 November, Dubček appeared with <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Havel" title="Václav Havel">Václav Havel</a> on a balcony overlooking Wenceslas Square, where he was greeted with uproarious applause from the throngs of protesters below and embraced as a symbol of democratic freedom. Several onlookers even chanted, "Dubček na hrad!" ("Dubček to <a href="/wiki/Prague_Castle" title="Prague Castle">the Castle</a>"—i. e., Dubček for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_of_Czechoslovakia" title="President of Czechoslovakia">President</a>). He disappointed the crowd somewhat by calling the revolution a chance to continue the work he had started 20 years earlier, and prune out what was wrong with Communism.</p>
<p>By that time, the demonstrators in Prague wanted nothing to do with Communism of any sort, even the humane version represented by Dubček. Later that night, Dubček was on stage with Havel at the <a href="/wiki/Laterna_Magika" title="Laterna Magika">Laterna Magika</a> theatre, the headquarters of Civic Forum, when the entire leadership of the Communist Party resigned, in effect ending Communist rule in Czechoslovakia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dubček was elected Chairman of the Federal Assembly (the Czechoslovak Parliament) on 28 December 1989, and re-elected in 1990 and 1992.</p>
<p>At the time of the overthrow of Communist party rule, Dubček described the Velvet Revolution as a victory for his <i>humanistic socialist outlook</i>. In 1990, he received the International Humanist Award from the <a href="/wiki/International_Humanist_and_Ethical_Union" title="International Humanist and Ethical Union">International Humanist and Ethical Union</a>. He also gave the commencement address to the graduates of the Class of 1990 at The American University in Washington, D.C.; it was his first trip to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, he became leader of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Slovakia" title="Social Democratic Party of Slovakia">Social Democratic Party of Slovakia</a> and represented that party in the Federal Assembly. At that time, Dubček passively supported the union between Czechs and Slovaks in a single Czecho-Slovak federation against the (ultimately successful) <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Dissolution of Czechoslovakia">push towards an independent Slovak state</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Dubček died on 7 November 1992, as a result of injuries sustained in a car crash that took place on 1 September on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highway_D1_(Czech_Republic)" title="Highway D1 (Czech Republic)">Czech D1 highway</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Humpolec" title="Humpolec">Humpolec</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He was buried in <a href="/wiki/Sl%C3%A1vi%C4%8Die_%C3%BAdolie_cemetery" title="Slávičie údolie cemetery">Slávičie údolie cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>, Slovakia.</p>
