<p><b>Alexander Mikhailovich</b> "<b>Alex</b>" <b>Ovechkin</b> (<span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Александр Михайлович Овечкин</span>; <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Russian" title="Help:IPA for Russian">[ɐlʲɪˈksandr ɐˈvʲetɕkʲɪn]</a></span>; born September 17, 1985) is a Russian professional <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> <a href="/wiki/Winger_(ice_hockey)" title="Winger (ice hockey)">winger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Captain_(ice_hockey)" title="Captain (ice hockey)">captain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> (NHL). Prior to entering the NHL, Ovechkin played for <a href="/wiki/HC_Dynamo_Moscow" title="HC Dynamo Moscow">HC Dynamo Moscow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Superleague" title="Russian Superleague">Russian Superleague</a> for four seasons, from 2001 until 2005, and returned to play for them briefly during the <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_NHL_lockout" title="2012–13 NHL lockout">2012–13 NHL lockout</a>. A highly touted prospect, Ovechkin was selected by the Capitals first overall in the <a href="/wiki/2004_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2004 NHL Entry Draft">2004 NHL Entry Draft</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_NHL_lockout" title="2004–05 NHL lockout">2004–05 NHL lockout</a> cancelling the season, Ovechkin remained in Russia until 2005, joining the Capitals for the <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_NHL_season" title="2005–06 NHL season">2005–06 season</a>. He won the <a href="/wiki/Calder_Memorial_Trophy" title="Calder Memorial Trophy">Calder Memorial Trophy</a> as rookie of the year, scoring 52 goals and 54 assists to lead all rookies with 106 points and finishing third overall in league scoring.</p>
<p>Ovechkin has led the NHL in goal scoring, and won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_Richard_Trophy" title="Rocket Richard Trophy">Rocket Richard Trophy</a>, six times in his career. He first did so in <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_NHL_season" title="2007–08 NHL season">2007–08 season</a>, when he recorded 65 goals and 112 points. He led the league in points, winning the <a href="/wiki/Art_Ross_Trophy" title="Art Ross Trophy">Art Ross Trophy</a>, and also won the <a href="/wiki/Hart_Memorial_Trophy" title="Hart Memorial Trophy">Hart Memorial Trophy</a> as the league's most valuable player and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Lindsay_Award" title="Ted Lindsay Award">Lester B. Pearson Award</a> as the best player as voted on by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHL_Players%27_Association" title="NHL Players' Association">NHL Players' Association</a>. Ovechkin would again win the Hart Trophy and Pearson Award in <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NHL_season" title="2008–09 NHL season">2009</a>, along with the Richard Trophy, and won the Ted Lindsay Award (the renamed Pearson Award) for a third consecutive year in <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_NHL_season" title="2009–10 NHL season">2010</a>; it also marked the fifth straight year that he was named to the <a href="/wiki/NHL_All-Star_Team" title="NHL All-Star Team">First All-Star Team</a>. After a couple years of decreased scoring, Ovechkin again led the league in goals, and earning the Richard Trophy, in <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_NHL_season" title="2012–13 NHL season">2013</a>, again winning the Hart Trophy. He would repeat as the Richard Trophy winner from <a href="/wiki/2013%E2%80%9314_NHL_season" title="2013–14 NHL season">2014</a> to <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_NHL_season" title="2015–16 NHL season">2016</a>, scoring at least 50 goals each season, becoming the first player to win the award six times, and the third to lead the NHL in goals that many times, as well as the third player to score 50 goals in a season seven times. He marked 500 career NHL goals in the 2015–16 season and has also led the league in scoring for the past four years; as such, Ovechkin is considered by many to be one of the greatest goal scorers in the history of the NHL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Internationally Ovechkin has represented <a href="/wiki/Russia_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team" title="Russia men's national ice hockey team">Russia</a> in multiple tournaments. His first <a href="/wiki/International_Ice_Hockey_Federation" title="International Ice Hockey Federation">IIHF</a> tournament was the <a href="/wiki/2002_IIHF_World_U18_Championships" title="2002 IIHF World U18 Championships">2002 World U18 Championship</a>. The following <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Junior_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2003 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships">year</a> he made his debut at the <a href="/wiki/IIHF_World_U20_Championship" title="IIHF World U20 Championship">World Junior Championship</a>, helping Russia win the gold medal. He played two more years at the World Juniors, as well as once more at the World U18 Championships. Ovechkin's first senior tournament was the <a href="/wiki/2004_Men%27s_World_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2004 Men's World Ice Hockey Championships">2004 World Championship</a>, and he also played in the <a href="/wiki/2004_World_Cup_of_Hockey" title="2004 World Cup of Hockey">World Cup</a> that year. Ovechkin has also played for Russia at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Olympics" title="Winter Olympics">Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_2006_Winter_Olympics" title="Ice hockey at the 2006 Winter Olympics">2006</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_2010_Winter_Olympics" title="Ice hockey at the 2010 Winter Olympics">2010</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_2014_Winter_Olympics" title="Ice hockey at the 2014 Winter Olympics">2014</a>. Overall Ovechkin has represented Russia at eleven World Championships and three Olympics in his career, winning the World Championship three times.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alex Ovechkin is the son of Mikhail Ovechkin, a former professional soccer player, and <a href="/wiki/Tatyana_Ovechkina" title="Tatyana Ovechkina">Tatyana Ovechkina</a>, who won two Olympic gold medals while competing for the Soviet women's basketball team at the <a href="/wiki/1976_Summer_Olympics" title="1976 Summer Olympics">1976 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/1980_Summer_Olympics" title="1980 Summer Olympics">1980 Summer Olympics</a> in Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childhood_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Childhood-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HatTrick_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HatTrick-4">[4]</a></sup> The first sign of Ovechkin's future came when he was two years old—while in a toy store, he grabbed a toy <a href="/wiki/Hockey_stick" title="Hockey stick">hockey stick</a> and refused to let go. His parents treasure the picture to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childhood_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Childhood-3">[3]</a></sup> Whenever he saw a hockey game on TV, he "dropped all his toys" and ran to the TV, protesting if his parents tried to change the channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childhood_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Childhood-3">[3]</a></sup> His parents say they knew he would be an athlete when he chose to run up the steps to their 10th floor apartment instead of taking the elevator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They also encouraged him to be athletic, sending him out to play at nearby soccer fields and basketball courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childhood_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Childhood-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sergei, Ovechkin's older brother, had initially introduced him to hockey, and Alex enrolled in hockey school at the age of eight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childhood_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Childhood-3">[3]</a></sup> Soon after he began, however, he had to postpone his hockey career because his parents were unable to take him to the rink. But one of Ovechkin's coaches saw his talent and insisted to his parents that he should continue playing hockey.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="2009 (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sergei died in a car accident when Alex was ten. A childhood friend claims this is one of the reasons Ovechkin is so passionate on the ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Childhood_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Childhood-3">[3]</a></sup> He also has another older brother, Mikhail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biography_Today_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biography_Today-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamo_Moscow">Dynamo Moscow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dynamo Moscow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ovechkin began playing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Super_League" title="Russian Super League">Russian Super League</a> (RSL) in <a href="/wiki/HC_Dynamo_Moscow" title="HC Dynamo Moscow">Dynamo Moscow</a> at the age of 16. Making his professional debut in the 2001–02 season, he scored four points in 21 games. He would spend three seasons there prior to being drafted by the NHL, and he would rack up 36 goals and 32 assists in 152 career games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biography_Today.2C_pp.117_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biography_Today.2C_pp.117-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following off-season, Ovechkin was <a href="/wiki/List_of_first_overall_NHL_draft_picks" title="List of first overall NHL draft picks">selected first overall</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2004_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2004 NHL Entry Draft">2004 NHL Entry Draft</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a>. He had been projected as the first overall pick for nearly two years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draft_Prospect_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Draft_Prospect-8">[8]</a></sup> and had earned comparisons to <a href="/wiki/Mario_Lemieux" title="Mario Lemieux">Mario Lemieux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-Gazette_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post-Gazette-9">[9]</a></sup> He was so highly regarded that the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panthers" title="Florida Panthers">Florida Panthers</a> attempted to draft him in the <a href="/wiki/2003_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2003 NHL Entry Draft">2003 NHL Entry Draft</a> in the ninth round, even though his birthday was two days after the cut-off (September 15, 1985). <a href="/wiki/Rick_Dudley" title="Rick Dudley">Rick Dudley</a>, the general manager of the Panthers, claimed the pick was legitimate, claiming that Ovechkin was old enough with <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">leap years</a> taken into consideration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-Gazette_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Post-Gazette-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_NHL_lockout" title="2004–05 NHL lockout">2004–05 NHL lockout</a>, Ovechkin remained with Dynamo for one more season. He recorded 27 points in 34 games in 2004–05, while missing nearly two months of play because of a shoulder injury sustained in the gold medal game against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada_national_junior_hockey_team" title="Canada national junior hockey team">Canada</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2005_World_Junior_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2005 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships">2005 World Junior Championships</a>. In the playoffs, he helped Dynamo win the RSL title.</p>
<p>With the threat of the lockout cancelling another NHL season, Ovechkin signed a contract with rival Russian team <a href="/wiki/Avangard_Omsk" title="Avangard Omsk">Avangard Omsk</a>. In order to maintain his eligibility for the NHL in the event that the lockout ended, the contract contained an out clause with a July 20, 2005, deadline. Although a new NHL <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining_agreement" title="Collective bargaining agreement">collective bargaining agreement</a> (CBA) had not yet been reached between players and owners, Ovechkin decided to opt out and signed with the Capitals on August 5, 2005. The deal was a three-year, entry-level contract worth the rookie maximum of $984,200 per season with performance-based bonuses to inflate his annual salary to as much as $3.9 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington_Capitals">Washington Capitals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Washington Capitals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2005.E2.80.9306">2005–06</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2005–06">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Two days after signing, the lockout ended with a new CBA. Ovechkin played his first game with the Capitals on October 5, 2005, scoring two goals against goalie <a href="/wiki/Pascal_Leclaire" title="Pascal Leclaire">Pascal Leclaire</a> in a 3–2 victory over the <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Blue_Jackets" title="Columbus Blue Jackets">Columbus Blue Jackets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On January 13, 2006, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaheim" title="Anaheim">Anaheim</a>, Ovechkin scored his first career <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hat_trick#Hockey" title="Hat trick">hat trick</a> against <a href="/wiki/Jean-S%C3%A9bastien_Gigu%C3%A8re" title="Jean-Sébastien Giguère">Jean-Sébastien Giguère</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mighty_Ducks_of_Anaheim" title="Mighty Ducks of Anaheim">Mighty Ducks of Anaheim</a> to help Washington win the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HatTrick_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-HatTrick-4">[4]</a></sup> Three days later, on January 16, he scored a goal that veteran hockey reporter <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clement" title="Bill Clement">Bill Clement</a> called "one of the greatest goals of all time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Knocked down by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenix_Coyotes" title="Phoenix Coyotes">Phoenix Coyotes</a> defenseman <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mara" title="Paul Mara">Paul Mara</a> and sliding on his back facing away from the net, Ovechkin was able to hook the puck with one hand on his stick and slide it into the net past goalie <a href="/wiki/Brian_Boucher" title="Brian Boucher">Brian Boucher</a> for his second goal of the night. It became referred to as "The Goal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On February 1, Ovechkin was named NHL Rookie of the Month for January 2006 as well as being named Offensive Player of the Month, becoming only the third player in NHL history to earn both honors simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovechkin finished the 2005–06 season leading all NHL rookies in goals, points, power-play goals and shots. He finished third overall in the NHL in scoring with 106 points and tied for third in goals with 52. His 425 shots led the league, set an NHL rookie record and was the fourth-highest total in NHL history. Ovechkin's point total was the second-best in Washington Capitals history and his goals total tied for third in franchise history. He was also named to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHL_First_All-Star_Team" title="NHL First All-Star Team">NHL First All-Star Team</a>, the first rookie to receive the honor in 15 years. After the season ended, Ovechkin received the <a href="/wiki/Calder_Memorial_Trophy" title="Calder Memorial Trophy">Calder Memorial Trophy</a>, awarded to the NHL's best rookie.</p>
<p>He was also a finalist in his rookie season for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lester_B._Pearson_Award" title="Lester B. Pearson Award">Lester B. Pearson Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/EA_Sports" title="EA Sports">EA Sports</a> made him one of the cover athletes for <i><a href="/wiki/NHL_07" title="NHL 07">NHL 07</a></i>.</p>
<p>The following season, Ovechkin appeared in his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007_NHL_All-Star_Game" title="2007 NHL All-Star Game">NHL All-Star Game</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas</a> on January 24, 2007. He completed his second NHL season with 46 goals and 92 points.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2007.E2.80.9308">2007–08</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2007–08">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Playing in the final season of his rookie contract, in <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_NHL_season" title="2007–08 NHL season">2007–08</a>, Ovechkin signed a 13-year contract extension worth $124 million with the Capitals on January 10, 2008. The contract, which averages $9.5 million per year, was the richest in NHL history. Working without an agent, Ovechkin negotiated with Capitals owner <a href="/wiki/Ted_Leonsis" title="Ted Leonsis">Ted Leonsis</a> and former general manager <a href="/wiki/George_McPhee" title="George McPhee">George McPhee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13_year_contract_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-13_year_contract-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Late in the season, on March 3, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Ovechkin notched his 50th, 51st and 52nd goals of the campaign for his fourth career NHL hat trick and to hit the 50-goal mark for the second time in his career. Later that month, on March 21, 2008, Ovechkin scored his 59th and 60th goals of the season against the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Thrashers" title="Atlanta Thrashers">Atlanta Thrashers</a>, becoming the first NHL player to score 60 goals in a season since <a href="/wiki/Mario_Lemieux" title="Mario Lemieux">Mario Lemieux</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jarom%C3%ADr_J%C3%A1gr" title="Jaromír Jágr">Jaromír Jágr</a> in <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_NHL_season" title="1995–96 NHL season">1995–96</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reaches_60_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reaches_60-18">[18]</a></sup> and 19th player overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historical_60_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_60-19">[19]</a></sup> Four days later, on March 25, Ovechkin scored his 61st goal of the season to break the Washington Capitals' team record for goals in a single season previously held by <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Maruk" title="Dennis Maruk">Dennis Maruk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61st_Goal_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-61st_Goal-20">[20]</a></sup> He also went on to break <a href="/wiki/Luc_Robitaille" title="Luc Robitaille">Luc Robitaille</a>'s record for most goals by a left winger in one season on April 3, 2008, by scoring two goals for his 64th and 65th of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64_and_65_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-64_and_65-21">[21]</a></sup> He also became the first NHL player to score at least 40 even-strength goals in one season since <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Bure" title="Pavel Bure">Pavel Bure</a> in <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_NHL_season" title="1999–2000 NHL season">1999–2000</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leading the league in scoring with 65 goals and 112 points, Ovechkin captured both the <a href="/wiki/Art_Ross_Trophy" title="Art Ross Trophy">Art Ross Trophy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maurice_%22Rocket%22_Richard_Trophy" title='Maurice "Rocket" Richard Trophy'>Maurice "Rocket" Richard Trophy</a> in 2007–08. It was the first time in 41 seasons that a left-winger led the NHL in points since <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Hull" title="Bobby Hull">Bobby Hull</a> led the league with 97 points in <a href="/wiki/1965%E2%80%9366_NHL_season" title="1965–66 NHL season">1965–66</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovechkin helped lead a rejuvenated Capitals team back to the <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="Stanley Cup playoffs">Stanley Cup playoffs</a> with a stronger supporting cast that included countryman <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Semin" title="Alexander Semin">Alexander Semin</a>, rookie center <a href="/wiki/Nicklas_B%C3%A4ckstr%C3%B6m" title="Nicklas Bäckström">Nicklas Bäckström</a> and defenseman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Green_(ice_hockey_b._1985)" title="Mike Green (ice hockey b. 1985)">Mike Green</a>. He scored the game-winning goal in his NHL playoff debut with less than five minutes left in Game 1 against the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Flyers" title="Philadelphia Flyers">Philadelphia Flyers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He scored nine points in seven games against the Flyers as the Capitals were eliminated in the opening round.</p>
<p>In the off-season, Ovechkin was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lester_B._Pearson_Award" title="Lester B. Pearson Award">Lester B. Pearson Award</a> as the top player voted by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League_Players%27_Association" title="National Hockey League Players' Association">National Hockey League Players' Association</a> (NHLPA) and the <a href="/wiki/Hart_Memorial_Trophy" title="Hart Memorial Trophy">Hart Memorial Trophy</a> as the league's MVP, becoming the first player in the history of the NHL to win all four major awards, including the Art Ross and Rocket Richard Trophies. Ovechkin was also awarded his third consecutive <a href="/wiki/Kharlamov_Trophy" title="Kharlamov Trophy">Kharlamov Trophy</a>, named after Soviet hockey star <a href="/wiki/Valeri_Kharlamov" title="Valeri Kharlamov">Valeri Kharlamov</a> and presented by <i><a href="/wiki/Sovetsky_Sport" title="Sovetsky Sport">Sovetsky Sport</a></i> newspaper, as the best Russian NHL player as voted by other Russian NHL players.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.9309">2008–09</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2008–09">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In late October of the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NHL_season" title="2008–09 NHL season">2008–09 season</a>, Ovechkin returned home to Moscow to visit his ailing grandfather, missing only the second game of his career up to that point, snapping a consecutive streak of 203 games played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On February 5, 2009, Ovechkin scored his 200th goal, against the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Kings" title="Los Angeles Kings">Los Angeles Kings</a>, becoming only the fourth player in the NHL to reach the milestone in four seasons, joining <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Gretzky" title="Wayne Gretzky">Wayne Gretzky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Bossy" title="Mike Bossy">Mike Bossy</a> and Mario Lemieux.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On March 19, he scored his 50th goal of the season, becoming the first Washington Capitals player to reach the 50-goal mark three times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He finished the campaign with 56 goals to capture his second consecutive Rocket Richard Trophy, joining <a href="/wiki/Jarome_Iginla" title="Jarome Iginla">Jarome Iginla</a> and Pavel Bure as the third player to win the award twice and the second player after Bure (2000 and 2001) to win the award in back-to-back seasons. With 110 points, he finished as runner-up to countryman <a href="/wiki/Evgeni_Malkin" title="Evgeni Malkin">Evgeni Malkin</a> for the Art Ross.</p>
<p>Ovechkin and the Capitals repeated as division champions en route to meeting the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Rangers" title="New York Rangers">New York Rangers</a> in the opening round. After advancing to the second round in seven games, Ovechkin notched his first NHL playoff hat trick on May 4, 2009, in Game 2 against the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a> to help Washington to a 4–3 win. The Capitals were eventually defeated by Pittsburgh, the eventual Stanley Cup champions, in seven games. Ovechkin finished the 2009 playoffs with a post-season career-high 21 points in 14 games. He went on to win the Hart and Pearson Trophies for the second consecutive year, becoming the seventeenth player to win the Hart multiple times.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2009.E2.80.9310">2009–10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2009–10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Just over a month into the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_NHL_season" title="2009–10 NHL season">2009–10 season</a>, Ovechkin suffered an upper-body injury during a game against the <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Blue_Jackets" title="Columbus Blue Jackets">Columbus Blue Jackets</a> on November 1, 2009, after a collision with opposing forward <a href="/wiki/Raffi_Torres" title="Raffi Torres">Raffi Torres</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> After returning, Ovechkin was suspended by the NHL on December 1 for two games (one for the action, and one for a second game misconduct penalty during the season) for a knee-on-knee hit to <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Hurricanes" title="Carolina Hurricanes">Carolina Hurricanes</a> defenseman <a href="/wiki/Tim_Gleason" title="Tim Gleason">Tim Gleason</a> during a game the previous day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Both Gleason and Ovechkin had to be helped off the ice, although Gleason later returned during the game, while Ovechkin did not. Ovechkin was assessed a five-minute major penalty and a game misconduct at the time. Capitals Coach <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Boudreau" title="Bruce Boudreau">Bruce Boudreau</a> commented that Ovechkin's style of play was at times "reckless." The suspension was Ovechkin's first of his career, causing him to forfeit $98,844.16 in salary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p>On January 5, 2010, Ovechkin was named <a href="/wiki/Captain_(ice_hockey)" title="Captain (ice hockey)">captain</a> of the Washington Capitals after previous captain <a href="/wiki/Chris_Clark_(ice_hockey)" title="Chris Clark (ice hockey)">Chris Clark</a> was traded to the Columbus Blue Jackets. He became the first European, second-youngest and 14th overall captain in team history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 5, 2010, at a game against the New York Rangers, Ovechkin, with his second goal and third point of the game, reached the 500-point milestone of his NHL career. He is the fifth player to achieve the milestone in only five seasons, reaching it in 373 career games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 14, 2010, at a game against the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Blackhawks" title="Chicago Blackhawks">Chicago Blackhawks</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Center" title="United Center">United Center</a>, Ovechkin sent 'Hawks defenseman <a href="/wiki/Brian_Campbell" title="Brian Campbell">Brian Campbell</a> into the boards after Campbell had dumped the puck to the blue line. Ovechkin was called for boarding, receiving a five-minute major and a game misconduct,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and was suspended for two games (for a third game misconduct of the season, a two-game suspension is automatic).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Campbell suffered a fractured clavicle and fractured rib, and was expected to be out seven-to-eight weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovechkin won the 2009–10 <a href="/wiki/Ted_Lindsay_Award" title="Ted Lindsay Award">Ted Lindsay Award</a>, becoming only the second player in NHL history to win the award in three consecutive years. He also led the NHL in goals per game &amp; points per game for three straight seasons, from 2008 to 2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHL.com_.E2.80.93_Stats_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHL.com_.E2.80.93_Stats-36">[36]</a></sup> Ovechkin currently ranks third in Capitals history in goals (only <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bondra" title="Peter Bondra">Peter Bondra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Gartner" title="Mike Gartner">Mike Gartner</a> have tallied more goals) and is seventh in total points.</p>
<p>In 2009–10 Ovechkin surpassed the mark of <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a> <a href="/wiki/Hockey_Hall_of_Fame" title="Hockey Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a> goaltender <a href="/wiki/Bill_Durnan" title="Bill Durnan">Bill Durnan</a> (first four seasons from 1943–44 through 1946–47) and became the first player in NHL history voted a First Team All-Star in each of his first five seasons.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2010.E2.80.9316">2010–16</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010–16">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/2011_NHL_Winter_Classic" title="2011 NHL Winter Classic">2011</a>, Ovechkin and the Capitals took part in the New Year's Day <a href="/wiki/NHL_Winter_Classic" title="NHL Winter Classic">NHL Winter Classic</a>, facing the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a>. Ovechkin did not score any points, but the Capitals won 3–1. On March 8, 2011, in a 5–0 victory over the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Oilers" title="Edmonton Oilers">Edmonton Oilers</a>, Ovechkin recorded his 600th career point. On April 5, 2011, Ovechkin scored his 300th career goal, becoming the sixth-youngest and seventh-fastest player to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On December 12, 2011, Ovechkin registered his first NHL major fight against <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Dubinsky" title="Brandon Dubinsky">Brandon Dubinsky</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Rangers" title="New York Rangers">New York Rangers</a>.</p>
<p>On January 23, 2012, Ovechkin received a three-game suspension for a hit on <a href="/wiki/Zbyn%C4%9Bk_Mich%C3%A1lek" title="Zbyněk Michálek">Zbyněk Michálek</a> of the Pittsburgh Penguins. The following day, Ovechkin announced he would not attend the <a href="/wiki/59th_National_Hockey_League_All-Star_Game" title="59th National Hockey League All-Star Game">2012 NHL All-Star Game</a> due to the suspension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovechkin's numbers dipped in the 2011 and 2012 seasons, but in the 2013, lockout-shortened season, he led the NHL in goal-scoring with 32, earning him his third <a href="/wiki/Maurice_%22Rocket%22_Richard_Trophy" title='Maurice "Rocket" Richard Trophy'>Rocket Richard Trophy</a>. He combined his 32 goals with 24 assists, giving him 56 points, good for third most points in the League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> He was also awarded the Hart Memorial Trophy for the third time in his career. Ovechkin only scored two points in a first round exit of the <a href="/wiki/2013_Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="2013 Stanley Cup playoffs">2013 Stanley Cup playoffs</a> against the New York Rangers, during which he played with a hairline fracture in his foot.</p>
<p>On December 20, 2013, in a game against the <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Hurricanes" title="Carolina Hurricanes">Carolina Hurricanes</a>, Ovechkin scored his 400th career goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> He became the sixth fastest player to ever reach that mark, getting it in 634 games, one less than <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Bure" title="Pavel Bure">Pavel Bure</a>.</p>

<p>At the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/2013%E2%80%9314_NHL_season" title="2013–14 NHL season">2013–14 season</a>, Ovechkin had the strange distinction of winning the Rocket Richard Trophy, scoring 51 goals, while going −35, one of the League's worst, in the <a href="/wiki/Plus-minus" title="Plus-minus">plus-minus</a> stat. The Capitals, however, missed the playoffs for the first time since <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_NHL_season" title="2006–07 NHL season">2006–07</a>.</p>
<p>On November 4, 2014, in a game against the <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Flames" title="Calgary Flames">Calgary Flames</a>, Ovechkin recorded his 826th point, a franchise record, surpassing <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bondra" title="Peter Bondra">Peter Bondra</a>, who previously held the record with 825 points. The Flames, however, won the game 4–3 in overtime. On March 31, 2015, in a game against the <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Hurricanes" title="Carolina Hurricanes">Carolina Hurricanes</a>, Ovechkin scored his 50th goal of the year and became the sixth player in NHL history to have six 50-goal seasons, joining <a href="/wiki/Guy_Lafleur" title="Guy Lafleur">Guy Lafleur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Bossy" title="Mike Bossy">Mike Bossy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Gretzky" title="Wayne Gretzky">Wayne Gretzky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Dionne" title="Marcel Dionne">Marcel Dionne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mario_Lemieux" title="Mario Lemieux">Mario Lemieux</a>. On April 2, Ovechkin scored his 51st and 52nd goals of the season in a 5–4 shootout win versus the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a>, surpassing Bondra as the franchise leader in goals scored. It was also his 15th multi-goal game of the season, none of which were hat-tricks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2015–16 season, in the second period of a game vs the Toronto Maple Leafs, Ovechkin scored his 8th goal of the season to tie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Federov" title="Sergei Federov">Sergei Fedorov'</a>s tally for the most goals among Russian born players, with 483. On November 19, 2015, Ovechkin scored his ninth goal of the season in a 3–2 loss to the Dallas Stars; that goal broke Fedorov's record. On January 10, 2016, Ovechkin scored his 500th and 501st goals in a 7–1 victory over the Ottawa Senators, becoming the 43rd player to reach the 500-goal plateau, and the fifth-fastest player to do so, as well as the first Russian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On April 9, 2016, Ovechkin scored his 50th goal of the season and became the third player in NHL history to have seven or more 50-goal seasons.</p>
<p>During the 2015–16 season, Ovechkin, for the first time in his career, did not lead the Washington Capitals in points, although he still led the team in goals with 50, and finished seconnd on the team in points with 71, behind fellow countryman <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Evgeni_Kuznetsov" title="Evgeni Kuznetsov">Evgeni Kuznetsov</a>, who finished with 77.</p>
<p>In the second round of the 2016 Stanley Cup playoffs the Washington Capitals lost the series to the Pittsburgh Penguins in game 6 after a 4–3 overtime defeat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 11, 2017, Ovechkin scored his 1,000th career point. Ovechkin is the 37th person in NHL history to reach 1,000 points with only one team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the age of 16, Ovechkin helped lead the Junior National Team to the Gold medal with two hat tricks, one against Switzerland and one against USA, and an assist.</p>
<p>At the age of 17, when he was selected by Russian coach <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Tikhonov_(ice_hockey,_born_1930)" title="Viktor Tikhonov (ice hockey, born 1930)">Viktor Tikhonov</a> to play in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%8Cesk%C3%A1_Poji%C5%A1%C5%A5ovna_Cup" title="Česká Pojišťovna Cup">Česká Pojišťovna Cup</a> EuroTour tournament, Ovechkin became the youngest skater ever to play for the Russian national team. In that tournament, he also became the youngest player ever to score for the national team. He also was selected to play at the <a href="/wiki/2002_IIHF_World_U18_Championships" title="2002 IIHF World U18 Championships">2002 IIHF World U18 Championships</a>, which he amassed 14 goals and four assists in eight games, leading Russia to a silver medal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biography_Today.2C_pp.117_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biography_Today.2C_pp.117-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Ovechkin holds the record for most points scored in IIHF U18 World Championships with 31 points in 14 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the age of 18, Ovechkin was named captain of the junior Russian national team. Russia finished fifth in the tournament. In 2003, the team would go on to win a gold medal in the <a href="/wiki/IIHF_World_U20_Championship" title="IIHF World U20 Championship">IIHF World U20 Championship</a>.</p>
<p>At the age of 19, Ovechkin was named to the <a href="/wiki/Russia_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team" title="Russia men's national ice hockey team">Russian national team</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2004_World_Cup_of_Hockey" title="2004 World Cup of Hockey">2004 World Cup of Hockey</a>, making him the youngest player to play in the tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also at 19, Ovechkin was named captain of the junior team in the <a href="/wiki/2005_World_Junior_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2005 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships">2005 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships</a>. The tournament, lasting from December 25, 2004, to January 4, 2005, was Ovechkin's third and last. At the conclusion of the tournament, he had collected seven goals, tied for the tournament lead. His team received the silver medal after losing the gold medal game to Canada on January 4, and Ovechkin was named the Best Forward of the tournament as well as selected to the tournament All-Star Team. In 2005, Ovechkin played in his first <a href="/wiki/2005_Men%27s_World_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2005 Men's World Ice Hockey Championships">IIHF men's World Championships</a>. He scored five goals and three assists, landing eighth in the top scorers list and sharing third place in goal scoring.</p>
<p>In 2006, Ovechkin played in his first Winter Olympic Games. Although Russia came away from the games without a medal, Ovechkin scored five goals in the tournament, including the game-winner against Canada's <a href="/wiki/Martin_Brodeur" title="Martin Brodeur">Martin Brodeur</a>, eliminating Canada from the tournament. Ovechkin was the only player not on the Swedish (gold medal winners) or Finnish (silver medal winners) teams to be named to the all-tournament team.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2006_Men%27s_World_Ice_Hockey_Championships" title="2006 Men's World Ice Hockey Championships">2006 IIHF World Championships</a>, Ovechkin scored six goals and three assists (nine points) in seven games before Russia lost 4–3 to the Czech Republic in the quarter-finals. For his efforts, Ovechkin was one of six players selected to the Media All-Star Team.</p>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_IIHF_World_Championships" title="2008 IIHF World Championships">2008 IIHF World Championships</a>, Ovechkin helped lead Russia to the gold medal by finishing with 12 points (six goals, six assists) in nine games. He was selected to the Media All-Star Team for the second time in five tournament appearances.</p>
<p>In the 2010 Winter Olympics, Ovechkin and Team Russia were one of the favourites to win the Gold Medal. Despite high expectations, Russia lost to Canada 7–3 in the quarterfinals. Ovechkin finished with two goals and two assists in Russia's four games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>After being eliminated in the first round of the NHL playoffs, Ovechkin joined Team Russia for the <a href="/wiki/2010_IIHF_World_Championship" title="2010 IIHF World Championship">2010 IIHF World Championships</a> along with many other Russian stars, such as <a href="/wiki/Evgeni_Malkin" title="Evgeni Malkin">Evgeni Malkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Datsyuk" title="Pavel Datsyuk">Pavel Datsyuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Kovalchuk" title="Ilya Kovalchuk">Ilya Kovalchuk</a>. Despite being heavily favoured to win the tournament, Russia lost to the Czech Republic in the finals. This loss ended a disappointing year for Ovechkin on the international stage.</p>
<p>Ovechkin also joined the Russian team for the <a href="/wiki/2011_IIHF_World_Championship" title="2011 IIHF World Championship">2011 IIHF World Championships</a> after the Capitals were eliminated from the <a href="/wiki/2011_Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="2011 Stanley Cup playoffs">NHL playoffs</a>. He played in five games for the Russian team, but did not manage to score any points, the first time he failed to score any points in a World Championship tournament.</p>
<p>Ovechkin played in Russia's last three games of the <a href="/wiki/2012_IIHF_World_Championship" title="2012 IIHF World Championship">2012 IIHF World Championships</a>. He recorded two goals and two assists as Russia won the tournament.</p>
<p>Ovechkin also represented Russia in <a href="/wiki/2013_IIHF_World_Championship" title="2013 IIHF World Championship">2013 IIHF World Championships</a>. He joined the national team after Capitals were eliminated from 2013 Stanley Cup playoffs. Russia had already advanced to the first playoff round where they faced the U.S. The Americans defeated Russia 8–3, eliminating them from the tournament.</p>
<p>Ovechkin participated in the <a href="/wiki/2014_IIHF_World_Championship" title="2014 IIHF World Championship">2014 IIHF World Championships</a>. Russia won gold. He also joined the Russian team late in the <a href="/wiki/2015_IIHF_World_Championship" title="2015 IIHF World Championship">2015 IIHF World Championships</a>, where Russia won the silver medal.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Off_the_ice">Off the ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Off the ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The day after he received his first Hart Memorial Trophy as league MVP, he was given the key to the city by Washington Mayor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrian_M._Fenty" title="Adrian M. Fenty">Adrian M. Fenty</a> for being the first Washington MVP winner in a major sport since <a href="/wiki/Joe_Theismann" title="Joe Theismann">Joe Theismann</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington Redskins</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ovechkin was reportedly involved in a feud with <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a> forward <a href="/wiki/Evgeni_Malkin" title="Evgeni Malkin">Evgeni Malkin</a>, who was drafted second behind Ovechkin in the <a href="/wiki/2004_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2004 NHL Entry Draft">2004 NHL Entry Draft</a>. Though the two were reported to be good friends when they roomed together during the <a href="/wiki/2006_Winter_Olympics" title="2006 Winter Olympics">2006 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a>, Italy, this friendship quickly cooled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ovechkin.2FMalkin_Feud_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ovechkin.2FMalkin_Feud-55">[55]</a></sup> There is no definitive information on what caused the feud, but the most popular theory is that it began in August 2007, when Ovechkin supposedly punched Malkin's Russian agent, Gennady Ushakov, at a Moscow nightclub. Ovechkin has denied that version of events, while Malkin confirmed it, although he was not certain whether this was the precipitating event to the feud. The most notorious event took place on January 21, 2008, in Pittsburgh, when Ovechkin took a run at Malkin, which would have seemingly resulted in a devastating hit had Malkin not ducked out of the way just in time. The two would also not make eye contact at the 2008 NHL Awards Ceremony. Despite these incidents, Ovechkin has repeatedly denied "having it out" for Malkin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ovechkin.2FMalkin_Feud_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ovechkin.2FMalkin_Feud-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the feud raised many concerns as to its effect on the league,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ovechkin.2FMalkin_Feud_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ovechkin.2FMalkin_Feud-55">[55]</a></sup> and the Russian national team at the <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> it apparently dissipated as mysteriously as it started. On January 24, 2009, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHL_All-Star_Game_SuperSkills_Competition" title="NHL All-Star Game SuperSkills Competition">SuperSkills Competition</a>, Malkin assisted Ovechkin in his stunt during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHL_All-Star_Game_SuperSkills_Competition#Breakaway_Challenge" title="NHL All-Star Game SuperSkills Competition">Breakaway Challenge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Malkin handed Ovechkin his props for the stunt as well as handing him his stick and pouring some sports drink down Ovechkin's throat. Though there is no final word on the nature and status of the feud, considering their past interactions, this incident appears to show that the feud has effectively ended. It has been reported that <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Kovalchuk" title="Ilya Kovalchuk">Ilya Kovalchuk</a>, who was then the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Thrashers" title="Atlanta Thrashers">Atlanta Thrashers</a>' captain and is also teammate of Ovechkin and Malkin on the Russian national team, brokered the peace between the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<p>On January 24, 2009, Ovechkin won the Breakaway Competition at the SuperSkills Competition for the second consecutive year in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> after emerging in the final few seconds wearing a hat bestowed with a Canadian flag and white sunglasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> On January 25, 2009, Ovechkin scored one goal and notched two assists, as well as scoring the game-ending shootout goal in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2009_NHL_All-Star_Game" title="2009 NHL All-Star Game">2009 NHL All-Star Game</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Conference_(NHL)" title="Eastern Conference (NHL)">Eastern Conference</a> won 12–11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late in the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NHL_season" title="2008–09 NHL season">2008–09 season</a>, Ovechkin garnered some criticism over his exuberant after-goal celebrations. In the February 28, 2009, segment of <i><a href="/wiki/Hockey_Night_in_Canada" title="Hockey Night in Canada">Hockey Night in Canada</a>'</i>s Coach's Corner, Canadian hockey analyst <a href="/wiki/Don_Cherry" title="Don Cherry">Don Cherry</a> likened Ovechkin's celebrations of jumping into the boards and his teammates to that of soccer players, concluding that this was not the Canadian way and advising Canadian kids to ignore Ovechkin's example. Capitals coach <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Boudreau" title="Bruce Boudreau">Bruce Boudreau</a> came to Ovechkin's defense, stating Cherry "doesn't know Alex like we know Alex", and Ovechkin himself stated that he "doesn't care" about Cherry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The next notable incident happened on March 19, 2009, in a game against the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a>. After scoring his 50th goal of the season, Ovechkin put his stick on the ice, pretending to warm his hands over it because it was "hot." The incident sparked an immediate response from Tampa Bay coach <a href="/wiki/Rick_Tocchet" title="Rick Tocchet">Rick Tocchet</a>, who said that "[Ovechkin] went down a notch in my books." Boudreau had also stated that he would discuss the incident with Ovechkin, and teammate <a href="/wiki/Mike_Green_(ice_hockey,_born_1985)" title="Mike Green (ice hockey, born 1985)">Mike Green</a>, despite being the first to celebrate with Ovechkin afterwards, commented that he did not wish to join in the pre-meditated celebration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Ovechkin himself was unapologetic, and said about Don Cherry in particular, "He's going to be pissed off for sure...I love it!."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovechkin is the cover athlete of <a href="/wiki/2K_Sports" title="2K Sports">2K Sports</a> hockey simulation video game <i><a href="/wiki/NHL_2K10" title="NHL 2K10">NHL 2K10</a></i>, as well as the cover athlete of <a href="/wiki/EA_Sports" title="EA Sports">EA Sports</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/NHL_07" title="NHL 07">NHL 07</a></i>. On June 11, 2008, Ovechkin launched his own line of designer <a href="/wiki/Streetwear" title="Streetwear">streetwear</a> with <a href="/wiki/CCM_(ice_hockey)" title="CCM (ice hockey)">CCM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> On July 6, 2009, Ovechkin was named an ambassador for the <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">Sochi</a>, Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In late 2009, he was named <i><a href="/wiki/GQ" title="GQ">GQ</a>'</i>s 48th most powerful person in Washington, D.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2010–11 season, Ovechkin has been featured in one of <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/This_is_SportsCenter" title="This is SportsCenter">This is SportsCenter</a></i> commercials, in which he laughed off a question by ESPN personality <a href="/wiki/Steve_Levy" title="Steve Levy">Steve Levy</a> accusing him of being a Russian spy before being pulled upward by a line through an open ceiling tile by countryman and then-Capitals teammate <a href="/wiki/Semyon_Varlamov" title="Semyon Varlamov">Semyon Varlamov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2011, Ovechkin's agent made an announcement that he would no longer be endorsed by CCM and had made the move to <a href="/wiki/Bauer_Hockey" title="Bauer Hockey">Bauer Hockey</a>. This was a major move in Ovechkin's career, as he had used CCM most of his career. As his point production went down in the 2010–11 season, he made the decision to switch to Bauer. His current equipment includes a Bauer Supreme 1S stick, Bauer Vapor APX2 Pro gloves, Bauer Re-Akt Helmet and Bauer Vapor 1X skates.</p>
<p>Ovechkin is a dedicated car enthusiast, owning many fine automobiles, such as a Mercedes-Benz SL65 AMG Black Series and a custom Mercedes S63 AMG. At the 2015 NHL All Star game, Ovechkin lobbied Honda for a new car, and brought an element of fun silliness to the "draft" where he was chosen third to last; the last two players selected, <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Nugent-Hopkins" title="Ryan Nugent-Hopkins">Ryan Nugent-Hopkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Filip_Forsberg" title="Filip Forsberg">Filip Forsberg</a>, each received a new car, but Ovechkin would not give up. When Honda representatives asked his agent why he wanted a car so badly, they were told that he planned to donate it to the American Special Hockey Association, and at the end of the event, he was handed the keys to a new Honda Accord. That Accord will be auctioned off, and the proceeds used to benefit the charity Ovechkin highlighted and brought attention to with his antics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovechkin was formerly engaged to <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> player <a href="/wiki/Maria_Kirilenko" title="Maria Kirilenko">Maria Kirilenko</a>. On July 21, 2014, Kirilenko announced that the wedding was called off and that two were no longer seeing each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> On September 11, 2015, Ovechkin announced via Instagram his engagement to Nastya Shubskaya<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> whom he subsequently married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NHL_records">NHL records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ovechkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: NHL records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>First player to win the <a href="/wiki/Art_Ross_Trophy" title="Art Ross Trophy">Art Ross Trophy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurice_Richard_Trophy" title="Maurice Richard Trophy">Maurice Richard Trophy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lester_B._Pearson_Award" title="Lester B. Pearson Award">Lester B. Pearson Award</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hart_Memorial_Trophy" title="Hart Memorial Trophy">Hart Memorial Trophy</a> as well as win all four in a single season (Note: The Maurice Richard Trophy was established for the 1998–99 season).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
<li>Only player to be named to the NHL First All-Star Team in each of his first five seasons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></li>
<li>Most <a href="/wiki/Goal_(ice_hockey)" title="Goal (ice hockey)">goals</a> scored by a left-<a href="/wiki/Winger_(ice_hockey)" title="Winger (ice hockey)">winger</a> in a season (<a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_NHL_season" title="2007–08 NHL season">2007–08</a>) – 65 goals</li>
<li>Most <a href="/wiki/Point_(ice_hockey)" title="Point (ice hockey)">points</a> scored by a left-wing rookie (<a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_NHL_season" title="2005–06 NHL season">2005–06</a>) – 106 points</li>
<li>Most shots on goal by a left-winger in a season (2008–09) – 528 shots</li>
<li>Most shots on goal by a rookie in a season (2005–06) – 425 shots</li>
<li>Point streak in consecutive games to start an NHL career by a No. 1 overall pick (2005–06) – 8 games</li>
<li>Most regular season points by a Russian-born NHL rookie (2005–06) – 106 points</li>
<li>Fastest overtime goal – 6 seconds; (December 15, 2006 vs. <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Thrashers" title="Atlanta Thrashers">Atlanta Thrashers</a>; tied with <a href="/wiki/Mats_Sundin" title="Mats Sundin">Mats Sundin</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Legwand" title="David Legwand">David Legwand</a>)</li>
<li>Only player to be named to both the NHL First and Second All-Star Teams in the same season: <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_NHL_season" title="2012–13 NHL season">2012–13</a></li>
<li>Most goals by a Russian-born player – 525</li>
</ul>
