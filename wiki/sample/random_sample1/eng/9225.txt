<p><b>Aleksei Innokentievich Antonov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Алексе́й Инноке́нтьевич Анто́нов</span>) (9 September 1896 – 16 June 1962) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_of_the_Army" title="General of the Army">General</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Army" title="Soviet Army">Soviet Army</a>, awarded the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Victory" title="Order of Victory">Order of Victory</a> for his efforts in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aleksei_Antonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Grodno" title="Grodno">Grodno</a> in a family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kryashen" title="Kryashen">Kryashen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> ethnicity as the son of an artillery officer of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Army" title="Imperial Russian Army">Imperial Russian Army</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Antonov graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frunze_Military_Academy" title="Frunze Military Academy">Frunze Military Academy</a> in 1921 and joined the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>. He became an instructor at Frunze Military Academy in 1938.</p>
<p>In 1941, Antonov became Chief of Staff for the Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Southwestern_Front" title="Soviet Southwestern Front">Southwestern Front</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Southern_Front" title="Soviet Southern Front">Southern Front</a>. In December 1942 he became Deputy Chief General Staff of the combined Soviet forces and Head of the Operations Directorate, a pivotal role within the <a href="/wiki/Stavka" title="Stavka">Stavka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His duty was to liaise with other officers and inform Stalin of the military situation. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Overy" title="Richard Overy">Richard Overy</a> writes of him at this time:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Vasilevsky" title="Aleksandr Vasilevsky">Vasilevsky</a> came to rely on one subordinate above all others: the chief of operations, General Aleksei Antonov. [...] On December 11 [1942] the forty-six-year-old Antonov stepped into the role which was more directly exposed to Stalin's inquisitive leadership than any other. Antonov rose to the challenge. Instead of rushing off to report to Stalin when he arrived in Moscow, he spent the first week familiarizing himself thoroughly with the General Staff and the state of the front. Only when he was fully primed did he go to see his commander. The two men developed the most effective working relationship of the war. Antonov displayed a calm intelligence married to a massive energy and exceptional industry. According to his deputy, General <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Shtemenko" title="Sergei Shtemenko">Sergei Shtemenko</a>, Antonov never lost his temper or allowed circumstances to get the better of him. He was firm, caustic, slow to praise and a tough taskmaster, but the rigorous regimen that he imposed on his staff won their respect. Above all he was adept at manipulating Stalin. He did not sugar-coat his reports. He was prepared to stand up to Stalin with what his deputy regarded as a 'brave outspokenness'. So skilled was he at providing the evening situation reports concisely and accurately that even Zhukov bowed to his capability and allowed Antonov to present them in his place. The trust that Stalin came to place in Antonov was reflected in his survivability. He retained his office until February 1945, when he was made chief of staff in Vasilevsky's place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>By 1944 Antonov was Chief Spokesman and was present at both the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam</a> Conferences. At the Yalta Conference he briefed the Western Allies on co-ordinating military actions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and by stressing how the Allies could aid Soviets through bombing lines of communications contributed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">Dresden</a> raid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war Antonov became Deputy Commander-in-Chief and then Commander-in-Chief of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcaucasus_Military_District" title="Transcaucasus Military District">Transcaucasus Military District</a>. In 1955, he became Chief of Staff of the Combined Forces of the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a>. He held this post until his death in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aleksei_Antonov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1956, Antonov married ballerina <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olga_Vasiliyevna_Lepeshinskaya" title="Olga Vasiliyevna Lepeshinskaya">Olga Vasiliyevna Lepeshinskaya</a>. It was her second marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
