<p><b>Alexei Konstantinovich Yagudin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Ru-Alexei_Konstantinovich_Yagudin.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/69/Ru-Alexei_Konstantinovich_Yagudin.ogg" title="Ru-Alexei Konstantinovich Yagudin.ogg"><span class="unicode" lang="ru" style="white-space: normal;">Алексей Константинович Ягудин​</span></a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Ru-Alexei_Konstantinovich_Yagudin.ogg" title="File:Ru-Alexei Konstantinovich Yagudin.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>; 18 March 1980) is a former <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">figure skater</a>. His major achievements in his six years of eligible sports career include being the <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating_at_the_2002_Winter_Olympics" title="Figure skating at the 2002 Winter Olympics">2002 Olympic champion</a>, a four-time <a href="/wiki/World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="World Figure Skating Championships">World champion</a> (1998, 1999, 2000, 2002), a three-time <a href="/wiki/European_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="European Figure Skating Championships">European champion</a> (1998, 1999, 2002), a two-time <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final" title="Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">Grand Prix Final champion</a> (1998–1999, 2001–2002), a <a href="/wiki/World_Junior_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="World Junior Figure Skating Championships">World Junior champion</a> (1996) and a two-time <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="World Professional Figure Skating Championships">World Professional champion</a> (1998, 2002).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_overview">Career overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexei_Yagudin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexei_Yagudin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexei Yagudin was introduced to skating at age four by his mother, Zoya, who saw the activity as a way to improve his health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbm98_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jbm98-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gs41202_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gs41202-2">[2]</a></sup> He learned all his double jumps before age ten, the five triple jumps before age twelve, and the triple <a href="/wiki/Axel_jump" title="Axel jump">Axel jump</a> before he turned thirteen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autobiography_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autobiography-3">[3]</a></sup> His first coach was Alexander Mayorov, and then he was introduced to the famous Russian coach <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Mishin" title="Alexei Mishin">Alexei Mishin</a> when Mayorov moved to Sweden in 1992. Yagudin trained in Mishin's group from 1992 to 1998. He began competing at the international level in 1994, and won the <a href="/wiki/1996_World_Junior_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="1996 World Junior Figure Skating Championships">1996 World Junior Championships</a>. The famous rivalry with fellow Russian skater <a href="/wiki/Evgeni_Plushenko" title="Evgeni Plushenko">Evgeni Plushenko</a> began when they trained in Mishin's group, and intensified after Yagudin left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Senior_career">Senior career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexei_Yagudin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Senior career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, Yagudin competed in the <a href="/wiki/1997_World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="1997 World Figure Skating Championships">World Championships</a> for the first time and won a bronze medal.</p>
<p>In 1998, Yagudin led a Russian sweep of the medals at the <a href="/wiki/1998_European_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="1998 European Figure Skating Championships">1998 European Championships</a> with <a href="/wiki/Evgeni_Plushenko" title="Evgeni Plushenko">Evgeni Plushenko</a> in second and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Abt" title="Alexander Abt">Alexander Abt</a> in third. Later that year, he competed at the <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating_at_the_1998_Winter_Olympics" title="Figure skating at the 1998 Winter Olympics">1998 Nagano Winter Olympics</a> despite a severe case of pneumonia, and finished in 5th place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbm98_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-jbm98-1">[1]</a></sup> A month later, he won the <a href="/wiki/1998_World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="1998 World Figure Skating Championships">1998 World Championships</a>. He became the first Russian single skater from the post-Soviet era to win the World title. He was the second-youngest male World Champion at the age of 18 years and 15 days, 6 days older than <a href="/wiki/Donald_McPherson_(figure_skater)" title="Donald McPherson (figure skater)">Donald McPherson</a> in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-6">[6]</a></sup> About two months after the event, Yagudin left Mishin and joined <a href="/wiki/Tatiana_Tarasova" title="Tatiana Tarasova">Tatiana Tarasova</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio0001_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio0001-7">[7]</a></sup> who would coach him until his retirement in 2003.</p>
<p>In the 1998–99 season, Yagudin won eleven out of the thirteen competitions in which he participated, which included the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Browning" title="Kurt Browning">Kurt Browning</a> in the <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="World Professional Figure Skating Championships">World Professional Championships</a>, and winning the <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final" title="1998–99 Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">Grand Prix Final</a>. He claimed his second consecutive <a href="/wiki/2000_European_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2000 European Figure Skating Championships">2000 European</a> title over both Plushenko and former Olympic champion <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Urmanov" title="Alexei Urmanov">Alexei Urmanov</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/2000_World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2000 World Figure Skating Championships">2000 World Championships</a>, he successfully defended his World title against Plushenko. It was his second consecutive World title.</p>
<p>Yagudin struggled at the beginning of the 1999–2000 season. He was forced to withdraw from the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final" title="1999–2000 Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">1999–2000 Grand Prix Final</a> due to a knee injury, and then lost to Plushenko at the Russian Nationals and <a href="/wiki/2000_European_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2000 European Figure Skating Championships">2000 European Championships</a>. He recovered and won the <a href="/wiki/2000_World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2000 World Figure Skating Championships">2000 World Championships</a>, his third consecutive World title.</p>
<p>Yagudin's 2000–01 season was marred by injuries and inconsistency. He lost to Plushenko at the <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final" title="2000–01 Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">2000–01 Grand Prix Final</a>, Russian Championships, and the <a href="/wiki/2001_European_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2001 European Figure Skating Championships">2001 European Championships</a>. A foot injury sustained shortly before the <a href="/wiki/2001_World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2001 World Figure Skating Championships">2001 World Championships</a> led to a disastrous performance in the qualifying round. He stood in fifth place in his group before the short program. He staged a comeback with a stunning performance of his short program <i>The Revolutionary Etude</i>, winning a standing ovation and compliments of 'It was all about heart and guts'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He went on to win the silver medal.</p>
<p>Yagudin started the 2001–02 Olympic season with a third-place finish at the <a href="/wiki/Goodwill_Games" title="Goodwill Games">Goodwill Games</a> in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gs010908_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gs010908-9">[9]</a></sup> He altered his training regimen as a result, and then enjoyed the best season in his career. He defeated Plushenko at the <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating_Final" title="2001–02 Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final">2001–02 Grand Prix Final</a> and regained his <a href="/wiki/2002_European_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2002 European Figure Skating Championships">European</a> title. At the <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating_at_the_2002_Winter_Olympics" title="Figure skating at the 2002 Winter Olympics">2002 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, Yagudin won the men's event, receiving first-place votes from every judge throughout the competition. He received four 6.0 scores for his long program. Yagudin's perfect marks are the most for an Olympic performance since <a href="/wiki/Jayne_Torvill" title="Jayne Torvill">Jayne Torvill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Dean" title="Christopher Dean">Christopher Dean</a>'s free dance in 1984. and set a record for a men's skater in the Olympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Yagudin went on to win his fourth <a href="/wiki/2002_World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="2002 World Figure Skating Championships">World</a> title after the Olympics, and earned received six perfect 6.0s for his short program and another two for his free skating at the competition. He became the first singles skater to receive six perfect marks for the short program, including the first ever perfect mark for required elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gs41202_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-gs41202-2">[2]</a></sup> This record cannot be equaled or broken because the <a href="/wiki/International_Skating_Union" title="International Skating Union">International Skating Union</a> introduced its <a href="/wiki/ISU_Judging_System" title="ISU Judging System">new scoring system</a> after the 2003 season.</p>
<p>Alexei was diagnosed with a congenital hip disorder after the Olympic season. He was advised by doctors to stay off the ice for several months. Yagudin chose not to follow this advice and competed at <a href="/wiki/2002_Skate_America" title="2002 Skate America">2002 Skate America</a>. He won the short program, but had to withdraw due to his injury before the free skating. Yagudin later announced his retirement from competitive skating. His final performance as an eligible skater came during a farewell gala at Skate Canada with a performance of a new program, <i>Memorial</i>, and his short program from the previous season, <i>Racing</i>.</p>
<p>He was awarded with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_for_the_Fatherland" title="Order of Merit for the Fatherland">Order of Merit for the Fatherland</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a> in 2003.</p>
<p>He never won the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="Russian Figure Skating Championships">Russian Championships</a> despite his many other achievements, mainly because of <a href="/wiki/Evgeni_Plushenko" title="Evgeni Plushenko">his rival</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_career">Professional career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexei_Yagudin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Professional career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yagudin then turned professional in 2003, touring with <a href="/wiki/Stars_on_Ice" title="Stars on Ice">Stars on Ice</a> and Ice Symphony in Russia.</p>
<p>In 2004, Yagudin toured with <i>Stars on Ice</i> for the second year in a row. He also worked with the French figure skater <a href="/wiki/Brian_Joubert" title="Brian Joubert">Brian Joubert</a> as a consultant coach. In November he won two professional competitions with two new programs, <i>The Feeling Begins</i> (music by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a>) and <i>Moon Over Bourbon Street</i> (music by <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a>). The next year, he continued with the Stars on Ice tour and his <i>Passion</i> program was choreographed with a difficult acrobatic routine that took place seven meters up in the air. Since returning to his hometown of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> in 2005, Yagudin has skated in various Russian ice shows and took part in the Russian TV show <i>Stars on Ice</i>, later renamed <i>Ice Age</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, after a full Olympic cycle since Salt Lake City, Yagudin performed his famous <i>Winter</i> program on tour and a new program <i>Sway</i> (music by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pussycat_Dolls" title="Pussycat Dolls">Pussycat Dolls</a>). In fall he took part in the Russian TV show <i>Stars on Ice</i> having a former gymnast, Oksana Pushkina, as his partner.</p>
<p>In 2007, Yagudin first toured in the U.S. with the <i>Stars on Ice</i>, and then toured in Russia. He skated a comic number <i>Blues for Klook</i> and a flamenco number <i>Legenda</i>. In July 2007, Yagudin underwent surgery to have a titanium hip joint implanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-in102507_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-in102507-13">[13]</a></sup> In August, Yagudin announced that he intended to return to eligible sports after more than four years of competing as a professional skater. His former coach <a href="/wiki/Tatiana_Tarasova" title="Tatiana Tarasova">Tatiana Tarasova</a> and former choreographer <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Morozov_(figure_skater)" title="Nikolai Morozov (figure skater)">Nikolai Morozov</a> agreed to coach him should he return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap0708_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap0708-14">[14]</a></sup> However, Yagudin suffered another injury while on tour in November 2007. Afterward he stated that returning to competitive skating would be too difficult under the circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-in080606_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-in080606-15">[15]</a></sup> He later realized that a return to eligible skating would not be feasible, and continued his professional career, taking part in the Russian TV show again, which was renamed <i>Ice Age</i>. This time he was paired with a pop singer <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Dayneko" title="Victoria Dayneko">Victoria Dayneko</a> with whom he also recorded a song <i>Needle</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, Yagudin finished the <i>Ice Age</i> tour and then made his debut on the stage in a theater play where he played a Russian President. His career as an actor continued with getting one of the main roles in a Russian TV series about figure skating <i>My Hot Ice</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In fall he participated in the second season of <i>Ice Age</i> partnered with actress Valeria Lanskaya.</p>
<p>In 2009, Yagudin performed regularly on the <i>Ice Age</i> tour. He also adventured into a popular TV show <i>Good evening, Moscow!</i> as a host.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In fall he participated the third season of <i>Ice Age</i>, still paired with Valeria Lanskaya.</p>
<p>In 2010, Yagudin completed his third <i>Ice Age</i> tour. In June he skated in the <i>Supermatch: Medalist on Ice</i> show in Korea, performing <i>Sway</i> and <i>Winter</i>. On September 4, he participated in the <i>Artistry on Ice</i> show in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>. During the show, the wedding ceremony of the famous Chinese pair skaters <a href="/wiki/Shen_Xue" title="Shen Xue">Shen Xue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Hongbo" title="Zhao Hongbo">Zhao Hongbo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010 Olympic champions</a> was held. As one of the invited guests he gave his blessing to the couple and performed <i>Winter</i> and <i>Sway</i> afterward. It was his first visit to China.</p>
<p>In 2011, Yagudin told an interviewer that due to the hip replacement surgery he had undergone, he is no longer able to do all his triple jumps. He continues to perform his popular <i>Winter</i> program in shows around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AS2011_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-AS2011-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexei_Yagudin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alexei Yagudin was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad" title="Leningrad">Leningrad</a>, (now <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>), Russia. His parents divorced when he was young and he grew up as the only child of a single mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autobiography_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autobiography-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yagudin moved to the United States in 1999 to train with <a href="/wiki/Tatiana_Tarasova" title="Tatiana Tarasova">Tatiana Tarasova</a>. Later that year the <a href="/wiki/Champions_on_Ice" title="Champions on Ice">Champions on Ice</a> tour dismissed him because of his alleged excessive drinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt990612_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt990612-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reut000105_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-reut000105-21">[21]</a></sup> He lived in the United States for almost seven years.</p>
<p>Yagudin underwent hip surgery after touring with <i>Stars on Ice</i>. He assisted Tarasova with coaching over summer and early fall until his arrest for Driving While Intoxicated in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dui_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dui-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>He published his autobiography, <i>Alexei Yagudin: Overcome</i>, in Japan in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It was published in Russia in 2007 under the title, <i>НаPRолом</i>, with extra chapters and photos added to cover his recent life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 2, 2008, Yagudin's car was stolen with one of his World Championships gold medals in it. The medal and car were never located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>His fiancée, Olympic pair skating champion <a href="/wiki/Tatiana_Totmianina" title="Tatiana Totmianina">Tatiana Totmianina</a> gave birth to his first child, a daughter named Elizaveta ("Liza"), on November 20, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-child_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-child-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interviewnov2010_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-interviewnov2010-27">[27]</a></sup> They also have a <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Terrier" title="Yorkshire Terrier">Yorkshire Terrier</a> named Varia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interviewnov2010_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-interviewnov2010-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yagudin stated that he and Totmianina do not want Liza to become a competitive skater, and hope she will concentrate on studying and music as she grows up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AS2011_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-AS2011-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 20, 2015, it was announced that Totmianina was pregnant with the couple's second child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On October 2, 2015, the couple's second daughter, Michèle, was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Yagudin joined a Russian campaign to promote healthy lifestyles. He took part in free physical trainings held in Moscow, Saint Petersburg, Rostov-on-Don, Ekaterinburg, Samara, Kazan and Novosibirsk. He stated, "I would like to achieve through this campaign at least the understanding of people that 30 or 40 minutes of their day can improve their health now and in the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-healthcampaign_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-healthcampaign-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
