<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alexey Troitsky</h1>

<p><b>Alexey Alexeyevich Troitsky</b>, or <b>Alexei</b>, <b>Troitzky</b>, or <b>Troitzki</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Алексе́й Алексе́евич Тро́ицкий</span>) (March 14, 1866–August 1942) is considered to have been one of the greatest <a href="/wiki/Chess_composer" title="Chess composer">composers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endgame_studies" title="Endgame studies">endgame studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is widely regarded as the founder of the modern art of composing chess studies (<a href="#CITEREFSeirawan2003">Seirawan 2003</a>:91). Troitsky died of <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leningrad" title="Siege of Leningrad">siege of Leningrad</a>, where his notes were destroyed.</p>
<p>One of his most famous works involves analyzing the <a href="/wiki/Chess_endgame" title="Chess endgame">endgame</a> with two knights versus a pawn, see <a href="/wiki/Two_knights_endgame#Troitzky_line" title="Two knights endgame">Troitsky line</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Nunn" title="John Nunn">John Nunn</a> analyzed this endgame with an <a href="/wiki/Endgame_tablebase" title="Endgame tablebase">endgame tablebase</a> and stated that "the analysis of Troitsky ... is astonishingly accurate" (<a href="#CITEREFNunn1995">Nunn 1995</a>:265).</p>

The main line goes:</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>1. Nb6! Qe8</b></dd>
<dd><b>2. Nd7! Kc4</b></dd>
<dd><b>3. Qxc7+ Kb4</b></dd>
<dd><b>4. Qc5+ Kb3</b></dd>
<dd><b>5. Qc3+ Ka4</b></dd>
<dd><b>6. Qd4+ Ka3</b></dd>
<dd><b>7. Nc5 Qb8</b></dd>
<dd><b>8. Qa1+ Kb4</b></dd>
<dd><b>9. Na6+</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>and White wins (<a href="#CITEREFChernev1989">Chernev 1989</a>:207–8).</p>
