<p>The <b>Alentejo</b> (<small>Portuguese pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[ɐlẽˈtɛʒu]</a></span>) is the south-central part of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.</p>
<p>The greater region is defined within Portugal by the land bordering the left bank of the <a href="/wiki/Tagus" title="Tagus">Tagus</a> river to the North and extending to the South where it borders the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> region. The origin of its name, "além" + "Tejo" combined as Alentejo, literally translates to "Beyond-the-Tagus" or "Across-the-Tagus". However, a large portion of <a href="/wiki/Lez%C3%ADria_do_Tejo" title="Lezíria do Tejo">Lezíria do Tejo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ribatejo_Province" title="Ribatejo Province">Ribatejo Province</a> is located on the right margin of the Tagus River. The <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a> proper is all located beyond the left margin of the Tagus River.</p>
<p>Its main cities are <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vora" title="Évora">Évora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvas" title="Elvas">Elvas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portalegre,_Portugal" title="Portalegre, Portugal">Portalegre</a> (Upper Alentejo), <a href="/wiki/Beja,_Portugal" title="Beja, Portugal">Beja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moura,_Portugal" title="Moura, Portugal">Moura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serpa" title="Serpa">Serpa</a> (Lower Alentejo), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sines,_Portugal" title="Sines, Portugal">Sines</a> (Coastal Alentejo), <a href="/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Portugal" title="Santarém, Portugal">Santarém</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rio_Maior" title="Rio Maior">Rio Maior</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lez%C3%ADria_do_Tejo" title="Lezíria do Tejo">Lezíria do Tejo</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribatejo" title="Ribatejo">Ribatejo</a>).</p>
<p>It is one of the more recent seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regions_of_Portugal" title="Regions of Portugal">Regions of Portugal</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">NUTS</a> II subdivisions). Today, <a href="/wiki/Lez%C3%ADria_do_Tejo" title="Lezíria do Tejo">Lezíria do Tejo</a> subregion, formerly belonging to <a href="/wiki/Lisboa_e_Vale_do_Tejo" title="Lisboa e Vale do Tejo">Lisboa e Vale do Tejo</a> region, is part of the Alentejo NUTS II region.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alentejo_-_NUTSII&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The resident population of the Alentejo stands at around 759,000 (fourth quarter, 2008 – 2 700 less than the fourth quarter of 2007), with 49% men and 51% women. It is the least populated region in the country, representing over one third of national territory but only 7.1% of its population. It is also the region with the oldest population, 22.9% being 65 years of age or more (while the national average is 17.5%).</p>
<p>The population is still declining – especially in the east of the Alentejo. Locals are said to migrate from the villages to the towns and from the towns to cities beyond the Alentejo. Some migration into the Alentejo is from Northern Europeans looking to escape their overcrowded regions, though not always permanently, just for sunny holiday retreats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> People from China, Brazil, and mostly from (South-)Eastern Europe add to curbing population decline.</p>
