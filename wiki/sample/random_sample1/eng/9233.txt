<p><b>Alois Hitler, Sr.</b> (born <b>Alois Schicklgruber</b>; 7 June 1837 – 3 January 1903) was an Austrian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_servant" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a> and the father of German politician and leader of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hitler was born <b>Alois Schicklgruber</b> in the hamlet of Strones, parish of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6llersheim" title="Döllersheim">Döllersheim</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Waldviertel" title="Waldviertel">Waldviertel</a>, an area in northwest <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>, to a 42-year-old unmarried peasant, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Schicklgruber" title="Maria Schicklgruber">Maria Schicklgruber</a>, whose family had lived in the area for generations. At his <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a> in Döllersheim, the space for his father's name on the baptismal certificate was left blank and the priest wrote "illegitimate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His mother cared for Alois in a house she shared with her elderly father, Johannes Schicklgruber.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:House_36_in_Spital,_Lower_Austria.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/House_36_in_Spital%2C_Lower_Austria.jpg/220px-House_36_in_Spital%2C_Lower_Austria.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/House_36_in_Spital%2C_Lower_Austria.jpg/330px-House_36_in_Spital%2C_Lower_Austria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/House_36_in_Spital%2C_Lower_Austria.jpg/440px-House_36_in_Spital%2C_Lower_Austria.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:House_36_in_Spital,_Lower_Austria.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The home of Johann Nepomuk Hiedler (1807–1888)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sometime later, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Hiedler" title="Johann Georg Hiedler">Johann Georg Hiedler</a> moved in with the Schicklgrubers; he married Maria when Alois was five. By the age of 10, Alois had been sent to live with Hiedler's brother, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Nepomuk_Hiedler" title="Johann Nepomuk Hiedler">Johann Nepomuk Hiedler</a>, who owned a farm in the nearby village of <a href="/wiki/Weitra#Geography" title="Weitra">Spital</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/Weitra" title="Weitra">Weitra</a>). Alois attended elementary school and took lessons in shoe-making from a local <a href="/wiki/Shoemaking" title="Shoemaking">cobbler</a>. At the age of 13 he left the farm in Spital and went to Vienna as an apprentice cobbler, working there for about five years. In response to a recruitment drive by the Austrian government offering employment in the civil service to people from rural areas, Alois joined the frontier guards (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Customs_(tax)" title="Customs (tax)">customs</a> service) of the Austrian Finance Ministry in 1855 at the age of 18.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_father">Biological father</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biological father">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historians have discussed three candidates as Alois' biological father: <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Hiedler" title="Johann Georg Hiedler">Johann Georg Hiedler</a>, his brother <a href="/wiki/Johann_Nepomuk_Hiedler" title="Johann Nepomuk Hiedler">Johann Nepomuk Hiedler</a> (or <i>Hüttler</i>), and Leopold Frankenberger (whose existence has never been documented).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Johann Georg Hiedler was during his lifetime the stepfather and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/posthumous" title="wikt:posthumous">posthumously</a> legally declared birth father of Alois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to historian <a href="/wiki/Frank_McDonough" title="Frank McDonough">Frank McDonough</a>, the most plausible theory is that Johann Georg Hiedler was the real father of Alois. An explanation for Alois being sent to live on his uncle's farm as a child is that Hiedler and Maria were simply too poor to raise him, or could not raise him as well as his uncle, or perhaps Maria's health was in decline.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Werner Maser suggests that Alois' father was Johann Nepomuk, Georg's brother and Hitler's step-uncle, who raised Alois through adolescence and later willed him a considerable portion of his life savings, but never admitted publicly to be his real father. According to Maser, Nepomuk was a married farmer who had an affair and then arranged to have his single brother Hiedler marry Alois' mother Maria to provide a cover for Nepomuk's desire to assist and care for Alois without upsetting his wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maser_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-maser-7">[7]</a></sup> This assumes Hiedler was willing to marry Maria in this situation, and Adolf Hitler biographer <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Fest" title="Joachim Fest">Joachim Fest</a> thinks this is too contrived and unlikely to be true.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Alois' son Adolf, following the rumours that his paternal grandfather was a Jew, in 1931 ordered the SS (<i><a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">Schutzstaffel</a></i>) to investigate the alleged rumours regarding his ancestry; they found no evidence of any Jewish ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischel1998_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fischel1998-8">[8]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Laws" title="Nuremberg Laws">Nuremberg Laws</a> came into effect within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>, Hitler then ordered the genealogist Rudolf Koppensteiner to publish a large illustrated genealogical tree showing his ancestry; this was published in the book <i>Die Ahnentafel des Fuehrers</i> (<i>The Pedigree of the Leader</i>) in 1937, which concluded that Hitler's family were all <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a> with no Jewish ancestry and that Hitler had an unblemished "<a href="/wiki/Aryan_race" title="Aryan race">Aryan</a>" pedigree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wargs1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-wargs1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As Alois himself legitimised Johann Georg Hiedler as his father and the priest changed this on his birth certificate in 1876 this was considered certified proof for Hitler's ancestry, thus Hitler was considered a "pure" Aryan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wargs1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-wargs1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Johann Georg Hiedler was considered the officially accepted paternal grandfather of Adolf Hitler by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>, the question of who his grandfather was has caused much speculation and has remained unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> German historian <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Fest" title="Joachim Fest">Joachim Fest</a> wrote that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The indulgence normally accorded to a man's origins is out of place in the case of Adolf Hitler, who made <a href="/wiki/Ahnenpass" title="Ahnenpass">documentary proof</a> of Aryan ancestry a matter of life and death for millions of people but himself possessed no such document. He did not know who his grandfather was. Intensive research into his origins, accounts of which have been distorted by propagandist legends and which are in any case confused and murky, has failed so far to produce a clear picture. National Socialist versions skimmed over the facts and emphasized, for example, that the population of the so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Waldviertel" title="Waldviertel">Waldviertel</a></i>, from which Hitler came, had been 'tribally German since the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration of the Peoples'</a>, or more generally, that Hitler had 'absorbed the powerful forces of this German granite landscape into his blood through his father'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fest1999_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fest1999-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>After the war Hitler's former lawyer, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Frank" title="Hans Frank">Hans Frank</a>, claimed that Hitler told him in 1930 that one of his relatives was trying to blackmail him by threatening to reveal his alleged Jewish ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Hitler asked Frank to find out the facts. Frank says he determined that at the time Maria Schicklgruber gave birth to Alois she was working as a household cook in the town of <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, her employers were a Jewish family named Frankenberger, and that her child might have been conceived out of wedlock with the family's 19-year-old son, Leopold Frankenberger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, all Jews had been expelled from the province of <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a> (which includes Graz) in the 15th century and were not allowed to return until the 1860s when Alois was around 30. Also, there is no evidence of a Frankenberger family living in Graz at that time. Scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Ian_Kershaw" title="Ian Kershaw">Ian Kershaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brigitte_Hamann" title="Brigitte Hamann">Brigitte Hamann</a> dismiss the Frankenberger hypothesis (which had only Frank's speculation to support it) as baseless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HamannMommsen2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-HamannMommsen2010-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> (Kershaw cites several stories circulating in the 1920s about Hitler's alleged Jewish ancestry including one about a "Baron Rothschild" in Vienna in whose household Maria Schicklgruber had worked for some time as a servant).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Kershaw discusses and also lists Hitler's family tree in his biography of Adolf Hitler and gives no validity as to the Frankenberger tale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Further, Frank's story contains several inaccuracies and contradictions, such as he said "The fact that Adolf Hitler had no Jewish blood in his veins, seems, from what has been his whole manner, so blatant to me that it needs no further word",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> also the statement Frank had made that Maria Schicklgruber came from "Leonding near Linz", when in fact she came from the hamlet of Strones, near the village of Döllersheim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Rosenbaum suggests that Frank, who though he had turned against <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a> after 1945 remained an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Semitic" title="Anti-Semitic">anti-Semitic</a> fanatic, made the claim that Hitler had Jewish ancestry as a way of proving that Hitler was a <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jew</a> and <i>not</i> an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue-eyed_blonde" title="Blue-eyed blonde">Aryan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alois_Hitler.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1110" data-file-width="709" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Alois_Hitler.jpeg/170px-Alois_Hitler.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Alois_Hitler.jpeg/255px-Alois_Hitler.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Alois_Hitler.jpeg/340px-Alois_Hitler.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alois_Hitler.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A photo of Alois Hitler in uniform. He always wore his uniform and insisted on being addressed as <i>Herr Oberoffizial Hitler</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind115_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mind115-25">[25]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alois Schicklgruber made steady progress in the semi-<a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> profession of customs official. The work involved frequent reassignments and he served in a variety of places across Austria. By 1860, after five years of service, he reached the rank of <i>Finanzwach-Oberaufseher</i> (Revenue guard superintendent). By 1864, after special training and examinations, he had advanced further and was serving in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linz,_Austria" title="Linz, Austria">Linz, Austria</a>. He later became an inspector of customs posted at <a href="/wiki/Braunau_am_Inn" title="Braunau am Inn">Braunau am Inn</a> in 1875. He eventually rose to full inspector of customs and could go no higher because he lacked the necessary school degrees.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_of_surname">Change of surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Change of surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a rising young junior customs official, he used his birth name of Schicklgruber, but in mid-1876, 39 years old and well established in his career, he asked permission to use his stepfather's family name. He appeared before the parish priest in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6llersheim" title="Döllersheim">Döllersheim</a> and asserted that his father was <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Hiedler" title="Johann Georg Hiedler">Johann Georg Hiedler</a>, who had married his mother and now wished to legitimize him. Three relatives appeared with him as witnesses, one of whom was Johann Nepomuk, Hiedler's brother. The priest agreed to amend the birth certificate, the civil authorities automatically processed the church's decision and Alois Schicklgruber had a new name. The official change, registered at the government office in <a href="/wiki/Mistelbach" title="Mistelbach">Mistelbach</a> in 1877 transformed him into "Alois Hitler". It is not known who decided on the spelling of <i>Hitler</i> instead of <i>Hiedler</i>. Johann Georg's brother was sometimes known by the surname <i>Hüttler</i>.</p>
<p>Smith states that Alois Schicklgruber openly admitted having been born out of wedlock before and after the name change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Alois may have been influenced to change his name for the sake of legal expediency. Historian Werner Maser claims that in 1876, Franz Schicklgruber, the administrator of Alois' mother's estate, transferred a large sum of money (230 <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_gulden" title="Austro-Hungarian gulden">gulden</a>) to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maser_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-maser-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supposedly, Johann Georg Hiedler, who died in 1857, relented on his deathbed and left an inheritance to his illegitimate stepson (Alois) together with his name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Schicklgrubers remain in <a href="/wiki/Waldviertel" title="Waldviertel">Waldviertel</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illegitimate_daughter">Illegitimate daughter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Illegitimate daughter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1869 Hitler had an affair with Thekla Penz (born September 24, 1844) of Leopoldstein, Arbesbach in the district of Zwettel, Lower Austria. This led to the birth of Theresia Penz on October 31, 1869. Thekla later married a man by the name of Horner, while Theresia married Johan Ramer and gave birth to at least six children, while living in the town of Schwertberg.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_married_life">Early married life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early married life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hitler was 36 years old when he married for the first time. Anna Glasl-Hörer was a wealthy, 50-year-old daughter of a customs official. She was sick when they married and was either an invalid or became one shortly afterwards.</p>
<p>Not long after marrying his first wife, Anna, Alois Hitler began an affair with Franziska "Fanni" Matzelsberger, one of the young female servants employed at the Pommer Inn, house number 219, in the city of <a href="/wiki/Braunau_am_Inn" title="Braunau am Inn">Braunau am Inn</a>, where he was renting the top floor as a lodging. Smith states that Alois had numerous affairs in the 1870s, resulting in his wife initiating legal action; on 7 November 1880 Alois and Anna separated by mutual agreement. The 19-year-old Matzelsberger became the 43-year-old Hitler's girlfriend.</p>
<p>In 1876, three years after Hitler married Anna, he had hired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klara_P%C3%B6lzl" title="Klara Pölzl">Klara Pölzl</a> as a household servant. She was the 16-year-old granddaughter of Hitler's step-uncle (and possible father or biological uncle) Nepomuk. If Nepomuk was Hitler's father, Klara was Hitler's half-niece. If his father was Johann Georg, she was his first cousin once removed. Matzelsberger demanded that the "servant girl" Klara find another job, and Hitler sent Pölzl away.</p>
<p>On 13 January 1882, Matzelsberger gave birth to Hitler's illegitimate son, also named Alois, but since they were not married, the child's last name was Matzelsberger, making him "Alois Matzelsberger". Hitler kept Matzelsberger as his wife while his lawful wife (Anna) grew sicker and died on 6 April 1883. The next month, on 22 May at a ceremony in Braunau with fellow custom officials as witnesses, Hitler, 45, married Matzelsberger, 21. He then legitimized his son as Alois Hitler, Jr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Alois' second child, <a href="/wiki/Angela_Hitler" title="Angela Hitler">Angela</a>, was born on 28 July 1883.</p>
<p>Hitler was secure in his profession and no longer an ambitious climber. Historian <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bullock" title="Alan Bullock">Alan Bullock</a> described Alois as "hard, unsympathetic and short-tempered".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Matzelsberger, still only 23, acquired a lung disorder and became too ill to function. She was moved to Ranshofen, a small village near Braunau. During the last months of Matzelsberger's life, Klara Pölzl returned to Alois' home to look after the invalid and the two children (Alois Jr and Angela).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind114_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-mind114-30">[30]</a></sup> Matzelsberger died in Ranshofen on August 10, 1884 at the age of 23. After the death of his second wife, Pölzl remained in his home as housekeeper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind114_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-mind114-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_to_Klara_P.C3.B6lzl_and_family_life">Marriage to Klara Pölzl and family life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marriage to Klara Pölzl and family life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haus_Wiesinger.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="450" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Haus_Wiesinger.jpg/150px-Haus_Wiesinger.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Haus_Wiesinger.jpg/225px-Haus_Wiesinger.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Haus_Wiesinger.jpg/300px-Haus_Wiesinger.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Haus_Wiesinger.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gasthaus Wiesinger in February 2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pölzl was soon to be pregnant by Hitler. Smith writes that if Hitler had been free to do as he wished, he would have married Pölzl immediately but because of the affidavit concerning his paternity, Hitler was now legally Pölzl's first cousin once removed, too close to marry. He submitted an appeal to the church for a humanitarian waiver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Permission came, and on 7 January 1885 a wedding was held at Hitler's rented rooms on the top floor of the Pommer Inn. A meal was served for the few guests and witnesses. Hitler then went to work for the rest of the day. Even Klara found the wedding to be a short ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 May 1885, five months after the wedding, the new Frau Klara Hitler gave birth to her first child, Gustav. A year later, on 25 September 1886, she gave birth to a daughter, Ida. In 1887 <a href="/wiki/Diphtheria" title="Diphtheria">diphtheria</a> struck the Hitler household, resulting in the deaths of both Gustav and Ida. Klara had been Hitler's wife for three years, and both her children were dead, but Hitler still had the children from his relationship with Matzelsberger, Alois Jr., and Angela.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2045" data-file-width="1473" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg/150px-The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg/225px-The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg/300px-The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_graves_of_Alois_and_Klara_Hitler_at_Leonding_near_Linz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Hitlers' tombstone photographed c.1984</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 20 April 1889, she gave birth to another son, future leader of the National Socialist Party <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf</a>. He was a sickly child, and his mother fretted over him. Alois was 51 when he was born. Hitler had little interest in child rearing and left it all to his wife. When not at work he was either in a tavern or busy with his hobby, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apiculture" title="Apiculture">keeping bees</a>. In June 1892 Otto was born but died days later. Hitler was transferred from Braunau to <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a>. He was 55, Klara 32, Alois Jr. 10, Angela 9 and Adolf was three years old.</p>
<p>Beginning on 1 August, the family lived at Theresienstr. 23. One month after Hitler accepted a better paying position in Linz, on 1 April 1893, his wife and the children moved to a second floor room on Kapuzinerstr. 31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Klara had just given birth to Edmund, so it was decided she and the children would stay in Passau for the time being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On 21 January 1896, <a href="/wiki/Paula_Hitler" title="Paula Hitler">Paula</a>, Adolf's younger sister, was born. She was the last child of Alois Hitler and Klara Pölzl. Hitler was often home with his family. He had five children ranging in age from infancy to 14. Edmund (the youngest of the boys) died of <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> on 2 February 1900.</p>
<p>Hitler wanted his son Adolf to seek a career in the civil service. However, Adolf had become so alienated from his father that he was repulsed by whatever Hitler wanted. Adolf sneered at the thought of a lifetime spent enforcing petty rules. Hitler tried to browbeat his son into obedience while Adolf did his best to be the opposite of whatever his father wanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_G._L._Waite" title="Robert G. L. Waite">Robert G. L. Waite</a> noted, "Even one of his closest friends admitted that Alois was 'awfully rough' with his wife [Klara] and 'hardly ever spoke a word to her at home'." If Hitler was in a bad mood, he picked on the older children or Klara herself, in front of them. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Patrick_Hitler" title="William Patrick Hitler">William Patrick Hitler</a> says that he had heard from his father, Alois Jr, that Alois Hitler, Sr. used to beat his children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind115_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-mind115-25">[25]</a></sup> After Hitler and his oldest son Alois Jr. had a climactic and violent argument, Alois Jr. left home, and the elder Alois swore he would never give the boy a penny of inheritance beyond what the law required. According to reports, Alois Hitler liked to lord it over his neighbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind115_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-mind115-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 1895, Hitler purchased a house on a nine-acre (36,000 m²) plot in Hafeld near <a href="/wiki/Lambach" title="Lambach">Lambach</a>, approximately 30 miles (48 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a>. The farm was called the <i>Rauscher Gut</i>. He moved his family to the farm and retired on 25 June 1895 at the age of 58 after 40 years in the customs service. He found farming difficult; he lost money, and the value of the property declined.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="746" data-file-width="1200" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg/150px-Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg/225px-Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg/300px-Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alois_Hitler_%22deathbed%22.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The couch on which Alois Hitler died</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On the morning of 3 January 1903, Hitler went to the Gasthaus Wiesinger (No.1 Michaelsbergstrasse, Leonding) as usual to drink his morning glass of wine. He was offered the newspaper and promptly collapsed. He was taken to an adjoining room and a doctor was summoned, but Alois Hitler died at the inn, probably from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleural_hemorrhage" title="Pleural hemorrhage">pleural hemorrhage</a>. Adolf Hitler, who was 13 when his father died, says in <i><a href="/wiki/Mein_Kampf" title="Mein Kampf">Mein Kampf</a></i> that he died of a "stroke of <a href="/wiki/Apoplexy" title="Apoplexy">apoplexy</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In his book, <i>The Young Hitler I Knew</i>, Hitler's childhood friend <a href="/wiki/August_Kubizek" title="August Kubizek">August Kubizek</a> recalled, "When the fourteen-year-old [sic] son saw his dead father he burst out into uncontrollable weeping."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Removal_of_tombstone">Removal of tombstone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Hitler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Removal of tombstone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 28 March 2012, the tombstone marking Alois Hitler's grave, and that of his wife Klara, in Town Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Leonding" title="Leonding">Leonding</a> was removed by a descendant according to Kurt Pittertschatscher, the pastor of the parish. The descendant is said to be an elderly female relative of Alois Hitler's first wife, Anna, who has also given up any rights to the rented burial plot. The plot was covered in white gravel and a tree which has since been removed. The remains of Adolf Hitler's parents are still interred there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
