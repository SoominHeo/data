<p><b>Alu Dadashevich Alkhanov</b> (born January 20, 1957) is a Russian politician, the former president of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chechen_Republic" title="Chechen Republic">Chechen Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Alkhanov is a career <a href="/wiki/Police_officer" title="Police officer">police officer</a> who fought within the ranks of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Armed_Forces" title="Russian Armed Forces">Russian Armed Forces</a> during the <a href="/wiki/First_Chechen_War" title="First Chechen War">First Chechen War</a>. He was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chechen_presidential_election,_2004&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chechen presidential election, 2004 (page does not exist)">elected</a> president on August 30, 2004, under controversial circumstances. On February 15, 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_President" title="Russian President">Russian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> dismissed Alkhanov as the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Chechen_Republic" title="Head of the Chechen Republic">Chechen President</a> and appointed him a Deputy <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(Russia)" title="Ministry of Justice (Russia)">Justice Minister</a> of Russia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alu_Alkhanov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Taldykorgan_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Taldykorgan Province (page does not exist)">Taldykorgan Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, Alkhanov joined the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Armed_Forces" title="Soviet Armed Forces">Soviet Armed Forces</a> on leaving school. He joined the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Militsiya" title="Militsiya">Militsiya</a> service in 1983, graduating from the transport police school in Mogilev (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahilyow" title="Mahilyow">Mahilyow</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>). He went on to the High Police School in <a href="/wiki/Rostov-on-Don" title="Rostov-on-Don">Rostov-on-Don</a> before becoming Deputy Head of the North Caucasus Transport Department of the former Chechen-Ingushetia government in <a href="/wiki/Grozny" title="Grozny">Grozny</a> in 1992. He was later promoted to head the department, a post which he held until 1997.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/First_Chechen_War" title="First Chechen War">First Chechen War</a> broke out in 1994, Alkhanov supported the Russian side against the <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separatists</a>. He was decorated with the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Courage" title="Order of Courage">Order of Courage</a> for his actions during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Separatists" title="Separatists">separatists</a>' assault on <a href="/wiki/Grozny" title="Grozny">Grozny</a> in 1996. In April 2003, he was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_Minister" title="Interior Minister">Interior Minister</a> of Chechnya in the government of <a href="/wiki/Akhmad_Kadyrov" title="Akhmad Kadyrov">Akhmad Kadyrov</a> and was made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a> of the Chechen police. When Kadyrov was assassinated on May 9, 2004, Akhnanov became the favoured candidate of the Russian government.</p>
<p>On June 1, 2006, Alkhanov said he would prefer his republic be governed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharia_law" title="Sharia law">Sharia law</a> and suggested adapting the Islamic code.</p>
<p>He is widely seen to be conflicted with <a href="/wiki/Ramzan_Kadyrov" title="Ramzan Kadyrov">Ramzan Kadyrov</a>, a former rebel fighter and Chechen Republic's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> with presidential ambitions. Kadyrov eventually replaced Alkhanov as President in February 2007, following by placing his own people in all the leading positions.</p>
<p>Alu Alkhanov is married, with three children.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_controversy">Election controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alu_Alkhanov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Election controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chechen_presidential_election,_2004&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chechen presidential election, 2004 (page does not exist)">Chechen presidential election, 2004</a></div>
<p>Alu Alkhanov's election in August 2004 was controversial from the outset. The election of his predecessor had been marred by allegations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballot_stuffing" title="Ballot stuffing">ballot stuffing</a>, voter intimidation by Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soldiers" title="Soldiers">soldiers</a> and the exclusion of possible separatist candidates. As a career bureaucrat, Alkhanov had no obvious popular base and was seen by many observers as the placeman of the government of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_President" title="Russian President">Russian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>. Critics of Russian policy in Chechnya claimed that the government would not permit Alkhanov to be defeated, and that the outcome of the vote had been predetermined well in advance.</p>
<p>Alkhanov faced seven challengers. The most serious of these, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malik_Saidullayev" title="Malik Saidullayev">Malik Saidullayev</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>-based Chechen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Businessman" title="Businessman">businessman</a>, was barred from standing on the technicality of failing to fill his application correctly. The other six challengers had little recognition within Chechnya and several had ties with the government. They were:</p>
<ul>
<li>Abdula Bugayev, an historian and director of the Chechen branch of the Modern Humanities Academy. He finished a distant second to Kadyrov in 2003 with 5.7% of the votes.</li>
<li>Movsar Khamidov, a <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> in the Chechen department of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Security_Service" title="Federal Security Service">Federal Security Service</a> (FSB), the successor to the <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a>.</li>
<li>Vakha Visayev, an economist and an adviser to the acting Chechnya <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Abramov_(politician)" title="Sergey Abramov (politician)">Sergey Abramov</a>.</li>
<li>Mukhmud-Khasan Asakov, a staff member of the Chechen State Council.</li>
<li>Magomed Aidamirov, a businessman from the village of Tolstoy-Yurt.</li>
<li>Umar Abuyev, director-general of the Chechen Petrochemical Company.</li>
</ul>
<p>Alkhanov's platform was effectively a continuation of his predecessor's policies, with Chechnya continuing to remain part of Russia; economic <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a>; attracting aid and investment; cutting unemployment and the Russian military presence; and opening peace talks with separatist leader <a href="/wiki/Aslan_Maskhadov" title="Aslan Maskhadov">Aslan Maskhadov</a>.</p>
<p>In the event, Alkhanov won by a landslide majority with 73.67% of the votes on an 85.25% turnout. Khamidov was second, with 8.95 percent, and Abdula Bugayev came third, with 4.5%. Visayev was fourth, Abuyev fifth, Asakov sixth and Aidamarov seventh, gaining between 0.6% to 4.3% of the vote. 1% of voters voted "against all candidates".</p>
<p>The results of the election were regarded with scepticism by some outside observers and the Chechen opposition. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_State" title="U.S. Department of State">U.S. Department of State</a>, and <a href="/wiki/International_Helsinki_Federation_for_Human_Rights" title="International Helsinki Federation for Human Rights">International Helsinki Federation for Human Rights</a> questioned the fairness of the elections and highlighting the disqualification of Saidullayev. The elections was internationally monitored by the monitors from <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">CIS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arab_League" title="Arab League">LAS</a>; western monitors didn't participate in the monitoring of the elections despite being invited. Polling conditions have been questioned; Khamidov has said that his campaign staff had recorded numerous irregulaties and will contest the vote results in court.</p>
