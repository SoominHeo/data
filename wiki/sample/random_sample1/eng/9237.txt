<p><b>Alyutor</b> or <b>Alutor</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> that belongs to the Chukotkan branch of the <a href="/wiki/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages" title="Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages">Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociolinguistic_situation">Sociolinguistic situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sociolinguistic situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Alutor are the indigenous inhabitants of the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka Peninsula</a>. The language is unwritten and moribund; in the 1970s residents of the chief Alutor village of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vyvenka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vyvenka (page does not exist)">Vyvenka</a> under the age of 25 did not know the language. In recent<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> years the Vyvenka village school has started teaching the language. Until 1958 the language was considered the "village" (settled) dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Koryak_language" title="Koryak language">Koryak language</a>, but it is not intelligible with traditionally nomadic varieties of Koryak. The autonym <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈnəməlʔən]</span> means "villager".</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stress is generally on the second syllable of the word. However, it cannot fall on a schwa or the last syllable, so in two-syllable words stress is transferred to the first syllable, as long as that vowel is not a schwa. In cases where it is a schwa, a third syllable is added to the word,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the second syllable is stressed.</p>
<p>Examples: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈmi.məl/</span> 'water', <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/qə.ˈla.vul/</span> 'husband', <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pə.ˈla.kəl.ŋən/</span> 'a <a href="/wiki/Mukluk" title="Mukluk">mukluk</a> (boot)', <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈta.wə.ja.tək/</span> 'to feed'.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllable_structure">Syllable structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Syllable structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All Alyutor syllables begin with a single consonant. If the vowel is short, including a schwa, they may also close with a single consonant.</p>
<p>Examples are <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/vi.ˈta.tək/</span> 'to work', <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈtil.mə.til/</span> 'eagle', <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈʔit.ʔən/</span> 'parka'.</p>
<p>Alyutor word boundaries always coincide with syllable boundaries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alyutor has the following parts of speech: nouns, adjectives, numerals, pronouns, verbs, participles, adverbs, postpositions, conjunctions, and "particles".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nouns are inflected for number, case, definiteness, and grammatical person.</p>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">grammatical numbers</a>: singular, dual and plural.</p>
<p>There are eleven cases: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolutive" title="Absolutive">absolutive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ergative_case" title="Ergative case">ergative</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locative" title="Locative">locative</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative" title="Dative">dative</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lative" title="Lative">lative</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolative" title="Prolative">prolative</a>, contactive, <a href="/wiki/Causative" title="Causative">causative</a>, equative, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comitative" title="Comitative">comitative</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associative" title="Associative">associative</a>.</p>
<p>Number and case are expressed using a single affix. A suffix is used for all cases except the comitative and associative, which are expressed using <a href="/wiki/Circumfix" title="Circumfix">circumfixes</a>. There are two <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declensions</a>, taught as three noun classes. The first class are nonhuman nouns of the first declension. Number is only distinguished in the absolutive case, though verbal agreement may distinguish number when these nouns are in the ergative. The second class are proper names and kin terms for elders. They are second declension, and distinguish number in the ergative, locative, and lative cases, as well as the absolutive. The third class are the other human nouns; they may be either first or second declension.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_roles">Case roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Case roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The absolutive case is the citation form of a noun. It is used for the argument ("subject") of an intransitive clause and the object of a transitive clause, for "syntactic possessives",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocative" title="Vocative">vocative</a>.</li>
<li>The ergative is used for the agent ("subject") of a transitive verb, as an <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">instrumental case</a>, and as the argument of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antipassive" title="Antipassive">antipassive</a> clause.</li>
<li>The locative is used for position and direction (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essive" title="Essive">essive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lative_case" title="Lative case">lative cases</a>), as well as arguments which are "driven away" <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The dative is used for recipients, benefactors, directional objects (<a href="/wiki/Allative_case" title="Allative case">allative case</a>), and subjects of experiential verbs</li>
<li>Lative is used for motion toward a goal</li>
<li>Prolative is used for movement along and movement from (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perlative" title="Perlative">perlative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elative_case" title="Elative case">elative cases</a>)</li>
<li>Equative is used with the meanings 'like X', 'as X', usually with verbs like 'to become', 'to turn into', 'to work as,' etc.</li>
<li>Contactive is used for objects that make contact</li>
<li>Causative is used for noun phrases that cause or motivate an action</li>
<li>Comitative is used for ...<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Associative is used for ...<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. It is only attested in the declension of nouns of the first declension, usually inanimate.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_person">Grammatical person</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Grammatical person">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Verbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are finite (conjugated) and non-finite verbs. There are several conjugations.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Polypersonal_conjugation">Polypersonal conjugation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Polypersonal conjugation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Finite verbs agree in person and number with their nuclear <a href="/wiki/Argument_(linguistics)" title="Argument (linguistics)">arguments</a>; agreement is through both prefixes and suffixes. Transitive verbs agree with both arguments (ergative and absolutive), whereas intransitive verbs agree with their sole (absolutive) argument.</p>
<p>Verbs distinguish two aspects, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfective" title="Perfective">perfective</a>, the bare stem, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperfective" title="Imperfective">imperfective</a>, using the suffix <i>-tkə / -tkən / -tkəni</i>. There are five moods, <a href="/wiki/Realis_mood" title="Realis mood">indicative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optative_mood" title="Optative mood">optative</a>, potential (marked by the circumfix <i>ta…(ŋ)</i>), and conjunctive (prefix <i>ʔ-/a-</i>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Monopersonal_conjugation">Monopersonal conjugation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Monopersonal conjugation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Monopersonal verbs<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> include two declensions, one with the third-person singular in <i>ɣa-...-lin,</i> and the other in <i>n-...-qin.</i></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impersonal_conjugation">Impersonal conjugation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Impersonal conjugation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For impersonal forms of conjugation include verbal predicate (formed with the circumfix a…ka) and imperative (formed by circumfix ɣa…a/ta). Non-finite forms Impersonal forms include the verbal predicate<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with the circumfix <i>a…ka,</i> and the imperative in <i>ɣa…a/ta.</i></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-finite_forms">Non-finite forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alyutor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Non-finite forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These include the infinitive, <a href="/wiki/Supine" title="Supine">supine</a>, gerunds, and participles.</p>
