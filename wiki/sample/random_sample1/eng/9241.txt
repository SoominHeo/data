<p><b>Ali Amini</b> (12 December 1905 – 12 December 1992) was an <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> politician and writer who was the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Iran" title="Prime Minister of Iran">Prime Minister of Iran</a> from 6 May 1961 to 19 July 1962.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Amini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amini was born on 12 December 1905 in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moin92_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-moin92-1">[1]</a></sup> He was a grandson of <a href="/wiki/Mozaffar_ad-Din_Shah_Qajar" title="Mozaffar ad-Din Shah Qajar">Mozaffar ad-Din Shah Qajar</a> through his mother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fakhr_ol_dowleh" title="Fakhr ol dowleh">Fakhr ol dowleh</a>. He completed his studies first in <a href="/wiki/Dar_ul-Funun_(Persia)" title="Dar ul-Funun (Persia)">Darolfonoon</a> and then in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> where he graduated with a degree in law from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grenoble_University" title="Grenoble University">Grenoble University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin80_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubin80-2">[2]</a></sup> followed by his Ph.D in economics from <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p>
<p>He became involved in politics through the help of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ali_Akbar_Davar" title="Ali Akbar Davar">Ali Akbar Davar</a> who gave him a position in the judiciary.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Amini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the age of 37, Amini was selected as a minister to serve in the cabinet of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmad_Ghavam" title="Ahmad Ghavam">Ahmad Ghavam</a>. He was a member of <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(Iran)" title="National Front (Iran)">the National Front</a>, but broke away from the party in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin80_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-rubin80-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was minister of culture from 1952 to 1953. He became minister of economic affairs in the cabinet of <a href="/wiki/Fazlollah_Zahedi" title="Fazlollah Zahedi">Fazlollah Zahedi</a> and remain in office until 1955. The same year he was named ambassador to the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin80_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-rubin80-2">[2]</a></sup> His tendencies were pro-American to the extent that made the Shah uncomfortable. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Shah" title="Mohammad Reza Shah">Mohammad Reza Shah</a> in particular distrusted Amini's popularity and friendship with then-senator <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>. Therefore, his tenure ended in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubin80_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-rubin80-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the 1950s, Amini was a candidate for premiership. He was appointed prime minister in 1961. In July 1962, however, he was replaced by the Shah's close friend and a major <a href="/wiki/Birjand" title="Birjand">Birjand</a> landowner <a href="/wiki/Asadollah_Alam" title="Asadollah Alam">Asadollah Alam</a>. In the late 1970s, Amini attempted a comeback into Iranian politics at the age of 70. He served as advisor to the Shah during the final days of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pahlavi_Dynasty" title="Pahlavi Dynasty">Pahlavi Dynasty</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Amini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amini married Batoul Voosough (died 1992) in 1932 and they had a son, Iraj.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moin92_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-moin92-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Amini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amini was awarded Grand Croix of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Legion_of_Honor" title="The Legion of Honor">the Legion of Honor</a> in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat92_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat92-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years_and_death">Later years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Amini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1979, Amini moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. There he headed the Front for the Liberation of Iran, a monarchist opposition group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs3july_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cs3july-4">[4]</a></sup> He complained about internal struggle among the exiled Iranian monachists, saying "We're not even back in Tehran [and] they quarrel over the name of the country's future prime minister."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs3july_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cs3july-4">[4]</a></sup> He wrote his biography published by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.</p>
<p>He died in Paris on 12 December 1992, his 87th birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moin92_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-moin92-1">[1]</a></sup> His body was buried in <a href="/wiki/Passy_Cemetery" title="Passy Cemetery">Passy Cemetery</a>.</p>
