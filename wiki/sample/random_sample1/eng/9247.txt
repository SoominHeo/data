<p><b>Arlene Isabel Foster</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" title="Her Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council">PC</a>, (née <b>Kelly</b>; born 3 July 1970) is a Northern Irish politician who has been the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Leader of the Democratic Unionist Party</a> since December 2015. She also served as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Northern_Ireland" title="First Minister of Northern Ireland">First Minister of Northern Ireland</a> from January 2016 until her involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Heat_Incentive_scandal" title="Renewable Heat Incentive scandal">Renewable Heat Incentive scandal</a> caused <a href="/wiki/Martin_McGuinness" title="Martin McGuinness">Martin McGuinness</a> to resign from the post of deputy First Minister in protest in January 2017. Under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Northern Ireland power-sharing agreement</a>, Foster is not permitted to remain in office as First Minister without a Deputy First Minister. His resignation caused a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2017" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2017">snap election</a> to be held.</p>
<p>She has also been the <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Legislative_Assembly_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Member of the Legislative Assembly (Northern Ireland)">Member of the Northern Ireland Assembly</a> for <a href="/wiki/Fermanagh_and_South_Tyrone_(Assembly_constituency)" title="Fermanagh and South Tyrone (Assembly constituency)">Fermanagh and South Tyrone</a> since 2003. She previously served in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> as <a href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Environment_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Department of the Environment (Northern Ireland)">Minister of the Environment</a> (2007–08), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Enterprise,_Trade_and_Investment" title="Department of Enterprise, Trade and Investment">Minister for Enterprise and Investment</a> (2008–2015) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Finance_and_Personnel" title="Department of Finance and Personnel">Minister for Finance and Personnel</a> (2015–16).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arlene_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Foster was raised in the townland of Dernawilt, between <a href="/wiki/Lisnaskea" title="Lisnaskea">Lisnaskea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roslea" title="Roslea">Roslea</a>, in the south-east of <a href="/wiki/County_Fermanagh" title="County Fermanagh">County Fermanagh</a>. Her experience with <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> began early in her life when a night-time attempt was made to kill her father, a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> reservist, at their home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a teenager Foster was on a school bus that was bombed by the IRA, the vehicle targeted because its driver was a soldier in the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Defence_Regiment" title="Ulster Defence Regiment">Ulster Defence Regiment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was a pupil at <a href="/wiki/Enniskillen_Collegiate_Grammar_School" title="Enniskillen Collegiate Grammar School">Enniskillen Collegiate Grammar School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Enniskillen" title="Enniskillen">Enniskillen</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Fermanagh" title="County Fermanagh">County Fermanagh</a> from 1982-89. She was educated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University,_Belfast" title="Queen's University, Belfast">Queen's University, Belfast</a> where she graduated with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LL.B." title="LL.B.">LL.B.</a> degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was at Queen's University where her political career began after joining the Queen's Unionist Association, part of the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a> (UUP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>She served as the association's chairman from 1992 to 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DUPBio_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DUPBio-5">[5]</a></sup> After leaving Queen's University she remained active in the UUP, chairing its youth wing, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Young_Unionist_Council" title="Ulster Young Unionist Council">Ulster Young Unionist Council</a>, in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DUPBio_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DUPBio-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1996, she became an Honorary Secretary of the UUP's ruling body, the Ulster Unionist Council, a position which she held until her resignation from the UUP on 18 December 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DUPBio_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-DUPBio-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>She was elected as an Ulster Unionist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_Election,_2003" title="Northern Ireland Assembly Election, 2003">2003 Assembly elections</a>. While a member of the UUP, she was part of a "rightwing cabal within the UUP known as the 'baby barristers'." They actively opposed party leader <a href="/wiki/David_Trimble" title="David Trimble">David Trimble</a>, and were a "thorn in [his] side" after he supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" title="Belfast Agreement">Belfast Agreement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Profile_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Profile-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2004, Foster resigned from the UUP and joined the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> (DUP), together with fellow Assembly members <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Donaldson" title="Jeffrey Donaldson">Jeffrey Donaldson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norah_Beare" title="Norah Beare">Norah Beare</a>. She was selected as the DUP's candidate for <a href="/wiki/Fermanagh_and_South_Tyrone_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Fermanagh and South Tyrone (UK Parliament constituency)">Fermanagh and South Tyrone</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 UK general election</a>.</p>
<p>Negotiations took place between the local branches of the DUP and UUP with the aim of finding an agreed <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionist</a> candidate. The negotiations broke down with neither party willing to accept the electoral dominance of the other; the UUP claiming Foster's defection to the DUP disguised the reality of the UUP's electoral strength, while the DUP pointed to the change in the unionist political landscape following the 2003 Assembly election and the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2004_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 2004 (United Kingdom)">2004 European Parliament election</a>. The UUP candidate was <a href="/wiki/Tom_Elliott_(politician)" title="Tom Elliott (politician)">Tom Elliott</a>. Foster finished second in the 2005 general election with 14,056 votes.</p>
<p>On 11 January 2010, she assumed the duties of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Northern_Ireland" title="First Minister of Northern Ireland">First Minister of Northern Ireland</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Peter_Robinson_(Northern_Ireland_politician)" title="Peter Robinson (Northern Ireland politician)">Peter Robinson</a> stepped aside for a planned period of up to six weeks. Foster worked along with the deputy First Minister <a href="/wiki/Martin_McGuinness" title="Martin McGuinness">Martin McGuinness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Robinson returned earlier than planned, on 3 February 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRBack_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRBack-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minister_for_the_Environment">Minister for the Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arlene_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minister for the Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2007, a privately financed proposal for a new <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant's Causeway">Giant's Causeway</a> centre was given preliminary approval by Foster in her role as the new Northern Ireland Environment Minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Immediately afterwards, the public money that had been allocated to the Causeway development was frozen. The proposal resulted in a public row about the relationship between the private developer Seymour Sweeney and the DUP; Sweeney was a member of the DUP, although both parties denied that he had ever donated financially to the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 January 2008, Foster announced that she had decided against Sweeney's proposal for a new visitors' centre, reversing her earlier position of "being minded" to approve it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Although the public funds for a Causeway scheme remained frozen for the time being, it seemed highly likely that the publicly funded plan for the Causeway would now go ahead after all.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minister_for_Enterprise.2C_Trade_and_Investment">Minister for Enterprise, Trade and Investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arlene_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Minister for Enterprise, Trade and Investment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the minister responsible for energy policy, in June 2012 Foster attacked the Co-operative Group over the showing of a documentary opposing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracking" title="Fracking">fracking</a>, saying, "I find your claim that you take 'ethics to the next level' hard to reconcile with your demonstrable support for a film which presents a wholly one-sided and partial approach to the debate about hydraulic fracturing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arlene_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2014 Foster became embroiled in controversy after demanding an apology for what she described as "deeply insulting language" in a comment made by fellow MLA, <a href="/wiki/Anna_Lo" title="Anna Lo">Anna Lo</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party of Northern Ireland</a>. Lo had described herself as "anti-colonial" and said the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition of Ireland</a> was "artificial".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foster herself was challenged over the fact that she had chosen to speak out so robustly on the matter, after remaining silent about arguably much more controversial comments made the previous day by another unionist politician, <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Unionist_Party" title="Progressive Unionist Party">Progressive Unionist Party</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Billy_Hutchinson" title="Billy Hutchinson">Billy Hutchinson</a>. Hutchinson had said in a newspaper interview that he had "no regrets" over his random sectarian murder of two Catholics on their way to work in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2016, as she was poised to become First Minister, Foster caused controversy by saying she would not be travelling to Dublin for the official centenary celebrations of the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">1916 uprising</a> against British rule, describing the rising as "an attack on democracy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Heat_Incentive_scandal" title="Renewable Heat Incentive scandal">Renewable Heat Incentive scandal</a></div>
<p>In December 2016, Foster faced criticism and controversy after a whistleblower revealed that the Renewable Heat Incentive scheme overspent by £400m, a failure which has been nicknamed the <i>Cash for Ash</i> scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The scheme was originally set up by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Enterprise,_Trade_and_Investment" title="Department of Enterprise, Trade and Investment">Department of Enterprise, Trade and Investment</a> (DETI, now Department for the Economy) when she was Minister of the department and the scheme offered incentives to businesses if they installed renewable heating systems, such as burning wooden pellets.</p>
<p>She faced strong criticism after it was claimed that she personally campaigned to keep the scheme open, even when senior civil servants warned of the overspend and the Minister responsible, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Bell_(politician)" title="Jonathan Bell (politician)">Jonathan Bell</a>, planned on closing it. It remained open for an extra two weeks before it was finally closed. It was also revealed that the Northern Ireland budget would lose £400m over the next 20 years as a result of the failure of the scheme. An independent audit investigated 300 sites and found there were issues at half of them, including 14 cases where there were suspicions of 'serious fraud'. When senior civil servants suggested the closure of the scheme in September 2015, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_of_the_First_Minister_and_deputy_First_Minister" title="Office of the First Minister and deputy First Minister">Office of the First Minister and deputy First Minister</a> (now the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Office_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Executive Office (Northern Ireland)">Executive Office</a>) pressured the department to keep the scheme open, which is when there was a spike in applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There were calls for Foster to resign as First Minister after the scandal broke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 9 January 2017, McGuinness resigned as deputy First Minister in protest over the RHI scandal. Under the terms of the power-sharing agreement that created what is now the Executive Office, his resignation has also resulted in Foster being removed from office, until Sinn Féin nominates a new deputy First Minister; the party stated that it will not replace McGuinness. No nomination was made before 16 January, resulting in the collapse of the Executive. <a href="/wiki/James_Brokenshire" title="James Brokenshire">James Brokenshire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Secretary of State for Northern Ireland">Secretary of State for Northern Ireland</a>, assumed the powers of the Executive and called for a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2017" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2017">snap election</a> scheduled for 2 March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In a statement posted to Facebook, Foster said that she was "disappointed" with McGuinness' decision and condemned it as "not principled": "At a time when we are dealing with Brexit, needing to create more jobs and investing in our health and education system, Northern Ireland needs stability. But because of Sinn Féin's selfish reactions, we now have instability, and I very much regret that." She expressed concern over the possibility of another election less than a year after <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2016" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2016">the previous one</a>, and said "this is not an election of our making", but that "the DUP will always defend unionism and stand up for what is best for Northern Ireland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arlene_Foster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Foster and her husband Brian have three children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foster was a Councillor on <a href="/wiki/Fermanagh_District_Council" title="Fermanagh District Council">Fermanagh District Council</a> representing Enniskillen ward from 2005-10. In 2008, she was recognised as Assembly member of the year at the Women in Public Life Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DETI_Bio_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-DETI_Bio-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
