<p><b>Jaʿfar ibn Muhammad al-Muʿtasim bi'llah</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">جعفر بن محمد المعتصم بالله</span>‎‎; March 822 – 11 December 861), better known by his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laqab" title="Laqab">regnal name</a> <b>al-Mutawakkil ʿala Allah</b> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">المتوكل على الله</span>, "He who relies on God") was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">caliph</a> who reigned in <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Samarra" title="Abbasid Samarra">Samarra</a> from 847 until 861. He succeeded his brother <a href="/wiki/Al-Wathiq" title="Al-Wathiq">al-Wathiq</a>. His assassination on 11 December 861 by the Turkish guard with the support of his son, <a href="/wiki/Al-Muntasir" title="Al-Muntasir">al-Muntasir</a>, began the troubled period of civil strife known as "<a href="/wiki/Anarchy_at_Samarra" title="Anarchy at Samarra">Anarchy at Samarra</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caliphate">Caliphate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Mutawakkil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Caliphate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When al-Wathiq died unexpectedly in August 847, the leading officials,the <a href="/wiki/Vizier" title="Vizier">vizier</a> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_al-Zayyat" title="Muhammad ibn al-Zayyat">Muhammad ibn al-Zayyat</a>, the chief <i><a href="/wiki/Qadi" title="Qadi">qādī</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmad_ibn_Abi_Duwad" title="Ahmad ibn Abi Duwad">Ahmad ibn Abi Duwad</a>, the Turkish generals <a href="/wiki/Itakh" title="Itakh">Itakh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wasif_al-Turki" title="Wasif al-Turki">Wasif al-Turki</a>, and a few others, assembled to determine his successor. Ibn al-Zayyat initially proposed al-Wathiq's son Muhammad (the future <a href="/wiki/Al-Muhtadi" title="Al-Muhtadi">al-Muhtadi</a>), but due to his youth he was passed over, and instead the council chose the 26-year-old Ja'far, who became the caliph al-Mutawakkil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006232.E2.80.93233_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006232.E2.80.93233-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer198968_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer198968-2">[2]</a></sup> Unbeknownst to them, the new Caliph was resolved to destroy the coterie of his father's officials that controlled the state, and rule on his own authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Al-Mutawakkil's first target was the vizier Ibn al-Zayyat, against whom he harboured a deep grudge over the way he had disrespected him in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234-3">[3]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Al-Tabari" title="Al-Tabari">al-Tabari</a>, when al-Wathiq had grown angry and suspicious at his brother, al-Mutawakkil had visited the vizier in hopes of persuading him to intercede with the Caliph. Not only had Ibn al-Zayyat kept the Abbasid prince waiting until he finished going through his correspondence, but even mocked him, in the presence of others, for coming to him seeking assistance. Not only that, but when the dejected prince left, Ibn al-Zayyat wrote to the Caliph to complain about his appearance, noting that he was dressed in effeminate fashion, and that his hair was too long. As a result, al-Wathiq had his brother summoned to court. Al-Mutawakkil came in a brand-new court dress, hoping to mollify the Caliph, but instead al-Wathiq ordered that his hair be shorn off, and al-Mutawakkil be struck in the face with it. In later times, al-Mutawakkil confessed that he had never been so distressed by anything in his life than by this public humiliation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer198965.E2.80.9368_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer198965.E2.80.9368-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234.E2.80.93235_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234.E2.80.93235-5">[5]</a></sup> Thus, on 22 September 847, he sent Itakh to summon Ibn al-Zayyat as if for an audience. Instead, the vizier was brought to Itakh's residence, where he was placed under house arrest. His possessions were confiscated, and he was tortured to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer198965.E2.80.9371_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer198965.E2.80.9371-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234.E2.80.93236_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006234.E2.80.93236-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>This was the apogee of Itakh's career: he combined the positions of chamberlain (<i><a href="/wiki/Hajib" title="Hajib">ḥājib</a></i>), head of the Caliph's personal guard, intendant of the palace, and head of the <i><a href="/wiki/Barid_(caliphate)" title="Barid (caliphate)">barīd</a></i>, the public post, which doubled as the government's intelligence network. In 848, however, he was persuaded to go to the <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">pilgrimage</a>, and laid down his powers, only to be arrested on his return. His possessions were confiscated—reportedly, in his house alone the Caliph's agents found one million <a href="/wiki/Gold_dinar" title="Gold dinar">gold dinars</a>. He died of thirst in prison on 21 December 849.</p>
<p>One Mahmud ibn al-Faraj al-Nayshapuri arose claiming to be a prophet. He and some followers were arrested in Baghdad. He was imprisoned, beaten and on June 18, 850 he died.</p>
<p>In A.H. 236 (850) al-Mutawakkil issued a decree requiring all Christians and Jews in his realm including Jerusalem and Caesarea to wear a yellow (honey-colored) hood and belt to distinguish them from Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webcitation_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-webcitation-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In A.H. 237 (851–852) Armenians rebelled and defeated and killed the Abbasid governor. Al-Mutawakkil sent his general <a href="/wiki/Bugha_al-Kabir" title="Bugha al-Kabir">Bugha al-Kabir</a> to handle this. Bugha scored successes this year and the following year he attacked and burned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiflis" title="Tiflis">Tiflis</a>, capturing <a href="/wiki/Ishaq_ibn_Isma%27il" title="Ishaq ibn Isma'il">Ishaq ibn Isma'il</a>. The rebel leader was executed. That year (A.H. 238) the Byzantines <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Damietta_(853)" title="Sack of Damietta (853)">attacked Damietta</a>.</p>
<p>In A.H. 240 (854–855) the police chief in <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a> killed a prominent person stirring an uprising. He was driven out. Al-Mutawakkil offered another police chief. When the next year saw a revolt against this new police chief, al-Mutawakkil had this firmly suppressed. As Christians had joined in the second round of disturbances, the caliph had Christians expelled from Homs.</p>
<p>Also in 241 occurred the firm response to the revolt by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bujah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bujah (page does not exist)">Bujah</a>, people of descent just beyond <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a>. They had been paying a tax on their gold mines. They ceased paying this, drove out Muslims working in the mines and terrified people in Upper Egypt. Al-Mutawakkil sent al-Qummi to restore order. Al-Qummi sent seven ships with supplies that enabled him to persevere despite the very harsh terrain of this distant territory. He retook the mines, pressed on to the Bujah royal stronghold and defeated the king in battle. The Bujah resumed payment of the tax.</p>
<p>On February 23, 856, there was an exchange of captives with the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantines</a>. A second such exchange took place some four years later.</p>
<p>Al-Mutawakkil's reign is remembered for its many reforms and viewed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_age" title="Golden age">golden age</a> of the Abbasids. He would be the last great Abbasid caliph; after his death the dynasty would fall into a decline.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Mutawakkil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Al-Mutawakkil continued to rely on <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkish</a> statesmen and slave soldiers to put down rebellions and lead battles against foreign empires, notably the Byzantines, from whom <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> was captured. His secretary, <a href="/wiki/Al-Fath_ibn_Khaqan" title="Al-Fath ibn Khaqan">Al-Fath ibn Khaqan</a>, who was Turkish, was a famous figure of Al-Mutawakkil's era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> His reliance on Turkish soldiers would come back to haunt him. Al-Mutawakkil would have his Turkish commander-in-chief killed. This, coupled with his extreme attitudes towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shia</a>, made his popularity decline rapidly.</p>
<p>Al-Mutawakkil had appointed his oldest son, <a href="/wiki/Al-Muntasir" title="Al-Muntasir">al-Muntasir</a>, as his heir in 849/50, but slowly had shifted his favour to his second son, <a href="/wiki/Al-Mu%27tazz" title="Al-Mu'tazz">al-Mu'tazz</a>, encouraged by al-Fath ibn Khaqan and the vizier <a href="/wiki/Ubayd_Allah_ibn_Yahya_ibn_Khaqan" title="Ubayd Allah ibn Yahya ibn Khaqan">Ubayd Allah ibn Yahya ibn Khaqan</a>. This rivalry extended into the political sphere, as al-Mu'tazz's succession appears to have been backed by the traditional Abbasid elites as well, while al-Muntasir was backed by the Turkish and <i><a href="/wiki/Maghariba_(Abbasid_troops)" title="Maghariba (Abbasid troops)">Maghariba</a></i> guard troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGordon200182_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGordon200182-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004169_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004169-11">[11]</a></sup> In late autumn 861, matters came to a head: in October, al-Mutawakkil ordered the estates of the Turkish general <a href="/wiki/Wasif_al-Turki" title="Wasif al-Turki">Wasif</a> to be confiscated and handed over to al-Fath. Feeling backed into a corner, the Turkish leadership began a plot to assassinate the Caliph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989171_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989171-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004168.E2.80.93169_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004168.E2.80.93169-13">[13]</a></sup> They were soon joined, or at least had the tacit approval, of al-Mustansir, who smarted from a succession of humiliations: on 5 December, on the recommendation of al-Fath and Ubayd Allah, he was bypassed in favour of al-Mu'tazz for leading the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friday_prayer" title="Friday prayer">Friday prayer</a> at the end of <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a>, while three days later, when al-Mutawakkil was feeling ill and chose al-Mustansir to represent him on the prayer, once again Ubayd Allah intervened and persuaded the Caliph to go in person. Even worse, according to <a href="/wiki/Al-Tabari" title="Al-Tabari">al-Tabari</a>, on the next day al-Mutawakkil alternately vilified and threatened to kill his eldest son, and even had al-Fath slap him on the face. With rumours circulating that Wasif and the other Turkish leaders would be rounded up and executed on 12 December, the conspirators decided to act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004169_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004169-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989171.E2.80.93173.2C_176_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989171.E2.80.93173.2C_176-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to al-Tabari, a story later circulated that al-Fath and Ubayd Allah were forewarned of the plot by a Turkish woman, but had disregarded it, confident that no-one would dare carry it out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989xx.2C_181_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989xx.2C_181-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006265_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006265-16">[16]</a></sup> On the night of 10/11 December, about one hour after midnight, the Turks burst in the chamber where the Caliph and al-Fath were having supper. Al-Fath was killed trying to protect the Caliph, who was killed next. Al-Muntasir, who now assumed the caliphate, initially claimed that al-Fath had murdered his father, and that he had been killed after; within a short time, however, the official story changed to al-Mutawakkil choking on his wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989171.E2.80.93182.2C_184.2C_195_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKraemer1989171.E2.80.93182.2C_184.2C_195-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006264.E2.80.93267_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2006264.E2.80.93267-18">[18]</a></sup> The murder of al-Mutawakkil began the tumultuous period known as "<a href="/wiki/Anarchy_at_Samarra" title="Anarchy at Samarra">Anarchy at Samarra</a>", which lasted until 870 and brought the Abbasid Caliphate to the brink of collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004169.E2.80.93173_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedy2004169.E2.80.93173-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment_of_.22People_of_the_book.22">Treatment of "People of the book"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Mutawakkil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: Treatment of "People of the book"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 850 Mutawakkil made a decree ordering <a href="/wiki/Dhimmi" title="Dhimmi">Dhimmi</a> (Jews) wear garments to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumptuary_laws" title="Sumptuary laws">distinguish them from Muslims</a>, their places of worship destroyed, demonic effigies nailed to the door, and that they be allowed little involvement government or official matters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webcitation_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-webcitation-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mutawakkil ordered the ancient sacred Cypress of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoroastrian" title="Zoroastrian">Zoroastrians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cypress_of_Kashmar" title="Cypress of Kashmar">Cypress of Kashmar</a> to be cut down in order to use it in constructing his new palace despite the enormous protests from the Zoroastrian community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The cypress was of legendary values to the Zoroastrians, believed to be brought from <a href="/wiki/Paradise" title="Paradise">Paradise</a> to the earth by <a href="/wiki/Zoroaster" title="Zoroaster">Zoroaster</a> was more than 1400 years old at the time. He was killed before the cypress wood arrived for his new palace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accomplishments">Accomplishments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Mutawakkil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accomplishments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Al-Mutawakkil was unlike his brother and father in that he was not known for having a thirst for knowledge, but he had an eye for magnificence and a hunger to build. <a href="/wiki/Great_Mosque_of_Samarra" title="Great Mosque of Samarra">The Great Mosque of Samarra</a> was at its time, the largest mosque in the world; its minaret is a vast spiralling cone 55 m high with a spiral ramp. The mosque had 17 aisles and its wall were panelled with mosaics of dark blue glass.</p>
<p>The Great Mosque was just part of an extension of Samarra eastwards that built upon part of the walled royal hunting park. Al-Mutawakkil built as many as 20 palaces (the numbers vary in documents). Samarra became one of the largest cities of the ancient world; even the archaeological site of its ruins is one of the world's most extensive. The Caliph's building schemes extended in A.H. 245 (859–860) to a new city, <i>al-Jaʻfariyya</i>, which al-Mutawakkil built on the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> some eighteen kilometres from Samarra. More water, and al-Mutawakkil ordered a canal to be built to divert water from the Tigris, entrusting the project to two courtiers, who ignored the talents of a local engineer of repute and entrusted the work to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Farghani" title="Al-Farghani">al-Farghanī</a>, the great astronomer and writer. Al-Farghanī, who was not a specialist in public works, made a miscalculation and it appeared that the opening of the canal was too deep so that water from the river would only flow at near full flood.</p>
<p>News leaked to the infuriated caliph might have meant the heads of all concerned save for the gracious actions of the engineer, <a href="/wiki/Sind_ibn_Ali" title="Sind ibn Ali">Sind ibn ʻAlī</a>, who vouched for the eventual success of the project, thus risking his own life. Al-Mutawakkil was assassinated shortly before the error became public.</p>
<p>Al-Mutawakkil was keen to involve himself in many religious debates, something that would show in his actions against different minorities. His father had tolerated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia_Imam" title="Shia Imam">Shīʻa Imām</a> who taught and preached at <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>, and for the first years of his reign al-Mutawakkil continued the policy. Imām <a href="/wiki/Ali_al-Hadi" title="Ali al-Hadi">ʻAlī al-Hadī</a>'s growing reputation inspired a letter from the Governor of Medina, ʻAbdu l-Lāh ibn Muħammad, suggesting that a coup was being plotted, and al-Mutawakkil extended an invitation to Samarra to the Imām, an offer he could not refuse. In Samarra, the Imām was kept under virtuial house arrest and spied upon. However, no excuse to take action against him ever appeared. After al-Mutawakkil's death, his successor had the Imām poisoned: al-Hadī is buried at Samarra. The general Shīʻa population faced repression. and this was embodied in the destruction of the shrine of <a href="/wiki/Husayn_ibn_Ali" title="Husayn ibn Ali">Hussayn ibn ʻAlī</a>, an action that was carried out ostensibly in order to stop pilgrimages to that site,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the flogging and incarceration of the Alid <a href="/wiki/Yahya_ibn_Umar" title="Yahya ibn Umar">Yahya ibn Umar</a>.</p>
<p>Also during his reign, Al-Mutawakkil met the famous <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> theologian <a href="/wiki/Saints_Cyril_and_Methodius" title="Saints Cyril and Methodius">Cyril the Philosopher</a>, who was sent to tighten the diplomatic relations between the Empire and the Caliphate in a state mission by the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Michael_III" title="Michael III">Michael III</a>. Of his sons, <a href="/wiki/Al-Muntasir" title="Al-Muntasir">al-Muntasir</a> succeeded him and ruled until his death in 862, <a href="/wiki/Al-Mu%27tazz" title="Al-Mu'tazz">al-Mu'tazz</a> reigned as Caliph from 866 to his overthrow in 869, and <a href="/wiki/Al-Mu%27tamid" title="Al-Mu'tamid">al-Mu'tamid</a> reigned as Caliph in 870–892 with his brother <a href="/wiki/Al-Muwaffaq" title="Al-Muwaffaq">al-Muwaffaq</a> serving as an effective regent of the realm until his death in 891.</p>
