<p><b>Hugo Alvar Henrik Aalto</b> (<small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Estonian_and_Finnish" title="Help:IPA for Estonian and Finnish">[ˈhuɡo ˈɑlʋɑr ˈhenrik ˈɑːlto]</a></span>; 3 February 1898 – 11 May 1976) was a Finnish architect and designer, as well as a sculptor and painter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chilvers_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chilvers-1">[1]</a></sup> His work includes architecture, furniture, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glassware" title="Glassware">glassware</a>. Aalto's early career runs in parallel with the rapid economic growth and industrialization of Finland during the first half of the twentieth century and many of his clients were industrialists; among these were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahlstr%C3%B6m-Gullichsen_family" title="Ahlström-Gullichsen family">Ahlström-Gullichsen family</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahlstr.C3.B6m_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahlstr.C3.B6m-2">[2]</a></sup> The span of his career, from the 1920s to the 1970s, is reflected in the styles of his work, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Classicism" title="Nordic Classicism">Nordic Classicism</a> of the early work, to a rational <a href="/wiki/International_Style_(architecture)" title="International Style (architecture)">International Style</a> Modernism during the 1930s to a more organic modernist style from the 1940s onwards. His furniture designs were considered <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_design" title="Scandinavian design">Scandinavian Modern</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boy-3">[3]</a></sup> What is typical for his entire career, however, is a concern for design as a <a href="/wiki/Gesamtkunstwerk" title="Gesamtkunstwerk">Gesamtkunstwerk</a>, a <i>total work of art</i>; whereby he – together with his first wife <a href="/wiki/Aino_Aalto" title="Aino Aalto">Aino Aalto</a> – would design not just the building, but give special treatments to the interior surfaces and design furniture, lamps, and furnishings and glassware. The Alvar Aalto Museum, designed by Aalto himself, is located in what is regarded as his home city <a href="/wiki/Jyv%C3%A4skyl%C3%A4" title="Jyväskylä">Jyväskylä</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hugo Alvar Henrik Aalto was born in <a href="/wiki/Kuortane" title="Kuortane">Kuortane</a>, Finland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His father, Johan Henrik Aalto, was a Finnish-speaking land-surveyor and his mother, Selly (Selma) Matilda (née Hackstedt) was a Swedish-speaking postmistress. When Aalto was 5 years old, the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Alaj%C3%A4rvi" title="Alajärvi">Alajärvi</a>, and from there to <a href="/wiki/Jyv%C3%A4skyl%C3%A4" title="Jyväskylä">Jyväskylä</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Finland" title="Central Finland">Central Finland</a>. Aalto studied at the Jyväskylä Lyceum school, where he completed his basic education in 1916 and took drawing lessons from a local artist named <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Heiska" title="Jonas Heiska">Jonas Heiska</a>. In 1916 he then enrolled to study architecture at the <a href="/wiki/Helsinki_University_of_Technology" title="Helsinki University of Technology">Helsinki University of Technology</a>. His studies were interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Civil_War" title="Finnish Civil War">Finnish Civil War</a>, which he fought in. He fought on the side of the <i><a href="/wiki/White_Guard_(Finland)" title="White Guard (Finland)">White Army</a></i> and fought at the Battle of Länkipohja and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tampere" title="Battle of Tampere">Battle of Tampere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He built his first piece of architecture while still a student, a house for his parents, at <a href="/wiki/Alaj%C3%A4rvi" title="Alajärvi">Alajärvi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labo1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Labo1-8">[8]</a></sup> Afterwards, he continued his education, graduating in 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB-9">[9]</a></sup> In the summer of 1922 he began his official military service, finishing at the Hamina reserve officer training school, and was promoted to reserve second lieutenant in June 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heporauta_1999_10_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heporauta_1999_10-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1920, while still a student, Aalto made his first trip abroad, travelling via <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a>, where he even briefly found work with the architect Arvid Bjerke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1922, he accomplished his first independent piece at the Industrial Exposition in <a href="/wiki/Tampere" title="Tampere">Tampere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labo1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Labo1-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1923 he returned to <a href="/wiki/Jyv%C3%A4skyl%C3%A4" title="Jyväskylä">Jyväskylä</a>, where he opened his first architectural office under the name 'Alvar Aalto, Architect and Monumental Artist'. At that same time he also wrote articles for the Jyväskylä newspaper <i>Sisä-Suomi</i> under the pseudonym Remus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heporauta_1999_10_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heporauta_1999_10-10">[10]</a></sup> During this time, he designed a number of small single-family houses in Jyväskylä, and the office's workload steadily increased.</p>
<p>On October 6, 1924, Aalto married architect <a href="/wiki/Aino_Aalto" title="Aino Aalto">Aino Marsio</a>; their honeymoon journey to Italy was Aalto's first trip there, though Aino had previously made a study trip there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SK_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SK-12">[12]</a></sup> The latter trip together sealed an intellectual bond with the culture of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean</a> region that was to remain important to Aalto for the rest of his life. On their return, they continued with a number of local projects, notably the Jyväskylä Worker's Club, which incorporated a number of motifs which they had studied during their trip, most notably the decorations of the Festival hall modelled on the <a href="/wiki/Rucellai_Sepulchre" title="Rucellai Sepulchre">Rucellai Sepulchre</a> in Florence by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a>. Following winning the architecture competition for the Southwest Finland Agricultural Cooperative building in 1927 the Aaltos moved their office to <a href="/wiki/Turku" title="Turku">Turku</a>. They had made contact with the city's most progressive architect, <a href="/wiki/Erik_Bryggman" title="Erik Bryggman">Erik Bryggman</a>, already before moving, and they then began collaborating with him, most notably on the Turku Fair of 1928-29. Aalto's biographer, Göran Schildt, claimed that Bryggman was the only architect with whom Aalto cooperated as an equal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> With increasing works in the Finnish capital, the Aaltos' office moved again in 1933 to Helsinki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Aaltos designed and built a <a href="/wiki/Villa_Aalto" title="Villa Aalto">joint house-office</a> (1935–36) for themselves in <a href="/wiki/Munkkiniemi" title="Munkkiniemi">Munkkiniemi</a>, Helsinki, but later (1954–56) had a <a href="/wiki/Studio_Aalto" title="Studio Aalto">purpose-built office</a> erected in the same neighbourhood – nowadays the former is a "home museum" and the latter the premises of the Alvar Aalto Academy. In 1926 the young Aaltos designed and had built for themselves a summer cottage in <a href="/wiki/Alaj%C3%A4rvi" title="Alajärvi">Alajärvi</a>, Villa Flora. Aino Aalto died of cancer in 1949. Aino and Alvar Aalto had 2 children, a daughter Johanna "Hanni" Alanen, born Aalto, 1925, and a son Hamilkar Aalto, 1928. In 1952 Aalto married architect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elissa_M%C3%A4kiniemi" title="Elissa Mäkiniemi">Elissa Mäkiniemi</a> (died 1994), who had been working as an assistant in his office. In 1952 Aalto designed and had built a summer cottage, the <a href="/wiki/Alvar_Aalto_Museum#The_Muuratsalo_Experiental_House" title="Alvar Aalto Museum">so-called Experimental House</a>, for himself and his new wife in <a href="/wiki/Muuratsalo" title="Muuratsalo">Muuratsalo</a> in Central Finland. Alvar Aalto died on 11 May 1976, in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, and is buried in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hietaniemi_cemetery" title="Hietaniemi cemetery">Hietaniemi cemetery</a> in Helsinki. His wife and the office employees continued the works of the office which were still in progress. In 1978 the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Finnish_Architecture" title="Museum of Finnish Architecture">Museum of Finnish Architecture</a> in Helsinki arranged a major exhibition of Aalto's works.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_career">Architecture career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Architecture career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career:_classicism">Early career: classicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early career: classicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although he is sometimes regarded as among the first and most influential architects of Nordic <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a>, a closer examination of the historical facts reveals that Aalto (while a pioneer in Finland) closely followed and had personal contacts with other pioneers in Sweden, in particular <a href="/wiki/Gunnar_Asplund" title="Gunnar Asplund">Gunnar Asplund</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sven_Markelius" title="Sven Markelius">Sven Markelius</a>.<a href="#CITEREFAalto1998">Aalto 1998</a>, p. 76 What they and many others of that generation in the Nordic countries had in common was that they started off from a classical education and were first designing classical architecture, though what historians now call <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Classicism" title="Nordic Classicism">Nordic Classicism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> – a style that had been a reaction to the previous dominant style of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_romantic_style" title="National romantic style">National Romanticism</a> – before moving, in the late 1920s, towards Modernism. On returning to Jyväskylä in 1923 to establish his own architect's office, Aalto busied himself with a number of single-family homes, all designed in the Nordic Classicism style, such as the manor-like house for his mother's cousin Terho Manner in Töysa in 1923, a summer villa for the Jyväskylä chief constable in 1923 and the Alatalo farmhouse in Tarvaala in 1924. During this period he also completed his first public buildings, the Jyväskylä Workers' Club in 1925, the Jyväskylä Defence Corps building in 1926 and the Seinäjoki Defence Corp building in 1924–29. Aalto also entered several architectural competitions for prestigious state public buildings, both in Finland and abroad, including the two competitions for the Finnish Parliament building in 1923 and 1924, the extension to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">University of Helsinki</a> in 1931, and the building to house the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> in Geneva, Switzerland, in 1926–27. Furthermore, this was the period when Aalto was most prolific in his writings, with articles for professional journals and newspapers. Among his most well-known essays from this period are "Urban culture" (1924),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> "Temple baths on Jyväskylä ridge" (1925),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> "Abbé Coignard's sermon" (1925),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and "From doorstep to living room" (1926).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career:_functionalism">Early career: functionalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early career: functionalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The shift in Aalto's design approach from classicism to modernism is epitomised by the <a href="/wiki/Vyborg_Library" title="Vyborg Library">Viipuri Library</a> (1927–35), which went through a transformation from an originally classical competition entry proposal to the completed high-modernist building. Yet his humanistic approach is in full evidence in the library: the interior displays natural materials, warm colours, and undulating lines. Due to problems over financing and a change of site, the Viipuri Library project lasted eight years, and during that same time he also designed the Standard Apartment Building (1928-29) in Turku, Turun Sanomat Building (1929–30) and Paimio Sanatorium (1929–32). A number of factors heralded Aalto's shift towards modernism: on a personal level, Aalto's increased familiarization of international trends especially after travelling throughout Europe, but in terms of completed projects it was the client of the Standard Apartment Building giving Aalto the opportunity to experiment with concrete prefabrication, the cutting-edge Corbusian form language of the Turun Sanomat Building, and these were then carried forward both in the Paimio Sanatorium and in the ongoing design for the library. Although the Turun Sanomat Building and Paimio Sanatorium are comparatively pure modernist works, they too carried the seeds of his questioning of such an orthodox modernist approach and a move to a more daring, synthetic attitude. It has been pointed out that the planning principle for Paimio Sanatorium - the splayed wings - was indebted to the Zonnestraal Sanatorium (1925-31) by Jan Duiker, which Aalto visited while it was still under construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> But while these early Functionalist works by Aalto bear hallmarks of influences from <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gropius" title="Walter Gropius">Walter Gropius</a> and other key modernist figures of central Europe, in all these buildings Aalto nevertheless started to show his individuality in a departure from such norms with the introduction of organic references.</p>
<p>Through Sven Markelius, Aalto became a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congres_Internationaux_d%27Architecture_Moderne" title="Congres Internationaux d'Architecture Moderne">Congres Internationaux d'Architecture Moderne</a> (CIAM), attending the second congress in Frankfurt in 1929 and the fourth congress in Athens in 1933, where he established a close friendship with <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Moholy-Nagy" title="László Moholy-Nagy">László Moholy-Nagy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigfried_Giedion" title="Sigfried Giedion">Sigfried Giedion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morton_Shand" title="Philip Morton Shand">Philip Morton Shand</a>. It was during this time that he followed closely the work of the main driving force behind the new modernism, <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>, and visited him in his Paris office several times in the following years.</p>
<p>It was not until the completion of the Paimio Sanatorium (1932) and Viipuri Library (1935) that Aalto first achieved world attention in architecture. His reputation grew in the USA following the critical reception of his design for the Finnish Pavilion at the <a href="/wiki/1939_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1939 New York World's Fair">1939 New York World's Fair</a>, described by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a> as a "work of genius".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It could be said that Aalto's international reputation was sealed with his inclusion in the second edition of Sigfried Giedion's influential book on Modernist architecture, <i>Space, Time and Architecture: The growth of a new tradition</i> (1949), in which Aalto received more attention than any other Modernist architect, including <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>. In his analysis of Aalto, Giedion gave primacy to qualities that depart from direct functionality, such as mood, atmosphere, intensity of life and even national characteristics, declaring that "Finland is with Aalto wherever he goes".</p>
<p>In 1938, the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>, in New York organized an exhibit that eventually went on a 12-city tour. Afterwards he visited America for the first time and gave a series of lectures at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale" title="Yale">Yale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labo1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Labo1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid_career:_experimentation">Mid career: experimentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mid career: experimentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the 1930s Alvar spent some time experimenting with laminated wood, making sculptures, and abstract reliefs, characterized by irregular curved forms. Utilizing this knowledge he was able to solve technical problems concerning the flexibility of wood and also of working out spatial issues in his designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chilvers_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chilvers-1">[1]</a></sup> Aalto's early experiments with wood and his move away from a purist modernism would be tested in built form with the commission to design Villa Mairea (1939) in <a href="/wiki/Noormarkku" title="Noormarkku">Noormarkku</a>, the luxury home of the young industrialist couple Harry and <a href="/wiki/Maire_Gullichsen" title="Maire Gullichsen">Maire Gullichsen</a>. It was Maire Gullichsen who acted as the main client, and she worked closely not only with Alvar but also Aino Aalto on the design, inspiring them to be more daring in their work. The original design was to include a <a href="/wiki/Art_museum" title="Art museum">private art gallery</a>, but this was never built. The building forms a U-shape around a central inner "garden" the central feature of which is a kidney-shaped swimming pool. Adjacent to the pool is a sauna executed in a rustic style, alluding to both Finnish and Japanese precedents. The design of the house is a synthesis of numerous stylistic influences, from traditional Finnish vernacular to purist modernism, as well as influences from English and Japanese architecture. While the house is clearly intended for a wealthy family, Aalto nevertheless argued that it was also an experiment that would prove useful in the design of mass housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>His increased fame led to offers and commissions outside Finland. In 1941 he accepted an invitation as a visiting professor to <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> in the USA. This was during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, and he involved his students in designing low-cost, small-scale housing for the reconstruction of war-torn Finland. While teaching at MIT, Aalto also designed the student dormitory, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_MIT_undergraduate_dormitories#Baker_House" title="List of MIT undergraduate dormitories">Baker House</a>, completed in 1948. The dormitory lay along the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> and its undulating form provided maximum view and ventilation for each resident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This building was the first building of Aalto's redbrick period. Originally used in Baker House to signify the Ivy League university tradition, on his return to Finland Aalto used it in a number of key buildings, in particular, in several of the buildings in the new <a href="/wiki/Helsinki_University_of_Technology" title="Helsinki University of Technology">Helsinki University of Technology</a> campus (starting in 1950), <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A4yn%C3%A4tsalo_Town_Hall" title="Säynätsalo Town Hall">Säynätsalo Town Hall</a> (1952), Helsinki Pensions Institute (1954), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kulttuuritalo" title="Kulttuuritalo">Helsinki House of Culture</a> (1958), as well as in his own summer house, the so-called Experimental House in Muuratsalo (1957).</p>
<p>In the fifties Aalto immersed himself in his sculpting, be it with wood, bronze, marble, or mixed media. Among the notable works from this period is the memorial to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Suomussalmi" title="Battle of Suomussalmi">Battle of Suomussalmi</a> (1960); located on the battlefield, it consists of a leaning bronze pillar on a pedestal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chilvers_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chilvers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mature_career:_monumentalism">Mature career: monumentalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mature career: monumentalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The early 1960s and 1970s (up until his death in 1976) were marked by key works in Helsinki, in particular the huge town plan for the void in centre of Helsinki adjacent to <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B6%C3%B6l%C3%B6" title="Töölö">Töölö</a> Bay and the vast <a href="/wiki/VR_warehouses" title="VR warehouses">railway yards</a>, and marked on the edges by significant buildings such as the National Museum and the main railway station, both by <a href="/wiki/Eliel_Saarinen" title="Eliel Saarinen">Eliel Saarinen</a>. In his town plan Aalto proposed a line of separate marble-clad buildings fronting the bay which would house various cultural institutions, including a concert hall, opera, museum of architecture and headquarters for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_Academy" title="Finnish Academy">Finnish Academy</a>. The scheme also extended into the <a href="/wiki/Kamppi" title="Kamppi">Kamppi</a> district with a series of tall office blocks. Aalto first presented his scheme in 1961, but it went through various modifications during the early 1960s. Only two fragments of the overall plan were ever realized: the <a href="/wiki/Finlandia_Hall" title="Finlandia Hall">Finlandia Hall</a> concert hall (1976) fronting Töölö Bay, and an office building in the Kamppi district for the Helsinki Electricity Company (1975). The <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe" title="Ludwig Mies van der Rohe">Miesian</a> formal language of geometric grids employed in the buildings was also used by Aalto for other sites in Helsinki, including the <a href="/wiki/Stora_Enso" title="Stora Enso">Enso-Gutzeit</a> building (1962), the <a href="/wiki/Stockmann" title="Stockmann">Academic Bookstore</a> (1962) and the SYP Bank building (1969).</p>
<p>Following Aalto's death in 1976 his office continued to operate under the direction of his widow, Elissa, completing works already to some extent designed. These works include the Jyväskylä City Theatre and <a href="/wiki/Aalto_Theatre" title="Aalto Theatre">Essen opera house</a>. Since the death of Elissa Aalto the office has continued to operate as the Alvar Aalto Academy, giving advice on the restoration of Aalto buildings and organising the vast archive material.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Furniture_career">Furniture career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Furniture career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whereas Aalto was famous for his architecture, his furniture designs were well thought of and are still popular today. He studied <a href="/wiki/Josef_Hoffmann" title="Josef Hoffmann">Josef Hoffmann</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wiener_Werkst%C3%A4tte" title="Wiener Werkstätte">Wiener Werkstätte</a>, and for a period of time, worked under <a href="/wiki/Eliel_Saarinen" title="Eliel Saarinen">Eliel Saarinen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boy_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boy-3">[3]</a></sup> He also gained inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Gebr%C3%BCder_Thonet" title="Gebrüder Thonet">Gebrüder Thonet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boy_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boy-3">[3]</a></sup> During the late 1920s and 1930s he, working closely with Aino Aalto, also focused a lot of his energy on furniture design, partly due to the decision to design much of the individual furniture pieces and lamps for the Paimio Sanatorium. Of particular significance was the experimentation in bent plywood chairs, most notably the so-called Paimio chair, which had been designed for the sitting tuberculosis patient. The Aaltos, together with visual arts promoter Maire Gullichsen and art historian Nils-Gustav Hahl founded the <a href="/wiki/Artek_(company)" title="Artek (company)">Artek</a> company in 1935, ostensibly to sell Aalto products but also other imported products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He became the first furniture designer to use the cantilever principle in chair design using wood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boy_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Boy-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aalto's awards included the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Eugen_Medal" title="Prince Eugen Medal">Prince Eugen Medal</a> in 1954, the Royal Gold Medal for Architecture from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_British_Architects" title="Royal Institute of British Architects">Royal Institute of British Architects</a> in 1957 and the <a href="/wiki/AIA_Gold_Medal" title="AIA Gold Medal">Gold Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Architects" title="American Institute of Architects">American Institute of Architects</a> in 1963. He was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-28">[28]</a></sup> He also was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Finland" title="Academy of Finland">Academy of Finland</a>, and was its president from 1963 to 1968. From 1925 to 1956 he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Congr%C3%A8s_International_d%27Architecture_Moderne" title="Congrès International d'Architecture Moderne">Congrès International d'Architecture Moderne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1960 he received an honorary doctorate at the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_University_of_Science_and_Technology" title="Norwegian University of Science and Technology">Norwegian University of Science and Technology</a> (NTNU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aalto's career spans the changes in style from (<a href="/wiki/Nordic_Classicism" title="Nordic Classicism">Nordic Classicism</a>) to purist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_style_(architecture)" title="International style (architecture)">International Style</a> Modernism to a more personal, synthetic and idiosyncratic Modernism. Aalto's wide field of design activity ranges from the large scale of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_planning" title="City planning">city planning</a> and architecture to interior design, furniture and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glassware" title="Glassware">glassware</a> design and painting. It has been estimated that during his entire career Aalto designed over 500 individual buildings, approximately 300 of which were built, the vast majority of which are in Finland. He also has a few buildings in France, Germany, Italy and the USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GS_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-GS-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aalto's work with wood, was influenced by early Scandinavian architects; however, his experiments and departure from the norm brought attention to his ability to make wood do things not previously done. His techniques in the way he cut the <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> tree, for example, and also his ability to use plywood as structural and aesthetic. Other examples include the rough-hewn vertical placement of logs at his pavilion at the <a href="/wiki/Lapua" title="Lapua">Lapua</a> expo, looking similar to a medieval barricade, at the orchestra platform at <a href="/wiki/Turku" title="Turku">turku</a> and the Paris expo at the World Fair, he used varying sizes and shapes of planks. Also at Paris and at <a href="/wiki/Villa_Mairea" title="Villa Mairea">Villa Mairea</a> he utilized birch boarding in a vertical arrangement. Also his famous undulating walls and ceilings made of red pine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labo2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Labo2-31">[31]</a></sup> In his roofing, he created massive spans (155-foot at the covered statium at <a href="/wiki/Otaniemi" title="Otaniemi">Otaniemi</a>) all without tie rods. His stairway at Villa Mairea, he evokes feelings of a natural forest by binding beech wood with withes into columns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Labo3_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Labo3-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aalto claimed that his paintings were not made as individual artworks but as part of his process of architectural design, and many of his small-scale "sculptural" experiments with wood led to later larger architectural details and forms. These experiments also led to a number of patents: for example, he invented a new form of laminated bent-plywood furniture in 1932 (which was patented in 1933).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chilvers_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chilvers-1">[1]</a></sup> His experimental method had been influenced by his meetings with various members of the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> design school, especially <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Moholy-Nagy" title="László Moholy-Nagy">László Moholy-Nagy</a>, whom he first met in 1930. Aalto's furniture was exhibited in London in 1935, to great critical acclaim, and to cope with the consumer demand Aalto, together with his wife Aino, <a href="/wiki/Maire_Gullichsen" title="Maire Gullichsen">Maire Gullichsen</a> and Nils-Gustav Hahl founded the company <a href="/wiki/Artek_(company)" title="Artek (company)">Artek</a> that same year. Aalto glassware (Aino as well as Alvar) is manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Iittala" title="Iittala">Iittala</a>.</p>
<p>Aalto's 'High Stool' and 'Stool E60' (manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Artek_(company)" title="Artek (company)">Artek</a>) are currently used in <a href="/wiki/Apple_Store" title="Apple Store">Apple Stores</a> across the world to serve as seating for customers. Finished in black lacquer, the stools are used to seat customers at the 'Genius Bar' and also in other areas of the store at times when seating is required for a product workshop or special event. Aalto was also influential in bringing <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a> to the knowledge of the Finnish people, in particular the work of his friends, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Milne_Calder" title="Alexander Milne Calder">Alexander Milne Calder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Léger">Fernand Léger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chilvers_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chilvers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critique_of_Aalto.27s_architecture">Critique of Aalto's architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Critique of Aalto's architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As already mentioned, Aalto's international reputation was sealed with his inclusion in the second edition of Sigfried Giedion's influential book on Modernist architecture, <i>Space, Time and Architecture: The growth of a new tradition</i> (1949), in which Aalto received more attention than any other Modernist architect, including <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>. In his analysis of Aalto, Giedion gave primacy to qualities that depart from direct functionality, such as mood, atmosphere, intensity of life and even national characteristics, declaring that "Finland is with Aalto wherever he goes". However, a few more recent architecture critics and historians have questioned Aalto's position of influence in the canonic history. Italian Marxist architecture historians <a href="/wiki/Manfredo_Tafuri" title="Manfredo Tafuri">Manfredo Tafuri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Dal_Co" title="Francesco Dal Co">Francesco Dal Co</a> put forward the viewpoint that Aalto's "historical significance has perhaps been rather exaggerated; with Aalto we are outside of the great themes that have made the course of contemporary architecture so dramatic. The qualities of his works have a meaning only as masterful distractions, not subject to reproduction outside the remote reality in which they have their roots."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Their viewpoint was propounded by their own priority given to urbanism, seeing Aalto as an anti-urban, and thus consequently disparaging what they regarded as peripheral non-urban areas of the world: "Essentially his architecture is not appropriate to urban typologies." Similarly concerned with the appropriateness of Aalto's form language, at the other end of the political spectrum, American postmodernist critic <a href="/wiki/Charles_Jencks" title="Charles Jencks">Charles Jencks</a> made a claim for the need for buildings to signify meaning; however, he then lifted out Aalto's Pensions Institute building as an example of what he termed Aalto's 'soft paternalism': "Conceived as a fragmented mass to break up the feeling of bureaucracy, it succeeds all too well in being humane and killing the pensioner with kindness. The forms are familiar red brick and ribbon-strip windows broken by copper and bronze elements – all carried through with a literal-mindedness that borders on the soporific."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> But also during Aalto's lifetime he faced critique from his fellow architects in Finland, most notably Kirmo Mikkola and <a href="/wiki/Juhani_Pallasmaa" title="Juhani Pallasmaa">Juhani Pallasmaa</a>; by the last decade of his life Aalto's work was seen as idiosyncratic and individualistic, when the opposing tendencies of rationalism and constructivism – often championed under left-wing politics – argued for anonymous virtually non-aesthetic architecture. Mikkola wrote of Aalto's late works: "Aalto has moved to his present baroque line..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alvar_Aalto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aalto has been commemorated in a number of ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>Alvar Aalto is the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Alvar_Aalto_Medal" title="Alvar Aalto Medal">Alvar Aalto Medal</a>, now considered one of world architecture's most prestigious awards.</li>
<li>Aalto was featured in the 50 mk note in the last series of the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_markka" title="Finnish markka">Finnish markka</a> (before its replacement by the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a> in 2002).</li>
<li>The centenary of Aalto's birth in 1898 was marked in Finland not only by several books and exhibitions, but also by the promotion of specially bottled red and white Aalto Wine and a specially designed <a href="/wiki/Cupcake" title="Cupcake">cupcake</a>.</li>
<li>In 1976, the year of his death, Aalto was commemorated on a Finnish postage stamp.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aalto_University" title="Aalto University">Aalto University</a>, a Finnish university formed by merging <a href="/wiki/Helsinki_University_of_Technology" title="Helsinki University of Technology">Helsinki University of Technology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helsinki_School_of_Economics" title="Helsinki School of Economics">Helsinki School of Economics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Art_and_Design_Helsinki" title="University of Art and Design Helsinki">TaiK</a> in 2010, is named after Alvar Aalto.</li>
<li>An Alvar Aallon katu (Alvar Aalto Street) can be found in five different Finnish cities: Helsinki, Jyväskylä, Oulu, Kotka and Seinäjoki.</li>
</ul>

<dl>
<dt>Aalto research</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The extensive archives of Alvar Aalto are nowadays kept at the <a class="external text" href="http://www.alvaraalto.fi/museum/" rel="nofollow">Alvar Aalto Museum</a>, Jyväskylä, Finland. Material is also available from the former offices of Aalto, at Tiilimäki 20, Helsinki, nowadays the headquarters of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.alvaraalto.fi/" rel="nofollow">Alvar Aalto Foundation.</a></li>
<li>Since 1995 the Alvar Aalto Museum and Aalto Academy has published a journal, <a class="external text" href="http://www.alvaraalto.fi/ptah/" rel="nofollow"><i>Ptah</i></a>, which is devoted not only to Aalto scholarship but also to architecture generally as well as theory, design and art.</li>
</ul>
