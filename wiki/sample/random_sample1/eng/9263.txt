<p><b>Ludwig Karl Martin Leonhard Albrecht Kossel</b> (16 September 1853 – 5 July 1927) was a German <a href="/wiki/Biochemist" title="Biochemist">biochemist</a> and pioneer in the study of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>. He was awarded the Nobel Prize for <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1910 for his work in determining the chemical composition of <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>, the genetic substance of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">biological cells</a>.</p>
<p>Kossel isolated and described the five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a> that are present in <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acid</a>: <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>. These compounds were later shown to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleobases" title="Nucleobases">nucleobases</a>, and are key in the formation of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, the genetic material found in all living cells.</p>
<p>Kossel was an important influence on and collaborator with other important researchers in biochemistry, including <a href="/wiki/Henry_Drysdale_Dakin" title="Henry Drysdale Dakin">Henry Drysdale Dakin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Miescher" title="Friedrich Miescher">Friedrich Miescher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_B._Hart" title="Edwin B. Hart">Edwin B. Hart</a>, and his professor and mentor, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Hoppe-Seyler" title="Felix Hoppe-Seyler">Felix Hoppe-Seyler</a>. Kossel was editor of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Chemistry_(journal)" title="Biological Chemistry (journal)">Zeitschrift für Physiologische Chemie</a> (Journal of Physiological Chemistry) from 1895 until his death.</p>
<p>Kossel also conducted important research into the composition of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, and his research predicted the discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptide" title="Polypeptide">polypeptide</a> nature of the protein molecule.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Kossel_Institute_for_Neuroregeneration" title="Albrecht Kossel Institute for Neuroregeneration">Albrecht Kossel Institute for Neuroregeneration</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rostock" title="University of Rostock">University of Rostock</a> is named in his honor.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kossel was born in <a href="/wiki/Rostock" title="Rostock">Rostock</a>, Germany as the son of the <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consulate_general" title="Consulate general">consul</a> Albrecht Karl Ludwig Enoch Kossel and his wife Clara Jeppe Kossel. As a youth, Kossel attended the Gymnasium at Rostock, where he evidenced substantial interest in chemistry and botany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1872, Kossel attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Strassburg" title="University of Strassburg">University of Strassburg</a> to study medicine. He studied under <a href="/wiki/Felix_Hoppe-Seyler" title="Felix Hoppe-Seyler">Felix Hoppe-Seyler</a>, who was head of the department of biochemistry, the only such institution in Germany at the time. He attended lectures by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anton_de_Bary" title="Anton de Bary">Anton de Bary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Wilhelm_Gottfried_von_Waldeyer-Hartz" title="Heinrich Wilhelm Gottfried von Waldeyer-Hartz">Waldeyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/August_Kundt" title="August Kundt">August Kundt</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Wilhelm_Adolf_von_Baeyer" title="Johann Friedrich Wilhelm Adolf von Baeyer">Baeyer</a>. He completed his studies at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rostock" title="University of Rostock">University of Rostock</a>, and passed his German medical license exam in 1877.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_research_and_collaboration">Early research and collaboration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early research and collaboration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After completing his university studies, Kossel returned to the University of Strassburg as research assistant to Felix Hoppe-Seyler. At the time, Hoppe-Seyler was intensely interested in research concerning an acidic substance that had first been chemically isolated from pus cells by one of his former students, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Miescher" title="Friedrich Miescher">Friedrich Miescher</a>, in 1869. Unlike protein, the substance contained considerable amounts of phosphorus, but with its high acidity, it was unlike any cellular substance that had yet been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kossel showed that the substance, called "nuclein", consisted of a protein component and a non-protein component. Kossel further isolated and described the non-protein component. This substance has become known as <i><a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acid</a></i>, which contains the genetic information found in all living cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isolation_and_description_of_nucleobases">Isolation and description of nucleobases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isolation and description of nucleobases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1883, Kossel left Strassburg to become Director of the Chemistry Division of the Physiological Institute at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>. In this post, he succeeded <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Baumann" title="Eugen Baumann">Eugen Baumann</a> and worked under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Emil_du_Bois-Reymond" title="Emil du Bois-Reymond">Emil du Bois-Reymond</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kossel continued his previous work on the nucleic acids. During the period 1885 to 1901, he was able to isolate and name its five constituent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>: <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>. These compounds are now known collectively as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleobases" title="Nucleobases">nucleobases</a>, and they provide the molecular structure necessary in the formation of stable <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_into_the_chemical_composition_of_protein">Research into the chemical composition of protein</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research into the chemical composition of protein">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1895, Kossel was professor of physiology as well as director of the Physiological Institute at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Marburg" title="University of Marburg">University of Marburg</a>. Around this time, he began investigations into the chemical composition of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, the alterations in proteins during transformation into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptone" title="Peptone">peptone</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a> components of cells, and other investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1896, Kossel discovered <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a>, then worked out the classical method for the quantitative separation of the "hexone bases" (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-amino_acid" title="Alpha-amino acid">alpha-amino acids</a> <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>). He was also the first to isolate <a href="/wiki/Theophylline" title="Theophylline">Theophylline</a>, a therapeutic drug found naturally in tea and cocoa beans.</p>
<p>In 1901, Kossel was named to a similar post at <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_University" title="Heidelberg University">Heidelberg University</a>, and became director of the Heidelberg Institute for Protein Investigation. His research predicted the discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptide" title="Polypeptide">polypeptide</a> nature of the protein molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nobel_prize">Nobel prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nobel prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The processes of life are like a drama, and I am studying the actors, not the plot. There are many actors, and it is their characters which make this drama. I seek to understand their habits, their peculiarities.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Albrecht Kossel, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> interview</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_19110827_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_19110827-3">[3]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Kossel was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1910 for his research in <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>, the chemical composition of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>, and for his work in isolating and describing <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>. The award was presented on 10 December 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the autumn of 1911, Kossel was invited to the United States to deliver the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Archibald_Herter_(physician)#The_Christian_A._Herter_Lectures" title="Christian Archibald Herter (physician)">Herter Lecture</a> at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins</a>. Traveling with his wife Luise and daughter Gertrude, he took the opportunity to travel and to visit acquaintances, one of which was <a href="/wiki/Eugene_W._Hilgard" title="Eugene W. Hilgard">Eugene W. Hilgard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_emeritus" title="Professor emeritus">professor emeritus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_chemistry" title="Agricultural chemistry">agricultural chemistry</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Berkeley" title="University of California at Berkeley">University of California at Berkeley</a>, who was also his wife's cousin. He also visited and delivered lectures at several other universities, including the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the occasion of his visit to New York City, Kossel was interviewed by a reporter from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a>. Kossel's English was reportedly very good, and his self-effacing modesty is voluminously mentioned in the reporter's account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_19110827_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_19110827-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>His Herter lecture at Johns Hopkins was titled, "The Proteins". This was the only time Kossel ever visited the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_research_and_collaboration">Later research and collaboration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later research and collaboration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With his distinguished English pupil <a href="/wiki/Henry_Drysdale_Dakin" title="Henry Drysdale Dakin">Henry Drysdale Dakin</a>, Kossel investigated <a href="/wiki/Arginase" title="Arginase">arginase</a>, the ferment which hydrolyses <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> into <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ornithine" title="Ornithine">ornithine</a>. Later, he discovered <a href="/wiki/Agmatine" title="Agmatine">agmatine</a> in herring roe and devised a method for preparing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another of Kossel's students was American <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Edwin_B._Hart" title="Edwin B. Hart">Edwin B. Hart</a>, who would later return to the United States to participate in the "<a href="/wiki/Single-grain_experiment" title="Single-grain experiment">Single-grain experiment</a>" (1907–1911) and be part of research teams that would determine the <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutritive</a> causes of <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goitre" title="Goitre">goiter</a>. Another was <a href="/wiki/Otto_Folin" title="Otto Folin">Otto Folin</a>, an American chemist who discovered <a href="/wiki/Phosphocreatine" title="Phosphocreatine">Phosphocreatine</a>.</p>
<p>In 1923, Kossel was honored by being named Germany's representative to the Eleventh Physiological Congress in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, Scotland. When he appeared before the assembled scientists, they gave him an ovation that lasted several minutes. At the congress, he was conferred an honorary degree by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1924, Kossel became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_emeritus" title="Professor emeritus">professor emeritus</a>, but continued to lecture at <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_University" title="Heidelberg University">Heidelberg University</a>. In April, 1927, he attended the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Lister,_1st_Baron_Lister" title="Joseph Lister, 1st Baron Lister">Lister</a> Centenary Celebration held in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the last years of Kossel's life, he conducted important research into the composition of the protein types <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protamines" title="Protamines">protamines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histones" title="Histones">histones</a>, and discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavianic_acid" title="Flavianic acid">flavianic acid</a>. A monograph describing this work was published shortly after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeitschrift_f.C3.BCr_Physiologische_Chemie">Zeitschrift für Physiologische Chemie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Zeitschrift für Physiologische Chemie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kossel contributed to early issues of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Chemistry_(journal)" title="Biological Chemistry (journal)">Zeitschrift für Physiologische Chemie</a> (Journal of Physiological Chemistry). This publication was founded by his professor and mentor, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Hoppe-Seyler" title="Felix Hoppe-Seyler">Felix Hoppe-Seyler</a>, in 1877, the same year that Kossel started work as his research assistant. After Hoppe-Seyler's death in 1895, Kossel took over editorship of the Zeitschrift and continued in that role until his own death in 1927.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kossel_grab.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1118" data-file-width="1600" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Kossel_grab.JPG/220px-Kossel_grab.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Kossel_grab.JPG/330px-Kossel_grab.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Kossel_grab.JPG/440px-Kossel_grab.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kossel_grab.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kossel's grave in <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1886, Kossel married Luise Holtzman, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Holtzmann" title="Adolf Holtzmann">Adolf Holtzmann</a>. Holtzmann was Professor at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">University of Heidelberg</a>, lecturing in <a href="/wiki/German_literature" title="German literature">German literature</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>. He was also a noted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philologist" title="Philologist">philologist</a> of his day. The couple had three children, two of whom survived to maturity: Walther, born in 1888, and daughter Gertrude, born in 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Son <a href="/wiki/Walther_Kossel" title="Walther Kossel">Walther Kossel</a> (1888–1956) became a prominent <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> and was professor of <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a> and director of the Physics Institute at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of Tübingen">University of Tübingen</a>. He is known for his theory of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bond</a>/<a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Sommerfeld%E2%80%93Kossel_displacement_law" title="Sommerfeld–Kossel displacement law">Sommerfeld–Kossel displacement law</a>, and other achievements.</p>
<p>There is no record of any political activity by Kossel, and by all accounts he was apolitical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through his marriage to Luise, Kossel was related to several prominent Americans, including <a href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">soil science</a> pioneer <a href="/wiki/Eugene_W._Hilgard" title="Eugene W. Hilgard">Eugene W. Hilgard</a>, journalist and financier <a href="/wiki/Henry_Villard" title="Henry Villard">Henry Villard</a>, and abolitionist <a href="/wiki/William_Lloyd_Garrison" title="William Lloyd Garrison">William Lloyd Garrison</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Luise Kossel died in 1913 of <a href="/wiki/Acute_pancreatitis" title="Acute pancreatitis">acute pancreatitis</a>. Kossel died quietly on 5 July 1927, after a recurring attack of <a href="/wiki/Angina_pectoris" title="Angina pectoris">angina pectoris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup> He is buried in <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, Germany.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_Kossel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The study of the living organism has more and more led to the view that its smallest independent units morphologically speaking – the cells – also to a certain degree lead an independent life and are the real seats of the vital processes. The cells therefore attract special attention in biological research, and studies which widen our knowledge of the cells to any important extent deserve to be given prominence. Prof. Kossel has chosen to devote himself to this field of research, and it is for his work in this respect that the Nobel Prize has been awarded to him this year.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Nobel Prize Introduction Speech, December 10, 1910<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NobelBio_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NobelBio-4">[4]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Albrecht Kossel is considered one of the great scientists of biochemistry and genetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_Jones_1953_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Yale_Jones_1953-1">[1]</a></sup> By isolating and defining <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acid</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleobases" title="Nucleobases">nucleobases</a>, he provided the necessary precursors that led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_structure_of_Nucleic_Acids" title="Molecular structure of Nucleic Acids">double-helix model</a> of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, devised by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_D._Watson" title="James D. Watson">James D. Watson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a> in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Kossel_Institute_for_Neuroregeneration" title="Albrecht Kossel Institute for Neuroregeneration">Albrecht Kossel Institute for Neuroregeneration</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rostock" title="University of Rostock">University of Rostock</a> is named in his honor.</p>
