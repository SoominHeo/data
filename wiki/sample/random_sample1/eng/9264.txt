<p><b>Albert of Prussia</b> (17 May 1490 – 20 March 1568) was the 37th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Masters_of_the_Teutonic_Knights" title="Grand Masters of the Teutonic Knights">Grand Master</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a>, who after converting to <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a>, became the first monarch of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Prussia" title="Duchy of Prussia">Duchy of Prussia</a>, the secularized state that emerged from the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monastic_State_of_the_Teutonic_Knights" title="Monastic State of the Teutonic Knights">Monastic State of the Teutonic Knights</a>. Albert was the first European ruler to establish Lutheranism, and thus <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, as the official <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">state religion</a> of his lands. He proved instrumental in the political spread of Protestantism in its early stage, ruling the Prussian lands for nearly six decades (1510–1568).</p>
<p>A member of the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Ansbach" title="Principality of Ansbach">Brandenburg-Ansbach</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">House of Hohenzollern</a>, Albert became Grand Master, where his skill in political administrator and leadership ultimately succeeded in reversing the decline of the Teutonic Order. But Albert, who was sympathetic to the demands of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>, rebelled against the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> by converting the Teutonic state into a Protestant and hereditary realm, the Duchy of Prussia, for which he paid homage to his uncle, the <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_I_the_Old" title="Sigismund I the Old">King of Poland, Sigismund I</a>. That arrangement was confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Treaty of Kraków">Treaty of Kraków</a> in 1525. Albert pledged a personal oath to the King and in return was invested with the duchy for himself and his heirs.</p>
<p>Albert's rule in Prussia was fairly prosperous. Although he had some trouble with the peasantry, the confiscation of the lands and treasures of the Catholic Church enabled him to propitiate the nobles and provide for the expenses of the newly-established Prussian court. He was active in imperial politics, joining the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Torgau" title="League of Torgau">League of Torgau</a> in 1526, and acted in unison with the Protestants in plotting to overthrow Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> after the issue of the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Interim" title="Augsburg Interim">Augsburg Interim</a> in May 1548. Albert established schools in every town and founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_University" title="Königsberg University">Königsberg University</a> in 1544.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> He promoted culture and arts, patronising the works of <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Reinhold" title="Erasmus Reinhold">Erasmus Reinhold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Hennenberger" title="Caspar Hennenberger">Caspar Hennenberger</a>. During the final years of his rule, Albert was forced to raise taxes instead of further confiscating now-depleted church lands, causing peasant rebellion. The intrigues of the court favourites <a href="/wiki/Johann_Funck" title="Johann Funck">Johann Funck</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Skali%C4%87" title="Paul Skalić">Paul Skalić</a> also led to various religious and political disputes. Albert spent his final years virtually deprived of power and died at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tapiau" title="Tapiau">Tapiau</a> on 20 March 1568. His son, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Frederick,_Duke_of_Prussia" title="Albert Frederick, Duke of Prussia">Albert Frederick</a>, succeeded him as Duke of Prussia.</p>
<p>Albert's dissolution of the Teutonic State caused the founding of the Duchy of Prussia, paving the way for the rise of the House of Hohenzollern. He is therefore often seen as the father of the Prussian nation, and even as indirectly responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert,_Duke_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Albert was born in <a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a> in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> as the third son of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Ansbach" title="Frederick I, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach">Frederick I, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach</a>. His mother was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophia_of_Poland" title="Sophia of Poland">Sophia</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Casimir_IV_Jagiellon" title="Casimir IV Jagiellon">Casimir IV Jagiellon</a>, Grand Duke of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Lithuania" title="Grand Duchy of Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and King of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1385%E2%80%931569)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1385–1569)">Poland</a>, and his wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Austria_(d._1505)" title="Elisabeth of Austria (d. 1505)">Elisabeth of Austria</a>. He was raised for a career in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Church</a> and spent some time at the court of <a href="/wiki/Hermann_IV_of_Hesse" title="Hermann IV of Hesse">Hermann IV of Hesse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Cologne" title="Electorate of Cologne">Elector of Cologne</a>, who appointed him canon of the <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Cathedral" title="Cologne Cathedral">Cologne Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> Not only was he quite religious; he was also interested in mathematics and science and sometimes is claimed to have contradicted the teachings of the Church in favour of scientific theories. His career was forwarded by the Church, however, and institutions of the Catholic clerics supported his early advancement.</p>
<p>Turning to a more active life, Albert accompanied Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in 1508 and after his return spent some time in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_Frederick_of_Saxony" title="Duke Frederick of Saxony">Duke Frederick of Saxony</a>, Grand Master of the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a>, died in December 1510. Albert was chosen as his successor early in 1511 in the hope that his relationship to his maternal uncle, <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_I_the_Old" title="Sigismund I the Old">Sigismund I the Old</a>, Grand Duke of Lithuania and King of Poland, would facilitate a settlement of the disputes over eastern <a href="/wiki/Prussia_(region)" title="Prussia (region)">Prussia</a>, which had been held by the Order under Polish <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Second_Peace_of_Thorn_(1466)" title="Second Peace of Thorn (1466)">Second Peace of Thorn (1466)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new Grand Master, aware of his duties to the empire and to the papacy, refused to submit to the crown of Poland. As war over the Order's existence appeared inevitable, Albert made strenuous efforts to secure allies and carried on protracted negotiations with Emperor Maximilian I. The ill-feeling, influenced by the ravages of members of the Order in Poland, culminated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Teutonic_War_(1519%E2%80%931521)" title="Polish–Teutonic War (1519–1521)">a war</a> which began in December 1519 and devastated Prussia. Albert was granted a four-year truce early in 1521.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dispute was referred to Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> and other princes, but as no settlement was reached Albert continued his efforts to obtain help in view of a renewal of the war. For this purpose he visited the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Diet of Nuremberg</a> in 1522, where he made the acquaintance of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformer</a> <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Osiander" title="Andreas Osiander">Andreas Osiander</a>, by whose influence Albert was won over to <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Grand Master then journeyed to <a href="/wiki/Wittenberg" title="Wittenberg">Wittenberg</a>, where he was advised by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> to abandon the rules of his Order, to marry, and to convert Prussia into a hereditary duchy for himself. This proposal, which was understandably appealing to Albert, had already been discussed by some of his relatives; but it was necessary to proceed cautiously, and he assured <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_VI" title="Pope Adrian VI">Pope Adrian VI</a> that he was anxious to reform the Order and punish the knights who had adopted <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> doctrines. Luther for his part did not stop at the suggestion, but in order to facilitate the change made special efforts to spread his teaching among the Prussians, while Albert's brother, Margrave <a href="/wiki/George,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Ansbach" title="George, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach">George of Brandenburg-Ansbach</a>, laid the scheme before their uncle, <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_I_the_Old" title="Sigismund I the Old">Sigismund I the Old</a> of Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duke_in_Prussia">Duke in Prussia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert,_Duke_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Duke in Prussia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prussian_Homage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="575" data-file-width="1183" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Prussian_Homage.jpg/350px-Prussian_Homage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Prussian_Homage.jpg/525px-Prussian_Homage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Prussian_Homage.jpg/700px-Prussian_Homage.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>

<p>After some delay Sigismund assented to the offer, with the provision that Prussia should be treated as a Polish fiefdom; and after this arrangement had been confirmed by a <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Treaty of Kraków">treaty concluded at Kraków</a>, Albert pledged a <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Homage" title="Prussian Homage">personal oath</a> to Sigismund I and was invested with the duchy for himself and his heirs on 10 February 1525.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">Estates</a> of the land then met at <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> and took the oath of allegiance to the new duke, who used his full powers to promote the doctrines of Luther. This transition did not, however, take place without protest. Summoned before the imperial court of justice, Albert refused to appear and was proscribed, while the Order elected a new Grand Master, <a href="/wiki/Walter_von_Cronberg" title="Walter von Cronberg">Walter von Cronberg</a>, who received Prussia as a fief at the imperial <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Augsburg" title="Diet of Augsburg">Diet of Augsburg</a>. As the German princes were experiencing the tumult of the Reformation, the <a href="/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War" title="German Peasants' War">German Peasants' War</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_wars_in_Europe" title="Ottoman wars in Europe">wars against the Ottoman Turks</a>, they did not enforce the ban on the duke, and agitation against him soon died away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In imperial politics Albert was fairly active. Joining the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Torgau" title="League of Torgau">League of Torgau</a> in 1526, he acted in unison with the Protestants, and was among the princes who banded and plotted together to overthrow Charles V after the issue of the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Interim" title="Augsburg Interim">Augsburg Interim</a> in May 1548. For various reasons, however, poverty and personal inclination among others, he did not take a prominent part in the military operations of this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Albrecht_von_Brandenburg-Preu%C3%9Fen_Groschen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="243" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Albrecht_von_Brandenburg-Preu%C3%9Fen_Groschen.jpg/170px-Albrecht_von_Brandenburg-Preu%C3%9Fen_Groschen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b7/Albrecht_von_Brandenburg-Preu%C3%9Fen_Groschen.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a>

One <a href="/wiki/Groschen" title="Groschen">Groschen</a> coin, 1534, <i><a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Galatians" title="Epistle to the Galatians">Iustus ex fide vivit</a> — The Just lives on Faith</i></div>
</div>

<p>The early years of Albert's rule in Prussia were fairly prosperous. Although he had some trouble with the peasantry, the lands and treasures of the church enabled him to propitiate the nobles and for a time to provide for the expenses of the court. He did something for the furtherance of learning by establishing schools in every town and by freeing serfs who adopted a scholastic life. In 1544, in spite of some opposition, he founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_University" title="Königsberg University">Königsberg University</a>, where he appointed his friend Andreas Osiander to a professorship in 1549.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> Albert also paid for the printing of the Astronomical "<a href="/wiki/Prutenic_Tables" title="Prutenic Tables">Prutenic Tables</a>" compiled by <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Reinhold" title="Erasmus Reinhold">Erasmus Reinhold</a> and the first maps of Prussia by <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Hennenberger" title="Caspar Hennenberger">Caspar Hennenberger</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Osiander's appointment was the beginning of the troubles which clouded the closing years of Albert's reign. Osiander's divergence from Luther's doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Justification_(theology)" title="Justification (theology)">justification</a> by faith involved him in a violent quarrel with <a href="/wiki/Philip_Melanchthon" title="Philip Melanchthon">Philip Melanchthon</a>, who had adherents in Königsberg, and these theological disputes soon created an uproar in the town. The duke strenuously supported Osiander, and the area of the quarrel soon broadened. There were no longer church lands available with which to conciliate the nobles, the burden of taxation was heavy, and Albert's rule became unpopular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Osiander's death in 1552, Albert favoured a preacher named <a href="/wiki/Johann_Funck" title="Johann Funck">Johann Funck</a>, who, with an adventurer named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Skali%C4%87" title="Paul Skalić">Paul Skalić</a>, exercised great influence over him and obtained considerable wealth at public expense. The state of turmoil caused by these religious and political disputes was increased by the possibility of Albert's early death and the need, should that happen, to appoint a <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">regent</a>, as his only son, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Frederick,_Duke_of_Prussia" title="Albert Frederick, Duke of Prussia">Albert Frederick</a> was still a mere youth. The duke was forced to consent to a condemnation of the teaching of Osiander, and the climax came in 1566 when the <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">Estates</a> appealed to King <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_II_Augustus" title="Sigismund II Augustus">Sigismund II Augustus</a> of Poland, Albert's cousin, who sent a commission to Königsberg. Skalić saved his life by flight, but Funck was executed. The question of the regency was settled, and a form of Lutheranism was adopted and declared binding on all teachers and preachers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Virtually deprived of power, the duke lived for two more years, and died at <a href="/wiki/Gvardeysk" title="Gvardeysk">Tapiau</a> on 20 March 1568<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> of the plague, along with his wife. <a href="/wiki/Cornelis_Floris_de_Vriendt" title="Cornelis Floris de Vriendt">Cornelis Floris de Vriendt</a> designed his tomb within <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Cathedral" title="Königsberg Cathedral">Königsberg Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Albert was a voluminous letter writer, and corresponded with many of the leading personages of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert,_Duke_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="512" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg/220px-Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg/330px-Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg/440px-Dom_Fuerstengruft.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Although Albert has received relatively little recognition in German history,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> his dissolution of the Teutonic State caused the founding of the Duchy of Prussia, which would eventually become arguably the most powerful German state and instrumental in uniting the whole of Germany. Albert is therefore often seen as the father of the Prussian nation, and even as indirectly responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He was a skilled political administrator and leader, and effectively reversed the decline of the Teutonic Order, until he betrayed it by transforming the order's lands into his own duchy, secularizing it in the process.</p>
<p>Albert was the first German noble to support Luther's ideas and in 1544 founded the <a href="/wiki/University_of_K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="University of Königsberg">University of Königsberg</a>, the Albertina, as a rival to the Roman Catholic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cracow_Academy" title="Cracow Academy">Cracow Academy</a>. It was the second Lutheran university in the German states, after the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Marburg" title="University of Marburg">University of Marburg</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a> of Albert over the Renaissance-era portal of <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Castle" title="Königsberg Castle">Königsberg Castle</a>'s southern wing was created by Andreas Hess in 1551 according to plans by Christoph Römer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M90_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-M90-5">[5]</a></sup> Another relief by an unknown artist was included in the wall of the Albertina's original campus. This depiction, which showed the duke with his sword over his shoulder, was the popular "Albertus", the symbol of the university. The original was moved to <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Public_Library" title="Königsberg Public Library">Königsberg Public Library</a> to protect it from the elements, while the sculptor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Kimritz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Kimritz (page does not exist)">Paul Kimritz</a> created a duplicate for the wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M90_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-M90-5">[5]</a></sup> Another version of the "Albertus" by Lothar Sauer was included at the entrance of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%B6nigsberg_State_and_Royal_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Königsberg State and Royal Library (page does not exist)">Königsberg State and Royal Library</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M90_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-M90-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1880 <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Reusch" title="Friedrich Reusch">Friedrich Reusch</a> created a sandstone bust of Albert at the Regierungsgebäude, the administrative building for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk_K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Regierungsbezirk Königsberg">Regierungsbezirk Königsberg</a>. On 19 May 1891 Reusch premiered a famous statue of Albert at <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Castle" title="Königsberg Castle">Königsberg Castle</a> with the inscription: "Albert of Brandenburg, Last Grand Master, First Duke in Prussia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albert_Wolff_(sculptor)" title="Albert Wolff (sculptor)">Albert Wolff</a> also designed an equestrian statue of Albert located at the new campus of the Albertina. <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Gate_(Kaliningrad)" title="King's Gate (Kaliningrad)">King's Gate</a> contains a statue of Albert.</p>
<p>Albert was oft-honored in the quarter <a href="/wiki/Maraunenhof" title="Maraunenhof">Maraunenhof</a> in northern Königsberg. Its main street was named Herzog-Albrecht-Allee in 1906. Its town square, König-Ottokar-Platz, was renamed Herzog-Albrecht-Platz in 1934 to match its church, the <a href="/wiki/Herzog-Albrecht-Ged%C3%A4chtniskirche" title="Herzog-Albrecht-Gedächtniskirche">Herzog-Albrecht-Gedächtniskirche</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Albert married first, to Princess <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_of_Denmark,_Duchess_of_Prussia" title="Dorothea of Denmark, Duchess of Prussia">Dorothea</a> (1 August 1504 – 11 April 1547), daughter of King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I_of_Denmark" title="Frederick I of Denmark">Frederick I of Denmark</a>, in 1526. They had six children:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anna_Sophia_of_Prussia" title="Anna Sophia of Prussia">Anna Sophia</a> (11 June 1527 – 6 February 1591), married <a href="/wiki/John_Albert_I,_Duke_of_Mecklenburg" title="John Albert I, Duke of Mecklenburg">John Albert I, Duke of Mecklenburg-Güstrow</a>.</li>
<li>Katharina (b. and d. 24 February 1528).</li>
<li>Frederick Albert (5 December 1529 – 1 January 1530).</li>
<li>Lucia Dorothea (8 April 1531 – 1 February 1532).</li>
<li>Lucia (3 February 1537 – May 1539).</li>
<li>Albert (b. and d. March 1539).</li>
</ul>
<p>He married secondly to <a href="/wiki/Anna_Marie_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Anna Marie of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Anna Maria</a> (1532–20 March 1568), daughter of <a href="/wiki/Eric_I,_Duke_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Eric I, Duke of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Eric I</a>, Duke of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Brunswick-Lüneburg">Brunswick-Lüneburg</a>, in 1550. The couple had two children:</p>
<ul>
<li>Elisabeth (20 May 1551 – 19 February 1596).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albert_Frederick,_Duke_of_Prussia" title="Albert Frederick, Duke of Prussia">Albert Frederick</a> (29 April 1553 – 18 August 1618), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Prussia" title="Duke of Prussia">Duke of Prussia</a>.</li>
</ul>
