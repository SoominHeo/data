<p><b>Sigma Draconis</b> (σ Dra, σ Draconis) is a 4.7-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_magnitude" title="Visual magnitude">magnitude</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> located at a distance of 18.8 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Draco_(constellation)" title="Draco (constellation)">Draco</a>. Its traditional name is <b>Alsafi</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen1963_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-allen1963-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma_Draconis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sigma Draconis, the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_designation" title="Bayer designation">Bayer designation</a> for this star, was established in 1603 as part of the <i><a href="/wiki/Uranometria" title="Uranometria">Uranometria</a></i>, a star catalogue produced by German celestial cartographer <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bayer" title="Johann Bayer">Johann Bayer</a>. The proper name <i>Alsafi</i> was derived from Arabic <b>Athāfi</b>, erroneously transcribed from the Arabic plural <b>Athāfiyy</b>, by which the nomads designated the tripods of their open-air kitchens. It was the name of an association of this star, <a href="/wiki/Tau_Draconis" title="Tau Draconis">τ Dra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upsilon_Draconis" title="Upsilon Draconis">υ Dra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen1963_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-allen1963-10">[10]</a></sup> According to the catalogue of stars in the <i>Technical Memorandum 33-507 - A Reduced Star Catalog Containing 537 Named Stars</i>, <i>Athāfi</i> or <i>Alsafi</i> were the title for three stars: σ Dra as <i>Alsafi</i>, τ Dra as <i>Athāfi I</i> and υ Dra as <i>Athāfi II</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33_507_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-33_507-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">天廚</span> (<span lang="zh-Latn" xml:lang="zh-Latn">Tiān Chú</span>), meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Purple_Forbidden_enclosure" title="Purple Forbidden enclosure">Celestial Kitchen</a></i>, refers to an asterism consisting of σ Draconis, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Draconis" title="Delta Draconis">δ Draconis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_Draconis" title="Epsilon Draconis">ε Draconis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rho_Draconis" title="Rho Draconis">ρ Draconis</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=64_Draconis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="64 Draconis (page does not exist)">64 Draconis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pi_Draconis" title="Pi Draconis">π Draconis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Consequently, σ Draconis itself is known as <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">天廚二</span> (<span lang="zh-Latn" xml:lang="zh-Latn">Tiān Chú èr</span>, English: the Second Star of Celestial Kitchen.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma_Draconis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The star is a <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> dwarf of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_type" title="Spectral type">spectral type</a> K0 V, which has long served as a K0 V spectral standard star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keenan89_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keenan89-16">[16]</a></sup> Its classification as K0 V defines one of the rare anchor points of the Morgan–Keenan system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrison_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garrison-17">[17]</a></sup> that have remained unchanged since the original 1943 MKK Atlas. The stellar radius has been directly measured using interferometry with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CHARA_Array" title="CHARA Array">CHARA Array</a>, which yields a result of 77.8% of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_radius" title="Solar radius">Sun's radius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj683_1_424_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-apj683_1_424-7">[7]</a></sup> It has 87% of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">Sun's mass</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">luminosity</a> of this star is only 43% that of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa36_191_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-aaa36_191-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj683_1_424_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-apj683_1_424-7">[7]</a></sup> The projected <a href="/wiki/Stellar_rotation" title="Stellar rotation">rotation rate</a> (<i>v</i> sin <i>i</i>) is relatively low at 1.4 km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa555_A104_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-aaa555_A104-8">[8]</a></sup> It is considered a slightly <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metal-poor</a> star, meaning that it has a lower proportion of elements with masses greater than <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> when compared to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ab6_2_308_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ab6_2_308-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The temperature, luminosity and surface activity appear to vary slightly in a manner very similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspot_cycle" title="Sunspot cycle">sunspot cycle</a>, although the full length of the cycle has not yet been determined (as of 1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj400_2_681_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-apj400_2_681-19">[19]</a></sup> The total variability is among the lowest of all stars that have been measured by the <a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">Hipparcos</a> spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ab6_2_308_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ab6_2_308-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sigma Draconis has a high <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a>, advancing across the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a> at a rate of 1.835 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_second" title="Arc second">arc seconds</a> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lepine2005_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-lepine2005-20">[20]</a></sup> The star made its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> passage about 46,725 years ago, when it came within 16.55 ly (5.075 pc).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa575_A35_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-aa575_A35-21">[21]</a></sup> The components of Sigma Draconis's <a href="/wiki/Stellar_kinematics" title="Stellar kinematics">space velocity</a> are U=+36, V=+40 and W=-10 km/s. This gives the star an unusually large <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> galaxy of 0.30 (compared to 0.06 for the Sun.) The mean galactocentric distance for the orbit is 10.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiloparsec" title="Kiloparsec">kiloparsecs</a> (about 34,000 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ab6_2_308_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-ab6_2_308-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2013, no Jupiter-size or larger companion had been detected about the star and there was no indication of <a href="/wiki/Infrared_excess" title="Infrared excess">excess infrared radiation</a> that would be evidence of circumstellar matter (such as a <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">debris disk</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa555_A104_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-aaa555_A104-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aj125_6_3334_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-aj125_6_3334-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planet_search">Planet search</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigma_Draconis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Planet search">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 2004 and 2013, extensive radial velocity measurements were gathered on Sigma Draconis using the High Resolution Echelle Spectrometer on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_Observatory" title="Keck Observatory">Keck Observatory</a>. The Keck/HIRES data indicated a possible period of about 300 days and a likely <a href="/wiki/Aliasing" title="Aliasing">alias</a> period of 2800 days. Adding data taken with the Automated Planet Finder at the <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">Lick Observatory</a> strengthened and narrowed the 300-day period while reducing the significance of the 2800-day period. The combined analysis suggests there may be a <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>-mass planet on a 308-day orbit, though the authors do not yet consider the discovery to be publishable as they have not yet attempted to rule out other, non-planetary explanations for the velocity variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vogt_et_al_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vogt_et_al-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
