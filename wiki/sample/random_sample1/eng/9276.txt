<p><b>Altera Corporation</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Programmable_logic_device" title="Programmable logic device">programmable logic devices</a> (PLDs), reconfigurable complex digital circuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zacks13April2012_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zacks13April2012-1">[1]</a></sup> The company is a wholly owned <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a> of <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>. Altera released its first PLD in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Altera's main products are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratix_(FPGA)" title="Stratix (FPGA)">Stratix</a>, Arria and Cyclone series <a href="/wiki/Field-programmable_gate_array" title="Field-programmable gate array">FPGAs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zacks13April2012_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zacks13April2012-1">[1]</a></sup> the MAX series <a href="/wiki/Complex_programmable_logic_device" title="Complex programmable logic device">CPLDs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zacks13April2012_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zacks13April2012-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Altera_Quartus" title="Altera Quartus">Quartus II</a> design software,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Enpirion PowerSoC DC-DC power solutions.</p>
<p>Altera and <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> announced on June 1, 2015 that they have agreed that Intel would acquire Altera in an all-cash transaction valued at approximately $16.7 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup> As of December 28, 2015, the acquisition had been completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FPGAs">FPGAs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: FPGAs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Stratix" title="Stratix">Stratix</a> series FPGAs are the company's largest, highest bandwidth devices, with up to 1.1 million logic elements, integrated transceivers at up to 28 Gbit/s, up to 1.6 Tbit/s of serial switching capability, up to 1,840 GMACs of signal-processing performance, and up to 7 x72 DDR3 memory interfaces at 800 MHz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Cyclone series FPGAs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SoC" title="SoC">SoC</a> FPGAs are the company's lowest cost, lowest power FPGAs, with variants offering integrated transceivers up to 5 Gbit/s. In between these two device families are Arria series FPGAs, which provide a balance of performance, power, and cost for mid-range applications such as remote radio heads, video conferencing equipment, and wireline access equipment. Arria FPGAs have integrated transceivers up to 10 Gbit/s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SoC_FPGAs">SoC FPGAs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: SoC FPGAs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since December 2012, the company has been shipping SoC FPGA devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> According to Altera, fully depleted silicon on insulator (FDSOI) chip manufacturing process is beneficial for FPGAs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These devices integrate FPGAs with full hard processor systems based around <a href="/wiki/ARM_architecture" title="ARM architecture">ARM processors</a> onto a single device.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PowerSoC">PowerSoC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: PowerSoC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2013, Altera acquired embedded power chipmaker Enpirion for $134m in cash ($141m including the assumption of debt). Since that time, Enpirion has been incorporated into Altera by becoming its own product offering within the Altera portfolio of products. The Enpirion products are power system-on-a-chip DC-DC converters that enable greater power densities and lower noise performance compared with their discrete equivalent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike converters made from discrete components Enpirion dc-dc converters are simulated, characterized, validated and production qualified at delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ASICs">ASICs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: ASICs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Previously Altera offered a publicly available ASIC design flow based on HardCopy ASICs, which transitioned an FPGA design, once finalized, to a form which is not alterable. This design flow reduced design security risks as well as costs for higher volume production. Design engineers could prototype their designs in Stratix series FPGAs, and then migrate these designs to HardCopy ASICs when they were ready for volume production.</p>
<p>The unique design flow makes hardware/software co-design and co-verification possible. The flow has been benchmarked to deliver systems to market 9 to 12 months faster, on average, than with standard-cell solutions. Design engineers can employ a single <a href="/wiki/Register-transfer_level" title="Register-transfer level">RTL</a>, set of intellectual property (IP) cores, and Quartus II design software for both FPGA and ASIC implementations. Altera's HardCopy Design Center manages test insertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>BaySand provides similar service to Altera's HardCopy called MetalCopy. MetalCopy is based on BaySand's Metal Configurable Standard Cells (MCSC) and BaySand's ASIC design methodology flow. MetalCopy consists of taking the design from RTL to ASIC including Synthesis, Physical Layout, timing closure, test insertion, Build In Test, formal verification and static timing analysis. BaySand currently offers Metal Copy for all FPGA designs including 28nm and 14nm FPGA devices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IP_cores">IP cores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: IP cores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Altera and its partners offer an array of intellectual property (IP) cores that serve as building blocks that design engineers can drop into their system designs to perform specific functions. IP cores eliminate some of the time-consuming tasks of creating every block in a design from scratch.</p>
<p>Altera offers an embedded portfolio with a broad selection of soft processor cores:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nios_II" title="Nios II">Nios II</a> embedded processor</li>
<li>Freescale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ColdFire" title="ColdFire">ColdFire</a> v1 core (free for Cyclone III FPGA).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ARM_Cortex-M1" title="ARM Cortex-M1">ARM Cortex-M1</a> processor</li>
</ul>
<p>And one hard IP processor core:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/ARM_Cortex-A9" title="ARM Cortex-A9">ARM Cortex-A9</a> processor</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_software">Design software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Altera_Quartus" title="Altera Quartus">Altera Quartus</a></div>
<p>All of Altera's devices are supported by a common design environment, <a href="/wiki/Altera_Quartus#Quartus_II" title="Altera Quartus">Quartus II</a> design software. Quartus II software is available in a subscription-based edition and a free Web-based edition. It includes a number of tools to foster productivity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="40-nm_technology">40-nm technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 40-nm technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2008, Altera introduced the industry's first 40-nm programmable logic devices: the Stratix IV FPGAs and HardCopy IV ASICs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Both devices are available with integrated transceiver options. Since then, the company has also introduced Stratix IV GT FPGAs, which have 11.3 Gbit/s transceivers for 40G/100G applications,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and Arria II GX FPGAs, which have 3.75 Gbit/s transceivers for power- and cost-sensitive applications.</p>
<p>Semiconductors manufactured on a 40-nm process node address many of the industry's key challenges, including power consumption, device performance, and cost. Altera's devices are manufactured using techniques such as 193-nm <a href="/wiki/Immersion_lithography" title="Immersion lithography">immersion lithography</a> and technologies such as extreme low-k dielectrics and <a href="/wiki/Strained_silicon" title="Strained silicon">strained silicon</a>. These techniques and technologies bring enhancements to device performance and power efficiency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="28-nm_technology">28-nm technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 28-nm technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2010, Altera introduced the FPGA industry's second 28-nm device, the Stratix V FPGA (to Xilinx's Kintex-7 FPGA), available with transceivers at speeds up to 28 Gbit/s. This device family has more than 1 million logic elements, up to 53 Mb of embedded memory, up to 7 x72 DDR3 DIMMs at 800 MHz, 1.6 Gbit/s LVDS performance, and up to 3,680 variable-precision DSP blocks. In August 2011, Altera began shipping 28-nm Stratix V GT devices featuring 28-gigabits-per-second transceivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-August242011_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-August242011-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The devices also feature some unique features. Embedded HardCopy blocks harden standard or logic-intensive applications, increasing integration and delivering twice the density without a cost or power penalty. Altera has developed a user friendly method for partial reconfiguration, so core functionality can be changed easily and on the fly. And there is a path to HardCopy V ASICs, when designs are ready for volume production. Also, Altera’s 28 nm FPGAs aim to reduce power requirements to 200 mW per channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-August242011_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-August242011-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2012, the company announced the shipment of its first 28 nm Cyclone V SoC devices, which have a dual-core <a href="/wiki/ARM_architecture" title="ARM architecture">ARM</a> Cortex-A9 processor system with FPGA logic on a single chip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Embedded12Dec2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Embedded12Dec2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EETAsia21Dec2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-EETAsia21Dec2012-17">[17]</a></sup> The new SoCs are targeted for wireless communications, industrial, video surveillance, automotive and medical equipment markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Embedded12Dec2012_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Embedded12Dec2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EETAsia21Dec2012_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-EETAsia21Dec2012-17">[17]</a></sup> With these SoCs devices, users are able to create custom field-programmable SoC variants for power, board space, performance and cost optimization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Embedded12Dec2012_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Embedded12Dec2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EETAsia21Dec2012_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-EETAsia21Dec2012-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="14-nm_technology">14-nm technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 14-nm technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2013, Altera announced an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> to use Intel’s <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_fabrication_plant" title="Semiconductor fabrication plant">foundry</a> services to produce its 14-nm node for the future manufacturing of its FPGAs, based on Intel’s 14 nm tri-gate transistor technology, in place of Altera’s ongoing agreement with <a href="/wiki/TSMC" title="TSMC">Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Corporation</a> (TSMC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2016, nearly one year after Intel's integration with Altera, STRATIX 10 was announced, which is based on Intel's 14nm Tri-Gate process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Altera's largest competitor is FPGA founder and market-share leader <a href="/wiki/Xilinx" title="Xilinx">Xilinx</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xilinx_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-xilinx-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next closest competitors are <a href="/wiki/Lattice_Semiconductor" title="Lattice Semiconductor">Lattice Semiconductor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Actel" title="Actel">Actel</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Microsemi" title="Microsemi">Microsemi</a>), each representing less than 10 percent of the market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xilinx_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-xilinx-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>FPGA startup company <a href="/wiki/Achronix" title="Achronix">Achronix</a> is also a competitor but does not have any significant market share.</p>
<p>In broader terms, Altera competes with <a href="/wiki/Application-specific_integrated_circuit" title="Application-specific integrated circuit">ASIC</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structured_ASIC" title="Structured ASIC">structured ASIC</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.baysand.com/technology" rel="nofollow">Metal Configurable Standard Cell (MCSC) like BaySand</a> and Zero Mask-Charge ASIC companies like <a href="/wiki/EASIC" title="EASIC">eASIC</a>.</p>
<p>Specifically in ASIC, BaySand has introduced metal configurable FPGA (mcFPGA) products to fill the needs due to discontinued HardCopy from Altera.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatement">Restatement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Restatement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 21, 2006, Altera Corp. restated its 1996-2005 financial results to correct accounting errors related to stock-based compensation expense. Altera's CFO resigned after an SEC investigation revealed the decade of misstated earning reports resulted from the company's alleged culture of backdating stock options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_by_Intel">Acquisition by Intel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Altera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Acquisition by Intel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 1, 2015, Altera and <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> announced that Intel would acquire Altera in an all-cash transaction valued at approximately £15.73 billion ($16.7 billion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18">[18]</a></sup> As of December 28, 2015, the acquisition has been completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
