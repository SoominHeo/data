<p><b>Alfonso XII</b> (Alfonso Francisco de Asís Fernando Pío Juan María de la Concepción Gregorio Pelayo; 28 November 1857 – 25 November 1885) was <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_monarchs" title="List of Spanish monarchs">King of Spain</a>, reigning from 1874 to 1885, after a <i>coup d'état</i> restored the monarchy and ended the ephemeral <a href="/wiki/First_Spanish_Republic" title="First Spanish Republic">First Spanish Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Having been forced into exile after the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution_(Spain)" title="Glorious Revolution (Spain)">Glorious Revolution</a> deposed his mother <a href="/wiki/Isabella_II_of_Spain" title="Isabella II of Spain">Isabella II</a> from the throne in 1868, Alfonso studied in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. His mother abdicated in his favour in 1870, and he returned to Spain as king in 1874 following a military coup. Alfonso died aged 27 in 1885, and was succeeded by his unborn son, who became <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XIII of Spain">Alfonso XIII</a> on his birth the following year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_background.2C_early_life_and_paternity">Political background, early life and paternity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfonso_XII_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political background, early life and paternity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1860" data-file-width="1656" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg/220px-Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg/330px-Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg/440px-Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pr%C3%ADncipe_Alfonso.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Prince Alfonso.</div>

<p>Alfonso was born in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> as the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Isabella_II_of_Spain" title="Isabella II of Spain">Queen Isabella II</a>. Officially, his father was her husband, <a href="/wiki/Francis,_Duke_of_C%C3%A1diz" title="Francis, Duke of Cádiz">King Francis</a>. Alfonso's biological paternity is uncertain: there is speculation that his biological father may have been Enrique Puigmoltó y Mayans (a captain of the guard).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These rumours were used as political propaganda against Alfonso by the <a href="/wiki/Carlism" title="Carlism">Carlists</a>.</p>
<p>His mother's accession created the second cause of instability, which was the Carlist Wars. The supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Infante_Carlos,_Count_of_Molina" title="Infante Carlos, Count of Molina">Count of Molina</a> as king of Spain rose to have him enthroned. In addition, within the context of the post-Napoleonic restorations and revolutions which engulfed the West both in Europe and the Americas, both the Carlistas as well as the Isabelino conservatives were opposed to the new Napoleonic constitutional system. Much like in Britain, which subtracted itself from the liberal constitutional process, Spanish conservatives wanted to continue with the Traditional Spanish Organic Laws such as the <a href="/wiki/Fuero_Juzgo" title="Fuero Juzgo">Fuero Juzgo</a>, the Novísima Recopilación and the <a href="/wiki/Siete_Partidas" title="Siete Partidas">Partidas of Alfonso X</a>. This led to the third cause of instability of note, the "Independence of the American Kingdoms", recognized between 1823 and 1850.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_split_nation">A split nation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfonso_XII_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A split nation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Queen Isabella and her husband were forced to leave Spain by the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution_(Spain)" title="Glorious Revolution (Spain)">Revolution of 1868</a>, Alfonso accompanied them to Paris. From there, he was sent to the <a href="/wiki/Theresianum" title="Theresianum">Theresianum</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> to continue his studies. On 25 June 1870, he was recalled to Paris, where his mother abdicated in his favour, in the presence of a number of Spanish nobles who had tied their fortunes to that of the exiled queen. He assumed the name Alfonso XII, for although no King of united Spain had borne the name "Alfonso XI", the Spanish monarchy was regarded as continuous with the more ancient monarchy represented by the 11 kings of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">Asturias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">León</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Castile</a> also named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso" title="Alfonso">Alfonso</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Republic">The Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfonso_XII_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/First_Spanish_Republic" title="First Spanish Republic">First Spanish Republic</a></div>
<p>After Amadeo's abandonment in 1873, Parliament declared the Federal Republic (including Cuba, Puerto Rico and the Pacific Archipelagos), the first act of President Estanislao Figueras was to extend the Abolition Act to Puerto Rico. Cuban slaves would have to wait until 1889.</p>
<p>But the republicans were not in agreement either, and they had to contend with the War in Cuba, and Muslim uprisings in Spanish Morocco. In the midst of the crisis during and after the First Spanish Republic, the <a href="/wiki/Carlism" title="Carlism">Carlist</a> party made itself strong in areas with claims over their national and institutional specificity, such as Catalonia or the Basque districts. By 1872, the <a href="/wiki/Third_Carlist_War" title="Third Carlist War">Third Carlist War</a> erupted. This unrest led to the creation of a group in favor of the Bourbon restoration, made by some sectors of the conservatives led by Canovas del Castillo.</p>
<p>The Prince of Asturias, Alfonso, was the person chosen to develop the new road map proposed by Canovas, which led to the June 1870 abdication of Queen Isabel II in favour of her son Prince Alfonso. The new road map, which indeed ended the eternal crisis begun in 1810 was called 'Alfonsismo', and the moderate centrist Cánovas del Castillo became the spokesman. As having Alfonso in Spain would be a problem, Cánovas became responsible for his education. He sent Alfonso to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandhurst_Military_Academy" title="Sandhurst Military Academy">Sandhurst Military Academy</a> in England, where the training Alfonso received was severe but more cosmopolitan than in Spain, given the current atmosphere.</p>
<p>On 1 December 1874, Alfonso issued the Sandhurst Manifesto, where he set the ideological basis of the Bourbon Restoration. It was drafted in reply to a birthday greeting from his followers, a manifesto proclaiming himself the sole representative of the Spanish monarchy. At the end of 1874, Brigadier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenio_Mart%C3%ADnez_Campos" title="Arsenio Martínez Campos">Martínez Campos</a>, who had long been working more or less openly for the king, led some battalions of the central army to <a href="/wiki/Sagunto" title="Sagunto">Sagunto</a>, rallied to his own flag the troops sent against him, and entered <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> in the king's name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thereupon the President resigned, and his power was transferred to the king's plenipotentiary and adviser, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_C%C3%A1novas_del_Castillo" title="Antonio Cánovas del Castillo">Antonio Cánovas</a>. The 29 December 1874 military coup of Gen. Martinez Campos in Sagunto ended the failed republic and meant the rise of the young Prince Alfonso.</p>

<p>Within a few days after Canovas del Castillo took power as Premier, the new king, proclaimed on 29 December 1874, arrived at Madrid, passing through <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> and was acclaimed everywhere (1875). In 1876, a vigorous campaign against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlists" title="Carlists">Carlists</a>, in which the young king took part, resulted in the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Carlos,_Duke_of_Madrid" title="Carlos, Duke of Madrid">Don Carlos</a> and the Duke's abandonment of the struggle.</p>
<p>Initially led by Canovas del Castillo as moderate prime minister, what was thought at one time as a coup aimed at placing the military in the political-administrative positions of power, in reality ushered in a permanent civilian regime that lasted until the 1931 Second Republic. Cánovas was the real architect of the new regime of the Restoration.</p>
<p>In order to eliminate one of the problems of the reign of Isabel II, the single party and its destabilizing consequences, the Liberal Party was allowed to incorporate and participate in national politics, and the 'turnismo' or alternation was to become the new system. Turnismo would be endorsed in the Constitution of 1876 and the Pact of Pardo Palace (1885). It meant that liberal and conservative prime ministers would succeed each other ending thus the troubles.</p>
<p>This led to the end of the Carlist revolts and the victory over the New York-backed Cuban revolutionaries, and led to a huge backing both by insular and peninsular Spaniards of Alfonso as a wise and able king.</p>
<p>Alfonso's short reign established the foundations for the final socioeconomic recuperation of Spain after the 1808–1874 crisis. Both European (the coastal regions, such as the Basque Country, Catalonia, and Asturias) and Overseas – Antilles and Pacific were able to grow steadily. Cuba and Puerto Rico prospered to the point that Spain's first train was between Havana and Camaguey, and the world's first telegraph was in Puerto Rico, as Samuel Morse lived there with his daughter, married to a Puerto Rican businessman. Upon the American invasion of Puerto Rico, ten US dollars were needed to buy one Puerto Rican peso.</p>
<p>On 23 January 1878 at the Basilica of <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_de_Atocha" title="Basilica of Nuestra Señora de Atocha">Atocha</a> in Madrid, Alfonso married his first cousin, Princess <a href="/wiki/Mercedes_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Mercedes of Orléans">Maria de las Mercedes</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Antoine,_Duke_of_Montpensier" title="Antoine, Duke of Montpensier">Antoine, Duke of Montpensier</a>, but she died within six months of the marriage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_marriage_and_rule">Second marriage and rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfonso_XII_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second marriage and rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>On 29 November 1879 at the Basilica of Atocha in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, Alfonso married a much more distant relative, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_Austria" title="Maria Christina of Austria">Maria Christina of Austria</a>, daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Ferdinand,_Archduke_of_Austria" title="Karl Ferdinand, Archduke of Austria">Archduke Karl Ferdinand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archduchess_Elisabeth_Franziska_of_Austria" title="Archduchess Elisabeth Franziska of Austria">Archduchess Elisabeth of Austria</a>. During the honeymoon, a pastry cook named Otero fired at the young sovereign and his wife as they were driving in Madrid.</p>
<p>The children of this marriage were:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mercedes,_Princess_of_Asturias" title="Mercedes, Princess of Asturias">María de las Mercedes</a>, Princess of Asturias, (11 September 1880 – 17 October 1904), married on 14 February 1901 to <a href="/wiki/Prince_Carlos_of_Bourbon-Two_Sicilies" title="Prince Carlos of Bourbon-Two Sicilies">Prince Carlos of Bourbon-Two Sicilies</a>, and titular heiress from the death of her father until the posthumous birth of her brother</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Infanta_Maria_Teresa_of_Spain" title="Infanta Maria Teresa of Spain">María Teresa</a>, (12 November 1882 – 23 September 1912), married to <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ferdinand_of_Bavaria" title="Prince Ferdinand of Bavaria">Prince Ferdinand of Bavaria</a> on 12 January 1906</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XIII of Spain">Alfonso XIII</a> (17 May 1886 – 28 February 1941). Born posthumously. He married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Victoria_Eugenie_of_Battenberg" title="Princess Victoria Eugenie of Battenberg">Princess Victoria Eugenie of Battenberg</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Alfonso had two sons by Elena Armanda Nicolasa Sanz y Martínez de Arizala (<a href="/wiki/Castell%C3%B3n_de_la_Plana" title="Castellón de la Plana">Castellón de la Plana</a>, 15 December 1849 – Paris, 24 December 1898):</p>
<ul>
<li>Alfonso Sanz y Martínez de Arizala (28 January 1880, Madrid – 1970), married in 1922 to María de Guadalupe de Limantour y Mariscal (d. 1977, Marbella), daughter of Julio de Limantour y Marquet (17 June 1863, <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> – 11 October 1909, Mexico City) and wife Elena Mariscal y ..., paternal granddaughter of French Joseph Yves de Limantour y Rence de la Pagame (1812, <a href="/wiki/Ploemeur" title="Ploemeur">Ploemeur</a> – 1885, Mexico City) and wife Adèle Marquet y Cabannes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernando_Sanz_y_Mart%C3%ADnez_de_Arizala" title="Fernando Sanz y Martínez de Arizala">Fernando Sanz y Martínez de Arizala</a> (28 February 1881, Madrid – 1922, Nice, France), unmarried and without issue</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1881 Alfonso refused to sanction a law by which the ministers were to remain in office for a fixed term of 18 months. Upon the consequent resignation of Canovas del Castillo, he summoned <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A1xedes_Mateo_Sagasta" title="Práxedes Mateo Sagasta">Práxedes Mateo Sagasta</a>, the Liberal leader, to form a new cabinet.</p>

<p>In November 1885, Alfonso died, just short of his 28th birthday, at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_El_Pardo" title="Royal Palace of El Pardo">Royal Palace of El Pardo</a>. He had been suffering from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, but the immediate cause of his death was a recurrence of <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1902, his widow Maria Cristina initiated a national contest to build a monument in memory of Alfonso. The winning design, by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Grases_Riera" title="José Grases Riera">José Grases Riera</a>, was erected in an artificial lake in Madrid's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parque_del_Buen_Retiro" title="Parque del Buen Retiro">Parque del Buen Retiro</a> in 1922.</p>
<p>Coming to the throne at such an early age Alfonso had served no apprenticeship in the art of ruling. Benevolent and sympathetic in disposition, he won the affection of his people by fearlessly visiting districts ravaged by <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> or devastated by earthquake in 1885. His capacity for dealing with men was considerable, and he never allowed himself to become the instrument of any particular party. During his short reign, peace was established both at home and abroad, finances were well regulated, and the various administrative services were placed on a basis that afterwards enabled Spain to pass through the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">war with the United States</a> without the threat of a revolution.</p>

<td class="navbox-abovebelow" colspan="2">
<div>The generations indicate descent from <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Carlos I</a>, under whom the crowns of Castile and Aragon were united, forming the Kingdom of Spain. Previously, the title Infante had been largely used in the different realms.</div>
