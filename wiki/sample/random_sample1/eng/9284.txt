<p><b>Alfredo Stroessner Matiauda</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[alˈfreðo strosˈner]</a></span>; November 3, 1912 – August 16, 2006) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraguayan_military" title="Paraguayan military">Paraguayan military</a> officer who served as <a href="/wiki/President_of_Paraguay" title="President of Paraguay">President</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> from 1954 to 1989. He ascended to the position after leading an army coup in 1954. His 35-year-long rule is the longest in modern South American history. Stroessner's rule is ranked <a href="/wiki/List_of_longest-ruling_non-royal_national_leaders_since_1900" title="List of longest-ruling non-royal national leaders since 1900">20th-longest</a> among other non-royal national leaders since 1870, and made him one of the world's longest-serving non-royal heads of state.</p>
<p>In 1954, he ousted <a href="/wiki/Federico_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Federico Chávez">Federico Chávez</a>, becoming president after winning an election in which he was the sole candidate. An arch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-communist" title="Anti-communist">anti-communist</a>, Stroessner had the backing of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for most of his time in power. The constitution was modified in 1967 and 1977 after his six consecutive elections to the presidency. In 1988, he won an unprecedented eighth term on a majority, according to official figures, of between 90 and 98 percent of the registered vote.</p>
<p>Stroessner provided refuge for Argentina's <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">Juan Perón</a> and Nicaragua's <a href="/wiki/Anastasio_Somoza_Debayle" title="Anastasio Somoza Debayle">Anastasio Somoza Debayle</a> (later assassinated in Paraguay). Stroessner was ousted from power in 1989 in a military coup led by General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Rodr%C3%ADguez_(President)" title="Andrés Rodríguez (President)">Andrés Rodríguez</a> and forced into exile in Brazil, where he spent the last 17 years of his life. Following a bout of pneumonia, he tried to return to his homeland to die, but was rejected by the government. He died in <a href="/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia" title="Brasília">Brasília</a> on August 16, 2006, of complications from a hernia operation.</p>

<p>Stroessner's parents were Hugo Strößner, who emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Hof,_Bavaria" title="Hof, Bavaria">Hof, Bavaria</a>, Germany, and worked as an accountant for a brewery, and Heriberta Matiauda, who grew up in a wealthy Paraguayan family of <a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people">Criollo</a> Spanish descent. Stroessner was born in <a href="/wiki/Encarnaci%C3%B3n,_Paraguay" title="Encarnación, Paraguay">Encarnación, Paraguay</a>. He joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Paraguay" title="Military of Paraguay">Paraguayan army</a> in 1929, entering the national military school at the age of 16 and received his commission as a lieutenant in 1931. During the <a href="/wiki/Chaco_War" title="Chaco War">Chaco War</a> against <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> (1932–1935) he volunteered as an artillery cadet and fought in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Boquer%C3%B3n" title="Battle of Boquerón">Battle of Boquerón</a>. After the war he rose steadily in rank; by 1940, he had risen to the rank of major and joined the general staff in 1946.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_Civil_War_(1947)" title="Paraguayan Civil War (1947)">Paraguayan Civil War</a> broke out in 1947, he commanded the artillery division at Paraguarí that ensured that President <a href="/wiki/Higinio_Mor%C3%AD%C3%B1igo" title="Higinio Moríñigo">Higinio Moríñigo</a> won the war by staying loyal and destroying a working-class rebel area of <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">Asunción</a>. President Morínigo found Stroessner's military skills very useful and promoted him rapidly. As one of the few officers who had remained loyal to Morínigo, Stroessner became a formidable political and social player once he entered the higher echelons of the Paraguayan armed forces. He eventually became a <a href="/wiki/Brigadier" title="Brigadier">brigadier</a> and the youngest <a href="/wiki/General_officer" title="General officer">general officer</a> in South America in 1948. His accurate political sense failed him only once, when he found himself in 1948 on the wrong side of a failed coup attempt and had to be driven to the Brazilian embassy in the trunk of a car, earning him the nickname of "Colonel Trunk". Stroessner backed <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Molas_L%C3%B3pez" title="Felipe Molas López">Felipe Molas López</a> in a successful coup against <a href="/wiki/Juan_Natalicio_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Juan Natalicio González">Juan Natalicio González</a>. He then backed <a href="/wiki/Federico_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Federico Chávez">Federico Chávez</a> against Lopez and by 1951 he was army chief of staff.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidency">Presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfredo_Stroessner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Stroessner objected to President <a href="/wiki/Federico_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Federico Chávez">Federico Chávez</a>'s plans to arm the national police and threw him out of office in a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a> on May 4, 1954. After a brief interim presidency by <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Romero_Pereira" title="Tomás Romero Pereira">Tomás Romero</a>, Stroessner was the only candidate in a special <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_presidential_election,_1954" title="Paraguayan presidential election, 1954">election</a> on July 11 to complete Chávez's term. He was reelected seven times—in 1958, 1963, 1968, 1973, 1978, 1983 and 1988. He appeared alone on the ballot in 1958. In his other elections, he won by implausibly high margins; the opposition was lucky to get over 20 percent of the vote. He served for 35 years, with only <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> having a longer tenure among 20th-century Latin American leaders.</p>
<p>Soon after taking office, Stroessner declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of siege</a>, which allowed him to suspend civil liberties and rule by decree. It was renewed every 90 days until 1987. Although the state of siege was technically restricted to the capital after 1970, the courts ruled that anyone charged with security offenses could be brought to the capital and indicted under the state-of-siege provisions. Thus, for all intents and purposes, Stroessner ruled under what amounted to <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> for virtually his entire tenure. A devoted <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-communist</a>, he justified this action as a necessary tool to protect the country.</p>
<p>Paraguay enjoyed close military and economic ties with the United States and supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Power_Pack" title="Operation Power Pack">US invasion of Dominican Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Stroessner regime even offered to send troops to <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam</a> alongside the Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Between 1962 and 1975 the United States provided $146 million to Paraguay's military government and Paraguayan officers were trained at the US Army <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_the_Americas" title="School of the Americas">School of the Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper.2C_2000.3B_pg._167_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper.2C_2000.3B_pg._167-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the military and security forces under Stroessner received less material support from the United States than other South American countries, strong inter-military connections existed through military advisors and military training. Between 1962 and 1966, nearly 400 Paraguayan military personnel were trained by the United States in the Panama Canal Zone and on US soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Strong Paraguayan-U.S. relations continued until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carter_Administration" title="Carter Administration">Carter Administration</a> emphasized a foreign policy that recognized human rights abuses. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_Administration" title="Reagan Administration">Reagan Administration</a> boycotted the country as well largely because of the regime's involvement in narcotics trafficking and money-laundering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As leader of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Party_(Paraguay)" title="Colorado Party (Paraguay)">Colorado Party</a>, Stroessner exercised nearly complete control over the nation's political scene. Although opposition parties were nominally permitted after 1962 (the Colorado Party had been the only legal party in the country since 1947), Paraguay remained for all intents and purposes a one-party state. Elections were so heavily rigged in favor of the Colorados that the opposition had no realistic chance of winning, and opposition figures were subjected to varying degrees of harassment. Furthermore, Stroessner's Paraguay became a haven for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> war criminals, including <a href="/wiki/Josef_Mengele" title="Josef Mengele">Josef Mengele</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montefiore_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montefiore-10">[10]</a></sup> and non communist peaceful opposition was crushed.</p>
<p>While Stroessner's rule saw more stability than any living Paraguayan had known, it came at a high cost. <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">Corruption</a> was rampant (Stroessner himself did not dispute charges of corruption at some levels in his government) and Paraguay's <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> record was considered one of the poorest in South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> During Stroessner's regime, an estimated 3,000 to 4,000 people were murdered, 400 to 500 more were "<a href="/wiki/Forced_disappearance" title="Forced disappearance">disappeared</a>," and thousands more imprisoned and tortured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Press was also limited. Any outcry of government mistreatment or attacks toward the Colorado Party, would result in destruction of the media outlets. In which many of the people in charge of the outlets were sent to prison or tortured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Because of this, many political opponents were few and far between. The only ones he did allow, were primarily for the world to see it wasn't a complete dictatorship. Near the end of this presidency, he had declared that he would remove the state of siege but quickly removed his declaration after students began protesting trolley fares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Condor">Operation Condor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfredo_Stroessner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operation Condor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Paraguay was a leading participant in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Condor" title="Operation Condor">Operation Condor</a>, a campaign of state terror and security operations officially implemented in 1975 which were jointly conducted by the military governments of six South American countries (Chile, Argentina, Bolivia, Paraguay, Uruguay and Brazil) with the support of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Human rights violations characteristic of those in other South American countries such as <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">kidnappings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forced_disappearance" title="Forced disappearance">forced disappearances</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extrajudicial_killing" title="Extrajudicial killing">extrajudicial killings</a>, were routine and systematic during the Stroessner regime. Following executions, many of the bodies of those killed by the regime were dumped in the Chaco or the Río Paraguay. The discovery of the "<a href="/wiki/Archives_of_Terror" title="Archives of Terror">Archives of Terror</a>" in 1992 in the <a href="/wiki/Lambar%C3%A9" title="Lambaré">Lambaré</a> suburb of <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">Asunción</a>, confirmed allegations of widespread human rights violations.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pastor_Coronel" title="Pastor Coronel">Pastor Coronel</a> was the chief of the Department of Investigations, or secret police. He would interview people in a <i>pileta</i>, a bath of human excrement or ram electric cattle prods up their rectums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pileta_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pileta-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup> The Secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_Communist_Party" title="Paraguayan Communist Party">Paraguayan Communist Party</a>, Miguel Soler, was dismembered alive with a chainsaw while Stroessner listened on the phone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pileta_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-pileta-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The screams of tortured dissidents would often be recorded and played over the phone to family members, and sometimes the bloody garments of those killed were sent to their homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montefiore_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Montefiore-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under Stroessner, egregious human rights violations were committed against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ach%C3%A9" title="Aché">Aché</a> Indian population of Paraguay's eastern districts. The Aché Indians resided on land that was coveted and had resisted relocation attempts by the Paraguayan army. The government retaliated with massacres and forced many Aché into slavery. In 1974 the UN accused Paraguay of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper.2C_2000.3B_pg._167_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper.2C_2000.3B_pg._167-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stroessner was careful not to show off or draw attention from jealous generals or foreign journalists. He avoided rallies and took simple holidays in Patagonia. He became more tolerant of opposition as the years passed, but there was no change in the regime's basic character.</p>
<p>During Stroessner's rule, no <a href="/wiki/Socialist_state" title="Socialist state">socialist nations</a> had diplomatic relations with Paraguay, with the sole exception of <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">non-aligned</a> <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stroessner made many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_visits" title="State visits">state visits</a>, including to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, as well as to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, a country which Paraguay developed close bilateral ties with in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiardaKline2013_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-WiardaKline2013-22">[22]</a></sup> He also made several visits to <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>, although over the years his relations with that country deteriorated. Since he had always been known as pro-German, this worsening of relations, combined with his feeling that the U.S. had abandoned him, were regarded as personal blows to Stroessner.</p>
<p>It has been asserted that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> is the only reason Stroessner did not have absolute control over the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> After the destruction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n_University" title="Asunción University">Asunción University</a> in 1972 by police, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a> of Paraguay <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ismael_Rol%C3%B3n_Silvero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ismael Rolón Silvero (page does not exist)">Ismael Rolón Silvero</a> excommunicated the minister of the interior and the chief of police, and proscribed the celebration of <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Holy Mass</a> in a sign of protest against the Stroessner regime. When <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> visited Paraguay in 1988, his visit bolstered what was already a robust anti-Stroessner movement within the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stroessner gave a written television interview to <a href="/wiki/Alan_Whicker" title="Alan Whicker">Alan Whicker</a> as part of a documentary <i>The Last Dictator</i> (UK: April 7, 1970) for the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Whicker%27s_World" title="Whicker's World">Whicker's World</a></i>. The programme was released in a <a href="/wiki/DVD_region_code" title="DVD region code">Region 2</a> <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> boxset by the UK's <a href="/wiki/Network_Distributing" title="Network Distributing">Network</a> imprint.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfredo_Stroessner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stroessner enacted several positive economic policies, including the building of the largest <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power plant</a> in the world in <a href="/wiki/Itaipu_Dam" title="Itaipu Dam">Itaipu Dam</a>, developing <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Paraguay" title="Economy of Paraguay">Paraguay's economy</a>: although Paraguay received only 15% of the contracts, it was a major factor allowing the country to have the highest rate of growth in Latin America for most of the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stroessner also dedicated large proportions of the Paraguayan national budget to the military and police apparatus, both fundamental to the maintenance of the regime. According to a 1963 article from <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine, Stroessner spent 33% of the 1962 annual budget on army and police, 15% for <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Paraguay" title="Education in Paraguay">education</a>, and 2% for <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> There was no income tax and public spending was the smallest percentage of GDP in Latin America.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the construction of the Itaipu Dam, as well as the subsequently built <a href="/wiki/Yacyret%C3%A1_Dam" title="Yacyretá Dam">Yacyretá Dam</a> on the Paraguay–Argentina Border, displaced thousands of Paraguayans, pushing them from their homes, often without any restitution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Itaipu Dam displaced at least 80,000 Paraguayans, and the Yacyretá was estimated to have displaced at least that many by December 2008.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 160 workers died building the Itaipu Dam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stroessner was also known for many projects that improved the country's infrastructure. Amongst these were the improvement of highways and the issuing of 15–20 hectare land grants to military personnel upon completion of their service, provided that the land would be used for farming purposes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Over 10,000 soldiers took up this offer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Most impressive was that by the end of <a href="/wiki/El_Stronato" title="El Stronato">the <i>Stronato</i></a>, the second biggest city was Puerto Flor de Lis (renamed "Puerto Presidente Stroessner," then "<a href="/wiki/Ciudad_del_Este" title="Ciudad del Este">Ciudad del Este</a>"), founded just 32 years before.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downfall">Downfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfredo_Stroessner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Downfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1987, Stroessner lifted the state of siege as part of the run-up to elections the following spring. However, several draconian security laws remained in effect, meaning that the substance (if not the form) of the state of siege was still in place. As had been the case for over three decades, opposition leaders continued to be arbitrarily arrested and opposition meetings and demonstrations were broken up (often brutally). Stroessner was nominated by the Colorados once again, and was the only candidate who was allowed to campaign completely unmolested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cs-28">[28]</a></sup> Under these circumstances, the February 1988 election was no different from past elections, with Stroessner officially registering 89 percent of the vote—a margin that his rivals contended could only have been obtained through massive fraud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 3, 1989, only six months after being sworn in for his eighth full term, Stroessner was ousted in a bloody <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a> led by General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Rodr%C3%ADguez_(President)" title="Andrés Rodríguez (President)">Andrés Rodríguez</a>, his closest confidant for over three decades. One reason for the coup was that the generals feared one of Stroessner's offspring would succeed him. Of the two, Alfredo (aka 'Freddie') was a <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> addict and Gustavo, a pilot, was loathed for being homosexual. A more outlandish rumour was that <a href="/wiki/Lino_Oviedo" title="Lino Oviedo">Lino Oviedo</a> threatened Rodríguez with a grenade if he did not launch the coup. The two generals, Rodríguez and Oviedo, fought a brief artillery duel over Asunción.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the coup, Stroessner fled to Brazil, where he lived in exile for the next seventeen and a half years.</p>
<p>The eastern city Puerto Flor de Lis, which had been renamed Puerto Presidente Stroessner in his honor, in 1989 was again renamed <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_del_Este" title="Ciudad del Este">Ciudad del Este</a>. <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">Asunción</a>'s airport had been named after him during his regime, but was later renamed <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Pettirossi_International_Airport" title="Silvio Pettirossi International Airport">Silvio Pettirossi International Airport</a>.</p>
<p>Stroessner died on August 16, 2006, in <a href="/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia" title="Brasília">Brasília</a>, at the age of 93. The immediate cause of death was a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>. He had been suffering from <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> after undergoing a <a href="/wiki/Hernia" title="Hernia">hernia</a> operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Paraguayan government preemptively dismissed any suggestions for honoring the late president within Paraguay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He tried to return to Paraguay before his death, to die in his homeland, but he was rebuked and threatened with arrest by the government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfredo_Stroessner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stroessner was married to Eligia Mora. They had three children: Gustavo, Alfredo and Graciela. Alfredo Domínguez Stroessner, son of Graciela, is a senator.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon.
