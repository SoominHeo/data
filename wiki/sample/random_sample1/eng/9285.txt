<p><b>Alfred Jarry</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[al.fʁɛd ʒa.ʁi]</a></span>; 8 September 1873 – 1 November 1907) was a French <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">symbolist</a> writer who is best known for his play <i><a href="/wiki/Ubu_Roi" title="Ubu Roi">Ubu Roi</a></i> (1896).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He also coined the term and philosophical concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%27pataphysics" title="'pataphysics">'pataphysics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jarry was born in <a href="/wiki/Laval,_Mayenne" title="Laval, Mayenne">Laval, Mayenne</a>, France, and his mother was from <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was associated with the <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolist</a> movement. His play <i><a href="/wiki/Ubu_Roi" title="Ubu Roi">Ubu Roi</a></i> (1896) is often cited as a forerunner of <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dada</a>, and to the <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">Futurist</a> movements of the 1920s and 1930. Jarry wrote in a variety of hybrid genres and styles, prefiguring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-modern_literature" title="Post-modern literature">postmodern</a>. He wrote plays, novels, poetry, essays and speculative journalism. His texts present us with pioneering work in the fields of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absurdist_literature" title="Absurdist literature">absurdist literature</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-modern_philosophy" title="Post-modern philosophy">postmodern philosophy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography_and_works">Biography and works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Jarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography and works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Jarry,_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="926" data-file-width="722" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Alfred_Jarry%2C_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg/220px-Alfred_Jarry%2C_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Alfred_Jarry%2C_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg/330px-Alfred_Jarry%2C_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Alfred_Jarry%2C_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg/440px-Alfred_Jarry%2C_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Jarry,_Deux_aspects_de_la_marionnette_originale_d%27Ubu_Roi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Alfred Jarry, <i>Deux aspects de la marionnette original d'Ubu Roi</i>, premiered at the Théâtre de l'Œuvre on 10 December 1896</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the lycée in <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a> when he was 15, he led a group of boys who enjoyed poking fun at their well-meaning, but obese and incompetent physics teacher, a man named Hébert. Jarry and his classmate, Henri Morin, wrote a play they called <i>Les Polonais</i> and performed it with marionettes in the home of one of their friends. The main character, <i>Père Heb</i>, was a blunderer with a huge belly, three teeth (one of stone, one of iron and one of wood), a single, retractable ear and a misshapen body. In Jarry's later work <i>Ubu Roi</i>, Père Heb would develop into Ubu, one of the most monstrous and astonishing characters in French literature.</p>
<p>At 17 Jarry passed his <a href="/wiki/Baccalaur%C3%A9at" title="Baccalauréat">baccalauréat</a> and moved to Paris to prepare for admission to the École Normale Supérieure. Though he was not admitted, he soon gained attention for his original poems and prose-poems. A collection of his work, <i>Les minutes de sable mémorial</i>, was published in 1893.</p>
<p>That same year, both his parents died, leaving him a small inheritance which he quickly spent.</p>
<p>Jarry had meantime discovered the pleasures of alcohol, which he called "my sacred herb" or, when referring to <a href="/wiki/Absinthe" title="Absinthe">absinthe</a>, the "green goddess." A story is told that he once painted his face green and rode through town on his bicycle in its honour (and possibly under its influence).</p>
<p>When he was drafted into the army in 1894, his gift for turning notions upside down defeated attempts to instill military discipline. The sight of the small man in a uniform much too large for his less than 5-foot frame—the army did not issue uniforms small enough—was so disruptively funny that he was excused from parades and marching drills. Eventually the army discharged him for medical reasons. His military experience eventually inspired his novel <i>Days and Nights</i>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2880" data-file-width="1908" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png/220px-V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png/330px-V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png/440px-V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:V%C3%A9ritable_portrait_de_Monsieur_Ubu.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Père Ubu (later: Ubu Roi), from a woodcut by Alfred Jarry</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Jarry returned to Paris and applied himself to writing, drinking and the company of friends who appreciated his witty, sweet-tempered and unpredictable conversation. This period is marked by his intense involvement with <a href="/wiki/Remy_de_Gourmont" title="Remy de Gourmont">Remy de Gourmont</a> in the publication of <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Ymagier" title="L'Ymagier">L'Ymagier</a> ,</i> a luxuriously produced "art" magazine devoted to the symbolic analysis of medieval and popular prints. <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolism</a> as an art movement was in full swing at this time, and <i>L'Ymagier</i> provided a nexus for many of its key contributors. Jarry's play <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_Antichrist" title="Caesar Antichrist">Caesar Antichrist</a></i> (1895) drew on this movement for material. This is a work that bridges the gap between serious symbolic meaning and the type of critical absurdity with which Jarry would soon become associated. Using the biblical <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> as a point of departure, <i>Caesar Antichrist</i> presents a parallel world of extreme formal symbolism in which Christ is resurrected not as an agent of <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spirituality</a> but as an agent of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> that seeks to dominate spirituality. It is a unique narrative that effectively links the domination of the soul to contemporaneous advances in the field of <a href="/wiki/Egyptology" title="Egyptology">Egyptology</a> such as the 1894 excavation of the <a href="/wiki/Narmer_Palette" title="Narmer Palette">Narmer Palette</a>, an ancient artifact used for situating the <a href="/wiki/Rebus" title="Rebus">rebus</a> within <a href="/wiki/Hermeneutics" title="Hermeneutics">hermeneutics</a>. The character Ubu Roi first appears in this play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The spring of 1896 saw the publication, in Paul Fort's review <i>Le Livre d'art</i>, of Jarry's 5-act play <i><a href="/wiki/Ubu_Roi" title="Ubu Roi">Ubu Roi</a>,</i> the rewritten and expanded <i>Les Polonais</i> of his school days. <i>Ubu Roi</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s savage humor and monstrous absurdity, unlike anything thus far performed in French theater, seemed unlikely to ever actually be performed on stage. However, impetuous theater director Aurélien-Marie Lugné-Poe took the risk, producing the play at his Théâtre de l'Œuvre.</p>
<p>On opening night (10 December 1896), with traditionalists and the <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> in the audience, King Ubu (played by Firmin Gémier) stepped forward and intoned the opening word, "Merdre!" (often translated as "Pshit" or "Shittr!" in English). A quarter of an hour of pandemonium ensued: outraged cries, booing, and whistling by the offended parties, countered by cheers and applause by the more degenerate contingent. Such interruptions continued through the evening. At the time, only the dress rehearsal and opening night performance were held, and the play was not revived until after Jarry's death.</p>
<p>The play brought fame to the 23-year-old Jarry, and he immersed himself in the fiction he had created. Gémier had modeled his portrayal of Ubu on Jarry's own staccato, nasal vocal delivery, which emphasized each syllable (even the silent ones). From then on, Jarry would always speak in this style. He adopted Ubu's ridiculous and pedantic figures of speech; for example, he referred to himself using the <a href="/wiki/Royal_we" title="Royal we">royal <i>we</i></a>, and called the wind "that which blows" and the bicycle he rode everywhere "that which rolls."</p>
<p>Jarry moved into a flat which the landlord had created through the unusual expedient of subdividing a larger flat by means of a horizontal rather than a vertical partition. The diminutive Jarry could just manage to stand up in the place, but guests had to bend or crouch. Jarry also took to carrying a loaded revolver. In response to a neighbor's complaint that his target shooting endangered her children, he replied, "If that should ever happen, ma-da-me, we should ourselves be happy to get new ones with you."</p>

<p>With <a href="/wiki/Franc-Nohain" title="Franc-Nohain">Franc-Nohain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude_Terrasse" title="Claude Terrasse">Claude Terrasse</a> he co-founded the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Th%C3%A9atre_des_Pantins&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Théatre des Pantins (page does not exist)">Théatre des Pantins</a>, which in 1898 was the site of marionette performances of <i>Ubu Roi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Living in worsening poverty, neglecting his health and drinking excessively, Jarry went on to write the novel <i>Le Surmâle</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Supermale_(novel)" title="Supermale (novel)">The Supermale</a></i>), which is partly a satire on the <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolist</a> ideal of self-transcendence.</p>
<p>Unpublished until after his death, his fiction <i><a href="/wiki/Exploits_and_Opinions_of_Dr._Faustroll,_Pataphysician" title="Exploits and Opinions of Dr. Faustroll, Pataphysician">Exploits and Opinions of Dr. Faustroll, Pataphysician</a></i> (<i>Gestes et opinions du docteur Faustroll, pataphysicien</i>) describes the exploits and teachings of a sort of antiphilosopher who, born at age 63, travels through a hallucinatory Paris in a sieve and subscribes to the tenets of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%27pataphysics" title="'pataphysics">'pataphysics</a></i>. 'Pataphysics deals with "the laws which govern exceptions and will explain the universe supplementary to this one." In 'pataphysics, every event in the universe is accepted as an extraordinary event.</p>
<p>Jarry once wrote, expressing some of the bizarre logic of 'pataphysics, "If you let a coin fall and it falls, the next time it is just by an infinite coincidence that it will fall again the same way; hundreds of other coins on other hands will follow this pattern in an infinitely unimaginable fashion."</p>
<p>In his final years, he was a legendary and heroic figure to some of the young writers and artists in Paris. <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Apollinaire" title="Guillaume Apollinaire">Guillaume Apollinaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Salmon" title="André Salmon">André Salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Jacob" title="Max Jacob">Max Jacob</a> sought him out in his truncated apartment. <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a> was fascinated with Jarry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After Jarry's death Picasso acquired his revolver and wore it on his nocturnal expeditions in Paris. He later bought many of his manuscripts as well as executing a fine drawing of him.</p>
<p>Jarry died in Paris on 1 November 1907 of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, aggravated by drug and alcohol use. When he could not afford alcohol, he drank ether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is recorded that his last request was for a toothpick. He was interred in the <a href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_parisien_de_Bagneux" title="Cimetière parisien de Bagneux">Cimetière de Bagneux</a>, near Paris.</p>
<p>The complete works of Alfred Jarry are published in three volumes by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallimard" title="Gallimard">Gallimard</a> in the collection <i><a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_de_la_Pl%C3%A9iade" title="Bibliothèque de la Pléiade">Bibliothèque de la Pléiade</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_Jarry_works">Selected Jarry works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Jarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Selected Jarry works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plays">Plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Jarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>César-Antéchrist</i> (1895) - (<i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_Antichrist" title="Caesar Antichrist">Caesar Antichrist</a></i>) – which introduces Père Ubu and his symbolic meaning.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Ubu_Roi" title="Ubu Roi">Ubu Roi</a></i> (1896, revised from 1888) - (<i><a href="/wiki/Ubu_Roi" title="Ubu Roi">Ubu Rex</a></i>) – which portrays the ambition of Père Ubu.</li>
<li><i>Ubu Cocu, ou l'Archeopteryx (1897) - (Ubu Cuckolded</i>) - which portrays the inconstancy of Ubu's closest.</li>
<li><i>Ubu Enchaíné</i> (1899) - (<i>Ubu in Chains</i>) – which portrays Père Ubu in service.</li>
<li><i>Ubu Sur La Butte</i> (1906)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels">Novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Jarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Les Jours et Les Nuits, roman d'un déserteur</i> (1897) - (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Days_and_Nights,_novel_of_a_deserter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Days and Nights, novel of a deserter (page does not exist)">Days and Nights, novel of a deserter</a></i>).</li>
<li><i>L'Amour en Visites</i> (1897) - (<i>Love in Visits</i>).</li>
<li><i>L'Amour Absolu</i> (1899) - (<i>Absolute Love</i>).</li>
<li><i>Messaline</i> (1900) - (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Messalina_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Messalina (novel) (page does not exist)">Messalina</a></i>) – set in ancient Rome.</li>
<li><i>Le Surmâle</i> (1901) - (<i><a href="/wiki/Supermale_(novel)" title="Supermale (novel)">The Supermale</a></i>) - features a superhuman bicycle race that is propelled by perpetual motion food (alcohol).</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Exploits_and_Opinions_of_Dr._Faustroll,_Pataphysician" title="Exploits and Opinions of Dr. Faustroll, Pataphysician">Gestes et Opinions du Docteur Faustroll, Pataphysicien</a></i> (<i>Exploits and Opinions of Dr. Faustroll, Pataphysician</i>) – published posthumously in 1911.</li>
<li><i>La Dragonne</i> – assembled and published posthumously in 1943.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_notable_works">Other notable works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Jarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other notable works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Short story <i>La Passion considérée comme course de côte</i> (<i>The Passion Considered as an Uphill Bicycle Race</i>) has been widely circulated and imitated, notably by <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Anton_Wilson" title="Robert Anton Wilson">Robert Anton Wilson</a>.</li>
<li><i>Les Minutes de Sable Memorial</i> (1894) - (<i>Minutes of Memorial Sand</i>) – a collection of short early works including the symbolist play <i>Haldernablou</i>.</li>
<li><i>La Chandelle Verte: Lumières sur les Choses de ce Temps</i> - (<i>The Green Candle</i>) – a collection of absurdist essays originally published in reviews and collected in 1969.</li>
</ul>
