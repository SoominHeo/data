<p>A <b>dike</b> or <b>dyke</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological usage</a>, is a sheet of rock that formed in a <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">fracture</a> in a pre-existing rock body. Dikes can be either <a href="/wiki/Intrusive_rock" title="Intrusive rock">magmatic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary</a> in origin. Magmatic dikes form when <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> intrudes into a crack then crystallizes as a <a href="/wiki/Sheet_intrusion" title="Sheet intrusion">sheet intrusion</a>, either cutting across layers of rock or through an unlayered mass of rock. <a href="/wiki/Clastic_dike" title="Clastic dike">Clastic dikes</a> are formed when <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> fills a pre-existing crack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EG_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EG-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magmatic_dikes">Magmatic dikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dike_(geology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Magmatic dikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An intrusive dike is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">igneous</a> body with a very high <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a>, which means that its thickness is usually much smaller than the other two dimensions. Thickness can vary from sub-centimeter scale to many meters, and the lateral dimensions can extend over many kilometres. A dike is an intrusion into an opening cross-cutting <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">fissure</a>, shouldering aside other pre-existing layers or bodies of rock; this implies that a dike is always younger than the rocks that contain it. Dikes are usually high-angle to near-vertical in orientation, but subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> deformation may rotate the sequence of strata through which the dike propagates so that the dike becomes horizontal. Near-horizontal, or conformable intrusions, along bedding planes between <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> are called intrusive <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a>.</p>
<p>Sometimes dikes appear in swarms, consisting of several to hundreds of dikes emplaced more or less contemporaneously during a single intrusive event. The world's largest <a href="/wiki/Dike_swarm" title="Dike swarm">dike swarm</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_dike_swarm" title="Mackenzie dike swarm">Mackenzie dike swarm</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dikes often form as either radial or concentric swarms around plutonic intrusives, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_neck" title="Volcanic neck">volcanic necks</a> or feeder vents in <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_cone" title="Volcanic cone">volcanic cones</a>. The latter are known as <a href="/wiki/Ring_dike" title="Ring dike">ring dikes</a>.</p>
<p>Dikes can vary in texture and their composition can range from <a href="/wiki/Diabase" title="Diabase">diabase</a> or <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granitic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolitic</a>, but on a global perspective the basaltic composition prevails, manifesting ascent of vast volumes of mantle-derived magmas through fractured <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> throughout Earth history. <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">Pegmatite</a> dikes comprise extremely coarse crystalline granitic rocks - often associated with late-stage granite intrusions or <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> segregations. <a href="/wiki/Aplite" title="Aplite">Aplite</a> dikes are fine-grained or sugary-textured intrusives of granitic composition.</p>
<p>In contrast to magmatic dikes, a <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sill</a> is a magmatic sheet intrusion that forms within and parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Bed_(geology)" title="Bed (geology)">bedding</a> of layered rock.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentary_dikes">Sedimentary dikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dike_(geology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sedimentary dikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clastic_dike_UT.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="500" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Clastic_dike_UT.jpg/220px-Clastic_dike_UT.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Clastic_dike_UT.jpg/330px-Clastic_dike_UT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Clastic_dike_UT.jpg/440px-Clastic_dike_UT.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clastic_dike_UT.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Clastic_dike" title="Clastic dike">Clastic dike</a> (left of notebook) in the <a href="/wiki/Chinle_Formation" title="Chinle Formation">Chinle Formation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canyonlands_National_Park" title="Canyonlands National Park">Canyonlands National Park</a>, Utah</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sedimentary dikes or <a href="/wiki/Clastic_dike" title="Clastic dike">clastic dikes</a> are vertical bodies of sedimentary rock that cut off other rock layers. They can form in two ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>When a shallow unconsolidated sediment is composed of alternating coarse grained and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permeability_(fluid)" title="Permeability (fluid)">impermeable</a> <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> layers the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_pressure" title="Fluid pressure">fluid pressure</a> inside the coarser layers may reach a critical value due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithostatic_pressure" title="Lithostatic pressure">lithostatic</a> overburden. Driven by the fluid pressure the sediment breaks through overlying layers and forms a dike.</li>
<li>When a soil is under <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> conditions the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pore_water" title="Pore water">pore water</a> is totally frozen. When cracks are formed in such rocks, they may fill up with sediments that fall in from above. The result is a vertical body of sediment that cuts through horizontal layers: a dike.</li>
</ul>
