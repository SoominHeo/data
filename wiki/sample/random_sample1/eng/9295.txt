<p><b>Ammonia solution</b>, also known as <b>ammonium hydroxide</b>, <b>ammonia water</b>, <b>ammonical liquor</b>, <b>ammonia liquor</b>, <b>aqua ammonia</b>, <b>aqueous ammonia</b>, or simply <b>ammonia</b>, is a solution of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> in water. It can be denoted by the symbols NH<sub>3</sub>(aq). Although the name ammonium hydroxide suggests an alkali with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">composition</a> [NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>][OH<sup>â</sup>], it is actually impossible to isolate samples of NH<sub>4</sub>OH, as these ions do not comprise a significant fraction of the total amount of ammonia except in extremely dilute solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basicity_of_ammonia_in_water">Basicity of ammonia in water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basicity of ammonia in water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In aqueous solution, ammonia <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonates</a> a small fraction of the water to give <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> according to the following <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>NH<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O â NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> + OH<sup>â</sup>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>In a 1M <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> solution, about 1.42% of the ammonia is converted to ammonium, equivalent to a pH of 11.63. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_ionization_constant" title="Base ionization constant">base ionization constant</a> is</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>K</i><sub>b</sub> = [NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>][OH<sup>â</sup>]/[NH<sub>3</sub>] = 1.8Ã10<sup>â5</sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Saturated_solutions">Saturated solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Saturated solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">

<p>Like other gases, ammonia exhibits decreasing solubility in solvent liquids as the temperature of the solvent increases. Ammonia solutions decrease in density as the concentration of dissolved ammonia increases. At 15.6Â Â°C (60.1Â Â°F), the density of a saturated solution is 0.88Â g/ml and contains 35% ammonia by mass, 308Â g/l w/v, (308Â grams of ammonia per litre of solution) and has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molarity" title="Molarity">molarity</a> of approximately 18Â <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a> L<sup>â1</sup>. At higher temperatures, the molarity of the saturated solution decreases and the density increases.</p>
<p>When solutions that are saturated at cold temperatures are sealed in containers and subsequently warmed, the concentration of the solution decreases and the vapor pressure of ammonia gas increases. Unsealing such containers can lead to a burst of ammonia gas. In extreme cases, the containers could rupture.</p>
<p>From a chemist's perspective, one should be aware that the concentration of a saturated solution is continually dropping as the container is handled in a warmer environment. Thus, old samples of ammonium hydroxide will deviate from 18Â M, as can be verified by <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titration</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Household_cleaner">Household cleaner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Household cleaner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Household ammonia is dilute ammonium hydroxide, which is also an ingredient of numerous other <a href="/wiki/Cleaning_agent" title="Cleaning agent">cleaning agents</a>, including many window cleaning formulas. In addition to use as an ingredient in cleansers with other cleansing ingredients, ammonium hydroxide in water is also sold as a cleaning agent by itself, usually labeled as simply "ammonia". It may be sold plain, lemon-scented (and typically colored yellow), or pine-scented (green). Commonly available ammonia with soap added is known as "Cloudy ammonia".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkyl_amine">Alkyl amine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alkyl amine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In industry, ammonium hydroxide is used as a precursor to some <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> amines, although anhydrous ammonia is usually preferred. <a href="/wiki/Hexamethylenetetramine" title="Hexamethylenetetramine">Hexamethylenetetramine</a> forms readily from aqueous ammonia and <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ethylenediamine" title="Ethylenediamine">Ethylenediamine</a> forms from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,2-dichloroethane" title="1,2-dichloroethane">1,2-dichloroethane</a> and aqueous ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Furniture_darkening">Furniture darkening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Furniture darkening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In furniture-making, ammonium hydroxide was traditionally used to darken or stain wood containing <a href="/wiki/Tannic_acid" title="Tannic acid">tannic acid</a>. After being sealed inside a container with the wood, fumes from the ammonium hydroxide react with the tannic acid and iron salts naturally found in wood, creating a rich, dark stained look to the wood. This was commonly used during the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">arts and crafts movement</a> in furniture- a furniture style which was primarily constructed of oak and stained using these methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_production">Food production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ammonium hydroxide is used as a <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agent</a> or <a href="/wiki/Acidity_regulator" title="Acidity regulator">acidity regulator</a> and is classified by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> as <a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a> (GRAS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Its pH control abilities make it an effective <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial" title="Antimicrobial">antimicrobial</a> agent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tobacco_processing">Tobacco processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tobacco processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Phillip Morris USA, quantities of up to 0.3% ammonium hydroxide are used in tobacco for flavor enhancement and as a processing aid. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquarium_Fishless_cycling">Aquarium Fishless cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aquarium Fishless cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ammonium hydroxide is used by <a href="/wiki/Fishkeeping" title="Fishkeeping">aquarists</a> for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Fishless_cycling" title="Fishless cycling">fishless cycling</a>, but this requires that there are no surfactants or scents, it should be colorless and shouldn't foam when shaken up.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treat_straw_for_cattle">Treat straw for cattle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Treat straw for cattle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ammonium hydroxide is used to treat straw, producing "ammoniated straw" making it more edible for <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_use">Laboratory use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Laboratory use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aqueous ammonia is used in traditional <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis" title="Qualitative inorganic analysis">qualitative inorganic analysis</a> as a complexant and base. Like many amines, it gives a deep blue coloration with copper(II) solutions. Ammonia solution can dissolve silver oxide residues, such as that formed from <a href="/wiki/Tollens%27_reagent" title="Tollens' reagent">Tollens' reagent</a>. It is often found in solutions used to clean gold, silver, and platinum jewelry, but may have negative effects on porous gem stones like opals and pearls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>When ammonium hydroxide is mixed with dilute <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> in the presence of a metal ion, such as Cu<sup>2+</sup>, the peroxide will undergo rapid decomposition.</p>
