This detail scene from the <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">Papyrus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hunefer" title="Hunefer">Hunefer</a> (ca. 1375 B.C.) shows <a href="/wiki/Hunefer" title="Hunefer">Hunefer</a>'s heart being weighed on the scale of <a href="/wiki/Maat" title="Maat">Maat</a> against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feather_of_truth" title="Feather of truth">feather of truth</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackal</a>-headed <a href="/wiki/Anubis" title="Anubis">Anubis</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a>-headed <a href="/wiki/Thoth" title="Thoth">Thoth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scribe" title="Scribe">scribe</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a>, records the result. If his heart is lighter than the feather, <a href="/wiki/Hunefer" title="Hunefer">Hunefer</a> is allowed to pass into the <a href="/wiki/Afterlife" title="Afterlife">afterlife</a>. If not, he is eaten by the waiting <b>Ammit</b>. <a href="/wiki/Vignette_(graphic_design)" title="Vignette (graphic design)">Vignettes</a> such as these were a common <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustration</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_the_dead" title="Book of the dead">books of the dead</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Ammit</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>; "devourer" or "soul-eater"; also called <b>Ammut</b> or <b>Ahemait</b>) was a female <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">ancient Egyptian religion</a> with a body that was part <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodile</a>—the three largest "man-eating" animals known to ancient Egyptians. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funerary" title="Funerary">funerary</a> deity, her titles included "Devourer of the Dead", "Eater of <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">Hearts</a>", and "Great of Death".</p>
<p>Ammit lived near the <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">scales of justice</a> in <a href="/wiki/Duat" title="Duat">Duat</a>, the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Underworld" title="Underworld">underworld</a>. In the Hall of Two Truths, <a href="/wiki/Anubis" title="Anubis">Anubis</a> weighed the heart of a person against the feather of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ma%27at" title="Ma'at">Ma'at</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a> of truth, which was depicted as an ostrich feather (the feather was often pictured in Ma'at's headdress). If the heart was judged to be not pure, Ammit would devour it, and the person undergoing judgement was not allowed to continue their voyage towards <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortality</a>. Once Ammit swallowed the heart, the soul was believed to become restless forever; this was called "to die a second time". Ammit was also sometimes said to stand by a <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_fire" title="Lake of fire">lake of fire</a>. In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditions" title="Traditions">traditions</a>, the unworthy hearts were cast into the fiery lake to be destroyed. Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholars" title="Scholars">scholars</a> believe Ammit and the lake represent the same concept of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/destroy" title="wikt:destroy">destruction</a>.</p>
<p>Ammit was not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worshipped" title="Worshipped">worshipped</a>; instead she embodied all that the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> feared, threatening to bind them to eternal restlessness if they did not follow the principle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ma%27at" title="Ma'at">Ma'at</a>.</p>
<p>Ammit has been linked<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> with the goddess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tawaret" title="Tawaret">Tawaret</a>, who has a similar physical appearance and, as a companion of <a href="/wiki/Bes" title="Bes">Bes</a>, also protected others from evil. Other authors<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have noted that Ammit's lion characteristics, and the <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_fire" title="Lake of fire">lake of fire</a>, may be pointers to a connection with the goddess <a href="/wiki/Sekhmet" title="Sekhmet">Sekhmet</a>. The relation to afterlife punishment and lake of fire location are also shared with the baboon deity <a href="/wiki/Babi_(mythology)" title="Babi (mythology)">Babi</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_popular_culture">Influence on popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Influence on popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Ammit was made male as a character in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladium_RPG" title="Palladium RPG">Palladium RPG</a>. A monster of the same name is also a card in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yu-Gi-Oh" title="Yu-Gi-Oh">Yu-Gi-Oh</a>.</li>
<li>In addition, Ammit also intermittenly appears in <a href="/wiki/The_Kane_Chronicles" title="The Kane Chronicles">The Kane Chronicles</a>, a trilogy based on Egyptian Myths. In the book series, Ammit follows suit as the original myths, being the servant and companion of <a href="/wiki/Anubis" title="Anubis">Anubis</a>.</li>
<li>Ammit is also featured in the <a href="/wiki/Lara_Croft_and_the_Temple_of_Osiris" title="Lara Croft and the Temple of Osiris">Lara Croft and the Temple of Osiris</a> video game.</li>
<li>Ammit Cryearth is a Grimoire in form of a hand mirror that reflects the true form of the holder; it appears in <i>BlazBlue: Remix Heart</i> manga.</li>
</ul>
