<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">E-3 visa</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>E-3 visa</b> is a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> visa for which only citizens of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> are eligible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was created by an Act of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> as a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australia-United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="Australia-United States Free Trade Agreement">Australia-United States Free Trade Agreement</a> (AUSFTA), although it is not formally a part of the AUSFTA. The legislation creating the E-3 visa was signed into law by U.S. President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> on May 11, 2005. It is widely believed to have grown out of the negotiation of a trade deal between the USA and Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-3_visa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The E-3 visa is similar in many respects to the <a href="/wiki/H-1B_visa" title="H-1B visa">H-1B visa</a>. Important differences include the fact that spouses of E-3 visa holders may work in the United States without restrictions (unlike other US non-immigrant visas, even the <a href="/wiki/TN_status" title="TN status">TN visa</a> issued to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> citizens), even if they are of a different nationality;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> that the E-3 visa is renewable indefinitely (in two-year increments); and that the application process is much quicker. Australian citizens applying for an E-3 visa are also no longer subject to the 65,000 annual visa limit for H-1B visas; although there is a separate annual quota of 10,500 E-3 visas, this is believed to be much more generous to Australians than requiring them to compete with all other nations for H-1B visas. (One calculation is that the E-3 guarantees Australians 15% of the combined annual total of US visas for persons in specialty occupations, should they want such visas, even though with a population of approximately 23.4 million Australia accounts for only 0.3% of the world’s non-US population.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Visas issued to spouses and children are not included in the E3 quota and spouses and children do not need to be Australian citizens.</p>
<p>Although INA § 101(a)(15)(E) requires that all E nonimmigrants maintain an intention to depart the United States upon expiration of their authorized E stay, the guidelines issued by the USCIS state that applications for E classification, including extensions or change of status, cannot be denied solely on the basis of an approved permanent <a href="/wiki/Labor_certification" title="Labor certification">labor certification</a> or pending or approved immigrant visa petition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, immigrant intent should not be a bar to eligibility for E-3 classification.</p>
<p>The regulations for applying for an E-3 visa were published in the United States Federal Register on September 2, 2005. Following these procedures will allow an Australian citizen to apply for an E-3 visa at a U.S. embassy or consulate.</p>
<p>Similar to an <a href="/wiki/H-1B_visa" title="H-1B visa">H-1B visa</a>, the prospective employer of the E-3 visa holder will first apply for a <a href="/wiki/Labor_Condition_Application" title="Labor Condition Application">Labor Condition Application</a> (LCA) with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Labor" title="U.S. Department of Labor">U.S. Department of Labor</a>, with a note at the top of the form indicating it is for an E-3 visa for an Australian citizen. After the LCA is approved, the Australian citizen will then apply for the actual visa at a U.S. consulate and then enter the U.S. Australians who are already in the United States on another type of temporary/non-immigrant visa may also apply to change their status to an E-3 visa. Change of visa status is not possible if the applicant has entered the country under the visa waiver program, however, if the applicant has entered on a different visa (F-1 student) then a change of status is allowed.</p>

<p>Since the introduction of the E-3 visas, between 2,000-3,000 E-3 visas have been issued by US consulates to Australian professionals every year; in addition to this, E3R visas are issued to the returning Australian professionals whose original visas had expired. The numbers are as follows: four new visa in Fiscal Year 2005; 1,918 new visas in FY 2006; 2,572 new and 6 returning in FY 2007; 2,961 new and 1,568 returning in FY 2008; and 2,191 new and 1,421 returning in FY 2009. In addition to this, 1,000 to 1,500 E3D visas are issued annually to these professionals' dependents. (Each US fiscal year starts and ends 3 months before the calendar year with the same number).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In U.S. Fiscal Year 2006 (October 2005 through September 2006), which was the first full year during which E-3 regulations were in effect, the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> recorded 2,123 admissions of Australian citizens as E-3 status foreign workers under the treaty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> 9,294 admissions were recorded in U.S. Fiscal Year 2007 (October 2006 through September 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Importantly, the number of admissions in a given fiscal year is different from the number of visas issued: an E-3 visa is a multiple-entry visa valid for 24 months,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and every reentry of the visa holder into the USA (other than after short trips to the "near abroad", i.e. Canada or Mexico) during this time will generate a new <a href="/wiki/Form_I-94" title="Form I-94">admission record</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reciprocity">Reciprocity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-3_visa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reciprocity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Australian <i><a href="/wiki/457_visa" title="457 visa">subclass 457 long stay business visa</a></i> provides similar working rights in Australia for U.S. citizens. However this visa is available to any nationality and hence no special work permit for U.S. citizens has been introduced in Australia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_residence_in_Canada">Permanent residence in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-3_visa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Permanent residence in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Up to 23 August 2010, it was possible to use one year of residence in the United States as a holder of an H1B, H1B1, H1C, or E-3 visa, while employed in an eligible occupation, to obtain provincial nomination from <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> for permanent residence in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This was withdrawn due to Alberta being limited to a maximum of 5,000 nominations of any type, at all, under Canada's provincial nominee program.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-3_visa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The E-3 visa was enacted by section 501 of the Emergency Supplemental Appropriations Act for Defense, the Global War on Terror, and Tsunami Relief on May 11, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (Section 502 of the Act addressed certain visas for nurses.) Although the bill, H.R. 1268, originated in the House of Representatives, the E-3 visa was added in the Senate's amendment to the House bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The visa was created within the E category of visas. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=E-1_visa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="E-1 visa (page does not exist)">E-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/E-2_visa" title="E-2 visa">E-2</a> visas are commonly called the "treaty trader" and "treaty investor" visas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act_of_1965" title="Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965">Immigration and Nationality Act</a>, the E category of visas is reserved for aliens who "enter the United States under and in pursuance of the provisions of a treaty of commerce and navigation between the United States and the foreign state of which he is a national."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Australia is eligible for E visas pursuant to a process enacted in the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_of_1990" title="Immigration Act of 1990">Immigration Act of 1990</a> (Pub. L. No. 101-649).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Section 204 of that Act authorized E visas for any country that "extends reciprocal nonimmigrant treatment to nationals of the United States," and is either the largest foreign state in a region or an "adversely affected foreign state for purposes of section 314 of the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Reform_and_Control_Act_of_1986" title="Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986">Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986</a>." The enactment of the E-3 visa within the E category reflects that the E-3 was closely related to the U.S.-Australia Free Trade Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Although the visa is within the E category, its substantive provisions draw on other provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act_of_1965" title="Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965">Immigration and Nationality Act</a>, including the definitions of a "specialty occupation" and the labor attestation process used for <a href="/wiki/H-1B_visa" title="H-1B visa">H-1B</a> visas.</p>
<p>To enact the E-3 visa, the government of the Commonwealth of Australia worked with Congress and the Bush Administration following the enactment of the U.S.-Australia Free Trade Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> According to press reports and filings with the U.S. Department of Justice under the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Agents_Registration_Act" title="Foreign Agents Registration Act">Foreign Agents Registration Act</a>, the Washington law firm of <a href="/wiki/Covington_%26_Burling" title="Covington &amp; Burling">Covington &amp; Burling LLP</a> assisted the government of Australia in pursuing the visa legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-19">[19]</a></sup> The Covington team included <a href="/wiki/Stuart_E._Eizenstat" title="Stuart E. Eizenstat">Stuart Eizenstat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_B._Gold" title="Martin B. Gold">Martin Gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roderick_Allen_DeArment" title="Roderick Allen DeArment">Roderick DeArment</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Marchick" title="David Marchick">David Marchick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Baldwin_Letchworth" title="Elizabeth Baldwin Letchworth">Elizabeth Letchworth</a>, Les Carnegie, and Brian Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-3_visa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the E-3 visa is reserved exclusively for nationals of Australia, other free trade partners of the United States have reportedly sought similar visas. Public filings at the U.S. Department of Justice under the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Agents_Registration_Act" title="Foreign Agents Registration Act">Foreign Agents Registration Act</a> indicate that South Korea is seeking professional visa legislation following the entry-in-force of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_trade_agreement_between_the_United_States_of_America_and_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Free trade agreement between the United States of America and the Republic of Korea">Korea-U.S. Free Trade Agreement</a> in March 2012. On November 20, 2012, the LegalTimes reported that the Embassy of South Korea had hired <a href="/wiki/Covington_%26_Burling" title="Covington &amp; Burling">Covington &amp; Burling LLP</a>, which assisted Australia in the development of the E-3 visa, to advise on a similar visa for Korea. Covington counsel Brian Smith<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and senior international policy adviser <a href="/wiki/Alan_Larson" title="Alan Larson">Alan Larson</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> reportedly led the matter, assisted by senior counsel <a href="/wiki/Martin_B._Gold" title="Martin B. Gold">Martin Gold</a> and associate Jonathan Wakely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
