<p>The <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> known as the <b>Defence Line of Amsterdam</b> (in <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> named <i><b>Stelling van Amsterdam</b></i>) is a 135 km long ring of <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortifications</a> around <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, consisting of 42 forts located between 10 and 15 kilometers from the centre, and lowlands that can easily be flooded in time of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The flooding was designed to give a depth of about 30 cm, insufficient for boats to traverse. Any buildings within 1 km of the line had to be made of wood, so that they could be burnt and the obstruction removed.</p>
<p>The Stelling van Amsterdam was constructed between 1880 and 1920. The invention of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeroplane" title="Aeroplane">aeroplane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> made the forts obsolete almost as soon as they were finished. Many of the forts now are under both the control of the town councils and the nature department and may be visited. Monuments Day, on the second Saturday in September, is the ideal day to visit, as entrance is free.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stelling_van_Amsterdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Stelling van Amsterdam was primarily a <a href="/wiki/Defense_line" title="Defense line">defensive "water line"</a> (in Dutch: <i>waterlinie</i>). In the event of an enemy attack, large tracts of land around Amsterdam would be inundated with water, preventing the enemy from advancing. Amsterdam would function as a <a href="/wiki/National_redoubt" title="National redoubt">national redoubt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reduit" title="Reduit">reduit</a>, as the last stronghold of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. Forts were built where roads, railways, or dikes crossed through the water line. At such locations there would be no water to stop the enemy, and therefore the forts were intended to shell the enemy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stelling_van_Amsterdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The law for the construction of the Stelling van Amsterdam was passed in 1874 - a few years after the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">Unification of Germany</a> which placed a powerful new Great Power on the Netherlands' eastern border. During the preparations prior to construction, it became apparent that the design was already outdated by modern technical advances. The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Brisance" title="Brisance">brisance grenade</a> (which would explode on impact of the target) necessitated the change from <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a> to <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> forts. The Dutch did not have the required experience yet using and building with concrete so extensive tests had to be performed; concrete structures were shelled with the heaviest artillery available at that time. Further delays resulted from the fact that the sand foundations had to settle for several years before the forts could be built on them. Only in 1897 could the actual construction finally begin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service">Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stelling_van_Amsterdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Stelling van Amsterdam has never seen combat service and the use of aircraft rendered it obsolete after World War I. It was however maintained and kept in service until it was decommissioned in 1963.</p>
<p>The dike through the <a href="/wiki/Haarlemmermeer" title="Haarlemmermeer">Haarlemmermeer</a>, which made it possible to flood the southern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Polder" title="Polder">polder</a> while the northern portion could continue to produce food for Amsterdam, is now cut by the <a href="/wiki/A4_motorway_(Netherlands)" title="A4 motorway (Netherlands)">A4 Motorway</a>. This motorway also goes under the <a href="/wiki/Ringvaart" title="Ringvaart">Ringvaart</a> at <a href="/wiki/Roelofarendsveen" title="Roelofarendsveen">Roelofarendsveen</a>, making flooding of the Haarlemmermeer Polder, and future use of the Stelling no longer possible.</p>
<p>In 1996, the complete Stelling van Amsterdam was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.</p>
