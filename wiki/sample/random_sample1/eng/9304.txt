<p><b>Carcinoma</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> that develops from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epithelial_cells" title="Epithelial cells">epithelial cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Specifically, a carcinoma is a cancer that begins in a tissue that lines the inner or outer surfaces of the body, and that generally arises from cells originating in the <a href="/wiki/Endoderm" title="Endoderm">endodermal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ectoderm" title="Ectoderm">ectodermal</a> germ layer during <a href="/wiki/Embryogenesis" title="Embryogenesis">embryogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Definition-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carcinomas occur when the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of a cell is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_damage" title="DNA damage">damaged or altered</a> and the cell begins to grow uncontrollably and become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malignant" title="Malignant">malignant</a>. It is from the Greek καρκίνωμα 'karkinoma' meaning sore, ulcer, or cancer, itself derived from <i>karkinos</i> 'crab'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2004, no simple and comprehensive classification system has been devised and accepted within the scientific community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermanAristotle_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BermanAristotle-4">[4]</a></sup> Traditionally, however, malignancies have generally been classified into various types using a combination of criteria, including:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermanLineage_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BermanLineage-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>the cell type from which they start, specifically:</p>
<ol>
<li>Epithelial cells ⇨ carcinoma</li>
<li>Non-hematopoietic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesenchymal" title="Mesenchymal">mesenchymal</a> cells ⇨ <a href="/wiki/Sarcoma" title="Sarcoma">sarcoma</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hematopoietic" title="Hematopoietic">Hematopoietic</a> cells
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>-derived cells that normally mature in the bloodstream ⇨ <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">Leukemia</a></li>
<li>bone marrow-derived cells that normally mature in the lymphatic system ⇨ <a href="/wiki/Lymphoma" title="Lymphoma">Lymphoma</a></li>
</ol>
</li>
<li>Germ cells ⇨ <a href="/wiki/Germinoma" title="Germinoma">Germinoma</a></li>
</ol>
<p>Other criteria that play a role in a cancer diagnosis include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The degree to which the malignant cells resemble their normal, untransformed counterparts</li>
<li>the appearance of the local tissue and stromal architecture</li>
<li>the anatomical location from which tumors arise</li>
<li>genetic, epigenetic, and molecular features</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Histological_types">Histological types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Histological types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Adenocarcinoma" title="Adenocarcinoma">Adenocarcinoma</a></dt>
<dd>(<i>adeno</i> = <a href="/wiki/Gland" title="Gland">gland</a>) Refers to a carcinoma featuring microscopic glandular-related tissue cytology, tissue architecture, and/or gland-related molecular products, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Mucin" title="Mucin">mucin</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Squamous_cell_carcinoma" title="Squamous cell carcinoma">Squamous cell carcinoma</a></dt>
<dd>Refers to a carcinoma with observable features and characteristics indicative of squamous differentiation (intercellular bridges, keratinization, squamous pearls).</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Adenosquamous_carcinoma" title="Adenosquamous carcinoma">Adenosquamous carcinoma</a></dt>
<dd>Refers to a mixed tumor containing both adenocarcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma, wherein each of these cell types comprise at least 10% of the tumor volume.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">Anaplastic</a> carcinoma</dt>
<dd>Refers to a heterogeneous group of high-grade carcinomas that feature cells lacking distinct histological or cytological evidence of any of the more specifically differentiated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoplasms" title="Neoplasms">neoplasms</a>. These tumors are referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaplastic" title="Anaplastic">anaplastic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">undifferentiated</a> carcinomas.</dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_cell_carcinoma" title="Large cell carcinoma">Large cell carcinoma</a></dt>
<dd>Composed of large, monotonous rounded or overtly polygonal-shaped cells with abundant <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a>.</dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_cell_carcinoma" title="Small cell carcinoma">Small cell carcinoma</a></dt>
<dd>Cells are usually round and are less than approximately 3 times the diameter of a resting <a href="/wiki/Lymphocyte" title="Lymphocyte">lymphocyte</a> and little evident cytoplasm. Occasionally, small cell malignancies may themselves have significant components of slightly polygonal and/or spindle-shaped cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who2004_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-who2004-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>There are a large number of rare subtypes of anaplastic, undifferentiated carcinoma. Some of the more well known include the lesions containing pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Sarcoma" title="Sarcoma">sarcomatous</a> components: <a href="/wiki/Spindle_cell_carcinoma" title="Spindle cell carcinoma">spindle cell carcinoma</a> (containing elongated cells resembling connective tissue cancers), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giant_cell_carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giant cell carcinoma (page does not exist)">giant cell carcinoma</a> (containing huge, bizarre, multinucleated cells), and <a href="/wiki/Sarcomatoid_carcinoma" title="Sarcomatoid carcinoma">sarcomatoid carcinoma</a> (mixtures of spindle and giant cell carcinoma). <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pleomorphic_carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pleomorphic carcinoma (page does not exist)">Pleomorphic carcinoma</a> contains spindle cell and/or giant cell components, plus at least a 10% component of cells characteristic of more highly differentiated types (i.e. adenocarcinoma and/or squamous cell carcinoma). Very rarely, tumors may contain individuals components resembling both carcinoma and true <a href="/wiki/Sarcoma" title="Sarcoma">sarcoma</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Carcinosarcoma" title="Carcinosarcoma">carcinosarcoma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_blastoma" title="Pulmonary blastoma">pulmonary blastoma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who2004_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-who2004-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carcinoma_of_unknown_primary">Carcinoma of unknown primary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carcinoma of unknown primary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term carcinoma has also come to encompass malignant tumors composed of transformed <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> whose origin or developmental lineage is unknown (see <a href="/wiki/Cancer_of_unknown_primary_origin" title="Cancer of unknown primary origin">cancer of unknown primary origin</a> (CUP)), but that possess certain specific molecular, cellular, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histological" title="Histological">histological</a> characteristics typical of epithelial cells. This may include the production of one or more forms of <a href="/wiki/Cytokeratin" title="Cytokeratin">cytokeratin</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermediate_filaments" title="Intermediate filaments">intermediate filaments</a>, intercellular bridge structures, <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a> pearls, and/or tissue architectural motifs such as stratification or pseudo-stratification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermanAristotle_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BermanAristotle-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermanLineage_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BermanLineage-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carcinoma_In_situ">Carcinoma <i>In situ</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carcinoma In situ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Carcinoma_in_situ" title="Carcinoma in situ">carcinoma in situ</a></i> (or CIS) is a term for cells that are significantly <a href="/wiki/Dysplasia" title="Dysplasia">abnormal</a> but not cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They are thus not typically carcinomas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogenesis">Pathogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pathogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cancer occurs when a single progenitor cell accumulates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutations" title="Mutations">mutations</a> and other changes in the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histones" title="Histones">histones</a>, and other biochemical compounds that make up the cell's <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>. The cell genome controls the structure of the cell's biochemical components, the biochemical reactions that occur within the cell, and the biological interactions of that cell with other cells. Certain combinations of mutations in the given progenitor cell ultimately result in that cell (also called a cancer stem cell) displaying a number of abnormal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malignant" title="Malignant">malignant</a> cellular properties that, when taken together, are considered characteristic of cancer, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>the ability to continue to divide perpetually, producing an exponentially (or near-exponentially) increasing number of new malignant cancerous "daughter cells" (uncontrolled <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a>);</li>
<li>the ability to penetrate normal body surfaces and barriers, and to bore into or through nearby body structures and tissues (local invasiveness);</li>
<li>the ability to spread to other sites within the body (<a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasize</a>) by penetrating or entering into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphatic_vessels" title="Lymphatic vessels">lymphatic vessels</a> (regional metastasis) and/or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_vessels" title="Blood vessels">blood vessels</a> (distant metastasis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Credo_Reference_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Credo_Reference-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>If this process of continuous growth, local invasion, and regional and distant metastasis is not halted via a combination of stimulation of immunological defenses and medical treatment interventions, the end result is that the host suffers a continuously increasing burden of tumor cells throughout the body. Eventually, the tumor burden increasingly interferes with normal biochemical functions carried out by the host's <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> ultimately ensues.</p>
<p>Carcinoma is but one form of cancer - one composed of cells that have developed the cytological appearance, histological architecture, or molecular characteristics of epithelial cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermanAristotle_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BermanAristotle-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermanLineage_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-BermanLineage-5">[5]</a></sup> A progenitor carcinoma stem cell can be formed from any of a number of oncogenic combinations of mutations in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totipotent" title="Totipotent">totipotent</a> cell,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multipotent" title="Multipotent">multipotent</a> cell,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or a mature <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">differentiated</a> cell.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasion_and_metastasis">Invasion and metastasis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Invasion and metastasis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hallmark of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malignant" title="Malignant">malignant</a> tumor is its tendency to invade and infiltrate local and adjacent structures and, eventually, spread from the site of its origin to non-adjacent regional and distant sites in the body, a process called <a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasis</a>. If unchecked, tumor growth and metastasis eventually creates a tumor burden so great that the host succumbs. Carcinoma metastasizes through both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymph_nodes" title="Lymph nodes">lymph nodes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutation">Mutation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mutation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause_of_mutations">Cause of mutations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cause of mutations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The likely major underlying cause of mutations in carcinomas is DNA damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, in the case of lung cancer, DNA damage is caused by agents in <a href="/wiki/Exogeny" title="Exogeny">exogenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Genotoxicity" title="Genotoxicity">genotoxic</a> tobacco smoke (e.g. acrolein, formaldehyde, acrylonitrile, 1,3-butadiene, acetaldehyde, ethylene oxide and isoprene).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring)" title="DNA damage (naturally occurring)">Endogenous (metabolically-caused) DNA damage</a> is also very frequent, occurring on average more than 60,000 times a day in the genomes of human cells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein-12">[12]</a></sup> (also see <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring)" title="DNA damage (naturally occurring)">DNA damage (naturally occurring)</a>). Externally and endogenously caused damages may be converted into mutations by inaccurate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translesion_synthesis" title="Translesion synthesis">translesion synthesis</a> or inaccurate DNA repair (e.g. by <a href="/wiki/Non-homologous_end_joining" title="Non-homologous end joining">non-homologous end joining</a>).</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="High_frequency">High frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: High frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The high frequency of mutations in the total genome within carcinomas suggests that, often, an early carcinogenic alteration may be a deficiency in DNA repair. For instance, mutation rates substantially increase (sometimes by 100-fold) in cells defective in <a href="/wiki/DNA_mismatch_repair" title="DNA mismatch repair">DNA mismatch repair</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hegan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hegan-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A deficiency in DNA repair, itself, can allow DNA damages to accumulate, and error-prone <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair" title="DNA repair">translesion synthesis</a> past some of those damages may give rise to mutations. In addition, faulty repair of these accumulated DNA damages may give rise to <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetic alterations or epimutations</a>. While a mutation or epimutation in a DNA repair gene, itself, would not confer a selective advantage, such a repair defect may be carried along as a passenger in a cell when the cell acquires an additional mutation/epimutation that does provide a proliferative advantage. Such cells, with both proliferative advantages and one or more DNA repair defects (causing a very high mutation rate), likely give rise to the high frequency of total genome mutations seen in carcinomas.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_repair">DNA repair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: DNA repair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In somatic cells, deficiencies in DNA repair sometimes arise by mutations in DNA repair genes, but much more often are due to <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetic</a> reductions in expression of DNA repair genes. Thus, in a sequence of 113 colorectal carcinomas, only four had somatic missense mutations in the DNA repair gene <i>MGMT</i>, while the majority of these cancers had reduced MGMT protein expression due to methylation of the <i>MGMT</i> promoter region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15888787_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15888787-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carcinomas can be definitively diagnosed through <a href="/wiki/Biopsy" title="Biopsy">biopsy</a>, including fine-needle aspiration (FNA), core biopsy, or subtotal removal of single node,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Microscopic examination by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathologist" title="Pathologist">pathologist</a> is then necessary to identify molecular, cellular, or tissue architectural characteristics of epithelial cells.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Oral: Most of oral cancers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squamous-cell_carcinoma" title="Squamous-cell carcinoma">squamous-cell carcinoma</a></li>
<li>Lung: Carcinoma comprises &gt;98% of all <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancers</a>.</li>
<li>Breast: Nearly all breast cancers are <a href="/wiki/Ductal_carcinoma" title="Ductal carcinoma">ductal carcinoma</a>.</li>
<li>Prostate: The most common form of <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">carcinoma of the prostate</a> is adenocarcinoma.</li>
<li>Colon and rectum: Nearly all malignancies of the colon and rectum are either adenocarcinoma or squamous cell carcinoma.</li>
<li>Pancreas: Pancreatic carcinoma is almost always of the adenocarcinoma type and is highly lethal.</li>
<li>Ovaries: One of the most deadly forms due to late detection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_Direct_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science_Direct-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Some carcinomas are named for their or the putative cell of origin, (e.g.<a href="/wiki/Hepatocellular_carcinoma" title="Hepatocellular carcinoma">hepatocellular carcinoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renal_cell_carcinoma" title="Renal cell carcinoma">renal cell carcinoma</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Staging">Staging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Staging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cancer_staging" title="Cancer staging">Staging</a> of carcinoma refers to the process of combining physical/clinical examination, pathological review of cells and tissues, surgical techniques, laboratory tests, and imaging studies in a logical fashion to obtain information about the size of the neoplasm and the extent of its invasion and <a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasis</a>.</p>
<p>Carcinomas are usually staged with Roman numerals. In most classifications, Stage I and Stage II carcinomas are confirmed when the tumor has been found to be small and/or to have spread to local structures only. Stage III carcinomas typically have been found to have spread to regional lymph nodes, tissues, and/or organ structures, while Stage IV tumors have already metastasized through the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> to distant sites, tissues, or organs.</p>
<p>In some types of carcinomas, Stage 0 carcinoma has been used to describe <a href="/wiki/Carcinoma_in_situ" title="Carcinoma in situ">carcinoma <i>in situ</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a> carcinomas detectable only via examination of <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">sputum</a> for malignant cells (in <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung carcinomas</a>).</p>
<p>In more recent staging systems, substages (a, b, c) are becoming more commonly used to better define groups of patients with similar prognosis or treatment options.</p>
<p>Carcinoma stage is the variable that has been most consistently and tightly linked to the <a href="/wiki/Prognosis" title="Prognosis">prognosis</a> of the malignancy.</p>
<p>The criteria for staging can differ dramatically based upon the organ system in which the tumor arises. For example, the colon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PuppaSonzogni_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-PuppaSonzogni-18">[18]</a></sup> and bladder cancer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharir_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharir-19">[19]</a></sup> staging system relies on depth of invasion, staging of breast carcinoma is more dependent on the size of the tumor, and in renal carcinoma, staging is based on both the size of the tumor and the depth of the tumor invasion into the renal sinus. Carcinoma of the lung has a more complicated staging system, taking into account a number of size and anatomic variables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PepekChino_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-PepekChino-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UICC/AJCC <a href="/wiki/TNM_staging_system" title="TNM staging system">TNM</a> systems are most often used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> For some common tumors, however, classical staging methods (such as the <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_cancer#Dukes_system" title="Colorectal cancer">Dukes classification</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_cancer" title="Colon cancer">colon cancer</a>) are still used.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grading">Grading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Grading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grading of carcinomas refers to the employment of criteria intended to semi-quantify the degree of cellular and tissue maturity seen in the transformed cells relative to the appearance of the normal parent epithelial tissue from which the carcinoma derives.</p>
<p>Grading of carcinoma is most often done after a treating physician and/or surgeon obtains a sample of suspected tumor tissue using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgical_resection" title="Surgical resection">surgical resection</a>, needle or surgical <a href="/wiki/Biopsy" title="Biopsy">biopsy</a>, direct washing or brushing of tumor tissue, <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">sputum</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytopathology" title="Cytopathology">cytopathology</a>, etc. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathologist" title="Pathologist">pathologist</a> then examines the tumor and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stroma_(animal_tissue)" title="Stroma (animal tissue)">stroma</a>, perhaps utilizing <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immunohistochemistry" title="Immunohistochemistry">immunohistochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flow_cytometry" title="Flow cytometry">flow cytometry</a>, or other methods. Finally, the pathologist classifies the tumor semi-quantitatively into one of three or four grades, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Grade 1, or well differentiated: there is a close, or very close, resemblance to the normal parent tissue, and the tumor cells are easily identified and classified as a particular malignant histological entity;</li>
<li>Grade 2, or moderately differentiated: there is considerable resemblance to the parent cells and tissues, but abnormalities can commonly be seen and the more complex features are not particularly well-formed;</li>
<li>Grade 3, or poorly differentiated: there is very little resemblance between the malignant tissue and the normal parent tissue, abnormalities are evident, and the more complex architectural features are usually rudimentary or primitive;</li>
<li>Grade 4, or undifferentiated carcinoma: these carcinomas bear no significant resemblance to the corresponding parent cells and tissues, with no visible formation of glands, ducts, bridges, stratified layers, keratin pearls, or other notable characteristics consistent with a more highly differentiated neoplasm.</li>
</ul>
<p>Although there is definite and convincing statistical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">correlation</a> between carcinoma grade and tumor prognosis for some tumor types and sites of origin, the strength of this association can be highly variable. It may be stated generally, however, that the higher the grade of the lesion, the worse is its prognosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SunAubry_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SunAubry-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carcinoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While cancer is generally considered a disease of old age, children can also develop cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In contrast to adults, carcinomas are exceptionally rare in children.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>The two biggest risk factors for ovarian carcinoma are age and family history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_Reference_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_Reference-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
