<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ampere</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>ampere</b> (<a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">unit</a> symbol: A), often <a href="/wiki/Clipping_(morphology)" title="Clipping (morphology)">shortened</a> to "amp",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2006-1">[1]</a></sup> is the SI unit of <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPMdefinition_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMdefinition-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimension_of_a_physical_quantity" title="Dimension of a physical quantity">dimension</a> symbol: I)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and is one of the seven<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base units</a>. It is named after <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re" title="André-Marie Ampère">André-Marie Ampère</a> (1775–1836), French mathematician and physicist, considered the father of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamics" title="Electrodynamics">electrodynamics</a>.</p>
<p>SI defines ampere as follows:</p>
<p>The ampere is that constant current which, if maintained in two straight parallel conductors of infinite length, of negligible circular cross-section, and placed apart in vacuum, would produce between these conductors a force equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6993200000000000000♠</span>2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−7</sup></span> newton per metre of length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The old definition (valid until 1948) is:</p>
<p><i>The ampere is equivalent to one <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulomb</a> (roughly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018624200000000000♠</span>6.242<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup></span> times the <a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">elementary charge</a>) per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Amperes are used to express flow rate of electric charge. For any point experiencing a current, if the number of charged particles passing through it — or the charge on the particles passing through it — is increased, the amperes of current at that point will proportionately increase.</i></p>
<p>The ampere should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulomb</a> (also called "ampere-second") or the <a href="/wiki/Ampere_hour" title="Ampere hour">ampere hour</a> (A⋅h). The ampere is a unit of current, the amount of charge transiting per unit time, and the coulomb is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charge</a>. When <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI units</a> are used, constant, instantaneous and average current are expressed in amperes (as in "the charging current is 1.2 A") and the charge accumulated, or passed through a circuit over a period of time is expressed in coulombs (as in "the <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> charge is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004300000000000000♠</span>30<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> C</span>"). The relation of the ampere (C/s) to the coulomb is the same as that of the <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> (J/s) to the <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_force_law" title="Ampère's force law">Ampère's force law</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Serway_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Serway-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beyond_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beyond-9">[9]</a></sup> states that there is an attractive or repulsive force between two parallel wires carrying an electric current. This force is used in the formal definition of the ampere, which states that the ampere is the constant current that will produce an attractive force of 2 × 10<sup>−7</sup> <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newtons</a> per metre of length between two straight, parallel conductors of infinite length and negligible circular <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">cross section</a> placed one <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> apart in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPMdefinition_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMdefinition-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SI unit of charge, the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulomb</a>, "is the quantity of electricity carried in 1 second by a current of 1 ampere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Conversely, a current of one ampere is one coulomb of charge going past a given point per second:</p>

<p>In general, charge <i>Q</i> is determined by steady current <i>I</i> flowing for a time <i>t</i> as <span class="nowrap"><i>Q</i> = <i>It</i></span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ampere was originally defined as one tenth of the unit of <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units" title="Centimetre–gram–second system of units">centimetre–gram–second system of units</a>. That unit, now known as the <a href="/wiki/Abampere" title="Abampere">abampere</a>, was defined as the amount of current that generates a force of two <a href="/wiki/Dyne" title="Dyne">dynes</a> per centimetre of length between two wires one centimetre apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The size of the unit was chosen so that the units derived from it in the <a href="/wiki/MKS_system_of_units" title="MKS system of units">MKSA</a> system would be conveniently sized.</p>
<p>The "international ampere" was an early realization of the ampere, defined as the current that would deposit <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997111800000000000♠</span>0.001<span style="margin-left:.25em;">118</span> grams</span> of silver per second from a <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a> solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Later, more accurate measurements revealed that this current is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999999850000000000♠</span>0.999<span style="margin-left:.25em;">85</span> A</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Realization">Realization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Realization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The standard ampere is most accurately realized using a <a href="/wiki/Watt_balance" title="Watt balance">watt balance</a>, but is in practice maintained via <a href="/wiki/Ohm%27s_law" title="Ohm's law">Ohm's law</a> from the units of <a href="/wiki/Electromotive_force" title="Electromotive force">electromotive force</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">resistance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ohm" title="Ohm">ohm</a>, since the latter two can be tied to physical phenomena that are relatively easy to reproduce, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josephson_junction" title="Josephson junction">Josephson junction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_Hall_effect" title="Quantum Hall effect">quantum Hall effect</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electrical_quantities_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Electrical_quantities-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>At present, techniques to establish the realization of an ampere have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_uncertainty" title="Relative uncertainty">relative uncertainty</a> of approximately a few parts in 10<sup>7</sup>, and involve realizations of the watt, the ohm and the volt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electrical_quantities_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Electrical_quantities-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_future_definition">Proposed future definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proposed future definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_SI_definitions" title="New SI definitions">New SI definitions</a></div>
<p>Rather than a definition in terms of the force between two current-carrying wires, it has been proposed that the ampere should be defined in terms of the rate of flow of elementary charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beyond_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beyond-9">[9]</a></sup> Since a <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulomb</a> is approximately equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018624150930000000♠</span>6.241<span style="margin-left:.25em;">5093</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup></span> <a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">elementary charges</a> (such as those carried by <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>, or the negative of those carried by <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>), one ampere is approximately equivalent to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018624150930000000♠</span>6.241<span style="margin-left:.25em;">5093</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup></span> elementary charges moving past a boundary in one second. (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018624150930000000♠</span>6.241<span style="margin-left:.25em;">5093</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup></span> is the reciprocal of the value of the elementary charge in coulombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>) The proposed change would define 1 A as being the current in the direction of flow of a particular number of elementary charges per second. In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> (CIPM) agreed to study the proposed change. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_SI_definitions" title="New SI definitions">new definition</a> was discussed at the 25th <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> (CGPM) in 2014 but for the time being was not adopted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Everyday_examples">Everyday examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Everyday examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(current)" title="Orders of magnitude (current)">Orders of magnitude (current)</a></div>
<p>The current drawn by typical constant-voltage energy distribution systems is usually dictated by the power (<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>) consumed by the system and the operating voltage. For this reason the examples given below are grouped by voltage level.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portable_devices">Portable devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Portable devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Hearing aid (typically 1 mW at 1.4 V): 0.7 mA</li>
<li>USB charging cable (typically 10.5 W at 5.0 V): 2.1 A</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_combustion_engine_vehicles_.E2.80.93_12_V_DC">Internal combustion engine vehicles – 12 V DC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Internal combustion engine vehicles – 12 V DC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A typical motor vehicle has a 12 V battery. The various accessories that are powered by the battery might include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Instrument panel light (typically 2 W): 166 mA</li>
<li>Headlight (each, typically 60 W): 5 A</li>
<li>Starter motor (typically 1–2 kW): 80–160 A</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_domestic_supply_.E2.80.93_120_V_AC">North American domestic supply – 120 V AC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: North American domestic supply – 120 V AC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most Canada, Mexico and United States domestic power suppliers run at 120 V.</p>
<p>Household <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">circuit breakers</a> typically provide a maximum of 15 A or 20 A of current to a given set of outlets.</p>
<ul>
<li>22-inch/56-centimeter portable television (35 W): 290 mA</li>
<li>Tungsten light bulb (60–100 W): 500–830 mA</li>
<li>Toaster, kettle (1.5 kW): 12.5 A</li>
<li>Hair dryer (1.8 kW): 15 A</li>
<li>USB cell phone charger (typically 10 W): 83 mA</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_.26_Commonwealth_domestic_supply_.E2.80.93_230.E2.80.93240_V_AC">European &amp; Commonwealth domestic supply – 230–240 V AC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ampere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: European &amp; Commonwealth domestic supply – 230–240 V AC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most European domestic power supplies run at 230 V, and most <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> domestic power supplies run at 240 V. For the same amount of power (in watts), the current drawn by a particular European or Commonwealth appliance (in Europe or a Commonwealth country) will be less than for an equivalent North American appliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[Note 1]</a></sup> Typical circuit breakers will provide 16 A.</p>
<p>The current drawn by a number of typical appliances are:</p>
<ul>
<li>22-inch/56-centimeter portable television (35 W): 145–150 mA</li>
<li>Tungsten light bulb (60–100 W): 240–450 mA</li>
<li>Compact fluorescent lamp (11–30 W): 56–112 mA</li>
<li>Toaster, kettle (2 kW): 9 A</li>
<li>Immersion heater (4.6 kW): 19–20 A</li>
</ul>
