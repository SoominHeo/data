<p><b>Amfleet</b> is a fleet of single-level intercity railroad <a href="/wiki/Passenger_car_(rail)" title="Passenger car (rail)">passenger cars</a> built by the <a href="/wiki/Budd_Company" title="Budd Company">Budd Company</a> for <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Budd based the Amfleet design on its earlier <a href="/wiki/Budd_Metroliner" title="Budd Metroliner">Metroliner</a> electric multiple unit. They were the first new locomotive-hauled cars ordered by Amtrak. As of 2015, Amfleet cars are used extensively in the eastern and mid-western <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, forming the backbone of Amtrak's single-level fleet.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amfleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Fleet" title="Heritage Fleet">Heritage Fleet</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amfleet_postcard,_circa_1975.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="1460" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Amfleet_postcard%2C_circa_1975.jpg/220px-Amfleet_postcard%2C_circa_1975.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Amfleet_postcard%2C_circa_1975.jpg/330px-Amfleet_postcard%2C_circa_1975.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Amfleet_postcard%2C_circa_1975.jpg/440px-Amfleet_postcard%2C_circa_1975.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amfleet_postcard,_circa_1975.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mid-1970s postcard advertising the then new Amfleet cars</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Amtrak inherited a fleet of approximately 1,200 steam-heated coaches from private sector railroads when it began operations in 1971. These cars were aging, in need of maintenance, and in many cases incompatible with each other. Amtrak recognized the need and opportunity to standardize on a single design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amfleet_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-amfleet-1">[1]</a></sup> Amtrak ordered the Amfleets from Budd in two batches. It placed the first order on October 12, 1973, along with orders for 70 <a href="/wiki/EMD_SDP40F" title="EMD SDP40F">EMD SDP40F</a> diesel locomotives and 11 <a href="/wiki/GE_E60" title="GE E60">GE E60</a> locomotives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ink201009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ink201009-2">[2]</a></sup> The first batch, which came to be known as "Amfleet I", consisted of 492 cars in the following configurations:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>54 "Amcafe" cafe cars with 51 coach seats and a snack bar in the middle</li>
<li>37 "Amdinette" cafe/dining cars with a snack bar, eight four-person booths and 23 coach seats</li>
<li>40 "Amclub" club/parlor cars with a snack bar, 18 club seats and 23 coach seats</li>
<li>90 "Amcoach" long-distance coaches with 60 seats</li>
<li>271 "Amcoach" short-distance coaches with 84 seats</li>
</ul>
<p>The first Amfleet cars began running on the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a> on August 5, 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ink201009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ink201009-2">[2]</a></sup> Amtrak heralded their arrival, calling 1975 as "the Year of the Amfleet". As the cars were distributed throughout the system, timetables would note that trains now had "Amfleet Service" and that trains had been "Amfleeted".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amfleet_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-amfleet-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amtrak followed up in 1981–1983 with a second order, dubbed "Amfleet II". This order consisted of 150 cars:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>125 "Amcoach" long-distance cars with 59 seats</li>
<li>25 "Amlounge" lounge cars with a snack bar, ten four-person booths, and 17 assorted seats</li>
</ul>
<p>The Amfleet II cars were intended to replace rolling stock on Amtrak long-distance trains, featuring larger windows, more legroom, and folding legrests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trainmaster198111_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-trainmaster198111-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amfleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because Amfleet cars can fit through the tunnels and under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overhead_wires" title="Overhead wires">catenary</a> of the northeastern United States, Amtrak uses them heavily in that area.</p>
<p>Amfleet rolling stock mostly disappeared from service in California when the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Joaquins" title="San Joaquins">San Joaquins</a></i> switched to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizon_Fleet" title="Horizon Fleet">Horizon Fleet</a> in the 1990s and then to "<a href="/wiki/Surfliner_(railcar)" title="Surfliner (railcar)">Surfliner</a>" railcars later in the decade. The <i><a href="/wiki/San_Diegan_(train)" title="San Diegan (train)">San Diegans</a></i> also stopped using the <i>Amfleet</i> when their <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Surfliner" title="Pacific Surfliner">Pacific Surfliner</a> cars were delivered (with the exception of a single trainset that runs during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Del_Mar_Racetrack" title="Del Mar Racetrack">Del Mar Racetrack</a> season in July and August, and during Thanksgiving week). The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascades_(passenger_train)" title="Cascades (passenger train)">Cascades</a></i> now use <a href="/wiki/Talgo" title="Talgo">Talgo</a> train sets instead of Amfleet.</p>

<p>Each type of Amfleet, I and II, currently is composed of coaches and food service cars. Amfleet IIs include lounge cars. Amfleet I cars can be identified by having vestibules at both ends of the cars; Amfleet II cars have a single vestibule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> North American standard (120 V, 60 Hz) electrical outlets are provided in most cars of both types. Amfleet I cars are mostly used on corridor trains in the northeast, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Downeaster_(train)" title="Downeaster (train)">Downeaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Empire_Service_(train)" title="Empire Service (train)">Empire Service</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Northeast_Regional" title="Northeast Regional">Northeast Regional</a></i>; Amfleet II cars are used on long-distance trains, such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Amtrak)" title="Cardinal (Amtrak)">Cardinal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Shore_Limited" title="Lake Shore Limited">Lake Shore Limited</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Star_(Amtrak)" title="Silver Star (Amtrak)">Silver Star</a></i>.</p>
<p>Amfleet I coaches are configured as either Regional coach class (72 seats per car, with 2x2 seating)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIP-25_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIP-25-7">[7]</a></sup> or business class (62 seats per car, with 2x2 seating).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Amfleet I food service cars are either Club-Dinettes or Full-Dinettes. Club-Dinettes have 6 tables at one end and a snack bar in the middle, followed by 18 business class seats (1x2 seating). Some tables may be used by train crew members for paperwork. Full-Dinettes have tables on both sides of the snack bar. Table seats in food service cars are not sold and may be used by any passenger.</p>
<p>An Amfleet II coach seats 60 passengers (2x2 seating). Amfleet II food service cars are referred to as Diner-Lites to distinguish them from other types of Amtrak dining cars. When first delivered, the Diner-Lites were configured as <i>Amfleet II Lounge</i> containing 17 lounge seats on one end, a snack bar in the middle, and 8 tables at the other end of the car. Some cars were later rebuilt with an enclosed smoking room in place of the lounge seats. All Amlounge IIs were later converted to Diner-Lites between 2006-2010 with additional tables added where the lounge seats once were and increased food service capability (including new ovens).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the late 1970s Amtrak converted two Amfleet coaches into <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_car" title="Sleeping car">sleepers</a> (#22900 and #22901). Two prototype <a href="/wiki/Superliner_(railcar)" title="Superliner (railcar)">Superliner</a> roomette modules were installed, displacing twelve seats. The cars were used on the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_(Amtrak_train)" title="Shenandoah (Amtrak train)">Shenandoah</a></i>. Regular sleepers returned to the <i>Shenandoah</i> in 1979 and the two coaches were returned to a standard configuration. These conversions were termed "Ampad."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amtrak_paint_schemes" title="Amtrak paint schemes">Amtrak paint schemes</a></div>
<p>Amfleet I cars are the only Amtrak rolling stock to have carried all five normal paint schemes. These paint schemes are referred to as "Phases". Phase I-painted Amfleet cars had large red and blue stripes around the windows with thin white stripes on each end of the pattern, and featured the original Amtrak logo on one end of the car.</p>
<p>Amtrak's Phase II paint scheme eliminated the arrow logo. The Amtrak logo and coach number were printed in white.</p>
<p>Phase III paint featured stripes that border the windows, and had equal-width red, white, and blue stripes. The Phase III paint scheme marked a switch to black Amtrak logos and coach numbers.</p>
<p>Phase IV (aka "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NortheastDirect" title="NortheastDirect">NortheastDirect</a>") paint departed more noticeably from the previous designs. This scheme consists of a large blue stripe outlining the windows, and smaller red and white stripes above the blue stripe. On more recently painted cars, a red reflective stripe runs along the bottom of the car.</p>
<p>For a short period of time in the early 2000s, some Amfleet cars were painted in the Phase V style, also known as the Acela Phase. The Acela paint scheme varied depending upon the type of car, with the different types having different colored "splotches" on them. The Coach class cars were decorated by a <a href="/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a> window stripe and a darker-shaded turquoise splotch, and the Business class cars were decorated by a navy blue window stripe with a light-turquoise splotch. Cafe cars were decorated with light-turquoise splotches and navy blue window stripes, and instead of a window where the snack bar was, there was a splotch of spring green.</p>
<p>All active Amfleet cars are currently painted in the Phase IVb scheme, which is Phase IV with the newer Amtrak logo, although some business Amfleet cars now wear the Phase IIIb paint scheme, which is Phase III with the current Amtrak logo.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_and_build">Specifications and build</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amfleet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Specifications and build">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Amfleet cars are largely based upon the 1966 <a href="/wiki/Budd_Metroliner" title="Budd Metroliner">Metroliner</a> design and were originally designed to operate at speeds up to 120 mph (193 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amfleet_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-amfleet-1">[1]</a></sup> Both the Amfleet I and Amfleet II are now rated for 125 mph (201 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIP-25_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-CIP-25-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>An Amfleet car is 12 feet 8 inches (3,860 mm) tall (relative to the railhead), 10 feet 6 inches (3,200 mm) wide, and 85 feet 4 inches (26,010 mm) in length over the <a href="/wiki/Vestibule_(architecture)" title="Vestibule (architecture)">vestibule</a> diaphragm faceplates. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carbody&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carbody (page does not exist)">carbody</a> itself is built up from <a href="/wiki/Spot_welding" title="Spot welding">spot-welded</a> <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> sections, resulting in an exceptionally strong structure that is resistant to corrosion. Due to the length of the car, a noticeable arch is built into the carbody to prevent sagging when carrying a full passenger load.</p>
<p>A cafe car weighs about 110,000 pounds (50,000 kg), while a coach weighs approximately 106,000 pounds (48,000 kg). Amfleet seats have swing-down tray-tables for at-seat food service, overhead and underseat luggage storage (similar to that on a commercial <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a>), and all cars (including cafes) are equipped with at least one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Restroom" title="Restroom">restroom</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_heat" title="Electric heat">Electric heating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>, operated by <a href="/wiki/Head-end_power" title="Head-end power">head-end power</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotive</a>, are used to maintain passenger comfort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A feature inherited from older Budd-built cars is the use of dual <a href="/wiki/Disc_brake" title="Disc brake">disc brakes</a> on each <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a>, with electronic <a href="/wiki/Anti-lock_braking_system" title="Anti-lock braking system">anti-slide</a> controls to prevent wheel lockup during full service or emergency brake applications. Although this braking system is more costly than the traditional wheel tread shoe braking design, experience has shown it to be a better-performing and lower maintenance alternative.</p>
<p>One major difference between the Amfleet I and II is the <a href="/wiki/Passenger_train_toilet" title="Passenger train toilet">toilet type</a>. The Amfleet I has chemical flush toilets; while the Amfleet II has retention toilets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIP-25_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-CIP-25-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
