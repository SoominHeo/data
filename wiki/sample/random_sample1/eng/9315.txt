<p><b>`Abdu’l-Bahá'</b> <span style="font-size:100%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Knight Commander of the Order of the British Empire">KBE</a></span> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">عبد البهاء</span>‎‎‎, 23 May 1844 – 28 November 1921), born <b>‘Abbás Effendí</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">عباس افندی</span>‎‎), was the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>. In 1892, `Abdu'l-Bahá was appointed in his father's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tablets_of_Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h#Kit.C3.A1b-i-.60Ahd_.28Book_of_the_Covenant.29" title="Tablets of Bahá'u'lláh">will</a> to be his successor and head of the Bahá'í Faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320-4">[3]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá was born in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a> to an aristocratic family of the realm. At the age of eight his father was imprisoned and the family's possessions were looted, leaving them in virtual poverty. Along with his father, `Abdu'l-Bahá was exiled to <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> where the family lived for nine years.</p>
<p>During his youth he was faithful to his father and was regarded as an outstanding member of the Bahá’í exile community. As a teenager he was his father’s <a href="/wiki/Amanuensis" title="Amanuensis">amanuensis</a> and was regularly seen debating theological issues with the learned men of the area. In 1863, Bahá'u'lláh and his family were banished from Baghdad to <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>. During the 1860s the family was further banished from Constantinople to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrianople" title="Adrianople">Adrianople</a>, and then finally to the penal-colony of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acre,_Palestine" title="Acre, Palestine">Acre, Palestine</a>.</p>
<p>With his father's death in 1892, and his appointment as head of the Bahá’í Faith, there was much opposition to him, including virtually all his family members. Notwithstanding this, practically all of the worldwide Bahá’í community accepted his leadership. In 1908, at the age of 64 and after forty years imprisonment, `Abdu’l-Bahá was freed by the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turks" title="Young Turks">Young Turks</a> and he and his family began to live in relative safety. His <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1%27s_journeys_to_the_West" title="`Abdu'l-Bahá's journeys to the West">journeys to the West</a>, and his "<a href="/wiki/Tablets_of_the_Divine_Plan" title="Tablets of the Divine Plan">Tablets of the Divine Plan</a>" spread the Bahá'í message beyond its middle-eastern roots, and his <a href="/wiki/Will_and_Testament_of_%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="Will and Testament of `Abdu'l-Bahá">Will and Testament</a> laid the foundation for the current "<a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_administration" title="Bahá'í administration">Bahá'í administrative order</a>. Many of his writings, prayers and letters are extant, and his discourses with the Western Bahá'ís emphasize the growth of the faith by the late 1890s. `Abdu'l-Bahá's <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> was `Abbás, but he preferred the title of `Abdu'l-Bahá (servant of the glory of God). He is commonly referred to in Bahá'í texts as "The Master", and received the title of <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">KBE</a> after his personal storage of grain was used to relieve famine in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Palestine#Ottoman_era" title="History of Palestine">Palestine</a> following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, but never used the title.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>`Abdu'l-Bahá was born in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, Iran on 23 May 1844 (5th of <a href="/wiki/Jumada_al-awwal" title="Jumada al-awwal">Jamadiyu'l-Avval</a>, 1260 AH),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qazvini_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Qazvini-5">[4]</a></sup> the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%81s%C3%ADyih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Ásíyih Khánum">Navváb</a>. He was born on the very same night on which the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b" title="Báb">Báb</a> declared his mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> Born with the given name of `Abbás,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320-4">[3]</a></sup> he was named after his grandfather <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADrz%C3%A1_%60Abb%C3%A1s_N%C3%BAr%C3%AD" title="Mírzá `Abbás Núrí">Mírzá `Abbás Núrí</a>, a prominent and powerful nobleman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup> As a child, `Abdu'l-Bahá was shaped by his father's position as a prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b%C3%AD" title="Bábí">Bábí</a>. He recalled how he met the Bábí <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1hirih" title="Táhirih">Táhirih</a> and how she would take "me on to her knee, caress me, and talk to me. I admired her most deeply".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> `Abdu’l-Bahá had a happy and carefree childhood. The family’s Tehran home and country houses were comfortable and beautifully decorated. `Abdu'l-Bahá enjoyed playing in the gardens with his younger sister with whom he was very close.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomfield_1975_40_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blomfield_1975_40-9">[8]</a></sup> Along with his younger siblings – a sister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bah%C3%ADyyih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Bahíyyih Khánum">Bahíyyih</a>, and a brother, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADrz%C3%A1_Mihd%C3%AD" title="Mírzá Mihdí">Mihdí</a> – the three lived in an environment of privilege, happiness and comfort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup> With his father declining a position as minister of the royal court; during his young boyhood `Abdu’l-Bahá witnessed his parents' various charitable endeavours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> which included converting part of the home to a hospital ward for women and children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomfield_1975_40_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blomfield_1975_40-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>`Abdu'l-Bahá received a haphazard education during his childhood. It was customary not to send children of nobility to schools. Most noblemen were educated at home briefly in scripture, rhetoric, calligraphy and basic mathematics. Many were educated to prepare themselves for life in the royal court. Despite a brief spell at a traditional preparatory school at the age of seven for one year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá received no formal education. As he grew he was educated by his mother, and uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Most of his education however, came from his father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Years later in 1890 <a href="/wiki/Edward_Granville_Browne" title="Edward Granville Browne">Edward Granville Browne</a> described how `Abdu'l-Bahá was "one more eloquent of speech, more ready of argument, more apt of illustration, more intimately acquainted with the sacred books of the Jews, the Christians, and the Muhammadans...scarcely be found even amongst the eloquent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>When `Abdu'l-Bahá was seven, he contracted tuberculosis and was expected to die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Though the malady faded away,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200112_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200112-16">[15]</a></sup> he would be plagued with bouts of illness for the rest of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>One event that affected `Abdu'l-Bahá greatly during his childhood was the imprisonment of his father when `Abdu'l-Bahá was eight years old; the imprisonment led to his family being reduced to poverty and being attacked in the streets by other children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá accompanied his mother to visit <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a> who was then imprisoned in the infamous subterranean dungeon the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%ADy%C3%A1h-Ch%C3%A1l" title="Síyáh-Chál">Síyáh-Chál</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup> He described how "I saw a dark, steep place. We entered a small, narrow doorway, and went down two steps, but beyond those one could see nothing. In the middle of the stairway, all of a sudden we heard His [Bahá’u’lláh's]…voice: 'Do not bring him in here', and so they took me back".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200112_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200112-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baghdad">Baghdad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Baghdad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bahá'u'lláh was eventually released from prison but ordered into exile, and `Abdu'l-Bahá then eight joined his father on the journey to <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> in the winter (January to April)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter.27sChron_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winter.27sChron-18">[17]</a></sup> of 1853.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200112_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200112-16">[15]</a></sup> During the journey `Abdu'l-Bahá suffered from frost-bite. After a year of difficulties Bahá'u'lláh absented himself rather than continue to face the conflict with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirza_Yahya" title="Mirza Yahya">Mirza Yahya</a> and secretly secluded himself in the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Sulaymaniyah" title="Sulaymaniyah">Sulaymaniyah</a> in April 1854 a month before `Abdu'l-Bahá's tenth birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter.27sChron_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Winter.27sChron-18">[17]</a></sup> Mutual sorrow resulted in him, his <a href="/wiki/%C3%81s%C3%ADyih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Ásíyih Khánum">mother</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bahiyyih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Bahiyyih Khánum">sister</a> becoming constant companions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá was particularly close to both, and his mother took active participation in his education and upbringing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomfield_1975_69_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blomfield_1975_69-20">[19]</a></sup> During the two-year absence of his father `Abdu'l-Bahá took up the duty of managing the affairs of the family,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> before his <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of maturity</a> (14 in middle-eastern society)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> and was known to be occupied with reading and, at a time of hand-copied scriptures being the primary means of publishing, was also engaged in copying the writings of the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b" title="Báb">Báb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200114_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200114-23">[22]</a></sup> `Abdu’l-Bahá also took an interest in the art of horse riding and, as he grew, became a renowned rider.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1856, news of an ascetic carrying on discourses with local Súfí leaders that seemed to possibly be Bahá'u'lláh reached the family and friends. Immediately, family members and friends went to search for the elusive dervish – and in March<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter.27sChron_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Winter.27sChron-18">[17]</a></sup> brought Bahá'u'lláh back to Baghdad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith17_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith17-25">[24]</a></sup> On seeing his father, `Abdu'l-Bahá fell to his knees and wept loudly "Why did you leave us?", and this followed with his mother and sister doing the same.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200115_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200115-26">[25]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá soon became his father's secretary and shield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> During the sojourn in the city `Abdu’l-Bahá grew from a boy into a young man. He was noted as a "remarkably fine looking youth",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> and remembered for his charity and amiableness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> Having passed the age of maturity `Abdu'l-Bahá was regularly seen in the mosques of Baghdad discussing religious topics and the scripture as a young man. Whilst in Baghdad, `Abdu'l-Bahá composed a commentary at the request of his father on the Muslim tradition of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_was_a_Hidden_Treasure" title="I was a Hidden Treasure">I was a Hidden Treasure</a>" for a Súfí leader named `Alí Shawkat Páshá.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwaht_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-iwaht-27">[26]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá was fifteen or sixteen at the time and `Alí Shawkat Páshá regarded the more than 11000 word essay as a remarkable feat for somebody of his age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> In 1863 in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Ridv%C3%A1n,_Baghdad" title="Garden of Ridván, Baghdad">Garden of Ridván</a> Bahá'u'lláh announced to a few that he was the <a href="/wiki/Manifestation_of_God" title="Manifestation of God">manifestation of God</a> and <a href="/wiki/He_whom_God_shall_make_manifest" title="He whom God shall make manifest">He whom God shall make manifest</a> whose coming had been foretold by the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b" title="Báb">Báb</a>. On day eight of the twelve days, it is believed `Abdu'l-Baha was the first person Baha'u'llah revealed his claim to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constantinople.2FAdrianople">Constantinople/Adrianople</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Constantinople/Adrianople">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brothers1868.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="920" data-file-width="700" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brothers1868.jpg/160px-Brothers1868.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brothers1868.jpg/240px-Brothers1868.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brothers1868.jpg/320px-Brothers1868.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brothers1868.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
`Abdu'l-Bahá (right) with his brother <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADrz%C3%A1_Mihd%C3%AD" title="Mírzá Mihdí">Mírzá Mihdí</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1863 Bahá'u'lláh was summoned to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (<a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>), and thus his whole family including `Abdu'l-Bahá, then nineteen, accompanied him on his 110-day journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200117_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200117-30">[29]</a></sup> The journey to Constantinople was another wearisome journey,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> and `Abdu'l-Bahá helped feed the exiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup> It was here that his position became more prominent amongst the Bahá’ís.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320-4">[3]</a></sup> This was further solidified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%E2%80%99u%E2%80%99ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá’u’lláh">Bahá’u’lláh</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Tablet_of_the_Branch" title="Tablet of the Branch">tablet of the Branch</a> in which he constantly exalts his son's virtues and station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totb_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-totb-31">[30]</a></sup> The family were soon exiled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrianople" title="Adrianople">Adrianople</a> and `Abdu'l-Bahá went with the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320-4">[3]</a></sup> `Abdu’l-Bahá again suffered from frostbite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Adrianople `Abdu’l-Bahá was regarded as the sole comforter of his family – in particular to his <a href="/wiki/%C3%81s%C3%ADyih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Ásíyih Khánum">mother</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> At this point `Abdu'l-Bahá was known by the Bahá'ís as "the Master", and by non-Bahá'ís as `Abbás Effendi ("Effendi" signifies "Sir"). It was in Adrianople that Bahá’u’lláh referred to his son as "the Mystery of God".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> The title of "Mystery of God" symbolises, according to Bahá'ís, that `Abdu'l-Bahá is not a <a href="/wiki/Manifestation_of_God" title="Manifestation of God">manifestation of God</a> but how a "person of `Abdu'l-Bahá the incompatible characteristics of a human nature and superhuman knowledge and perfection have been blended and are completely harmonized".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcob_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcob-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwo_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-bwo-33">[32]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá was at this point noted for having black hair which flowed to his shoulders, large blue eyes, alabaster coloured skin and a slight Roman nose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Bahá'u'lláh gave his son many other titles such as "the Most Mighty Branch" the "Branch of Holiness", "the Center of the Covenant" and the apple of his eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320-4">[3]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá ("the Master") was devastated when hearing the news that he and his family were to be exiled separately from <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a>. It was, according to Bahá'ís, through his intercession that the idea was reverted and the family were allowed to be exiled together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-7"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acre">Acre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>At the age of 24, `Abdu'l-Bahá was clearly chief-steward to his father and an outstanding member of the Bahá’í community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200117_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200117-30">[29]</a></sup> Bahá’u’lláh and his family were – in 1868 – exiled to the penal colony of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acre,_Palestine" title="Acre, Palestine">Acre, Palestine</a> where it was expected that the family would perish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Arrival in Acre was distressing for the family and exiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2000_14.E2.80.9320-4">[3]</a></sup> They were greeted in a hostile manner by the surrounding population and his sister and father fell dangerously ill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> When told that the women were to sit on the shoulders of the men to reach the shore, `Abdu'l-Bahá took a chair and carried the women to the bay of Acre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-8"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá was able to procure some anesthetic and nursed the sick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-9"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> The Bahá’ís were imprisoned under horrendous conditions in a cluster of cells covered in excrement and dirt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá himself fell dangerously ill with <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> however a sympathetic soldier permitted a physician to help cure him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-10"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> The population shunned them, the soldiers treated them the same, and the behaviour of Siyyid Muhammad-i-Isfahani (an <a href="/wiki/Azali" title="Azali">Azali</a>) did not help matters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200122_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200122-36">[35]</a></sup> Morale was further destroyed with the accidental death of `Abdu'l-Bahá’s youngest brother <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADrz%C3%A1_Mihd%C3%AD" title="Mírzá Mihdí">Mírzá Mihdí</a> at the age of 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-11"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup> His death devastated the family – particularly his <a href="/wiki/%C3%81s%C3%ADyih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Ásíyih Khánum">mother</a> and father – and the grieving `Abdu'l-Bahá kept a night-long vigil beside his brother’s body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lotoae_24-12"><a href="#cite_note-lotoae-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_in_Acre">Later in Acre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later in Acre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over time, he gradually took over responsibility for the relationships between the small <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'i</a> exile community and the outside world. It was through his interaction with the people of Acre that, according to the Bahá'ís, they recognized the innocence of the Bahá'ís, and thus the conditions of imprisonment were eased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200133.E2.80.9343_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200133.E2.80.9343-37">[36]</a></sup> Four months after the death of Mihdí the family moved from the prison to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_%60Abb%C3%BAd" title="House of `Abbúd">House of `Abbúd</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200133_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200133-38">[37]</a></sup> The people of Acre started to respect the Bahá'ís and in particular, `Abdu'l-Bahá. `Abdu'l-Bahá was able to arrange for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_%60Abb%C3%BAd#Akk.C3.A1_area" title="House of `Abbúd">houses to be rented</a> for the family, the family later moved to the <a href="/wiki/Mansion_of_Bahj%C3%AD" title="Mansion of Bahjí">Mansion of Bahjí</a> around 1879 when an epidemic caused the inhabitants to flee.</p>
<p>`Abdu'l-Bahá soon became very popular in the penal colony and <a href="/wiki/Myron_Henry_Phelps" title="Myron Henry Phelps">Myron Henry Phelps</a> a wealthy New York lawyer described how "a crowd of human beings...Syrians, Arabs, Ethiopians, and many others",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> all waited to talk and receive `Abdu'l-Bahá.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> He undertook a history of the Bábí religion through publication of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Traveller%27s_Narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Traveller's Narrative (page does not exist)">A Traveller's Narrative</a> (Makála-i-Shakhsí Sayyáh) in 1886,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> later translated and published in translation in 1891 through Cambridge University by the agency of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Granville_Browne" title="Edward Granville Browne">Edward Granville Browne</a> who described `Abdu'l-Bahá as:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Seldom have I seen one whose appearance impressed me more. A tall strongly built man holding himself straight as an arrow, with white turban and raiment, long black locks reaching almost to the shoulder, broad powerful forehead indicating a strong intellect combined with an unswerving will, eyes keen as a hawk's, and strongly marked but pleasing features – such was my first impression of 'Abbás Efendí, "the master".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family_life">Marriage and family life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marriage and family life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abbaseffendi1868.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="227" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Abbaseffendi1868.jpg/190px-Abbaseffendi1868.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ce/Abbaseffendi1868.jpg 1.5x" width="190"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abbaseffendi1868.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
`Abdu'l-Bahá at age 24</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As a young man speculation was rife amongst the Bahá’ís to whom `Abdu'l-Bahá would marry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogenson.2C_p.87_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hogenson.2C_p.87-43">[42]</a></sup> Several young girls were seen as marriage prospects but `Abdu’l-Bahá seemed disinclined to marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> On 8 March 1873, at the urging of his father,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> the twenty-eight-year-old `Abdu’l-Bahá married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mun%C3%ADrih_Kh%C3%A1num" title="Munírih Khánum">Fátimih Nahrí of Isfahán</a> (1847–1938) a twenty-five-year-old from an upper-class family of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2000-p255_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2000-p255-45">[44]</a></sup> Her father was Mírzá Muḥammad `Alí Nahrí of <a href="/wiki/Isfahan" title="Isfahan">Isfahan</a> an eminent Bahá’í with prominent connections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogenson.2C_p.87_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hogenson.2C_p.87-43">[42]</a></sup> Fátimih was brought from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acre,_Palestine" title="Acre, Palestine">Acre, Palestine</a> after both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%E2%80%99u%E2%80%99ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá’u’lláh">Bahá’u’lláh</a> and his wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navv%C3%A1b" title="Navváb">Navváb</a> expressed an interest in her to marry `Abdu’l-Bahá.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2000-p255_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2000-p255-45">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup> After a wearisome journey from Isfahán to Akka she finally arrived accompanied by her brother in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup> The young couple were betrothed for about five months before the marriage itself commenced. In the meantime, Fátimih lived in the home of `Abdu'l-Bahá’s uncle <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADrz%C3%A1_M%C3%BAs%C3%A1" title="Mírzá Músá">Mírzá Músá</a>. According to her later memoirs, Fátimih fell in love with `Abdu'l-Bahá on seeing him. `Abdu'l-Bahá himself had showed little inkling to marriage until meeting Fátimih;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup> who was entitled Munírih by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%E2%80%99u%E2%80%99ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá’u’lláh">Bahá’u’lláh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Harvnb.7CKazemzadeh.7C2009-7">[6]</a></sup> Munírih is a title meaning "Luminous".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aittbf_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-aittbf-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The marriage resulted in nine children. The first born was a son Mihdí Effendi who died aged about 3. He was followed by Ḍiyá'iyyih <u>Kh</u>ánum, Fu’ádíyyih <u>Kh</u>ánum (d. few years old), Rúhangíz Khánum (d. 1893), Túbá Khánum, Husayn Effendi (d.1887 aged 5), Túbá <u>Kh</u>ánum, Rúhá <u>Kh</u>ánum and Munnavar <u>Kh</u>ánum. The death of his children caused `Abdu’l-Bahá immense grief – in particular the death of his son Husayn Effendi came at a difficult time following the death of his mother and uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup> The surviving children (all daughters) were; Ḍiyá'iyyih <u>Kh</u>ánum (mother of <a href="/wiki/Shoghi_Effendi" title="Shoghi Effendi">Shoghi Effendi</a>) (d. 1951) Túbá <u>Kh</u>ánum (1880–1959) Rúḥá <u>Kh</u>ánum and Munavvar <u>Kh</u>ánum (d. 1971).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bne_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-bne-6">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a> wished that the Bahá'ís follow the example of `Abdu'l-Bahá and gradually move away from polygamy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aittbf_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-aittbf-48">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[48]</a></sup> The marriage of `Abdu’l-Bahá to one woman and his choice to remain monogamous,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup> from advice of his father and his own wish,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-5"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aittbf_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-aittbf-48">[46]</a></sup> legitimised the practice of monogamy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aittbf_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-aittbf-48">[46]</a></sup> to a people whom hitherto had regarded polygamy as a righteous way of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltoae_47-6"><a href="#cite_note-ltoae-47">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aittbf_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-aittbf-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_of_his_ministry">Early years of his ministry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early years of his ministry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baha_(Lafayette).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="792" data-file-width="543" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Baha_%28Lafayette%29.jpg/150px-Baha_%28Lafayette%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Baha_%28Lafayette%29.jpg/225px-Baha_%28Lafayette%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Baha_%28Lafayette%29.jpg/300px-Baha_%28Lafayette%29.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baha_(Lafayette).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
`Abdu'l-Bahá</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After Bahá'u'lláh died on 29 May 1892, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tablets_of_Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h#Kit.C3.A1b-i-.60Ahd_.28Book_of_the_Covenant.29" title="Tablets of Bahá'u'lláh">Will and Testament</a> of Bahá'u'lláh named `Abdu'l-Bahá as Centre of the Covenant, successor and interpreter of Bahá'u'lláh's writings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup> In the Will and Testament `Abdu'l-Bahá's half-brother, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADrz%C3%A1_Muhammad_%60Al%C3%AD" title="Mírzá Muhammad `Alí">Muhammad `Alí</a>, was mentioned by name as being subordinate to `Abdu'l-Bahá. Muhammad `Alí became jealous of his half-brother and set out to establish authority for himself as an alternative leader with the support of his brothers Badi'u'llah and Diya'u'llah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qazvini_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Qazvini-5">[4]</a></sup> He began correspondence with Bahá'ís in Iran, initially in secret, casting doubts in others' minds about `Abdu'l-Bahá.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200153_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200153-51">[49]</a></sup> While most Bahá'ís followed `Abdu'l-Bahá, a handful followed Muhammad `Alí including such leaders as Mirza Javad and <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim_George_Kheiralla" title="Ibrahim George Kheiralla">Ibrahim George Kheiralla</a>, an early Bahá'í missionary to America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Muhammad `Alí and Mirza Javad began to openly accuse `Abdu'l-Bahá of taking on too much authority, suggesting that he believed himself to be a <a href="/wiki/Manifestation_of_God" title="Manifestation of God">Manifestation of God</a>, equal in status to Bahá'u'lláh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[51]</a></sup> It was at this time that `Abdu'l-Bahá, in order to provide proof of the falsity of the accusations leveled against him, in tablets to the West, stated that he was to be known as "`Abdu'l-Bahá" an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> phrase meaning the Servant of Bahá to make it clear that he was not a Manifestation of God, and that his station was only servitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200160_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200160-54">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[53]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá left a <a href="/wiki/Will_and_Testament_of_%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="Will and Testament of `Abdu'l-Bahá">Will and Testament</a> that set up the framework of administration. The two highest institutions were the Universal House of Justice, and the <a href="/wiki/Guardian_(Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith)" title="Guardian (Bahá'í Faith)">Guardianship</a>, for which he appointed <a href="/wiki/Shoghi_Effendi" title="Shoghi Effendi">Shoghi Effendi</a> as the Guardian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup> With the exception of `Abdu'l-Bahá and Shoghi Effendi, Muhammad `Alí was supported by all of the remaining <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h%27s_family" title="Bahá'u'lláh's family">male relatives of Bahá'u'lláh</a>, including Shoghi Effendi's father, Mírzá Hádí Shírází.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[54]</a></sup> However Muhammad `Alí's and his families statements had very little effect on the Bahá'ís in general - in the `Akká area, the followers of Muhammad `Alí represented six families at most, they had no common religious activities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WarburgStudies_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-WarburgStudies-57">[55]</a></sup> and were almost wholly assimilated into Muslim society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maceoin3_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-maceoin3-58">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Western_pilgrims">First Western pilgrims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: First Western pilgrims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</div>
<p>By the end of 1898, Western pilgrims started coming to Akka on pilgrimage to visit `Abdu'l-Bahá; this group of pilgrims, including <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_Hearst" title="Phoebe Hearst">Phoebe Hearst</a>, was the first time that Bahá'ís raised up in the West had met `Abdu'l-Bahá.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200169_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200169-59">[57]</a></sup> The first group arrived in 1898 and throughout late 1898 to early 1899 Western Bahá’ís sporadically visited `Abdu'l-Bahá. The group was relatively young containing mainly women from high American society in their 20s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[58]</a></sup> The group of Westerners aroused suspicion for the authorities, and consequently `Abdu'l-Bahá’s confinement was tightened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[59]</a></sup> During the next decade `Abdu'l-Bahá would be in constant communication with Bahá'ís around the world, helping them to teach the religion; the group included <a href="/wiki/May_Maxwell" title="May Maxwell">May Ellis Bolles</a> in Paris, Englishman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Breakwell" title="Thomas Breakwell">Thomas Breakwell</a>, American Herbert Hopper, French <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hippolyte_Dreyfus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hippolyte Dreyfus (page does not exist)">Hippolyte Dreyfus</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippolyte_Dreyfus-Barney" title="fr:Hippolyte Dreyfus-Barney">fr</a>)</span>, Susan Moody, Lua Getsinger, and American Laura Clifford Barney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200172.E2.80.9396_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200172.E2.80.9396-62">[60]</a></sup> It was Laura Clifford Barney who, by asking questions of `Abdu'l-Bahá over many years and many visits to Haifa, compiled what later became the book <a href="/wiki/Some_Answered_Questions" title="Some Answered Questions">Some Answered Questions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200182_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200182-63">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ministry.2C_1901.E2.80.931912">Ministry, 1901–1912</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ministry, 1901–1912">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the final years of the 19th century, while `Abdu'l-Bahá was still officially a prisoner and confined to `Akka, he organized the transfer of the remains of the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b" title="Báb">Báb</a> from Iran to Palestine. He then organized the purchase of land on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a> that Bahá'u'lláh had instructed should be used to lay the remains of the Báb, and organized for the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Shrine of the Báb">Shrine of the Báb</a>. This process took another 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200190.E2.80.9393_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200190.E2.80.9393-64">[62]</a></sup> With the increase of pilgrims visiting `Abdu'l-Bahá, Muhammad `Alí worked with the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman authorities</a> to re-introduce stricter terms on `Abdu'l-Bahá's imprisonment in August 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200194.E2.80.9395_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200194.E2.80.9395-65">[63]</a></sup> By 1902, however, due to the Governor of `Akka being supportive of `Abdu'l-Bahá, the situation was greatly eased; while pilgrims were able to once again visit `Abdu'l-Bahá, he was confined to the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200194.E2.80.9395_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi200194.E2.80.9395-65">[63]</a></sup> In February 1903, two followers of Muhammad `Alí, including Badi'u'llah and Siyyid `Aliy-i-Afnan, broke with Muhammad `Ali and wrote books and letters giving details of Muhammad `Ali's plots and noting that what was circulating about `Abdu'l-Bahá was fabrication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001102_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001102-66">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1902 to 1904, in addition to the building of the <a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Shrine of the Báb">Shrine of the Báb</a> that `Abdu'l-Bahá was directing, he started to put into execution two different projects; the restoration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="House of the Báb">House of the Báb</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiraz,_Iran" title="Shiraz, Iran">Shiraz, Iran</a> and the construction of the first <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_House_of_Worship" title="Bahá'í House of Worship">Bahá'í House of Worship</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ashgabat" title="Ashgabat">Ashgabat</a>, Turkmenistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001107_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001107-68">[66]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá asked Aqa Mirza Aqa to coordinate the work so that the house of the Báb would be restored to the state that it was at the time of the Báb's declaration to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mulla_Husayn" title="Mulla Husayn">Mulla Husayn</a> in 1844;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001107_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001107-68">[66]</a></sup> he also entrusted the work on the House of Worship to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vakil-u%27d-Dawlih" title="Vakil-u'd-Dawlih">Vakil-u'd-Dawlih</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001109_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001109-69">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this period, `Abdu'l-Bahá communicated with a number <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_Turk" title="Young Turk">Young Turks</a>, opposed to the reign of <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Sultan Abdul Hamid II</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Nam%C4%B1k_Kemal" title="Namık Kemal">Namık Kemal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ziya_Pasha" title="Ziya Pasha">Ziya Pasha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midhat_Pasha" title="Midhat Pasha">Midhat Pasha</a>, in an attempt to disseminate Bahá'í thought into their political ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[68]</a></sup> He emphasized Bahá'ís "seek freedom and love liberty, hope for equality, are well-wishers of humanity and ready to sacrifice their lives to unite humanity" but on a more broad approach than the Young Turks. <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_Cevdet" title="Abdullah Cevdet">Abdullah Cevdet</a>, one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Union_and_Progress" title="Committee of Union and Progress">Committee of Union and Progress</a> who considered the Bahá'í Faith an intermediary step between Islam and the ultimate abandonment of religious belief, would go on trial for defense of Bahá'ís in a periodical he founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>‛Abdu'l-Bahá also had contact with military leaders as well, including such individuals as <a href="/wiki/Bursal%C4%B1_Mehmet_Tahir_Bey" title="Bursalı Mehmet Tahir Bey">Bursalı Mehmet Tahir Bey</a> and Hasan Bedreddin. The latter, who was involved in the overthrow of <a href="/wiki/Abd%C3%BClaziz_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Abdülaziz of the Ottoman Empire">Sultan Abdülaziz</a>, is commonly known as Bedri Paşa or Bedri Pasha and is referred to in Persian Bahá'í sources as Bedri Bey (Badri Beg). He was a Bahá'í who translated ‛Abdu’l-Baha's works into French.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>`Abdu'l-Bahá also met <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Abduh" title="Muhammad Abduh">Muhammad Abduh</a>, one of the key figures of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Modernism" title="Islamic Modernism">Islamic Modernism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Salafi_movement" title="Salafi movement">Salafi movement</a>, in Beirut, at a time when the two men were both opposed to the Ottoman <i><a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">ulama</a></i> and shared similar goals of religious reform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rashid_Rida" title="Rashid Rida">Rashid Rida</a> asserts that during his visits to Beirut, `Abdu'l-Bahá would attend Abduh's study sessions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup> Regarding the meetings of `Abdu'l-Bahá and Muhammad 'Abduh, Shoghi Effendi asserts that "His several interviews with the well-known Shaykh Muhammad ‘Abdu served to enhance immensely the growing prestige of the community and spread abroad the fame of its most distinguished member."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to `Abdu'l-Bahá's political activities and alleged accusation against him by Muhammad `Ali, a Commission of Inquiry interviewed `Abdu'l-Bahá in 1905, with the result that he was almost exiled to <a href="/wiki/Fezzan" title="Fezzan">Fezzan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001111.E2.80.93113_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001111.E2.80.93113-79">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[78]</a></sup> In response, `Abdu'l-Bahá wrote the sultan a letter protesting that his followers refrain from involvement in partisan politics and that his <i><a href="/wiki/Tariqa" title="Tariqa">tariqa</a></i> had guided many Americans to Islam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup> The next few years in `Akka were relatively free of pressures and pilgrims were able to come and visit `Abdu'l-Bahá. By 1909 the mausoleum of the Shrine of the Báb was completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001109_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001109-69">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journeys_to_the_West">Journeys to the West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Journeys to the West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abdulbaha2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="323" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Abdulbaha2.jpg/220px-Abdulbaha2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/82/Abdulbaha2.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abdulbaha2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
`Abdu'l-Bahá, during his trip to the United States</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1%27s_journeys_to_the_West" title="`Abdu'l-Bahá's journeys to the West">`Abdu'l-Bahá's journeys to the West</a></div>
<p>The 1908 <a href="/wiki/Young_Turks" title="Young Turks">Young Turks</a> revolution freed all political prisoners in the Ottoman Empire, and `Abdu'l-Bahá was freed from imprisonment. His first action after his freedom was to visit the <a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Shrine of Bahá'u'lláh">Shrine of Bahá'u'lláh</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahji" title="Bahji">Bahji</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001131_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001131-82">[80]</a></sup> While `Abdu'l-Bahá continued to live in `Akka immediately following the revolution, he soon moved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="House of `Abdu'l-Bahá">to live</a> in <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a> near the Shrine of the Báb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001131_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001131-82">[80]</a></sup> In 1910, with the freedom to leave the country, he embarked on a three-year journey to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, Europe, and North America, spreading the Bahá'í message.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>From August to December 1911, `Abdu'l-Bahá visited cities in Europe, including London, Bristol, and Paris. The purpose of these trips was to support the Bahá'í communities in the west and to further spread his father's teachings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001159.E2.80.93397_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001159.E2.80.93397-83">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the following year, he undertook a much more extensive journey to the United States and Canada to once again spread his father's teachings. He arrived in New York City on 11 April 1912, after declining an offer of passage on the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS Titanic</a>, telling the Bahá'í believers, instead, to "Donate this to charity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lacroix_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lacroix-84">[82]</a></sup> He instead travelled on a slower craft, the S.S. Cedric, and cited preference of a longer sea journey as the reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001171_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001171-85">[83]</a></sup> After hearing of the Titanic's sinking on 16 April he was quoted as saying "I was asked to sail upon the Titanic, but my heart did not prompt me to do so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lacroix_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lacroix-84">[82]</a></sup> While he spent most of his time in New York, he visited Chicago, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh</a>, Washington, D.C., Boston and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia</a>. In August of the same year he started a more extensive journey to places including <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Acre" title="Green Acre">Green Acre</a> school in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> (his only visit to Canada). He then travelled west to <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, San Francisco, <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a>, and Los Angeles before starting to return east at the end of October. On 5 December 1912 he set sail back to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001159-397_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001159-397-86">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his visit to North America he visited many missions, churches, and groups, as well as having scores of meetings in Bahá'ís' homes, and offering innumerable personal meetings with hundreds of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallagher196_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gallagher196-87">[85]</a></sup> During his talks he proclaimed Bahá'í principles such as the <a href="/wiki/God_in_the_Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="God in the Bahá'í Faith">unity of God</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_and_the_unity_of_religion" title="Bahá'í Faith and the unity of religion">unity of the religions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_and_the_unity_of_humanity" title="Bahá'í Faith and the unity of humanity">oneness of humanity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_and_gender_equality" title="Bahá'í Faith and gender equality">equality of women and men</a>, world peace and economic justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallagher196_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gallagher196-87">[85]</a></sup> He also insisted that all his meetings be open to all races.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallagher196_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gallagher196-87">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>His visit and talks were the subject of hundreds of newspaper articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallagher196_87-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gallagher196-87">[85]</a></sup> In Boston newspaper reporters asked `Abdu'l-Bahá why he had come to America, and he stated that he had come to participate in conferences on peace and that just giving warning messages is not enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001232_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001232-88">[86]</a></sup> `Abdu'l-Bahá's visit to Montreal provided notable newspaper coverage; on the night of his arrival the editor of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montreal_Daily_Star" title="Montreal Daily Star">Montreal Daily Star</a></i> met with him and that newspaper along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Montreal_Gazette" title="The Montreal Gazette">The Montreal Gazette</a>, <i>Montreal Standard</i>, <a href="/wiki/Le_Devoir" title="Le Devoir">Le Devoir</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Presse_(Canada)" title="La Presse (Canada)">La Presse</a> among others reported on `Abdu'l-Bahá's activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanderHoonaard56_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanderHoonaard56-89">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001256_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001256-90">[88]</a></sup> The headlines in those papers included "Persian Teacher to Preach Peace", "Racialism Wrong, Says Eastern Sage, Strife and War Caused by Religious and National Prejudices", and "Apostle of Peace Meets Socialists, Abdul Baha's Novel Scheme for Distribution of Surplus Wealth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001256_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001256-90">[88]</a></sup> The <i>Montreal Standard</i>, which was distributed across Canada, took so much interest that it republished the articles a week later; the Gazette published six articles and Montreal's largest French language newspaper published two articles about him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanderHoonaard56_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-vanderHoonaard56-89">[87]</a></sup> His 1912 visit to Montreal also inspired humourist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Leacock" title="Stephen Leacock">Stephen Leacock</a> to parody him in his bestselling 1914 book <i><a href="/wiki/Arcadian_Adventures_with_the_Idle_Rich" title="Arcadian Adventures with the Idle Rich">Arcadian Adventures with the Idle Rich</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[89]</a></sup> In Chicago one newspaper headline included "His Holiness Visits Us, Not Pius X but A. Baha,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001256_90-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001256-90">[88]</a></sup> and `Abdu'l-Bahá's visit to California was reported in the <i>Palo Altan</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001313_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001313-92">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>Back in Europe, he visited London, Paris (where he stayed for two months), <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. Finally, on June 12, 1913, he returned to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, where he stayed for six months before returning to <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001159-397_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001159-397-86">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 23, 1914, at the eve of World War I, `Abdu'l-Bahá hosted Baron <a href="/wiki/Edmond_James_de_Rothschild" title="Edmond James de Rothschild">Edmond James de Rothschild</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_family" title="Rothschild family">Rothschild banking family</a> who was a leading advocate and financier of the Zionist movement, during one of his early trips to Palestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="241" data-file-width="450" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg/275px-AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg/413px-AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AAbdul-baha_late_life.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
`Abdu'l-Bahá on Mount Carmel with pilgrims in 1919</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During World War I `Abdu'l-Bahá stayed in Palestine, under the continued threat of Allied bombardment and threats from the Turkish commander. As the war ended, the British Mandate over Palestine brought relative security to `Abdu'l-Bahá. During his final year, a growing number of visitors and pilgrims came to see him in <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001400.E2.80.93431_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalyuzi2001400.E2.80.93431-94">[92]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Harry_Luke" title="Harry Luke">Harry Charles Luke</a>, an official in the British <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Office" title="Colonial Office">Colonial Office</a> who served as assistant Governor of Jerusalem, `Abdu'l-Bahá "was created by King <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">George V</a> a K.B.E." on December 4, 1919, being conferred his knighthood "for valuable services rendered to the British Government in the early days of the Occupation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[93]</a></sup> This has been judged to be humanitarian service to people following the privations of war and the chaos in its immediate aftermath by averting a famine in Northern Palestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[94]</a></sup> He was ceremonially knighted on April 27, 1920, an event which was prominently reported in the <i><a href="/wiki/Star_of_the_West_(Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_magazine)" title="Star of the West (Bahá'í magazine)">Star of the West</a></i> as " a most wonderful celebration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequent to the British occupation of Palestine, during the arrival of Jews under the <a href="/wiki/Palestine_Jewish_Colonization_Association" title="Palestine Jewish Colonization Association">Palestine Jewish Colonization Association</a>, `Abdu'l-Bahá praised the Zionist movement, proclaiming that "There is too much talk today of what the Zionists are going to do here. There is no need of it. Let them come and do more and say less" and that "A Jewish government might come later."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>`Abdu'l-Bahá died on November 28, 1921 (27th of <a href="/wiki/Rabi%27_al-awwal" title="Rabi' al-awwal">Rabi'u'l-Avval</a>, 1340 AH.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Qazvini_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Qazvini-5">[4]</a></sup> On his funeral, Esslemont notes:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"... a funeral the like of which Haifa, nay Palestine itself, had surely never seen... so deep was the feeling that brought so many thousands of mourners together, representative of so many religions, races and tongues".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[97]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>He is buried in the front room of the <a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Shrine of the Báb">Shrine of the Báb</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a>. Plans are in place to one day build a <a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="Shrine of `Abdu'l-Bahá">Shrine of `Abdu'l-Bahá</a>. In his <a href="/wiki/Will_and_Testament_of_%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="Will and Testament of `Abdu'l-Bahá">Will and Testament</a> he appointed his grandson <a href="/wiki/Shoghi_Effendi" title="Shoghi Effendi">Shoghi Effendi Rabbani</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Guardian_(Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith)" title="Guardian (Bahá'í Faith)">Guardian</a> of the Bahá'í Faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The total estimated number of <a href="/wiki/Tablet_(religious)" title="Tablet (religious)">tablets</a> that `Abdu'l-Bahá wrote are over 27,000, of which only a fraction have been translated into English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[98]</a></sup> His works fall into two groups including first his direct writings and second his lectures and speeches as noted by others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup> The first group includes <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_Divine_Civilization" title="The Secret of Divine Civilization">The Secret of Divine Civilization</a></i> written before 1875, <i>A Traveller's Narrative</i> written around 1886, the Resāla-ye sīāsīya or <i>Sermon on the Art of Governance</i> written in 1893, the <i>Memorials of the Faithful</i>, and a large number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tablets_(religious)" title="Tablets (religious)">tablets</a> written to various people;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup> including various Western intellectuals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/August_Forel" title="August Forel">August Forel</a> which has been translated and published as the <a href="/wiki/Tablet_to_Dr._Forel" title="Tablet to Dr. Forel">Tablet to Auguste-Henri Forel</a>. The <i>Secret of Divine Civilization</i> and the <i>Sermon on the Art of Governance</i> were widely circulated anonymously.</p>
<p>The second group includes <a href="/wiki/Some_Answered_Questions" title="Some Answered Questions">Some Answered Questions</a>, which is an English translation of a series of table talks with Laura Barney, and <a href="/wiki/Paris_Talks" title="Paris Talks">Paris Talks</a>, <i>`Abdu'l-Baha in London</i> and <i>Promulgation of Universal Peace</i> which are respectively addresses given by `Abdu'l-Bahá in Paris, London and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iranica_abdulbaha_b_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_abdulbaha_b-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
