<p><b>Abdullah bin Abdul Kadir</b> (1796–1854)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>: <b>عبد الله بن عبد القادر</b> <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">'Abd Allāh bin 'Abd al-Qādir</span></i>) also known as <b>Munshi Abdullah</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malayan</a> writer of Indian origin. He was a famous <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a>-born Indian <a href="/wiki/Munshi" title="Munshi">munshi</a> of Singapore<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popular_science_1899_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-popular_science_1899-2">[2]</a></sup> and died in <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a>, a part of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.</p>
<p>Munshi Abdullah is regarded as the most cultured Malay who ever wrote,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popular_science_1899_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-popular_science_1899-2">[2]</a></sup> one of the greatest innovators in Malay letters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Indonesian_language:_its_history_and_role_in_modern_society_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Indonesian_language:_its_history_and_role_in_modern_society-3">[3]</a></sup> and the father of modern Malay literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_.26_Its_People_page_1218_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_.26_Its_People_page_1218-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>Munshi</i> means "teacher" or "educator". Munshi Abdullah was a great-grandson of a <a href="/wiki/Hadhrami_people" title="Hadhrami people">Hadhrami</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> trader,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and also had <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Muslim" title="Tamil Muslim">Tamil</a> and to a smaller extent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malay_(ethnic_group)" title="Malay (ethnic group)">Malay</a> ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Owing to his ethnic and religious background, the Malays would refer to him as a <i><a href="/wiki/Jawi_Peranakan" title="Jawi Peranakan">Jawi Peranakan</a></i> or <i>Jawi Pekan</i>.</p>
<p>Munshi Abdullah followed his father's career path as a translator and teacher of colonial officials in the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">Malay Archipelago</a>, mainly the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Empire" title="Dutch Empire">Dutch</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abdullah_Abdul_Kadir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Munshi Abdullah was born in Kampung Pali in Malacca, from parents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamil_people" title="Tamil people">Tamil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">[1]</a></sup> He was the youngest of five sons. All of his brothers died in infancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was sick most of the time and his mother took great care of him. As per the customary practices of the Malay community of that period, he was taken care of by various individuals as it was held that a child with poor immunity to diseases should be cared for by caretakers other than his or her biological parents. Munshi Abdullah was critical of the practice, describing it as stupid in the Hikayat Abdullah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He became a teacher or <b>munshi</b>, first by teaching <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">Indian</a> soldiers of the Malacca Garrison. He then taught the Malay language to British and American missionaries and businessmen. He became a functionary in the <a href="/wiki/Straits_Settlements" title="Straits Settlements">Straits Settlements</a> next. He became a scribe and copyist for Sir <a href="/wiki/Stamford_Raffles" title="Stamford Raffles">Stamford Raffles</a>, followed by, in 1815, becoming translator of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospels</a> and other text for the <a href="/wiki/London_Missionary_Society" title="London Missionary Society">London Missionary Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">[1]</a></sup> He also worked with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Board_of_Missions" title="American Board of Missions">American Board of Missions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abdullah_Abdul_Kadir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His writing career took off after a missionary, Alfred North, encouraged him to write an autobiography after reading an Abdullah's account of a voyage along the east coast of Malaya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">[1]</a></sup> His most important works are the <i><a href="/wiki/Hikayat_Abdullah" title="Hikayat Abdullah">Hikayat Abdullah</a></i> (an autobiography), <i><a href="/wiki/Kisah_Pelayaran_Abdullah_ke_Kelantan" title="Kisah Pelayaran Abdullah ke Kelantan">Kisah Pelayaran Abdullah ke Kelantan</a></i> (an account of his trip for the government to <a href="/wiki/Kelantan" title="Kelantan">Kelantan</a>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Kisah_Pelayaran_Abdullah_ke_Mekah" title="Kisah Pelayaran Abdullah ke Mekah">Kisah Pelayaran Abdullah ke Mekah</a></i> (a narrative of his <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">pilgrimage</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> 1854). His work was an inspiration to future generations of writers and marks an early stage in the transition from the classical Malay literature to modern Malay literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Indonesian_language:_its_history_and_role_in_modern_society_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Indonesian_language:_its_history_and_role_in_modern_society-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Hikayat_Abdullah" title="Hikayat Abdullah">Hikayat Abdullah</a></i> was the major literary work of Munshi Abdullah. It was completed in 1843<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-EB-1">[1]</a></sup> and first published in 1849,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref_1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref_1-9">[9]</a></sup> making it one of the first Malay literary texts to be published commercially. Abdullah's authorship was prominently displayed in this text and the contents were conveyed in simple, contemporary Malay. Unlike typical classical Malay literary works that contain fantasies and legendary stories, Abdullah's work was realistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The book remains a reliable and accurate reference on early Malay history to this day.</p>
<p>Abdullah was known as an ardent critic of the Malay political system of <i>Kerajaan</i> ("kingdom"). His work, <i><a href="/wiki/Kisah_Pelayaran_Abdullah_ke_Kelantan" title="Kisah Pelayaran Abdullah ke Kelantan">Kisah Pelayaran Abdullah ke Kelantan</a></i> contained his advice to Malay rulers and comparisons he made between the British system of governing and that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malay_rulers" title="Malay rulers">Malay rulers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abdullah argued that the system of <i>Kerajaan</i> was detrimental to the Malay individual, as it was an impediment to the social improvement of the Malays. The Malay <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> was deemed to be someone who was selfish, with no concern toward his subjects, to the extent they were treated like animals rather than humans.</p>
<p>The idea of modernity and striving for excellence within the Malay community stemmed from his ideas and stinging criticisms of the ancient Malay polity of the <i>Kerajaan</i>. Under the <i>Kerajaan</i>, the Malays were deprived of education and hence they were easily oppressed. Without education, they did not have the ability to question the injustice meted out to them and could not take the initiative to institute changes to improve their lives.</p>
<p>Although the condemnation may be exaggerated, Munshi Abdullah's allegations were not without basis. He is regarded by many to be the first Malayan journalist, taking Malay literature out of its preoccupation with folk-stories and legends into accurate historical descriptions.</p>
<p>Abdullah died in <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a> in October 1854 at the age of 58, before he reached Mecca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
