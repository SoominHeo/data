<p><b><a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> Abdul Rahman Mohammed Arif Aljumaily</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> عبد الرحمن محمد عارف الجميلي<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">`Abd al-Raḥmān `Ārif</span></i>; April 14, 1916 – August 24, 2007) was the third <a href="/wiki/President_of_Iraq" title="President of Iraq">President of Iraq</a>, a position he held from April 16, 1966 to July 17, 1968.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abdul_Rahman_Arif&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nasser,_Aref,_Atassi,_Boumeiddin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="940" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Nasser%2C_Aref%2C_Atassi%2C_Boumeiddin.jpg/220px-Nasser%2C_Aref%2C_Atassi%2C_Boumeiddin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Nasser%2C_Aref%2C_Atassi%2C_Boumeiddin.jpg/330px-Nasser%2C_Aref%2C_Atassi%2C_Boumeiddin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Nasser%2C_Aref%2C_Atassi%2C_Boumeiddin.jpg/440px-Nasser%2C_Aref%2C_Atassi%2C_Boumeiddin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nasser,_Aref,_Atassi,_Boumeiddin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
From left to right, <a href="/wiki/Houari_Boumediene" title="Houari Boumediene">Houari Boumediene</a> of Algeria, <a href="/wiki/Nureddin_al-Atassi" title="Nureddin al-Atassi">Nureddin al-Atassi</a> of Syria, Abdul Rahman Aref of Iraq, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamel_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamel Abdel Nasser">Gamel Abdel Nasser</a> of Egypt, and <a href="/wiki/Ismail_al-Azhari" title="Ismail al-Azhari">Ismail al-Azhari</a> of Sudan in 1968</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He was a career soldier, and supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/14_July_Revolution" title="14 July Revolution">military coup</a> in 1958 that overthrew the monarchy. He also supported the coup that brought his brother, <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Salam_Arif" title="Abdul Salam Arif">Abdul Salam Arif</a>, to power in 1963. His brother appointed him head of the army following the coup, and when the younger Arif died in an aircraft crash in April 1966, Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdul_Rahman_al-Bazzaz" title="Abdul Rahman al-Bazzaz">Abdul Rahman al-Bazzaz</a> ("a Western-oriented lawyer" and the first civilian to head an Iraqi government since the 1958 revolution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) became acting president; three days later, al-Bazzaz was chosen to become president, then al-Bazzaz immediately relinquished the presidency to Abdul Rahman Arif. It is speculated that the transfer of power possibly occurred because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraqi_military" title="Iraqi military">Iraqi military</a> thought that Abdul Salam should be succeeded by his weak and easier to manipulate brother instead. Arif was appointed president by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraqi_Revolutionary_Command_Council" title="Iraqi Revolutionary Command Council">Iraqi Revolutionary Command Council</a>. He continued his brother's policies, but with a more nationalistic profile.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Like his brother Abdul Salam, he was an overt supporter of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan-Arabist" title="Pan-Arabist">pan-Arabist</a> president <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Gamal Abdel Nasser</a>. Arif Abdul Razzak, another pro-Nasser Air Force Commander, attempted a coup d'état on Arif, and lightly bombed the Presidential Palace with Soviet MiG 17 jets, but the coup failed and he was arrested. It was his second failed coup attempt on the Arif government. President Arif went on TV to declare that on this occasion, Abdul Razzak would definitely be punished, only to then release him with a pardon.</p>
<p>His presidency was widely believed to be slack and indecisive. However, there are historical clues that he was not corrupt. The ill-advised law that he passed in 1967 to absolve himself from paying income tax is probably an indication that he was not capable of getting wealth otherwise. Shortly after Arif came to power, the Iraqi military launched a major offensive against Kurdish rebels led by <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Barzani" title="Mustafa Barzani">Mustafa Barzani</a> in the <a href="/wiki/First_Iraqi%E2%80%93Kurdish_War" title="First Iraqi–Kurdish War">First Iraqi–Kurdish War</a>, pitting 40,000 Iraqi troops against Barzani's 3,500-strong <a href="/wiki/Peshmerga" title="Peshmerga">Peshmerga</a>. Iranian and Israeli support contributed to a decisive Kurdish victory at the Battle of Mount Handren on May 11, where a battle plan devised by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Force" title="Israel Defense Force">IDF</a> military officer Zuri Sagy resulted in the killing of 1,400-2,000 Iraqi troops and the capture of hundreds more. Regime hardliners were discredited by the debacle, and on June 29, Bazzaz announced a Twelve Point Plan for peace, which included "administrative decentralization" in Iraqi Kurdistan and "Kurdish representation in Parliament". Throughout July, Bazzaz began implementing the agreement by "approving a massive rehabilitation program, lifting the economic blockade, releasing hundreds of Kurdish prisoners, removing Arab tribes from former Kurdish lands, and passing a general amnesty law", but opposition within the military forced Arif to dismiss Bazzaz in favor of General <a href="/wiki/Naji_Talib" title="Naji Talib">Naji Talib</a> on August 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Regardless, American officials in the <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> administration welcomed Iraq's brief return to civilian rule under Bazzaz as well as Arif's thwarting of Razzak's second coup attempt on June 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Arif was considered "one of the few forces of moderation" in Iraq, having previously established a friendship with U.S. ambassador Robert Strong and making a number of friendly gestures to the United States between April 1966 and January 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At Arif's request, President Johnson met five Iraqi generals and Iraqi ambassador Nasir Hani in Washington, D.C. on January 25, 1967, reiterating his "desire to build an ever closer relationship between [the] two governments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to Johnson's <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Advisor_(United_States)" title="National Security Advisor (United States)">National Security Adviser</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Rostow" title="Walt Rostow">Walt Rostow</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._National_Security_Council" title="U.S. National Security Council">U.S. National Security Council</a> even contemplated welcoming Arif on a state visit to Washington, although this proposal was ultimately rejected due to concerns about the stability of Arif's government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Prior to the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">Six-Day War</a>, Iraqi foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Adnan_Pachachi" title="Adnan Pachachi">Adnan Pachachi</a> met with a number of U.S. officials to discuss the escalating Middle East crisis on June 1, including U.S. ambassador to the UN <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Goldberg" title="Arthur Goldberg">Arthur Goldberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Under_Secretary_of_State_for_Political_Affairs" title="Under Secretary of State for Political Affairs">Under Secretary of State for Political Affairs</a> <a href="/wiki/Eugene_V._Rostow" title="Eugene V. Rostow">Eugene V. Rostow</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Dean_Rusk" title="Dean Rusk">Dean Rusk</a>, and President Johnson himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The political atmosphere engendered by the costly Arab defeat prompted Iraq to break relations with the U.S. on June 7, and ultimately ensured the collapse of Arif's relatively moderate government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 17, 1968, while Arif was sleeping, his own assistants along with members of the <a href="/wiki/Ba%27ath_Party" title="Ba'ath Party">Ba'ath Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Hassan_al-Bakr" title="Ahmed Hassan al-Bakr">Ahmed Hassan al-Bakr</a>, overthrew him in a bloodless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coup" title="Coup">coup</a>. As Arif and his brother had done in the 1963 coup against Qasim, the coalition declared victory once they had captured the radio station and the ministry of defense. It was accomplished when the defense minister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardan_Al-Tikriti" title="Hardan Al-Tikriti">Hardan Al-Tikriti</a>, phoned Arif informing him that he was no longer president. Arif was exiled to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</p>
<p>He returned to Iraq in 1979, when <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a> came to power, and largely stayed out of the public and political spotlight afterwards. He was allowed to leave the country once to undertake the <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a>. Arif left Iraq permanently after Hussein was removed from power by the U.S.-led invasion, and lived in <a href="/wiki/Amman" title="Amman">Amman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> from 2004. He died in Amman on August 24, 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> being yet the only Iraqi leader to die a normal death. He was married to Faika Abdul-Mageed Faris Alanee.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abdul_Rahman_Arif&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>"The existence of Israel is an error which we must put right. This is our opportunity to wipe out the disgrace which is Israel which has been with us since 1948. Our goal is clear – to wipe Israel off the map", speaking on the Radio, 1 June 1967<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>"I hope there will be stability and security in all parts of Iraq and neighboring Arab countries", he said. "I hope there will be national unity in Iraq by forgetting the past and looking for the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
