<p>In <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Signal_processing" title="Signal processing">signal processing</a> <b>companding</b> (occasionally called <b>compansion</b>) is a method of mitigating the detrimental effects of a channel with limited <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a>. The name is a combination of the words <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range_compression" title="Dynamic range compression"><b>com</b>pressing</a> and ex<b>panding</b>. The use of companding allows signals with a large dynamic range to be transmitted over facilities that have a smaller dynamic range capability. Companding is employed in <a href="/wiki/Telephony" title="Telephony">telephony</a> and other audio applications such as professional <a href="/wiki/Wireless_microphone" title="Wireless microphone">wireless microphones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Analog_recording" title="Analog recording">analog recording</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_it_works">How it works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Companding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: How it works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range_compression" title="Dynamic range compression">dynamic range compression</a> used in audio recording and the like depends on a <a href="/wiki/Variable-gain_amplifier" title="Variable-gain amplifier">variable-gain amplifier</a>, and so is a locally linear process (linear for short regions, but not globally), companding is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a> and takes place in the same way at all points in time. The dynamic range of a signal is compressed before <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">transmission</a> and is expanded to the original value at the receiver.</p>
<p>The electronic circuit that does this is called a <b>compander</b> and works by compressing or expanding the <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a> of an analog electronic signal such as sound recorded by a microphone. One variety is a triplet of amplifiers: a logarithmic amplifier, followed by a variable-gain linear amplifier and an exponential amplifier. Such a triplet has the property that its output voltage is proportional to the input voltage raised to an adjustable power.</p>
<p>Companded quantization is the combination of three functional building blocks – namely, a (continuous-domain) signal <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range_compression" title="Dynamic range compression">dynamic range <i>compressor</i></a>, a limited-range uniform quantizer, and a (continuous-domain) signal dynamic range <i>expander</i> that inverts the compressor function. This type of quantization is frequently used in telephony systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrayNeuhoff_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrayNeuhoff-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In practice, companders are designed to operate according to relatively simple dynamic range compressor functions that are designed to be suitable for implementation using simple analog electronic circuits. The two most popular compander functions used for telecommunications are the <a href="/wiki/A-law_algorithm" title="A-law algorithm">A-law</a> and <a href="/wiki/%CE%9C-law_algorithm" title="Μ-law algorithm">μ-law</a> functions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Companding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Companding is used in digital telephony systems, compressing before input to an <a href="/wiki/Analog-to-digital_converter" title="Analog-to-digital converter">analog-to-digital converter</a>, and then expanding after a <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">digital-to-analog converter</a>. This is equivalent to using a non-linear ADC as in a <a href="/wiki/T-carrier" title="T-carrier">T-carrier</a> telephone system that implements <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-law" title="A-law">A-law</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9C-law" title="Μ-law">μ-law</a> companding. This method is also used in digital file formats for better <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">signal-to-noise ratio</a> (SNR) at lower bit rates. For example, a linearly encoded 16-bit <a href="/wiki/Pulse-code_modulation" title="Pulse-code modulation">PCM</a> signal can be converted to an 8-bit <a href="/wiki/WAV" title="WAV">WAV</a> or <a href="/wiki/Au_file_format" title="Au file format">AU</a> file while maintaining a decent SNR by compressing before the transition to 8-bit and expanding after a conversion back to 16-bit. This is effectively a form of lossy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_data_compression" title="Audio data compression">audio data compression</a>.</p>
<p>Professional <a href="/wiki/Wireless_microphone" title="Wireless microphone">wireless microphones</a> do this since the dynamic range of the microphone audio signal itself is larger than the dynamic range provided by radio transmission. Companding also reduces the noise and crosstalk levels at the receiver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Companders are used in concert audio systems and in some noise reduction schemes such as <a href="/wiki/Dbx_(noise_reduction)" title="Dbx (noise reduction)">dbx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolby_noise-reduction_system" title="Dolby noise-reduction system">Dolby NR (all versions)</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Companding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of companding in an analog picture transmission system was patented by A. B. Clark of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Telephone_%26_Telegraph" title="American Telephone &amp; Telegraph">AT&amp;T</a> in 1928 (filed in 1925):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the transmission of pictures by electric currents, the method which consists in sending currents varied in a non-linear relation to the light values of the successive elements of the picture to be transmitted, and at the receiving end exposing corresponding elements of a sensitive surface to light varied in inverse non-linear relation to the received current.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— A. B. Clark patent</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1942, Clark and his team completed the <a href="/wiki/SIGSALY" title="SIGSALY">SIGSALY</a> secure voice transmission system that included the first use of companding in a <a href="/wiki/Pulse-code_modulation" title="Pulse-code modulation">PCM</a> (digital) system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1953, B. Smith showed that a nonlinear <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">DAC</a> could be complemented by the inverse nonlinearity in a successive-approximation <a href="/wiki/Analog-to-digital_converter" title="Analog-to-digital converter">ADC</a> configuration, simplifying the design of digital companding systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1970, H. Kaneko developed the uniform description of segment (piecewise linear) companding laws that had by then been adopted in digital telephony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s (and '90s), many of the music equipment manufacturers (Roland, Yamaha, Korg) used companding when compressing the library waveform data in their digital synthesizers. This dates back to the late '80s when memory chips were often one of the most costly components in the instrument. Manufacturers usually quoted the amount of memory in its compressed form: i.e. 24MB of physical waveform ROM in a Korg Trinity is actually 48MB when uncompressed. Similarly, Roland SR-JV expansion boards were usually advertised as 8MB boards with '16MB-equivalent content'. Careless copying of this technical information, omitting the "equivalence" reference, can often cause confusion.</p>
