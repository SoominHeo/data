<p><i><b>Ang mo</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B4%85%E6%AF%9B" title="wikt:紅毛">紅毛</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">hóng máo</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Pe%CC%8Dh-%C5%8De-j%C4%AB" title="Pe̍h-ōe-jī">Pe̍h-ōe-jī</a>: <i><span lang="nan" xml:lang="nan">âng-mô͘ / âng-mn̂g</span></i>) or <i><b>ang moh</b></i> is a racial <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> describing <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">white people</a>, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, and sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. It literally means "red-haired" and originates from <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a>, a variety of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Min" title="Southern Min">Southern Min</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The usage is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> term <i><a href="/wiki/Gweilo" title="Gweilo">gweilo</a></i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">鬼佬</span>; "ghost man").</p>
<p>Other similar terms include <i><b>ang mo kow</b></i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">紅毛猴</span>; "red-haired monkeys"), <i><b>ang mo kui</b></i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">紅毛鬼</span>; "red-haired devil"), <i><b>ang mo lang</b></i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">紅毛人</span>; "red-haired people"). Although the term had some derogatory connotations, it has entered common usage in Singapore and Malaysia and refers to a white person or, when used as an adjective, Western culture in general.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_history">Etymology and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ang_mo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest origin for the term <i>ang mo</i> could be traced to the contact between <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a> (Min Nan) speakers in Southern <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> province of China with the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch people</a> during the 16th and 17th century.</p>
<p>During the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch people</a> failed in their attempt to force their way into <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> to trade in the 1620s during the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Dutch_conflicts" title="Sino-Dutch conflicts">Sino-Dutch conflicts</a> and were called <i>ang mo</i> by the locals. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch people</a> had colonized <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and the Spanish built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Santo_Domingo" title="Fort Santo Domingo">Fort Santo Domingo</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamsui" title="Tamsui">Tamsui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. The Dutch later drove the Spanish out and seized the Fort which also became known as "City of the Red-Haired" (Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">紅毛城</span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">hóng máo chéng</span></i>; Pe̍h-ōe-jī: <i><span lang="nan" xml:lang="nan">Âng-mn̂g-siâⁿ</span></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">Taiwanese Hokkien</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch people</a> were known in Taiwan as <i>ang mo lang</i> ("red-haired people") in <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">Taiwanese Hokkien</a>. This is most likely because red hair is a common trait among the Dutch. This historical term <i>ang mo lang</i> continues to be used in the context of Taiwanese history to refer to Dutch people.</p>
<p>The Chinese characters for <i>ang mo</i> are the same as those in the historical Japanese term <i>kōmō</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">紅毛</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which was used during the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (1603–1868) as an epithet for (northwestern European) white people. It primarily referred to <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> traders who were the only Europeans allowed to trade with Japan during <a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">its 200-year period of isolation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Portuguese and Spanish traders were in contrast referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Nanban_trade" title="Nanban trade">nanban</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">南蛮</span>)</span>, which is in turn cognate to the Chinese <i><a href="/wiki/Nanman" title="Nanman">nanman</a></i> and means "southern barbarians".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Henry_Medhurst" title="Walter Henry Medhurst">Walter Henry Medhurst</a> made a reference in his academic work <i>A Dictionary of the Hok-Këèn Dialect of the Chinese Language</i> that <i>âng mô</i> ("red haired"), generally applied to the <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English people</a>. With the large migration of <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a> people to South-East Asia, predominantly <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, the term <i>ang moh</i> became more widespread and was used to refer to white people in general.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_controversy">Racial controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ang_mo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Racial controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>ang mo</i> is sometimes viewed as racist and derogatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Others, however, maintain it is acceptable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> making it in some contexts a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reclaimed_word" title="Reclaimed word">reclaimed word</a>. Despite this ambiguity, it is a widely used term. It appears, for instance, in Singaporean newspapers such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Straits_Times" title="The Straits Times">The Straits Times</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and in television programs and films. The term was used in the film <i><a href="/wiki/I_Not_Stupid" title="I Not Stupid">I Not Stupid</a></i>, in which when several employees in the marketing department of their company resented a particular white individual because they perceived that preference had been shown to him because of his race.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derogatory_context">Derogatory context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ang_mo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Derogatory context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the term <i>ang mo sai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">紅毛屎</span>; literally: "red-haired shit") is a derogatory term used within the Chinese community for mocking other Chinese who are not able to read Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
