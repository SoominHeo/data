<p>The <b>Battle of Angaur</b> was a battle of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific campaign</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, fought on the island of <a href="/wiki/Angaur" title="Angaur">Angaur</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palau_Islands" title="Palau Islands">Palau Islands</a> from 17 September—22 October 1944. This battle was part of a larger offensive campaign known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Forager" title="Operation Forager">Operation Forager</a> which ran from June 1944 to November 1944 in the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific Theater of Operations</a>, and Operation Stalemate II in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Angaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Angaur is a tiny limestone island, just 3 mi (4.8 km) long, separated from <a href="/wiki/Peleliu" title="Peleliu">Peleliu</a> by a 7 mi (11 km) strait, from which <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> was mined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> In mid-1944, the Japanese had 1,400 troops on the island, under the overall command of Palau Sector Group commander Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Sadae_Inoue" title="Sadae Inoue">Sadae Inoue</a> and under the direct command of Major Goto who was stationed on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:39</sup></p>
<p>The weak defenses of the Palaus and the potential for airfield construction made them attractive targets for the Americans after the capture of the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>, but a shortage of landing craft meant that operations against the Palaus could not begin until the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a> were secure.</p>
<p>Once the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Peleliu" title="Battle of Peleliu">assault on Peleliu</a> was "well in hand", the 322nd <a href="/wiki/Regimental_combat_team" title="Regimental combat team">RCT</a> would land on the northern Beach Red, and the 321st RCT on the eastern Beach Blue, both of the <a href="/wiki/81st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="81st Infantry Division (United States)">81st Infantry Division</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:21</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Angaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bombardment of Angaur by the <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Tennessee_(BB-43)" title="USS Tennessee (BB-43)"><i>Tennessee</i></a>, four cruisers, and forty <a href="/wiki/Douglas_SBD_Dauntless" title="Douglas SBD Dauntless">Douglas SBD Dauntless</a> <a href="/wiki/Dive_bomber" title="Dive bomber">dive bombers</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Wasp_(CV-18)" title="USS Wasp (CV-18)"><i>Wasp</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:68</sup> began on 11 September 1944. Six days later on 17 September, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/81st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="81st Infantry Division (United States)">81st Infantry Division</a>—commanded by Major General <a href="/wiki/Paul_J._Mueller" title="Paul J. Mueller">Paul J. Mueller</a>—landed on the northeast and southeast coasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:67–68</sup> Both RCTs were counterattacked during the night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup> Both RCTs linked up the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup> By the end of the third day, 19 Sept., the main area of Japanese resistance was to the northeast around Romauldo Hill, so the 323rd RCT was sent to <a href="/wiki/Ulithi" title="Ulithi">Ulithi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70</sup></p>
<p>Resistance stiffened as the Americans advanced on "the Bowl", a hill near Lake Salome in the northwest of the island where the Japanese planned to make their last stand, after the rest of Angaur and Saipan town were taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:61</sup> There was another small position where the Japanese had about 400 soldiers in a defense at the southeast corner of the island, around Beach Green, that was neutralized on September 20<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70</sup> after 2 days of harsh fighting and about 300 U.S. casualties.</p>
<p>From 20 September, the 322nd Infantry Regiment repeatedly attacked the Bowl, but the 750 defenders repulsed them with artillery, mortars, grenades and machine guns. Gradually, hunger, thirst, and American shellfire and bombing took their toll on the Japanese, and by 25 September the Americans had penetrated the Bowl. Rather than fight for possession of the caves, they used bulldozers to seal the entrances. By 30 September, the island was said to be secure although the Japanese still had about 300 more soldiers in the Bowl and surrounding areas that held out for almost four more weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70</sup> Toward the end of the first week of October, 1944, the protracted conflict had degenerated into minor patrol action with sniping, ambushing, and extensive booby-trapping employed by both sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The island's defense commander, Major Goto was killed on 19 October<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> fighting to keep possession of a cave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70–71</sup> The last day of fighting was October 22 with a total of 36 days of fighting and blasting the Japanese resistance from their caves with explosives, tanks, artillery and flamethrowers. The 81 Infantry Division had finally taken the whole of Angaur, albeit suffering more casualties than they had inflicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:71</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Angaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Airfields were being constructed as the battle was still being fought, but the delay in the start of the Palaus operation meant that the airfields were not ready in time for the start of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> operations in October 1944. Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_F._Halsey,_Jr." title="William F. Halsey, Jr.">William F. Halsey, Jr.</a> had argued before the invasion of the Palaus that the operation was unnecessary, and military historians have agreed with him, suggesting that the main benefit was the combat experience gained by the 81st Infantry Division.</p>
<p>During the fighting, <a href="/wiki/Seabee" title="Seabee">Seabees</a> created an airstrip that would house B 24 Liberator bombers of the 494th Bomb Group, 7th Air Force. The group engaged in frequent bombings of the Philippines and other Palau Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moran_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:91</sup></p>
<p>The 81st Division moved on directly to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Peleliu" title="Battle of Peleliu">battle on Peleliu Island</a> to aid the <a href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Marine Division (United States)">1st Marine Division</a>, which had encountered extremely stiff resistance in the central highland of that island. They would remain on Peleliu for another month taking the island and mopping-up.</p>
