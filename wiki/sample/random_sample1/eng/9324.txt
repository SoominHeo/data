<p>The <b>National Front for the Liberation of Angola</b> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>Frente Nacional de Libertação de Angola</i></span>, <b>FNLA</b>) was a militant organization that fought for <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angolan</a> independence from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Angolan_War_of_Independence" title="Angolan War of Independence">war of independence</a>, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Holden_Roberto" title="Holden Roberto">Holden Roberto</a>.</p>
<p>Founded in 1954 as the <b>União dos Povos do Norte de Angola</b> guerrilla movement, it was known after 1959 as the <b>União dos Povos de Angola</b> (<b>UPA</b>) guerrilla movement, and from 1961 as the FNLA guerilla movement.</p>
<p>Ahead of the first multiparty elections in 1992, the FNLA was reorganized as a political party. The FNLA received 2.4% of the votes and had five Members of Parliament elected. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angolan_parliamentary_election,_2008" title="Angolan parliamentary election, 2008">2008 parliamentary election</a>, the FNLA received 1.11% of the vote, winning three out of 220 seats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1954, the United People of Northern Angola (UPNA) was formed as a separatist movement for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakongo" title="Bakongo">Bakongo</a> tribe who wished to re-establish its 16th century feudal kingdom but was also a protest movement against forced labour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup> Holden Roberto was to be the king of that land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup> By 1958, the organisations name changed to the “Uniao das Populcoes de Angola” (UPA) under Holden Roberto who came from <a href="/wiki/M%27banza-Kongo" title="M'banza-Kongo">São Salvador</a>, Bakongo, Northern Angola with the newly named organisation described as an ethnic political movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:224</sup> In March 1961, the UPA begun an uprising in the north attacking white coffee plantation owners and servants, “assimilados”, African Catholics and tribal members other than the Bakongo tribe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup> The Portuguese government respond by sending soldiers to Angola and more than 50,000 people would die in the violence by the end of 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:222</sup> It was said more than a million refugees would flee the north of Angola for Zaire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup> In an attempt to become a national political movement, it merged with the “Partido Democratico de Angola” (PDA) to form the “Frente Nacional de Libertação de Angola” (FNLA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:224</sup> By February 1962, the FNLA had merged into an organisation called the <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Government_of_Angola_in_Exile" title="Revolutionary Government of Angola in Exile">Angolan Revolutionary Government in Exile</a> (GRAE) with Roberto as its President and <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Savimbi" title="Jonas Savimbi">Jonas Savimbi</a> as its foreign minister, based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinsasha" title="Kinsasha">Kinsasha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a> and recognised by the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity" title="Organisation of African Unity">Organisation of African Unity</a> {OAU} as Angola's only freedom movement until 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:224</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup> Its core membership were Angolan refugees and expatiates in Zaire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:224</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_aid">Foreign aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Foreign aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States government began aiding the FNLA in 1961 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kennedy_administration" title="Kennedy administration">Kennedy administration</a>, and rerouted one-third of its official aid to Zaire to the FNLA and <a href="/wiki/UNITA" title="UNITA">UNITA</a> organisations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foreign_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-foreign-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennedy-8">[8]</a></sup> Over the course of many years, the governments of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> actively supported and aided the FNLA. The French government supplied men and loaned one million pounds sterling without interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foreign_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-foreign-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-algeriaandghana_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-algeriaandghana-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israeli_government" title="Israeli government">Israeli government</a> gave aid to the FNLA between 1963 and 1969. <a href="/wiki/Holden_Roberto" title="Holden Roberto">Holden Roberto</a> visited Israel during the 1960s, and FNLA members were sent to Israel for training. During the 1970s the Israeli government shipped arms to the FNLA through <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israel_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-israel-11">[11]</a></sup> The People's Republic of China began supplying the FNLA with arms in 1964. It gave the FNLA military equipment and at least 112 military advisers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-prc-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Break_up_of_GRAE">Break up of GRAE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Break up of GRAE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By July 1964, GRAE's right as the only liberation movement was challenged with the resignation of Congo Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Cyrille_Adoula" title="Cyrille Adoula">Cyrille Adoula</a>, their backer, and the departure of Jonas Savimbi, who would go on to form his only liberation movement <a href="/wiki/UNITA" title="UNITA">UNITA</a> because of Roberto's dictitorial leadership, unwillingness to accept non-western support and a lack of a political program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:225-6</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup> Roberto would see off a “coup de'tat” in June 1965 by his defence minister and in November of the same year, his brother in law, <a href="/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko" title="Mobutu Sese Seko">Mobutu Sese Seko</a> took control of Zaire in a coup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:226</sup> But by 1968, GRAE's unity had begun to disintegrate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nixon.27s_policy_to_Angola">Nixon's policy to Angola</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nixon's policy to Angola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> assuming office in 1969, he ordered a review of the United States policy towards Angola as well as South Africa and <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a>. In January 1970, National Security Council Study Memorandum 39 was adopted which acknowledge that the white regimes in those countries should not be politically and economically isolated and that engaging them was the best means of achieving changes in their systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:773</sup> This meant a reduction in aid to the FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:773</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coup_in_Portugal">Coup in Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Coup in Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late 1972, the FNLA concluded a ceasefire with the <a href="/wiki/MPLA" title="MPLA">MPLA</a> which was being attacked by former in north-west Angola and by Portuguese in north-east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup> A condition of the ceasefire was it needed more arms as it had no American aid apart from Roberto's CIA retainer, and Neto spoke to the Tanzanians who intercede with the Chinese government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> After all groups met the Chinese, the FNLA received military aid and training from early 1973 until the fall of 1974, and then only diplomatic aid thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> In April 1974, a military coup occurred in Portugal with a later announcement of future independence for its colonies which began the process by the FNLA, MPLA and UNITA in attempting to strengthen their reach throughout Angola and resulted in conflict amongst each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> The Romanian government delivered arms to the FNLA in August 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uspolicy_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-uspolicy-14">[14]</a></sup> In August 1974, the Portuguese Angolan government had proposed a two year plan for independence with the three groups and white settlers forming a coalition government but this was rejected outright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:227</sup> In order to end the conflict amongst the groups, individual ceasefires were arranged between the groups and the Portuguese Angolan Military Council, with the FNLA signing on 15 October 1974 that allowed it and the other three liberation parties to set up political offices in Luanda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:226-7</sup> By 25 November 1974, a ceasefire was concluded between the FNLA and UNITA and with the MPLA on 18 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:229</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ford.27s_policy_to_Angola">Ford's policy to Angola</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ford's policy to Angola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> assumed the presidency in August 1974, the new foreign policy moved away from National Security Council Study Memorandum 39 to one of support for black rule in Angola as well as passive support for white rule and so minimal aid was returned to the FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:774</sup> But by November 1974, the Americans decided they did not want a future government dominated by a pro-Soviet MPLA so the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> funded the FNLA with $300,000 to help it achieve that objective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa.27s_attempt_to_mediate">Africa's attempt to mediate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Africa's attempt to mediate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The increasing violence would result in the gathering of the FNLA and the other two parties in <a href="/wiki/Mombasa" title="Mombasa">Mombasa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, from 3-5 January 1975 by the invitation of President <a href="/wiki/Jomo_Kenyatta" title="Jomo Kenyatta">Jomo Kenyatta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:228</sup> The object of the meeting was to unite the parties and find common ground prior to the independence talks in Portugal later that month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:228</sup> Roberto speaking on behalf of all, declared an accord had been reached and that all parties had overcome their differences and had agreed firstly to a just and democratic society without ethnic discrimination; agreeing to a transitional government, armed forces and civil service and lastly to co-operate in the country's decolonisation and defence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekaney_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-Ekaney-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:228</sup> The FNLA and the other parties would meet in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portimao" title="Portimao">Portimao</a>, Portugal on 10 January 1975 and resulted in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Alvor_Agreement" title="Alvor Agreement">Alvor Agreement</a>, signed on 15 January 1975, which would grant Angola independence from Portugal on 11 November ending the war of independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrett_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Garrett-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> The plan also called for a coalition government and a united army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:774</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transitional_government.27s_failure">Transitional government's failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transitional government's failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within 24 hours of the Alvor Agreement, fighting broke out in Luanda amongst the FNLA and MPLA with further violence on 23 March when the MPLAs <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lopo_de_Nascimento&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lopo de Nascimento (page does not exist)">Lopo de Nascimento</a> was subject to an assassination attempt by the FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup> The transitional government's failure to work was also be said to be the result of a lack of interest by the Portuguese government in Angola as it tackled a failed counter-coup in Lisbon by General Spinola and the lack of will of the Portuguese troops to serve in Angola and end the violence between the MPLA and FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup> The final straw was the dismissal in August of the Portuguese High Commissioner <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_da_Silva_Cardoso&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antonio da Silva Cardoso (page does not exist)">Antonio da Silva Cardoso</a> who's attempt to reign in the MPLA had the support of the FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup> The FNLA saw its only alternative as a military one after having been expelled from Luanda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup> On 29 August 1975 the Alvor Agreement was suspended by Portugal except for independence in November, and withdrawal of its troops that signal an escalation of violence for the control of Angola prior to that date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:60</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_covert_aid_to_the_FNLA_increases">American covert aid to the FNLA increases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: American covert aid to the FNLA increases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The American government did not believe the Portuguese plan would work and the MPLA would attempt to seize power installing a Soviet back regime in power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:774</sup> In late January 1975, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/40_Committee" title="40 Committee">40 Committee</a>, part of the executive branch of the American government, met and reviewed a proposal from the CIA to fund the FNLA with $300,00 and UNITA, $100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:774</sup> The committee would approve the funding for the FNLA but not for UNITA. The money was to be used by the FNLA to purchase newspapers and radio stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinghoffer_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klinghoffer-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup> In addition to the money, the Americans supplied weapons to Zaire who in turn passed them on to the FNLA and also supplied several thousand troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:774</sup> With this funding, Roberto believe any future coalition could be abandoned and in doing so the Soviets would begin to increase its aid to the MPLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:775</sup> By June 1975, the CIA requested a meeting with the 40 Committee were it proposed increased aid for the FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:774</sup> A decision was not made for a month as the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Council" title="National Security Council">National Security Council</a> (NSC) and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">US State Department</a> wished to consider the proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:775</sup> Assistant Secretary of State Nathaniel Davies objected to further aid as he believed it would not help the FNLA to become as militarily strong as the MPLA except with massive amounts money; escalate Soviet and Cuban involvement and feared South Africa would intervene and this would have negative diplomatic connotations for the United States in Africa, so the only option was a diplomatic solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-10"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:775</sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> ensured that the NSC viewpoint prevailed and that aid not diplomacy could prevent a MPLA win so aid of $14 million was approved for the FNLA and UNITA in July and that increased to $25 million in August and reached $32 million by September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-11"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:775</sup> The assistance would become known as <a href="/wiki/Operation_IA_Feature" title="Operation IA Feature">Operation IA Feature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:61</sup> The CIAs covert plan begun sending supplies to the FNLA through Zaire and Zambia and was able to supply by November 1975, 12 <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">APC</a>, 50 <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">SAMs</a>, 1000 mortars, 50,000 rifles and machine guns, 100,000 grenades, 25 million rounds of ammunition, 60 trucks, trailers, boats, radios, spare parts, medicine and food with training by retired American military advisors and five spotter planes as well as mercenaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-12"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:776</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinghoffer_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klinghoffer-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup> The CIA appointed <a href="/wiki/John_Stockwell" title="John Stockwell">John Stockwell</a> to manage the Angolan Task Force but found that many members of the CIA doubted the FNLAs ability to beat the MPLA and this was confirmed when he visited Angola and discovered the lack political support for the organisation and also feared any entry of South African forces in Angola would undermine the United States diplomatically in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-13"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:776</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinghoffer_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Klinghoffer-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africans_enter_the_civil_war">South Africans enter the civil war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: South Africans enter the civil war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the pretext of attacks around the <a href="/wiki/Calueque" title="Calueque">Calueque</a> hydroelectric facility, the South Africans army would enter Angola to defend its interests in the facility and would develop into <a href="/wiki/Operation_Savannah_(Angola)" title="Operation Savannah (Angola)">Operation Savannah</a> to assist the FNLA and UNITA to gain as much control of southern and central Angola prior to independence day in November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70</sup> The Americans appeared to give the green light for the South Africans covert invasion but this would soon change as their involvement became public knowledge, the Americans would distance themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:70</sup> The South Africans would advance close to Luanda rom the south while a small force of South African artillery and advisors would support the FNLA in the north.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FNLA_attacks_Luanda">FNLA attacks Luanda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: FNLA attacks Luanda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Without the control of Luanda on independence day, Roberto saw that the FNLAs international legitimacy would be in doubt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-8"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:86</sup> The only 'suitable' attack on Luanda was from the north through Quifangondo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-9"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup> Attacks were carried out by the FNLA on 5 and 8 November 1975 but were repulsed each time by the MPLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-10"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup> With independence day looming on the 11 November 1975, Roberto gave the final order to attack Quifangondo on 10 November unaware that the Cubans had reinforced the positions with troops and new Soviet equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-11"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup> Roberto would claim the South African were sending men to help him while the South Africans claimed they warned against a frontal assault but what ever the real story was, the FNLAs final assault on what became know as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Quifangondo" title="Battle of Quifangondo">Battle of Quifangondo</a> failed disastrously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-12"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:89-90</sup> The MPLA maintained Luanda and Angola gain independence from the Portuguese High Commissioner with Neto declaring People's Republic of Angola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-13"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:91</sup> The FNLA would continue its fight inside Angola for another four months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-14"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:91</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_aid_ceases">American aid ceases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: American aid ceases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the 6 November 1975, CIA Director <a href="/wiki/William_Colby" title="William Colby">William Colby</a> appeared before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_Foreign_Relations_Committee" title="Senate Foreign Relations Committee">Senate Foreign Relations Committee</a> and briefed them that his organisation had not informed the committee fully on its activities in Angola and the following day, the <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a></i> released the testimony to the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-14"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:777</sup> By 26 November, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, against the United States wishes, had recognised the MPLA government and soon convinced twenty-two other African nation to recognise them too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-15"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:777</sup> By December, President Ford and Kissinger decide that the aid to the opposition parties should not be abandoned and the CIA was ordered to draw up further aid plans which would need Senate approval but meanwhile the Senate Foreign Relations Committee drew up the <a href="/wiki/Clark_Amendment" title="Clark Amendment">Clark Amendment</a>, named after Senator <a href="/wiki/Dick_Clark_(senator)" title="Dick Clark (senator)">Dick Clark</a>, after his visit to Angola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-16"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:778</sup> He concluded that the White House and CIA had lied about their involvement and that the Americans effort were responsible for dragging the Cubans and South Africans into the country's conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-17"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:778</sup> The State Department and CIA, unable to stay on message, were unable to convince the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> and on 19 December 1975, the Clark Amendment passed the Senate and covert American aid in Angola ended with the House following suite on 27 January 1976 .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noer_13-18"><a href="#cite_note-Noer-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:778-9</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FNLA_retreats_from_Angola">FNLA retreats from Angola</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: FNLA retreats from Angola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The MPLA and Cubans would maintain the initiative in Northern Angola after the defeat of the FNLA at Quifangondo with the advances on <a href="/wiki/Caxito" title="Caxito">Caxito</a> and the latters airbases at Camabatela and <a href="/wiki/Negage" title="Negage">Negage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-15"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:107</sup> Caxito would fall on 27 December 1975 and the FNLAs main airbase at Camabatela was captured on 1 January and Negage on 3 January while their capital at <a href="/wiki/U%C3%ADge" title="Uíge">Carmona</a> fell on the 4 January and so the FNLA rout began in earnest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-16"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:107-8</sup> By early 1976, defeated by the MPLA, the FNLA began retreating, looting villages in northern Angola, as they headed for the Zairian border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kosnett_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kosnett-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> On the 11 January 1976, FAPLA and the Cubans captured <a href="/wiki/Ambriz" title="Ambriz">Ambriz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambrizete" title="Ambrizete">Ambrizete</a> from the FNLA and then advanced on the latters headquarters at São Salvador its route defended by foreign mercenaries under <a href="/wiki/Costas_Georgiou" title="Costas Georgiou">Colonel Callan</a> and elements of the FNLA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-17"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:108-9</sup> With Colonel Callan committing atrocities in the fighting including against his own men he would be stripped of his command in the FNLA and so mercenary support which had begun the previous December ended and São Salvador was captured on 15 February 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-18"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:109</sup> The South Africans would withdraw from Angola on 27 March 1976 after receiving guarantees from Angola and United Nations on the safety of the installations at the <a href="/wiki/Calueque" title="Calueque">Calueque</a> hydroelectric facility so ending <a href="/wiki/Operation_Savannah_(Angola)" title="Operation Savannah (Angola)">Operation Savannah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-19"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:112</sup> Elements of the FNLA that had taken part as South African army's Task Force Zulu, would be reformed into <a href="/wiki/32_Battalion_(South_Africa)" title="32 Battalion (South Africa)">32 Battalion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-20"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:71</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demise_of_the_FNLA">Demise of the FNLA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Front_of_Angola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Demise of the FNLA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 29 February 1976, the Angolan President <a href="/wiki/Agostinho_Neto" title="Agostinho Neto">Agostinho Neto</a> and Zairian President Mobuto Sese Seko met in Brazzaville to sign a non-aggression pact which was meant to see the end of Angola's support for Katangese rebels in their country while the Zairians promised to expel the FNLA and Unita from bases in Zaire but the deal did not hold and the <a href="/wiki/Shaba_I" title="Shaba I">Shaba I</a> invasion would occur in March 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-21"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:117-8</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Shaba_II" title="Shaba II">Shaba II</a> invasion of the Zairian <a href="/wiki/Katanga_Province" title="Katanga Province">Shaba Province</a> in May 1978, by separatists based in eastern Angola, was the beginning of the end for the FNLA based in Zaire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-22"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:136</sup> The Angolan President Neto and Zairian President Mobuto Sese Seko would meet again in Brazzaville during June 1978 were a reconciliation pact was signed between the two countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kosnett_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kosnett-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> The result of this pact saw Holden Roberto exiled to <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a> by the Zairian President in November 1979 while he was in France for medical treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-23"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kosnett_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kosnett-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> Elements of the FNLA would continue the fight after Roberto left, now called the FNLA-COMIRA (Angolan Military Resistance Committee) but ceased to exist by 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-George_15-24"><a href="#cite_note-George-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138</sup></p>
