<p><b>André Marie Chénier</b> (30 October 1762 – 25 July 1794) was a French poet of <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> and Franco-Levantine origin, associated with the events of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> of which he was a victim. His sensual, emotive poetry marks him as one of the precursors of the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> movement. His career was brought to an abrupt end when he was <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotined</a> for supposed "crimes against the state", near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Reign of Terror</a>. Chénier's life has been the subject of <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Giordano" title="Umberto Giordano">Umberto Giordano</a>'s opera <i><a href="/wiki/Andrea_Ch%C3%A9nier" title="Andrea Chénier">Andrea Chénier</a></i> and other works of art.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_Ch%C3%A9nier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3500" data-file-width="2318" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg/150px-David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg/225px-David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg/300px-David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:David_d%27Angers_-_Chenier.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bust of André Chénier by <a href="/wiki/David_d%27Angers" title="David d'Angers">David d'Angers</a> (1839).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He was born in the <a href="/wiki/Galata" title="Galata">Galata</a> district of Constantinople. His family home, destroyed in a fire, was located on the site of the present Saint Pierre Han, in today's <a href="/wiki/Karak%C3%B6y" title="Karaköy">Karaköy</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>. His father, Louis Chénier, a native of <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a>, after twenty years in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> as a cloth-merchant, was appointed to a position equivalent to that of French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consulate_general" title="Consulate general">consul</a> at Constantinople. His mother, Élisabeth Santi-Lomaca, whose sister was grandmother of <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Thiers" title="Adolphe Thiers">Adolphe Thiers</a>, was of <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When André was three years old, his father returned to France, and from 1768 to 1775 served as consul-general of France in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. The family, of which André was the third son, and Marie-Joseph (see below) the fourth, remained in France; and after a few years, during which André was given his youthful freedom while living with an aunt in <a href="/wiki/Carcassonne" title="Carcassonne">Carcassonne</a>; a square in Carcassonne is named to commemorate him. He distinguished himself as a verse-translator from the classics at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_Navarre" title="Collège de Navarre">Collège de Navarre</a> in Paris.</p>
<p>In 1783 he enlisted in a French regiment at <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, but the novelty soon wore off. He returned to Paris before the end of the year, was well received by his family, and mixed in the cultivated circle which frequented his mother's <a href="/wiki/Salon_(gathering)" title="Salon (gathering)">salon</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Ponce_Denis_%C3%89couchard_Lebrun" title="Ponce Denis Écouchard Lebrun">Lebrun-Pindare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Fran%C3%A7ois_Lesueur" title="Jean François Lesueur">Jean François Lesueur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Joseph_Dorat" title="Claude Joseph Dorat">Claude Joseph Dorat</a>, and, a little later, the painter <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a>.</p>
<p>He had already decided to become a poet, and worked in the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> style of the time. He was especially inspired by a 1784 visit to Rome, <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>. For nearly three years, he studied and experimented in verse without any pressure or interruption from his family. He wrote mostly <a href="/wiki/Idyll" title="Idyll">idylls</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bucolic" title="Bucolic">bucolics</a>, imitated to a large extent from <a href="/wiki/Theocritus" title="Theocritus">Theocritus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bion_of_Smyrna" title="Bion of Smyrna">Bion of Smyrna</a> and the Greek anthologists. Among the poems written or at least sketched during this period were <i>L'Oaristys</i>, <i>L'Aveugle</i>, <i>La Jeune Malode</i>, <i>Bacchus</i>, <i>Euphrosine</i> and <i>La Jeune Tarentine</i>. He mixed classical mythology with a sense of individual emotion and spirit.</p>
<p>Apart from his idylls and his elegies, Chénier also experimented with didactic and philosophic verse, and when he commenced his <i>Hermès</i> in 1783 his ambition was to condense the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Denis Diderot</a> into a long poem somewhat after the manner of <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a>. Now extant only in fragments, this poem was to treat of man's place in the universe, first in an isolated state, and then in society. Another fragment called "L'Invention" sums up Chénier's thoughts on poetry: "De nouvelles pensées, faisons des vers antiques" ("From new thoughts, let us make antique verses").</p>
<p>Chénier remained unpublished. In November 1787 an opportunity for a fresh career presented itself. The <a href="/wiki/Anne-C%C3%A9sar,_Chevalier_de_la_Luzerne" title="Anne-César, Chevalier de la Luzerne">Chevalier de la Luzerne</a>, a friend of the Chénier family, had been appointed ambassador to Britain. When he offered to take André with him as his secretary, André knew the offer was too good to refuse, but was unhappy in England. He bitterly ridiculed "... ces Anglais. Nation toute à vendre à qui peut la payer. De contrée en contrée allant au monde entier, Offrir sa joie ignoble et son faste grossier." Although <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Thomson_(poet)" title="James Thomson (poet)">James Thomson</a> seem to have interested him, and a few of his verses show slight inspiration from <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gray" title="Thomas Gray">Thomas Gray</a>, it would be an exaggeration to say Chénier studied English literature.</p>
<p>The events of 1789 and the startling success of his younger brother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Ch%C3%A9nier" title="Joseph Chénier">Marie-Joseph</a>, as political <a href="/wiki/Playwright" title="Playwright">playwright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pamphleteer" title="Pamphleteer">pamphleteer</a>, concentrated all his thoughts upon France. In April 1790 he could stand London no longer, and once more joined his parents at Paris in the rue de Cléry. France was on the verge of <a href="/wiki/Anarchy" title="Anarchy">anarchy</a>. A <i>monarchien</i> believing in a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> for France, Chénier believed that the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">Revolution</a> was already complete and that all that remained to be done was the inauguration of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">reign of law</a>. Though his political viewpoint was moderate, his tactics were dangerously aggressive: he abandoned his gentle idyls to write poetical <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satires</a>. His prose "Avis au peuple français" (24 August 1790) was followed by the rhetorical "Jeu de paume", a somewhat declamatory moral ode occasioned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tennis_court_oath" title="Tennis court oath">Tennis court oath</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> addressed to the radical painter Jacques-Louis David.</p>
<p>In the meantime he orated at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feuillants_Club" title="Feuillants Club">Feuillants Club</a>, and contributed frequently to the <i>Journal de Paris</i> from November 1791 to July 1792, when he wrote his scorching <a href="/wiki/Iambus_(genre)" title="Iambus (genre)">iambs</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Marie_Collot_d%27Herbois" title="Jean Marie Collot d'Herbois">Jean Marie Collot d'Herbois</a>, <i>Sur les Suisses révoltés du regiment de Châteauvieux</i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10th_of_August_(French_Revolution)" title="10th of August (French Revolution)">insurrection</a> of 10 August 1792 uprooted his party, his paper and his friends, and he only escaped the <a href="/wiki/September_Massacres" title="September Massacres">September Massacres</a> by staying with relatives in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. In the month following these events his brother, Marie-Joseph, had entered the anti-monarchical <a href="/wiki/National_Convention" title="National Convention">National Convention</a>. André raged against all these events, in such poems as <i>Ode à <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Corday" title="Charlotte Corday">Charlotte Corday</a></i> congratulating France that "un scélérat de moins rampe dans cette fange," "one scoundrel less creeps in this mire". At the request of <a href="/wiki/Guillaume-Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Lamoignon_de_Malesherbes" title="Guillaume-Chrétien de Lamoignon de Malesherbes">Malesherbes</a>, the defense counsel to King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a>, Chénier provided some arguments for the king's defense.</p>
<p>After the king's execution he sought a secluded retreat on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plateau_de_Satory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plateau de Satory (page does not exist)">Plateau de Satory</a> at <a href="/wiki/Versailles,_Yvelines" title="Versailles, Yvelines">Versailles</a> and only went out after nightfall. There he wrote the poems inspired by Fanny (Mme Laurent Lecoulteux), including the exquisite <i>Ode à Versailles</i>. His solitary life at Versailles lasted nearly a year. On 7 March 1794 he was arrested at the house of Mme Piscatory at <a href="/wiki/Passy" title="Passy">Passy</a>. Two obscure agents of the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Public_Safety" title="Committee of Public Safety">Committee of Public Safety</a> (one of them named Nicolas Guénot) were in search of a marquise who had fled, but an unknown stranger was found in the house and arrested on suspicion of being the aristocrat they were searching for. This was Chénier, who had come on a visit of sympathy.</p>
<p>He was taken to the <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_Palace" title="Luxembourg Palace">Luxembourg Palace</a> and afterwards to the <a href="/wiki/Prison_Saint-Lazare" title="Prison Saint-Lazare">Prison Saint-Lazare</a>. During the 140 days of his imprisonment he wrote a series of iambs (in alternate lines of 12 and 8 syllables) denouncing the Convention, which, in the words of the 1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i>, "hiss and stab like poisoned bullets", and which were smuggled to his family by a jailer. In prison he also composed his most famous poem, "Jeune captive", a poem at once of enchantment and of despair, inspired by the misfortunes of his fellow captive the duchesse de Fleury, née de Coigny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ten days before Chénier's death, the painter <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Beno%C3%AEt_Suv%C3%A9e" title="Joseph-Benoît Suvée">Joseph-Benoît Suvée</a> completed the well-known portrait of him, shown in the box above.</p>
<p>Chénier might have been overlooked but for the well-meant, indignant officiousness of his father. Marie-Joseph did his best to prevent his brother's execution, but he could do nothing more. <a href="/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre" title="Maximilien Robespierre">Maximilien Robespierre</a>, who was himself in dangerous straits, remembered Chénier as the author of the venomous verses in the <i>Journal de Paris</i> and had him hauled before the Revolutionary Tribunal, which sentenced him to death. Chénier was one of the last people executed by Robespierre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>At sundown, Chénier was taken by <a href="/wiki/Tumbrel" title="Tumbrel">tumbrel</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotine</a> at what is now the <a href="/wiki/Place_de_la_Nation" title="Place de la Nation">Place de la Nation</a>. He was executed along with <a href="/wiki/Marie_Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_de_Choiseul" title="Marie Thérèse de Choiseul">Françoise-Thérèse de Choiseul-Stainville, Princesse Joseph de Monaco</a>, on a charge of conspiracy. Robespierre was seized and executed only three days later. Chénier, aged 31 at his execution, was buried in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_de_Picpus" title="Cimetière de Picpus">Cimetière de Picpus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_Ch%C3%A9nier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During Chénier's lifetime only his <i>Jeu de paume</i> (1791) and <i>Hymne sur les Suisses</i> (1792) had been published. For the most part, then, his reputation rests on his posthumously published work, retrieved from oblivion page by page.</p>
<p>The <i>Jeune Captive</i> appeared in the <i>Décade philosophique</i>, on 9 January 1795; <i>La Jeune Tarentine</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mercure_de_France" title="Mercure de France">Mercure de France</a></i> of 22 March 1801. <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Ren%C3%A9_de_Chateaubriand" title="François-René de Chateaubriand">François-René de Chateaubriand</a> quoted three or four passages in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G%C3%A9nie_du_Christianisme" title="Génie du Christianisme">Génie du Christianisme</a></i>. Fayolle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jules_Lef%C3%A8vre-Deumier" title="Jules Lefèvre-Deumier">Jules Lefèvre-Deumier</a> also gave a few fragments; but it was not until 1819 that an attempt was made by <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Latouche" title="Henri de Latouche">Henri de Latouche</a> to collect the poems in a substantive volume, from manuscripts retained by Marie-Joseph Chénier. Many more poems and fragments were discovered after Latouche's publication, and were collected in later editions. Latouche also wrote an account of Chénier's last moments, which the 1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> described as "melodramatic and certainly not above suspicion."</p>
<p>Critical opinions of Chénier have varied wildly. He experimented with classical precedents rendered in French verse to a much greater extent than other 18th-century poets; on the other hand, the ennui and melancholy of his poetry recalls Romanticism. In 1828, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Augustin_Sainte-Beuve" title="Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve">Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve</a> praised Chénier as an heroic forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic movement</a> and a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a>. Chénier, he said, had "inspired and determined" <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>. Many other critics also wrote about Chénier as modern and proto-Romantic. However, <a href="/wiki/Anatole_France" title="Anatole France">Anatole France</a> contests Sainte-Beuve's theory: he claims that Chénier's poetry is one of the last expressions of 18th-century classicism. His work should not be compared to Hugo and the <i><a href="/wiki/Parnassianism" title="Parnassianism">Parnassien</a></i> poets, but to <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophes" title="Philosophes">philosophes</a></i> like <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Morellet" title="André Morellet">André Morellet</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Morillot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Morillot (page does not exist)">Paul Morillot</a> has argued that judged by the usual test of 1820s Romanticism (love for strange literature of the North, medievalism, novelties and experiments), Chénier would have been excluded from Romantic circles.</p>
<p>The poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_de_Heredia" title="José María de Heredia">José María de Heredia</a> held Chénier in great esteem, saying "I do not know in the French language a more exquisite fragment than the three hundred verses of the <i>Bucoliques</i>" and agreeing with Sainte-Beuve's judgment that Chénier was a poet ahead of his time. Chénier has been very popular in Russia, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandr_Pushkin" title="Alexandr Pushkin">Alexandr Pushkin</a> wrote a poem about his last hours based on Latouche and <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Kozlov" title="Ivan Kozlov">Ivan Kozlov</a> translated <i>La Jeune Captive</i>, <i>La Jeune Tarentine</i> and other famous pieces. Chénier has also found favor with English-speaking critics; for instance, his love of nature and of political freedom has been compared to <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">Shelley</a>, and his attraction to Greek art and myth recalls <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">Keats</a>.</p>
<p>Chénier's fate has become the subject of many plays, pictures and poems, notably in the opera <i><a href="/wiki/Andrea_Ch%C3%A9nier" title="Andrea Chénier">Andrea Chénier</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Giordano" title="Umberto Giordano">Umberto Giordano</a>, the epilogue by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Fran%C3%A7ois_Armand_Sully-Prudhomme" title="René François Armand Sully-Prudhomme">Sully-Prudhomme</a>, the <i>Stello</i> by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_de_Vigny" title="Alfred de Vigny">Alfred de Vigny</a>, the delicate statue by <a href="/wiki/Denys_Puech" title="Denys Puech">Denys Puech</a> in the Luxembourg, and the well-known portrait in the centre of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Louis_M%C3%BCller" title="Charles Louis Müller">Muller</a>'s <i>Last Days of the Terror</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="per WP:CIRCULAR (December 2016)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
