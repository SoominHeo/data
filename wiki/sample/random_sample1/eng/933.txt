<p>The <b>EADS Harfang</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> formerly known as <i><b>Système intérimaire de drone MALE</b></i> (SIDM, "Interim medium-altitude, long-endurance drone system") is a drone system used by the <a href="/wiki/French_Air_Force" title="French Air Force">French Air Force</a>, supplementing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RQ-5_Hunter" title="RQ-5 Hunter">RQ-5 Hunter</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EADS_Harfang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The French Air Force purchased its first drones in 1995, with a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RQ-5_Hunter" title="RQ-5 Hunter">RQ-5 Hunter</a>. The <a href="/wiki/IAI_Heron" title="IAI Heron">IAI Heron</a> was put on display at the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Paris_Air_Show" title="Paris Air Show">Paris Air Show</a> and generated some interest; a project to field a derivative was started two years later. On 9 September 2006, a prototype was launched for trials from <a href="/wiki/Istres-Le_Tub%C3%A9_Air_Base" title="Istres-Le Tubé Air Base">Istres-Le Tubé Air Base</a>. Trials by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_d%27essais_en_vol" title="Centre d'essais en vol">Centre d'essais en vol</a> followed, before transfer of the plane to <a href="/wiki/Mont-de-Marsan_Air_Base" title="Mont-de-Marsan Air Base">Mont-de-Marsan Air Base</a></p>
<p>The drone was originally planned to be delivered to the Air Force for the summer of 2003 as a replacement for the Hunters, which were phased out in September 2004. A number of industrial setbacks delayed the delivery, notably that the satellite communication system did not conform with Air Force requirements. The drones were modified to satisfy the requirements, changing them from an "<i>off-the-shelf"</i> product into a custom plane built specially for the French Air Force. The plane and onboard computer are built by Malat, a division of <a href="/wiki/Israel_Aerospace_Industries" title="Israel Aerospace Industries">Israel Aerospace Industries</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/IAI_Heron" title="IAI Heron">IAI Heron</a>.</p>
<p>The French Air Force ordered three planes and two ground control stations. The SIDM entered service in June 2008 in the Drone Experimentation Squadron 1/330 Adour, in Cognac, a unit of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_des_exp%C3%A9rimentations_militaires&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Centre des expérimentations militaires (page does not exist)">Centre des expérimentations militaires</a>. A four-phase programme took place between 2008 and 2009:</p>
<ul>
<li>instruction of the squadron in the use of the system</li>
<li>instruction of the operators in the sensors</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C4ISTAR" title="C4ISTAR">C4ISTAR</a> management and communication to upper levels</li>
<li>communication with ground forces</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EADS_Harfang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Harfang is composed of two main components: the drone and the ground control station.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drones">Drones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EADS_Harfang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Harfang drone is an unmanned airplane propelled by a rear-mounted turbo-supercharged water-cooled flat-four engine, driving a pusher propeller. It features a flapped high wing with anti-icing system, and has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twin_boom" title="Twin boom">twin boom</a> tail with two vertical surfaces. With a take-off mass of 1.2 tonnes, the Harfang can carry a 250-kg payload to height of 7500 metres for a duration of 24 hours.</p>
<p>The mission pack is a three-sensor sphere stabilised by gyroscope, mounted under the forward part of the fuselage. It contains optronic and IR sensors, a laser designator that doubles as a rangefinder, and a high-resolution, fixed echo suppressing <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar" title="Synthetic aperture radar">synthetic aperture radar</a>. The radar can be used to observe vehicle movement on the ground under any meteorological condition. The antenna is mounted on the centre of the body.</p>
<p>The Harfang carries an <a href="/wiki/Inertial_measurement_unit" title="Inertial measurement unit">Inertial measurement unit</a> with GPS recalibration, and a differential GPS that provides for automatic take-off and landing capabilities. In case of loss of communications, the drone follows a path that brings it back to its start point, while trying to re-establish contact.</p>
<p>The drone carries an IFF transponder and a flight recorder. The Harfang lacks electromagnetic sensors and is wanting in terms of maneuverability. It is limited by bad weather, notably by icing or high humidity conditions.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_station">Control station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EADS_Harfang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Control station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The control station is composed of four modules, numbered M1 through M4:</p>
<ul>
<li>M1 is the mission preparation station. It is based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirage_2000" title="Mirage 2000">Mirage 2000</a> briefing station.</li>
<li>M2 is the command and control unit, used mostly during take-off and landing. Most of the mission is automated.</li>
<li>M3 is the data collection unit, receiving information through satellite communication</li>
<li>M4 is the military intelligence centre.</li>
</ul>
<p>A line-of-sight link allows for direct communication up to 150 to 200 kilometres away. Longer ranges utilise a directional antenna and geostationary satellites. The Very High Frequency and Ultra high frequency communication relay provides for links to ground units and air traffic control.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EADS_Harfang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, the Harfang was deployed in Lourdes to provide security for <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2009, the 3 Harfang were sent to <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and deployed at Bagram, under Lieutenant-colonel Cyril Carcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In late March or early April, drone number <i>F-SDAY</i> was damaged when it crashed due to freezing, and possibly to human or software error. Purchase of a fourth vehicle to replace <i>F-SDAY</i> is under consideration, while it is being repaired and redeployed in France for training.</p>
<p>In August 2011, Harfangs were deployed to the <a href="/wiki/2011_military_intervention_in_Libya" title="2011 military intervention in Libya">Libyan conflict</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>France intends to replace the Harfang by 2014 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MQ-9_Reaper" title="MQ-9 Reaper">MQ-9 Reaper</a> UAVs ordered in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5–6 June 2013, Harfang UAVs carried out their 100th sortie during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Serval" title="Operation Serval">Operation Serval</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>. Deployed since 17 January, Harfangs flew 1,600 hours during the day and night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>From January 2013 to September 2013, the Harfang has flown 2,000 hours in support of French operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2014, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_Air_Force" title="Moroccan Air Force">Moroccan Air Force</a> became interested in purchasing 3 former French Harfang unmanned air systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2016, Harfang drones are being used for Homeland security missions in mainland France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EADS_Harfang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Eagle 0</b> : prototype, first flight in 1998.</li>
<li><b>Eagle 1</b> : first production versions, first flight on 2 June 2003 in Israel.</li>
<li><b>Eagle MPR</b> : naval version of Eagle 1.</li>
<li><b>Eagle 2</b> : larger version of Eagle 1 with a <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a> PT6 turbopropeller.</li>
</ul>

<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> none</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 9,30 metres ()</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 16,6 metres ()</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> ()</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 657 kg ()</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 1 250 kg ()</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 1 × Rotax 914 F turbocharged engine, 115 hp (86 kW)</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 207 km/h</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 1 000 km ()</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 7 620 metres ()</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Avionics</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li>Optronic sensors</li>
<li>SAR/MTI mobile target detection radar</li>
<li>Line-of-sight and satellite communication systems</li>
<li>Laser telemeter-designator</li>
</ul>
