<p><b>Henri Farman</b> (26 May 1874 – 17 July 1958)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightObit_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FlightObit-1">[1]</a></sup> was an Anglo-French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">aviator</a> and aircraft designer and manufacturer with his brother <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Farman" title="Maurice Farman">Maurice Farman</a>. His family was British and he took French nationality in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightObit_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FlightObit-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henri_Farman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in Paris, France, and given the name Henry, he was the son of a well-to-do British newspaper correspondent working there and his French wife. Farman trained as a painter at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux_Arts" title="École des Beaux Arts">École des Beaux Arts</a>, but quickly become obsessed with the new mechanical inventions that were rapidly appearing at the end of the 19th century. Since his family had money, he was able to pursue this interest as an amateur sportsman. In the 1890s he became a championship <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cyclist</a>, and at the turn of the century he discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_racing" title="Motor racing">motor racing</a>, competing for <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordon_Bennett_Cup_in_Auto_Racing" title="Gordon Bennett Cup in Auto Racing">Gordon Bennett Cup</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="930" data-file-width="650" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg/220px-Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg/330px-Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg/440px-Henry_Farman_first_cross-country_flight_with_aeroplane_Scientific_American_1908-11-21.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Voisin" title="Voisin">Voisin</a> brothers started their aircraft construction business in 1907 Farman was one of their first customers, ordering a copy of the aircraft that had been built for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Delagrange" title="Leon Delagrange">Leon Delagrange</a>. He used this aircraft, the <a href="/wiki/Voisin_1907_biplane" title="Voisin 1907 biplane">Voisin 1907 biplane</a> to set numerous official records for both distance and duration. These include the first to fly a complete circuit of 1 kilometre (13 January 1908, winning the 50,000 franc <i>Grand Prix d'Aviation</i> offered by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Deutsch_de_la_Meurthe" title="Henri Deutsch de la Meurthe">Henri Deutsch de la Meurthe</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wright_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wright-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) and 2 kilometres (21 March 1908<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f12_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-f12-4">[4]</a></sup>). Some sources state that on 29 March, he became the first to take a passenger into the air,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f12_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-f12-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Delagrange" title="Leon Delagrange">Leon Delagrange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-v-5">[5]</a></sup> (Others, however, believe that record belongs to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilbur_Wright" title="Wilbur Wright">Wilbur Wright</a> and passenger Charles Furnas on 14 May of the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>) Later in 1908, on 30 October, Farman went on to make the first cross-country flight in Europe, flying from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2lons-sur-Marne" title="Châlons-sur-Marne">Châlons</a> to <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a> (27 kilometres in 20 minutes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1909, he opened a flying school at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2lons-sur-Marne" title="Châlons-sur-Marne">Châlons-sur-Marne</a> at which <a href="/wiki/George_Bertram_Cockburn" title="George Bertram Cockburn">George Bertram Cockburn</a> was the first pupil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The same year he made further record breaking flights of 180 kilometres in just over 3 hours (at <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a> on 27 August) and 232 kilometres in 4 hours 17 minutes and 53 seconds (at <a href="/wiki/Mourmelon-le-Grand" title="Mourmelon-le-Grand">Mourmelon-le-Grand</a> on 3 November).</p>
<p>At the end of 1909 Farman fell out with <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Voisin" title="Gabriel Voisin">Gabriel Voisin</a> because Voisin had sold an aircraft that had been built to Farman's specifications to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.T.C._Moore-Brabazon" title="J.T.C. Moore-Brabazon">J.T.C. Moore-Brabazon</a>, and started manufacturing aircraft to his own design. The first of these, the <a href="/wiki/Farman_III" title="Farman III">Farman III</a>, was an immediate success and was widely imitated.</p>
<p>In partnership with his two brothers <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Farman" title="Maurice Farman">Maurice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Farman" title="Richard Farman">Richard (Dick)</a>, he built a highly successful and innovative aircraft manufacturing plant. Their 1914 model was used extensively for artillery observation and reconnaissance during World War I. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farman_Aircraft" title="Farman Aircraft">Farman Aircraft</a> company's <a href="/wiki/Farman_F.60_Goliath" title="Farman F.60 Goliath"><i>Goliath</i></a> was the first long-distance passenger <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a>, beginning regular Paris-London (<a href="/wiki/Croydon_Airport" title="Croydon Airport">Croydon Airport</a>) flights on 8 February 1919.</p>
<p>He was made a <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">chevalier</a> of the French <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Légion d'honneur</a> (French: "Legion of Honour") in 1919. He, along with Maurice, retired in 1937 when the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_(France)#May_1936_elections_and_the_formation_of_the_Blum_government" title="Popular Front (France)">French Popular Front government</a> nationalised the aircraft industry; Farman's company becoming part of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Societe_Nationale_de_Constructions_Aeronautiques_du_Centre" title="Societe Nationale de Constructions Aeronautiques du Centre">Societe Nationale de Constructions Aeronautiques du Centre</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightObit_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FlightObit-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Henry Farman took French nationality in 1937.</p>
<p>He died in Paris and is buried in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_de_Passy" title="Cimetière de Passy">Cimetière de Passy</a> in Paris.</p>
