<p>The <b>ankh</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> <i><span lang="egy" xml:lang="egy">ꜥnḫ</span></i>), also known as <i><b>crux ansata</b></i> (the Latin for "cross with a handle") is an ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyph" title="Egyptian hieroglyph">Egyptian hieroglyphic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideograph" title="Ideograph">ideograph</a> with the meaning "life".</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_gods" title="Egyptian gods">Egyptian gods</a> are often portrayed carrying it by its loop, or bearing one in each hand, arms crossed over their chest. The ankh appears in hand or in proximity of almost every deity in the Egyptian pantheon (including Pharaohs).</p>
<p>The ankh symbol was so prevalent that it has been found in digs as far as Mesopotamia and Persia, and even on the seal of the biblical king <a href="/wiki/Hezekiah" title="Hezekiah">Hezekiah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The symbol became popular in <a href="/wiki/New_Age" title="New Age">New Age</a> mysticism in the 1960s.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> has two characters encoding the symbol, U+2625 ☥ in the <a href="/wiki/Miscellaneous_Symbols" title="Miscellaneous Symbols">Miscellaneous Symbols</a> block and U+132F9 𓋹 in the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Hieroglyphs_(Unicode_block)" title="Egyptian Hieroglyphs (Unicode block)">Egyptian Hieroglyphs</a> block.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_Gardiner" title="Alan Gardiner">Alan Gardiner</a> (1957) explains the hieroglyph as a depiction of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandal_(footwear)" title="Sandal (footwear)">sandal</a>-strap (<i>ꜥnḫ</i>) which came to be read phonetically and could be used (as "<a href="/wiki/Rebus" title="Rebus">rebus writing</a>") for the homophone <i>ꜥnḫ</i> "live".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There have been alternative suggestions. One of the earliest proposals was that of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Inman" title="Thomas Inman">Thomas Inman</a>, first published in 1869, according to which the symbol combines "the male triad and the female unit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/E._A._Wallis_Budge" title="E. A. Wallis Budge">E. A. Wallis Budge</a> (1904) postulated that the symbol originated as the belt buckle of the mother goddess <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>More recently, the authors of <i>The Quick and the Dead</i> (2004) speculated that the ankh, <a href="/wiki/Djed" title="Djed">djed</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Was_(sceptre)" title="Was (sceptre)">was</a> symbols have a basis in "<a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">cattle culture</a>", with the ankh representing the <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thoracic</a> <a href="/wiki/Vertebra" title="Vertebra">vertebra</a> of a bull (seen in <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">cross section</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Djed" title="Djed">djed</a> the base or <a href="/wiki/Sacrum" title="Sacrum">sacrum</a> of a bull's spine, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Was_(sceptre)" title="Was (sceptre)">was</a> a staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ankh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ankh appears frequently in Egyptian tomb paintings and other art, often at the fingertips of a god or goddess in images that represent the deities of the <a href="/wiki/Afterlife" title="Afterlife">afterlife</a> conferring the gift of life on the dead person's <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummy</a>; this is thought to symbolize the act of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conception_(biology)" title="Conception (biology)">conception</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Additionally, an ankh was often carried by <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">amulet</a>, either alone, or in connection with two other hieroglyphs that mean "strength" and "health" (see explication of <a href="/wiki/Djed" title="Djed">djed</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Was_(sceptre)" title="Was (sceptre)">was</a>, above).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">Mirrors</a> of beaten metal were also often made in the shape of an ankh, either for decorative reasons or to symbolize a perceived view into another world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A symbol similar to the ankh appears frequently in <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helladic_period" title="Helladic period">Mycenaean</a> sites<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2015)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup>. This is a combination of the <i>sacral knot</i> (symbol of holiness) with the <i>double-edged axe</i> (symbol of matriarchy)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but it can be better compared with the Egyptian <i><a href="/wiki/Tyet" title="Tyet">tyet</a></i> which is similar. This symbol can be recognized on the two famous figurines of the <a href="/wiki/Chthonic" title="Chthonic">chthonian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snake_Goddess" title="Snake Goddess">Snake Goddess</a> discovered in the palace of <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>. Both snake goddesses have a knot with a projecting loop cord between their breasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenean_Greek" title="Mycenean Greek">Mycenean Greek</a>) script, ankh is the phonetic sign <i>za</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The ankh also appeared frequently in coins from <a href="/wiki/History_of_Cyprus" title="History of Cyprus">ancient</a> <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> and Asia Minor (particularly the city of <a href="/wiki/Mallus" title="Mallus">Mallus</a> in Cilicia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In some cases, especially with the early coinage of King Euelthon of <a href="/wiki/Salamis,_Cyprus" title="Salamis, Cyprus">Salamis</a>, the letter <i>ku</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Cypriot_syllabary" title="Cypriot syllabary">Cypriot syllabary</a>, appeared within the circle ankh, representing <i>Ku(prion)</i> (<i>Cypriots</i>). To this day, the ankh is also used to represent the planet <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> (the namesake of which, the goddess <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a>, was chiefly worshipped on the island) and the metal <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> (the heavy mining of which gave Cyprus its name).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coptic_Christians" title="Coptic Christians">Coptic Christians</a> preserved the shape of the ankh by sometimes representing the <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross_variants" title="Christian cross variants">Christian cross</a> with a circle in place of the upper bar. This is known as the Coptic ankh or <b>crux ansata</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNANKH_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNANKH-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
