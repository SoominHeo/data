<p><b>Incisors</b> (from Latin <i>incidere</i>, "to cut") are the front <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">teeth</a> present in most <a href="/wiki/Heterodont" title="Heterodont">heterodont</a> <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. They are located in the <a href="/wiki/Premaxilla" title="Premaxilla">premaxilla</a> above and on the <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a> below. Humans have a total of eight (two on each side, top and bottom). <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">Opossums</a> have 18, whereas <a href="/wiki/Armadillo" title="Armadillo">armadillos</a> have none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Incisor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adult <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> normally have eight incisors, two of each type. The types of incisor are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maxillary_central_incisor" title="Maxillary central incisor">maxillary central incisor</a> (upper jaw, closest to the center of the lips)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maxillary_lateral_incisor" title="Maxillary lateral incisor">maxillary lateral incisor</a> (upper jaw, beside the maxillary central incisor)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mandibular_central_incisor" title="Mandibular central incisor">mandibular central incisor</a> (lower jaw, closest to the center of the lips)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mandibular_lateral_incisor" title="Mandibular lateral incisor">mandibular lateral incisor</a> (lower jaw, beside the mandibular central incisor)</li>
</ul>
<p>Children with a full set of <a href="/wiki/Deciduous_teeth" title="Deciduous teeth">deciduous teeth</a> (primary teeth) also have eight incisors, named the same way as in <a href="/wiki/Permanent_teeth" title="Permanent teeth">permanent teeth</a>. Young children may have from zero to eight incisors depending on the stage of their <a href="/wiki/Tooth_eruption" title="Tooth eruption">tooth eruption</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tooth_development" title="Tooth development">tooth development</a>. Typically, the mandibular central incisors erupt first, followed by the maxillary central incisors, the mandibular lateral incisors and finally the maxillary laterals. The rest of the primary dentition erupts after the incisors. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_molar" title="First molar">first molars</a>, the incisors are also the first permanent teeth to erupt, following the same order as the primary teeth, among themselves.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Incisor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among other animals, the number varies from species to species. <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">Opossums</a> have 18, whereas <a href="/wiki/Armadillo" title="Armadillo">armadillos</a> have none. Cats, dogs, foxes, pigs, and horses have twelve. <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">Rodents</a> have four. Rabbits and hares (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagomorph" title="Lagomorph">lagomorphs</a>) were once considered rodents, but are distinguished by having six — one small pair, called "peg teeth", is located directly behind the most anterior pair. Incisors are used to bite off tough foods, such as red meat.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">Cattle</a> (cows, bulls, etc.) have none on top but a total of six on the bottom.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Incisor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, the incisors are small; biting off meat is done with the <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">canines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carnassial" title="Carnassial">carnassials</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>, the upper incisors are modified into curved <a href="/wiki/Tusk" title="Tusk">tusks</a> (unlike with <a href="/wiki/Narwhal" title="Narwhal">Narwhals</a>, where it is a canine that develops into a straight and twisted tusk).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The incisors of <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> grow throughout life and are worn by gnawing. In humans, the incisors serve to cut off pieces of food, as well as grip other food items.</p>
