<p>The <b>silky anteater</b>, or <b>pygmy anteater</b>, (<i>Cyclopes didactylus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anteater" title="Anteater">anteaters</a> from <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, the only living species in the genus <i><b>Cyclopes</b></i> and the family <a href="/wiki/Cyclopedidae" title="Cyclopedidae">Cyclopedidae</a>. A single extinct cyclopedid genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Palaeomyrmidon" title="Palaeomyrmidon">Palaeomyrmidon</a></i>, known from the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, may be ancestral to the living species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silky_anteater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silky anteaters are the smallest living anteaters, and have proportionately shorter faces and larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranium" title="Cranium">crania</a> than other species. Adults have a total length ranging from 36 to 45Â cm (14 to 18Â in), including a <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> 17 to 24Â cm (6.7 to 9.4Â in) long, and weigh from 175 to 400Â g (6.2 to 14.1Â oz). They have dense and soft <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a>, which ranges from grey to yellowish in colour, with a silvery sheen. Many subspecies have darker, often brownish, streaks, and paler underparts or limbs. The eyes are black, and the soles of the feet are red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scientific name translates roughly as "two-toed circle-foot", and refers to the presence of two <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">claws</a> on the fore feet, and their ability to almost encircle a branch to which the animal is clinging. The claws are present on the <a href="/wiki/Index_finger" title="Index finger">second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_finger" title="Middle finger">third</a> toes, with the latter being much the larger. The fourth toe is very small, and lacks a claw, while the other two toes are vestigial or absent, and are not visible externally. The hind feet have four toes of equal length, each with long claws, and a vestigial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallux" title="Hallux">hallux</a> that is not externally visible. The <a href="/wiki/Rib" title="Rib">ribs</a> are broad and flat, overlapping to form an internal armoured casing that protects the chest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>They have partially <a href="/wiki/Prehensile_tail" title="Prehensile tail">prehensile tails</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silky_anteater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silky anteaters are found from <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, through Central America (except <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>), and south to <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. A smaller, isolated population is also found in the northern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Forest" title="Atlantic Forest">Atlantic Forest</a> of eastern Brazil, and there are also silky anteaters on the island of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>. They inhabit a range of different forest types, including semideciduous, tropical evergreen, and <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forests, from sea level to 1,500Â m (4,900Â ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The seven recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <i>C. didactylus</i> are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>C. d. didactylus</i> <small>Linnaeus, 1758</small> - the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guyanas" title="Guyanas">Guyanas</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, Trinidad, Atlantic Forest</li>
<li><i>C. d. catellus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Oldfield_Thomas" title="Oldfield Thomas">Thomas</a>, 1928</small> - northern Bolivia, southeastern Peru, western Brazil</li>
<li><i>C. d. dorsalis</i> <small><a href="/wiki/John_Edward_Gray" title="John Edward Gray">Gray</a>, 1865</small> - extreme southern Mexico, Central America, northern <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a></li>
<li><i>C. d. eva</i> <small>Thomas, 1902</small> - western Ecuador, southwestern Colombia</li>
<li><i>C. d. ida</i>, <small>Thomas, 1900</small> - western Brazil, eastern Ecuador and Peru</li>
<li><i>C. d. melini</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Einar_L%C3%B6nnberg" title="Einar LÃ¶nnberg">LÃ¶nnberg</a>, 1928</small> - northern Brazil, eastern Colombia</li>
<li><i>C. d. mexicanus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Ned_Hollister" title="Ned Hollister">Hollister</a>, 1914</small> - southern Mexico</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silky_anteater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2261" data-file-width="2036" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG/220px-Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG/330px-Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG/440px-Cyclopes_didactylus_Museum_de_Gen%C3%A8ve.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Silky anteaters are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup> found in lowland rainforests with continuous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a>, where they can move to different places without the need to descend from trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ben-5">[5]</a></sup> They can occur at fairly high densities of 0.77 individuals/ha, for example, in some areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Females have smaller home ranges than males.</p>
<p>The silky anteater is a slow-moving animal and feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, eating between 700 and 5,000 a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miranda2009_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miranda2009-6">[6]</a></sup> Sometimes, it also feeds on other insects, such as <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> and small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccinellid" title="Coccinellid">coccinellid</a> beetles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ben-5">[5]</a></sup> The silky anteater defecates once a day. Some of those feces contain a large quantity of <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> fragments of insects, indicating the silky anteater does not possess either <a href="/wiki/Chitinase" title="Chitinase">chitinase</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chitobiase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chitobiase (page does not exist)">chitobiase</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ben-5">[5]</a></sup> digestive enzymes found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> bats.</p>
<p>It is a solitary animal and gives birth to a single young, up to twice a year. The young are born already furred, and with a similar colour pattern to the adults. They begin to take solid food when they are about one-third of the adult mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup> The young is usually placed inside a nest of dead leaves built in tree holes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ben-5">[5]</a></sup> and left for about eight hours each night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Some authors suggest the silky anteater usually dwells in <a href="/wiki/Bombacaceae" title="Bombacaceae">silk cotton trees</a> (<a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ceiba" title="Ceiba">Ceiba</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wildinfo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wildinfo-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of its resemblance to the seed pod fibers of these trees, it can use the trees as camouflage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ben-5">[5]</a></sup> and avoid attacks of predators such as <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a> and, especially, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harpy_Eagle" title="Harpy Eagle">harpy eagles</a>. During the day, they typically sleep curled up in a ball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunquist1973_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sunquist1973-8">[8]</a></sup> Although they are rarely seen in the forest, they can be found more easily when they are foraging on <a href="/wiki/Liana" title="Liana">lianas</a> at night.</p>
<p>When threatened, the silky anteater, like other anteaters, defends itself by standing on its hind legs and holding its fore feet close to its face so it can strike any animal that tries to get close with its sharp claws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayssen2012_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hayssen2012-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
