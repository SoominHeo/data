<p><b>Anna Nicole Smith</b> (born <b>Vickie Lynn Hogan</b>; November 28, 1967 – February 8, 2007) was an American model, actress, and television personality. Smith first gained popularity in <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i>, winning the title of 1993 <a href="/wiki/Playboy_Playmate" title="Playboy Playmate">Playmate of the Year</a>. She modeled for fashion companies including <a href="/wiki/Guess_(clothing)" title="Guess (clothing)">Guess</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%26M" title="H&amp;M">H&amp;M</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heatherette" title="Heatherette">Heatherette</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lane_Bryant" title="Lane Bryant">Lane Bryant</a>.</p>
<p>Smith dropped out of high school and was married three years later in 1985. Her highly publicized second marriage to <a href="/wiki/J._Howard_Marshall" title="J. Howard Marshall">J. Howard Marshall</a> resulted in speculation that she married the octogenarian mainly for his money, which she denied. Following Marshall's death, Smith began a lengthy legal battle over a share of his estate. Her case, <i><a href="/wiki/Marshall_v._Marshall" title="Marshall v. Marshall">Marshall v. Marshall</a></i>, reached the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">U.S. Supreme Court</a> on a question of <a href="/wiki/Federal_jurisdiction_(United_States)" title="Federal jurisdiction (United States)">federal jurisdiction</a>, and again on a question of bankruptcy court authority (now called <i><a href="/wiki/Stern_v._Marshall" title="Stern v. Marshall">Stern v. Marshall</a></i>).</p>
<p>Smith died on February 8, 2007, in a <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood, Florida</a>, hotel room as a result of an overdose of prescription drugs. During the final six months of her life, Smith was the focus of renewed press coverage surrounding the death of her son, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Wayne_Smith" title="Daniel Wayne Smith">Daniel</a>, and the paternity and custody battle over her newborn daughter, <a href="/wiki/Dannielynn_Birkhead" title="Dannielynn Birkhead">Dannielynn</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born <b>Vickie Lynn Hogan</b> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>, and raised in <a href="/wiki/Mexia,_Texas" title="Mexia, Texas">Mexia, Texas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tbi1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tbi1-2">[2]</a></sup> Smith was the daughter of Donald Eugene Hogan (1947 – 2009) and Virgie Mae Arthur (<i>née</i> Tabers; b. 1951),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tbi1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tbi1-2">[2]</a></sup> who married on February 22, 1967<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancestry_2005_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry_2005-3">[3]</a></sup> and divorced on November 4, 1969. She had five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_siblings" title="Half siblings">half siblings</a>: Donna Hogan, David Tacker Jr., Donnie Hogan, Amy Hogan, and Donald Hart. Smith was raised by her mother and aunt. Virgie subsequently married Donald R. Hart in 1971. After Virgie married Donald, Smith changed her name from Vickie Hogan to Nikki Hart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith attended Durkee Elementary School and Aldine Senior High School in Houston. When she was in the ninth grade, she was sent to live with her mother's younger sister, Kay Beall, in Mexia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Mexia_High_School" title="Mexia High School">Mexia High School</a>, Smith failed her <a href="/wiki/Freshman" title="Freshman">freshman</a> year and dropped out of school during her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophomore_year" title="Sophomore year">sophomore</a> year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> While working at Jim's Krispy Fried Chicken in Mexia, Smith met Billy Wayne Smith, who was a cook at the restaurant; the couple married on April 4, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancestry.com-Tx_Marriage_Collection_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry.com-Tx_Marriage_Collection-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-employment_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-employment-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling_career">Modeling career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modeling career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992, Smith appeared on the March cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i> magazine, with her name given as Vickie Smith. She later appeared as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playmate_of_the_Month" title="Playmate of the Month">Playmate of the Month</a> for the May 1992 issue, in a pictorial shot by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Wayda" title="Stephen Wayda">Stephen Wayda</a>.</p>
<p>Smith next secured a contract to replace supermodel <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Schiffer" title="Claudia Schiffer">Claudia Schiffer</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guess_jeans" title="Guess jeans">Guess jeans</a> ad campaign featuring a series of sultry black-and-white photographs. During the Guess campaign Smith changed her name to Anna Nicole Smith. Guess photographers noticed Smith bore a striking resemblance to bombshell <a href="/wiki/Jayne_Mansfield" title="Jayne Mansfield">Jayne Mansfield</a> and showcased her in several Mansfield-inspired photo sessions. In 1993 before Christmas, she modeled for the Swedish clothing company <a href="/wiki/H%26M" title="H&amp;M">H&amp;M</a>. This led to her being pictured on large billboards in Sweden and Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to <i>Playboy</i>, Smith appeared on the cover of German <i><a href="/wiki/Marie_Claire" title="Marie Claire">Marie Claire</a></i> magazine, photographed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lindbergh" title="Peter Lindbergh">Peter Lindbergh</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Arena_(magazine)" title="Arena (magazine)">Arena</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/FHM" title="FHM">FHM</a></i>, and in editorials for <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Bazaar" title="Harper's Bazaar">Harper's Bazaar</a></i>, Italian <i><a href="/wiki/Vogue_(magazine)" title="Vogue (magazine)">Vogue</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/W_(magazine)" title="W (magazine)">W</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Photo_(French_magazine)" title="Photo (French magazine)">Photo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Face_(magazine)" title="The Face (magazine)">The Face</a></i>.</p>
<p>A photograph of Smith was used by <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i> magazine on the cover of its August 22, 1994, issue titled <i>White Trash Nation</i>. In the photo she appears squatting in a short skirt with cowboy boots as she eats chips. In October 1994, her lawyer T. Patrick Freydl initiated a $5 million lawsuit against the magazine claiming unauthorized use of her photo, and that the article damaged her reputation. Her lawyer stated Smith was under the impression that she was being photographed to embody the "all-American girl look", and that they wanted more glamour shots. He further stated the photo the magazine used was taken during a break just for fun. Editor Kurt Anderson said that the photo was one of dozens taken for the cover, further stating "I guess they just found the picture we chose unflattering." The lawsuit was reported to be settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_to_J._Howard_Marshall">Marriage to J. Howard Marshall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage to J. Howard Marshall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While performing in October 1991 at Gigi's, a Houston <a href="/wiki/Strip_club" title="Strip club">strip club</a> later renamed as "Pleasures", Smith met elderly <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil tycoon</a> <a href="/wiki/J._Howard_Marshall" title="J. Howard Marshall">J. Howard Marshall</a>, and they began a relationship. During their two-year affair, he reportedly lavished gifts on her and asked her to marry him several times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> She divorced her husband Billy on February 3, 1993, in Houston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On June 27, 1994, 26-year-old Smith and 89-year-old Marshall were married in Houston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancestry.com-Tx_Marriage_Collection_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ancestry.com-Tx_Marriage_Collection-7">[7]</a></sup> This resulted in a great deal of gossip about her marrying him for his money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> She reportedly never lived with him, never made love with him, or kissed him on the mouth more than ten times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-About-17">[17]</a></sup> Smith, however, maintained that she loved her husband, and age did not matter to her. Thirteen months after his marriage to Smith, Marshall died in Houston on August 4, 1995, at age 90.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inheritance_court_cases">Inheritance court cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inheritance court cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marshall_v._Marshall" title="Marshall v. Marshall">Marshall v. Marshall</a></div>
<p>Within weeks of J. Howard Marshall's death, one of his sons, <a href="/wiki/E._Pierce_Marshall" title="E. Pierce Marshall">E. Pierce Marshall</a>, disputed her claim for half of her late husband's US$1.6 billion estate. She temporarily joined forces with J. Howard's other son, James Howard Marshall III, whom the elder Howard had disowned. Howard III claimed that J. Howard Marshall had verbally promised him a portion of the estate; like Smith, Howard III was also left out of J. Howard's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Will_(law)" title="Will (law)">will</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The case had gone on for more than a <a href="/wiki/Decade" title="Decade">decade</a>, producing a highly publicized court battle in Texas and several judicial decisions that have gone both for and against Smith in that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, Smith filed for bankruptcy in California as a result of a $850,000 judgment against her for sexual <a href="/wiki/Harassment" title="Harassment">harassment</a> of an employee. As any money potentially due to her from the Marshall estate was part of her potential assets, the bankruptcy court involved itself in the matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith claimed that J. Howard had orally promised her half of his estate if she married him. In September 2000, a Los Angeles bankruptcy judge awarded her $449,754,134. In July 2001, <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> judge Mike Wood affirmed the jury findings in the probate case by ruling that Smith was entitled to nothing. The judge ordered Smith to pay over $1 million in fees and expenses to Pierce's legal team. The conflict between the Texas probate court and California bankruptcy court judgments forced the matter into federal court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2002, a federal judge vacated the California bankruptcy court's ruling and issued a new ruling but reduced the award to $88 million. In December 2004, a three-judge panel of the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9th_Circuit_Court_of_Appeals" title="9th Circuit Court of Appeals">9th Circuit Court of Appeals</a> reversed the March 2002 decision, on the reasoning that the federal courts lacked jurisdiction to overrule this probate court decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">U.S. Supreme Court</a> decided in September 2005 to hear the appeal of that decision. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_administration" title="George W. Bush administration">Bush administration</a> subsequently directed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Solicitor_General" title="United States Solicitor General">Solicitor General</a> to intercede on Smith's behalf out of an interest to expand federal court jurisdiction over state probate disputes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> After months of waiting, Smith and her stepson Pierce learned of the Supreme Court's decision on May 1, 2006. The justices unanimously decided in favor of Smith; Justice <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Bader_Ginsburg" title="Ruth Bader Ginsburg">Ruth Bader Ginsburg</a> wrote the majority opinion. The decision did not give Smith a portion of her husband's estate, but affirmed her right to pursue a share of it in federal court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytmay_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytmay-24">[24]</a></sup> On June 20, 2006, <a href="/wiki/E._Pierce_Marshall" title="E. Pierce Marshall">E. Pierce Marshall</a> died at age 67 from an "aggressive infection". Following his death, his widow, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elaine_T._Marshall" title="Elaine T. Marshall">Elaine T. Marshall</a>, pursued the case on behalf of his estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The case was remanded to the 9th Circuit to adjudicate the remaining appellate issues not previously resolved.</p>
<p>After Smith's death, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> reported that the case over the Marshall fortune "is likely to continue in the name of Ms. Smith's infant daughter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The current situation is that Anna Nicole Smith's estate will not inherit any of her late husband's estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Following the decision by the Appeals Court for the Ninth Circuit, lawyers for the estate of Anna Nicole Smith requested the appeal be heard before the entire 9th circuit. However, on May 6, 2010 the appeal was denied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On September 28, 2010, the U.S. Supreme Court again agreed to hear the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 23, 2011, the U.S. Supreme Court issued a ruling against the estate of Anna Nicole Smith, holding that a bankruptcy court ruling giving her estate a sum of $475 million was decided without <a href="/wiki/Subject-matter_jurisdiction" title="Subject-matter jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> (now called <i><a href="/wiki/Stern_v._Marshall" title="Stern v. Marshall">Stern v. Marshall</a></i>). A California bankruptcy court awarded Smith part of the estate, but the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeal said that a bankruptcy court could not make a decision on an issue outside bankruptcy law. The U.S. Supreme Court agreed with the ruling of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">9th Circuit Court of Appeals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2014, a federal judge in <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_California" title="Orange County, California">Orange County, California</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_O._Carter" title="David O. Carter">David O. Carter</a> rejected the effort to obtain about $44 million from the estate of J. Howard Marshall. U.S. District Court Judge Carter also denied request from Smith's estate to sanction the estate of Marshall's son, E. Pierce Marshall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television_career">Film and television career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film and television career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite her success as a model, Smith never found the same recognition or success as an actress. A twenty-six-year-old Smith debuted on the screen in the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Screwball_comedy_film" title="Screwball comedy film">screwball comedy film</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hudsucker_Proxy" title="The Hudsucker Proxy">The Hudsucker Proxy</a></i>. She appeared as Za-Za, a flirtatious celebrity who flirts with the lead character, played by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Robbins" title="Tim Robbins">Tim Robbins</a>, in a barbershop scene. Smith was next given a larger role as Tanya Peters in <i><a href="/wiki/Naked_Gun_33%E2%85%93:_The_Final_Insult" title="Naked Gun 33⅓: The Final Insult">Naked Gun 33⅓: The Final Insult</a></i> (1994), which was released seven days after her initial film debut. Her role in the film, that of a pivotal contact to a crime, earned her favorable reviews and the film enjoyed box office success. Despite the publicity for her performance in both films, they each did very little to improve her acting career.</p>
<p>Smith wanted to be taken more seriously as an actress, but Hollywood studios were reluctant. Her persona of a ditzy dumb blonde was compressed heavily in her film roles, which sought only to market her physical assets. In an attempt to earn acting respect, Smith agreed to appear in <i><a href="/wiki/To_the_Limit_(1995_film)" title="To the Limit (1995 film)">To the Limit</a></i> (1995), which would be her first starring role. In the film she played Colette Dubois, a retired spy seeking revenge on the murderer of her late husband. Although the film was highly publicized and boasted a lavish budget and script, Smith's performance drew negative reviews and it was an ultimate <a href="/wiki/Box_office_bomb" title="Box office bomb">box office bomb</a>. It offered Smith's first and only venture in a mainstream Hollywood leading role.</p>
<p>By 1996, Smith's acting career had declined considerably. After the failure of her previous motion picture, Hollywood studios began to realize her public popularity did not affect her acting abilities or ticket sales at the box office. She appeared as herself in the 1995 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilot_episode" title="Pilot episode">pilot episode</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Naked_Truth_(TV_series)" title="The Naked Truth (TV series)">The Naked Truth</a></i>. Smith attempted to revitalize her film career with a leading role in <i><a href="/wiki/Skyscraper_(1996_film)" title="Skyscraper (1996 film)">Skyscraper</a></i> in 1996. The low-budget, <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">direct-to-video</a> film offered Smith no more than "soft-core exploitation" and her movie career again stalled.</p>
<p>In the late 1990s, Smith focused her acting career on television. She appeared on the variety series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sin_City_Spectacular" title="Sin City Spectacular">Sin City Spectacular</a></i> in 1998. That same year, Smith appeared in the tell-all self-promoting film, <i>Anna Nicole Smith: Exposed</i>, which was based on several photo sessions during her Playboy career. She appeared as Donna, the friend of Veronica Chase played by <a href="/wiki/Kirstie_Alley" title="Kirstie Alley">Kirstie Alley</a>, on the sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Veronica%27s_Closet" title="Veronica's Closet">Veronica's Closet</a></i> in 1999. Smith guest-starred as Myra Jacobs in a 1999 episode of the surreal series, <i><a href="/wiki/Ally_McBeal" title="Ally McBeal">Ally McBeal</a></i>.</p>
<p>In the early 2000s, Smith had very few acting roles. As a result of her rising popularity with tabloids and gossip columnists, Smith was given her own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reality_show" title="Reality show">reality show</a> on the <a href="/wiki/E!" title="E!">E!</a> cable network. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Anna_Nicole_Show" title="The Anna Nicole Show">The Anna Nicole Show</a></i> premiered on August 4, 2002, achieving the highest cable rating ever issued for a reality show. Coincidentally, the day the series premiered was the seventh anniversary of the death of <a href="/wiki/J._Howard_Marshall" title="J. Howard Marshall">J. Howard Marshall</a>. The series attempted to focus on the private life of Smith, her boyfriend/attorney <a href="/wiki/Howard_K._Stern" title="Howard K. Stern">Howard K. Stern</a>, her son <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Wayne_Smith" title="Daniel Wayne Smith">Daniel</a>, her assistant Kimberly "Kimmie" Walther, her miniature poodle Sugar Pie, her gay interior decorator <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Trendy" title="Bobby Trendy">Bobby Trendy</a>, and her cousin from Texas, Shelly Cloud.</p>
<p>Despite the popularity of the show amongst college students and pop culture fans, the show declined considerably in viewership at the end of its first season. It was, however, renewed for a second season, before being cancelled on June 1, 2003, after two seasons and twenty-seven episodes. E! announced to the press that the series ended because of "creative differences" between the network and Smith, although she acknowledged the series ended because the network had lost interest in both her and the show.</p>
<p>Smith returned to film acting in 2003 with the comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Wasabi_Tuna" title="Wasabi Tuna">Wasabi Tuna</a></i>. In the film she played an over-the-top version of herself, whose miniature poodle Sugar Pie is stolen from her on Halloween by a team of <a href="/wiki/Drag_queen" title="Drag queen">drag queens</a> dressed like her. Neither the film nor her performance drew positive reviews. In 2005, she briefly appeared as a spectator at a basketball game in <i><a href="/wiki/Be_Cool" title="Be Cool">Be Cool</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">John Travolta</a>. In late 2005 she agreed to play Lucy in the self-produced independent parody film <i><a href="/wiki/Illegal_Aliens_(film)" title="Illegal Aliens (film)">Illegal Aliens</a></i>, alongside wrestler Joani "<a href="/wiki/Chyna" title="Chyna">Chyna</a>" Laurer. It attempted to parody several popular television shows from the 1970s and 1980s as well as several film franchises. It would be released direct-to-video on May 1, 2007, three months after her death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smith_as_spokeswoman">Smith as spokeswoman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Smith as spokeswoman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an interview on <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Conan_O%27Brien" title="Late Night with Conan O'Brien">Late Night with Conan O'Brien</a></i>, Smith was asked what her "Playmate diet" consisted of. She instantly replied, "fried chicken". In October 2003, she became a spokeswoman for <a href="/wiki/TrimSpa" title="TrimSpa">TrimSpa</a>, which helped her lose a reported 69 pounds (31 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weightloss1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-weightloss1-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2004, she appeared at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Music_Awards" title="American Music Awards">American Music Awards</a> to introduce Kanye West and attracted attention because of her slurred speech and behavior. During her live appearance, she threw her arms up and exclaimed, "Like my body?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draw_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-draw-33">[33]</a></sup> Smith murmured other comments and alluded to TrimSpa. The incident became comic material for presenters throughout the rest of the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following day, her appearance was featured in the media. <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">Tabloids</a> speculated that Smith was under the influence of pills or some other controlled substance. Her representatives explained that she was in pain due to a series of grueling workouts.</p>
<p>In March 2005, at the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Australia_Video_Music_Awards" title="MTV Australia Video Music Awards">MTV Australia Video Music Awards</a> in Sydney's <a href="/wiki/Luna_Park_Sydney" title="Luna Park Sydney">Luna Park</a>, she did <a href="/wiki/Janet_Jackson" title="Janet Jackson">Janet Jackson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Wardrobe_malfunction" title="Wardrobe malfunction">wardrobe malfunction</a> by pulling down her dress to reveal both breasts, each covered with the MTV logo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith was also featured in advertisements for the animal rights group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETA" title="PETA">PETA</a>. Spoofing Marilyn Monroe's "Diamonds Are a Girl's Best Friend" segment in <i><a href="/wiki/Gentlemen_Prefer_Blondes_(1953_film)" title="Gentlemen Prefer Blondes (1953 film)">Gentlemen Prefer Blondes</a></i>, a 2004 ad states "Gentlemen prefer fur-free blondes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Due to her support of the anti-fur movement, in particular her criticism of Canadian <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">seal hunting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETA" title="PETA">PETA</a> began a petition in memory of Smith to the Canadian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a> to end the annual tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In another ad the following year, Smith posed with her dogs in a campaign against <a href="/wiki/Iams" title="Iams">Iams</a> dog food for their alleged cruelty to animals, as well as the manufacturer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procter_and_Gamble" title="Procter and Gamble">Procter and Gamble</a>, and sister company <a href="/wiki/Eukanuba" title="Eukanuba">Eukanuba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smith was addicted to prescription medications.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrist" title="Psychiatrist">Psychiatrist</a> Dr. Nathalie Maullin said she met Smith in April 2006 in <a href="/wiki/Cedars-Sinai_Medical_Center" title="Cedars-Sinai Medical Center">Cedars-Sinai Medical Center</a>. Maullin said Smith had <a href="/wiki/Borderline_personality_disorder" title="Borderline personality disorder">borderline personality disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_daughter">Birth of daughter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Birth of daughter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dannielynn_Birkhead_paternity_case" title="Dannielynn Birkhead paternity case">Dannielynn Birkhead paternity case</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Smith announced on June 1, 2006, in a video clip posted on her official website that she was pregnant. "Let me stop all the rumors," she said, while floating on an inflatable raft in a swimming pool. "Yes, I am pregnant. I'm happy, I'm very, very happy about it. Everything's goin' really, really good and I'll be checking in and out periodically on the web, and I'll let you see me as I'm growing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though her announcement did not provide any details, in an interview with <a href="/wiki/Larry_King" title="Larry King">Larry King</a> on <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Larry_King_Live" title="Larry King Live">Larry King Live</a></i> after her daughter's birth and her son's death, Smith's longtime personal attorney <a href="/wiki/Howard_K._Stern" title="Howard K. Stern">Howard K. Stern</a> said that he and Smith had been in a secret relationship for "a very long time" and that, due to the timing of the pregnancy, he was confident that he was the father of the baby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Entertainment photographer <a href="/wiki/Larry_Birkhead" title="Larry Birkhead">Larry Birkhead</a>, steadfastly maintained that he was the baby's father and filed a lawsuit to establish paternity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Smith's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Dannielynn_Birkhead" title="Dannielynn Birkhead">Dannielynn Hope Marshall Stern</a>, was born September 7, 2006, at Doctors Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau, Bahamas</a>. The Bahamian <a href="/wiki/Birth_certificate" title="Birth certificate">birth certificate</a> recorded the father as Howard K. Stern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>A judge in the United States ordered that <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> tests be performed to determine the biological father of Dannielynn. Following Smith's death, Debra Opri, the lawyer for Larry Birkhead, asked for an emergency DNA sample to be taken from her body. Smith's lawyer, Ron Rale objected strongly to this request.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The request was denied by a judge, who instead ordered Smith's body preserved until February 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a story published in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i>, Donna Hogan, Smith's younger half-sister, has said that the model froze the sperm of her second husband, Marshall, prior to his death. The newspaper said that Hogan wrote in her unpublished manuscript about her sister, entitled <i>Train Wreck</i>, that "To her family, she hinted that she had used the old man's frozen sperm, and would be giving birth to Howard Marshall's child".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> However, the publisher of Hogan's book described the newspaper's claims as a hoax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On February 9, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Zsa_Zsa_Gabor" title="Zsa Zsa Gabor">Zsa Zsa Gabor</a>'s husband <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Prinz_von_Anhalt" title="Frédéric Prinz von Anhalt">Frédéric Prinz von Anhalt</a> said that he had had a decade-long affair with Smith and could potentially be the father of her infant girl, Dannielynn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Alexander Denk, a former bodyguard for Anna Nicole Smith, reportedly told the tabloid television program <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra_(TV_series)" title="Extra (TV series)">Extra</a></i> that he had had an affair with his former employer, and that it was possible he could be Dannielynn's father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Smith's death, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TMZ.com" title="TMZ.com">TMZ.com</a></i> reported that Smith had been given a prescription for <a href="/wiki/Methadone" title="Methadone">methadone</a> under a false name while she was in her eighth month of pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Medical Board of California launched a review into the matter; the prescribing doctor, Sandeep Kapoor, said his treatment was "sound and appropriate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 10, 2007, a Bahamian judge ruled Larry Birkhead, photographer, as the father of Dannielynn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> DNA tests had established Birkhead as the father, with 99.99% certainty. Commenting on the revelation, Birkhead stated, "I hate to be the one to tell you this but, I told you so. I'm the father...My baby's going to be coming home pretty soon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Birkhead subsequently applied for an amended birth certificate listing him as the father, which paved the way for him to obtain a passport for the baby to leave with him for the United States. Howard K. Stern did not contest the DNA results or the ruling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Subsequent to the ruling, Birkhead returned to the United States with the baby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Smith's mother, Virgie Arthur, appealed the ruling but it was later denied and she was ordered to pay costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_son">Death of son</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death of son">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Wayne_Smith" title="Daniel Wayne Smith">Daniel Wayne Smith</a></div>
<p>Smith's 20-year-old son <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Wayne_Smith" title="Daniel Wayne Smith">Daniel Smith</a>, died on September 10, 2006, in his mother's hospital room while visiting her and his half sister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> After the coroner labeled the death "reserved", Smith hired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic_pathologist" title="Forensic pathologist">forensic pathologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Cyril_Wecht" title="Cyril Wecht">Cyril Wecht</a> to perform a second <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daniel's <a href="/wiki/Death_certificate" title="Death certificate">death certificate</a> was issued on September 21, 2006, so that he could be buried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> While Smith remained in the Bahamas with Dannielynn and Stern, Daniel's family in the United States, including his father, Billy Smith, gathered with friends on October 7, 2006, in <a href="/wiki/Mexia,_Texas" title="Mexia, Texas">Mexia, Texas</a>, for a <a href="/wiki/Funeral" title="Funeral">memorial service</a>. Daniel was buried at Lake View Cemetery on <a href="/wiki/New_Providence" title="New Providence">New Providence</a>, Bahamas, on October 19, 2006, almost six weeks after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> According to Howard K. Stern, Smith's longtime boyfriend, she was devastated over her son's death. "Anna and Daniel were inseparable. Daniel was without question the most important person in Anna's life," Stern told Florida Circuit Judge <a href="/wiki/Larry_Seidlin" title="Larry Seidlin">Larry Seidlin</a> during his testimony in Smith's body custody trial: "At Daniel's funeral, she had them open the coffin and tried to climb inside. She said that 'if Daniel has to be buried, I want to be buried with him,'" Stern testified. "She was ready to go down with him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Howard K. Stern revealed that "Anna saw herself as both mother and father to Daniel. From the time I met her, everything was for Daniel. I would say that physically, she died last week, but in a lot of ways, emotionally she died when Daniel died," he added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dr. Wecht announced on <i><a href="/wiki/Larry_King_Live" title="Larry King Live">Larry King Live</a></i> that the autopsy he performed showed that Daniel died from a lethal combination of antidepressants <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoloft" title="Zoloft">Zoloft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexapro" title="Lexapro">Lexapro</a> and painkiller <a href="/wiki/Methadone" title="Methadone">methadone</a>. Dr. Wecht explained that methadone is used in the treatment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heroin_addiction" title="Heroin addiction">heroin addiction and chronic pain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commitment_ceremony_with_Stern">Commitment ceremony with Stern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commitment ceremony with Stern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 28, 2006, Smith and <a href="/wiki/Howard_K._Stern" title="Howard K. Stern">Howard K. Stern</a> exchanged their vows and rings in an informal commitment ceremony aboard the 41-foot (12 m) <a href="/wiki/Catamaran" title="Catamaran">catamaran</a> Margaritaville off the coast of the Bahamas. She wore a white dress and carried a bouquet of red roses, while he wore a black suit with white shirt. Although they pledged their love and made a commitment to be there for each other before a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist_minister" title="Baptist minister">Baptist minister</a>, no marriage certificate was issued and the ceremony was not legally binding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the ceremony, they landed on the island of Sandy Cay where they had a party and celebrated with champagne and <a href="/wiki/Apple_cider" title="Apple cider">apple cider</a> that had been brought over for the occasion by sailboat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-About-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding the questionable timing of the ceremony, Smith's attorney in Nassau, stated, "They needed a little adrenaline boost because things have been so hectic and devastating in their life recently."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The photos of their ceremony were sold through <a href="/wiki/Getty_Images" title="Getty Images">Getty Images</a> to <i><a href="/wiki/People_(magazine)" title="People (magazine)">People</a></i> magazine for around $1,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Residency_in_the_Bahamas">Residency in the Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Residency in the Bahamas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Smith and Stern were reportedly staying in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> to avoid <a href="/wiki/DNA_paternity_testing" title="DNA paternity testing">paternity testing</a> of her daughter in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In late 2006, Smith was granted permanent resident status in the Bahamas by Immigration Minister <a href="/wiki/Shane_Gibson_(politician)" title="Shane Gibson (politician)">Shane Gibson</a>. On February 11, 2007, newspaper photographs were published showing Smith lying clothed in bed in an embrace with Gibson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Opposition politicians in the Bahamas accused the minister of improper behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Gibson resigned as a result of the controversy and claimed that the photos, taken by Stern, were innocent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The basis of Smith's permanent residency status was the claim that she owned a $900,000 mansion, which she said was given to her by a former boyfriend, real estate developer <a href="/wiki/Dannielynn_Birkhead_paternity_case" title="Dannielynn Birkhead paternity case">Gaither Ben Thompson</a> of South Carolina. Thompson asserted that he loaned Smith the finances to purchase the property, but that she failed to repay the loan, and was attempting to regain control of the property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Thompson sued to evict Smith from the property in Bahama Court, and received a <a href="/wiki/Default_judgment" title="Default judgment">default judgment</a> against her when she failed to respond to the eviction, or appear in court on November 28, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Ford Shelley, son-in-law of G. Ben Thompson, claimed that <a href="/wiki/Methadone" title="Methadone">methadone</a> was found in Anna's bedroom refrigerator while the mansion was being reclaimed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> A photograph provided by TMZ shows a large bottle of methadone along with vials of injectable vitamin B12 (<a href="/wiki/Cyanocobalamin" title="Cyanocobalamin">cyanocobalamin</a>) in her refrigerator and diet product Slimfast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_funeral">Death and funeral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death and funeral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 8, 2007, Smith was found dead in Room 607 at the <a href="/wiki/Hard_Rock_Cafe#Acquisition_by_the_Seminole_Tribe_of_Florida" title="Hard Rock Cafe">Seminole</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_Rock_Hotel_and_Casino" title="Hard Rock Hotel and Casino">Hard Rock Hotel and Casino</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Tasma Brighthaupt, a friend of Smith who was a trained emergency nurse, performed <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">CPR</a> for 15 minutes until her husband, Maurice "Big Moe" Brighthaupt, Smith's friend and bodyguard,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANS_Final_24_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANS_Final_24-76">[76]</a></sup> took over CPR. He had driven back to the hotel after being notified by his wife of Smith's condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANS_Final_24_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-ANS_Final_24-76">[76]</a></sup> According to Seminole Police Chief Charlie Tiger, at 1:38 p.m. (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">EST</a>) Maurice Brighthaupt, who was also a trained paramedic, called the hotel front desk from her sixth-floor room. The front desk in turn called security, who then called <a href="/wiki/9-1-1" title="9-1-1">911</a>. At 1:45 p.m. (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">EST</a>) the bodyguard administered <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">CPR</a> until paramedics arrived and Smith was rushed to Memorial Regional Hospital at 2:10 p.m. (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">EST</a>) and pronounced <a href="/wiki/Dead_on_arrival" title="Dead on arrival">dead on arrival</a> at 2:49 p.m. (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">EST</a>)</p>
<p>A phone call was released to the public on February 13, 2007, involving Seminole police and the local 911 operators, saying, "We need assistance to Room 607 at the Hard Rock. It's in reference to a white female. She's not breathing and not responsive... actually, it's Anna Nicole Smith."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a seven-week investigation led by Broward County Medical Examiner and Forensic Pathologist Dr. <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Perper" title="Joshua Perper">Joshua Perper</a> in combination with the Seminole police and several independent forensic pathologists and toxicologists, Dr. Perper announced that Smith died of "<a href="/wiki/Combined_drug_intoxication" title="Combined drug intoxication">combined drug intoxication</a>" with the sleeping medication <a href="/wiki/Chloral_hydrate" title="Chloral hydrate">chloral hydrate</a> as the "major component."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> No illegal drugs were found in her system. The official report states that her death was not considered to be due to homicide, suicide, or natural causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The full investigative report has been made public and can be found online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Additionally, an official copy of the autopsy report was publicly released on March 26, 2007, and can be found <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">online</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her death was ultimately ruled an accidental drug overdose of the sedative <a href="/wiki/Chloral_hydrate" title="Chloral hydrate">chloral hydrate</a> that became increasingly lethal when combined with other prescription drugs in her system, specifically four <a href="/wiki/Benzodiazepine" title="Benzodiazepine">benzodiazepines</a>: Klonopin (<a href="/wiki/Clonazepam" title="Clonazepam">Clonazepam</a>), Ativan (<a href="/wiki/Lorazepam" title="Lorazepam">Lorazepam</a>), Serax (<a href="/wiki/Oxazepam" title="Oxazepam">Oxazepam</a>), and Valium (<a href="/wiki/Diazepam" title="Diazepam">Diazepam</a>). Furthermore, she had taken Benadryl (<a href="/wiki/Diphenhydramine" title="Diphenhydramine">Diphenhydramine</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topamax" title="Topamax">Topamax</a> (Topiramate), an anticonvulsant AMPA/Kainate antagonist, which likely contributed to the sedative effect of chloral hydrate and the benzodiazepines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Although the individual levels of any of the benzodiazepines in her system would not have been sufficient to cause death, their combination with a high dose of chloral hydrate led to her overdose. The autopsy report indicates that chloral hydrate was the "toxic/lethal" drug, but it is difficult to know whether chloral hydrate ingestion alone would have killed her, since Dr. Perper indicated (in the March 26 press conference) that she had built up a tolerance to the drug and took more than the average person. He indicated that she took about three tablespoons, whereas the normal dosage is between one and two teaspoons. Chloral hydrate, first synthesized in 1832, was the first depressant developed for the specific purpose of inducing sleep. The infamous "Mickey Finn" or "knockout drops" was a solution of alcohol and chloral hydrate that was popular in Victorian England and in that era's literature. When used properly, and without the introduction of alcohol or other depressants, chloral hydrate is effective in easing sleeplessness due to pain or insomnia. But according to Avis (1990), the effective dose and lethal dose of chloral hydrate are so close that the sedative should be considered dangerous. Today, the use of chloral hydrate has declined as other agents, including barbiturates and benzodiazepines, have largely replaced them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Despite rumors of methadone use due to its involvement in Smith's son's death, Dr. Perper only found methadone in her <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a>, indicating it could only have been ingested 2–3 days prior to her death and therefore was not a contributing factor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The autopsy report indicates that abscesses on her buttocks (presumably from prior injections of vitamin B12 in the form of <a href="/wiki/Cyanocobalamin" title="Cyanocobalamin">cyanocobalamin</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_growth_hormone" title="Human growth hormone">human growth hormone</a>), and viral <a href="/wiki/Enteritis" title="Enteritis">enteritis</a> were contributory causes of death. Tests for influenza A and B were negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was reported that 8 of the 11 drugs in Smith's system, including the chloral hydrate, were prescribed to <a href="/wiki/Howard_K._Stern" title="Howard K. Stern">Howard K. Stern</a>, not Smith. Additionally, two of the prescriptions were written for Alex Katz and one was written for Smith's friend and psychiatrist, Dr. Khristine Elaine Eroshevich. Dr. Perper acknowledged that all 11 prescriptions were written by Dr. Eroshevich herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith's funeral took place March 2, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Before Smith's body was buried, it began <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposing</a> at a faster-than-normal pace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Possible factors in her more rapid decomposition were the drugs found in her body in the <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a>, the fact that the legal battles delayed her <a href="/wiki/Embalming" title="Embalming">embalming</a> until a week after her death, and the nearly month-long wait for her burial in the warm Bahama weather. This resulted in the family having a closed-casket funeral.</p>
<p>Smith's will in April 2001 named her son Daniel as the sole beneficiary of her estate, specifically excluded other children, and named Howard K. Stern as the executor. It indicated personal property valued at $10,000 and real property valued at $1.8 million (with a $1.1 million mortgage) at the time of death. A petition to probate Smith's will was filed in Los Angeles County Superior Court. The petition to probate lists Larry Birkhead as a party with interest to Anna's estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> A six-foot-long black granite monument was installed at Smith's grave in the Bahamas in February 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Nicole_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The opera <i><a href="/wiki/Anna_Nicole" title="Anna Nicole">Anna Nicole</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Mark-Anthony_Turnage" title="Mark-Anthony Turnage">Mark-Anthony Turnage</a> premiered on February 17, 2011, at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Royal Opera House</a>, to mixed reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Barone" title="Richard Barone">Richard Barone</a> released "(She's A Real) Live Wire," inspired by Smith and featured in the documentary <i>Addicted to Fame</i> about the making of Smith's last film and her final days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itunes_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-itunes-99">[99]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
