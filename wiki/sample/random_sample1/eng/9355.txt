<p><b>Animatronics</b> refers to the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robotic" title="Robotic">robotic</a> devices to emulate a human or an animal, or bring lifelike characteristics to an otherwise inanimate object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsa_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tsa-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A robot designed to be a convincing imitation of a human is more specifically labeled as an <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">android</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-android-defined_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-android-defined-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishiguro_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ishiguro-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Modern animatronics have found widespread applications in <a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">movie special effects</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_parks" title="Theme parks">theme parks</a> and have, since their inception, been primarily used as a spectacle of amusement.</p>
<p>Animatronics is a multi-disciplinary field which integrates <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>, robots, <a href="/wiki/Mechatronics" title="Mechatronics">mechatronics</a>, and puppetry resulting in lifelike animation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shooter_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-shooter-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animatronics-defined_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-animatronics-defined-7">[7]</a></sup> Animatronic figures are often powered by pneumatics, hydraulics, and/or by electrical means, and can be implemented using both computer control and human control, including <a href="/wiki/Teleoperation" title="Teleoperation">teleoperation</a>. Motion actuators are often used to imitate muscle movements and create realistic motions in limbs. Figures are covered with body shells and flexible skins made of hard and soft plastic materials and finished with details like colors, hair and feathers and other components to make the figure more realistic.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Animatronics</i> is <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <i>animate</i> and <i>electronics</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>audio-animatronics</i> was coined by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> when he started developing animatronics for entertainment and film. Audio-Animatronics does not differentiate between animatronics and androids.</p>
<p><i>Autonomatronics</i> was also defined by Walt Disney Imagineers, to describe a more advanced audio-animatronic technology featuring cameras and complex sensors to process information around the character's environment and respond to that stimulus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-advancements_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-advancements-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="686" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg/220px-Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg/330px-Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg/440px-Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Al-Jazari_-_A_Musical_Toy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Al-Jazari's toy boat, musical automata</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The 3rd-century BC text of the <i><a href="/wiki/Liezi" title="Liezi">Liezi</a></i> describes an encounter between <a href="/wiki/King_Mu_of_Zhou" title="King Mu of Zhou">King Mu of Zhou</a> and an 'artificer' known as Yan Shi, who presented the king with a life-size automaton. The 'figure' was described as able to walk, pose and sing, and when dismantled was observed to consist of anatomically accurate organs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinese_science_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinese_science-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 5th-century BC <a href="/wiki/Mohism" title="Mohism">Mohist</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Mozi" title="Mozi">Mozi</a> and his contemporary Lu Ban are attributed with the invention of artificial wooden birds (ma yuan) that could successfully fly in the Han Fei Zi<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_2_54_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_2_54-21">[21]</a></sup> and in 1066, the Chinese inventor Su Song built a <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">water clock</a> in the form of a tower which featured mechanical figurines which chimed the hours.</p>
<p>In 1515, <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> designed and built the Automata Lion, one of the earliest described animatrons. The mechanical lion was presented by Giuliano de’ Medici of Florence to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francois_I" title="Francois I">Francois I</a>, King of France as a symbol of an alliance between France and Florence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davinci-lion_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-davinci-lion-22">[22]</a></sup> The Automata Lion was rebuilt in 2009 according to contemporary descriptions and da Vinci's own drawings of the mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davinci-lion_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-davinci-lion-22">[22]</a></sup> Prior to this, da Vinci had designed and exhibited a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_knight" title="Mechanical knight">mechanical knight</a> at a celebration hosted by <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Sforza" title="Ludovico Sforza">Ludovico Sforza</a> at the court of Milan in 1495.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-nih-23">[23]</a></sup> The 'robot' was capable of standing, sitting, opening its visor and moving its arms. The drawings were rediscovered in the 1950s and a functional replica was later built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-nih-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_implementations">Early implementations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early implementations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clocks">Clocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1595" data-file-width="1215" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png/220px-Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png/330px-Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png/440px-Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Washstand_by_Philo_of_Byzantium.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Greek washstand automaton of the 3rd century BC</div>
</div>

<p>While functional, early clocks were also designed as novelties and spectacles which integrated features of early animatronics.</p>
<p>Approximately 1220–1230, <a href="/wiki/Villard_de_Honnecourt" title="Villard de Honnecourt">Villard de Honnecourt</a> wrote <i>The Portfolio of Villard de Honnecourt</i> which depicts an early escapement mechanism in a drawing titled <i>How to make an angel keep pointing his finger toward the Sun</i> and an automaton of a bird, with jointed wings which led to their design implementation in clocks. Because of their size and complexity, the majority of these clocks were built as public spectacles in the <a href="/wiki/Town_centre" title="Town centre">town centre</a>. One of the earliest of these large clocks was the Strasbourg Clock, built in the fourteenth century which takes up the entire side of a cathedral wall. It contained an astronomical calendar, automata depicting animals, saints and the life of Christ. The clock still functions to this day but has undergone several restorations since its initial construction. The <a href="/wiki/Prague_astronomical_clock" title="Prague astronomical clock">Prague astronomical clock</a> was built in 1410, animated figures were added from the 17th century onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realhistory_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-realhistory-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3763" data-file-width="5000" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg/220px-Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg/330px-Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg/440px-Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Czech-2013-Prague-Astronomical_clock_face.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Face of the Astronomical Clock, in Old Town Square, Prague</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first description of a modern <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo_clock" title="Cuckoo clock">cuckoo clock</a> was by the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> nobleman <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Hainhofer" title="Philipp Hainhofer">Philipp Hainhofer</a> in 1629.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cuckoo_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-cuckoo-25">[25]</a></sup> The clock belonged to Prince Elector <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/August,_Elector_of_Saxony" title="August, Elector of Saxony">August von Sachsen</a>. By 1650, the workings of mechanical cuckoos were understood and were widely disseminated in <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher" title="Athanasius Kircher">Athanasius Kircher</a>'s handbook on music, <i>Musurgia Universalis</i>. In what is the first documented description of how a mechanical cuckoo works, a mechanical organ with several automated figures is described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 18th-century Germany, clockmakers began making cuckoo clocks for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realhistory_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-realhistory-24">[24]</a></sup> Clock shops selling cuckoo clocks became commonplace in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> region by the middle of the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A banquet in Camilla of Aragon's honor in Italy, 1475, featured a lifelike automated camel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sill2000_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sill2000-28">[28]</a></sup> The spectacle was a part of a larger parade which continued over days.</p>
<p>In 1454, <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Good" title="Philip the Good">Duke Philip</a> created an entertainment show named The extravagant <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Pheasant" title="Feast of the Pheasant">Feast of the Pheasant</a>, which was intended to influence the Duke's peers to participate in a crusade against the Ottomans but ended up being a grand display of automata, giants, and dwarves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Fontana_(engineer)" title="Giovanni Fontana (engineer)">Giovanni Fontana</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Paduan</a> engineer in 1420, developed <i>Bellicorum instrumentorum liber</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[a]</a></sup> which includes a puppet of a camelid driven by a clothed primate twice the height of a human being and an automaton of Mary Magdalene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementations">Implementations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Implementations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_attractions">Modern attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Disney_attractions_using_Audio-Animatronics" title="List of Disney attractions using Audio-Animatronics">List of Disney attractions using Audio-Animatronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Animatronic_attractions" title="Category:Animatronic attractions">Category:Animatronic attractions</a></div>
<p>The earliest modern animatronics can actually be found in old robots. While some of these robots were, in fact, animatronics, at the time they were thought of simply as robots because the term animatronics had yet to become popularized.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="465" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg/220px-Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg/330px-Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg/440px-Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sparko_The_Robot_Dog.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sparko the Robot Dog from 1940s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first animatronics characters to be displayed to the public were a dog and a horse. Each were the attraction at two separate spectacles during the 1939 New York World's Fair. Sparko, The Robot Dog, pet of <a href="/wiki/Elektro" title="Elektro">Elektro</a> the Robot, performs in front of the public at the <a href="/wiki/1939_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1939 New York World's Fair">1939 New York World's Fair</a> but Sparko is not like normal robots. Sparko represents a living animal, thus becoming the very first modern day animatronic character,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sparko_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-sparko-14">[14]</a></sup> along with an unnamed horse which was reported to gallop realistically. The animatronic galloping horse was also on display at the 1939 World's Fair, in a different exhibit than Sparko's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horse_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-horse-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Walt Disney is often credited for popularizing animatronics for entertainment after he bought an animatronic bird while he was vacationing, although it is disputed whether it was in New Orleans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Disney_built_an_animatronic_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Disney_built_an_animatronic-33">[32]</a></sup> or Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Disney's vision for audio-animatronics was primarily focused on patriotic displays rather than amusements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyondlincoln_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-beyondlincoln-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1951, two years after Walt Disney discovered animatronics, he commissioned machinist Roger Broggie and sculptor Wathel Rogers to lead a team tasked with creating a 9" tall figure that could move and talk simulating dance routines performed by actor Buddy Ebsen. The project was titled 'Project Little Man' but was never finished. A year later, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Imagineering" title="Walt Disney Imagineering">Walt Disney Imagineering</a> was created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-museum-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>After "Project Little Man", the Imagineering team at Disney's first project was a "Chinese Head" which was on display in the lobby of their office. Customers could ask the head questions and it would reply with words of wisdom. The eyes blinked and its mouth opened and closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-museum-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Walt Disney Production company started using animatronics in 1955 for <a href="/wiki/Disneyland" title="Disneyland">Disneyland</a>'s ride, the <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Cruise" title="Jungle Cruise">Jungle Cruise</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Animating_with_Animatronics_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Animating_with_Animatronics-37">[36]</a></sup> and later for its attraction <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney%27s_Enchanted_Tiki_Room" title="Walt Disney's Enchanted Tiki Room">Walt Disney's Enchanted Tiki Room</a> which featured animatronic Enchanted Tiki Birds.</p>
<p>The first fully completed human audio-animatronic figure was <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>, created by Walt Disney in 1964 for the <a href="/wiki/1964_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1964 New York World's Fair">1964 World's Fair in the New York</a>. In 1965, Disney upgraded the figure and coined it as the Lincoln Mark II, which appeared at the Opera House at <a href="/wiki/Disneyland_Resort" title="Disneyland Resort">Disneyland Resort</a> in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyondlincoln_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-beyondlincoln-35">[34]</a></sup> For three months, the original Lincoln performed in New York, while the Lincoln Mark II played 5 performances per hour at Disneyland. Body language and facial motions were matched to perfection with the recorded speech. Actor <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dano" title="Royal Dano">Royal Dano</a> voiced the animatronics version of Abraham Lincoln.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyondlincoln_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-beyondlincoln-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lucky_the_Dinosaur" title="Lucky the Dinosaur">Lucky the Dinosaur</a> is an approximately 8-foot-tall (2.4 m) green <a href="/wiki/Segnosaurus" title="Segnosaurus">Segnosaurus</a> which pulls a flower-covered cart and is led by "Chandler the Dinosaur Handler". Lucky is notable in that he was the first free-roving audio-animatronic figure ever created by Disney's Imagineers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> The flower cart he pulls conceals the computer and power source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Muppet_Mobile_Lab" title="Muppet Mobile Lab">Muppet Mobile Lab</a> is a free-roving, audio-animatronic entertainment attraction designed by Walt Disney Imagineering. Two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muppet" title="Muppet">Muppet</a> characters, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._Bunsen_Honeydew" title="Dr. Bunsen Honeydew">Dr. Bunsen Honeydew</a> and his assistant, <a href="/wiki/Beaker_(Muppet)" title="Beaker (Muppet)">Beaker</a>, pilot the vehicle through the park, interacting with guests and deploying special effects such as foggers, ﬂashing lights, moving signs, confetti cannons and spray jets. It is currently deployed at <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Disneyland" title="Hong Kong Disneyland">Hong Kong Disneyland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Laffing_Sal" title="Laffing Sal">Laffing Sal</a> is one of the several automated characters that were used to attract carnival and amusement park patrons to <a href="/wiki/Funhouse" title="Funhouse">funhouses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dark_ride" title="Dark ride">dark rides</a> throughout the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laffing_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laffing-40">[39]</a></sup> Its movements were accompanied by a raucous laugh that sometimes frightened small children and annoyed adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Musee_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Musee-41">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Rock-afire_Explosion" title="The Rock-afire Explosion">The Rock-afire Explosion</a> in an animatronic band that played in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Showbiz_Pizza_Place" title="Showbiz Pizza Place">Showbiz Pizza Place</a> from 1980 to 1992.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The film industry has been a driving force revolutionizing the technology used to develop animatronics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ces-blog_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-ces-blog-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Animatronics are used in situations where a creature does not exist, the action is too risky or costly to use real actors or animals, or the action could never be obtained with a living person or animal. Its main advantage over <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">stop motion</a> is that the simulated creature has a physical presence moving in front of the camera in real time. The technology behind animatronics has become more advanced and sophisticated over the years, making the <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">puppets</a> even more lifelike.</p>
<p>Animatronics were first introduced by Disney in the 1964 film <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Poppins_(film)" title="Mary Poppins (film)">Mary Poppins</a></i> which featured an animatronic bird. Since then, animatronics have been used extensively in such movies as <i><a href="/wiki/Jaws_(film)" title="Jaws (film)">Jaws</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial</a></i>, which relied heavily on animatronics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realhistory_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-realhistory-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Directors such as <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Henson" title="Jim Henson">Jim Henson</a> have been pioneers in using animatronics in the film industry.</p>
<p>The 1993 film <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> used a combination of computer-generated imagery in conjunction with life-sized animatronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinosaurs" title="Dinosaurs">dinosaurs</a> built by <a href="/wiki/Stan_Winston" title="Stan Winston">Stan Winston</a> and his team. Winston's animatronic "T. rex" stood almost 20 feet (6.1 m),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW2013_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW2013-43">[42]</a></sup> 40 feet (12 m) in length<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magic_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-magic-44">[43]</a></sup> and even the largest animatronics weighing 9,000 pounds (4,100 kg) were able to perfectly recreate the appearance and natural movement on screen of a full-sized tyrannosaurus rex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magid_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-magid-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jack_Horner_(paleontologist)" title="Jack Horner (paleontologist)">Jack Horner</a> called it "the closest I've ever been to a live dinosaur".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magic_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-magic-44">[43]</a></sup> Critics referred to Spielberg's <i>dinosaurs</i> as <i>breathtakingly — and terrifyingly — realistic</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-46">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neale_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-neale-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1999 BBC miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Dinosaurs" title="Walking with Dinosaurs">Walking with Dinosaurs</a></i> was produced using a combination of about 80% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_generated_imagery" title="Computer generated imagery">CGI</a> and 20% animatronic models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inno_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-inno-48">[47]</a></sup> The quality of computer imagery of the day was good, but animatronics were still better at distance shots, as well as closeups of the dinosaurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inno_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-inno-48">[47]</a></sup> Animatronics for the series were designed by British animatronics firm Crawley Creatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inno_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-inno-48">[47]</a></sup> The show was followed up in 2007 with a live adaptation of the series, <i><a href="/wiki/Walking_with_Dinosaurs#The_Arena_Spectacular" title="Walking with Dinosaurs">Walking with Dinosaurs: The Arena Spectacular</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geoff_Peterson" title="Geoff Peterson">Geoff Peterson</a> is an animatronic human skeleton that serves as the <a href="/wiki/Sidekick" title="Sidekick">sidekick</a> on the late-night talk show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Late_Late_Show_with_Craig_Ferguson" title="The Late Late Show with Craig Ferguson">The Late Late Show with Craig Ferguson</a></i>. Often referred to as a "robot skeleton", Peterson is a radio-controlled animatronic robot puppet designed and built by <a href="/wiki/Grant_Imahara" title="Grant Imahara">Grant Imahara</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/MythBusters" title="MythBusters">MythBusters</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myth_Busters_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myth_Busters-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising">Advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Advertising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The British advertisement campaign for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadbury_Schweppes" title="Cadbury Schweppes">Cadbury Schweppes</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/Gorilla_(advertisement)" title="Gorilla (advertisement)">Gorilla</a></i> featured an actor inside a gorilla suit with an animatronically animated face.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Slowskys" title="The Slowskys">The Slowskys</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> campaign for <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a> Cable's Xfinity <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service</a>. The ad features two animatronic turtles, and it won the gold <a href="/wiki/Effie_Award" title="Effie Award">Effie Award</a> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Toys">Toys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Toys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some examples of animatronic toys include <a href="/wiki/Teddy_Ruxpin" title="Teddy Ruxpin">Teddy Ruxpin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Mouth_Billy_Bass" title="Big Mouth Billy Bass">Big Mouth Billy Bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kota_the_triceratops" title="Kota the triceratops">Kota the triceratops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pleo" title="Pleo">Pleo</a>, <a href="/wiki/WowWee_Alive_Chimpanzee" title="WowWee Alive Chimpanzee">WowWee Alive Chimpanzee</a>, <a href="/wiki/ActiMates" title="ActiMates">Microsoft Actimates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Furby" title="Furby">Furby</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Animatronic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="902" data-file-width="600" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Animatronic.jpg/170px-Animatronic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Animatronic.jpg/255px-Animatronic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Animatronic.jpg/340px-Animatronic.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Animatronic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An animatronic</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An animatronics character is built around an internal supporting frame, usually made of steel. Attached to these "bones" are the "muscles" which can be manufactured using elastic netting composed of styrene beads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> The frame provides the support for the electronics and mechanical components, as well as providing the shape for the outer skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "skin" of the figure is most often made of foam rubber, silicone or urethane poured into moulds and allowed to cure. To provide further strength a piece of fabric is cut to size and embedded in the foam rubber after it is poured into the mould. Once the mould has fully cured, each piece is separated and attached to the exterior of the figure providing the appearance and texture similar to that of "skin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An animatronics character is typically designed to be as realistic as possible and thus, is built similarly to how it would be in real life. The framework of the figure is like the "skeleton". Joints, motors, and <a href="/wiki/Actuator" title="Actuator">actuators</a> act as the "muscles". Connecting all the electrical components together are wires, such as the "nervous system" of a real animal or person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animatedholidaydisplays_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-animatedholidaydisplays-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Frame_or_skeleton">Frame or skeleton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Frame or skeleton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Steel, aluminum, plastic, and wood are all commonly used in building animatronics but each has its best purpose. The relative strength, as well as the weight of the material itself, should be considered when determining the most appropriate material to use. The cost of the material may also be a concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animatedholidaydisplays_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-animatedholidaydisplays-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior_or_skin">Exterior or skin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Exterior or skin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several materials are commonly used in the fabrication of an animatronics figure's exterior. Dependent on the particular circumstances, the best material will be used to produce the most lifelike form.</p>
<p>For example, "eyes" and "teeth" are commonly made completely out of acrylic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buffingtonfx-arvidseyes_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-buffingtonfx-arvidseyes-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Latex">Latex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Latex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>White <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> is commonly used as a general material because it has a high level of elasticity. It is also pre-vulcanized, making it easy and fast to apply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> Latex is produced in several grades. Grade 74 is a popular form of latex that dries rapidly and can be applied very thick, making it ideal for developing molds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buffingtonfx-skin_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-buffingtonfx-skin-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Foam_latex" title="Foam latex">Foam latex</a> is a lightweight, soft form of latex which is used in <a href="/wiki/Mask" title="Mask">masks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facial_prosthetic" title="Facial prosthetic">facial prosthetics</a> to change a person's outward appearance, and in animatronics to create a realistic "skin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buffingtonfx-skin_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-buffingtonfx-skin-57">[56]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i> was one of the first films to make extensive use of foam latex prosthetics in the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Silicone">Silicone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Silicone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Disney has a research team devoted to improving and developing better methods of creating more lifelike animatronics exteriors with <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siliconeresearch_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-siliconeresearch-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/RTV_silicone" title="RTV silicone">RTV silicone</a> (room temperature vulcanization silicone) is used primarily as a molding material as it is very easy to use but is relatively expensive. Few other materials stick to it, making molds easy to separate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-molding_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-molding-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bubbles are removed from silicone by pouring the liquid material in a thin stream or processing in a vacuum chamber prior to use. <a href="/wiki/Fumed_silica" title="Fumed silica">Fumed silica</a> is used as a bulking agent for thicker coatings of the material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyurethane">Polyurethane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Polyurethane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Polyurethane rubber is a more cost effective material to use in place of silicone. Polyurethane comes in various levels of hardness which are measured on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shore_scale" title="Shore scale">Shore scale</a>. Rigid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyurethane_foam" title="Polyurethane foam">polyurethane foam</a> is used in prototyping because it can be milled and shaped in high density. Flexible polyurethane foam is often used in the actual building of the final animatronic figure because it is flexible and bonds well with latex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buffingtonfx-skin_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-buffingtonfx-skin-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Plaster">Plaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Plaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>As a commonplace construction and home decorating material, plaster is widely available. Its rigidity limits its use in moulds, and plaster moulds are unsuitable when undercuts are present. This may make plaster far more difficult to use than softer materials like latex or silicone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-molding_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-molding-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movement">Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic" title="Pneumatic">Pneumatic</a> actuators can be used for small animatronics but are not powerful enough for large designs and must be supplemented with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulics</a>. To create more realistic movement in large figures, an analog system is generally used to give the figures a full range of fluid motion rather than simple two position movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emotion_modeling">Emotion modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Emotion modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mimicking the often subtle displays of humans and other living creatures, and the associated movement is a challenging task when developing animatronics. One of the most common emotional models is the <a href="/wiki/Facial_Action_Coding_System" title="Facial Action Coding System">Facial Action Coding System</a> (FACS) developed by Ekman and Friesen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ekman_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-ekman-64">[63]</a></sup> FACS defines that through facial expression, humans can recognize 6 basic emotions: anger, disgust, fear, joy, sadness, and surprise. Another theory is that of Ortony, Clore, and Collins, or the OCC model<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> which defines 22 different emotional categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_and_education">Training and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animatronics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Training and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Animatronics has been developed as a career which combines the disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">casting</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sculpting" title="Sculpting">sculpting</a>, control technologies, electrical/electronic systems, <a href="/wiki/Radio_control" title="Radio control">radio control</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbrushing" title="Airbrushing">airbrushing</a>.</p>
<p>Some colleges and universities do offer degree programs in animatronics. Individuals interested in animatronics typically earn a degree in robotics which closely relate to the specializations needed in animatronics engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Students achieving a bachelor's degree in robotics commonly complete courses in:</p>
<ul>
<li>Mechanical engineering</li>
<li>Industrial robotics</li>
<li>Mechatronics systems</li>
<li>Modeling of robotics systems</li>
<li>Robotics engineering</li>
<li>Foundational theory of robotics</li>
<li>Introduction to robotics</li>
</ul>
