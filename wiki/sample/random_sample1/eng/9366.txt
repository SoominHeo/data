<p><b>Amherst College</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="noexcerpt"><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/En-us-Amherst.ogg" title="Listen"><img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a><sup><span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-Amherst.ogg" title="File:En-us-Amherst.ogg">i</a></span></sup></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="/Ã¦/ short 'a' in 'bad'">Ã¦</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/Ér/ 'er' in 'finger'">Ér</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary-Amherst-Pronounce_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary-Amherst-Pronounce-5">[5]</a></sup> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AM</span></span></b>-Érst</i></a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private</a> <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_college" title="Liberal arts college">liberal arts college</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_Massachusetts" title="Amherst, Massachusetts">Amherst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, United States. Founded in 1821 as an attempt to relocate <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">Williams College</a> by its president, <a href="/wiki/Zephaniah_Swift_Moore" title="Zephaniah Swift Moore">Zephaniah Swift Moore</a>, Amherst is the third oldest institution of higher education in <a href="/wiki/List_of_colleges_and_universities_in_Massachusetts" title="List of colleges and universities in Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The institution was named after the town, which in turn had been named after <a href="/wiki/Jeffery_Amherst,_1st_Baron_Amherst" title="Jeffery Amherst, 1st Baron Amherst">Lord Jeffery Amherst</a>. Amherst was established as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men%27s_college" title="Men's college">men's college</a> and became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coeducation" title="Coeducation">coeducational</a> in 1975.</p>
<p>Amherst is an exclusively undergraduate four-year institution and enrolled 1,795 students in the fall of 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommonDataSet_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-CommonDataSet-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carnegie-7">[7]</a></sup> Students choose courses from 38 major programs in an <a href="/wiki/Curriculum#Open_curriculum" title="Curriculum">open curriculum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Students are not required to study a core curriculum or fulfill any distribution requirements and may even design their own unique interdisciplinary major.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">[9]</a></sup> Freshmen may take advanced courses, and seniors may take introductory ones. For the class of 2020, Amherst received 8,397 applications and accepted 1,149 yielding a 13.7% acceptance rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Amherst was ranked as the second best liberal arts college in the country by <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNews_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNews-11">[11]</a></sup> and 12th out of all U.S. colleges and universities by <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-12">[12]</a></sup> in their 2016 rankings. Amherst competes in the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Small_College_Athletic_Conference" title="New England Small College Athletic Conference">New England Small College Athletic Conference</a>.</p>
<p>Amherst has historically had close relationships and rivalries with <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">Williams College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wesleyan_University" title="Wesleyan University">Wesleyan University</a> which form the <a href="/wiki/Little_Three" title="Little Three">Little Three</a> colleges. The college is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Five_College_Consortium" title="Five College Consortium">Five College Consortium</a>, which allows its students to attend classes at four other <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Valley" title="Pioneer Valley">Pioneer Valley</a> institutions. These include <a href="/wiki/Mount_Holyoke_College" title="Mount Holyoke College">Mount Holyoke College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smith_College" title="Smith College">Smith College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College" title="Hampshire College">Hampshire College</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>.</p>

<p>Founded in 1821, Amherst College developed out of the secondary school Amherst Academy. The college was originally suggested as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">Williams College</a>, which was struggling to stay open. Although Williams remained open, Amherst was formed and diverged from its Williams roots into an individual institution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1812, funds were raised in Amherst for a secondary school, Amherst Academy; it opened December 1814.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The academy incorporated in 1816.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams1853_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams1853-15">[15]</a></sup> The institution was named after the town, which in turn had been named after <a href="/wiki/Jeffery_Amherst,_1st_Baron_Amherst" title="Jeffery Amherst, 1st Baron Amherst">Jeffery, Lord Amherst</a>, a veteran from the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> and later commanding general of the British forces in North America. On November 18, 1817, a project was adopted at the Academy to raise funds for the free instruction of "indigent young men of promising talents and hopeful piety, who shall manifest a desire to obtain a liberal education with a sole view to the Christian ministry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> This required a substantial investment from benefactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the fundraising for the project, it became clear that without larger designs, it would be impossible to raise sufficient funds. This led the committee overseeing the project to conclude that a new institution should be created. On August 18, 1818, the Amherst Academy board of trustees accepted this conclusion and began building a new college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Zephaniah_Swift_Moore" title="Zephaniah Swift Moore">Moore</a>, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Williams_College_Presidents" title="List of Williams College Presidents">President of Williams College</a>, however, still believed that <a href="/wiki/Williamstown,_Massachusetts" title="Williamstown, Massachusetts">Williamstown</a> was an unsuitable location for a college, and with the advent of Amherst College was elected its first president on May 8, 1821. At its opening, Amherst had forty-seven students. Fifteen of these had followed Moore from Williams College. Those fifteen represented about one-third of the whole number at Amherst, and about one-fifth of the whole number in the three classes to which they belonged in Williams College. President Moore died on June 29, 1823, and was replaced with a Williams College trustee, Heman Humphrey. Williams alumni are fond of an apocryphal story ascribing the removal of books from the Williams College library to Amherst College, but there is no contemporaneous evidence to verify the story. In 1995, Williams president <a href="/wiki/Harry_C._Payne" title="Harry C. Payne">Harry C. Payne</a> declared the story false, but many still nurture the legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amherst grew quickly, and for two years in the mid-1830s it was the second largest college in the United States, second only to <a href="/wiki/Yale_College" title="Yale College">Yale</a>. In 1835, Amherst attempted to create a course of study parallel to the classical liberal arts education. This parallel course focused less on <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, instead focusing on <a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, etc. The parallel course did not take hold, however, until the next century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amherst was founded as a non-sectarian institution "for the classical education of indigent young men of piety and talents for the Christian ministry." (Tyler, <i>A History of Amherst College</i>) One of the hallmarks of the new college was its Charity Fund, an early form of financial aid that paid the tuition of poorer students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEcollege_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEcollege-17">[17]</a></sup> Although officially non-denominational, the initial Amherst was widely seen as a religiously conservative institution with a strong connection to Calvinism, and as a result, there was considerable debate in the Massachusetts government over whether the new college should receive an official charter from the state, and a charter was not granted until February 21, 1825.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEcollege_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NEcollege-17">[17]</a></sup> As a result of the official charter being granted four years after the official founding of the college, the Amherst seal lists a date of 1825. A tradition of religious conservatism persisted at Amherst until the mid-nineteenth century; students who consumed alcohol or played cards were subject to expulsion, and there were a number of religious revivals at Amherst where mobs of righteous students would herd less religious students into the chapel and berate them for lack of piety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEcollege_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-NEcollege-17">[17]</a></sup> Towards the end of the nineteenth century, however, the college began a transition towards secularism, culminating in the demolishing of the college church in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_academic_reform">Development and academic reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development and academic reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Academic hoods in the United States are traditionally lined with the official colors of the school, in theory so watchers can tell where the hood wearer earned his or her degree. Amherst's hoods are purple (Williams' official color) with a white stripe or chevron, said to signify that Amherst was born of Williams. Amherst records one of the first uses of <a href="/wiki/Latin_honors" title="Latin honors">Latin honors</a> of any American college, dating back to 1881.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The college was an all-male school until the late 1960s, when a few female students from nearby schools in the Five-College Consortium attended on an experimental basis. In October 1974, the faculty voted in favor of coeducation and in November 1974 the board of trustees voted to admit female students starting in the 1975-1976 school year. In 1975, nine women who were already attending classes as part of an inter-college exchange program were admitted as transfer students. In June 1976, they became the first female graduates of the college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The college established the Black Studies Department in 1969. In 1973, it launched the nation's first undergraduate neuroscience program. In 1983, it established a Department of Asian Languages and Literatures, which was later to become the Department of Asian Languages and Civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amherst.edu_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-amherst.edu-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984, on-campus fraternities were abolished. The former fraternity buildings, which were owned by the college, were converted into residence halls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amherst.edu_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-amherst.edu-21">[21]</a></sup> The Department of Women's and Gender Studies, which later became the Department of Sexuality, Women's, and Gender Studies, was established in 1987 and the Department of Law, Jurisprudence, and Social Thought in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amherst.edu_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-amherst.edu-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since the inception of the <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a></i> rankings, Amherst College has been ranked ten times as the first overall among 266 liberal arts colleges in the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and in 2016 ranked second, behind <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">Williams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNews_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-USNews-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2016, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> ranked Amherst College as the 12th best college or university in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Princeton_Review" title="The Princeton Review">The Princeton Review</a></i>, Amherst ranks in the top 20 among all colleges and universities in the nation for "Students Satisfied With Financial Aid," "School Runs Like Butter," and "Top 10 Best Value Private Schools."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>Amherst has been dubbed one of the "most selective" liberal arts colleges in the United States;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Foundation_for_the_Advancement_of_Teaching" title="Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching">Carnegie Foundation</a> classifies Amherst as one of the "more selective" institutions whose first-year studentsâ test scores places these institutions in roughly the top fifth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baccalaureate_college" title="Baccalaureate college">baccalaureate institutions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Foundation_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Foundation-37">[37]</a></sup> For the class first enrolled in Fall 2015, Amherst received 8,568 applications and accepted 1,210 for a 14% acceptance rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommonDataSet_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-CommonDataSet-3">[3]</a></sup> Of the 477 enrolling, 53% were women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommonDataSet_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-CommonDataSet-3">[3]</a></sup> In terms of class rank, 86% of enrolled freshmen were in the top 10% of their high school classes; 96% ranked in the top quarter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommonDataSet_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-CommonDataSet-3">[3]</a></sup> The middle 50% range of SAT scores of enrolled freshmen was 680-780 for critical reading, 680-780 for math, and 680-770 for writing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommonDataSet_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-CommonDataSet-3">[3]</a></sup> the middle 50% range of the <a href="/wiki/ACT_(test)" title="ACT (test)">ACT</a> Composite score was 31-34.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CommonDataSet_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-CommonDataSet-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amherst's comprehensive tuition, room, and board fee for the 2012â13 academic year is $55,510. Once miscellaneous expenses are factored in the total cost to attend for the 2012â13 academic year amounts to $60,809â$63,259.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite its high cost of attendance, Amherst College meets the full demonstrated need of every admitted student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Sixty percent of current students receive scholarship aid, and the average financial aid package award amounts to $41,150; the average net price of attendance is $13,809 per year. College expenditures exceed $85,000 per student each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2007, Amherst announced that grants would replace loans in all financial aid packages beginning in the 2008-09 academic year. Amherst had already been the first school to eliminate loans for low-income students, and with this announcement it joined <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davidson_College" title="Davidson College">Davidson College</a>, then the only colleges to completely eliminate loans from need-based financial aid packages. Increased rates of admission of highly qualified lower income students has resulted in greater equality of opportunity at Amherst than is usual at elite American colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2008-2009 academic year, Amherst College also extended its need-blind admission policy to international applicants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amherst College offers 36 fields of study (with 850 courses)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> in the sciences, arts, humanities, mathematics and computer sciences, social sciences, foreign languages, classics, and several interdisciplinary fields (including premedical studies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>) and provides an unusually <a href="/wiki/Curriculum#Open_curriculum" title="Curriculum">open curriculum</a>. Students are not required to study a core curriculum or fulfill any distribution requirements and may even design their own unique interdisciplinary major.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">[9]</a></sup> Freshmen may take advanced courses, and seniors may take introductory ones.</p>
<p>Thirty-five percent of Amherst students in the class of 2007 were double majors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Amherst College has been the first college to have undergraduate departments in the interdisciplinary fields of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Studies" title="American Studies">American Studies</a>; Law, Jurisprudence and Social Thought; and <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">Neuroscience</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and has helped to pioneer other interdisciplinary programs, including Asian Languages and Civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Amherst library is named for long-time faculty member, poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Amherst College has been recognized for its commitment to quality teaching with professor-student interaction. The <a href="/wiki/Student%E2%80%93teacher_ratio" title="Studentâteacher ratio">student-faculty ratio</a> is 8:1 and 90% of classes have fewer than 30 students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable faculty members include, among others, modern literature and poetry critic William H. Pritchard, <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a> translator Howell Chickering, Jewish and Latino studies scholar <a href="/wiki/Ilan_Stavans" title="Ilan Stavans">Ilan Stavans</a>, novelist and legal scholar <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Douglas" title="Lawrence Douglas">Lawrence Douglas</a>, physicist Arthur Zajonc, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> biographer <a href="/wiki/William_Taubman" title="William Taubman">William Taubman</a>, African art specialist Rowland Abiodun, Natural Law expert <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Arkes" title="Hadley Arkes">Hadley Arkes</a>, Mathematician Daniel Velleman, Biblical scholar Susan Niditch, law and society expert <a href="/wiki/Austin_Sarat" title="Austin Sarat">Austin Sarat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning composer <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Spratlan" title="Lewis Spratlan">Lewis Spratlan</a>, professor emeritus of the music faculty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_freedom_debate">Academic freedom debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Academic freedom debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The writings of Amherst College political science Professor <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Arkes" title="Hadley Arkes">Hadley Arkes</a> about homosexuality led to a dispute in 2013 over whether a college seeking to create a diverse, respectful academic community should speak out when a faculty member disparages community members or should instead remain silent as a way to protect academic freedom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The issue arose when a group of alumni petitioned the college trustees and President Biddy Martin to âdissociate the institutionâ from Arkesâs âdivisive and destructiveâ views,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-terrasirradient.org_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-terrasirradient.org-54">[54]</a></sup> focusing particularly on his May 2013 comparison of homosexuality to bestiality, pedophilia and necrophilia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The alumni said, âAmherst College cannot credibly maintain its professed commitment to be an inclusive community as long as it chooses to remain silent while a sitting professor disparages members of its community in media of worldwide circulation and accessibility.â<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-terrasirradient.org_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-terrasirradient.org-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martin disagreed, citing past debates over the collegeâs position on Vietnam War and apartheid in South Africaâissues on which the college initially remained silent but eventually took a public position. In such times, she said, colleges should âavoid taking institutional positions on controversial political matters, except in extraordinary circumstancesâ and should simultaneously both âprotect their communities from discrimination and disrespectâ and âcherish a diversity of viewpoints.â<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Five_College_Consortium">Five College Consortium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Five College Consortium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amherst is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Five_College_Consortium" title="Five College Consortium">Five Colleges</a> consortium, which allows its students to attend classes at four other <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Valley" title="Pioneer Valley">Pioneer Valley</a> institutions. These include <a href="/wiki/Mount_Holyoke_College" title="Mount Holyoke College">Mount Holyoke College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smith_College" title="Smith College">Smith College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College" title="Hampshire College">Hampshire College</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>. In addition to the 850 courses available on campus, Amherst students have an additional 5,300 classes to consider through the Consortium (without paying additional tuition) and access to 8 million library volumes. The Five Colleges are geographically close to one another and are linked by <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Valley_Transit_Authority" title="Pioneer Valley Transit Authority">buses that run between the campuses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Five Colleges share resources and develop common academic programs. <a href="/wiki/Museums10" title="Museums10">Museums10</a> is a consortium of local art, history and science museums. The Five College Dance Department is one of the largest in the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The joint Astronomy department shares use of the <a href="/wiki/Five_College_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory" title="Five College Radio Astronomy Observatory">Five College Radio Astronomy Observatory</a>, which contributed to work that won the 1993 Nobel Prize in Physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Five College Coastal and Marine Sciences Program offers an interdisciplinary curriculum to undergraduates in the Five Colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amherst College is reducing its energy consumption through a computerized monitoring system for lighting and the use of an efficient cogeneration facility. The cogeneration facility features a gas turbine that generates electricity in addition to steam for heating the campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9CIt_is_Easy_Being_Green_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9CIt_is_Easy_Being_Green-62">[62]</a></sup> Amherst also operates a composting program, in which a portion of the food waste from dining halls is sent to a farmer in Vermont.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9CIt_is_Easy_Being_Green_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9CIt_is_Easy_Being_Green-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amherst claims its athletics program as the oldest in the nation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ath_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-ath-63">[63]</a></sup> pointing to its compulsory physical fitness regimen put in place in 1860 (the mandate that all students participate in sports or pursue physical education has been discontinued).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tyler_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-tyler-64">[64]</a></sup> One-third of the student body participates in sports at the intercollegiate level, and eighty percent participate in intramural and club sports teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ath_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-ath-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>The school participates in the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_III_(NCAA)" title="Division III (NCAA)">Division III</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_College_Athletic_Conference" title="Eastern College Athletic Conference">Eastern College Athletic Conference</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Small_College_Athletic_Conference" title="New England Small College Athletic Conference">New England Small College Athletic Conference</a>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Bates_College" title="Bates College">Bates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowdoin_College" title="Bowdoin College">Bowdoin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colby_College" title="Colby College">Colby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_College" title="Connecticut College">Connecticut College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_College_(New_York)" title="Hamilton College (New York)">Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlebury_College" title="Middlebury College">Middlebury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College_(Connecticut)" title="Trinity College (Connecticut)">Trinity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University" title="Tufts University">Tufts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wesleyan_University" title="Wesleyan University">Wesleyan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">Williams College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amherst is also one of the "<a href="/wiki/Little_Three" title="Little Three">Little Three</a>," along with Williams and Wesleyan. A Little Three champion is informally recognized by most teams based on the head-to-head records of the three schools, but three-way competitions are held in some of the sports.</p>
<p>Amherst has placed in the top ten of the NACDA Director's Cup in the NCAA Division III in seven of the last ten years, including fourth in 2007 and 2008 and third in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> On May 3, 2009, Williams College and Amherst alumni played a game of vintage baseball at <a href="/wiki/Wahconah_Park" title="Wahconah Park">Wahconah Park</a> according to 1859 rules to commemorate the 150th anniversary of the first <a href="/wiki/College_baseball" title="College baseball">college baseball</a> game played on July 2, 1859, between the two schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baseballgame_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-baseballgame-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amherst fields several club athletic teams, including <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_polo" title="Water polo">water polo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_(sport)" title="Ultimate (sport)">ultimate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">equestrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain biking</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_rowing" title="Sport rowing">crew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fencing" title="Fencing">fencing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>. <a href="/wiki/Intramural_sports" title="Intramural sports">Intramural sports</a> include soccer, tennis, golf, basketball, volleyball and softball.</p>
<p>The sport of <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_(sport)" title="Ultimate (sport)">Ultimate</a> was started and named at Amherst College in the mid-1960s by Jared Kass '69.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herndon_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-herndon-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Students">Students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amherst's resources, faculty, and academic life allow the college to enroll students with a range of talents, interests, and commitments. Students represent all fifty states, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, and fifty countries. Ninety-seven percent of students live on campus. Ninety-seven percent of Amherst freshmen return for their sophomore year; ninety-six percent graduate, among the highest retention and graduation rates in the country. There are more than 140 student groups at Amherst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Students pursue their interests through student-led organizations funded by the student government, including a variety of cultural and religious groups, publications, fine and performing arts and political advocacy and service groups. Groups include a medieval sword-fighting club, a knitting club, and a club devoted to random acts of kindness, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Community service groups and opportunities (locallyâthrough the Center for Community Engagement, nationally, and internationally) have been a priority at Amherst and for former President <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Marx" title="Anthony Marx">Anthony Marx</a>, who helped start a secondary school for black students in apartheid South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campus-revolutionary_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-campus-revolutionary-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the longstanding traditions at the college involves the <a href="/wiki/Sabrina_Statue" title="Sabrina Statue">Sabrina Statue</a>. Even year and odd year classes battle for possession of the historic statue, often engaging in elaborate pranks in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_assault">Sexual assault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sexual assault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2012, President Biddy Martin begin a community-wide review of the sexual misconduct and disciplinary policies at the College.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> This review was sparked by several factors, including a fraternity's T-shirt design that critics alleged was misogynist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and allegations of inappropriate handling of a case of sexual assault in an essay by <a href="/wiki/Angie_Epifano" title="Angie Epifano">Angie Epifano</a> published in <i>The Amherst Student</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> In January 2013, a college committee published a report noting Amherst's rate of sexual assault as similar to other colleges and universities, and making recommendations to address the problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a complaint was filed by Epifano and an anonymous former student in November 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mishkin_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mishkin-79">[79]</a></sup> the US Department of Education opened an investigation into the college's handling of sexual violence and potential violations of <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a>. In May 2014, the Department of Education announced a list of 55 colleges and universities (including Amherst) currently under investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>A report from Amherst College stated that 2009 to 2011, Amherst reported 35 instances of "forcible sex offenses", a term that encompasses rape, attempted rape, and lesser forms of sexual contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Discrimination_lawsuit">Discrimination lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Discrimination lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In December 2015, a lawsuit against Amherst College was filed alleging that "teaching assistants [...] were encouraged to sleep with and socialize with students to boost enrollment in the Spanish department."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascot">Mascot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mascot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The original unofficial mascot of Amherst College, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Jeffrey_Amherst" title="Lord Jeffrey Amherst">Lord Jeffrey Amherst</a>, concerned many members of the Amherst community,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> who sought to separate the school from the passive condoning of the wrongs committed by Lord Jeffrey Amherst, in particular, his advocating the use of <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> against <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2014, after a wild moose found its way onto the Amherst College campus and into the backyard of the house of the college president,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> students organized a Facebook campaign to change the mascot of the school to a moose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bullwinkle_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bullwinkle-89">[89]</a></sup> The page grew rapidly in popularity, receiving over 900 "likes" in under two weeks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bullwinkle_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bullwinkle-89">[89]</a></sup> and inspiring both a Twitter and Tumblr account for the newly proposed mascot. On May 25, at the Commencement ceremony for the class of 2014, the moose mascot was mentioned by Biddy Martin in her address, and the Dining Hall served Moose Tracks ice cream in front of an ice sculpture of a moose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2015, discussion of a mascot change continued when the editorial board of the Amherst Student, the college's official student-run newspaper, came out in favor of "the moose-scot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bullwinkle_89-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bullwinkle-89">[89]</a></sup> In November 2015 the student body and the faculty overwhelmingly voted to vacate the mascot; the decision to drop the mascot was made official on January 26, 2016 after student anti-racism protests on campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alumni">Alumni</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amherst_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Alumni">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Amherst_College_people" title="List of Amherst College people">List of Amherst College people</a></div>
<p>Although a relatively small college, Amherst has many accomplished alumni, including <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crafoord_Prize" title="Crafoord Prize">Crafoord Prize</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lasker_Award" title="Lasker Award">Lasker Award</a> laureates, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MacArthur_Fellowship" title="MacArthur Fellowship">MacArthur Fellowship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> winners, <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">National Book Award</a> recipients, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> winners; a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">U.S. President</a>, the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovereign_Prince_of_Monaco" title="Sovereign Prince of Monaco">Sovereign Prince of Monaco</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Greece" title="Prime Minister of Greece">Prime Ministers</a> and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Minister" title="Foreign Minister">Foreign Minister</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Uhuru_Kenyatta" title="Uhuru Kenyatta">Uhuru Kenyatta</a>, the fourth <a href="/wiki/President_of_Kenya" title="President of Kenya">President</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice of the United States</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speakers</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._House_of_Representatives" title="U.S. House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a>, a U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_Consultant_in_Poetry_to_the_Library_of_Congress" title="Poet Laureate Consultant in Poetry to the Library of Congress">Poet Laureate</a>, the legal architect of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_v_Board_of_Education" title="Brown v Board of Education">Brown v Board of Education</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> and the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Blood_bank" title="Blood bank">blood bank</a>;
