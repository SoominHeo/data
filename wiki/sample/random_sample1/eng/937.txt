<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle</b> (<b>EELV</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable launch system</a> program of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> (USAF), intended to assure access to space for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States government</a> payloads. The program, which began in the 1990s with the goal of making government space launches more affordable and <a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">reliable</a>, resulted in the development of two launch systems, <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>. These were later joined by the <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-falcon9_cert_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-falcon9_cert-1">[1]</a></sup> These launch systems are the primary methods for launching U.S. <a href="/wiki/Military_satellite" title="Military satellite">military satellites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The USAF plans to use the EELV family of launch vehicles until at least 2030. Follow-on technologies are being considered, one of which was originally the <a href="/wiki/Reusable_Booster_System" title="Reusable Booster System">Reusable Booster System</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> until its discontinuation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>United States' liquid rocket propulsion technology has evolved over the past five decades to meet the changing needs of the commercial community, agree with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> (DoD) diminishing budgets, and ensure national access to space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The importance of the United States having assured access to space is best laid out by the National Presidential Directive Number 40 which states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Access to space through U.S. space transportation capabilities is essential to:</p>
<ol>
<li>place critical United States Government assets and capabilities into space;</li>
<li>augment space-based capabilities in a timely manner in the event of increased operational needs or minimize disruptions due to on-orbit satellite failures, launch failures, or deliberate actions against U.S. space assets;</li>
<li>support government and commercial human space flight.</li>
</ol>
<p>The United States, therefore, must maintain robust, responsive, and resilient U.S. space transportation capabilities to assure access to space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The United States’ current solution to assure space access for operational space assets is to maintain two families of launch vehicles under the EELV program. The EELV program was initiated in 1995 as the Air Force’s premium space lift modernization program. The purpose of this program was to reduce the cost of operational space launch by 25–50% and to improve reliability over the heritage launch systems (<a href="/wiki/Atlas_II" title="Atlas II">Atlas II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a>). Procurement of EELV boosters for military space launch was to evolve into a "commercial like" nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buzzatto_2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buzzatto_2003-6">[6]</a></sup> The EELV program eventually produced two families of launch vehicles as the solution to US space lift needs. These two families are the <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a> launch system, developed by <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> (now The <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> Company), and the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> launch system, developed by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a> space launch boosters initially made up the EELV program, with the United States Air Force (USAF) overseeing the development of the two launch systems in just five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buzzatto_2003_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buzzatto_2003-6">[6]</a></sup> Boeing’s <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a> and Lockheed Martin’s <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> programs eventually merged to form the <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a> (ULA). ULA then held a monoply on United States military launches until <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> gained approval for its <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> launch vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Booster_Systems">Current Booster Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current Booster Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a> systems evolved through different paths. Major differences include launch processing, launch pad operational concept, and some major components. <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> was developed privately by <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlas_V">Atlas V</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Atlas V">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> space launch system has a lineage which began in 1954 under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercontinental_Ballistic_Missile" title="Intercontinental Ballistic Missile">Intercontinental Ballistic Missile</a> (ICBM) program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minami_1991_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Minami_1991-8">[8]</a></sup> The first US Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (ICBM) was the <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">SM-65 Atlas</a>. Although the Atlas started as ICBM technology, emergence as a space launch vehicle (SLV) followed soon thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minami_1991_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Minami_1991-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlas_V_Configuration"><a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> Configuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atlas V Configuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Main features include the <a href="/wiki/Common_Core_Booster" title="Common Core Booster">Common Core Booster</a> powered by a RD AMROSS <a href="/wiki/RD-180" title="RD-180">RD-180</a> engine, <a href="/wiki/Aerojet" title="Aerojet">Aerojet</a> strap-on solid boosters (up to five), <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur (rocket stage)</a>, powered by a single or dual <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Rocketdyne" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Rocketdyne">Pratt &amp; Whitney Rocketdyne</a> RL10A-4-2 engine(s), and an option of a 4.2 or 5.4 meter <a href="/wiki/Payload_fairing" title="Payload fairing">payload fairing</a> (PLF). A three-digit (XYZ) naming convention is used for the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> configuration identification. The first digit represents the <a href="/wiki/Payload_fairing" title="Payload fairing">payload fairing</a> size (either 4 or 5), the second digit represents the number of solid rocket boosters used (0 through 5), and the third digit represents the number of engines used on the Centaur (1 or 2)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Delta_IV">Delta IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Delta IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Delta SLV is a direct descendant of the Thor missile, <a href="/wiki/PGM-17_Thor" title="PGM-17 Thor">PGM-17 Thor</a>. Originating as a launch vehicle in the 1950s, the Delta program was initiated by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Delta_IV_Configuration"><a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a> Configuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Delta IV Configuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Main features include the <a href="/wiki/Common_Booster_Core" title="Common Booster Core">Common Booster Core</a> (CBC) powered by a Pratt and Whitney Rocketdyne RS-68 engine, a <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cryogenic_Second_Stage" title="Delta Cryogenic Second Stage">Delta Cryogenic Second Stage</a> (DCSS), and off pad horizontal vehicle integration. The Delta IV M configuration consists of a CBC first stage and a 4-m diameter DCSS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There are three variants of Delta IV M+ configuration. The Delta IV M+(4,2) uses two strap-on solid rocket motors (SRMs) <a href="/wiki/Solid_rocket_booster" title="Solid rocket booster">solid rocket booster</a> to augment the first-stage CBC and a 4-m diameter DCSS and PLF. The Delta IV M+(5,2) and Delta IV M+(5,4) have two and four SRMs, respectively and 5-m diameter DCSS and PLF. The Heavy Lift Vehicle (HLV) variant has two strap-on CBC cores with a 5-m DCSS and PLF. The Delta IV-Heavy utilizes the same configuration as the Delta IV-M (no strap on Aerojet boosters) but with two additional Common Booster Cores.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Falcon_9"><a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Falcon 9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Falcon 9 is a clean-sheet rocket designed by <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>. Announced in 2005, the Falcon 9 had its first flight in 2010. As of January 2016, it has gone through three major revisions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Falcon_9_Configuration"><a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> Configuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Falcon 9 Configuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>All Falcon 9 versions are <a href="/wiki/Two-stage-to-orbit" title="Two-stage-to-orbit">two-stage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">LOX</a>/<a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a>-powered launch vehicles.</p>
<p>The Falcon 9 tank walls and domes are made from <a href="/wiki/Aluminium-lithium_alloy" title="Aluminium-lithium alloy">aluminum lithium</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2195_aluminum_alloy" title="2195 aluminum alloy">alloy</a>. SpaceX uses an all <a href="/wiki/Friction_stir_welding" title="Friction stir welding">friction-stir welded</a> tank, the highest strength and most reliable welding technique available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-falcon9-2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-falcon9-2010-10">[10]</a></sup> The second stage tank of a Falcon 9 is simply a shorter version of the first stage tank and uses most of the same tooling, material and manufacturing techniques, reducing production costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-falcon9-2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-falcon9-2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>SpaceX uses multiple redundant <a href="/wiki/Category:Avionics_computers" title="Category:Avionics computers">flight computers</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fault-tolerant_design" title="Fault-tolerant design">fault-tolerant design</a>. Each <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(rocket_engine_family)" title="Merlin (rocket engine family)">Merlin rocket engine</a> is controlled by three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voting_logic" title="Voting logic">voting</a> computers, each of which has two physical processors that constantly check each other. The software runs on <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and is written in <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw20121118_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-aw20121118-11">[11]</a></sup> For flexibility, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_off-the-shelf" title="Commercial off-the-shelf">commercial off-the-shelf</a> parts and system-wide <i>radiation-tolerant</i> design are used instead of <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hardening" title="Radiation hardening">rad-hardened</a> parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw20121118_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-aw20121118-11">[11]</a></sup> Each stage has stage-level flight computers, in addition to the Merlin-specific engine controllers, of the same fault-tolerant triad design to handle stage control functions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> assembled its initial blue print for the EELV in 1994, following many years of government funded studies into an improved system and architecture, which was intended to replace most if not all existing "legacy" spacelifters (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_II" title="Atlas II">Atlas II</a>/<a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a>, etc.). The architecture called for the spacelifter to be based on standardized fairings, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_core_vehicles" title="Liquid core vehicles">liquid core vehicles</a>, upper stages, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_rocket" title="Solid rocket">solid rockets</a>. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_Payload_Interface&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Standard Payload Interface (page does not exist)">Standard Payload Interface</a> bus was also proposed as another way to save money and improve efficiency.</p>
<p>The initial bids came from four major defense contractors: <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>, <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alliant_Techsystems" title="Alliant Techsystems">Alliant Techsystems</a>. Each of the bids had a variety of different concepts. One of the contractors, Boeing, initially proposed utilizing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Main_Engine" title="Space Shuttle Main Engine">Space Shuttle Main Engines</a> (SSME).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> McDonnell Douglas merged with Boeing in 1997 and used its Delta IV spacelifter for their EELV proposal.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boeing and Lockheed Martin were both collectively awarded US$100 million for the final phase of the bid. Both companies built their designs around modularization, standardization and minimizing the amount of equipment and using proven, reliable and simplified systems. Boeing developed the <a href="/wiki/Common_Booster_Core" title="Common Booster Core">Common Booster Core</a> (CBC) that would be the center of the <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a>. For the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> Lockheed Martin did something similar, calling it a <a href="/wiki/Common_Core_Booster" title="Common Core Booster">Common Core Booster</a> (CCB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFSPC_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFSPC-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_espionage">Industrial espionage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Industrial espionage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boeing was found to be in possession of proprietary documents from Lockheed Martin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> To end <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litigation" title="Litigation">litigation</a>, both companies agreed to join forces and formed the <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a> joint venture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Each company has a 50% stake in ULA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_services_contracts">Launch services contracts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Launch services contracts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1998 two initial launch services contracts (known as Buy 1) were awarded. Along with the award of two development agreements, the total amount was more than $3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFSPC_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFSPC-13">[13]</a></sup> Boeing was awarded a contract for 19 out of the 28 launches; Lockheed Martin was awarded a contract for the other 9. Boeing received $1.38 billion, and Lockheed Martin received $650 million for the launches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2003 the USAF moved 7 launches from Delta IV to Atlas V.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2012, the DoD announced a re-opening of the EELV-class launch vehicle market to <a href="/wiki/Competition_(economics)" title="Competition (economics)">competition</a> beginning in 2015. "Under the new plan, the Air Force is authorized to proceed with a block buy of "up to" 36 launch cores from current monopoly vendor <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a>, while at the same time opening up another 14 cores to be purchased <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competitively</a>. The new era will begin in 2015 with initial launches to be performed in 2017."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-is20121204_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-is20121204-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Air Force signed a contract at that time with <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> for two launches in 2014 and 2015 to serve as proving flights to support the certification process for the <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> v1.1 and <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Heavy" title="Falcon Heavy">Falcon Heavy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Following these, in April 2014, SpaceX sued the United States Air Force in order to gain access to the EELV program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> SpaceX argued the RD-180 engines used by the Atlas V violated sanctions against the Russian government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Air Force and SpaceX settled the lawsuit in 2015 by opening up more launches to competitive bidding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Air Force certified the Falcon 9 in May 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and SpaceX won a contract under the EELV program to launch a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS_III" title="GPS III">GPS III</a> payload to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MEO" title="MEO">MEO</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sn20160427_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-sn20160427-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human-rating">Human-rating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Human-rating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion spacecraft</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_Corporation" title="Aerospace Corporation">Aerospace Corporation</a> was asked by NASA three times, in 2005, 2008 and 2009, to assess technical feasibility and cost of modifying an EELV to be <a href="/wiki/Human-rating_certification" title="Human-rating certification">human-rated</a> for use in NASA <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Two later assessments also addressed the possibility of replacing the Ares I with a Delta IV Heavy. The reports indicate that the Delta IV Heavy meets ISS and lunar target performance requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Unlike other modifications of the Delta IV, the Heavy variant does not use solid rocket boosters.</p>
<p>In a presentation to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Review_of_U.S._Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee" title="Review of U.S. Human Space Flight Plans Committee">Review of U.S. Human Space Flight Plans Committee</a>, an Aerospace Corporation representative presented a summary of the study. The summary asserts that even without a newly developed upper stage, it would be feasible for a human-rated Delta IV Heavy to launch a crewed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">Orion</a> spacecraft to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> ULA has since published a paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> detailing the changes needed for man-rating EELVs. On February 2, 2010 NASA awarded<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> ULA $6.7 million in stimulus funds under the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Crew_Development" title="Commercial Crew Development">Commercial Crew Development</a> (CCDev) program. A <a href="/wiki/Space_Act_Agreement" title="Space Act Agreement">Space Act Agreement</a> was set up to develop an <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Detection_System" title="Emergency Detection System">Emergency Detection System</a> (EDS) that could be used on both EELVs. An EDS monitors critical launch vehicle and spacecraft systems and issues status, warning and abort commands to the crew during their mission to low Earth orbit.</p>
