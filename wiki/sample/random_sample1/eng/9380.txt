<p>The <b>Atlanta Dream</b> is a professional basketball team based in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, playing in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Conference_(WNBA)" title="Eastern Conference (WNBA)">Eastern Conference</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_National_Basketball_Association" title="Women's National Basketball Association">Women's National Basketball Association</a> (WNBA). The team was founded before the <a href="/wiki/2008_WNBA_season" title="2008 WNBA season">2008 WNBA season</a> began. The team is owned by Dream Too LLC, which is composed of two Atlanta businesswomen: Mary Brock, and Kelly Loeffler. Like some other WNBA teams, the Dream is not affiliated with an <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a> counterpart, even though the Dream share the market with the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Hawks" title="Atlanta Hawks">Atlanta Hawks</a>.</p>
<p>The Dream has qualified for the <a href="/wiki/WNBA_Playoffs" title="WNBA Playoffs">WNBA Playoffs</a> in seven of its nine years in Atlanta and has reached the <a href="/wiki/WNBA_Finals" title="WNBA Finals">WNBA Finals</a> three times. The franchise has been home to many high-quality players such as <a href="/wiki/University_of_Louisville" title="University of Louisville">University of Louisville</a> standouts <a href="/wiki/Angel_McCoughtry" title="Angel McCoughtry">Angel McCoughtry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shoni_Schimmel" title="Shoni Schimmel">Shoni Schimmel</a>, former <a href="/wiki/2004_WNBA_Finals" title="2004 WNBA Finals">Finals MVP</a> <a href="/wiki/Betty_Lennox" title="Betty Lennox">Betty Lennox</a>, and Brazilian sharpshooter <a href="/wiki/Izi_Castro_Marques" title="Izi Castro Marques">Izi Castro Marques</a>. In <a href="/wiki/2010_WNBA_Finals" title="2010 WNBA Finals">2010</a>, the Dream went to the <a href="/wiki/WNBA_Finals" title="WNBA Finals">WNBA Finals</a> but fell short to <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Storm" title="Seattle Storm">Seattle</a>. They lost to the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Lynx" title="Minnesota Lynx">Minnesota Lynx</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2011_WNBA_Finals" title="2011 WNBA Finals">2011</a> and <a href="/wiki/2013_WNBA_Finals" title="2013 WNBA Finals">2013 WNBA Finals</a>. They have been in the finals for three of four years, but haven't won a single game in them, going 0-9.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Franchise_history">Franchise history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Franchise history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Franchise_Inception">Franchise Inception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Franchise Inception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even before the success of the United States women's basketball team in the 1996 Olympic Games, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Basketball_League_(1996%E2%80%931998)" title="American Basketball League (1996–1998)">American Basketball League</a> had interest in placing a women's professional basketball team in Atlanta. Plans were made to place a women's pro team in Atlanta as early as 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Eight of the 12 Olympians would play on ABL teams when the league began play in October 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Glory" title="Atlanta Glory">Atlanta Glory</a> played at <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Arena" title="Forbes Arena">Forbes Arena</a> and would last two seasons before folding before the start of the 1998–99 season, which would be the ABL's final.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_dream_of_a_new_team">The dream of a new team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The dream of a new team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Atlanta had been mentioned as a possible future city for WNBA expansion, but efforts did not come together until the beginning of 2007. An organizing committee with Atlanta businessmen and politicians began the effort to attract an expansion team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The inability of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Hawks" title="Atlanta Hawks">Atlanta Hawks</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a> to draw crowds was a concern of the WNBA, and the committee kicked off an effort in February 2007 to gain volunteers and petition signatures.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Philips_Arena" title="Philips Arena">Philips Arena</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gwinnett_Arena" title="Gwinnett Arena">Gwinnett Arena</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Memorial_Coliseum" title="Alexander Memorial Coliseum">Alexander Memorial Coliseum</a> were candidates for venues. By May 2007, the committee had over 1,000 pledges for season tickets, although the goal was 8,000 season tickets in ninety days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> By July the committee had 1,200 commitments. The next step was to find an owner for the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>On October 16, 2007 word broke that <a href="/wiki/Ron_Terwilliger" title="Ron Terwilliger">Ron Terwilliger</a>, an Atlanta businessman and CEO of a national real estate company would be the future owner of an Atlanta franchise. The next day, at a news conference at Atlanta's <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Olympic_Park" title="Centennial Olympic Park">Centennial Olympic Park</a>, WNBA president <a href="/wiki/Donna_Orender" title="Donna Orender">Donna Orender</a> made the announcement that Atlanta would officially be granted a WNBA expansion franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_the_Dream">Building the Dream</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Building the Dream">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 27, 2007 Atlanta named <a href="/wiki/Marynell_Meadors" title="Marynell Meadors">Marynell Meadors</a> the first <a href="/wiki/Head_coach" title="Head coach">head coach</a>/<a href="/wiki/General_manager" title="General manager">general manager</a> in franchise history. For Meadors, a coach with extensive experience at the college level, this was her second role as a coach/general manager in the WNBA. Meadors had been one of the WNBA's first eight coaches as the head coach/general manager of the <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Sting" title="Charlotte Sting">Charlotte Sting</a> when that team began in 1997. Afterwards, Meadors had served as a scouting director for the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Sol" title="Miami Sol">Miami Sol</a> and had been an assistant coach under <a href="/wiki/Richie_Adubato" title="Richie Adubato">Richie Adubato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tree_Rollins" title="Tree Rollins">Tree Rollins</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Mystics" title="Washington Mystics">Washington Mystics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Former NBA player <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Rodman" title="Dennis Rodman">Dennis Rodman</a> volunteered his name as head coach for the Dream. Terwilliger declined, stating that he wanted someone with more coaching experience and he felt that the head coach should be a woman, as the WNBA was a women's league.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 5, 2007, an online contest was announced for people to choose the team name and team colors – the final choice would rest with owner Ron Terwilliger. The names offered as choices were "Dream", "Flight", "Surge" and "Sizzle". There were also options for team colors such as lime green or hot pink. Voters had four different names and four different colors from which to choose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Atlanta's WNBA franchise announced that the team name would be the Dream on January 23, 2008. The name was inspired, in part, by <a href="/wiki/I_Have_a_Dream" title="I Have a Dream">the famous speech</a> of Atlanta native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The team colors would be sky blue, red, and white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Atlanta held their expansion draft on February 6, 2008 when they selected one player from each of the 13 teams in the league. Atlanta traded <a href="/wiki/Roneeka_Hodges" title="Roneeka Hodges">Roneeka Hodges</a> and their number four pick in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_WNBA_Draft" title="2008 WNBA Draft">2008 WNBA Draft</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Storm" title="Seattle Storm">Seattle Storm</a> for <a href="/wiki/Izi_Castro_Marques" title="Izi Castro Marques">Izi Castro Marques</a> and Seattle's eighth pick in the 2008 WNBA Draft. Also, the Dream traded the 18th pick and <a href="/wiki/LaToya_Thomas" title="LaToya Thomas">LaToya Thomas</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Shock" title="Detroit Shock">Detroit Shock</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Latta" title="Ivory Latta">Ivory Latta</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_the_Dream_.282008.E2.80.93present.29">Living the Dream (2008–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Living the Dream (2008–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From May 17, 2008 with a season opening loss against the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Sun" title="Connecticut Sun">Connecticut Sun</a> to July 3, 2008 with a home loss against the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Comets" title="Houston Comets">Houston Comets</a>, the Dream lost 17 consecutive games, setting the WNBA all-time record for both consecutive losses and losses from opening day. The 2006 <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sky" title="Chicago Sky">Chicago Sky</a> had previously lost 13 consecutive games, and the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Shock" title="Detroit Shock">Detroit Shock</a> had opened their season 0–13. (The <a href="/wiki/Portland_Fire" title="Portland Fire">Portland Fire</a> had lost 14 straight games over two seasons, ending the 2001 season with 11 straight losses and opening 2002 with three straight losses.) They later finished with a 4–30 record.</p>
<p>On July 5, 2008, the Dream earned their first win in Atlanta against the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sky" title="Chicago Sky">Chicago Sky</a>, ending the losing streak. They beat the Sky 91–84.</p>
<p>The 2008/2009 offseason was a busy one. Head coach/general manager <a href="/wiki/Marynell_Meadors" title="Marynell Meadors">Marynell Meadors</a> did not want a repeat of 2008. She made key moves by acquiring players such as <a href="/wiki/Sancho_Lyttle" title="Sancho Lyttle">Sancho Lyttle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Teasley" title="Nikki Teasley">Nikki Teasley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamique_Holdsclaw" title="Chamique Holdsclaw">Chamique Holdsclaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angel_McCoughtry" title="Angel McCoughtry">Angel McCoughtry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Snow" title="Michelle Snow">Michelle Snow</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009, Atlanta reached the playoffs at 18–16, exceeding their previous record by 14 wins. Despite being in the playoffs, the Dream ended their season in disappointment and lost in the first round to the 2008 champion <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Shock" title="Detroit Shock">Detroit Shock</a> in a sweep. After the season, their coach, Marynell Meadors, was awarded the <a href="/wiki/WNBA_Coach_of_the_Year_Award" title="WNBA Coach of the Year Award">Coach of the Year Award</a>.</p>
<p>The Dream's owner, Ron Terwilliger, announced in August that he wanted to give up his position as the primary owner of the Atlanta franchise. On October 29, 2009, Kathy Betty took control of the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2010 season proved even better for the Dream. Although finishing in fourth place in the Eastern Conference, the Dream sprinted through the first two rounds of the playoffs. On September 7, 2010, the Dream secured a trip to the WNBA Finals with a win over the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Liberty" title="New York Liberty">New York Liberty</a>, as they swept New York in two games in the Eastern Conference Finals. They eventually faced the best team in the league, the 28–6 <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Storm" title="Seattle Storm">Seattle Storm</a>. Seattle took the first two games at <a href="/wiki/KeyArena" title="KeyArena">home</a> with two close wins. Atlanta needed to win game three in order to keep the series alive. Seattle was too much to handle as they completed the sweep and won the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Even though they were swept, the Dream did not lose any game by a margin of more than three points.</p>
<p>Addressing arguably Atlanta's biggest concern, the team traded for All-Star point guard <a href="/wiki/Lindsey_Harding" title="Lindsey Harding">Lindsey Harding</a> prior to the 2011 season. Despite the major addition, the Dream struggled to open the season, starting just 2–7 due to an injury that sidelined <a href="/wiki/Angel_McCoughtry" title="Angel McCoughtry">Angel McCoughtry</a> and overseas commitments by <a href="/wiki/Sancho_Lyttle" title="Sancho Lyttle">Sancho Lyttle</a>. The team did not give up, however, and went on an impressive run of 14 wins and 5 losses after the All Star break. They carried that momentum into the playoffs, sweeping the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Sun" title="Connecticut Sun">Connecticut Sun</a> and defeating the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Fever" title="Indiana Fever">Indiana Fever</a> to return to the WNBA Finals. But, once again they would be denied, losing to the formidable 27–7 <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Lynx" title="Minnesota Lynx">Minnesota Lynx</a> in three games.</p>
<p>They played in the 2016 WNBA playoffs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_home">Current home</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Current home">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Atlanta Dream currently play in <a href="/wiki/Philips_Arena" title="Philips Arena">Philips Arena</a> in downtown <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. In 2013, the team qualified for the <a href="/wiki/2013_WNBA_Finals" title="2013 WNBA Finals">WNBA Finals</a>, but a scheduling conflict forced them to play home games at the <a href="/wiki/Infinite_Energy_Arena" title="Infinite Energy Arena">Arena at Gwinnett Center</a>. For the 2016 playoffs, should the Dream qualify, as well as the entirety of the 2017 and 2018 WNBA seasons, the Dream will play home games at <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Tech's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hank_McCamish_Pavilion" title="Hank McCamish Pavilion">Hank McCamish Pavilion</a> due to planned renovations at Philips Arena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_coverage">Media coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Media coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, some Dream games are broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Fox_Sports_South" title="Fox Sports South">Fox Sports South</a> (FS-S) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SportSouth" title="SportSouth">SportSouth</a> (SSO), which are local television stations for the Southern United States. More often than not, <a href="/wiki/NBA_TV" title="NBA TV">NBA TV</a> will pick up the feed from the local broadcast, which is shown nationally. Broadcasters for the Dream games are Bob Rathbun and LaChina Robinson. Prior to the 2010 season, the Dream had a deal with <a href="/wiki/Comcast/Charter_Sports_Southeast" title="Comcast/Charter Sports Southeast">Comcast/Charter Sports Southeast</a> (CSS). Games on that station were called by Art Eckman and LaChina Robinson.</p>
<p>All games (excluding blackout games, which are available on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESPN3.com" title="ESPN3.com">ESPN3.com</a>) are broadcast to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WNBA_LiveAccess" title="WNBA LiveAccess">WNBA LiveAccess</a> game feeds on the league website. Furthermore, some Dream games are broadcast nationally on <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a>, <a href="/wiki/WNBA_on_ESPN" title="WNBA on ESPN">ESPN2</a> and <a href="/wiki/ESPN_on_ABC" title="ESPN on ABC">ABC</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_National_Basketball_Association" title="Women's National Basketball Association">WNBA</a> has reached an eight-year agreement with <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a>, which will pay right fees to the Dream, as well as other teams in the league.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trades">Trades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlanta_Dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Trades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>February 6, 2008</b>: The Dream traded LaToya Thomas and the 18th pick in the 2008 Draft to the Detroit Shock in exchange for Ivory Latta.</li>
<li><b>February 6, 2008</b>: The Dream traded the fourth pick in the 2008 Draft and Roneeka Hodges to the Seattle Storm for Izi Castro Marques and the eighth pick in the draft.</li>
<li><b>February 6, 2008</b>: The Dream acquired the 24th pick in the 2008 Draft from the Indiana Fever in exchange for agreeing not to select specific unprotected Fever players in the expansion draft.</li>
<li><b>April 9, 2008</b>: The Dream traded Ann Wauters, draft rights to Morenike Atunrase, and the a second-round pick in the 2009 Draft to the San Antonio Silver Stars in exchange for Camille Little, draft rights to Chioma Nnamaka, and the a first-round pick in the 2009 Draft.</li>
<li><b>June 22, 2008</b>: The Dream traded Camille Little to the Seattle Storm in exchange for a second-round pick in the 2009 Draft.</li>
<li><b>July 4, 2008</b>: The Dream traded Kristen Mann to the Indiana Fever in exchange for Alison Bales.</li>
<li><b>December 17, 2008</b>: The Dream traded the 13th pick in the 2009 Draft to the Los Angeles Sparks in exchange for the rights to Chamique Holdsclaw.</li>
<li><b>January 21, 2009</b>: The Dream traded Alison Bales to the Phoenix Mercury in exchange for the 18th pick in the 2009 Draft.</li>
<li><b>April 9, 2009</b>: The Dream traded the 18th pick in the 2009 Draft to the Detroit Shock in exchange for Ashley Shields.</li>
<li><b>August 12, 2009</b>: The Dream traded Tamera Young to the Chicago Sky in exchange for Armintie Price.</li>
<li><b>March 11, 2010</b>: The Dream traded Michelle Snow to the San Antonio Silver Stars in exchange for Dalma Ivanyi and the right to swap second-round picks in the 2010 Draft.</li>
<li><b>April 11, 2011</b>: The Dream traded Rachel Jarry and second-round pick in 2012 Draft to the Minnesota Lynx in exchange for Felicia Chester.</li>
<li><b>April 11, 2011</b>: The Dream traded Ta'Shia Phillips, Kelly Miller and first-round pick in 2012 Draft to the Washington Mystics in exchange for Lindsey Harding and second-round pick in 2012 Draft.</li>
<li><b>February 19, 2013</b>: The Dream traded the 7th and 19th pick in the 2013 Draft to the Washington Mystics in exchange for Jasmine Thomas and second-round pick in 2013 Draft.</li>
<li><b>March 12, 2014</b>: The Dream traded Alex Bentley and the 32nd pick in the 2014 Draft to the Connecticut Sun in exchange for Matee Ajavon and the 18th pick in the 2014 Draft from the Washington Mystics.</li>
<li><b>May 7, 2014</b>: The Dream traded Courtney Clements to the Chicago Sky in exchange for Swin Cash.</li>
<li><b>July 9, 2014</b>: The Dream traded Swin Cash to the New York Liberty in exchange for DeLisha Milton-Jones.</li>
<li><b>April 16, 2015</b>: The Dream traded Jasmine Thomas to the Connecticut Sun in exchange for Brittany Hrynko.</li>
<li><b>July 27, 2015</b>: The Dream traded Érika de Souza to the Chicago Sky in exchange for Damiris Dantas, Reshanda Gray, and a first-round pick in 2016 Draft from the Minnesota Lynx. Minnesota received Sylvia Fowles and a second-round pick in 2016 Draft from Chicago as part of this trade.</li>
<li><b>February 3, 2016</b>: The Dream traded the 4th pick in the 2016 Draft to the Connecticut Sun in exchange for Elizabeth Williams.</li>
<li><b>May 2, 2016</b>: The Dream traded Shoni Schimmel to the New York Liberty in exchange for second-round pick in 2017 Draft.</li>
</ul>
