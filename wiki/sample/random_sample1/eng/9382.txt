<p>The <b>Apple III</b> (often styled as <b>apple ///</b>) is a business-oriented personal computer produced and released by <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Computer</a> that was intended as the successor to the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II_series" title="Apple II series">Apple II series</a>, but was largely considered a failure in the market. Development work on the Apple III started in late 1978 under the guidance of Dr. Wendell Sander. It had the internal code name of "Sara", named after Sander's daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sara_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sara-2">[2]</a></sup> The machine was first announced and released on May 19, 1980, but due to serious stability issues that required a design overhaul and a recall of existing machines, it was formally reintroduced in the second half of 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-intro-3">[3]</a></sup> Development stopped and the Apple III was discontinued on April 24, 1984, and its last successor—the III Plus, was dropped from the Apple product line in September 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apconf_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-apconf-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Apple III could be viewed as an enhanced Apple II – then the newest heir to a line of <a href="/wiki/8-bit" title="8-bit">8-bit</a> machines dating back to 1976. However, the Apple III was not part of the Apple II line, but rather a close cousin. The key features business users wanted in a personal computer were a true typewriter-style upper/lowercase keyboard (as opposed to the Apple II, which only supported uppercase letters) and 80-column display. In addition, the machine had to pass U.S. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_frequency_interference" title="Radio frequency interference">radio frequency interference</a> (RFI) qualifications for business equipment. In 1981, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Business_Machines" title="International Business Machines">International Business Machines</a> (IBM) unveiled the <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer" title="IBM Personal Computer">IBM Personal Computer</a> (IBM PC) – a completely new <a href="/wiki/16-bit" title="16-bit">16-bit</a> design soon available in a wide range of inexpensive clones. The business market moved rapidly towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PC_DOS" title="PC DOS">PC DOS</a>/<a href="/wiki/MS-DOS" title="MS-DOS">MS-DOS</a> platform, eventually pulling away from the Apple 8-bit computer line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBMPC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBMPC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite numerous stability issues and a recall that included the first 14,000 units off the assembly line, Apple was eventually able to produce a reliable and dependable version of the machine. However, damage to the computer's reputation had already been done and it failed to do well commercially as a direct result. In the end, an estimated 65,000–75,000 Apple III computers were sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-intro-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apconf_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-apconf-4">[4]</a></sup> The Apple III Plus brought this up to ~120,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apconf_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-apconf-4">[4]</a></sup> Apple co-founder <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" title="Steve Wozniak">Steve Wozniak</a> stated that the primary reason for the Apple III's failure was that the system was designed by Apple's marketing department, unlike Apple's previous engineering-driven projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwoz_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-iwoz-6">[6]</a></sup> The Apple III's failure led to Apple reevaluating their plan to phase out the Apple II, and eventual continuation of development of the older machine. As a result, later Apple II models incorporated some hardware, such as the Apple Scribe Printer, a thermal printer, and software technologies of the Apple III.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Apple III was designed to be a business computer and an eventual successor for the Apple II. While the Apple II contributed to the inspirations of several important business products, such as <a href="/wiki/VisiCalc" title="VisiCalc">VisiCalc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiplan" title="Multiplan">Multiplan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_Writer" title="Apple Writer">Apple Writer</a>, the computer's hardware architecture, <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> and developer environment were limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apple2hist_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-apple2hist-7">[7]</a></sup> The Apple III addressed these weaknesses. According to <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" title="Steve Wozniak">Steve Wozniak</a>, VisiCalc and <a href="/wiki/Disk_II" title="Disk II">Disk II</a> had caused the Apple II's popularity, with 90% of sales going to businesses as opposed to the hobbyists that were its original market. Apple management intended to clearly establish <a href="/wiki/Market_segmentation" title="Market segmentation">market segmentation</a> by designing the Apple III to appeal to the business market, leaving the Apple II to home and education users. Management believed that "once the Apple III was out, the Apple II would stop selling in six months", Wozniak said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byte198501_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-byte198501-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Apple III was powered by a 1.8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a> <a href="/wiki/Synertek" title="Synertek">Synertek 6502A</a> or B<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/8-bit" title="8-bit">8-bit</a> <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> and, like some of the later machines in the Apple II family, used <a href="/wiki/Bank_switching" title="Bank switching">bank switching</a> techniques to address memory beyond the 6502's traditional 64KB limit, up to 256 K in the IIIs case. Third-party vendors also produced memory upgrade kits that allowed the Apple III to reach up to 512 KB. Other Apple III built-in features included an 80-column, 24-line display with upper and lowercase characters, a numeric keypad, dual-speed (pressure-sensitive) cursor control keys, 6-bit (DAC) audio, and a built-in 140 KB 5.25" <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disk</a> drive. Graphics modes included 560x192 in black and white, and 280x192 with 16 colors or shades of gray. Unlike the Apple II, the Disk III controller was built into the logic board.</p>
<p>The Apple III was the first Apple product that allowed the user to choose both a screen font and a keyboard layout: either <a href="/wiki/QWERTY" title="QWERTY">QWERTY</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_Simplified_Keyboard" title="Dvorak Simplified Keyboard">Dvorak</a>. These choices could not be changed while programs were running, unlike the <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIc" title="Apple IIc">Apple IIc</a>, which had a keyboard switch directly above the keyboard, allowing switching on the fly.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A major limitation of the Apple II and DOS 3.3 was the way it addressed resources, which made it highly desirable for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripherals" title="Peripherals">peripherals</a> to be installed in standardized locations (slot 5 and 6 reserved for storage devices, slot 2 reserved for serial communication interfaces, etc.) This forced the user to identify a peripheral by its physical location, such as PR#6, CATALOG, D1, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beneathdos_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-beneathdos-10">[10]</a></sup> The Apple III introduced an advanced operating system called <a href="/wiki/Apple_SOS" title="Apple SOS">Apple SOS</a>, pronounced "apple sauce". Its ability to address resources by name instead of a physical location allowed the Apple III to be more scalable. Apple SOS also allowed the full capacity of a storage device to be used as a single volume, such as the <a href="/wiki/Apple_ProFile" title="Apple ProFile">Apple ProFile</a> hard disk drive. Also, Apple SOS supported a hierarchical file system (HFS). Some of the features and code base of Apple SOS made their way into the Apple II's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ProDOS" title="ProDOS">ProDOS</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GS/OS" title="GS/OS">GS/OS</a> operating systems, as well as <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lisa" title="Apple Lisa">Lisa 7/7</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" title="Apple Macintosh">Macintosh system software</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>With a starting price between $4,340 to $7,800 US, the Apple III was more expensive than many of the <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a>-based business computers that were available at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avuqmb_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-avuqmb-1">[1]</a></sup> Little Apple III software was available besides VisiCalc, and because Apple did not view the Apple III as suitable for hobbyists, it did not provide much of the technical software information that accompanied the Apple II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moore198209_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-moore198209-11">[11]</a></sup> Originally intended as a direct replacement to the Apple II series, it was designed to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backward_compatible" title="Backward compatible">backward compatible</a> with Apple II software. However, since Apple did not want to encourage continued development of the II platform, Apple II compatibility existed only in a special "Apple II Mode" which was limited in its capabilities to the emulation of a basic 48 KB Apple II+ configuration. Special chips were intentionally added to prevent access to the III's advanced features such as its larger memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byte198501_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-byte198501-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peripherals">Peripherals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peripherals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Apple III had four expansion slots, a number that <i>inCider</i> in 1986 called "miserly".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obrien198609_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-obrien198609-12">[12]</a></sup> Apple II cards were compatible but risked violating government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_interference" title="Radio-frequency interference">RFI</a> regulations, and required Apple III-specific device drivers; <i>BYTE</i> stated that "Apple provides virtually no information on how to write them". As with software, Apple provided little hardware technical information with the computer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moore198209_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-moore198209-11">[11]</a></sup> but Apple III-specific products became available, such as one that made the computer compatible with the <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIe" title="Apple IIe">Apple IIe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obrien198609_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-obrien198609-12">[12]</a></sup> Several new Apple-produced peripherals were developed for the Apple III. The original Apple III came with a built-in real-time clock, which was recognized by Apple SOS. The clock was later removed from the "revised" model, and instead was made available as an add-on.</p>
<p>Along with the built-in floppy drive, the Apple III could also handle up to three additional external Disk III floppy disk drives. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disk_III" title="Disk III">Disk III</a> was only officially compatible with the Apple III. The Apple III Plus required an adapter from Apple to use the Disk III with its DB-25 disk port. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the introduction of the revised Apple III a year after launch Apple began offering the <a href="/wiki/Apple_ProFile" title="Apple ProFile">ProFile</a> external hard disk system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bott_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bott-14">[14]</a></sup> Costing US$3499 for 5MB, it also required a peripheral slot for the ProFile controller card.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revisions">Revisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Revisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Once the logic board design flaws were discovered, a newer logic board design was produced – which included a lower power requirement, wider traces and better designed chip sockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bott_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-bott-14">[14]</a></sup> The $3,495 revised model also included 256 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KiB" title="KiB">KiB</a> RAM as a standard configuration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bott_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-bott-14">[14]</a></sup> The 14,000 units of the original Apple III sold were returned and replaced with the entirely new revised model.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apple_III_Plus">Apple III Plus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Apple III Plus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apple discontinued the III in October 1983 because it violated Federal Communications Commission (FCC) regulations, and the FCC required the company to change the redesigned computer's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maceiie19840409_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-maceiie19840409-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shea19840123_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-shea19840123-16">[16]</a></sup> It introduced the <b>Apple III Plus</b> in December 1983 at a price of US$2995. This newer version included a built-in clock, video interlacing, standardized rear port connectors, 55-watt power supply, 256 KiB RAM as standard, and a redesigned, IIe-like keyboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bott_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-bott-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shea19840123_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-shea19840123-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Owners of the Apple III could purchase individual III Plus upgrades, like the clock and interlacing feature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shea19840123_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-shea19840123-16">[16]</a></sup> and obtain the newer logic board as a service replacement. A keyboard upgrade kit, dubbed "Apple III Plus upgrade kit" was also made available – which included the keyboard, cover, keyboard encoder ROM and logo replacements. This upgrade had to be installed by an authorized service technician.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_flaws">Design flaws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design flaws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Wozniak, the Apple III "had 100 percent hardware failures".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byte198501_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-byte198501-8">[8]</a></sup> Former Apple executive Taylor Pohlman recalled:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bartimo19841210_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bartimo19841210-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There was way too short a time frame in manufacturing and development. When the decision was made to announce, there were only three Apple IIIs in existence, and they were all <a href="/wiki/Wire_wrap" title="Wire wrap">wire-wrapped</a> boards.</p>
<p>The case of the Apple III had long since been set in concrete, so they had a certain size logic board to fit the circuits on ... They went to three different outside houses and nobody could get a layout that would fit on the board.</p>
<p>They used the smallest line circuit boards that could be used. They ran about 1,000 of these boards as preproduction units to give to the dealers as demonstration units. They really didn't work ... Apple swapped out the boards. The problem was, at this point there were other problems, things like chips that didn't fit. There were a million problems that you would normally take care of when you do your preproduction and pilot run. Basically, customers were shipped the pilot run.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> insisted on the idea of no fan or air vents – in order to make the computer run quietly. Jobs would later push this same ideology onto almost all Apple models he had control of – from the <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lisa" title="Apple Lisa">Apple Lisa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macintosh_128K" title="Macintosh 128K">Macintosh 128K</a> to the <a href="/wiki/IMac" title="IMac">iMac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> To allow the computer to dissipate heat, the base of the Apple III was made of heavy cast aluminum, which supposedly acted as a heat sink. One undeniable advantage to the aluminum case was a reduction in RFI (Radio Frequency Interference), a problem which had plagued the Apple II series throughout its history. Unlike the Apple II series, the power supply was mounted – without its own shell – in a compartment separate from the logic board. The decision to use an aluminum shell ultimately led to engineering issues which resulted in the Apple III's reliability problems. The lead time for manufacturing the shells was high, and this had to be done before the motherboard was finalized. Later it was realized that there wasn't enough room on the motherboard for all of the components unless narrow traces were used.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apple_III%2B_case.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2500" data-file-width="2500" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Apple_III%2B_case.jpg/200px-Apple_III%2B_case.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Apple_III%2B_case.jpg/300px-Apple_III%2B_case.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Apple_III%2B_case.jpg/400px-Apple_III%2B_case.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apple_III%2B_case.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Apple III Plus showing the RFI shield over the floppy drive and the cast aluminum case</div>

<p>Many Apple IIIs were thought to have failed due to their inability to properly dissipate heat. <i>inCider</i> stated in 1986 that "Heat has always been a formidable enemy of the Apple ///",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obrien198609_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-obrien198609-12">[12]</a></sup> and some users reported that their Apple IIIs became so hot that the chips started dislodging from the board, causing the screen to display garbled data or their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floppy_disks" title="Floppy disks">disk</a> to come out of the slot "melted".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BYTE" title="BYTE">BYTE</a></i> wrote, "the integrated circuits tended to <a href="/wiki/Chip_creep" title="Chip creep">wander out of their sockets</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moore198209_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-moore198209-11">[11]</a></sup> Apple advised customers to tilt the front of the Apple III six inches above the desk, and then drop it to reseat the chips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apconf_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-apconf-4">[4]</a></sup> Other analyses blame a faulty automatic chip insertion process, not heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Case designer <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Manock" title="Jerry Manock">Jerry Manock</a> denied the design flaw charges, stating that tests proved that the unit adequately dissipated the internal heat. The primary cause, he claimed, was a major logic board design problem. The logic board used "fineline" technology that was not fully mature at the time, with narrow, closely spaced traces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youtube.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-youtube.com-20">[20]</a></sup> When chips were "stuffed" into the board and <a href="/wiki/Wave_soldering" title="Wave soldering">wave-soldered</a>, solder bridges would form between traces that were not supposed to be connected. This caused numerous short circuits, which required hours of costly diagnosis and hand rework to fix. Apple designed a new circuit board, with more layers and normal-width traces. The new logic board was laid out by one designer on a huge drafting board, rather than using the costly <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">CAD</a>-<a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">CAM</a> system used for the previous board, and the new design worked.</p>
<p>Earlier Apple III units came with a built-in real time clock. The hardware, however, would fail after prolonged use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moore198209_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-moore198209-11">[11]</a></sup> Assuming that <a href="/wiki/National_Semiconductor" title="National Semiconductor">National Semiconductor</a> would test all parts before shipping them, Apple did not perform this level of testing. Apple was soldering chips directly to boards, and could not easily change out a bad chip if one was found. Eventually, Apple solved this problem by removing the real-time clock from the Apple III's specification rather than shipping the Apple III with the clock pre-installed, and then sold the peripheral as a level 1 technician add-on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apconf_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-apconf-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_failure">Commercial failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pohlman stated that Apple was only selling 500 units a month by late 1981, mostly as replacements. The company was able to raise monthly sales to 5,000, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a>'s successful launch encouraged software companies to develop for it instead, causing Apple to shift focus to the Lisa and Macintosh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bartimo19841210_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-bartimo19841210-17">[17]</a></sup> By early 1984 sales were only to existing III owners, Apple itself—its 4500 employees had about 3000-4500 units—and some small businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maceiie19840409_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-maceiie19840409-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shea19840123_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-shea19840123-16">[16]</a></sup> Despite formerly devoting the majority of its R&amp;D to the III and so ignoring the II that for a while dealers had difficulty in obtaining the latter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcmullen19840221_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcmullen19840221-21">[21]</a></sup> Apple discontinued the Apple III series on 24 April 1984, four months after introducing the III Plus, after selling 65-75,000 computers and replacing 14,000 defective units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coventry20131006_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-coventry20131006-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jobs stated that Apple lost "infinite, incalculable amounts" of money on the Apple III.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coventry20131006_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-coventry20131006-22">[22]</a></sup> Pohlman claimed that there was a "stigma" at the company associated with having contributed to the computer. Most employees who worked on the III reportedly left Apple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bartimo19841210_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-bartimo19841210-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filesystem" title="Filesystem">filesystem</a> and some design ideas from <a href="/wiki/Apple_SOS" title="Apple SOS">Apple SOS</a>, the Apple III's operating system, were part of <a href="/wiki/Apple_ProDOS" title="Apple ProDOS">Apple ProDOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_GS/OS" title="Apple GS/OS">Apple GS/OS</a>, the major operating systems for the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II_series" title="Apple II series">Apple II series</a> following the demise of the Apple III, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lisa" title="Apple Lisa">Apple Lisa</a>, which was the de facto business-oriented successor to the Apple III. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierarchical_file_system" title="Hierarchical file system">hierarchical file system</a> influenced the evolution of the Macintosh: while the original <a href="/wiki/Macintosh_File_System" title="Macintosh File System">Macintosh File System</a> (MFS) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_file_system" title="Flat file system">flat file system</a> designed for a floppy disk without subdirectories, subsequent file systems were hierarchical. By comparison, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a>'s first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAT12" title="FAT12">file system</a> (again designed for floppy disks) was also flat and later versions (designed for hard disks) were hierarchical.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the start of the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Pictures" title="Walt Disney Pictures">Walt Disney Pictures</a> film <a href="/wiki/Tron" title="Tron">TRON</a>, lead character Kevin Flynn (played by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges" title="Jeff Bridges">Jeff Bridges</a>) is seen hacking into the ENCOM <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainframe" title="Mainframe">mainframe</a> using an Apple III.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
