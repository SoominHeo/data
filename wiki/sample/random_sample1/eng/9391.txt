<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Frame story</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>frame story</b> (also known as a <b>frame tale</b> or <b>frame narrative</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_technique" title="Literary technique">literary technique</a> that sometimes serves as a companion piece to a <a href="/wiki/Story_within_a_story" title="Story within a story">story within a story</a>, whereby an introductory or main narrative is presented, at least in part, for the purpose of setting the stage either for a more emphasized second narrative or for a set of shorter stories. The frame story leads readers from a first story into another, smaller one (or several ones) within it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frame_story&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest known frame stories are those preserved on the ancient Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papyrus_Westcar" title="Papyrus Westcar">Papyrus Westcar</a>. Other early examples are from 1st millennium BC <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">India</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Indian_epic_poetry" title="Indian epic poetry">Sanskrit epics</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vishnu_Sarma" title="Vishnu Sarma">Vishnu Sarma</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Panchatantra" title="Panchatantra">Panchatantra</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Syntipas" title="Syntipas">Syntipas</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Wise_Masters" title="Seven Wise Masters">The Seven Wise Masters</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fable</a> collections <i><a href="/wiki/Hitopadesha" title="Hitopadesha">Hitopadesha</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Baital_Pachisi" title="Baital Pachisi">Vikram and The Vampire</a></i> were written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This form gradually spread west through the centuries and became popular, giving rise to such classic frame tale collections as the <i><a href="/wiki/One_Thousand_and_One_Nights" title="One Thousand and One Nights">One Thousand and One Nights</a></i> (<i>Arabian Nights</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Decameron" title="The Decameron">The Decameron</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canterbury_Tales" title="Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i>. This format had flexibility in that various narrators could retain the stories they liked or understood, while dropping ones they didn't and adding new ones they heard from other places. This occurred particularly with <i>One Thousand and One Nights</i>, where different versions over the centuries have included different stories.</p>
<p>The use of a frame story in which a single narrative is set in the context of the telling of a story is also a technique with a long history, dating back at least to the beginning section of the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i>, in which the narrator <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a> tells of his wandering in the court of King <a href="/wiki/Alcinous" title="Alcinous">Alcinous</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_set_of_stories">A set of stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frame_story&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A set of stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_device" title="Literary device">literary device</a> acts as a convenient <a href="/wiki/Conceit" title="Conceit">conceit</a> for the organization of a set of smaller narratives which are either of the devising of the author, or taken from a previous stock of popular tales slightly altered by the author for the purpose of the longer narrative. Sometimes a story within the main narrative can be used to sum up or encapsulate some aspect of the framing story, in which case it is referred to in literary criticism by the French term <i><a href="/wiki/Mise_en_abyme" title="Mise en abyme">mise en abyme</a></i>.</p>
<p>A typical example of a frame story is <i><a href="/wiki/One_Thousand_and_One_Nights" title="One Thousand and One Nights">One Thousand and One Nights</a></i>, in which the character <a href="/wiki/Scheherazade" title="Scheherazade">Scheherazade</a> narrates a set of <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a> to the Sultan <a href="/wiki/List_of_One_Thousand_and_One_Nights_characters#Shahry.C4.81r" title="List of One Thousand and One Nights characters">Shahriyar</a> over many nights. Many of Scheherazade's tales are also frame stories, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sindbad_the_Sailor" title="Sindbad the Sailor">Tale of Sindbad the Seaman and Sindbad the Landsman</a></i>, a collection of adventures related by Sindbad the Seaman to Sindbad the Landsman.</p>
<p>Extensive use of this device is found in <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphoses_(poem)" title="Metamorphoses (poem)">Metamorphoses</a></i>, where the stories nest several deep, to allow the inclusion of many different tales in one work. <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Brontë">Emily Brontë</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights" title="Wuthering Heights">Wuthering Heights</a></i> uses this literary device to tell the story of Heathcliff and Catherine, along with the subplots. Her sister <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bront%C3%AB" title="Anne Brontë">Anne</a> also uses this device in her <a href="/wiki/Epistolary_novel" title="Epistolary novel">epistolary novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tenant_of_Wildfell_Hall" title="The Tenant of Wildfell Hall">The Tenant of Wildfell Hall</a></i>. The main heroine's diary is framed by the narrator's story and letters.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">Mary Shelley</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a></i> is another good example of a book with multiple framed narratives. In the book, Robert Walton writes letters to his sister describing the story told to him by Victor Frankenstein; Frankenstein's story contains the creature's story; the creature's story even briefly contains the story of a family among whom it had been living.</p>
<p>Frame stories have also appeared in other media, such as <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>. <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">Neil Gaiman</a>'s comic book series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sandman_(Vertigo)" title="The Sandman (Vertigo)">The Sandman</a></i> featured a story arc called <i>Worlds End</i> which consisted of frame stories, and sometimes even featured stories within stories within stories.</p>
<p>Frame stories are often organized as a gathering of people in one place for the exchange of stories. Each character tells his or her tale, and the frame tale progresses in that manner. Historically famous frame stories include <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Chaucer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales" title="The Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i>, about a group of pilgrims who tell stories on their journey to Canterbury; and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Boccaccio</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Decameron" title="The Decameron">Decameron</a></i> about a group of young aristocrats escaping the Black Death in the countryside and spending the time telling stories.</p>

<p>Sometimes only one storyteller exists, and in this case there might be different levels of distance between the reader and author. In this mode, the frame tale can become more fuzzy. In <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">Washington Irving</a>'s <i>Sketch Book</i>, which contains "<a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Sleepy_Hollow" title="The Legend of Sleepy Hollow">The Legend of Sleepy Hollow</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Rip_Van_Winkle" title="Rip Van Winkle">Rip Van Winkle</a>" among others, the conceit is that the author of the book is not Irving, but a certain gentleman named Crayon. Here the frame includes the world of the imagined Crayon, his stories, and the possible<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> reader who is assumed to play along and "know" who Crayon is.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donald_Westlake" title="Donald Westlake">Donald Westlake</a>'s short story "No Story" is a parody of frame stories, in which a series of narrators start to tell stories, each of which contains a narrator who starts to tell a story, culminating in a narrator who announces that there will be no story. Essentially, it is a frame story without a story to be framed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_story">Single story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frame_story&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Single story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When there is a single story, the frame story is used for other purposes – chiefly to position the reader's attitude toward the tale. One common one is to draw attention to the <a href="/wiki/Unreliable_narrator" title="Unreliable narrator">narrator's unreliability</a>. By explicitly making the narrator a character within the frame story, the writer distances himself from the narrator; he may also characterize the narrator to cast doubt on his truthfulness. In <a href="/wiki/P._G._Wodehouse" title="P. G. Wodehouse">P. G. Wodehouse</a>'s stories of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mr_Mulliner" title="Mr Mulliner">Mr Mulliner</a>, Mulliner is made a fisherman in order to cast doubt on the outrageous stories he tells. The movie <i><a href="/wiki/Amadeus_(film)" title="Amadeus (film)">Amadeus</a></i> is framed as a story an old <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salieri" title="Antonio Salieri">Antonio Salieri</a> tells to a young priest, because the movie is based more on stories Salieri told about <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> than on historical fact.</p>
<p>Another use is a form of <a href="/wiki/Procatalepsis" title="Procatalepsis">procatalepsis</a>, where the writer puts the readers' possible reactions to the story in the characters listening to it. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Princess_Bride" title="The Princess Bride">The Princess Bride</a></i> the frame of a grandfather reading the story to his reluctant grandson puts the cynical reaction a viewer might have to the romantic fairytale into the story in the grandson's persona, and helps defuse it. This is the use when the frame tells a story that lacks a strong <a href="/wiki/Narrative_hook" title="Narrative hook">narrative hook</a> in its opening; the narrator can engage the reader's interest by telling the story to answer the curiosity of his listeners, or by warning them that the story began in an ordinary seeming way, but they must follow it to understand later actions, thereby identifying the reader's wondering whether the story is worth reading to the listeners'. Such an approach was used by Edith Wharton in her novella <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Frome" title="Ethan Frome">Ethan Frome</a>, in which a nameless narrator hears from many characters in the town of Starkfield about the main character Ethan's story.</p>
<p>A specialized form of the frame is a <a href="/wiki/Dream_vision" title="Dream vision">dream vision</a>, where the narrator claims to have gone to sleep, dreamed the events of the story, and then awoken to tell the tale. In medieval Europe, this was a common device, used to indicate that the events included are fictional; <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> used it in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_the_Duchess" title="The Book of the Duchess">The Book of the Duchess</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_House_of_Fame" title="The House of Fame">The House of Fame</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Parlement_of_Foules" title="Parlement of Foules">Parlement of Foules</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Good_Women" title="The Legend of Good Women">The Legend of Good Women</a></i> (the last also containing a multi-story frame story within the dream). In modern usage, it is sometimes used in works of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> as a means toward <a href="/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief" title="Suspension of disbelief">suspension of disbelief</a> about the marvels depicted in the story. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>, in his essay "<a href="/wiki/On_Fairy-Stories" title="On Fairy-Stories">On Fairy-Stories</a>" complained of such devices as unwillingness to treat the genre seriously. <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s <i>Alice</i> stories (<i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i>) includes such a frame, but unlike most usages, the stories themselves use dream-like logic and sequences; most dream frames frame stories that appear exactly as if occurring in real life. The writer <a href="/wiki/John_Bunyan" title="John Bunyan">John Bunyan</a> used a dream device in the Christian allegory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrim%27s_Progress" title="Pilgrim's Progress">Pilgrim's Progress</a> and its sequel, explaining that they were dreams he had while he was in prison and felt God wanted him to write down. This worked because it made what might have been seen as a fantasy something more realistic and meaningful to other who believed as he did.</p>
<p>Still, even when the story proceeds realistically, the dream frame casts doubt on the events. In the book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i>, the events really occur; the dream frame added for <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">the movie</a> detracts from the validity of the fantasy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frame_story&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with all <a href="/wiki/Conceit" title="Conceit">literary conceits</a>, the frame tale has many variations, some clearly within the confines of the conceit, some on the border, and some pushing the boundaries of understanding. The main goal of a frame tale is as a conceit which can adequately collect otherwise disparate tales. It has been mostly replaced, in modern literature, by the short story collection or anthology absent of any authorial conceit and other rhetorical devices.</p>
<p>To be a frame narrative, the story must act primarily as an occasion for the telling of other stories. If the framing narrative has primary or equal interest, then it is not usually a frame narrative. For example, <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a> narrates much of the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i> to the <a href="/wiki/Scheria" title="Scheria">Phaeacians</a>, but, even though this recollection forms a great part of the poem, the events after and before the interpolated recollection are of greater interest than the memory.</p>
<p>Another notable example that plays with frame narrative is the 1994 film <i><a href="/wiki/Forrest_Gump" title="Forrest Gump">Forrest Gump</a></i>. Most of the film is narrated by Forrest to various companions on the park bench. However, in the last fifth or so of the film, Forrest gets up and leaves the bench, and we follow him as he meets with Jenny and her son. This final segment suddenly has no narrator unlike the rest of the film that came before it, but is instead told through Forrest and Jenny's dialogues.</p>
<p>This approach is also demonstrated in the 2008 film <i><a href="/wiki/Slumdog_Millionaire" title="Slumdog Millionaire">Slumdog Millionaire</a></i> (adapted from the 2005 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Q_%26_A_(novel)" title="Q &amp; A (novel)">Q &amp; A</a></i>), about a poor street kid Jamal coming close to winning <i><a href="/wiki/Kaun_Banega_Crorepati" title="Kaun Banega Crorepati">Kaun Banega Crorepati</a></i> (the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> version of <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Wants_to_Be_a_Millionaire%3F" title="Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?">Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?</a></i>) and then being suspected of cheating. Most of the story is narrated at a police station by Jamal, who narrates how he knew the answers to the questions as the show is played back on video. The show itself then serves as another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Framing_device" title="Framing device">framing device</a>, as Jamal sees <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">flashbacks</a> of his past as each question is asked. The last portion of the film then unfolds without any narrator.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Darkness" title="Heart of Darkness">Heart of Darkness</a></i> demonstrates a narrator telling a story, while the protagonist is quoted so as to give the framed appearance that he is telling the story. The narrator provides the transition to the one speaking the story.</p>
<p>A famous literary example is Emily Brontë's <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights" title="Wuthering Heights">Wuthering Heights</a></i>, whereby we learn events through a visitor to the house of the title, who in turn has been told these events by the housekeeper of the Linton family. None of the main characters ever directly narrates.</p>
<p>Another example is <a href="/wiki/David_Mitchell_(author)" title="David Mitchell (author)">David Mitchell's</a> 2004 novel <a href="/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_(novel)" title="Cloud Atlas (novel)">Cloud Atlas</a>, which is a frame story inside a frame story inside a frame story – and so on. There are six stories in all, each framed inside the other, forming a <a href="/wiki/Chiastic_structure" title="Chiastic structure">chiastic structure</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Catharism" title="Catharism">semi-catharistic</a> tale spanning centuries of narrative.</p>
<p>Frame stories are found in many <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a>, such as the early <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Quest_IV" title="Dragon Quest IV">Dragon Quest IV</a></i>, released in 1990. This literary device can also be sparingly used to achieve secondary ends. For instance, the <i><a href="/wiki/Shining_Force" title="Shining Force">Shining Force</a></i> series of role playing games use narrators within frame stories to implement things like starting, saving and exiting the game without breaking the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_wall" title="Fourth wall">fourth wall</a> entirely, or rather by constructing a second fourth wall to shield the player from having to suspend his/her disbelief as much. A similar approach was used in the <a href="/wiki/Open_world" title="Open world">open world</a> video game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mafia:_The_City_of_Lost_Heaven" title="Mafia: The City of Lost Heaven">Mafia: The City of Lost Heaven</a></i>, where the game's missions are presented as a series of flashbacks narrated by Tommy Angelo, as he confesses his involvement with an <a href="/wiki/American_Mafia" title="American Mafia">American Mafia</a> family. Another example of frame stories in video games is the game <a href="/wiki/Catherine_(video_game)" title="Catherine (video game)">Catherine</a>. Catherine's first scene is a frame story.</p>
<p>In the game <i><a href="/wiki/Bastion_(video_game)" title="Bastion (video game)">Bastion</a></i>, the Kid's actions are narrated by one of the other characters, Rucks, as he tells the Kid's story to another character while the Kid is in the final area. Because Rucks never learns about the Kid's actions explicitly, the narration is mostly anecdotal and primarily serves to fill in background information about each level; however, it also serves to keep the fourth wall intact, as player deaths are portrayed as jokes on the narrator's part. After the player's actions catch up to the story, the narration switches to a more speculative tone, as Rucks no longer knows what the player is doing.</p>
