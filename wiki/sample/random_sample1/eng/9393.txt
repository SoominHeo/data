<p><b>Liquid oxygen</b>—abbreviated <b>LOx</b>, <b>LOX</b> or <b>Lox</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> industries—is one of the physical forms of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elemental</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_oxygen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Liquid oxygen has a pale blue color and is strongly <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a>; it can be suspended between the poles of a powerful horseshoe magnet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Liquid oxygen has a density of 1.141 g/cm<sup>3</sup> (1.141 kg/L or 1141 kg/m<sup>3</sup>) and is <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenic</a> with a freezing point of 54.36 K (−218.79 °C; −361.82 °F) and a boiling point of 90.19 K (−182.96 °C; −297.33 °F) at 101.325 kPa (760 mmHg). Liquid oxygen has an <a href="/wiki/Expansion_ratio" title="Expansion ratio">expansion ratio</a> of 1:861 under 1 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmosphere</a> (100 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>) and 20 °C (68 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and because of this, it is used in some commercial and military aircraft as transportable source of breathing oxygen.</p>
<p>Because of its cryogenic nature, liquid oxygen can cause the materials it touches to become extremely brittle. Liquid oxygen is also a very powerful oxidizing agent: organic materials will burn rapidly and energetically in liquid oxygen. Further, if soaked in liquid oxygen, some materials such as coal briquettes, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a>, etc., can detonate unpredictably from sources of ignition such as flames, sparks or impact from light blows. <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">Petrochemicals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>, often exhibit this behavior.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tetraoxygen" title="Tetraoxygen">tetraoxygen</a> molecule (O<sub>4</sub>) was first predicted in 1924 by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis" title="Gilbert N. Lewis">Gilbert N. Lewis</a>, who proposed it to explain why liquid oxygen defied <a href="/wiki/Curie%27s_law" title="Curie's law">Curie's law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Modern computer simulations indicate that although there are no stable O<sub>4</sub> molecules in liquid oxygen, O<sub>2</sub> molecules do tend to associate in pairs with antiparallel <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spins</a>, forming transient O<sub>4</sub> units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">Liquid nitrogen</a> has a lower boiling point at −196 °C (77 K) than oxygen's −183 °C (90 K), and vessels containing liquid nitrogen can condense oxygen from air: when most of the nitrogen has evaporated from such a vessel there is a risk that liquid oxygen remaining can react violently with organic material. Conversely, liquid nitrogen or <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">liquid air</a> can be oxygen-enriched by letting it stand in open air; atmospheric oxygen dissolves in it, while nitrogen evaporates preferentially.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_oxygen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In commerce, liquid oxygen is classified as an <a href="/wiki/Industrial_gas" title="Industrial gas">industrial gas</a> and is widely used for industrial and medical purposes. Liquid oxygen is obtained from the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> found naturally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> by <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">cryogenic air separation plant</a>.</p>
<p>Liquid oxygen is a common <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic</a> liquid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> propellant for <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">spacecraft rocket</a> applications, usually in combination with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_methane" title="Liquid methane">methane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20140307_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf20140307-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fg20121120_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fg20121120-7">[7]</a></sup> Liquid oxygen is useful in this role because it creates a high <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was used in the very first rocket applications like the <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V2</a> missile (under the name <i>A-Stoff</i> and <i>Sauerstoff</i>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redstone_(rocket)" title="Redstone (rocket)">Redstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(rocket)" title="Atlas (rocket)">Atlas</a> boosters, and the ascent stages of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(rocket_family)" title="Saturn (rocket family)">Apollo Saturn rockets</a>. Liquid oxygen was also used in some early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBMs</a>, although more modern ICBMs do not use liquid oxygen because its cryogenic properties and need for regular replenishment to replace boiloff make it harder to maintain and launch quickly. Many modern rockets use liquid oxygen, including the main engines on the now-retired <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>.</p>
<p>Liquid oxygen also had extensive use in making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyliquit_explosive" title="Oxyliquit explosive">oxyliquit explosives</a>, but is rarely used now due to a high rate of accidents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is also used in the <a href="/wiki/Activated_sludge" title="Activated sludge">activated sludge</a> processing in waste water treatment to maintain a high level of micro-organisms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_oxygen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>By 1845, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> had managed to liquefy most gases then known to exist. Six gases, however, resisted every attempt at liquefaction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and were known at the time as "permanent gases". They were oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>.</li>
<li>In 1877, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Paul_Cailletet" title="Louis Paul Cailletet">Louis Paul Cailletet</a> in France and <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Pictet" title="Raoul Pictet">Raoul Pictet</a> in Switzerland succeeded in producing the first droplets of liquid air.</li>
<li>The first measurable quantity of liquid oxygen was produced by Polish professors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zygmunt_Wr%C3%B3blewski" title="Zygmunt Wróblewski">Zygmunt Wróblewski</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karol_Olszewski" title="Karol Olszewski">Karol Olszewski</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">Jagiellonian University</a> in Kraków) on April 5, 1883.</li>
</ul>
