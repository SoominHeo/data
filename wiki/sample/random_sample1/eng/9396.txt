<p><b>Ann-Margret</b> (born <b>Ann-Margret Olsson</b>; April 28, 1941) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish-American" title="Swedish-American">Swedish-American</a> actress, singer, and dancer. As an actress, she is best known for her roles in <i><a href="/wiki/Bye_Bye_Birdie_(film)" title="Bye Bye Birdie (film)">Bye Bye Birdie</a></i> (1963), <i><a href="/wiki/Viva_Las_Vegas" title="Viva Las Vegas">Viva Las Vegas</a></i> (1964), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cincinnati_Kid" title="The Cincinnati Kid">The Cincinnati Kid</a></i> (1965), <i><a href="/wiki/Carnal_Knowledge" title="Carnal Knowledge">Carnal Knowledge</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/Tommy_(1975_film)" title="Tommy (1975 film)">Tommy</a></i> (1975), <i><a href="/wiki/Grumpy_Old_Men_(film)" title="Grumpy Old Men (film)">Grumpy Old Men</a></i> (1993), and <i><a href="/wiki/Grumpier_Old_Men" title="Grumpier Old Men">Grumpier Old Men</a></i> (1995). She has won five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Awards" title="Golden Globe Awards">Golden Globe Awards</a> and been nominated for two <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Awards</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award" title="Screen Actors Guild Award">Screen Actors Guild Award</a>, and six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy_Awards" title="Emmy Awards">Emmy Awards</a>. In 2010, she won her first Emmy Award for her guest appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit">Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit</a></i>. Her singing and acting careers span five decades, starting in 1961; initially, she was billed as a female version of <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>. She had a <a href="/wiki/I_Just_Don%27t_Understand" title="I Just Don't Understand">minor hit</a> in 1961 and a charting album in 1964, and scored a disco hit in 1979. In 2001, she recorded a critically acclaimed gospel album, and an album of Christmas songs from 2004 continues to be available.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ann-Margret was born in Valsjöbyn, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4mtland_County" title="Jämtland County">Jämtland County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmReference_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FilmReference-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the daughter of Anna Regina (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names" title="Married and maiden names">née</a> Aronsson) and Carl Gustav Olsson, a native of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96rnsk%C3%B6ldsvik" title="Örnsköldsvik">Örnsköldsvik</a>. She later described Valsjöbyn as a small town "of lumberjacks and farmers high up near the Arctic Circle".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:MOSQUOTE" title="Wikipedia:MOSQUOTE"><span title="The text near this tag needs citation. (February 2014)">this quote needs a citation</span></a></i>]</sup> Her father worked in the United States during his youth and moved there again in 1942, working with the Johnson Electrical Company, while his wife and daughter stayed behind.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Ann-Margret and her mother moved to the United States in November 1946, and her father took her to <a href="/wiki/Radio_City_Music_Hall" title="Radio City Music Hall">Radio City Music Hall</a> on the day they arrived. They settled just outside Chicago, in <a href="/wiki/Wilmette,_Illinois" title="Wilmette, Illinois">Wilmette, Illinois</a>. She became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalized" title="Naturalized">naturalized</a> citizen of the United States in 1949 and took her first dance lessons at the Marjorie Young School of Dance, showing natural ability from the start, easily mimicking all the steps. Her parents were supportive; her mother handmade all her costumes. Ann-Margret's mother became a funeral parlor receptionist<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FilmReference_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FilmReference-1">[1]</a></sup> after her husband suffered a severe injury on his job.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yahoo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo-3">[3]</a></sup> While a teenager, Ann-Margret appeared on the Morris B. Sachs <i>Amateur Hour</i>, Don McNeill's <i>Breakfast Club</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Mack_(radio-TV_host)" title="Ted Mack (radio-TV host)">Ted Mack</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Original_Amateur_Hour" title="The Original Amateur Hour">Amateur Hour</a></i>.</p>
<p>While she attended <a href="/wiki/New_Trier_High_School" title="New Trier High School">New Trier High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Winnetka,_Illinois" title="Winnetka, Illinois">Winnetka, Illinois</a>, she continued to star in theatricals. In 1959, she enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a>, where she was a member of the sorority <a href="/wiki/Kappa_Alpha_Theta" title="Kappa Alpha Theta">Kappa Alpha Theta</a>, but did not graduate. As part of a group known as the Suttletones, she performed at the Mist, a Chicago nightclub, and went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas, Nevada</a>, for a promised club date which fell through after the group arrived. They then moved on to Los Angeles, and through agent Georgia Lund, secured club dates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newport_Beach" title="Newport Beach">Newport Beach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reno,_Nevada" title="Reno, Nevada">Reno, Nevada</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The group finally arrived at <a href="/wiki/Dunes_(hotel_and_casino)" title="Dunes (hotel and casino)">the Dunes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas</a>, which also headlined <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Al_Hirt" title="Al Hirt">Al Hirt</a> at that time. <a href="/wiki/George_Burns" title="George Burns">George Burns</a> heard of her performance, and she auditioned for his annual holiday show, in which Burns and she performed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Softshoe" title="Softshoe">softshoe</a> routine. <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> proclaimed, "George Burns has a gold mine in Ann-Margret ... she has a definite style of her own, which can easily guide her to star status."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret199477_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret199477-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ann-Margret began recording for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" title="RCA Victor">RCA Victor</a> in 1961. Her first RCA Victor recording was "Lost Love" from her debut album <i>And Here She Is: Ann-Margret</i>, produced in Nashville with <a href="/wiki/Chet_Atkins" title="Chet Atkins">Chet Atkins</a> on guitar, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jordanaires" title="Jordanaires">Jordanaires</a> (Elvis Presley's backup singers), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anita_Kerr_Singers" title="Anita Kerr Singers">Anita Kerr Singers</a>, with liner notes by mentor George Burns. She had a sexy, throaty singing voice, and RCA attempted to capitalize on the 'female Elvis' comparison by having her record a version of "<a href="/wiki/Heartbreak_Hotel" title="Heartbreak Hotel">Heartbreak Hotel</a>" and other songs stylistically similar to Presley's. She scored the minor hit "I Just Don't Understand" (from her second LP), which entered the Billboard <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a> in the third week of August 1961 and stayed six weeks, peaking at number 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-5">[5]</a></sup> The song was later covered in live performances by <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> and was recorded during a live performance at the BBC. Her only charting album was <i>The Beauty and the Beard</i> (1964), on which she was accompanied by trumpeter <a href="/wiki/Al_Hirt" title="Al Hirt">Al Hirt</a>. Ann-Margret appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jack_Benny_Program" title="The Jack Benny Program">The Jack Benny Program</a></i> in 1961 (season 11, episode24). She also sang at the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a> presentation in 1962, singing the Oscar-nominated song "Theme from <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_in_Paradise_(film)" title="Bachelor in Paradise (film)">Bachelor in Paradise</a>." Her contract with RCA Victor ended in 1966.</p>
<p>In the late 1970s and early 1980s, she had hits on the dance charts, the most successful being 1979's "Love Rush," which peaked at number eight on the disco/dance charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitburn2004p21_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitburn2004p21-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, working with <a href="/wiki/Art_Greenhaw" title="Art Greenhaw">Art Greenhaw</a>, she recorded the album <i>God Is Love: The Gospel Sessions</i>. The album went on to earn a Grammy nomination and a Dove nomination for best album of the year in a gospel category. Her album <i>Ann-Margret's Christmas Carol Collection</i>, also produced and arranged by Greenhaw, was recorded in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting">Acting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="892" data-file-width="785" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg/220px-Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg/330px-Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg/440px-Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ann-Margret_Publicity.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Publicity photo from 1960s</div>

<p>In 1961, she filmed a <a href="/wiki/Screen_test" title="Screen test">screen test</a> at <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> and was signed to a seven-year contract. Ann-Margret made her film debut in a loan-out to <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">United Artists</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Pocketful_of_Miracles" title="Pocketful of Miracles">Pocketful of Miracles</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Bette_Davis" title="Bette Davis">Bette Davis</a>. It was a remake of the 1933 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_for_a_Day" title="Lady for a Day">Lady for a Day</a></i>. Both versions were directed by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Capra" title="Frank Capra">Frank Capra</a>.</p>
<p>Then came a 1962 remake of <a href="/wiki/Rodgers_and_Hammerstein" title="Rodgers and Hammerstein">Rodgers and Hammerstein</a>'s musical <i><a href="/wiki/State_Fair_(1962_film)" title="State Fair (1962 film)">State Fair</a></i>, playing the "bad girl" role of Emily opposite <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Darin" title="Bobby Darin">Bobby Darin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pat_Boone" title="Pat Boone">Pat Boone</a>. She had tested for the part of Margie, the "good girl", but seemed too seductive to the studio bosses, who decided on the switch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret199491_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret199491-8">[8]</a></sup> The two roles represented two sides of her real-life personality — shy and reserved offstage, but wildly exuberant and sensuous onstage. In her autobiography, the actress wrote that she changed "from Little Miss Lollipop to Sexpot-Banshee" once the music began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret199496_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret199496-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her next starring role, as the all-American teenager Kim from Sweet Apple, Ohio, in <i><a href="/wiki/Bye_Bye_Birdie_(film)" title="Bye Bye Birdie (film)">Bye Bye Birdie</a></i> (1963), made her a major star. The premiere at Radio City Music Hall, 16 years after her first visit to the famed theater, was a smash hit: the highest first-week grossing film to date at the Music Hall. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_magazine" title="Life magazine">Life</a></i> put her on the cover for the second time and announced that the "torrid dancing almost replaces the central heating in the theater."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994102_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994102-10">[10]</a></sup> She was then asked to sing "Baby, Won't You Please Come Home" at President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>'s private birthday party at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waldorf-Astoria" title="Waldorf-Astoria">Waldorf-Astoria</a>, one year after <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a>'s famous "Happy Birthday".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994104_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994104-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ann-Margret met Elvis Presley on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MGM" title="MGM">MGM</a> soundstage when the two filmed <i><a href="/wiki/Viva_Las_Vegas" title="Viva Las Vegas">Viva Las Vegas</a></i> (1964). She recorded three duets with Presley for the film: "The Lady Loves Me", "You're The Boss", and a duet version [with Presley] of his song, "Today, Tomorrow, and Forever"; only "The Lady Loves Me" made it into the final film and none of them were commercially released until years after Presley's death, due to concerns by <a href="/wiki/Colonel_Tom_Parker" title="Colonel Tom Parker">Colonel Tom Parker</a> that Ann-Margret's presence threatened to overshadow Elvis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Ann-Margret introduced Presley to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Winters_(choreographer)" title="David Winters (choreographer)">David Winters</a>, whom she recommended as a choreographer for their film. <i>Viva Las Vegas</i> was Winters' first feature film choreography job and was his first of four movies with Presley, and his first of five films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Kitten_with_a_Whip" title="Kitten with a Whip">Kitten with a Whip</a></i> (1964), <i><a href="/wiki/Bus_Riley%27s_Back_in_Town" title="Bus Riley's Back in Town">Bus Riley's Back in Town</a></i> (1965), <i><a href="/wiki/Made_in_Paris" title="Made in Paris">Made in Paris</a></i> (1966), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Swinger" title="The Swinger">The Swinger</a></i> (1966), and two TV specials with Ann-Margret. Winters was nominated for the 1970 <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> for Outstanding Achievement in Choreography for his CBS Television Special: <i>Ann-Margret: From Hollywood with Love</i> (1969)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, Ann-Margret guest-starred in a popular episode of the <a href="/wiki/Animated_cartoon" title="Animated cartoon">animated TV series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Flintstones" title="The Flintstones">The Flintstones</a></i>, voicing Ann-Margrock, an animated version of herself. She sang the ballad "The Littlest Lamb" as a <a href="/wiki/Lullaby" title="Lullaby">lullaby</a> and the (literally) rocking song, "Ain't Gonna Be a Fool". Decades later, she recorded the theme song, a modified version of the <i>Viva Las Vegas</i> theme, to the live-action film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Flintstones_in_Viva_Rock_Vegas" title="The Flintstones in Viva Rock Vegas">The Flintstones in Viva Rock Vegas</a></i>, in character as Ann-Margrock.</p>
<p>While working on the film <i><a href="/wiki/Once_a_Thief_(1965_film)" title="Once a Thief (1965 film)">Once a Thief</a></i> (1965), she met future husband <a href="/wiki/Roger_Smith_(actor)" title="Roger Smith (actor)">Roger Smith</a>, who after his successful run on the private-eye television series <i><a href="/wiki/77_Sunset_Strip" title="77 Sunset Strip">77 Sunset Strip</a></i>, was performing a live club show at the <a href="/wiki/Hungry_i" title="Hungry i">Hungry i</a> on a bill with <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cosby" title="Bill Cosby">Bill Cosby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Don_Adams" title="Don Adams">Don Adams</a>. That meeting began their courtship, which met with resistance from her parents.</p>
<p>Ann-Margret starred in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cincinnati_Kid" title="The Cincinnati Kid">The Cincinnati Kid</a></i> in 1965 opposite <a href="/wiki/Steve_McQueen" title="Steve McQueen">Steve McQueen</a>. She also co-starred along with friend <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a> in the spy spoof <i><a href="/wiki/Murderers%27_Row_(film)" title="Murderers' Row (film)">Murderers' Row</a></i> (1966). Finally, she starred as the lead in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Swinger" title="The Swinger">The Swinger</a></i> in 1966 with <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Franciosa" title="Anthony Franciosa">Tony Franciosa</a>.</p>
<p>Her red hair color (she is a "natural brunette") was the idea of <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Guilaroff" title="Sydney Guilaroff">Sydney Guilaroff</a>, a hairdresser who changed the hair color of other famous actresses such as <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Ball" title="Lucille Ball">Lucille Ball</a>.</p>
<p>She was offered the title role in <i><a href="/wiki/Cat_Ballou" title="Cat Ballou">Cat Ballou</a></i> (1965), but her manager turned it down without telling her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In March 1966, Ann-Margret and entertainers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Wayne_%22Chuck%22_Day" title='Charles Wayne "Chuck" Day'>Chuck Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Jones" title="Mickey Jones">Mickey Jones</a> teamed up for a <a href="/wiki/United_Service_Organizations" title="United Service Organizations">USO</a> tour to entertain U.S. servicemen in remote parts of <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and other parts of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. She still has great affection for the veterans and refers to them as "my gentlemen". Ann-Margret, Day, and Jones reunited in November 2005 for an encore of this tour for veterans and troops at <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Air_Force_Base" title="Nellis Air Force Base">Nellis Air Force Base</a>, Nevada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LasVegasEvents_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-LasVegasEvents-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During a lull in her film career in July 1967, Ann-Margret gave her first live performance in Las Vegas, with her husband Roger Smith (whom she had married in 1967) taking over as her manager after that engagement. Elvis Presley and his entourage came to see her during the show's five-week run and to celebrate backstage. From thereon until his death, Presley sent her a guitar-shaped floral arrangement for each of her Vegas openings. After the first Vegas run ended, she followed up with a CBS television special <i>The Ann-Margret Show</i>, produced and directed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Winters_(choreographer)" title="David Winters (choreographer)">David Winters</a> on December 1, 1968, with guest-stars <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">Bob Hope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Benny" title="Jack Benny">Jack Benny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Thomas" title="Danny Thomas">Danny Thomas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carol_Burnett" title="Carol Burnett">Carol Burnett</a>. Then, she went back to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> as part of Hope's Christmas show. A second CBS television special followed, <i>Ann Margret: From Hollywood With Love</i>, directed and choreographed by David Winters and produced and distributed by Winters' company Winters-Rosen, with guest-stars Dean Martin and Lucille Ball. David Winters and the show were nominated for a Primetime Emmy in Outstanding Choreography.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1970, she returned to films with <i><a href="/wiki/R._P._M." title="R. P. M.">R. P. M.</a></i>, where she starred alongside <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Quinn" title="Anthony Quinn">Anthony Quinn</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/C.C._and_Company" title="C.C. and Company">C.C. and Company</a></i> which had Joe Namath as a biker and Ann-Margret playing a fashion journalist.</p>
<p>In 1971, she starred in <a href="/wiki/Mike_Nichols" title="Mike Nichols">Mike Nichols</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Carnal_Knowledge" title="Carnal Knowledge">Carnal Knowledge</a></i>, playing the over-loving girlfriend of a viciously abusive Jack Nicholson and garnering a nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress</a>.</p>
<p>On the set of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Train_Robbers" title="The Train Robbers">The Train Robbers</a></i> in Durango, Mexico, in June 1972, she told Nancy Anderson of <a href="/wiki/Copley_Press" title="Copley Press">Copley News Service</a> that she had been on the "<a href="/wiki/Grapefruit_diet" title="Grapefruit diet">grapefruit diet</a>" and had lost almost twenty pounds (134 to 115) eating unsweetened citrus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Sunday, September 10, 1972, while performing at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tahoe" title="Lake Tahoe">Lake Tahoe</a>, she fell 22 feet from an elevated platform to the stage and suffered injuries including a broken left arm, cheekbone, and jawbone. Husband Roger Smith flew a stolen plane from <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>, to Lake Tahoe to get his wife to the surgeons at the medical center at <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">UCLA</a> for treatment. She required meticulous <a href="/wiki/Plastic_surgery" title="Plastic surgery">facial reconstructive surgery</a> that required wiring her mouth shut and putting her on a liquid diet. Unable to work for 10 weeks, she ultimately returned to the stage almost back to normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994236.E2.80.93254_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994236.E2.80.93254-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>For her contribution to motion pictures, during the summer of 1973, Ann-Margret was inducted to the Hollywood Walk of Fame.</p>

<p>Throughout the 1970s, Ann-Margret balanced her live musical performances with a string of dramatic film roles that played against her glamorous image. In 1973, she starred with <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">John Wayne</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Train_Robbers" title="The Train Robbers">The Train Robbers</a></i>. Then came the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Tommy_(1975_film)" title="Tommy (1975 film)">Tommy</a></i> in 1975, for which she was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a>. In addition, she has been nominated for 10 <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Awards</a> and has won five, including her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actress_-_Motion_Picture_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actress - Motion Picture Musical or Comedy">Best Actress in a Musical or Comedy</a> for <i>Tommy.</i> On August 17, 1977, Ann-Margret and Roger Smith traveled to Memphis to attend Elvis Presley's funeral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nash2003_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nash2003-18">[18]</a></sup> Three months later, she hosted <i>Memories Of Elvis</i> featuring abridged versions of the <i>Elvis</i> 1968 TV and <i>Aloha from Hawaii</i> specials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaar2011_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaar2011-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1978, she co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hopkins" title="Anthony Hopkins">Anthony Hopkins</a> in the horror/suspense thriller <i><a href="/wiki/Magic_(1978_film)" title="Magic (1978 film)">Magic</a></i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1982, Ann-Margret co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Matthau" title="Walter Matthau">Walter Matthau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinah_Manoff" title="Dinah Manoff">Dinah Manoff</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Ought_to_Be_in_Pictures_(film)" title="I Ought to Be in Pictures (film)">film version</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neil_Simon" title="Neil Simon">Neil Simon</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/I_Ought_to_Be_in_Pictures" title="I Ought to Be in Pictures">I Ought to Be in Pictures</a></i>. That same year, she appeared with a six-year-old <a href="/wiki/Angelina_Jolie" title="Angelina Jolie">Angelina Jolie</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Lookin%27_to_Get_Out" title="Lookin' to Get Out">Lookin' to Get Out</a></i>, playing Jolie's mother. To round out 1982, she appeared alongside <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bates" title="Alan Bates">Alan Bates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenda_Jackson" title="Glenda Jackson">Glenda Jackson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Julie_Christie" title="Julie Christie">Julie Christie</a> in the film adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_Soldier" title="The Return of the Soldier">The Return of the Soldier</a></i>. She also starred in the TV movies <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Will_Love_My_Children%3F" title="Who Will Love My Children?">Who Will Love My Children?</a></i> (1983) and a remake of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(1984_film)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (1984 film)">A Streetcar Named Desire</a></i> (1984). These performances collectively won her two Golden Globe Awards and two Emmy nominations. She appeared as the wife of <a href="/wiki/Roy_Scheider" title="Roy Scheider">Roy Scheider</a>'s character in the 1986 crime thriller <i><a href="/wiki/52_Pick-Up" title="52 Pick-Up">52 Pick-Up</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1989, an illustration was done of <a href="/wiki/Oprah_Winfrey" title="Oprah Winfrey">Oprah Winfrey</a> that was on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i>, and although the head was Oprah's, the body was referenced from a 1979 publicity shot of Ann-Margret. The illustration was rendered so tightly in color pencil by freelance artist Chris Notarile that most people thought it was a composite photograph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s_and_2000s">1990s and 2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1990s and 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1991, she starred in the groundbreaking <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Sons" title="Our Sons">Our Sons</a></i> opposite <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">Julie Andrews</a> as mothers of sons who are lovers, one of whom is dying of AIDS. In 1992, she co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Duvall" title="Robert Duvall">Robert Duvall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">Christian Bale</a> in the Disney musical, <i><a href="/wiki/Newsies" title="Newsies">Newsies</a></i>. In 1993, Ann-Margret starred in the hit comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Grumpy_Old_Men_(film)" title="Grumpy Old Men (film)">Grumpy Old Men</a></i> reuniting with Matthau and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lemmon" title="Jack Lemmon">Jack Lemmon</a>. Her character returned for <i><a href="/wiki/Grumpier_Old_Men" title="Grumpier Old Men">Grumpier Old Men</a></i> (1995), the equally successful sequel which this time co-starred <a href="/wiki/Sophia_Loren" title="Sophia Loren">Sophia Loren</a>.</p>
<p>Ann-Margret published an <a href="/wiki/Autobiography" title="Autobiography">autobiography</a> in 1994 titled <i>Ann-Margret: My Story</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnn-Margret1994-21">[21]</a></sup> in which she publicly acknowledged her battle with and ongoing recovery from <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>. In 1995, she was chosen by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> as one of the 100 Sexiest Stars in film history; she ranked 10th.</p>
<p>She also filmed <i><a href="/wiki/Any_Given_Sunday" title="Any Given Sunday">Any Given Sunday</a></i> (1999) for director <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Stone" title="Oliver Stone">Oliver Stone</a>, portraying the mother of football team owner <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Diaz" title="Cameron Diaz">Cameron Diaz</a>. She filmed a cameo appearance for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limey" title="The Limey">The Limey</a></i>, but her performance was cut from the movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ann-Margret also starred in several TV movies, including <i><a href="/wiki/Queen:_The_Story_of_an_American_Family" title="Queen: The Story of an American Family">Queen: The Story of an American Family</a></i> (1993) and <i>Life of the Party</i> (1999), the latter of which she received nominations for an Emmy Award, a Golden Globe Award, and a <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award" title="Screen Actors Guild Award">Screen Actors Guild Award</a>.</p>
<p>She made guest appearances on the television shows <i><a href="/wiki/Touched_by_an_Angel" title="Touched by an Angel">Touched by an Angel</a></i> in 2000 and three episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/Third_Watch" title="Third Watch">Third Watch</a></i> in 2003. In 2001, she made her first appearance in a stage musical, playing the character of <a href="/wiki/Brothel" title="Brothel">brothel</a> owner Mona Stangley in a new touring production of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Best_Little_Whorehouse_in_Texas" title="The Best Little Whorehouse in Texas">The Best Little Whorehouse in Texas</a></i>. The production co-starred <a href="/wiki/Gary_Sandy" title="Gary Sandy">Gary Sandy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ed_Dixon" title="Ed Dixon">Ed Dixon</a>. She played <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a>'s mother in the 2004 comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Taxi_(2004_film)" title="Taxi (2004 film)">Taxi</a></i>, co-starring <a href="/wiki/Queen_Latifah" title="Queen Latifah">Queen Latifah</a>. In 2001, Ann-Margret worked with <a href="/wiki/Art_Greenhaw" title="Art Greenhaw">Art Greenhaw</a> on the album <i>God Is Love: The Gospel Sessions</i>. The critically acclaimed project resulted in her first Grammy Award nomination and first Dove Award nomination for Best Album of the Year in a Gospel category. They teamed up again in 2004 for the album <i>Ann-Margret's Christmas Carol Collection</i>. She performed material from the album at two auditorium church services at Crystal Cathedral in Garden Grove, California, and broadcast worldwide on the program <i><a href="/wiki/Hour_of_Power" title="Hour of Power">Hour of Power</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Ann-Margret had supporting roles in the box-office hits <i><a href="/wiki/The_Break-Up" title="The Break-Up">The Break-Up</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Aniston" title="Jennifer Aniston">Jennifer Aniston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vince_Vaughn" title="Vince Vaughn">Vince Vaughn</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Santa_Clause_3:_The_Escape_Clause" title="The Santa Clause 3: The Escape Clause">The Santa Clause 3</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Allen" title="Tim Allen">Tim Allen</a>. She also starred in several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_films" title="Independent films">independent films</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Memory_(2006_film)" title="Memory (2006 film)">Memory</a></i> (2006) with <a href="/wiki/Billy_Zane" title="Billy Zane">Billy Zane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Hopper" title="Dennis Hopper">Dennis Hopper</a>. In 2009, she appeared in the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Dogs_(film)" title="Old Dogs (film)">Old Dogs</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">John Travolta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Williams" title="Robin Williams">Robin Williams</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2010.E2.80.93present">2010–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Ann-Margret guest-starred in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit">Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit_(season_11)#ep242" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit (season 11)">Bedtime</a>", which first aired on March 31, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVGuide_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TVGuide-24">[24]</a></sup> She received her sixth Emmy nomination for her performance. She also appeared in the Lifetime series, <i><a href="/wiki/Army_Wives" title="Army Wives">Army Wives</a></i>, in the episode "Guns and Roses" (season four, episode five), which originally aired May 9, 2010. On August 29, 2010, she won an Emmy Award for Guest Performance by an Actress for her <i>SVU</i> performance. It was the first Emmy win of her career, and she received a standing ovation from the Emmy venue audience as she approached the stage to receive her award.</p>
<p>On October 14, 2010, Ann-Margret appeared on CBS' <i><a href="/wiki/CSI:_Crime_Scene_Investigation" title="CSI: Crime Scene Investigation">CSI</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, she began appearing in a recurring role in the Showtime original series <i><a href="/wiki/Ray_Donovan" title="Ray Donovan">Ray Donovan</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She has been married to <a href="/wiki/Roger_Smith_(actor)" title="Roger Smith (actor)">Roger Smith</a> since May 8, 1967; he was an actor who later became her manager. Prior to this, she was romantically linked to Elvis Presley during the filming of <i>Viva Las Vegas</i>.</p>
<p>She rode a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Triumph_motorcycles" title="List of Triumph motorcycles">500 cc Triumph T100C Tiger motorcycle</a> in <i>The Swinger</i> (1966) and used the same model, fitted with a nonstandard electric starter, in her stage show and her TV specials. A keen motorcyclist, she was featured in <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Engineering" title="Triumph Engineering">Triumph Motorcycles</a>' official advertisements in the 1960s. She suffered three broken ribs and a fractured shoulder when she was thrown off a motorcycle she was riding in rural <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayal">Portrayal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ann-Margret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portrayal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2005 CBS miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elvis_(TV_miniseries)" title="Elvis (TV miniseries)">Elvis</a></i> includes the story of her affair with Elvis Presley during the filming of <i>Viva Las Vegas</i>.</p>
