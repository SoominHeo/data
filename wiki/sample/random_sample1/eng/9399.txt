<p><b>Andrew Thomas McCarthy</b> (born November 29, 1962) is an American actor, travel writer and television director. He is known for his roles in the 1980s films <i><a href="/wiki/St._Elmo%27s_Fire_(film)" title="St. Elmo's Fire (film)">St. Elmo's Fire</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mannequin_(1987_film)" title="Mannequin (1987 film)">Mannequin</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Weekend_at_Bernie%27s" title="Weekend at Bernie's">Weekend at Bernie's</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pretty_in_Pink" title="Pretty in Pink">Pretty in Pink</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Less_Than_Zero_(film)" title="Less Than Zero (film)">Less Than Zero</a></i>, and more recently for his roles in the television shows <i><a href="/wiki/Lipstick_Jungle_(TV_series)" title="Lipstick Jungle (TV series)">Lipstick Jungle</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/White_Collar_(TV_series)" title="White Collar (TV series)">White Collar</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Pains" title="Royal Pains">Royal Pains</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Family_(2016_TV_series)" title="The Family (2016 TV series)">The Family</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_McCarthy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCarthy was born in <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_New_Jersey" title="Westfield, New Jersey">Westfield, New Jersey</a>. His mother worked for a newspaper and his father was involved in investments and stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hotticket_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hotticket-1">[1]</a></sup> McCarthy moved to <a href="/wiki/Bernardsville,_New_Jersey" title="Bernardsville, New Jersey">Bernardsville, New Jersey</a>, as a teenager and attended the <a href="/wiki/Pingry_School" title="Pingry School">Pingry School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He also attended the town's public school, <a href="/wiki/Bernards_High_School" title="Bernards High School">Bernards High School</a>, for part of a year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting.2Fdirecting_career">Acting/directing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_McCarthy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting/directing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCarthy gained recognition in Hollywood during the 1980s. His boy-next-door looks<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> continually had him placed as the sincere and kind leading man. His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breakout_role" title="Breakout role">breakout role</a> was in the 1983 theatrical film <i><a href="/wiki/Class_(film)" title="Class (film)">Class</a></i>. As McCarthy's career grew, he involuntarily became a member of the '80s Hollywood group of young actors known as the "<a href="/wiki/Brat_Pack_(actors)" title="Brat Pack (actors)">Brat Pack</a>"; McCarthy's better-known films include the Brat Pack films <i><a href="/wiki/St._Elmo%27s_Fire_(film)" title="St. Elmo's Fire (film)">St. Elmo's Fire</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pretty_in_Pink" title="Pretty in Pink">Pretty in Pink</a></i>. He starred in the 1987 box office hits, <i><a href="/wiki/Mannequin_(1987_film)" title="Mannequin (1987 film)">Mannequin</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Less_Than_Zero_(film)" title="Less Than Zero (film)">Less Than Zero</a></i>, a theatrical adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Bret_Easton_Ellis" title="Bret Easton Ellis">Bret Easton Ellis</a>' popular novel. In 1985 McCarthy starred with <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sutherland" title="Donald Sutherland">Donald Sutherland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Dillon" title="Kevin Dillon">Kevin Dillon</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Heaven_Help_Us" title="Heaven Help Us">Heaven Help Us</a></i> (also known as <i>Catholic Boys</i>) playing Michael Dunn. In 1985, McCarthy made his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadway_theater" title="Broadway theater">Broadway</a> debut in <i>The Boys of Winter.</i> He quickly returned to Hollywood in 1988 to star in several films, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Fresh_Horses_(film)" title="Fresh Horses (film)">Fresh Horses</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kansas_(film)" title="Kansas (film)">Kansas</a></i>.</p>
<p>He had another hit with the 1989 comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Weekend_at_Bernie%27s" title="Weekend at Bernie's">Weekend at Bernie's</a></i>. In 1991 he played the lead role in the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Year_of_the_Gun_(film)" title="Year of the Gun (film)">Year of the Gun</a></i>, a thriller which received mixed reviews from critics. In 1992, he starred in the romantic comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Only_You_(1992_film)" title="Only You (1992 film)">Only You</a></i>, and in 1993, he reprised the lead role of Larry Wilson in the sequel to the box office hit <i>Weekend at Bernie's</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Weekend_at_Bernie%27s_II" title="Weekend at Bernie's II">Weekend at Bernie's II</a></i>. He also had a supporting role in the critically acclaimed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joy_Luck_Club_(film)" title="The Joy Luck Club (film)">The Joy Luck Club</a></i>. In 1994 he starred in the motion pictures <i><a href="/wiki/Getting_In" title="Getting In">Getting In</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs._Parker_and_the_Vicious_Circle" title="Mrs. Parker and the Vicious Circle">Mrs. Parker and the Vicious Circle</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Funny" title="Dead Funny">Dead Funny</a></i>. In 1996, he starred in the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_Clause" title="Escape Clause">Escape Clause</a></i>. In 1996 he starred in the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/I%27m_Losing_You_(film)" title="I'm Losing You (film)">I'm Losing You</a></i>, a theatrical adaptation of the novel <i><a href="/wiki/I%27m_Losing_You_(novel)" title="I'm Losing You (novel)">I'm Losing You</a></i>. He also starred in the period piece, <i><a href="/wiki/Mulholland_Falls" title="Mulholland Falls">Mulholland Falls</a></i>. In 1997 he starred in the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Stag_(film)" title="Stag (film)">Stag</a></i>. He also starred in the 1999 theatrical film <i>A Twist of Faith</i> as a police detective.</p>
<p>He returned to <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway theatre</a> to star in <i><a href="/wiki/Side_Man" title="Side Man">Side Man</a></i>, McCarthy's version of the play won a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Play" title="Tony Award for Best Play">Tony Award for Best Play</a> in 1999. In 2003 McCarthy was set to guest star in two episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Criminal_Intent" title="Law &amp; Order: Criminal Intent">Law &amp; Order: Criminal Intent</a></i>. Due to bad relations with actor <a href="/wiki/Vincent_D%27Onofrio" title="Vincent D'Onofrio">Vincent D'Onofrio</a>, series creator <a href="/wiki/Dick_Wolf" title="Dick Wolf">Dick Wolf</a> decided against it. Wolf later stated, "Mr. McCarthy engaged in fractious behavior from the moment he walked on the set." McCarthy fired back in a statement of his own saying, "I was fired because I refused to allow a fellow actor to threaten me with physical violence, bully me and try to direct me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite this incident, he later guest starred in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Criminal_Intent" title="Law &amp; Order: Criminal Intent">Law &amp; Order: Criminal Intent</a></i> (with Chris Noth, not D'Onofrio) that originally aired in November 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2004 he played Dr. Hook in <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hospital" title="Kingdom Hospital">Stephen King's Kingdom Hospital</a></i>. He appeared in five episodes of the now-cancelled <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/E-Ring" title="E-Ring">E-Ring</a></i>. In 2008 he starred in the NBC television series <i><a href="/wiki/Lipstick_Jungle_(TV_series)" title="Lipstick Jungle (TV series)">Lipstick Jungle</a></i> as a billionaire, but was eventually cancelled, and had a minor role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spiderwick_Chronicles_(film)" title="The Spiderwick Chronicles (film)">The Spiderwick Chronicles</a></i>. He is ranked #40 on VH1's 100 Greatest Teen Stars of all-time list. McCarthy recently directed several episodes of the hit CW television series, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gossip_Girl_(TV_series)" title="Gossip Girl (TV series)">Gossip Girl</a></i>, including <a href="/wiki/Touch_of_Eva" title="Touch of Eva">Touch of Eva</a> in the fourth season. McCarthy is also known for an incident at the 2013 Comic Con Philly event, where his security staff assaulted "Skippy," a popular <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> character, who was attempting to photograph the <a href="/wiki/Mannequin_(1987_film)" title="Mannequin (1987 film)">Mannequin</a> actor at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2010 and 2011 he also appeared in the hit USA show <i><a href="/wiki/White_Collar_(TV_series)" title="White Collar (TV series)">White Collar</a></i>; he was praised by several critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> for his performance in the episodes. He returned to the series in the next season to direct the episode "<a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_Watch_(White_Collar)" title="Neighborhood Watch (White Collar)">Neighborhood Watch</a>". In 2015 he directed 3 episodes (ep. 11, 13 &amp; 16) in season 2 of the <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> hit television show <a href="/wiki/The_Blacklist_(TV_series)" title="The Blacklist (TV series)">The Blacklist</a> starring <a href="/wiki/James_Spader" title="James Spader">James Spader</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Megan_Boone" title="Megan Boone">Megan Boone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="per WP:CIRCULAR (October 2015)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2016, he starred in the short-lived <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Family_(2016_TV_series)" title="The Family (2016 TV series)">The Family</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travel_writing">Travel writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_McCarthy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Travel writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCarthy has also become a travel writer, and is currently an Editor at Large at <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Traveler" title="National Geographic Traveler">National Geographic Traveler</a></i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2010, McCarthy was escorted out of an underground church in <a href="/wiki/Lalibela" title="Lalibela">Lalibela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, for entering the site without documentation. He had been in the church on assignment for the travel magazine <i>Afar</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A book written by McCarthy, <i>The Longest Way Home: One Man's Quest for the Courage to Settle Down</i>, was published in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In February/March 2015, <i>National Geographic</i> published his account, entitled "A Song for Ireland", of his return to the house in the townland of Lacka West in the parish of Duagh in County Kerry in Ireland from which his greatgrandfather John McCarthy had emigrated in the late 1800s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1999, McCarthy married his college sweetheart Carol Schneider 20 years after they first dated. He later stated his reasons for tracking her down after they had drifted apart: "I ran into someone who said they had seen Carol and her boyfriend and they seemed really happy, and for some reason it bothered me for a week. I called her and asked her if she was really with this guy and asked her out for coffee."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Schneider gave birth to a son, Sam. In 2005, the couple divorced.</p>
<p>On August 28, 2011, he married Dolores Rice. They have a daughter, Willow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usat_2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-usat_2008-13">[13]</a></sup> In September 2013 it was announced that the couple were expecting their second, and his third, child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, he announced that he once had a serious alcohol problem, which began at age 12. In 1992, he entered a detoxification program and has been sober since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
