<p>The Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen paradox or <b>EPR paradox</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> of 1935 is an influential <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiment</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> with which <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> and his colleagues <a href="/wiki/Boris_Podolsky" title="Boris Podolsky">Boris Podolsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rosen" title="Nathan Rosen">Nathan Rosen</a> ("EPR") claimed to demonstrate that the <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> does not provide a complete description of physical reality, and hence that the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> is unsatisfactory; resolutions of the paradox have important implications for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpretation_of_quantum_mechanics" title="Interpretation of quantum mechanics">interpretation of quantum mechanics</a>. The essence of the paradox is that particles can interact in such a way that it is possible to measure both their position and their momentum more accurately than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg%27s_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg's uncertainty principle">Heisenberg's uncertainty principle</a> allows, unless measuring one particle instantaneously affects the other to prevent this accuracy, which would involve information being transmitted faster than light as forbidden by the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_at_a_distance_(physics)" title="Action at a distance (physics)">spooky action at a distance</a>").</p>
<p>This consequence had not previously been noticed and seemed unreasonable at the time; the phenomenon involved is now known as <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglement</a>. While EPR felt that the paradox showed that quantum theory was incomplete and should be extended with <a href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory" title="Hidden variable theory">hidden variables</a>, the usual modern resolution is to say that due to the common preparation of the two particles (for example the creation of an electron-positron pair from a photon) the property we want to measure has a well defined meaning only when analyzed for the whole system while the same property for the parts individually remains undefined. Therefore, if similar measurements are being performed on the two entangled subsystems, there will always be a correlation between the outcomes resulting in a well defined global outcome i.e. for both subsystems together. However, the outcomes for each subsystem separately at each repetition of the experiment will not be well defined or predictable. This correlation does not imply any action of the measurement of one particle on the measurement of the other, therefore it doesn't imply any form of action at a distance. This modern resolution eliminates the need for hidden variables, action at a distance or other structures introduced over time in order to explain the phenomenon.</p>
<p>A preference for the latter resolution is supported by experiments suggested by <a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem" title="Bell's theorem">Bell's theorem</a> of 1964, which exclude some classes of hidden variable theory.</p>
<p>According to quantum mechanics, under some conditions, a pair of quantum systems may be described by a single <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a>, which encodes the probabilities of the outcomes of experiments that may be performed on the two systems, whether jointly or individually. At the time the EPR article discussed below was written, it was known from experiments that the outcome of an experiment sometimes cannot be uniquely predicted. An example of such indeterminacy can be seen when a beam of light is incident on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-silvered_mirror" title="Half-silvered mirror">half-silvered mirror</a>. One half of the beam will reflect, and the other will pass. If the intensity of the beam is reduced until only one photon is in transit at any time, whether that photon will reflect or transmit cannot be predicted quantum mechanically.</p>
<p>The routine explanation of this effect was, at that time, provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg%27s_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg's uncertainty principle">Heisenberg's uncertainty principle</a>. Physical quantities come in pairs called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_quantities" title="Conjugate quantities">conjugate quantities</a>. Examples of such conjugate pairs are position and momentum of a particle and components of spin measured around different axes. When one quantity was measured, and became determined, the conjugated quantity became indeterminate. Heisenberg explained this as a disturbance caused by measurement.</p>
<p>The EPR paper, written in 1935, was intended to illustrate that this explanation is inadequate. It considered two <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">entangled</a> particles, referred to as A and B, and pointed out that measuring a quantity of a particle A will cause the conjugated quantity of particle B to become undetermined, even if there was no contact, no classical disturbance. <p>Heisenberg's principle was an attempt to provide a classical explanation of a quantum effect sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Quantum_nonlocality" title="Quantum nonlocality">non-locality</a>. According to EPR there were two possible explanations. Either there was some interaction between the particles (even though they were separated) or the information about the outcome of all possible measurements was already present in both particles.</p>
<p>The EPR authors preferred the second explanation according to which that information was encoded in some '<a href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory#EPR_paradox" title="Hidden variable theory">hidden parameters</a>'. The first explanation of an effect propagating instantly across a distance is in conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>. They then concluded that quantum mechanics was incomplete since its formalism does not permit hidden parameters.</p>
<p>Violations of the conclusions of Bell's theorem are generally understood to have demonstrated that the hypotheses of Bell's theorem, also assumed by Einstein, Podolsky and Rosen, do not apply in our world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most physicists who have examined the issue concur that experiments, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Alain_Aspect" title="Alain Aspect">Alain Aspect</a> and his group, have confirmed that physical probabilities, as predicted by quantum theory, do exhibit the phenomena of Bell-inequality violations that are considered to invalidate EPR's preferred "local hidden-variables" type of explanation for the correlations to which EPR first drew attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell1964_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell1964-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aspect1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aspect1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_EPR_developments">History of EPR developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of EPR developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The article that first brought forth these matters, "Can Quantum-Mechanical Description of Physical Reality Be Considered Complete?" was published in 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Einstein1935_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Einstein1935-5">[5]</a></sup> The paper prompted a response by Bohr, which he published in the same journal, in the same year, using the same title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bohr1935_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bohr1935-6">[6]</a></sup> There followed a debate between Bohr and Einstein about the fundamental nature of reality. Einstein had been skeptical of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle and the role of chance in quantum theory. But the crux of this debate was not about chance, but something even deeper: Is there one objective physical reality, which every observer sees from his own vantage? (Einstein's view) Or does the observer co-create physical reality by the questions he poses with experiments? (Bohr's view)</p>
<p>Einstein struggled to the end of his life for a theory that could better comply with his idea of <a href="/wiki/Causality_(physics)" title="Causality (physics)">causality</a>, protesting against the view that there exists no objective physical reality other than that which is revealed through measurement interpreted in terms of quantum mechanical formalism. However, since Einstein's death, experiments analogous to the one described in the EPR paper have been carried out, starting in 1976 by French scientists Lamehi-Rachti and Mittig<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Saclay_Nuclear_Research_Centre" title="Saclay Nuclear Research Centre">Saclay Nuclear Research Centre</a>. These experiments appear to show that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_realism" title="Local realism">local realism</a> idea is false,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribbin_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gribbin-8">[8]</a></sup> vindicating Bohr.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_mechanics_and_its_interpretation">Quantum mechanics and its interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quantum mechanics and its interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interpretations_of_quantum_mechanics" title="Interpretations of quantum mechanics">Interpretations of quantum mechanics</a></div>
<p>Since the early twentieth century, quantum theory has proved to be successful in describing accurately the physical reality of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscopic_scale" title="Mesoscopic scale">mesoscopic</a> and microscopic world, in multiple reproducible physics experiments.</p>
<p>Quantum mechanics was developed with the aim of describing atoms and explaining the observed spectral lines in a measurement apparatus. Although disputed especially in the early twentieth century, it has yet to be seriously challenged. Philosophical interpretations of quantum phenomena, however, are another matter: the question of how to <i>interpret</i> the mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics has given rise to a variety of different answers from people of different philosophical persuasions (see <a href="/wiki/Interpretations_of_quantum_mechanics" title="Interpretations of quantum mechanics">Interpretations of quantum mechanics</a>).</p>
<p>Quantum theory and quantum mechanics do not provide single measurement outcomes in a deterministic way. According to the understanding of quantum mechanics known as the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a>, measurement causes an instantaneous collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> describing the quantum system into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenstate" title="Eigenstate">eigenstate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Observable" title="Observable">observable</a> that was measured. Einstein characterized this imagined collapse in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvay_conference#Fifth_Conference" title="Solvay conference">1927 Solvay Conference</a>. He presented a thought experiment in which electrons are introduced through a small hole in a sphere whose inner surface serves as a detection screen. The electrons will contact the spherical detection screen in a widely dispersed manner. Those electrons, however, are all individually described by wave fronts that expand in all directions from the point of entry. A wave as it is understood in everyday life would paint a large area of the detection screen, but the electrons would be found to impact the screen at single points and would eventually form a pattern in keeping with the probabilities described by their identical wave functions. Einstein asks what makes each electron's wave front "collapse" at its respective location. Why do the electrons appear as single bright scintillations rather than as dim washes of energy across the surface? Why does any single electron appear at one point rather than some alternative point? The behavior of the electrons gives the impression of some signal having been sent to all possible points of contact that would have nullified all but one of them, or, in other words, would have preferentially selected a single point to the exclusion of all others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein.27s_opposition">Einstein's opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Einstein's opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Einstein was the most prominent opponent of the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a>. In his view, quantum mechanics is incomplete. Commenting on this, other writers (such as <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/David_Bohm" title="David Bohm">David Bohm</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) hypothesized that consequently there would have to be <a href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory" title="Hidden variable theory">'hidden' variables</a> responsible for random measurement results, something which was not expressly claimed in the original paper.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1935_in_science" title="1935 in science">1935</a> EPR paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> condensed the philosophical discussion into a physical argument. The authors claim that given a specific experiment, in which the outcome of a measurement is known before the measurement takes place, there must exist something in the real world, an "element of reality", that determines the measurement outcome. They postulate that these elements of reality are <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">local</a>, in the sense that each belongs to a certain point in <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a>. Each element may only be influenced by events which are located in the backward <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">light cone</a> of its point in spacetime (i.e., the past). These claims are founded on assumptions about nature that constitute what is now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_realism" title="Local realism">local realism</a>.</p>
<p>Though the EPR paper has often been taken as an exact expression of Einstein's views, it was primarily authored by Podolsky, based on discussions at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Institute for Advanced Study">Institute for Advanced Study</a> with Einstein and Rosen. Einstein later expressed to <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">Erwin Schrödinger</a> that, "it did not come out as well as I had originally wanted; rather, the essential thing was, so to speak, smothered by the formalism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1936, Einstein presented an individual account of his <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">local realist</a> ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_of_the_paradox">Description of the paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description of the paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original EPR paradox challenges the prediction of quantum mechanics that it is impossible to know both the position and the momentum of a quantum particle. This challenge can be extended to other pairs of physical properties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EPR_paper">EPR paper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: EPR paper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original paper purports to describe what must happen to "two systems I and II, which we permit to interact ...", and, after some time, "we suppose that there is no longer any interaction between the two parts." As explained by <a href="/wiki/Manjit_Kumar" title="Manjit Kumar">Manjit Kumar</a> (2009), the EPR description involves "two particles, A and B, [which] interact briefly and then move off in opposite directions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kumar2011_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar2011-15">[15]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg%27s_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg's uncertainty principle">Heisenberg's uncertainty principle</a>, it is impossible to measure both the momentum and the position of particle B exactly. However, it is possible to measure the exact position of particle A. By calculation, therefore, with the exact position of particle A known, the exact position of particle B can be known. Alternatively, the exact momentum of particle A can be measured, so the exact momentum of particle B can be worked out. Kumar writes: "EPR argued that they had proved that ... [particle] B can have simultaneously exact values of position and momentum. ... Particle B has a position that is real and a momentum that is real."</p>
<blockquote>
<p>EPR appeared to have contrived a means to establish the exact values of <i>either</i> the momentum <i>or</i> the position of B due to measurements made on particle A, without the slightest possibility of particle B being physically disturbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kumar2011_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar2011-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>EPR tried to set up a paradox to question the range of true application of Quantum Mechanics: Quantum theory predicts that both values cannot be known for a particle, and yet the EPR thought experiment purports to show that they must all have determinate values. The EPR paper says: "We are thus forced to conclude that the quantum-mechanical description of physical reality given by wave functions is not complete."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kumar2011_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar2011-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The EPR paper ends by saying:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>While we have thus shown that the wave function does not provide a complete description of the physical reality, we left open the question of whether or not such a description exists. We believe, however, that such a theory is possible.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurements_on_an_entangled_state">Measurements on an entangled state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Measurements on an entangled state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>We have a source that emits electron–positron pairs, with the electron sent to destination <i>A</i>, where there is an observer named <a href="/wiki/Alice_and_Bob" title="Alice and Bob">Alice</a>, and the positron sent to destination <i>B</i>, where there is an observer named <a href="/wiki/Alice_and_Bob" title="Alice and Bob">Bob</a>. According to quantum mechanics, we can arrange our source so that each emitted pair occupies a quantum state called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin_singlet" title="Spin singlet">spin singlet</a>. The particles are thus said to be <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">entangled</a>. This can be viewed as a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">quantum superposition</a> of two states, which we call state I and state II. In state I, the electron has <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> pointing upward along the <i>z</i>-axis (<i>+z</i>) and the positron has spin pointing downward along the <i>z</i>-axis (−<i>z</i>). In state II, the electron has spin −<i>z</i> and the positron has spin +<i>z</i>. Because it is in a superposition of states it is impossible without measuring to know the definite state of spin of either particle in the spin singlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths2004_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths2004-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:421–422</sup></p>
<div class="center">
<div class="thumb tnone">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EPR_illustration.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="171" data-file-width="512" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/EPR_illustration.svg/500px-EPR_illustration.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/EPR_illustration.svg/750px-EPR_illustration.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/EPR_illustration.svg/1000px-EPR_illustration.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EPR_illustration.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The EPR thought experiment, performed with electron–positron pairs. A source (center) sends particles toward two observers, electrons to Alice (left) and positrons to Bob (right), who can perform spin measurements.</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Alice now measures the spin along the <i>z</i>-axis. She can obtain one of two possible outcomes: +<i>z</i> or −<i>z</i>. Suppose she gets +<i>z</i>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> of quantum mechanics, the quantum state of the system <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">collapses</a> into state I. The quantum state determines the probable outcomes of any measurement performed on the system. In this case, if Bob subsequently measures spin along the <i>z</i>-axis, there is 100% probability that he will obtain −<i>z</i>. Similarly, if Alice gets −<i>z</i>, Bob will get +<i>z</i>.</p>
<p>There is, of course, nothing special about choosing the <i>z</i>-axis: according to quantum mechanics the spin singlet state may equally well be expressed as a superposition of spin states pointing in the <i>x</i> direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laloe_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laloe-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:318</sup> Suppose that Alice and Bob had decided to measure spin along the <i>x</i>-axis. We'll call these states Ia and IIa. In state Ia, Alice's electron has spin +<i>x</i> and Bob's positron has spin −<i>x</i>. In state IIa, Alice's electron has spin −<i>x</i> and Bob's positron has spin +<i>x</i>. Therefore, if Alice measures +<i>x</i>, the system 'collapses' into state Ia, and Bob will get −<i>x</i>. If Alice measures −<i>x</i>, the system collapses into state IIa, and Bob will get +<i>x</i>.</p>
<p>Whatever axis their spins are measured along, they are always found to be opposite. This can only be explained if the particles are linked in some way. Either they were created with a definite (opposite) spin about every axis—a "hidden variable" argument—or they are linked so that one electron "feels" which axis the other is having its spin measured along, and becomes its opposite about that one axis—an "entanglement" argument. Moreover, if the two particles have their spins measured about <i>different</i> axes, once the electron's spin has been measured about the <i>x</i>-axis (and the positron's spin about the <i>x</i>-axis deduced), the positron's spin about the <i>z</i>-axis will no longer be certain, as if (a) it knows that the measurement has taken place, or (b) it has a definite spin already, about a <i>second</i> axis—a hidden variable. However, it turns out that the predictions of Quantum Mechanics, which have been confirmed by experiment, cannot be explained by any local hidden variable theory. This is demonstrated in <a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem" title="Bell's theorem">Bell's theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In quantum mechanics, the <i>x</i>-spin and <i>z</i>-spin are "incompatible observables", meaning the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">Heisenberg uncertainty principle</a> applies to alternating measurements of them: a quantum state cannot possess a definite value for both of these variables. Suppose Alice measures the <i>z</i>-spin and obtains <i>+z</i>, so that the quantum state collapses into state I. Now, instead of measuring the <i>z</i>-spin as well, Bob measures the <i>x</i>-spin. According to quantum mechanics, when the system is in state I, Bob's <i>x</i>-spin measurement will have a 50% probability of producing +<i>x</i> and a 50% probability of -<i>x</i>. It is impossible to predict which outcome will appear until Bob actually <i>performs</i> the measurement.</p>
<p><b>Here is the crux of the matter:</b></p>
<p>You might imagine that, when Bob measures the <i>x</i>-spin of his positron, he would get an answer with absolute certainty, since prior to this he hasn't disturbed his particle at all. But it turns out that Bob's positron has a 50% probability of producing +<i>x</i> and a 50% probability of −<i>x</i>, meaning the outcome is not certain. It's as if Bob's positron "knows" that Alice has measured the <i>z</i>-spin of her electron, and hence his positron's own <i>z</i>-spin must also be set, but its <i>x</i>-spin remains uncertain.</p>
<p>Put another way, how does Bob's positron know which way to point if Alice decides (based on information unavailable to Bob) to measure x (i.e., to be the opposite of Alice's electron's spin about the <i>x</i>-axis) and <i>also</i> how to point if Alice measures z, since it is only supposed to know one thing at a time? The Copenhagen interpretation rules that say the wave function "collapses" at the time of measurement, so there must be action at a distance (entanglement) or the positron must know more than it's supposed to know (hidden variables).</p>
<p><b>Here is the paradox summed up:</b></p>
<p>It is one thing to say that physical measurement of the first particle's momentum affects uncertainty in its <i>own</i> position, but to say that measuring the first particle's momentum affects the uncertainty in the position of the <i>other</i> is another thing altogether. Einstein, Podolsky and Rosen asked how can the second particle "know" to have precisely defined momentum but uncertain position? Since this implies that one particle is communicating with the other instantaneously across space, i.e., faster than light, this is the "paradox".</p>
<p>Incidentally, Bell used spin as his example, but many types of physical quantities—referred to as "observables" in quantum mechanics—can be used. The EPR paper used <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> for the observable. Experimental realisations of the EPR scenario often use <a href="/wiki/Photon_polarization" title="Photon polarization">photon polarization</a>, because polarized photons are easy to prepare and measure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locality_in_the_EPR_experiment">Locality in the EPR experiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Locality in the EPR experiment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The principle of locality states that physical processes occurring at one place should have no immediate effect on the elements of reality at another location. At first sight, this appears to be a reasonable assumption to make, as it seems to be a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>, which states that <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> can never be transmitted faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> without violating <a href="/wiki/Causality_(physics)" title="Causality (physics)">causality</a>. It is generally believed that any theory which violates causality would also be internally inconsistent, and thus useless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths2004_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths2004-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:427–428</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blaylock_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blaylock-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>It turns out that the usual rules for combining quantum mechanical and classical descriptions violate the principle of locality without violating causality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths2004_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths2004-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:427–428</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blaylock_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blaylock-19">[19]</a></sup> Causality is preserved because there is no way for Alice to transmit messages (i.e., information) to Bob by manipulating her measurement axis. Whichever axis she uses, she has a 50% probability of obtaining "+" and 50% probability of obtaining "−", completely at <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">random</a>; according to quantum mechanics, it is fundamentally impossible for her to influence what result she gets. Furthermore, Bob is only able to perform his measurement <i>once</i>: there is a fundamental property of quantum mechanics, known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_cloning_theorem" title="No cloning theorem">no cloning theorem</a>", which makes it impossible for him to make a million copies of the electron he receives, perform a spin measurement on each, and look at the statistical distribution of the results. Therefore, in the one measurement he is allowed to make, there is a 50% probability of getting "+" and 50% of getting "−", regardless of whether or not his axis is aligned with Alice's.</p>
<p>However, the principle of locality appeals powerfully to physical intuition, and Einstein, Podolsky and Rosen were unwilling to abandon it. Einstein derided the quantum mechanical predictions as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_at_a_distance_(physics)" title="Action at a distance (physics)">spooky action at a distance</a>". The conclusion they drew was that quantum mechanics is not a complete theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell1981_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell1981-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years, however, doubt has been cast on EPR's conclusion due to developments in understanding <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">locality</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">quantum decoherence</a>. The word <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">locality</a> has several different meanings in physics. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> "locality" means that quantum fields at different points of space do not interact with one another. However, quantum field theories that are "local" in this sense <i>appear</i> to violate the <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">principle of locality</a> as defined by EPR, but they nevertheless do not violate locality in a more general sense. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">Wavefunction collapse</a> can be viewed as an epiphenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">quantum decoherence</a>, which in turn is nothing more than an effect of the underlying local time evolution of the wavefunction of a system and <i>all</i> of its environment. Since the <i>underlying</i> behaviour doesn't violate local causality, it follows that neither does the additional effect of wavefunction collapse, whether real <i>or</i> apparent. Therefore, as outlined in the example above, neither the EPR experiment nor any quantum experiment demonstrates that <a href="/wiki/Faster-than-light" title="Faster-than-light">faster-than-light</a> signaling is possible.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resolving_the_paradox">Resolving the paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Resolving the paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hidden_variables">Hidden variables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hidden variables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several ways to resolve the EPR paradox. The one suggested by EPR is that quantum mechanics, despite its success in a wide variety of experimental scenarios, is actually an incomplete theory. In other words, there is some yet undiscovered theory of nature to which quantum mechanics acts as a kind of statistical approximation (albeit an exceedingly successful one). Unlike quantum mechanics, the more complete theory contains variables corresponding to all the "elements of reality". There must be some unknown mechanism acting on these variables to give rise to the observed effects of "non-commuting quantum observables", i.e. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg uncertainty principle">Heisenberg uncertainty principle</a>. Such a theory is called a <a href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory" title="Hidden variable theory">hidden variable theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kumar2011_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kumar2011-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:334</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WheelerZurek2014_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WheelerZurek2014-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:357–358</sup></p>
<p>To illustrate this idea, we can formulate a very simple hidden variable theory for the above thought experiment. One supposes that the quantum spin-singlet states emitted by the source are actually approximate descriptions for "true" physical states possessing definite values for the <i>z</i>-spin and <i>x</i>-spin. In these "true" states, the positron going to Bob always has spin values opposite to the electron going to Alice, but the values are otherwise completely random. For example, the first pair emitted by the source might be "(+<i>z</i>, −<i>x</i>) to Alice and (−<i>z</i>, +<i>x</i>) to Bob", the next pair "(−<i>z</i>, −<i>x</i>) to Alice and (+<i>z</i>, +<i>x</i>) to Bob", and so forth. Therefore, if Bob's measurement axis is aligned with Alice's, he will necessarily get the opposite of whatever Alice gets; otherwise, he will get "+" and "−" with equal probability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakurai_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sakurai-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:239–240</sup></p>
<p>Assuming we restrict our measurements to the <i>z</i>- and <i>x</i>-axes, such a hidden variable theory is experimentally indistinguishable from quantum mechanics. In reality, there may be an infinite number of axes along which Alice and Bob can perform their measurements, so there would have to be an infinite number of independent hidden variables. However, this is not a serious problem; we have formulated a very simplistic hidden variable theory, and a more sophisticated theory might be able to patch it up. It turns out that there is a much more serious challenge to the idea of hidden variables.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bell.27s_inequality">Bell's inequality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bell's inequality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem" title="Bell's theorem">Bell's theorem</a></div>
<p>In 1964, <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_Bell" title="John Stewart Bell">John Bell</a> showed that the predictions of quantum mechanics in the EPR thought experiment are significantly different from the predictions of a particular class of hidden variable theories (the <i>local</i> hidden variable theories). Roughly speaking, quantum mechanics has a much stronger statistical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">correlation</a> with measurement results performed on different axes than do these hidden variable theories. These differences, expressed using <a href="/wiki/Inequality_(mathematics)" title="Inequality (mathematics)">inequality relations</a> known as "Bell's inequalities", are in principle experimentally detectable. Later work by Eberhard showed that the key properties of local hidden variable theories which lead to Bell's inequalities are <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_locality" title="Principle of locality">locality</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter-factual_definiteness" title="Counter-factual definiteness">counter-factual definiteness</a>. Any theory in which these principles apply produces the inequalities. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Fine" title="Arthur Fine">Arthur Fine</a> subsequently showed that any theory satisfying the inequalities can be modeled by a local hidden variable theory.</p>
<p>After the publication of Bell's paper, a variety of experiments to test Bell's inequalities were devised. These generally relied on measurement of <a href="/wiki/Photon_polarization" title="Photon polarization">photon polarization</a>. All experiments conducted to date have found behavior in line with the predictions of standard quantum mechanics theory.</p>
<p>However, Bell's theorem does not apply to all possible <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">philosophically realist</a> theories. It is a common misconception that quantum mechanics is inconsistent with all notions of philosophical realism. Realist interpretations of quantum mechanics are possible, although as discussed above, such interpretations must reject either locality or counter-factual definiteness. Mainstream physics prefers to keep locality, while striving also to maintain a notion of realism that nevertheless rejects counter-factual definiteness. Examples of such mainstream realist interpretations are the <a href="/wiki/Consistent_histories" title="Consistent histories">consistent histories</a> interpretation and the <a href="/wiki/Transactional_interpretation" title="Transactional interpretation">transactional interpretation</a> (first proposed by <a href="/wiki/John_G._Cramer" title="John G. Cramer">John G. Cramer</a> in 1986). Fine's work showed that, taking locality as a given, there exist scenarios in which two statistical variables are correlated in a manner inconsistent with counter-factual definiteness, and that such scenarios are no more mysterious than any other, despite the fact that the inconsistency with counter-factual definiteness may seem 'counter-intuitive'.</p>
<p>Violation of locality is difficult to reconcile with <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>, and is thought to be incompatible with the principle of causality. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohm_interpretation" title="Bohm interpretation">Bohm interpretation</a> of quantum mechanics keeps counter-factual definiteness, while introducing a conjectured non-local mechanism in the form of the '<a href="/wiki/Quantum_potential" title="Quantum potential">quantum potential</a>' that is defined as one of the terms of the Schrödinger equation. Some workers in the field have also attempted to formulate hidden variable theories that exploit <a href="/wiki/Loopholes_in_Bell_test_experiments" title="Loopholes in Bell test experiments">loopholes in actual experiments</a>, such as the assumptions made in interpreting experimental data, although no theory has been proposed that can reproduce all the results of quantum mechanics.</p>
<p>Alternatives are still possible. A recent review article based on the <a href="/wiki/Wheeler%E2%80%93Feynman_absorber_theory" title="Wheeler–Feynman absorber theory">Wheeler–Feynman time-symmetric theory</a> rewrites the entire theory in terms of retarded <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A9nard%E2%80%93Wiechert_potential" title="Liénard–Wiechert potential">Liénard–Wiechert potentials</a> only, which becomes manifestly causal, and, establishes a conservation law for total generalized momenta held instantaneously for any closed system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott15_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-scott15-23">[23]</a></sup> The outcome results in correlation between particles from a "handshake principle" based on a variational principle applied to a system as a whole, an idea with a slightly non-local feature but the theory is nonetheless in agreement with the essential results of quantum electrodynamics and <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_chemistry" title="Relativistic quantum chemistry">relativistic quantum chemistry</a>.</p>
<p>There are also individual EPR-like experiments that have no local hidden variables explanation. Examples have been suggested by <a href="/wiki/David_Bohm" title="David Bohm">David Bohm</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Hardy" title="Lucien Hardy">Lucien Hardy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein.27s_hope_for_a_purely_algebraic_theory">Einstein's hope for a purely algebraic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Einstein's hope for a purely algebraic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohm_interpretation" title="Bohm interpretation">Bohm interpretation</a> of quantum mechanics hypothesizes that the state of the universe evolves smoothly through time with no collapsing of quantum wavefunctions. One problem for the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a> is to precisely define wavefunction collapse. Einstein maintained that quantum mechanics is physically incomplete and logically unsatisfactory. In "The Meaning of Relativity", Einstein wrote, "One can give good reasons why reality cannot at all be represented by a continuous field. From the quantum phenomena it appears to follow with certainty that a finite system of finite energy can be completely described by a finite set of numbers (quantum numbers). This does not seem to be in accordance with a continuum theory and must lead to an attempt to find a purely <a href="/wiki/Local_quantum_field_theory" title="Local quantum field theory">algebraic theory</a> for the representation of reality. But nobody knows how to find the basis for such a theory." If time, space, and energy are secondary features derived from a substrate below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck_scale" title="Planck scale">Planck scale</a>, then Einstein's hypothetical algebraic system might resolve the EPR paradox (although Bell's theorem would still be valid). If physical reality is totally finite, then the Copenhagen interpretation might be an approximation to an information processing system below the Planck scale.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Acceptable_theories.22_and_the_experiment">"Acceptable theories" and the experiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title='Edit section: "Acceptable theories" and the experiment'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the present view of the situation, quantum mechanics flatly contradicts Einstein's philosophical postulate that any acceptable physical theory must fulfill "local realism".</p>
<p>In the EPR paper (1935), the authors realised that quantum mechanics was inconsistent with their assumptions, but Einstein nevertheless thought that quantum mechanics might simply be augmented by hidden variables (i.e., variables which were, at that point, still obscure to him), without any other change, to achieve an acceptable theory. He pursued these ideas for over twenty years until the end of his life, in 1955.</p>
<p>In contrast, <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_Bell" title="John Stewart Bell">John Bell</a>, in his 1964 paper, showed that quantum mechanics and the class of hidden variable theories Einstein favored<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> would lead to different experimental results: different by a factor of <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> for certain correlations. So the issue of "acceptability", up to that time mainly concerning theory, finally became experimentally decidable.</p>
<p>There are many <a href="/wiki/Bell_test_experiments" title="Bell test experiments">Bell test experiments</a>, e.g., those of <a href="/wiki/Alain_Aspect" title="Alain Aspect">Alain Aspect</a> and others. They support the predictions of quantum mechanics rather than the class of hidden variable theories supported by Einstein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aspect1999_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aspect1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_for_quantum_mechanics">Implications for quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EPR_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Implications for quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most physicists today believe that quantum mechanics is correct, and that the EPR paradox is a "paradox" only because classical intuitions do not correspond to physical reality. How EPR is interpreted regarding locality depends on the <a href="/wiki/Interpretations_of_quantum_mechanics" title="Interpretations of quantum mechanics">interpretation of quantum mechanics</a> one uses. In the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation" title="Copenhagen interpretation">Copenhagen interpretation</a>, it is usually understood that instantaneous <a href="/wiki/Wave_function_collapse" title="Wave function collapse">wave function collapse</a> does occur. However, the view that there is no <i>causal</i> instantaneous effect has also been proposed within the Copenhagen interpretation: in this alternate view, measurement affects our ability to define (and measure) quantities in the physical system, not the system itself. In the <a href="/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation" title="Many-worlds interpretation">many-worlds interpretation</a>, locality is strictly preserved, since the effects of operations such as measurement affect only the state of the particle that is measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blaylock_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blaylock-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the results of the measurement are not unique—every possible result is obtained.</p>
<p>The EPR paradox has deepened our understanding of quantum mechanics by exposing the fundamentally non-classical characteristics of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measurement_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Measurement (quantum mechanics)">measurement</a> process. Before the publication of the EPR paper, a measurement was often visualized as a physical disturbance that had to be inflicted <i>directly</i> upon the measured subsystem. For instance, when measuring the position of an electron, one imagines shining a light on it, thus disturbing the electron and producing the quantum mechanical uncertainties in its position. Such pat and convenient but unhelpful explanations of quantum mechanics remain commonplace today,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but they fail to explain (among other things) the EPR paradox, which shows that a "measurement" can be performed on a particle without disturbing it directly, by performing a measurement on a distant entangled particle. In fact, <a href="/wiki/Yakir_Aharonov" title="Yakir Aharonov">Yakir Aharonov</a> and his collaborators have developed a whole theory of so-called <a href="/wiki/Weak_measurement" title="Weak measurement">Weak measurement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laloe_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laloe-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:181–184</sup></p>
<p>Technologies relying on quantum entanglement are now being developed. In <a href="/wiki/Quantum_cryptography" title="Quantum cryptography">quantum cryptography</a>, entangled particles are used to transmit signals that cannot be <a href="/wiki/Eavesdropping" title="Eavesdropping">eavesdropped</a> upon without leaving a trace. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computation" title="Quantum computation">quantum computation</a>, entangled quantum states are used to perform computations in <a href="/wiki/Parallel_computing" title="Parallel computing">parallel</a>, which may allow certain calculations to be performed much more quickly than they ever could be with classical computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haroche_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haroche-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:83–100</sup></p>
