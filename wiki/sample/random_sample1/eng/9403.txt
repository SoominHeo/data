<p><i><b>Andrewsarchus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɑːr/ 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an extinct <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> that lived during the middle <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> epoch in what is now <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Only one species is usually recognized, <i>A. mongoliensis,</i> known from a single skull of great size discovered in 1923 during the expeditions of central Asia by the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a> (AMNH). Generally classified as a <a href="/wiki/Mesonychid" title="Mesonychid">mesonychid</a> since its original description, most recent studies recover it as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artiodactyl" title="Artiodactyl">artiodactyl</a>, in one study specifically, as a member of the clade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetacodontamorpha" title="Cetacodontamorpha">Cetacodontamorpha</a>, closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entelodonts" title="Entelodonts">entelodonts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">whales</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The genus name was dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Roy_Chapman_Andrews" title="Roy Chapman Andrews">Roy Chapman Andrews</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn" title="Henry Fairfield Osborn">Osborn</a> and it derives from the surname "Andrews" + <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀρχός</span> (archos), "leader", "chief" or "commander". The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">species epithet</a> <i>mongoliensis</i> refers to the region where the type material was found, Inner Mongolia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborn1924_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osborn1924-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span></h2>
<p>The only known skull was found at a locality in the lower levels of the middle Eocene <a href="/wiki/Irdin_Manha_Formation" title="Irdin Manha Formation">Irdin Manha Formation</a> of Inner Mongolia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborn1924_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osborn1924-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_et_al_2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang_et_al_2010-2">[2]</a></sup> by the paleontological assistant Kan Chuen Pao during the spring of the second year (1923) of the Central Asiatic Expeditions (CAE) of the AMNH, leadered by the famous explorer and naturalist <a href="/wiki/Roy_Chapman_Andrews" title="Roy Chapman Andrews">Roy Chapman Andrews</a>. The skull is now on display at the American Museum of Natural History in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMNHblog_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMNHblog-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<p>It was classified in the clade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesonychia" title="Mesonychia">Mesonychia</a> due to the similarity in structure between its teeth and skull with those of other mesonychid species known from complete skeletons, however, much of this was based only on Osborn's original publication, and more recent studies have found it to have no special mesonychid affinities, instead grouping it with various artiodactyl clades. Indeed, one study (Spaulding <i>et al.</i>) has not only found them to be closer to <a href="/wiki/Entelodont" title="Entelodont">entelodonts</a>, but as kin to <a href="/wiki/Whippomorpha" title="Whippomorpha">Whippomorpha</a> in the clade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetacodontamorpha" title="Cetacodontamorpha">Cetacodontamorpha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spauldingetal2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spauldingetal2009-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="853" data-file-width="900" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg/220px-Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg/330px-Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg/440px-Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andrewsarchus_DB.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restoration of <i>A. mongoliensis</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type</a> skull of <i>Andrewsarchus mongoliensis</i> (AMNH 20135) is 83.4 cm (32.8 in) in basal length,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborn1924_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Osborn1924-1">[1]</a></sup> with a long snout comprising 60% of that measurement. The orbits of the eyes are set low and widely separated from one another by the snout, the saggital crest is small and the articulation for the mandible is shallow.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p><i>Andrewsarchus mongoliensis</i> has a complete placental tooth formula with 3 incisors, 1 canine, 4 premolars and 3 molars in each side of the jaws, like in entelodonts the incisors are arranged in a semicircular configuration, the second and third premolars are elongated and single cusped, the crowns of the molars are heavily wrinkled and the first and second molars are much more heavily worn than the precedent and subsequent teeth, in fact, the molars are so alike to those of entelodonts that it has been suggested that had they been found in isolation they would have been assigned to entelodonts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szalay.26Gould1966_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Szalay.26Gould1966-5">[5]</a></sup> Between the dental features unique to <i>A. mongoliensis</i> we find greatly enlarged second incisors, as big as the canines, which despite not being preserved can be estimated from the diameter of their tooth sockets, they were proportionally small compared to the whole dentition and the size of the skull according to Szalay and Gould (1966) contra Osborn (1924).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szalay.26Gould1966_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Szalay.26Gould1966-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comp-megapredators.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1204" data-file-width="3584" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Comp-megapredators.jpg/220px-Comp-megapredators.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Comp-megapredators.jpg/330px-Comp-megapredators.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Comp-megapredators.jpg/440px-Comp-megapredators.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comp-megapredators.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Size comparison of <i>Andrewsarchus</i> (orange) against other carnivores</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Osborn (1924) declared <i>Andrewsarchus</i> as the largest terrestrial mammalian carnivore known on the basis of the length of the skull, which he used to estimate its size comparing it to the mesonychid <i><a href="/wiki/Mesonyx" title="Mesonyx">Mesonyx</a></i>,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborn1924_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Osborn1924-1">[1]</a></sup> However, since the known morphology of <i>Andrewsarchus</i> is entelodont-like and consequently very different to mesonychids in habits and likely in body proportions, according to Szalay and Gould (1966) if a size estimate has to be made it would be more appropriate to follow the proportions of entelodonts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szalay.26Gould1966_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Szalay.26Gould1966-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
