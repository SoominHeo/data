<p><b>Antony Hewish</b> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup> (born 11 May 1924 in <a href="/wiki/Fowey" title="Fowey">Fowey</a>, Cornwall) is a British <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomer</a> who won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Physics" title="Nobel Prize for Physics">Nobel Prize for Physics</a> in 1974 (together with fellow radio-astronomer <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ryle" title="Martin Ryle">Martin Ryle</a>) for his work on the development of radio <a href="/wiki/Aperture_synthesis" title="Aperture synthesis">aperture synthesis</a> and its role in the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>. He was also awarded the <a href="/wiki/Eddington_Medal" title="Eddington Medal">Eddington Medal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a> in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NobelA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NobelA-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antony_Hewish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He attended <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Taunton" title="King's College, Taunton">King's College, Taunton</a>. His undergraduate degree at <a href="/wiki/Gonville_and_Caius_College,_Cambridge" title="Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge">Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge</a>, was interrupted by war service at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aircraft_Establishment" title="Royal Aircraft Establishment">Royal Aircraft Establishment</a>, and at the <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Research_Establishment" title="Telecommunications Research Establishment">Telecommunications Research Establishment</a> where he worked with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ryle" title="Martin Ryle">Martin Ryle</a>. Returning to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> in 1946, Hewish completed his degree and immediately joined <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Astrophysics_Group" title="Cavendish Astrophysics Group">Ryle's research team</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratory" title="Cavendish Laboratory">Cavendish Laboratory</a>, obtaining his PhD in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phd_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-phd-7">[7]</a></sup> Hewish made both practical and theoretical advances in the observation and exploitation of the apparent <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_scintillation" title="Interplanetary scintillation">scintillations</a> of radio sources due to their radiation impinging upon <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>. This led him to propose, and secure funding for, the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Scintillation_Array" title="Interplanetary Scintillation Array">Interplanetary Scintillation Array</a>, a large array radio telescope at the <a href="/wiki/Mullard_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory" title="Mullard Radio Astronomy Observatory">Mullard Radio Astronomy Observatory (MRAO)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> to conduct a high time-resolution radio survey of <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_scintillation" title="Interplanetary scintillation">interplanetary scintillation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nobel_prize">Nobel prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antony_Hewish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nobel prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the course of this survey, one of his graduate students, <a href="/wiki/Jocelyn_Bell_Burnell" title="Jocelyn Bell Burnell">Jocelyn Bell</a>, noted the radio source which was ultimately recognised as the first <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a>. The paper announcing the discovery<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> had five authors, Hewish's name being listed first, Bell's second. Hewish and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ryle" title="Martin Ryle">Martin Ryle</a> were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> in 1974 for work on the development of radio aperture synthesis and its role in the discovery of pulsars. The Nobel award to Ryle and Hewish without the inclusion of Bell as a co-recipient was controversial, and was roundly condemned by Hewish's fellow astronomer <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize_controversies" title="Nobel prize controversies">Nobel prize controversies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_research">Career and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antony_Hewish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hewish was professor of radio astronomy at the <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratory" title="Cavendish Laboratory">Cavendish Laboratory</a> from 1971 to 1989, and head of the <a href="/wiki/Mullard_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory" title="Mullard Radio Astronomy Observatory">MRAO</a> from 1982 to 1988. He developed an association with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution" title="Royal Institution">Royal Institution</a> in London when it was directed by Sir <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Bragg" title="Lawrence Bragg">Lawrence Bragg</a>. In 1965 he was invited to co-deliver the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_Christmas_Lectures" title="Royal Institution Christmas Lectures">Royal Institution Christmas Lecture</a> on <i>Exploration of the Universe</i>. He subsequently gave several Friday Evening Discourses<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NobelA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NobelA-6">[6]</a></sup> and was made a Professor of the Royal Institution in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WW_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-WW-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hewish is a fellow of <a href="/wiki/Churchill_College,_Cambridge" title="Churchill College, Cambridge">Churchill College, Cambridge</a>. He is also a member of the Advisory Council for the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Science_and_Engineering" title="Campaign for Science and Engineering">Campaign for Science and Engineering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CaSE_Advisory_Council_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CaSE_Advisory_Council-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antony_Hewish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hewish has Honorary degrees from six universities including Manchester, Exeter and Cambridge, is a Foreign Member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Royal_Academies_for_Science_and_the_Arts_of_Belgium" title="The Royal Academies for Science and the Arts of Belgium">Belgian Royal Academy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Science_Academy" title="Indian National Science Academy">Indian National Science Academy</a>. Other awards and honours include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WW_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-WW-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_1968" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 1968">Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 1968</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Eddington Medal, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a> (1969)</li>
<li>Dellinger Gold Medal, <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Radio_Science" title="International Union of Radio Science">International Union of Radio Science</a> (1972)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_A._Michelson_Medal" title="Albert A. Michelson Medal">Albert A. Michelson Medal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a> (1973, jointly with <a href="/wiki/Jocelyn_Bell_Burnell" title="Jocelyn Bell Burnell">Jocelyn Bell Burnell</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MichelsonMedal_Laureates_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MichelsonMedal_Laureates-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Nobel Prize for Physics (jointly) (1974)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hughes_Medal" title="Hughes Medal">Hughes Medal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> (1977)</li>
<li>Elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Physics" title="Institute of Physics">Institute of Physics</a> (FInstP) in 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WW_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-WW-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antony_Hewish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hewish married Marjorie Elizabeth Catherine Richards in 1950. They have a son, a physicist, and a daughter, a language teacher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NobelA_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NobelA-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antony_Hewish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hewish has argued that religion and science are complementary. In the foreword to <i><a href="/wiki/Questions_of_Truth" title="Questions of Truth">Questions of Truth</a></i> Hewish writes, "The ghostly presence of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">virtual particles</a> defies rational common sense and is non-intuitive for those unacquainted with physics. Religious belief in God, and Christian belief ... may seem strange to common-sense thinking. But when the most elementary physical things behave in this way, we should be prepared to accept that the deepest aspects of our existence go beyond our common-sense understanding."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
