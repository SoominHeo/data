<p><b>Ansel Easton Adams</b> (February 20, 1902 – April 22, 1984) was an American photographer and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalist</a>. His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">black-and-white</a> <a href="/wiki/Landscape_photography" title="Landscape photography">landscape photographs</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_West" title="American West">American West</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a>, have been widely reproduced on calendars, posters, books, and the internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams and <a href="/wiki/Fred_R._Archer" title="Fred R. Archer">Fred Archer</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Zone_System" title="Zone System">Zone System</a> as a way to determine proper exposure and adjust the contrast of the final print. The resulting clarity and depth characterized his photographs. He primarily used <a href="/wiki/Large_format" title="Large format">large-format</a> cameras because their high <a href="/wiki/Image_resolution" title="Image resolution">resolution</a> helped ensure sharpness in his images.</p>
<p>Adams founded the photography group known as <a href="/wiki/Group_f/64" title="Group f/64">Group f/64</a>, along with fellow photographers <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Dyke" title="Willard Van Dyke">Willard Van Dyke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adams was born in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Addition,_San_Francisco" title="Western Addition, San Francisco">Western Addition of San Francisco, California</a>, the only child of Charles Hitchcock Adams and Olive Bray Adams. He was named after his uncle Ansel Easton. His mother's family came from Baltimore, where his maternal grandfather had a successful freight-hauling business but lost his wealth investing in failed mining and real estate ventures in Nevada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams19854_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams19854-2">[2]</a></sup> The Adams family came from New England, having migrated from the north of Ireland in the early 18th century. His paternal grandfather founded and built a prosperous lumber business which his father later ran, though his father's natural talents lay more with sciences than with business. Later in life, Adams condemned that very same industry for cutting down many of the great redwood forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder19964_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder19964-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1907, his family moved 2 miles (3 km) west to a new home near the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Cliff,_San_Francisco" title="Sea Cliff, San Francisco">Seacliff</a> neighbourhood, just south of the <a href="/wiki/Presidio_of_San_Francisco" title="Presidio of San Francisco">Presidio Army Base</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhittington2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhittington2010-4">[4]</a></sup> The home had a "splendid view" of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate" title="Golden Gate">Golden Gate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marin_Headlands" title="Marin Headlands">Marin Headlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder19966_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder19966-5">[5]</a></sup> San Francisco was devastated by the April 18, <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">1906 San Francisco earthquake</a>. The four year-old Ansel Adams was uninjured in the initial shaking, but was tossed face-first into a garden wall during an <a href="/wiki/Aftershock" title="Aftershock">aftershock</a> three hours later, breaking and scarring his nose. Among his earliest memories was watching the smoke from the ensuing fire that destroyed much of the city a few miles to the east. A doctor recommended that his nose be reset once he reached maturity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder19962_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder19962-6">[6]</a></sup> but it remained crooked for his entire life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008a_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008a-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactive" title="Hyperactive">hyperactive</a> child and prone to frequent sickness and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypochondria" title="Hypochondria">hypochondria</a>. He had few friends, but his family home and surroundings on the heights facing the Golden Gate provided ample childhood activities. He had no patience for games or sports, but he took to the beauty of nature at an early age, collecting bugs and exploring <a href="/wiki/Lobos_Creek" title="Lobos Creek">Lobos Creek</a> all the way to <a href="/wiki/Baker_Beach" title="Baker Beach">Baker Beach</a> and the sea cliffs leading to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lands_End,_San_Francisco" title="Lands End, San Francisco">Lands End</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder19966_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder19966-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198514_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198514-8">[8]</a></sup> "San Francisco's wildest and rockiest coast, a place strewn with shipwrecks and rife with landslides."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>His father bought a three-inch telescope and they enthusiastically shared the hobby of <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomy</a>, visiting the <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">Lick Observatory</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hamilton_(California)" title="Mount Hamilton (California)">Mount Hamilton</a> together. His father went on to serve as the paid secretary-treasurer of the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Society_of_the_Pacific" title="Astronomical Society of the Pacific">Astronomical Society of the Pacific</a> from 1925 to 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ansel's father's business suffered great financial losses after the death of Ansel's grandfather and the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1907" title="Panic of 1907">Panic of 1907</a>. Some of the induced near-poverty was because Ansel's uncle Ansel Easton and <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Wright" title="Cedric Wright">Cedric Wright</a>'s father George Wright had secretly sold their shares of the company to the Hawaiian Sugar Trust for a large amount of money, "knowingly providing the controlling interest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198540_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198540-11">[11]</a></sup> By 1912, the family's standard of living had dropped sharply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder19969_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder19969-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ansel was dismissed from several private schools for being restless and inattentive, so his father decided to pull him out of school in 1915 at the age of 12. Adams was then educated by private tutors, his Aunt Mary, and by his father. His Aunt Mary was a follower of <a href="/wiki/Robert_G._Ingersoll" title="Robert G. Ingersoll">Robert G. Ingersoll</a>, a 19th-century agnostic, <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">women's suffrage</a> advocate. As a result of his aunt's influence, Ingersoll's teachings were important to Ansel's upbringing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199611_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199611-13">[13]</a></sup> During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panama-Pacific_International_Exposition" title="Panama-Pacific International Exposition">Panama-Pacific International Exposition</a> in 1915, his father insisted that Adams spend part of each day studying the exhibits as part of his education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198518_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198518-14">[14]</a></sup> After a while, he resumed and then completed his formal education by attending the Mrs. Kate M. Wilkins Private School, until he graduated from eighth grade on June 8, 1917. In his later years, he displayed his diploma in the guest bathroom of his home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996276_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996276-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>His father raised him to follow the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a>: to live a modest, moral life guided by a social responsibility to man and to nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199611_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199611-13">[13]</a></sup> Adams had a warm, loving, and supportive relationship with his father, but he had a distant relationship with his mother, who did not approve of his interest in photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199652_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199652-16">[16]</a></sup> The day after her death in 1950, Ansel had a dispute with the undertaker when choosing which casket she would be buried in. He chose the cheapest in the room, a $260 casket that seemed the least he could purchase without doing the job himself. The undertaker remarked, "Have you no respect for the dead?" Adams replied, "One more crack like that and I will take Mama elsewhere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198545_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198545-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth">Youth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Youth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adams became interested in piano at age 12, and music became the main focus of his later youth. His father sent him to piano teacher Marie Butler, who focused on perfectionism and accuracy. After four years of studying under her guidance, he moved on to other teachers, one being composer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cowell" title="Henry Cowell">Henry Cowell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond15_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond15-18">[18]</a></sup> For the next twelve years, the piano was Adams' primary occupation and, by 1920, his intended profession. Although he ultimately gave up music for photography, the piano brought substance, discipline, and structure to his frustrating and erratic youth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Moreover, the careful training and exacting craft required of a musician profoundly informed his visual artistry, as well as his influential writings and teachings on photography.</p>
<p>Adams first visited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yosemite" title="Yosemite">Yosemite National Park</a> in 1916 with his family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStillman200712_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStillman200712-19">[19]</a></sup> He wrote of his first view of the valley: "the splendor of Yosemite burst upon us and it <i>was</i> glorious.... One wonder after another descended upon us.... There was light everywhere.... A new era began for me." His father gave him his first camera during that stay, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" title="Eastman Kodak">Kodak</a> <a href="/wiki/Brownie_(camera)" title="Brownie (camera)">Brownie box camera</a>, and he took his first photographs with his "usual hyperactive enthusiasm".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198553_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198553-20">[20]</a></sup> He returned to Yosemite on his own the following year with better cameras and a tripod. In the winter, he learned basic <a href="/wiki/Darkroom" title="Darkroom">darkroom</a> technique working part-time for a San Francisco photo finisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199636_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199636-21">[21]</a></sup> Adams avidly read photography magazines, attended camera club meetings, and went to photography and art exhibits. He explored the High Sierra in summer and winter with retired geologist and amateur <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> Francis Holman, whom he called "Uncle Frank," developing the stamina and skill needed to photograph at high elevation and under difficult weather conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199623_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199623-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>While in Yosemite, he had frequent contact with the Best family, owners of Best's Studio, who allowed him to practice on their old <a href="/wiki/Square_piano" title="Square piano">square piano</a>. In 1928, he married Virginia Best in Best's Studio in Yosemite Valley. Virginia inherited the studio from her artist father on his death in 1935, and the Adams continued to operate it until 1971. The studio is now known as the Ansel Adams Gallery and remains in the hands of the Adams family.</p>
<p>At age 27, Adams joined the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>, a group dedicated to protecting the wild places of the earth, and he was hired as the summer caretaker of the Sierra Club visitor center in Yosemite Valley, the <a href="/wiki/LeConte_Memorial_Lodge" title="LeConte Memorial Lodge">LeConte Memorial Lodge</a>, from 1920 to 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He remained a member throughout his lifetime and served as a director, as did his wife. He was first elected to the Sierra Club's board of directors in 1934, and served on the board for 37 years until 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008a_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008a-7">[7]</a></sup> Adams participated in the club's annual <a href="/wiki/High_Trips" title="High Trips">High Trips</a>, and was later responsible for several <a href="/wiki/First_ascent" title="First ascent">first ascents</a> in the Sierra Nevada.</p>
<p>He contracted the Spanish Flu during the <a href="/wiki/1918_flu_pandemic" title="1918 flu pandemic">1918 flu pandemic</a> and fell seriously ill, but he recovered after several months to resume his outdoor life.</p>
<p>During his twenties, most of his friends came from musical connections, particularly violinist and amateur photographer <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Wright" title="Cedric Wright">Cedric Wright</a>, who became his best friend as well as his philosophical and cultural mentor. Their shared philosophy came from <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carpenter" title="Edward Carpenter">Edward Carpenter</a>'s <i>Towards Democracy</i>, a literary work which espoused the pursuit of beauty in life and art. For several years, Adams carried a pocket edition with him while at Yosemite,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199647_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199647-24">[24]</a></sup> and it became his personal philosophy as well. He later stated, "I believe in beauty. I believe in stones and water, air and soil, people and their future and their fate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams19859_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams19859-25">[25]</a></sup> He decided that the purpose of his art, whether photography or music, was to reveal that beauty to others and to inspire them to the same calling.</p>
<p>In summer, Adams would enjoy a life of hiking, camping, and photographing, and the rest of the year he worked to improve his piano playing, expanding his piano technique and musical expression. He also gave piano lessons for extra income, finally affording a grand piano suitable to his musical ambitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198527_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198527-26">[26]</a></sup> An early student was mountaineer and fellow Sierra Club leader <a href="/wiki/Jules_Eichorn" title="Jules Eichorn">Jules Eichorn</a>.</p>
<p>His first photographs were published in 1921, and Best's Studio began selling his Yosemite prints the following year. His early photos already showed careful composition and sensitivity to tonal balance. In letters and cards to family, he wrote of having dared to climb to the best view points and to brave the worst elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinderStillmanAdamsStegner19883_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinderStillmanAdamsStegner19883-27">[27]</a></sup> At this point, however, Adams was still planning a career in music, even though he felt that his small hands limited his repertoire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198528_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198528-28">[28]</a></sup> It took seven more years for him to conclude that, at best, he might only become a concert pianist of limited range, an accompanist, or a piano teacher.</p>
<p>In the mid-1920s, Adams experimented with soft-focus, etching, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_Print_Process#Bromoil_process" title="Oil Print Process">bromoil process</a>, and other techniques of the <a href="/wiki/Pictorialism" title="Pictorialism">pictorial</a> photographers, such as <a href="/wiki/Photo-Secession" title="Photo-Secession">Photo-Secession</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz" title="Alfred Stieglitz">Alfred Stieglitz</a> who strove to put photography on an equal artistic plane with painting by trying to mimic it. However, Adams steered clear of hand-coloring which was also popular at the time. He used a variety of lenses to get different effects, but eventually rejected pictorialism for a more realistic approach which relied more heavily on sharp focus, heightened contrast, precise exposure, and darkroom craftsmanship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199638.E2.80.9342_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199638.E2.80.9342-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography_career">Photography career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Photography career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1920s">1920s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1920s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1927, Adams produced his first portfolio in his new style <i><a href="/wiki/Parmelian_Prints_of_the_High_Sierras" title="Parmelian Prints of the High Sierras">Parmelian Prints of the High Sierras</a></i>, which included his famous image <i>Monolith, the Face of Half Dome</i>, taken with his Korona view camera using glass plates and a dark red filter (to heighten the tonal contrasts). On that excursion, he had only one plate left and he "visualized" the effect of the blackened sky before risking the last shot. He later said, "I had been able to realize a desired image: not the way the subject appeared in reality but how it <i>felt</i> to me and how it must appear in the finished print".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198576_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198576-30">[30]</a></sup> In April 1927, he wrote, "My photographs have now reached a stage when they are worthy of the world's critical examination. I have suddenly come upon a new style which I believe will place my work equal to anything of its kind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinderStillmanAdamsStegner198830_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinderStillmanAdamsStegner198830-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams's first portfolio was a success, earning nearly $3,900 with the sponsorship and promotion of <a href="/wiki/Albert_M._Bender" title="Albert M. Bender">Albert Bender</a>, an arts-connected businessman. Soon he received commercial assignments to photograph the wealthy patrons who bought his portfolio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199662_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199662-32">[32]</a></sup> He also came to understand how important it was that his carefully crafted photos were reproduced to best effect. At Bender's invitation, he joined the Roxburghe Club, an association devoted to fine printing and high standards in book arts. He learned much about printing techniques, inks, design, and layout which he later applied to other projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199668_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199668-33">[33]</a></sup> Unfortunately, at that time most of his darkroom work was still being done in the basement of his parents' home, and he was limited by barely adequate equipment.</p>
<p>He married Virginia Best in 1928 after a cooling-off period during 1925–26, during which he had short-lasting relationships with various women. The newly-weds moved in with his parents to save expenses. His marriage also marked the end of his serious attempt at a musical career, as well as her ambitions to be a classical singer.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s">1930s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1930s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1929 and 1942, Adams's work matured and he became more established. In the course of his 60-year career, the 1930s were a particularly productive and experimental time. He expanded his works, focusing on detailed close-ups as well as large forms from mountains to factories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArtInfo2006_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtInfo2006-34">[34]</a></sup> His first book <i><a href="/wiki/Taos_Pueblo_(book)" title="Taos Pueblo (book)">Taos Pueblo</a></i> was published in 1930 with text by writer <a href="/wiki/Mary_Hunter_Austin" title="Mary Hunter Austin">Mary Hunter Austin</a>. In New Mexico, he was introduced to notables from Stieglitz's circle, including painter <a href="/wiki/Georgia_O%27Keeffe" title="Georgia O'Keeffe">Georgia O'Keeffe</a>, artist <a href="/wiki/John_Marin" title="John Marin">John Marin</a>, and photographer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Strand" title="Paul Strand">Paul Strand</a>. Adams's talkative, high-spirited nature combined with his excellent piano playing made him a hit within his circle of artist friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199673.E2.80.9374_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199673.E2.80.9374-35">[35]</a></sup> Strand especially proved influential, sharing secrets of his technique with Adams, and finally convincing Adams to pursue photography with all his talent and energy. One of Strand's suggestions which Adams adopted was to use glossy paper to intensify tonal values.</p>
<p>Adams was able to put on his first solo museum exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> in 1931 through a friend who had connections in Washington, featuring 60 prints taken in the High Sierra. He received an excellent review from the <i>Washington Post</i>: "His photographs are like portraits of the giant peaks, which seem to be inhabited by mythical gods."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199677_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199677-36">[36]</a></sup> Despite his success, Adams felt that he was not yet up to the standards of Strand. He decided to broaden his subject matter to include still life and close-up photos, and to achieve higher quality by "visualizing" each image before taking it. He emphasized the use of small apertures and long exposures in natural light, which created sharp details with a wide range of focus, as demonstrated in <i>Rose and Driftwood</i> (1933), one of his finest still-life photographs.</p>
<p>In 1932, Adams had a group show at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._H._de_Young_Museum" title="M. H. de Young Museum">M. H. de Young Museum</a> with <a href="/wiki/Imogen_Cunningham" title="Imogen Cunningham">Imogen Cunningham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a>, and they soon formed <a href="/wiki/Group_f/64" title="Group f/64">Group f/64</a> which espoused "pure or straight photography" over <a href="/wiki/Pictorialism" title="Pictorialism">pictorialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/F-number" title="F-number"><span style="font-style:italic;font-family:Trebuchet MS,Candara,Georgia,Calibri,Corbel,serif">f</span>/64</a> being a very small <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a> setting that gives great <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">depth of field</a>. The group's manifesto stated, "Pure photography is defined as possessing no qualities of technique, composition or idea, derivative of any other art form."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199687_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199687-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams opened his own art and photography gallery in San Francisco in 1933, following Stieglitz's example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985115_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985115-38">[38]</a></sup> He also began to publish essays in photography magazines and wrote his first instructional book <i>Making a Photograph</i> in 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996114_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996114-39">[39]</a></sup> During the summers, he often participated in Sierra Club <a href="/wiki/High_Trips" title="High Trips">High Trips</a> outings, as a paid photographer for the group, and the rest of the year a core group of the Club members socialized regularly in San Francisco and Berkeley. During 1933, his first child Michael was born, followed by Anne two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996102_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996102-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1930s, Adams began to deploy his photographs in the cause of wilderness preservation. In part, he was inspired by the increasing desecration of Yosemite Valley by commercial development, including a pool hall, bowling alley, golf course, shops, and auto-mobile traffic. He created the limited-edition book <i>Sierra Nevada: The John Muir Trail</i> in 1938, as part of the Sierra Club's efforts to secure the designation of <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_National_Park" title="Sequoia National Park">Sequoia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kings_Canyon_National_Park" title="Kings Canyon National Park">Kings Canyon</a> as national parks. This book and his testimony before <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> played a vital role in the success of the effort, and Congress designated the area as a National Park in 1940.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Yosemite Valley, to me, is always a sunrise, a glitter of green and golden wonder in a vast edifice of stone and space. I know of no sculpture, painting or music that exceeds the compelling spiritual command of the soaring shape of granite cliff and dome, of patina of light on rock and forest, and of the thunder and whispering of the falling, flowing waters. At first the colossal aspect may dominate; then we perceive and respond to the delicate and persuasive complex of nature.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Ansel Adams, The Portfolios Of Ansel Adams</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1935, Adams created many new photos of the Sierra, and one of his most famous photographs <i>Clearing Winter Storm</i> captured the entire valley just as a winter storm relented, leaving a fresh coat of snow. He gathered his recent work and had a solo show at Stieglitz's "An American Place" gallery in New York in 1936. The exhibition proved successful with both the critics and the buying public, and earned Adams strong praise from the revered Stieglitz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996120_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996120-41">[41]</a></sup> During the balance of the 1930s, Adams took on many commercial assignments to supplement the income from the struggling Best's Studio. Until the 1970s, Adams was financially dependent on commercial projects. Some of his clients included Kodak, <i>Fortune</i> magazine, Pacific Gas and Electric, AT&amp;T, and the American Trust Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996158_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996158-42">[42]</a></sup> He photographed <a href="/wiki/Timothy_L._Pflueger" title="Timothy L. Pflueger">Timothy L. Pflueger</a>'s new Patent Leather Bar for the <a href="/wiki/Westin_St._Francis" title="Westin St. Francis">St. Francis</a> hotel in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamlin2003_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamlin2003-43">[43]</a></sup> The same year, he was named an editor of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Camera_%26_Travel" title="U.S. Camera &amp; Travel">U.S. Camera &amp; Travel</a></i>, the most popular photography magazine at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996158_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996158-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1940s">1940s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1940s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1940, Ansel put together <i>A Pageant of Photography</i>, the most important and largest photography show in the West to date, attended by millions of visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996159_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996159-44">[44]</a></sup> With his wife, Adams completed a children's book and the very successful <i>Illustrated Guide to Yosemite Valley</i> during 1940 and 1941. He also taught photography by giving workshops in Detroit. Adams also began his first serious stint of teaching in 1941 at the Art Center School of Los Angeles, now known as <i><a href="/wiki/Art_Center_College_of_Design" title="Art Center College of Design">Art Center College of Design</a></i>, which included the training of military photographers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985312_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985312-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1943, Adams had a camera platform mounted on his station wagon, to afford him a better vantage point over the immediate foreground and a better angle for expansive backgrounds. Most of his landscapes from that time forward were made from the roof of his car rather than from summits reached by rugged hiking, as in his earlier days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996239_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996239-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>On a trip in New Mexico in 1941, Adams shot a scene of the Moon rising above a modest village with snow-covered mountains in the background, under a dominating black sky. The photograph is one of his most famous and is named <i><a href="/wiki/Moonrise,_Hernandez,_New_Mexico" title="Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico">Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico</a></i>. Adams's description in his later books of how it was made probably enhanced the photograph's fame: the light on the crosses in the foreground was rapidly fading, and he could not find his exposure meter; however, he remembered the <a href="/wiki/Luminance" title="Luminance">luminance</a> of the Moon, and used it to calculate the proper exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1981127_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1981127-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985273.E2.80.93275_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985273.E2.80.93275-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198340.E2.80.9343_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198340.E2.80.9343-49">[49]</a></sup> Adams's earlier account<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaloney194288.E2.80.9389_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaloney194288.E2.80.9389-50">[50]</a></sup> was less dramatic, stating simply that the photograph was made after sunset, with exposure determined using his Weston Master meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[n 1]</a></sup> However the exposure was actually determined, the foreground was underexposed, the highlights in the clouds were quite dense, and the negative proved difficult to print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams198342_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams198342-52">[51]</a></sup> The initial publication of <i>Moonrise</i> was in <i>U.S. Camera 1943</i> annual, after being selected by the "photo judge" for <i>U.S. Camera</i>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Steichen" title="Edward Steichen">Edward Steichen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996192_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996192-53">[52]</a></sup> This gave <i>Moonrise</i> an audience before its first formal exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996193_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996193-54">[53]</a></sup> Over nearly 40 years, Adams re-interpreted the image, his most popular by far, using the latest darkroom equipment at his disposal, making over 1,300 unique prints, most in 16″ by 20″ format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrew_Smith_Gallery2008_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrew_Smith_Gallery2008-55">[54]</a></sup> Many of the prints were made in the 1970s, finally giving Adams financial independence from commercial projects. The total value of these original prints exceeds $25,000,000;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996189.E2.80.93199_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996189.E2.80.93199-56">[55]</a></sup> the highest price paid for a single print of <i>Moonrise</i> reached $609,600 at Sotheby's New York auction in 2006.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In September 1941, Adams contracted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[n 2]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior</a> to make photographs of National Parks, Indian reservations, and other locations for use as mural-sized prints for decoration of the Department's new building. Part of his understanding with the Department was that he might also make photographs for his own use, using his own film and processing. Although Adams kept meticulous records of his travel and expenses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWrightArmor1988vi_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWrightArmor1988vi-58">[56]</a></sup> he was less disciplined about recording the dates of his images, and neglected to note the date of <i>Moonrise</i>, so it was not clear whether it belonged to Adams or to the U.S. Government. But the position of the moon allowed the image to eventually be dated from astronomical calculations, and it was determined that <i>Moonrise</i> was made on November 1, 1941,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[n 3]</a></sup> a day for which he had not billed the Department, so the image belonged to Adams. The same was not true for many of his other negatives, including <i>The Tetons and the Snake River</i>, which, having been made for the Mural Project, became the property of the U.S. Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996201_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996201-60">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Edward_Steichen" title="Edward Steichen">Edward Steichen</a> formed his <a href="/wiki/Naval_Aviation_Photographic_Unit" title="Naval Aviation Photographic Unit">Naval Aviation Photographic Unit</a> in early 1942, he wanted Adams to be a member, to build and direct a state-of-the-art darkroom and laboratory in Washington, D.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996172_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996172-61">[58]</a></sup> In approximately February 1942, Steichen asked Adams to join.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996172_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996172-61">[58]</a></sup> Adams agreed, with two conditions: He wanted to be commissioned as an officer, and he also told Steichen he would not be available until July 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996173_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996173-62">[59]</a></sup> Steichen, who wanted the team assembled as quickly as possible, passed Adams by, and had his other photographers ready to go by early April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996173_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996173-62">[59]</a></sup></p>

<p>Adams was distressed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_American_Internment" title="Japanese American Internment">Japanese American Internment</a> that occurred after the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a> attack. He requested permission to visit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manzanar_War_Relocation_Center" title="Manzanar War Relocation Center">Manzanar War Relocation Center</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Owens_Valley" title="Owens Valley">Owens Valley</a>, at the foot of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Williamson" title="Mount Williamson">Mount Williamson</a>. The resulting photo-essay first appeared in a <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> exhibit, and later was published as <i><a href="/wiki/Born_Free_and_Equal" title="Born Free and Equal">Born Free and Equal: The Story of Loyal Japanese-Americans</a></i>. He also contributed to the war effort by doing many photographic assignments for the military, including making prints of secret Japanese installations in the Aleutians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996175_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996175-63">[60]</a></sup> "[I]t was met with some distressing resistance and was rejected by many as disloyal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985263_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985263-64">[61]</a></sup> Adams was the recipient of three <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Foundation" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Foundation">Guggenheim</a> fellowships during his career, the first in 1946 to photograph every National Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996217_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996217-65">[62]</a></sup> This series of photographs produced memorable images of <a href="/wiki/Old_Faithful" title="Old Faithful">Old Faithful Geyser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Teton" title="Grand Teton">Grand Teton</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_McKinley" title="Mount McKinley">Mount McKinley</a>.</p>
<p>In 1945, Adams was asked to form the first fine art photography department at the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Art_Institute" title="San Francisco Art Institute">San Francisco Art Institute</a>. Adams invited <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Lange" title="Dorothea Lange">Dorothea Lange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imogen_Cunningham" title="Imogen Cunningham">Imogen Cunningham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a> to be guest lecturers and <a href="/wiki/Minor_White" title="Minor White">Minor White</a> to be lead instructor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVernacular_Language_North5_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVernacular_Language_North5-66">[63]</a></sup> The photography department produced numerous notable photographers, including <a href="/wiki/Philip_Hyde_(photographer)" title="Philip Hyde (photographer)">Philip Hyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamen_Chinn" title="Benjamen Chinn">Benjamen Chinn</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Heick" title="William Heick">Bill Heick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/C._Cameron_Macauley" title="C. Cameron Macauley">C. Cameron Macauley</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1952 Adams was one of the founders of the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Aperture_(magazine)" title="Aperture (magazine)">Aperture</a></i>, which was intended as a serious journal of photography showcasing its best practitioners and newest innovations. He was also a contributor to <i><a href="/wiki/Arizona_Highways" title="Arizona Highways">Arizona Highways</a></i>, a photo-rich travel magazine. His article on <i><a href="/wiki/Mission_San_Xavier_del_Bac" title="Mission San Xavier del Bac">Mission San Xavier del Bac</a></i>, with text by longtime friend <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Newhall" title="Nancy Newhall">Nancy Newhall</a>, was enlarged into a book published in 1954. This was the first of many collaborations with her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996251_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996251-67">[64]</a></sup> In June 1955, Adams began his annual workshops, teaching thousands of students until 1981,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985316_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985316-68">[65]</a></sup> He continued with commercial assignments for another twenty years, and became a consultant on a monthly retainer for <a href="/wiki/Polaroid_Corporation" title="Polaroid Corporation">Polaroid Corporation</a>, which was founded by good friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwin_Land" title="Edwin Land">Edwin Land</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996260_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996260-69">[66]</a></sup> He made thousands of photographs with Polaroid products, <i>El Capitan, Winter, Sunrise</i> (1968) being the one he considered his most memorable. In the final twenty years of his life, the 6x6cm medium format <a href="/wiki/Hasselblad" title="Hasselblad">Hasselblad</a> was his camera of choice, with <i>Moon and Half Dome</i> (1960) being his favorite photo made with that marque of camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985375_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985375-70">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams published his fourth portfolio, <i>What Majestic Word,</i> in 1963, and dedicated it to the memory of his Sierra Club friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russell_Varian" title="Russell Varian">Russell Varian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond108_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond108-71">[68]</a></sup> who was a co-inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">klystron</a> and who had died in 1959. The title was taken from the poem "Sand Dunes," by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Varian" title="John Varian">John Varian</a>, Russell's father,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond15_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond15-18">[18]</a></sup> and the fifteen photographs were accompanied by the writings of both John and Russell Varian. Russell's widow, Dorothy, wrote the preface, and explained that the photographs were selected to serve as interpretations of the character of Russell Varian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond108_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond108-71">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1960s, a few mainstream art galleries (without a photographic emphasis) which originally would have considered photos unworthy of exhibit alongside fine paintings decided to show Adams's images, particularly the former Kenmore Gallery in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoldbloom19903_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldbloom19903-72">[69]</a></sup> In March 1963, Ansel Adams and Nancy Newhall accepted a commission from <a href="/wiki/Clark_Kerr" title="Clark Kerr">Clark Kerr</a>, the president of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a>, to produce a series of photographs of the University's campuses to commemorate its centennial celebration. The collection, titled <i>Fiat Lux</i> after the University's motto, was published in 1967 and now resides in the Museum of Photography at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Riverside" title="University of California, Riverside">University of California, Riverside</a>.</p>
<p>In 1974, Adams was guest of honor at the <a href="/wiki/Rencontres_d%27Arles" title="Rencontres d'Arles">Rencontres d'Arles</a> festival (in France). An evening screening at the <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a>'s Théâtre Antique and an exhibition were presented. The festival celebrated the artist three more times after that: in 1976, 1982 and 1985, through screenings and exhibitions.</p>
<p>In 1974, Adams had a major retrospective exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>. Much of his time during the 1970s was spent <a href="/wiki/Content_curation" title="Content curation">curating</a> and reprinting negatives from his vault, in part to satisfy the great demand of art museums which had finally created departments of photography and desired his iconic works. He also devoted his considerable writing skills and prestige to the cause of <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, focusing particularly on the <a href="/wiki/Big_Sur" title="Big Sur">Big Sur</a> coastline of California and the protection of Yosemite from overuse. <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">President Jimmy Carter</a> commissioned him to make the first official portrait of a president made by a photograph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996294.E2.80.93295_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder1996294.E2.80.93295-73">[70]</a></sup> That year he also cofounded the Center for Creative Photography at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a>, which handles some of his estate matters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_with_color_film">Work with color film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Work with color film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adams was mostly known for his boldly printed, large format black-and-white images, but he also worked extensively in color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[72]</a></sup> However, he felt closest to black-and-white photography, which he believed could be manipulated to produce a wide range of bold, expressive tones, and he felt constricted by the rigidity of the color process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_and_influence">Contributions and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Contributions and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg"><img alt="A dramatically-lit black-and-white photograph depicts a large river, which snakes from the bottom right to the center left of the picture. The sky is overcast in parts, but only partly cloudy in others, and the sun shines through to illuminate the scene and reflect off the river in these places." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2402" data-file-width="3000" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg/220px-Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg/330px-Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg/440px-Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Adams_The_Tetons_and_the_Snake_River.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Tetons and the Snake River</i> (1942)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Romantic landscape artists <a href="/wiki/Albert_Bierstadt" title="Albert Bierstadt">Albert Bierstadt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Moran" title="Thomas Moran">Thomas Moran</a> portrayed the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yosemite" title="Yosemite">Yosemite</a> at the end of their reign, and were subsequently displaced by daredevil photographers Carleton Watkins, <a href="/wiki/Eadweard_Muybridge" title="Eadweard Muybridge">Eadweard Muybridge</a>, and George Fiske.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinder199633_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinder199633-77">[74]</a></sup> But it was Adams's black-and-white photographs of the West which became the foremost record of what many of the National Parks were like before tourism, and his persistent advocacy helped expand the National Park system. He used his works to promote many of the goals of the Sierra Club and of the nascent environmental movement, but always insisted that, as far as his photographs were concerned, "beauty comes first". His images are still very popular in calendars, posters, and books.</p>
<p>Realistic about development and the subsequent loss of habitat, Adams advocated for balanced growth, but was pained by the ravages of "progress". He stated, "We all know the tragedy of the dustbowls, the cruel unforgivable erosions of the soil, the depletion of fish or game, and the shrinking of the noble forests. And we know that such catastrophes shrivel the spirit of the people... The wilderness is pushed back, man is everywhere. Solitude, so vital to the individual man, is almost nowhere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985290.E2.80.93291_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985290.E2.80.93291-78">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams co-founded <a href="/wiki/Group_f/64" title="Group f/64">Group f/64</a> with other masters like <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Dyke" title="Willard Van Dyke">Willard Van Dyke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imogen_Cunningham" title="Imogen Cunningham">Imogen Cunningham</a>. With <a href="/wiki/Fred_R._Archer" title="Fred R. Archer">Fred Archer</a>, he pioneered the Zone System, a technique for translating perceived light into specific densities on negatives and paper, giving photographers better control over finished photographs. Adams also advocated the idea of <i>visualization</i> (which he often called "<a href="/wiki/Previsualization#Origins" title="Previsualization">previsualization</a>", though he later acknowledged that term to be <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(linguistics)" title="Redundancy (linguistics)">a redundancy</a>) whereby the final image is "seen" in the mind's eye before the photo is taken, toward the goal of achieving all together the aesthetic, intellectual, spiritual, and mechanical effects desired. He taught these and other techniques to thousands of amateur photographers through his publications and his workshops. His many books about photography, including the Morgan &amp; Morgan Basic Photo Series (<i>The Camera</i>, <i>The Negative</i>, <i>The Print</i>, <i>Natural Light Photography</i>, and <i>Artificial Light Photography</i>) have become classics in the field.</p>
<p>In 1966 he was elected a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">fellow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>. In 1980 Jimmy Carter awarded him the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a>, the nation's highest civilian honor.</p>
<p>Adams's photograph <i>The Tetons and the Snake River</i> was one of the 115 images recorded on the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_Golden_Record" title="Voyager Golden Record">Voyager Golden Record</a> aboard the Voyager spacecraft. These images were selected to convey information about humans, plants and animals, and geological features of the Earth to a possible alien civilization.</p>
<p>His legacy includes helping to elevate photography to an art comparable with painting and music, and equally capable of expressing emotion and beauty. He told his students, "It is easy to take a photograph, but it is harder to make a masterpiece in photography than in any other art medium."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAdams1985327_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams1985327-79">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Szarkowski" title="John Szarkowski">John Szarkowski</a> wrote "Ansel Adams attuned himself more precisely than any photographer before him to a visual understanding of the specific quality of the light that fell on a specific place at a specific moment. For Adams the natural landscape is not a fixed and solid sculpture but an insubstantial image, as transient as the light that continually redefines it. This sensibility to the specificity of light was the motive that forced Adams to develop his legendary photographic technique."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESzarkowski1976_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESzarkowski1976-80">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adams died on April 22, 1984 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive-care_unit" title="Intensive-care unit">ICU</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Community_Hospital_of_the_Monterey_Peninsula" title="Community Hospital of the Monterey Peninsula">Community Hospital of the Monterey Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Monterey,_California" title="Monterey, California">Monterey, California</a> at the age of 82 from <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>. He was survived by his wife, children Michael and Anne, and five grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlinderStillmanAdamsStegner1988396_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlinderStillmanAdamsStegner1988396-81">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Publishing rights for most of Adams's photographs are now handled by the trustees of The Ansel Adams Publishing Rights Trust. An archive of Ansel Adams's work is located at the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Creative_Photography" title="Center for Creative Photography">Center for Creative Photography</a> (CCP) at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">Tucson</a>. Numerous works by the artist have been sold at auction, including a mural-sized print of "CLEARING WINTER STORM, YOSEMITE NATIONAL PARK," which sold at Sotheby's New York in 2010 for $722,500, the highest price ever paid for an original Ansel Adams photograph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIlnytzky2010_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIlnytzky2010-82">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Szarkowski" title="John Szarkowski">John Szarkowski</a> states in the introduction to <i>Ansel Adams: Classic Images</i> (1985, p. 5), "The love that Americans poured out for the work and person of Ansel Adams during his old age, and that they have continued to express with undiminished enthusiasm since his death, is an extraordinary phenomenon, perhaps even unparalleled in our country's response to a visual artist."</p>
<p>An <b>Ansel Adams "lost negatives" controversy</b> occurred when 61 glass plates were bought for $45 at a <a href="/wiki/Garage_sale" title="Garage sale">garage sale</a>, then claimed by purchaser Nick Norsigian to be <a href="/wiki/Negative_(photography)" title="Negative (photography)">negatives</a> by Adams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[80]</a></sup> This claim was denied by Adams' grandson Matthew Adams and by William Turnage, manager of the Ansel Adams Publishing Rights Trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[81]</a></sup> The Trust filed a lawsuit against Norsigian in 2011, and Norsigian agreed in a settlement to cease making any claims associating the negatives with Ansel Adams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawsuit_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawsuit-85">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansel_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adams received a number of awards during his lifetime and posthumously, and there have been a few awards named for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnsel_Adams_Gallery_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnsel_Adams_Gallery-87">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adams received an honorary artium doctor degree from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and an honorary Doctor of Fine Arts degree from <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-88">[85]</a></sup> He was awarded the Conservation Service Award by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior</a> in 1968, a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a> in 1980, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_John_Muir_Award" title="Sierra Club John Muir Award">Sierra Club John Muir Award</a> in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008b_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008b-89">[86]</a></sup> and was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/California_Hall_of_Fame" title="California Hall of Fame">California Hall of Fame</a> by California Governor <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a> and First Lady <a href="/wiki/Maria_Shriver" title="Maria Shriver">Maria Shriver</a> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalifornia_Museum2007_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalifornia_Museum2007-90">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Minarets Wilderness in the <a href="/wiki/Inyo_National_Forest" title="Inyo National Forest">Inyo National Forest</a> and a 11,760-foot (3,580 m) peak therein were renamed the <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams_Wilderness" title="Ansel Adams Wilderness">Ansel Adams Wilderness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ansel_Adams" title="Mount Ansel Adams">Mount Ansel Adams</a> respectively in 1985.</p>
<p>Adams was presented with the <a href="/wiki/Hasselblad_Award" title="Hasselblad Award">Hasselblad Award</a> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHasselblad_Foundation1981_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHasselblad_Foundation1981-91">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams_Award_for_Conservation_Photography" title="Ansel Adams Award for Conservation Photography">Ansel Adams Award for Conservation Photography</a> was established in 1971,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008b_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESierra_Club2008b-89">[86]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams_Award_(The_Wilderness_Society)" title="Ansel Adams Award (The Wilderness Society)">Ansel Adams Award for Conservation</a> was established in 1980 by <a href="/wiki/The_Wilderness_Society_(United_States)" title="The Wilderness Society (United States)">The Wilderness Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilderness_Society_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilderness_Society-92">[89]</a></sup> The Wilderness Society also has a large permanent gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[90]</a></sup> of his work on display at its Washington DC Headquarters.</p>

<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-51"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-51">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFAlinder1996">Alinder 1996</a>, p. 192, states that the image caption for <i>Moonrise</i> in <i>U.S. Camera 1943</i> was inaccurate, citing discrepancies in several technical details.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-57"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-57">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although verbal agreement was given on September 30, 1941, the contract was actually approved on November 3 and backdated to October 14 (<a href="#CITEREFWrightArmor1988">Wright &amp; Armor 1988</a>, p. vi).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-59"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-59">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">David Elmore of the High Altitude Observatory in Boulder, Colorado, determined that <i>Moonrise</i> was taken on October 31, 1941, at 4:03 pm (<a href="#CITEREFCallahan1981">Callahan 1981</a>, pp. 30–31). Dennis di Cicco of <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_%26_Telescope" title="Sky &amp; Telescope">Sky &amp; Telescope</a></i> magazine noticed that the moon's position at the time Elmore had determined did not match the Moon's position in the image, and after an independent analysis, determined the time to be 4:49:20 pm on November 1, 1941. He reviewed his results with Elmore, who agreed with di Cicco's conclusions (<a href="#CITEREFdi.C2.A0Cicco1991">di Cicco 1991</a>, pp. 529–33).</span></li>
</ol>
