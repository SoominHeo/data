<p>The <b>Antillean ghost-faced bat</b> (<i>Mormoops blainvillei</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bat</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Mormoopidae" title="Mormoopidae">Mormoopidae</a>. It is found in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These bats range in color from a pale cinnamon to a more reddish color, showing darker pigmentation on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsal_(anatomy)" title="Dorsal (anatomy)">dorsal</a> side as opposed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">ventral</a>. No molting specimens have been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SilvaTaboada1979_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SilvaTaboada1979-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, no geographic variation in color has been studied, observed or documented.</p>
<p>Like other species in the <a href="/wiki/Mormoops" title="Mormoops">Mormoops</a> genus, the Antillean ghost-faced bat shows elaborate, intricate facial outgrowths and leaflike appendages. The face features nostrils located on a fleshy pad, and coarse bristles protruding from both lips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthony1918_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anthony1918-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_Record">Fossil Record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fossil Record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fossil record for this species is limited and sparse, but the evidence found indicates a wider range which previously extended to all of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Caribbean_islands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Caribbean islands (page does not exist)">the Caribbean islands</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koopman1957_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koopman1957-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Antillean ghost-faced bat has been predominantly observed roosting in deeper, more sheltered caves or abandoned mine shafts, as opposed to caves with multiple entrances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SilvaTaboada1979_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SilvaTaboada1979-1">[1]</a></sup> Here, it coexists with other bat species.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Analysis of stomach contents has shown that these bats feed exclusively on insects, which they catch utilizing a pouch formed by the large leaflike tail membrane. Target prey, mostly moths, are caught in midair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SilvaTaboada1979_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-SilvaTaboada1979-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activity">Activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>M. blainvilli is a strictly nocturnal species. The onset of activity varies from 22–55 minutes after sunset. This late onset has been associated with the peak activity of <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a>, which is their preferred prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SilvaTaboada1979_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-SilvaTaboada1979-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_and_Echolocation">Flight and Echolocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flight and Echolocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The flight of this bat is faster and higher than that of other bats in its genus, and a distinct humming sound has been correlated with its flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin1970_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin1970-4">[4]</a></sup> They are known to use the same biosonar vocalizations as in other echolocating bats. As the bat searches for a target (typically moths), it produces search signals with an average duration of 1.8 ms, which change from a shallow FM-sweep to a steep one. When approaching its target, M. blainvilli reduces the duration of its vocalizations. In the terminal phase, it produces a higher number of calls which are drastically shorter in duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schnitzler1991_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schnitzler1991-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mating season begins with copulation spanning from January to February. From March to May, pregnancies occur, and the earliest births occur in June. Weaning is completed from August to September, and in October to December, females are non-responsive to mating.</p>
<p>Each reproductive female typically bears one child per year, with twins being reported in only two instances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SilvaTaboada1979_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-SilvaTaboada1979-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The morphology of M. blainvilli is similar to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=M._megalophylla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="M. megalophylla (page does not exist)">M. megalophylla</a>, as the main adaptations by <a href="/wiki/Mormoops" title="Mormoops">Mormoops</a> are to reduce body weight to increase flying ability. These adaptations include decreased muscular structures, some of which are absent altogether as compared to species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phyllostomidae" title="Phyllostomidae">Phyllostomidae</a>. These restrictions result in a decreased range of extension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughn1970_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaughn1970-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a> of the wings of one bat were calculated as 6.23, similar to M. megalophylla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith1979_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith1979-7">[7]</a></sup> These characteristics are hypothesized to give the bat an advantage in terms of flight endurance, and have been adapted for its insectivorous diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughn1970_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vaughn1970-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoregulation">Thermoregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antillean_ghost-faced_bat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Thermoregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The thermoneutral zone for M. blainvilli extends from 31–34 °C, and body temperature is maintained between these two temperatures over a wide range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambient_temperature" title="Ambient temperature">ambient temperature</a> (15–41 °C). However, as ambient temperature decreases below 28 °C, the maintenance of body temperature in individuals is less stable and has been observed in some to decrease below 25 °C. In these low temperatures, bats respond by elevating their <a href="/wiki/Basal_metabolic_rate" title="Basal metabolic rate">basal metabolic rate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodriguez-Duran1995_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodriguez-Duran1995-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
