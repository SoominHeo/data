<p><b>Alameda</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AL</span></span></b>-ə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">MEE</span></span></b>-də</i></a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[ala'meða]</a></span>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Alameda_County,_California" title="Alameda County, California">Alameda County</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is located on <a href="/wiki/Alameda_(island)" title="Alameda (island)">Alameda Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bay_Farm_Island,_Alameda,_California" title="Bay Farm Island, Alameda, California">Bay Farm Island</a>, and is adjacent to and south of <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a> and east of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> across the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>. Bay Farm Island, a portion of which is also known as "Harbor Bay Isle", is not actually an island, and is part of the mainland adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_International_Airport" title="Oakland International Airport">Oakland International Airport</a>. The city's estimated 2016 population was 79,277.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-E1-9">[9]</a></sup> Alameda is a <a href="/wiki/Charter_city" title="Charter city">charter city</a>, rather than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_law_city" title="General law city">general law city</a>, allowing the city to provide for any form of government. Alameda became a charter city and adopted a <a href="/wiki/Council%E2%80%93manager_government" title="Council–manager government">council–manager government</a> in 1916, which it retains to the present.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">

<p>The island Alameda occupies what was originally a <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsula</a> connected to Oakland. Much of it was low-lying and marshy, but on higher ground than the peninsula and adjacent parts of what is now <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Oakland" title="Downtown Oakland">downtown Oakland</a> were home to one of the largest coastal oak forests in the world. The area was therefore called <i>Encinal</i>, Spanish for "forest of evergreen oak".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>Alameda</i> is Spanish for "grove of poplar trees" or "tree-lined avenue",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and was chosen in 1853 by popular vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The inhabitants at the time of the arrival of the Spanish in the late 18th century were a local band of the <a href="/wiki/Ohlone" title="Ohlone">Ohlone</a> tribe. The peninsula became part of the vast <a href="/wiki/Rancho_San_Antonio_(Peralta)" title="Rancho San Antonio (Peralta)">Rancho San Antonio</a> granted in 1820 to Luis Peralta by the Spanish king who claimed California. The grant was later confirmed by the new <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Republic of Mexico</a> upon its independence from Spain.</p>
<p>Over time, the place became known as Bolsa de Encinal or Encinal de San Antonio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was founded on June 6, 1853, and the town originally contained three small settlements. "Alameda" referred to the village at Encinal and High Streets, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hibbardsville,_Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hibbardsville, Alameda, California (page does not exist)">Hibbardsville</a> was at the North Shore ferry and shipping terminal, and <a href="/wiki/Woodstock,_Alameda,_California" title="Woodstock, Alameda, California">Woodstock</a> was on the west near the ferry piers of the <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Coast_Railroad" title="South Pacific Coast Railroad">South Pacific Coast Railroad</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Railroad" title="Central Pacific Railroad">Central Pacific</a>. Eventually, the Central Pacific's ferry pier became the <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Mole" title="Alameda Mole">Alameda Mole</a>, featuring transit connections between San Francisco ferries, local trollies and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Railroad" title="Southern Pacific Railroad">Southern Pacific</a> (formerly Central Pacific) commuter lines.</p>
<p>The first post office opened in 1854.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup> The first school, Schmermerhorn School, was opened in 1855 (and eventually became Lincoln School), Encinal School was opened in 1860 (and closed in 1980). The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_and_Alameda_Railroad" title="San Francisco and Alameda Railroad">San Francisco and Alameda Railroad</a> opened the Encinal station in 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup> The Encinal area was also known as Fasskings Station in honor of Frederick Louis Fassking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup> Encinal's own post office opened in 1876, was renamed West End in 1877, and closed in 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup> The West End area was originally called Bowman's Point in honor of Charles G. Bowman, an early settler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Terminal" title="Alameda Terminal">Alameda Terminal</a> was the site of the arrival of the first train via the <a href="/wiki/First_Transcontinental_Railroad" title="First Transcontinental Railroad">First Transcontinental Railroad</a> into the San Francisco Bay Area on September 6, 1869. The transcontinental terminus was switched to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland_Mole" title="Oakland Mole">Oakland Mole</a> two months later, on November 8, 1869.</p>
<p>The borders of Alameda were made coextensive with the island in 1872, incorporating Woodstock into Alameda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGN_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-CGN-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> described Alameda as being "The Garden of California." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1917, an attraction called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neptune_Beach,_California" title="Neptune Beach, California">Neptune Beach</a> was built in the area now known as Crab Cove. Often compared to <a href="/wiki/Coney_Island" title="Coney Island">Coney Island</a>, the park was a major attraction in the 1920s and 1930s. The original owners of the facility, the Strehlow family, partnered with a local confectioner to create tastes unique to Neptune Beach. Both the American <a href="/wiki/Snow_cone" title="Snow cone">snow cone</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popsicle" title="Popsicle">popsicle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> were first sold at Neptune Beach. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kewpie_doll_(toy)" title="Kewpie doll (toy)">Kewpie doll</a>, hand-painted and dressed in unique hand-sewn dresses, became the original prize for winning games at the beach – another Neptune Beach invention.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Strehlows owned and operated the beach on their own, even filling in a section of the bay to add an additional Olympic-size swimming pool and an exceptional roller coaster which must have given riders a tremendous view of the bay. The <i>Cottage Baths</i> were available for rent.</p>
<p>Neptune Beach's two huge outdoor pools hosted swimming races and exhibitions by such famous swimmers as Olympian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johnny_Weismuller" title="Johnny Weismuller">Johnny Weismuller</a>, who later starred as the original Tarzan, and <a href="/wiki/Jack_LaLanne" title="Jack LaLanne">Jack LaLanne</a>, who started a chain of health clubs. The park closed down in 1939 because of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the completion of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco%E2%80%93Oakland_Bay_Bridge" title="San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge">San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge</a>, people circumventing paying the admission price, and the rise of car culture. Once the Bay Bridge was complete, the rail lines, which ran right past the entrance to Neptune Beach on the way to the Alameda Mole and the Ferry, lost riders in droves. People began using their cars to escape the city and the immediate suburbs like Alameda and traveling further afield in California. Alameda lost its resort status as more distant locations became more attractive to cash-rich San Francisco tourists. Youngsters in town became aware of ways to avoid paying the dime for admission to the park. Strong swimmers or even waders could sneak in on the bay side just by swimming around the fence.</p>
<p>Some of the resort homes and buildings from the Neptune beach era still exist in present-day Alameda. The <a href="/wiki/Croll_Building" title="Croll Building">Croll Building</a>, on the corner of Webster Street and Central Avenue, was the site of Croll's Gardens and Hotel, famous as training quarters for some of the greatest fighters in boxing history from 1883 to 1914. James J. Corbett, Bob Fitzsimmons, Jim Jefferies, Jack Johnson, and many other champions all stayed and trained here. Today this beautiful preserved building is home to Croll's Pizza and the 1400 Bar &amp; Grill Restaurant. Neptune Court, just a block away on the corner of Central Ave. and McKay Ave., provides another glimpse of what resort life was like in Alameda in the 1920s. A short walk near Crab Cove will reveal many more historic gems.</p>
<p>The vast majority of the Neptune Beach structures – the hand-carved carousel from the world-famed Dentzel Company, the <a href="/wiki/Ferris_wheel" title="Ferris wheel">Ferris wheel</a>, the roller coaster, and other rides – were auctioned off in 1940 for mere pennies on the dollar of their original cost. Today,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> A consequence of the Neptune Beach closing around 1940 was a total dearth of quality, clean swimming facilities in town. A grass roots effort to create swimming pools at two high schools and two city parks would continue into the early 1960s.</p>
<p>When the railroad came to town in the 1860s Park Street developed into the major thoroughfare of the city and the location of the main Alameda train station, residents of Old Alameda pulled up stakes and moved across town to the new downtown. The street's location was chosen by two landowners who wished to attract tenants and development to their land. As a result, they designated their mutual property line as Park Street.</p>
<p>In 1902, the need for expanded shipping facilities led to the dredging of a canal through the marshland between Oakland and Alameda, turning Alameda into an island. Most of the soil from the canal was used to fill in nearby marshland. The area of Alameda called Bay Farm Island is no longer an island, but is attached by fill to Oakland. In his youth, author <a href="/wiki/Jack_London" title="Jack London">Jack London</a> was known to take part in oyster pirating in the highly productive oyster beds near Bay Farm Island, today long gone. The <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Works_Shipyard" title="Alameda Works Shipyard">Alameda Works Shipyard</a> was one of the largest and best equipped shipyards in the country. In the 1950s, Alameda's industrial and ship building industries thrived along the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Estuary" title="Oakland Estuary">Alameda Estuary</a>, where the world's first-ever, land-based, containerized shipping crane was used. Today,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Oakland" title="Port of Oakland">Port of Oakland</a> across the estuary serves as one of the largest ports on the West Coast, using the shipping technologies originally experimented with in Alameda. As of March 21, 2006, Alameda is a "Coast Guard City", one of seven in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the regular trains running to the Alameda Mole, Alameda was also served by local steam commuter lines of the Southern Pacific (initially, the Central Pacific) which were later transformed into the <a href="/wiki/East_Bay_Electric_Lines" title="East Bay Electric Lines">East Bay Electric Lines</a>. Southern Pacific's electrified trains were not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streetcar" title="Streetcar">streetcars</a>, but full-sized railroad cars which connected to the mainland by bridges at Webster Street and Fruitvale (only the latter bridge survives today). The trains ran to both the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Long_Wharf" title="Oakland Long Wharf">Oakland Mole</a> and the Alameda Mole. In fact, one line which ran between the two moles was dubbed the "Horseshoe Line" for the shape of the route on a map. Soon after the completion of the Bay Bridge, Alameda trains ran directly to San Francisco on the lower deck of the bridge, the ferries having been rendered unnecessary. Alameda was the site of the Southern Pacific's West Alameda Shops where all the electric trains were maintained and repaired.</p>
<p>In the 1930s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_American_Airways" title="Pan American Airways">Pan American Airways</a> established a <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">seaplane</a> port along the fill that led to the Alameda Mole. This was the original home base for the famous <a href="/wiki/China_Clipper" title="China Clipper">China Clipper</a> flying boat. In 1929, the University of California established the San Francisco Airdrome located near the current Webster Street tube as a public airport. The Bay Airdrome had its gala christening party in 1930. The airfield was a busy place, as an early home base for Coastal Air Freight, Varney Air Lines, West Coast Air Transport, Western Air Express, the transbay Air Ferries, and Boeing's Pacific Air Transport. The Airdrome was closed in 1941 when its air traffic interfered with the newly built <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Alameda" title="Naval Air Station Alameda">Naval Air Station Alameda</a> (NAS Alameda).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> With the advent of World War II, a vast stretch of the marshy area southwest of the Alameda Mole was filled and the NAS Alameda established. This major Naval facility included a large airfield, as well as docks for several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_carriers" title="Aircraft carriers">aircraft carriers</a>. It closed in 1997.</p>
<p>In the late 1950s the Utah Construction Company began a landfill beyond the <i>Old Sea Wall</i> and created <i>South Shore</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1973_air_disaster">1973 air disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1973 air disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 7, 1973, a USN <a href="/wiki/Ling-Temco-Vought" title="Ling-Temco-Vought">Vought</a> <a href="/wiki/LTV_A-7_Corsair_II" title="LTV A-7 Corsair II">A-7E Corsair II</a> fighter jet on a routine training mission from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lemoore_Naval_Air_Station" title="Lemoore Naval Air Station">Lemoore Naval Air Station</a>, suddenly caught fire, 28,000 feet over the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> and crashed into the Tahoe Apartments in Alameda. Eleven people, including pilot Lieutenant Robert Lee Ward died in the crash and fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1324" data-file-width="1799" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg/220px-Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg/330px-Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg/440px-Oakland_and_Alameda.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Aerial view of Alameda Island (center), with Oakland in the foreground and San Francisco behind</div>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 23.0 square miles (60 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 10.6 square miles (27 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 12.3 square miles (32 km<sup>2</sup>) (53.79%) is water.</p>
<p>Although Alameda's nickname is "The Island City" (or simply "the island"), the current city occupies two islands as well as a small section of the mainland. Today, the city consists of the main original section, with the former <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Alameda" title="Naval Air Station Alameda">Naval Air Station Alameda</a> (NAS Alameda) at the west end of Alameda Island, <a href="/wiki/South_Shore,_Alameda,_California" title="South Shore, Alameda, California">Southshore</a> along the southern side of Alameda Island, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_Farm_Island" title="Bay Farm Island">Bay Farm Island</a>, which is part of the mainland proper. The area of the former NAS is now known as "Alameda Point." The Southshore area is separated from the main part of Alameda Island by a lagoon; the north shore of the lagoon is located approximately where the original south shore of the island was. Alameda Point and Southshore are built on bay fill.</p>
<p>Not all of Alameda Island is part of the City of Alameda. Although nearly all of the island is in Alameda city limits, a small portion of a dump site west of the former runways at Alameda Point extends far enough into San Francisco Bay that it is over the county line and part of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">City and County of San Francisco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Island" title="Coast Guard Island">Coast Guard Island</a>, a small island between Alameda Island and Oakland, is also part of Alameda and is the home of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Support_Command_Alameda" title="Integrated Support Command Alameda">Integrated Support Command Alameda</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This region experiences warm (but not hot) and dry summers, with no average monthly temperatures above 71.6 °F. According to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> system, Alameda has a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate#Warm-summer_Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">warm-summer Mediterranean climate</a>, abbreviated "Csb" on climate maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Annual precipitation is about 20 inches, all rain (snow is extremely rare at sea level in the San Francisco Bay area).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> reported that Alameda had a population of 73,812. The population density was 3,214.9 people per square mile (1,241.3/km²). The racial makeup of Alameda was 37,460 (50.8%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 23,058 (31.2%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 4,759 (6.4%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 426 (0.6%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 381 (0.5%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 2,463 (3.3%) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 5,265 (7.1%) from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 8,092 persons (11.0%).</p>
<p>The Census reported that 72,316 people (98.0% of the population) lived in households, 857 (1.2%) lived in non-institutionalized group quarters, and 639 (0.9%) were institutionalized.</p>
<p>There were 30,123 households, out of which 9,144 (30.4%) had children under the age of 18 living in them, 13,440 (44.6%) were opposite-sex married couples living together, 3,623 (12.0%) had a female householder with no husband present, 1,228 (4.1%) had a male householder with no wife present. There were 1,681 (5.6%) <a href="/wiki/POSSLQ" title="POSSLQ">unmarried opposite-sex partnerships</a>, and 459 (1.5%) same-sex married couples or same-sex partnerships. 9,347 households (31.0%) were made up of individuals and 2,874 (9.5%) had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.40. There were 18,291 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_(U.S._Census)" title="Family (U.S. Census)">families</a> (60.7% of all households); the average family size was 3.06.</p>
<p>The age distribution of the population shows 15,304 people (20.7%) under the age of 18, 5,489 people (7.4%) aged 18 to 24, 21,000 people (28.5%) aged 25 to 44, 22,044 people (29.9%) aged 45 to 64, and 9,975 people (13.5%) who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 40.7 years. For every 100 females there were 91.7 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 88.5 males.</p>
<p>Per capita money income in past 12 months (2013 dollars), 2009 – 2013 was $41,340.00 per US Census. Median household income, 2009 – 2013 was $74,606.00 per US Census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were 32,351 housing units at an average density of 1,409.0 per square mile (544.0/km²), of which 14,488 (48.1%) were owner-occupied, and 15,635 (51.9%) were occupied by renters. The homeowner vacancy rate was 1.1%; the rental vacancy rate was 5.7%. 37,042 people (50.2% of the population) lived in owner-occupied housing units and 35,274 people (47.8%) lived in rental housing units.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000">2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the census<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-30">[30]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 72,259 people, 30,226 households, and 17,863 families residing in the city. The population density was 2,583.3/km² (6,693.4/mi²). There were 31,644 housing units at an average density of 1,131.3/km² (2,931.2/mi²). The racial makeup of the city was 56.95% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 6.21% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 0.67% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 26.15% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.60% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 3.29% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 6.13% from two or more races. 9.31% of the population were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.</p>
<p>There were 30,226 households out of which 27.7% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 43.7% were married couples living together, 11.4% had a female householder with no husband present, and 40.9% were non-families. 32.2% of all households were made up of individuals and 9.4% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.35 and the average family size was 3.04.</p>
<p>In the city the age distribution of the population shows 21.5% under the age of 18, 7.0% from 18 to 24, 33.6% from 25 to 44, 24.6% from 45 to 64, and 13.3% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 38 years. For every 100 females there were 92.3 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 89.5 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $56,285, and the median income for a family was $68,625. Males had a median income of $49,174 versus $40,165 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $30,982. About 6.0% of families and 8.2% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 11.4% of those under age 18 and 6.1% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<p>There is a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_American" title="Filipino American">Filipino</a> community; and also a major <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> community, from which <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a>' mother came and where <a href="/wiki/Lyndsy_Fonseca" title="Lyndsy Fonseca">Lyndsy Fonseca</a> was raised for some time. Alameda also has a historic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_American" title="Japanese American">Japanese American</a> community and had a small Japanese business district on a portion of Park Street prior to World War II, when the city's Japanese population was interned. A Japanese Buddhist church is one of the few remaining buildings left of Alameda's pre-war Japanese American community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like much of the heavily <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> Alameda County, the City of Alameda is considered a <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> city. In addition to voting solidly for Democratic candidates in statewide elections, voters in the city have approved a number of progressive ballot initiatives.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vehicle access to Alameda Island is via three bridges from Oakland (<a href="/wiki/Park_Street_Bridge" title="Park Street Bridge">Park Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruitvale_Bridge" title="Fruitvale Bridge">Fruitvale Avenue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/High_Street_Bridge" title="High Street Bridge">High Street Bridges</a>), as well as the two one-way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posey_and_Webster_Street_Tubes" title="Posey and Webster Street Tubes">Posey and Webster Street Tubes</a> leading into <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Oakland" title="Chinatown, Oakland">Oakland's Chinatown</a>. Connections from Alameda to Bay Farm Island is provided via the Bay Farm Island Bridge for vehicular traffic as well as the Bay Farm Island Bicycle Bridge (the only pedestrian/bicycle-only drawbridge in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_61" title="California State Route 61">California State Route 61</a> runs down city streets from the Posey and Webster Street Tubes, across the Bay Farm Island Bridge, and south to the Oakland Airport.</p>
<p>Public transportation includes the <a href="/wiki/AC_Transit" title="AC Transit">AC Transit</a> buses (which include express buses to San Francisco) and two ferry services — the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alameda/Oakland_Ferry" title="Alameda/Oakland Ferry">Alameda/Oakland Ferry</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alameda_Harbor_Bay_Ferry" title="Alameda Harbor Bay Ferry">Alameda Harbor Bay Ferry</a>. <a href="/wiki/AC_Transit" title="AC Transit">AC Transit</a> buses also cover 3 bus times in the morning and afternoon to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Middle_School_(Alameda,_California)" title="Lincoln Middle School (Alameda, California)">Lincoln Middle School</a>, located in Alameda. Both ferry services may soon be transferred to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area_Water_Emergency_Transportation_Authority" title="San Francisco Bay Area Water Emergency Transportation Authority">Water Transit Authority</a>. The closest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BART" title="BART">BART</a> stations are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Merritt_(BART_station)" title="Lake Merritt (BART station)">Lake Merritt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12th_Street_Oakland_City_Center_(BART_station)" title="12th Street Oakland City Center (BART station)">12th Street</a>, near the exit to the Posey Tube, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruitvale_(BART_station)" title="Fruitvale (BART station)">Fruitvale</a>, near the Fruitvale Bridge.</p>
<p>Even though the island is just minutes off <a href="/wiki/Interstate_880" title="Interstate 880">Interstate 880</a> in Oakland, the speed limit for the city is 25 mph (40 km/h) on almost every road. Many unaware drivers fail to slow down after exiting the highway. Groups like Pedestrian Friendly Alameda and BikeAlameda advocate stronger enforcement of speeding laws.</p>
<p>Alameda has also featured prominently on automotive <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jalopnik" title="Jalopnik">Jalopnik</a>, with their "Down on The Street" segment consisting of cars found on the streets of Alameda. Jalopnik has nicknamed it "The Island That Rust Forgot".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vichouse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Vichouse.jpg/220px-Vichouse.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Vichouse.jpg/330px-Vichouse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Vichouse.jpg/440px-Vichouse.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vichouse.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Victorian house in Alameda</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to its proximity to the Bay, <a href="/wiki/Windsurfing" title="Windsurfing">wind surfers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_surfing" title="Kite surfing">kite surfers</a> can often be seen at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_W._Crown_Memorial_State_Beach" title="Robert W. Crown Memorial State Beach">Robert W. Crown Memorial State Beach</a>. From the beach there are also views of the San Francisco skyline and the San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> <i><a href="/wiki/USS_Hornet_(CV-12)" title="USS Hornet (CV-12)">USS <i>Hornet</i></a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Museum_ship" title="Museum ship">museum ship</a>, has been moored at the former Naval Air Station as the <a href="/wiki/USS_Hornet_Museum" title="USS Hornet Museum">USS <i>Hornet</i> Museum</a> since 1998. This ship was originally named the <i>USS Kearsarge</i>, but was renamed in honor of the previous Hornet CV-8 (famous for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doolittle_raid" title="Doolittle raid">Doolittle raid</a>), which was lost in October 1942.</p>
<p>Alameda is known for its <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> houses; 9% of all single-family houses (1500) in Alameda are Victorian, and many more have been divided into two to four-unit dwellings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It is said that Alameda has more pre-1906 earthquake era homes in the Gold Coast section than any other city in the Bay Area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Alameda is home to the official offices and training facility of the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Raiders" title="Oakland Raiders">Oakland Raiders</a> <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> team, which is located on Bay Farm Island. The facility is also home to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Raider_Image&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Raider Image (page does not exist)">The Raider Image</a>, the merchandise arm of the franchise, which the public can visit.</p>
<p>At the turn of the 19th century, the city of Alameda took a large chunk of Charles Froling's land away to build a street. Froling had planned to build his dream house on the plot of land he received through inheritance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neighbor_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-neighbor-35">[35]</a></sup> To spite the city and an unsympathetic neighbor, Froling built a house 10 feet (3.0 m) wide, 54 feet (16 m) long and 20 feet (6.1 m) high on the tiny strip of land left to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neighbor_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-neighbor-35">[35]</a></sup> The Froling <a href="/wiki/Spite_house" title="Spite house">spite house</a> is still standing and occupied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neighbor_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-neighbor-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alameda is also known for its Fourth of July parade, which is advertised as the second oldest and second longest Fourth of July parade in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It features homemade floats, classic cars, motorized living room furniture, fire-breathing dragons, marching bands, and large crowds. The parade route is about 3 miles (5 km) long.</p>
<p>The Historic Park Street Business District is known for its many buildings that date back to the 1800s and is a designated Historic Commercial District on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register</a>. This main thoroughfare of downtown Alameda Is filled with local shops, restaurants, drinking establishments, and services. The renovated 1932 Alameda Theatre &amp; Cineplex is the cultural centerpiece of the commercial district. In addition, popular attractions include High Scores Arcade Museum (a retro video game arcade) and Subpar Miniature Golf (an indoor miniature golf complex that features Bay Area landmarks such as the Golden Gate Bridge and Coit Tower at each hole).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_development">Economic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After two previous failures, voters in the city passed a ballot measure in 2000 authorizing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_measure" title="Bond measure">bond measure</a> for construction of a new library to replace the city's <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_library" title="Carnegie library">Carnegie library</a>, damaged during the <a href="/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake" title="1989 Loma Prieta earthquake">1989 Loma Prieta earthquake</a>. The city also received state funds for the new library and opened the doors to the new facility in November 2006.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alameda_Point">Alameda Point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alameda Point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Alameda" title="Naval Air Station Alameda">Naval Air Station Alameda</a> (NAS), at <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Point,_Alameda,_California" title="Alameda Point, Alameda, California">Alameda Point</a>, was decommissioned in 1997, and is in process of being turned over to the City of Alameda for civilian development. The area of the former NAS is now known as Alameda Point. In late July 2006, the City of Alameda announced a deal with the Navy that would turn the land over to the city for $108M. The transfer process was initially slowed down by disputes between the Navy and the city regarding payment for environmental cleanup of the land.</p>
<p>In September 2010 the US Veterans Administration proposed construction of a $209 million state-of-the-art facility at Alameda Point that would provide primary care, specialty care, and mental health, substance abuse and other services. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://alamedapointva.blogspot.com" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> The VA received Congressional $17.33 million in budget authority for the project in 2011. But concerns over the proximity to a nesting site for an endangered bird, the <a href="/wiki/California_least_tern" title="California least tern">California least tern</a>, have led to delays in moving the project forward. The VA's 2012 and 2013 budget requests to Congress contain no funding requests for Alameda Point. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://alamedapointva.blogspot.com" rel="nofollow">[3]</a></p>
<p>In September 2011 Alameda and the Navy reached an agreement on the terms of a no-cost conveyance for the entire 918 acres at Alameda Point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="America.27s_Cup">America's Cup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: America's Cup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 33rd <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Cup" title="America's Cup">America's Cup</a> Race was won by Golden Gate Yacht Club racing team BMW Oracle, founded by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Ellison" title="Larry Ellison">Larry Ellison</a>. One possible use of the air station would be an alternate or partnered site with San Francisco for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/34th_America%27s_Cup" title="34th America's Cup">34th America's Cup</a>. Within 2 weeks of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Yacht_Club" title="Golden Gate Yacht Club">Golden Gate Yacht Club</a> winning the America's Cup, <a class="external text" href="http://alamedawaterfront.com/alameda-locals-and-city-start-americas-cup-campaign" rel="nofollow">Alameda city council with local support</a> sent a unanimous letter of support to hold AC 34 in San Francisco Bay Area. In early 2011, the City Council created an ad hoc America's Cup Citizens Advisory Committee to look for ways that Alameda could draw interest from teams and potential spectators. Through those efforts, in mid-2012, the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Artemis_Racing" title="Artemis Racing">Artemis Racing</a> team announced that they would create their team base in one of the former air station hangars on Alameda Point.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine_and_spirits_production">Wine and spirits production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Wine and spirits production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rosenblum Cellars Winery, Rock Wall Winery, and <a href="/wiki/St._George_Spirits" title="St. George Spirits">St. George Spirits</a> are located at Alameda Point. In 1978, Alameda veterinarian Kent Rosenblum and his wife Kathy founded Rosenblum Cellars. In 2008, the company was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Diageo" title="Diageo">Diageo</a> Estates. Shauna Rosenblum, daughter of Kent and Kathy, is the wine maker for Rock Wall Winery. In December, 2007, St. George Absinthe Verte, produced by St. George Spirits became the first brand of American-made <a href="/wiki/Absinthe" title="Absinthe">absinthe</a> to be legally produced in the United States since a ban was enacted in 1912.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theaters">Theaters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Theaters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The city restored the historic <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> city landmark <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alameda_Theatre" title="Alameda Theatre">Alameda Theatre</a>, expanding it to include a theater multiplex. The public opening was May 21, 2008.</p>
<p>The South Shore Mall Twin Cinema opened in 1969 and served as a prominent theater on the island until its closure in 1998. In 2002, the building was demolished and its former site is now a parking lot.</p>
<p>Alameda also hosts the Altarena Playhouse, which since 1957 has been home to the Bay Area's oldest continuously operating community theater organization.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_newspapers_and_magazines">Local newspapers and magazines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Local newspapers and magazines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alameda's first newspaper, the <i>Encinal</i>, appeared in the early 1850s and the paper's editor was instrumental in the movement to incorporate the city. Following the <i>Encinal</i>, several other papers appeared along geographic lines, and the <i>Daily Argus</i> eventually rose to prominence. A young Alameda native, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_R._Knowland" title="Joseph R. Knowland">Joseph R. Knowland</a>, wrote political and historical articles for the Alameda papers. Later, Knowland owned the powerful <i><a href="/wiki/Oakland_Tribune" title="Oakland Tribune">Oakland Tribune</a></i>. Around 1900, the <i>Daily Argus</i> began to fade in importance and east and west papers <i>The Times</i> and <i>The Star</i> combined to take the leading role as the <i><a href="/wiki/Alameda_Times-Star" title="Alameda Times-Star">Alameda Times-Star</a></i> in the 1930s. The <i>Times-Star</i> was sold to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alameda_Newspaper_Group" title="Alameda Newspaper Group">Alameda Newspaper Group</a> in the 1970s.</p>
<p>In 1997, the Hills Newspaper chain was bought by <a href="/wiki/Knight_Ridder" title="Knight Ridder">Knight Ridder</a>, at the time, the second-largest newspaper chain in the U.S. Following the buyout, former Hills Newspapers employees recognized the lack of a local community voice in Alameda, and again formed a new locally based newspaper, the <i>Alameda Sun</i>, in 2001. In 2006, Knight Ridder announced its impending sale to <a href="/wiki/The_McClatchy_Company" title="The McClatchy Company">McClatchy Corp.</a>, a Sacramento-based publishing firm. McClatchy Corp. has put the <i>Contra Costa Times</i>, which under the Knight Ridder reorganization included all five of the original Hills Newspapers, up for sale. The current owners of the <i>Alameda Times-Star</i>, <a href="/wiki/MediaNews_Group" title="MediaNews Group">MediaNews, Inc.</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, have announced a strong interest in buying both the <i>Contra Costa Times</i> chain and the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Jose_Mercury_News" title="San Jose Mercury News">San Jose Mercury News</a></i>, consolidating the daily newspaper market of the East Bay, effectively under one owner. MediaNews closed the <i>Times-Star</i> in 2011.</p>
<p>The Alameda community is currently served by two weekly newspapers, the Alameda Journal, owned by the MediaNews Group, and the Alameda Sun, along with a news website, <a class="external text" href="http://www.thealamedan.org" rel="nofollow">The Alamedan</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alameda_Municipal_Power">Alameda Municipal Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Alameda Municipal Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike surrounding communities, Alameda has a municipal power service, <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Municipal_Power" title="Alameda Municipal Power">Alameda Municipal Power</a> (AMP), that delivers services directly to consumers. AMP sold the majority of its telecommunications business to <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a> in 2008 but continues to provide telecommunication service at Alameda Point.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/California_electricity_crisis" title="California electricity crisis">California electricity crisis</a> of 2000 and 2001, Alameda Municipal Power did not raise electricity rates, while residents in most of the state endured significant price increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films_shot_in_Alameda">Films shot in Alameda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Films shot in Alameda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alameda has been home to many movie sets. Some of the movies filmed on the island have included <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicentennial_Man" title="Bicentennial Man">Bicentennial Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Net_(1995_film)" title="The Net (1995 film)">The Net</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix_Reloaded" title="The Matrix Reloaded">The Matrix Reloaded</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Matrix:_Revolutions" title="The Matrix: Revolutions">The Matrix: Revolutions</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bee_Season" title="Bee Season">Bee Season</a></i>, the original 1968 <i>Your, Mine and Ours</i> and the movie musical <i><a href="/wiki/Rent_(film)" title="Rent (film)">Rent</a></i>. Parts of <a href="/wiki/Alameda_High_School" title="Alameda High School">Alameda High School</a> were animated for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animatrix" title="Animatrix">Animatrix</a></i> episode "Kid's Story". A massive hangar at the former Naval Air Station Alameda was used to film special scenes requiring <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated imagery</a> for movies such as <i>Bicentennial Man</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Flubber_(film)" title="Flubber (film)">Flubber</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/What_Dreams_May_Come_(film)" title="What Dreams May Come (film)">What Dreams May Come</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mission:_Impossible_II" title="Mission: Impossible II">Mission: Impossible II</a></i> and many scenes from the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">Matrix</a></i> trilogy, including the signature <a href="/wiki/Bullet_time" title="Bullet time">bullet time</a> scene. The open space of the decommissioned naval base often hosts <i><a href="/wiki/MythBusters" title="MythBusters">MythBusters</a>'</i> more dangerous experiments. The movie "Spirit Of '76" was filmed all throughout Alameda.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/USS_Hornet_Museum" title="USS Hornet Museum">USS Hornet Museum</a>, permanently moored at <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Point,_Alameda,_California" title="Alameda Point, Alameda, California">Alameda Point</a>, has been the site for scenes used in major theatrical releases: <i><a href="/wiki/XXX:_State_of_the_Union" title="XXX: State of the Union">XXX: State of the Union</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rescue_Dawn" title="Rescue Dawn">Rescue Dawn</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Master_(2012_film)" title="The Master (2012 film)">The Master</a></i>. In addition, the aircraft carrier has been used for television shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/JAG_(TV_series)" title="JAG (TV series)">JAG</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carrier_(documentary)" title="Carrier (documentary)">Carrier</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Looking" title="Looking">Looking</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Escape_(U.S._TV_series)" title="The Great Escape (U.S. TV series)">The Great Escape</a></i>, and the special military episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Fear_Factor" title="Fear Factor">Fear Factor</a></i>; plus a number of television commercials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Altarena_Playhouse">The Altarena Playhouse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: The Altarena Playhouse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Altarena Playhouse, which performs comedies, dramas and musicals, was founded in 1938 and is the longest continuously operating community theater in the San Francisco Bay Area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhythmix_Cultural_Works">Rhythmix Cultural Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Rhythmix Cultural Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhythmix Cultural Works (RCW) brings people of all ages together to experience and explore music, dance, visual art and educational opportunities. RCW's programs are housed in a restored industrial waterfront building that speaks to Alameda’s history while creating an inclusive venue that reflects the Bay Area’s rich diversity. The organization offers an array of arts programming to the community including Island Arts, Island City Waterways, Round the World Festival and its Performance Art and Learning program (PAL) that serves more than 2000 youth with free cultural arts assemblies each year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals_on_Webster_Street">Festivals on Webster Street</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Festivals on Webster Street">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Webster Street in Alameda has long been the host of many arts, crafts and holiday festivals. During some of these festivals, the Chamber of Commerce along with the West Alameda Business Association (WABA) will block of a portion of Webster St. for the entertainment of festival goers. Festivals such as The JAM at Neptune Beach formerly known as the <a class="external text" href="http://westalamedabusiness.com/events/" rel="nofollow">Peanut Butter Jam Festival</a> brings a lot of local and outside visitors. Other event on the "West-End" include Trick-or-Treat on Webster Street where merchants supply goodies for local children and culminates with a parade and costume contest; in December "Santa Claus Meet-n-Greet on Webster Street" happens with elves, and a photo with the big guy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals_on_Park_Street">Festivals on Park Street</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Festivals on Park Street">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three major events when the street in Alameda's historic downtown district is closed to vehicular traffic. The <i>Park Street Spring Festival</i> takes place every May during the weekend of Mother's Day and attracts over 50,000 visitors. The <i>Park Street Art &amp; Wine Faire</i> takes place the last weekend of every July and attracts over 100,000 visitors. Both street fairs feature over 150 arts &amp; crafts vendors, food vendors, beer and wine pouring, a children's area, and two stages with regional entertainment. The <i>Park Street Classic Car Show</i> is held on the second Saturday every October and displays over 400 vintage vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Unified_School_District" title="Alameda Unified School District">Alameda Unified School District</a></div>
<p>Public primary and secondary education in Alameda is the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Alameda_Unified_School_District" title="Alameda Unified School District">Alameda Unified School District</a>, which is legally separate from the City government (as is common throughout California). The <a href="/wiki/College_of_Alameda" title="College of Alameda">College of Alameda</a>, a two-year <a href="/wiki/Community_college" title="Community college">community college</a> in the West End is part of the <a href="/wiki/Peralta_Community_College_District" title="Peralta Community College District">Peralta Community College District</a>. The city has numerous private primary schools, and one private high school, <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph_Notre_Dame_High_School" title="St. Joseph Notre Dame High School">St. Joseph Notre Dame High School</a>, a Catholic school.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alameda,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September, 2013, a Tibetan rights group initiated a social media and e-mail campaign <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> targeting the Mayor of Alameda, complaining that City of Alameda's participation in, and association with, a flag-raising ceremony to recognize <a href="/wiki/National_Day_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="National Day of the People's Republic of China">National Day of the People's Republic of China</a> on October 1 was tantamount to endorsing the communist regime in China, its human rights abuses, and the occupation of <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>. The City of Alameda responded that the ceremony was a function of the Alameda Sister City Association and the Alameda Wuxi Friendship Committee, not a function of the City of Alameda. The Tibetan rights group responded that on September 26, the City of Alameda Social Service and Human Relations board appointed a member, Michael Robles-Wong, as a representative to the Sister City Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 1, 2013, the Tibetan rights groupsTibetTruth and Bay Area Friends of Tibet sent roughly 75 protesters to Alameda City Hall to protest the ceremony, which organizers ultimately canceled before it began. Former City of Alameda Councilmember Frank Matarrese announced the cancellation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Then-city councilmember <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stewart_Chen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stewart Chen (page does not exist)">Stewart Chen</a> subsequently defended the ceremony, as a diplomatic, not political, exercise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
