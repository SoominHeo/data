<p><b>Alan Jay Heeger</b> (born January 22, 1936) is an American <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic" title="Academic">academic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_laureate" title="Nobel Prize laureate">Nobel Prize laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">in chemistry</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_J._Heeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heeger was born in <a href="/wiki/Sioux_City,_Iowa" title="Sioux City, Iowa">Sioux City, Iowa</a>, to a Jewish family. He grew up in <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Iowa" title="Akron, Iowa">Akron, Iowa</a>, where his father owned a general store. At age nine, following his father's death, the family moved to Sioux City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heeger earned a B.S. in physics and mathematics from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska-Lincoln" title="University of Nebraska-Lincoln">University of Nebraska-Lincoln</a> in 1957, and a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D</a> in physics from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> in 1961. From 1962 to 1982 he was on the faculty of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>. In 1982 he commenced his present appointment as a professor in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Santa_Barbara_Physics_Department" title="UC Santa Barbara Physics Department">Physics Department</a> and the Materials Department at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>. His research has led to the formation of numerous start-up companies including Uniax, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konarka" title="Konarka">Konarka</a>, and Sirigen, founded in 2003 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guillermo_C._Bazan" title="Guillermo C. Bazan">Guillermo C. Bazan</a>, Patrick J. Dietzen, Brent S. Gaylord. Alan Heeger was a founder of Uniax, which was acquired by <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>.</p>
<p>He won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize for Chemistry">Nobel Prize for Chemistry</a> in 2000 along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alan_G._MacDiarmid" title="Alan G. MacDiarmid">Alan G. MacDiarmid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hideki_Shirakawa" title="Hideki Shirakawa">Hideki Shirakawa</a> "for their discovery and development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductive_polymers" title="Conductive polymers">conductive polymers</a>;" They published their results on <a href="/wiki/Polyacetylene" title="Polyacetylene">polyacetylene</a> a <a href="/wiki/Conductive_polymer" title="Conductive polymer">conductive polymer</a> in 1977<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HMacSh_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HMacSh-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nob_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nob-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He had won the <a href="/wiki/Oliver_E._Buckley_Condensed_Matter_Prize" title="Oliver E. Buckley Condensed Matter Prize">Oliver E. Buckley Prize</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a> in 1983 and, in 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Balzan_Prize" title="Balzan Prize">Balzan Prize</a> for Science of Non-Biological Materials.</p>
<p>His sons are the <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">neuroscientist</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Heeger" title="David Heeger">David Heeger</a> and the immunologist Peter Heeger.</p>
<p>In October 2010, Heeger participated in the <a href="/wiki/USA_Science_and_Engineering_Festival" title="USA Science and Engineering Festival">USA Science and Engineering Festival</a>'s Lunch with a Laureate program where middle and high school students engage in an informal conversation with a Nobel Prize-winning scientist over a brown-bag lunch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Heeger is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/USA_Science_and_Engineering_Festival" title="USA Science and Engineering Festival">USA Science and Engineering Festival</a>'s Advisory Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Heeger has been a judge of the <a href="/wiki/Scientists,_Technologists_and_Artists_Generating_Exploration" title="Scientists, Technologists and Artists Generating Exploration"><i>STAGE</i></a> International Script Competition three times (2006, 2007, 2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Perhaps the greatest pleasure of being a scientist is to have an abstract idea, then to do an experiment (more often a series of experiments is required) that demonstrates the idea was correct; that is, Nature actually behaves as conceived in the mind of the scientist. This process is the essence of creativity in science. I have been fortunate to have experienced this intense pleasure many times in my life." Alan J Heeger, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Never_Lose_Your_Nerve!&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Never Lose Your Nerve! (page does not exist)">Never Lose Your Nerve!</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
