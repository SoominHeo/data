<p><b>Allan Hills 84001</b> (commonly abbreviated <b>ALH84001</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-database_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-database-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a> that was found in <a href="/wiki/Allan_Hills" title="Allan Hills">Allan Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> on December 27, 1984 by a team of U.S. meteorite hunters from the <a href="/wiki/ANSMET" title="ANSMET">ANSMET</a> project. Like other members of the group of <a href="/wiki/Martian_meteorite" title="Martian meteorite">SNCs</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shergottite" title="Shergottite">shergottite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakhlite" title="Nakhlite">nakhlite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chassignite" title="Chassignite">chassignite</a>), ALH84001 is thought to be from <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. However, it does not fit into any of the previously discovered SNC groups. On discovery, its <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> was 1.93 kilograms (4.3 lb).</p>
<p>The meteorite is best known for gaining intense media attention in 1996 when a group of scientists claimed to have found evidence for microscopic fossils of Martian bacteria in it, culminating in then U.S. president Bill Clinton giving a speech about the potential discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These claims were controversial from the beginning, and the wider scientific community ultimately rejected the hypothesis once all the unusual features in the meteorite had been explained without requiring life to be present. Despite there being no convincing evidence of Martian life, the initial paper and scientific and public attention caused by it are considered to be turning points in the history of the developing science of <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">astrobiology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_description">History and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allan_Hills_84001&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="369" data-file-width="355" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg/220px-ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg/330px-ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ALH84001_meteorite_Smithsonian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
ALH84001 on display at Smithsonian Museum of Natural History</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This rock is considered to be one of the oldest Martian meteorites, proposed to have crystallized from molten rock 4.091 billion years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-age3-4">[4]</a></sup> Based on chemical analyses, it is thought to have originated on Mars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> from a period when <a href="/wiki/Water_on_Mars" title="Water on Mars">liquid water</a> existed on the now arid planet's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aqueous_environment_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-aqueous_environment-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2005, Vicky Hamilton of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii_at_Manoa" title="University of Hawaii at Manoa">University of Hawaii at Manoa</a> presented an analysis of the origin of ALH84001 using data from the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">Mars Global Surveyor</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Odyssey" title="Mars Odyssey">Mars Odyssey</a> spacecraft orbiting Mars. According to the analysis, <a href="/wiki/Eos_Chasma" title="Eos Chasma">Eos Chasma</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Valles_Marineris" title="Valles Marineris">Valles Marineris</a> canyon appears to be the source of the meteorite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-location_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-location-9">[9]</a></sup> The analysis was not conclusive, in part because it was limited to areas of Mars not obscured by dust.</p>
<p>The theory holds that ALH84001 was blasted off from the surface of Mars by a meteorite impact about 17 million years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-impact-10">[10]</a></sup> and fell on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> roughly 13,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These dates were established by a variety of <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a> techniques, including <a href="/wiki/Samarium-neodymium_dating" title="Samarium-neodymium dating">samarium-neodymium</a> (Sm-Nd), <a href="/wiki/Rubidium-strontium_dating" title="Rubidium-strontium dating">rubidium-strontium</a> (Rb-Sr), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potassium-argon_dating" title="Potassium-argon dating">potassium-argon</a> (K-Ar), and <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">carbon-14</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-age1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-age2-13">[13]</a></sup> Other meteorites that have potential biological markings have generated less interest because they do not contain rock from a "wet" Mars. ALH84001 is the only meteorite collected from such a time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2011 it was reported that isotopic analysis indicated that the carbonates in ALH84001 were precipitated at a temperature of 18 °C with water and carbon dioxide from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_atmosphere" title="Martian atmosphere">Martian atmosphere</a>. The carbonate carbon and oxygen isotope ratios imply deposition of the carbonates from a gradually evaporating subsurface water body, probably a shallow aquifer meters or tens of meters below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aqueous_environment_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-aqueous_environment-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypothetical_biogenic_features">Hypothetical biogenic features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allan_Hills_84001&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hypothetical biogenic features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 6, 1996,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup> ALH84001 became newsworthy when it was claimed that the meteorite may contain evidence of traces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(planet)" title="Life on Mars (planet)">life from Mars</a>, as published in an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/David_S._McKay" title="David S. McKay">David S. McKay</a> of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-life_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-life-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALH84001_structures.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1359" data-file-width="1987" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/ALH84001_structures.jpg/300px-ALH84001_structures.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/ALH84001_structures.jpg/450px-ALH84001_structures.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/ALH84001_structures.jpg/600px-ALH84001_structures.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ALH84001_structures.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The electron microscope revealed chain structures in meteorite fragment ALH84001</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope" title="Scanning electron microscope">scanning electron microscope</a> structures were revealed that some scientists interpreted as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossils" title="Fossils">fossils</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>-like lifeforms. The structures found on ALH84001 are <span class="nowrap">20–100 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometres</a></span> in diameter, similar in size to theoretical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanobacteria" title="Nanobacteria">nanobacteria</a>, but smaller than any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellular_life" title="Cellular life">cellular life</a> known at the time of their discovery. If the structures had been fossilized lifeforms, as was proposed by the so-called biogenic hypothesis of their formation, they would have been the first solid evidence of the existence of <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a>, aside from the chance of their origin being terrestrial contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The announcement of possible <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a> caused considerable controversy. When the discovery was announced many immediately conjectured that the fossils were the first true evidence of extraterrestrial life—making headlines around the world, and even prompting the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> to make a formal televised announcement to mark the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clinton_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-clinton-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>David S. McKay at NASA argued that likely microbial terrestrial contamination found in other Martian meteorites does not resemble the microscopic shapes in the ALH84001. In particular, the shapes within the ALH84001 look intergrown or embedded in the indigenous material, while likely contamination does not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-jsc2009-17">[17]</a></sup> While it has not yet conclusively been shown how the features in the meteorite were formed, similar features have been recreated in the lab without biological inputs by a team led by D.C. Golden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc2004_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jsc2004-18">[18]</a></sup> David McKay says these results were obtained using unrealistically pure raw materials as a starting point,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup> and "will not explain many of the features described by us in ALH84001." According to McKay, a plausible inorganic model "must explain simultaneously all of the properties that we and others have suggested as possible biogenic properties of this meteorite."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc2004_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-jsc2004-18">[18]</a></sup> The rest of the scientific community disagreed with McKay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2009, a team of scientists at <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a>, including McKay, argued that since their original paper was published, the biogenic hypothesis has been "further strengthened by the presence of abundant fossil-like structures in other Martian meteorites."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc2009_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-jsc2009-17">[17]</a></sup> However, the scientific consensus is that "morphology alone cannot be used unambiguously as a tool for primitive life detection."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morphology_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-morphology-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Interpretation of morphology is notoriously subjective, and its use alone has led to numerous errors of interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morphology_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-morphology-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List">List</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allan_Hills_84001&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: List">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These are some of the main features that have been interpreted as suggesting the presence of microfossils.</p>
<ul>
<li>The structures resemble some modern terrestrial bacteria and their appendages. Though some are smaller than any present day Earth microbes, others are of the order of 100 - 200 nm in size, within the size limits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanobacteria" title="Nanobacteria">nanobacteria</a> and some are up to 1 to 2 microns in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-impact-10">[10]</a></sup> The smallest ones are too small to contain all the systems of modern life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Some of the structures resemble colonies and biofilms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-impact-10">[10]</a></sup> However, there are many instances of morphologies that suggested life and were later shown to be due to inorganic processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-impact-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>The meteorite contains <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> crystals of the unusual rectangular prism type, and organized into domains all about the same size, indistinguishable from magnetite produced biologically on Earth and not matching any known nonbiological magnetite that forms naturally on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-impact-10">[10]</a></sup> The magnetite is embedded in the carbonate. If found on Earth it would be a very strong biosignature. However, in 2001, scientists were able to explain and produce carbonate globules containing similar magnetite grains through an inorganic process simulating conditions ALH84001 experienced on Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>It contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAHs) concentrated in the regions containing the carbonate globules, and these have been shown to be indigenous. Other organics such as amino acids do not follow this pattern and are probably due to Antarctic contamination. However PAHs are also found in asteroids, comets and meteorites, and in deep space, all in the absence of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disbelief_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-disbelief-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
