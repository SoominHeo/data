<p><b>Alan Wolf Arkin</b> (born March 26, 1934) is an American actor, director, comedian, musician, and singer. With a film career spanning more than 55 years, Arkin is known for his performances in <i><a href="/wiki/Popi" title="Popi">Popi</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wait_Until_Dark_(film)" title="Wait Until Dark (film)">Wait Until Dark</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Russians_Are_Coming,_the_Russians_Are_Coming" title="The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming">The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heart_Is_a_Lonely_Hunter_(film)" title="The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter (film)">The Heart is a Lonely Hunter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Catch-22_(film)" title="Catch-22 (film)">Catch-22</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_In-Laws_(1979_film)" title="The In-Laws (1979 film)">The In-Laws</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Edward_Scissorhands" title="Edward Scissorhands">Edward Scissorhands</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross_(film)" title="Glengarry Glen Ross (film)">Glengarry Glen Ross</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Conversations_About_One_Thing" title="Thirteen Conversations About One Thing">Thirteen Conversations About One Thing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Miss_Sunshine" title="Little Miss Sunshine">Little Miss Sunshine</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Argo_(2012_film)" title="Argo (2012 film)">Argo</a></i>.</p>
<p>He has been nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Academy Award for Best Actor</a> twice for his performances in <i>The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming</i> and <i>The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter</i>. He won an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor</a> for his performance in <i>Little Miss Sunshine</i> and received a Best Supporting Actor nomination for his performance in <i>Argo</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arkin was born in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/David_I._Arkin" title="David I. Arkin">David I. Arkin</a>, a painter and writer, and his wife, Beatrice (Wortis), a teacher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was raised in a Jewish family with "no emphasis on religion". His grandparents were immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArkinJournal07_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArkinJournal07-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His parents moved to Los Angeles when Alan was 11,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArkinJournal07_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArkinJournal07-2">[2]</a></sup> but an eight-month Hollywood strike cost his father his job as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_designer" title="Set designer">set designer</a>. During the 1950s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Red_Scare" title="Second Red Scare">Red Scare</a>, Arkin's parents were accused of being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communists</a>, and his father was fired when he refused to answer questions about his political ideology. David Arkin challenged the dismissal, but he was vindicated only after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arkin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-arkin-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">Early work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Arkin, who had been taking acting lessons since age 10, became a scholarship student at various drama academies, including one run by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantin_Stanislavski" title="Constantin Stanislavski">Stanislavsky</a> student <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Zemach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Benjamin Zemach (page does not exist)">Benjamin Zemach</a>, who taught Arkin a psychological approach to acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Arkin attended <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_City_College" title="Los Angeles City College">Los Angeles City College</a> from 1951 to 1953. He also attended <a href="/wiki/Bennington_College" title="Bennington College">Bennington College</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With two friends, he formed the <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> group <a href="/wiki/The_Tarriers" title="The Tarriers">The Tarriers</a>, in which Arkin sang and played guitar. The band members co-composed the group's 1956 hit "<a href="/wiki/Day-O_(The_Banana_Boat_Song)" title="Day-O (The Banana Boat Song)">The Banana Boat Song</a>", a reworking, with some new lyrics, of a traditional, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaican</a> <a href="/wiki/Calypso_music" title="Calypso music">calypso</a> folk song of the same name, combined with another titled "Hill and Gully Rider".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsday_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsday-6">[6]</a></sup> It reached #4 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> magazine chart the same year as <a href="/wiki/Harry_Belafonte" title="Harry Belafonte">Harry Belafonte</a>'s better-known hit version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The group appeared in the 1957 Calypso-exploitation film <i>Calypso Heat Wave</i>, singing "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banana_Boat_Song" title="Banana Boat Song">Banana Boat Song</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Choucoune_(song)" title="Choucoune (song)">Choucoune</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From 1958 to 1968, Arkin performed and recorded with the children's <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a> group, <a href="/wiki/The_Baby_Sitters_(folk_group)" title="The Baby Sitters (folk group)">The Baby Sitters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also performed the role of Dr. Pangloss in a concert staging of <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a>'s operetta <i><a href="/wiki/Candide" title="Candide">Candide</a></i>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Madeline_Kahn" title="Madeline Kahn">Madeline Kahn</a>'s Cunegonde. Arkin was an early member of <a href="/wiki/The_Second_City" title="The Second City">The Second City</a> comedy troupe in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting">Acting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Arkin is one of only six<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> actors to receive an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Award</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Best Actor</a> for his first screen appearance (for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Russians_Are_Coming,_the_Russians_Are_Coming" title="The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming">The Russians Are Coming, the Russians Are Coming</a></i> in 1966). Two years later, he was again nominated, for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heart_Is_a_Lonely_Hunter_(film)" title="The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter (film)">The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1968, he appeared in the title role of <i><a href="/wiki/Inspector_Clouseau_(film)" title="Inspector Clouseau (film)">Inspector Clouseau</a></i>, after <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sellers" title="Peter Sellers">Peter Sellers</a> disassociated himself from the role, but the film was not well received by Sellers' fans. Arkin and his second wife, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Barbara_Dana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Barbara Dana (page does not exist)">Barbara Dana</a>, appeared together on the 1970–71 season of <i><a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a></i> as a comical couple named Larry and Phyllis who resolve their conflicts when they remember how to pronounce the word "cooperate."</p>
<p>His best-known films include <i><a href="/wiki/Wait_Until_Dark_(film)" title="Wait Until Dark (film)">Wait Until Dark</a></i>, as the erudite killer stalking <a href="/wiki/Audrey_Hepburn" title="Audrey Hepburn">Audrey Hepburn</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heart_Is_a_Lonely_Hunter_(film)" title="The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter (film)">The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Catch-22_(film)" title="Catch-22 (film)">Catch-22</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven-Per-Cent_Solution" title="The Seven-Per-Cent Solution">The Seven-Per-Cent Solution</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Murders" title="Little Murders">Little Murders</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/The_In-Laws_(1979_film)" title="The In-Laws (1979 film)">The In-Laws</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross_(film)" title="Glengarry Glen Ross (film)">Glengarry Glen Ross</a>,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Miss_Sunshine" title="Little Miss Sunshine">Little Miss Sunshine</a></i>, for which he won a Best Supporting Actor Oscar; and <a href="/wiki/Argo_(2012_film)" title="Argo (2012 film)"><i>Argo</i></a>. His portrayal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grosse_Point_Blank#Cast" title="Grosse Point Blank">Dr. Oatman</a>, a scared and emotionally conflicted psychiatrist treating <a href="/wiki/John_Cusack" title="John Cusack">John Cusack</a>'s hit man character <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grosse_Point_Blank#Cast" title="Grosse Point Blank">Martin Q. Blank</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grosse_Point_Blank" title="Grosse Point Blank">Grosse Point Blank</a></i> was also well received.</p>
<p>His role in <i>Little Miss Sunshine</i>, as Grandfather Edwin, who was foul-mouthed and had a taste for heroin, won him the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTA Film Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Actor_in_a_Supporting_Role" title="BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role">Best Actor in a Supporting Role</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor">Best Actor in a Supporting Role</a>. On receiving his Academy Award on February 25, 2007, Arkin said, "More than anything, I'm deeply moved by the open-hearted appreciation our small film has received, which in these fragmented times speaks so openly of the possibility of innocence, growth, and connection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> At 72 years old, Arkin was the sixth oldest winner of the Best Supporting Actor Oscar.</p>
<p>In 2006–07, Arkin was cast in supporting roles in <i><a href="/wiki/Rendition_(film)" title="Rendition (film)">Rendition</a></i> as a US senator and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Santa_Clause_3:_The_Escape_Clause" title="The Santa Clause 3: The Escape Clause">The Santa Clause 3: The Escape Clause</a></i> as Bud Newman (Carol's Dad), starring with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Allen" title="Tim Allen">Tim Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Short" title="Martin Short">Martin Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Mitchell" title="Elizabeth Mitchell">Elizabeth Mitchell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judge_Reinhold" title="Judge Reinhold">Judge Reinhold</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Crewson" title="Wendy Crewson">Wendy Crewson</a>.</p>
<p>On Broadway, Arkin starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_Laughing" title="Enter Laughing">Enter Laughing</a></i>, for which he won a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Luv_(play)" title="Luv (play)">Luv</a></i>. He also directed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunshine_Boys" title="The Sunshine Boys">The Sunshine Boys</a></i>, among others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Directing">Directing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Directing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arkin's directorial debut, in 1969, was a 12-minute children's film, <i>People Soup</i>, starring his sons <a href="/wiki/Adam_Arkin" title="Adam Arkin">Adam Arkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Arkin" title="Matthew Arkin">Matthew Arkin</a>. Based on <a class="external text" href="https://archive.org/stream/galaxymagazine-1958-11/Galaxy_1958_11#page/n29/mode/2up" rel="nofollow">a story of the same name</a> he published in <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Science_Fiction" title="Galaxy Science Fiction">Galaxy Science Fiction</a></i> in 1958, <i>People Soup</i> is a fantasy about two boys who experiment with various kitchen ingredients until they concoct a magical soup which transforms them into different animals and objects.</p>

<p>Arkin's most acclaimed directorial effort is <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Murders" title="Little Murders">Little Murders</a></i>, released in 1971. Written by cartoonist Jules Feiffer, <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Murders" title="Little Murders">Little Murders</a></i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_comedy_film" title="Black comedy film">black comedy film</a> starring <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Gould" title="Elliott Gould">Elliott Gould</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcia_Rodd" title="Marcia Rodd">Marcia Rodd</a> about a girl, Patsy (Rodd), who brings home her boyfriend, Alfred (Gould), to meet her severely dysfunctional family amidst a series of random shootings, garbage strikes and electrical outages ravaging the neighborhood. The film opened to a lukewarm review by Roger Greenspan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and a more positive one by Vincent Canby<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> in the New York Times. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>'s review in the Chicago Sun Times was more enthusiastic, saying, "One of the reasons it works and is indeed a definitive reflection of America's darker moods, is that it breaks audiences down into isolated individuals, vulnerable and uncertain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arkin also directed <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_Sale_(film)" title="Fire Sale (film)">Fire Sale</a></i> (1977), <i>Samuel Beckett Is Coming Soon</i> (1993) and <i>Arigo</i> (2000).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arkin is the author of many books, including the children's stories <i>Tony's Hard Work Day</i> (illustrated by <a href="/wiki/James_Stevenson_(illustrator)" title="James Stevenson (illustrator)">James Stevenson</a>, 1972), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lemming_Condition&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Lemming Condition (page does not exist)">The Lemming Condition</a></i> (illustrated by Joan Sandin, 1976), <i>Halfway Through the Door: An Actor's Journey Toward Self</i> (1979), and <i>The Clearing</i> (1986 continuation of <i>Lemming</i>). In March 2011, he released his memoir, <i>An Improvised Life</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singing">Singing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Singing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1985, he sang two selections by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(writer)" title="Tom Jones (writer)">Jones</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Schmidt" title="Harvey Schmidt">Schmidt</a> on <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bagley" title="Ben Bagley">Ben Bagley</a>'s album <i>Contemporary Broadway Revisited</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Arkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arkin has been married three times. He and Jeremy Yaffe (m. 1955-61) have two sons: <a href="/wiki/Adam_Arkin" title="Adam Arkin">Adam Arkin</a>, born August 19, 1956, and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Arkin" title="Matthew Arkin">Matthew Arkin</a>, born March 21, 1960. He was married to actress-screenwriter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Barbara_Dana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Barbara Dana (page does not exist)">Barbara Dana</a> from 1964-mid 1990s. They lived in <a href="/wiki/Chappaqua,_New_York" title="Chappaqua, New York">Chappaqua, New York</a>. In 1967, they had son Anthony (Tony) Dana Arkin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1996, Arkin married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychotherapist" title="Psychotherapist">psychotherapist</a>, Suzanne Newlander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arkin_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-arkin-4">[4]</a></sup> They live in <a href="/wiki/Carlsbad,_California" title="Carlsbad, California">Carlsbad, California</a>.</p>
