<p><b>Allan Richard Williams</b> (17 March 1930 – 30 December 2016)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">[1]</a></sup> was a British businessman and promoter who was the original booking agent and first manager of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>. He personally drove the van to take the young band to <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in 1960, where they gained the vital show business experience that led to their emergence on the world stage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestry_and_early_life">Ancestry and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allan_Williams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancestry and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Williams was the son of Richard Edward Williams, a local council building inspector and Annie Cheetham, tracing part of his ancestry back to <a class="extiw" href="https://cy.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owen_Williams_(Owain_Gwyrfai)" title="cy:Owen Williams (Owain Gwyrfai)">Owen Williams (Owain Gwyrfai)</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Caernarfonshire" title="Caernarfonshire">Caernarfonshire</a> millwright, poet and pioneer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexicographer" title="Lexicographer">lexicographer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a>. His mother died when he was very young and his father remarried to Millie Twigg, the family living in <a href="/wiki/Litherland" title="Litherland">Litherland</a> and being completed by Williams' half-sister Olwyn b. 1937 and half-brother Graham b. 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In his mid teens he left home to sing with <a href="/wiki/Joe_Loss" title="Joe Loss">Joe Loss</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>. Later he sang with the <a href="/wiki/D%27Oyly_Carte_Opera_Company" title="D'Oyly Carte Opera Company">D'Oyly Carte Opera Company</a> and even tried to sell <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackpool_rock" title="Blackpool rock">Blackpool rock</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liverpool_music_scene.2C_and_the_Beatles_in_Hamburg">Liverpool music scene, and the Beatles in Hamburg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allan_Williams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Liverpool music scene, and the Beatles in Hamburg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1958 Williams leased a former watch-repair shop at 21 Slater Street, Liverpool, which he converted into a coffee bar. He named the venue the <a href="/wiki/Jacaranda" title="Jacaranda">Jacaranda</a>, after an exotic species of ornamental flowering tree, <i>jacaranda mimosifolia</i>. The Jac (as it became known) opened in September 1958. The Beatles were frequent customers, with <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Sutcliffe" title="Stuart Sutcliffe">Stuart Sutcliffe</a> attending <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverpool_Art_College" title="Liverpool Art College">Liverpool Art College</a> nearby and <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> being at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverpool_Institute" title="Liverpool Institute">Liverpool Institute</a> adjacent to the college. Asking for the chance to play the club, Williams instead put them to work redecorating, with Lennon and Sutcliffe painting a <a href="/wiki/Mural" title="Mural">mural</a> for the Ladies room. Finally, the Beatles began playing at the Jac on occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Between May and August 1960, Williams secured a number of bookings for the group at other places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> One was backing a local stripper, named Janice; when she discovered the Beatles were not familiar with the "Gypsy Fire Dance", they instead backed her with a rendition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lives_of_Harry_Lime" title="The Lives of Harry Lime">Harry Lime</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Third_Man_Theme" title="The Third Man Theme">theme tune</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALittleBare_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALittleBare-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Williams gives an extended interview in the 1980 documentary, "The Compleat Beatles", in which he tells the story of preparing the group for their Hamburg venture. He recounts having to reassure <a href="/wiki/Howie_Casey" title="Howie Casey">Howie Casey</a>, leader of <a href="/wiki/Derry_and_the_Seniors" title="Derry and the Seniors">The Seniors</a> who were already established in Hamburg, who had cautioned Allan: "Listen, we've got a good thing going here in Hamburg. But if you send that bum group, the Beatles, you're going to louse it up for all of us." He also recalls auditioning drummer <a href="/wiki/Pete_Best" title="Pete Best">Pete Best</a>, asking him to do a drum roll, which he did "Not too cleverly, but passable."</p>
<p>In August 1960, with Best joining as the group's new drummer, Williams and The Beatles left Liverpool in a small, crowded van which took them to Hamburg for the first time. He continued to get them bookings, until he fell out with The Beatles in 1961 over the payment of his ten per cent commission in a later trip to Hamburg. Williams had no further business dealings with the group and was especially disappointed that Sutcliffe, of whom he was especially fond, was the one who told him the band would not pay. In 1962, before <a href="/wiki/Brian_Epstein" title="Brian Epstein">Brian Epstein</a> became the band's manager, he contacted Williams to make sure there were no remaining contractual ties. There were none, but Williams forthrightly told Epstein: 'Don't touch them with a fucking bargepole, they will let you down.'</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_the_Beatles">Life after the Beatles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allan_Williams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life after the Beatles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Years later, Williams and the Beatles spoke fondly of one another, with McCartney describing Williams in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_Anthology" title="The Beatles Anthology">The Beatles Anthology</a></i> as 'a great guy'. In the 1970s, Williams played a crucial role in producing the first Beatles conventions to be staged in Liverpool, and he was a perennial VIP guest at the city's annual Beatle Week Festivals. In 1975, he published a memoir, <i>The Man Who Gave The Beatles Away</i>, to which Lennon gave his endorsement. Recovering a tape of a latter-day Beatles show in Hamburg (performing on New Year's Eve of 1962–63), he saw it released (in 1977) as <i><a href="/wiki/Live!_at_the_Star-Club_in_Hamburg,_Germany;_1962" title="Live! at the Star-Club in Hamburg, Germany; 1962">Live! at the Star-Club in Hamburg, Germany; 1962</a></i>. In 1999 the micro budget film All Those Years Ago was released by Shotmaker Productions. The film is largely based on Allan's own recollections of his time managing the fledgling Beatles. Although initially flattered and sympathetic to the film, in his second book A Fool on the Hill, Williams described the film makers as being deceitful and the film as "utter rubbish" <i>The Man Who Gave The Beatles Away</i> is also the title of a musical by Irish playwright Ronan Wilmot, which was performed at the New Theatre in Dublin in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Man_Who_Gave_The_Beatles_Away_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Man_Who_Gave_The_Beatles_Away-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Williams carried on speaking at Beatles conventions from Liverpool to Singapore and South America. The Jacaranda reopened under new management in the mid-1990s and saw success build upon its cult status throughout the following decade; it was a popular venue for young and old lovers of live music and hosted many gigs for Liverpool's Sound City<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> music festival.</p>
<p>In 2012 French comics <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gihef" title="fr:Gihef">Gihef</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanders" title="fr:Vanders">Vanders</a> published <i>Liverfool</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Proust" title="fr:Emmanuel Proust">Emmanuel Proust Editions</a>) in which they relate Allan Williams' encounter with the "Fab Four" and their first steps together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He died on 30 December 2016 in Liverpool at 86 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
