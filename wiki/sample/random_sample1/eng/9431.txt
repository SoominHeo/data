<p><i><b>Ally McBeal</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Legal_drama" title="Legal drama">legal</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">comedy-drama</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">television series</a>, originally aired on <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a> from September 8, 1997 to May 20, 2002. Created by <a href="/wiki/David_E._Kelley" title="David E. Kelley">David E. Kelley</a>, the series stars <a href="/wiki/Calista_Flockhart" title="Calista Flockhart">Calista Flockhart</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ally_McBeal_(character)" title="Ally McBeal (character)">title role</a> as a young lawyer working in the fictional <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_law_firms" title="List of fictional law firms">law firm</a> Cage and Fish, with other young lawyers whose lives and loves were eccentric, humorous and dramatic. The series received critical acclaim in its early seasons, winning the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Television_Series_%E2%80%93_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Television Series – Musical or Comedy">Golden Globe Award for Best Television Series – Musical or Comedy</a> in 1997 and 1998, and also winning the <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Comedy Series">Emmy Award for Outstanding Comedy Series</a> in 1999.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The series, set in the fictional <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_law_firms" title="List of fictional law firms">law firm</a> Cage and Fish, begins with main character <a href="/wiki/Ally_McBeal_(character)" title="Ally McBeal (character)">Allison Marie "Ally" McBeal</a> joining the firm (co-owned by her law school classmate Richard Fish, played by <a href="/wiki/Greg_Germann" title="Greg Germann">Greg Germann</a>) after leaving her previous job due to sexual harassment. On her first day Ally is horrified to find that she will be working alongside her ex-boyfriend Billy Thomas (<a href="/wiki/Gil_Bellows" title="Gil Bellows">Gil Bellows</a>)—whom she has never gotten over. To make things worse, Billy is now married to fellow lawyer Georgia (<a href="/wiki/Courtney_Thorne-Smith" title="Courtney Thorne-Smith">Courtney Thorne-Smith</a>), who also later joins Cage and Fish. The triangle among the three forms the basis for the main plot for the show's first three seasons.</p>
<p>Although ostensibly a legal drama, the main focus of the series was the romantic and personal lives of the main characters, often using legal proceedings as <a href="/wiki/Plot_device" title="Plot device">plot devices</a> to contrast or reinforce a character's drama. For example, bitter divorce litigation of a client might provide a backdrop for Ally's decision to break up with a boyfriend. Legal arguments were also frequently used to explore multiple sides of various social issues.</p>
<p>Cage &amp; Fish (which becomes Cage/Fish &amp; McBeal or Cage, Fish, &amp; Associates towards the end of the series), the fictional law firm where most of the characters work, is depicted as a highly sexualized environment symbolized by its unisex restroom. Lawyers and secretaries in the firm routinely date, flirt with, or have a romantic history with each other and frequently run into former or potential romantic interests in the courtroom or on the street outside.</p>
<p>The series had many offbeat and frequently surreal running gags and themes, such as Ally's tendency to immediately fall over whenever she met somebody she found attractive, or Richard Fish's <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattle</a> fetish and humorous mottos ("Fishisms" &amp; "Bygones"), or John's gymnastic dismounts out of the office's unisex bathroom stalls, or the dancing twins (played by Eric &amp; Steve Cohen) at the bar, that ran through the series. The show used vivid, dramatic <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> sequences for Ally's and other characters' wishful thinking; particularly notable is the <a href="/wiki/Dancing_baby" title="Dancing baby">dancing baby</a>.</p>
<p>The series also featured regular visits to a local bar where singer <a href="/wiki/Vonda_Shepard" title="Vonda Shepard">Vonda Shepard</a> regularly performed (though occasionally handing over the microphone to the characters). The series also took place in the same <a href="/wiki/Continuity_(fiction)" title="Continuity (fiction)">continuity</a> as David E. Kelley's legal drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Practice" title="The Practice">The Practice</a></i> (which aired on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>), as the two shows crossed over with one another on occasion, a very rare occurrence for two shows that aired on different networks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Episodes">Episodes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Episodes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Ally_McBeal_episodes" title="List of Ally McBeal episodes">List of Ally McBeal episodes</a></div>
<p>In Australia, <i>Ally McBeal</i> was aired by the Seven Network from 1997 to 2002. In 2010, it was aired repeatedly by Network Ten.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossovers_with_The_Practice">Crossovers with <i>The Practice</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crossovers with The Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/The_Practice#Crossovers" title="The Practice">The Practice § Crossovers</a></div>
<p>Seymore Walsh, a stern judge often exasperated by the eccentricities of the Cage &amp; Fish lawyers and played by actor Albert Hall, was also a recurring character on <i>The Practice</i>. In addition, Judge Jennifer (Whipper) Cone appears on <i>The Practice</i> episode "Line of Duty" (S02 E15), while Judge Roberta Kittelson, a recurring character on <i>The Practice</i>, has a featured guest role in the <i>Ally McBeal</i> episode "Do you Wanna Dance?"</p>
<p>Most of the primary <i>Practice</i> cast members guest starred in the <i>Ally McBeal</i> episode "The Inmates" (S01 E20), in a storyline that concluded with the <i>Practice</i> episode "Axe Murderer" (S02 E26), featuring Calista Flockhart and Gil Bellows reprising their <i>Ally</i> characters. What's unusual about this continuing storyline is that <i>Ally McBeal</i> and <i>The Practice</i> aired on different networks. Bobby Donnell, the main character of <i>The Practice</i> played by <a href="/wiki/Dylan_McDermott" title="Dylan McDermott">Dylan McDermott</a>, was featured heavily in both this crossover and another <i>Ally McBeal</i> episode, "These are the Days".</p>
<p>Regular <i>Practice</i> cast members <a href="/wiki/Lara_Flynn_Boyle" title="Lara Flynn Boyle">Lara Flynn Boyle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Badalucco" title="Michael Badalucco">Michael Badalucco</a> each had a cameo in <i>Ally McBeal</i> (Boyle as a woman who trades insults with Ally in the episode "Making Spirits Bright" and Badalucco as one of Ally's dates in the episode "I Know him by Heart") but it is unclear whether they were playing the same characters they play on <i>The Practice</i>.</p>

<p>Upon premiering in 1997, the show was an instant hit, averaging around 11 million viewers per episode. The show's second season saw an increase in ratings and soon became a top 20 show, averaging around 13 million viewers per episode. The show's ratings began to decline in the third season, but stabilized in the fourth season after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Downey,_Jr." title="Robert Downey, Jr.">Robert Downey, Jr.</a> joined the regular cast as Ally's boyfriend Larry Paul, and a fresher aesthetic was created by new art director Matthew DeCoste. However, Downey's character was written out after the end of the season due to the actor's troubles with drug addiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first two seasons, as well as the fourth, remain the most critically acclaimed and saw the most awards success at the Emmys, SAG Awards and the Golden Globes. In 2007 Ally McBeal placed #48 on <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> 2007 "New TV Classics" list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feminist_criticism">Feminist criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feminist criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Ally McBeal</i> received some negative criticism from TV critics and feminists who found the title character annoying and demeaning to women (specifically regarding professional women<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[8]</a></sup>) because of her perceived flightiness, lack of demonstrated legal knowledge, short skirts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[9]</a></sup> and emotional instability. Perhaps the most notorious example of the debate sparked by the show was the June 29, 1998 cover story of <i>Time</i> magazine, which juxtaposed McBeal with three pioneering feminists (<a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betty_Friedan" title="Betty Friedan">Betty Friedan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Steinem" title="Gloria Steinem">Gloria Steinem</a>) and asked "Is Feminism Dead?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[10]</a></sup> In episode 12 of the second season of the show, Ally talks to her co-worker John Cage about a dream she had, saying "You know, I had a dream that they put my face on the cover of <i>Time</i> magazine as 'the face of feminism'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Ally McBeal</i> was a heavily music-oriented show. <a href="/wiki/Vonda_Shepard" title="Vonda Shepard">Vonda Shepard</a>, a virtually unknown musician at the time, was featured continually on the show. Her song "Searchin' My Soul" became the show's theme song. Many of the songs Shepard performed were established hits with lyrics that paralleled the events of the episode, including "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Both_Sides_Now_(song)" title="Both Sides Now (song)">Both Sides Now</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hooked_on_a_Feeling" title="Hooked on a Feeling">Hooked on a Feeling</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Tell_Him_(Bert_Berns_song)" title="Tell Him (Bert Berns song)">Tell Him</a>". Besides recording background music for the show, Shepard frequently appeared at the ends of episodes as a musician performing at a local <a href="/wiki/Piano_bar" title="Piano bar">piano bar</a> frequented by the main characters. On rare occasions, her character would have conventional dialogue. A portion of "Searchin' My Soul" was played at the beginning of each episode, but remarkably the song was never played in its entirety.</p>
<p>Several of the characters had a musical <a href="/wiki/Leitmotif" title="Leitmotif">leitmotif</a> that played when they appeared. John Cage's was "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_the_First,_the_Last,_My_Everything" title="You're the First, the Last, My Everything">You're the First, the Last, My Everything</a>", Ling Woo's was the <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">Wicked Witch of the West</a> theme from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i>, and Ally McBeal herself picked "<a href="/wiki/Tell_Him_(Bert_Berns_song)" title="Tell Him (Bert Berns song)">Tell Him</a>", when told by a psychiatrist that she needed a theme.</p>
<p>Due to the popularity of the show and Shepard's music, a soundtrack titled <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_from_Ally_McBeal" title="Songs from Ally McBeal">Songs from Ally McBeal</a></i> was released in 1998, as well as a successor soundtrack titled <i>Heart and Soul: New Songs From Ally McBeal</i> in 1999. Two compilation albums from the show featuring Shepard were also released in 2000 and 2001. A Christmas album was also released under the title <i>Ally McBeal: A Very Ally Christmas</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[13]</a></sup> The album received positive reviews, and Shephard’s version of <a href="/wiki/Kay_Starr" title="Kay Starr">Kay Starr</a>’s Christmas song <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/(Everybody%27s_Waitin%27_For)_The_Man_with_the_Bag" title="(Everybody's Waitin' For) The Man with the Bag">(Everybody's Waitin' For) The Man with the Bag</a>, received considerable airplay during the holiday season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other artists featured on the show include <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_White" title="Barry White">Barry White</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Green" title="Al Green">Al Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macy_Gray" title="Macy Gray">Macy Gray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Gaynor" title="Gloria Gaynor">Gloria Gaynor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chayanne" title="Chayanne">Chayanne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Manilow" title="Barry Manilow">Barry Manilow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anastacia" title="Anastacia">Anastacia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a>. <a href="/wiki/Josh_Groban" title="Josh Groban">Josh Groban</a> played the role of Malcolm Wyatt in the May 2001 season finale, performing "You're Still You". The series creator, David E. Kelley, was impressed with Groban's performance at The Family Celebration event and based on the audience reaction to Groban's singing, Kelley created a character for him in that finale. The background score for the show was composed by <a href="/wiki/Danny_Lux" title="Danny Lux">Danny Lux</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DVD_releases">DVD releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: DVD releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to <a href="/wiki/Music_licensing" title="Music licensing">music licensing</a> issues, none of the seasons of <i>Ally McBeal</i> were available on DVD in the United States (only 6 random episodes could be found on the R1 edition) until 2009, though the show had been available in Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, Japan, Hong Kong, Portugal, Spain, France, Germany, the United Kingdom, Mexico, Taiwan, Australia, Brazil, and the Czech Republic with all the show's music intact since 2005. In the UK, Ireland, and Spain all seasons are available in a complete box set.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox_Home_Entertainment" title="20th Century Fox Home Entertainment">20th Century Fox</a> released the complete first season on DVD in Region 1 on October 6, 2009. They also released a special complete series edition on the same day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[15]</a></sup> Season 1 does not contain any special features, but the complete series set contains several bonus features, including featurettes, an all-new retrospective, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Practice_episodes#Season_2" title="List of The Practice episodes">episode</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Practice" title="The Practice">The Practice</a></i> in which Calista Flockhart guest starred, and a bonus disc entitled "The Best of Ally McBeal Soundtrack." In addition, both releases contain all of the original music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[16]</a></sup> Season 2 was released on April 6, 2010. Seasons 3, 4, and 5 were all released on October 5, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ally_.281999.29"><i>Ally</i> (1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ally (1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1999, at the height of the show's popularity, a half-hour version entitled <i>Ally</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[19]</a></sup> began airing in parallel with the main program. This version, designed in a sitcom format, used re-edited scenes from the main program, along with previously unseen footage. The intention was to further develop the plots in the comedy-drama in a sitcom style. It also focused only on Ally's personal life, cutting all the courtroom plots. The repackaged show was cancelled partway through its initial run. While 13 episodes of <i>Ally</i> were produced, only ten aired.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ally_McBeal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McBeal and 1990s <a href="/wiki/Yuppie" title="Yuppie">young affluent professional</a> women were parodied in the song <i>Ally McBeal</i> (tune of "<a href="/wiki/Like_a_Rolling_Stone" title="Like a Rolling Stone">Like a Rolling Stone</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>) by a cappella group <a href="/wiki/Da_Vinci%27s_Notebook" title="Da Vinci's Notebook">Da Vinci's Notebook</a> on their album <i>The Life and Times of Mike Fanning</i>, released in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In episode 2, season 3 of the British comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adam_and_Joe_Show" title="The Adam and Joe Show">The Adam and Joe Show</a></i>, the show was parodied as "Ally McSqeal" using soft toys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Episode 12 of the first season of <i><a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/When_Aliens_Attack" title="When Aliens Attack">When Aliens Attack</a>", centers on an invasion of Earth by the Omicronians precipitated by a signal loss during the climax of an episode of "Single Female Lawyer", whose main character is Jenny McNeal.</p>
