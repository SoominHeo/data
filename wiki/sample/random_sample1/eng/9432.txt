<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="True_alligators">True alligators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligatoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: True alligators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lineage including alligators proper (<a href="/wiki/Alligatorinae" title="Alligatorinae">Alligatorinae</a>) occurs in the fluvial deposits of the age of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Upper Chalk</a> in Europe, where they did not die out until the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> age. The true alligators are today represented by two species, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Alligator" title="American Alligator">A. mississippiensis</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">southeastern United States</a>, which can grow to 15 ft (4.6 m) and weigh 1000 lbs (453 kg)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the small <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Alligator" title="Chinese Alligator">A. sinensis</a></i> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze River</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, which grows to an average of 5 ft (1.5 m). Their name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <i>el lagarto</i>, which means "the lizard".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caimans">Caimans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligatoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Caimans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, the alligator family is represented by six species of the subfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caimaninae" title="Caimaninae">Caimaninae</a>, which differ from the alligator by the absence of a bony septum between the nostrils, and having ventral armour composed of overlapping bony <a href="/wiki/Scute" title="Scute">scutes</a>, each of which is formed of two parts united by a suture. Besides the three species in <i><a href="/wiki/Caiman_(genus)" title="Caiman (genus)">Caiman</a></i>, there are the smooth-fronted caimans in genus <i><a href="/wiki/Paleosuchus" title="Paleosuchus">Paleosuchus</a></i> and the black caiman in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melanosuchus" title="Melanosuchus">Melanosuchus</a></i>. Caimans tend to be more agile and crocodile-like in their movements, and have longer, sharper teeth than alligators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guggisberg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guggisberg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>C. crocodilus</i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caiman_crocodilus" title="Caiman crocodilus">spectacled caiman</a>, has the widest distribution, from southern Mexico to the northern half of Argentina, and grows to a modest size of about 2.2 metres (7 ft 3 in). The largest is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_list" title="Red list">near-threatened</a> <i>Melanosuchus niger</i>, the <i>jacare-assu</i> or large or <a href="/wiki/Black_caiman" title="Black caiman">black caiman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> basin. Black caimans grow to 16.5 feet (5.0 m), with the largest recorded size 5.79 metres (19.0 ft). The black caiman and <a href="/wiki/American_alligator" title="American alligator">American alligator</a> are the only members of the alligator family that pose the same danger to humans as the larger species of the <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodile</a> family.</p>
<p>Although caimans have not been studied in depth, scientists have learned their mating cycles (previously thought to be spontaneous or year-round) are linked to the rainfall cycles and the river levels, which increases chances of survival for their offspring.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_from_crocodiles">Differences from crocodiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligatoridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Differences from crocodiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alligator" title="Alligator">Alligators</a> differ from crocodiles principally in having wider and shorter heads, with more obtuse snouts; in having the fourth, enlarged tooth of the under jaw received, not into an external notch, but into a pit formed for it within the upper one; in lacking a jagged fringe which appears on the hind legs and feet of the <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodile</a>; in having the toes of the hind feet webbed not more than half way to the tips; and an intolerance to salinity, alligators strongly preferring fresh water, while crocodiles can tolerate salt water due to specialized glands for filtering out salt. In general, crocodiles tend to be more dangerous to humans than alligators. Another recently discovered trait is that of both caimans and American alligators partaking of foliage and fruit in addition to their normal diet of fish and meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
