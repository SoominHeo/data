<p><b>Alice Springs Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_airport_code" title="International Air Transport Association airport code">IATA</a>: <b>ASP</b>, <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>YBAS</b>) is a regional airport 7 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> (13 km; 8.1 mi) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_Springs,_Northern_Territory" title="Alice Springs, Northern Territory">Alice Springs</a>, Northern Territory. The airport was notably involved in Australia's first domestic airline hijacking, and later a suicide attack by a former airline employee which claimed the lives of four others.</p>
<p>The airport has two <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runways</a>, the largest of which can accommodate a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777" title="Boeing 777">777</a> <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a> (but not a fully laden <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a> due to high temperatures and the runway length). The only scheduled flights using the airport are domestic, although international charters do use the airport on occasions. The airport is not subject to a <a href="/wiki/Night_flying_restrictions" title="Night flying restrictions">curfew</a> and operates 24 hours a day.</p>
<p>During 2010–11 a total of 640,519 domestic passengers passed through Alice Springs Airport making it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_Australia" title="List of the busiest airports in Australia">18th busiest airport in Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BITRE_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BITRE-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The facility is also extensively used to launch stratospheric <a href="/wiki/Research_balloon" title="Research balloon">research balloons</a>; the runways used for a balloon launch are closed for aircraft traffic during the balloon launch process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_(DH-114)_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="973" data-file-width="1355" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_%28DH-114%29_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg/220px-Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_%28DH-114%29_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_%28DH-114%29_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg/330px-Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_%28DH-114%29_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_%28DH-114%29_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg/440px-Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_%28DH-114%29_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Connair_Riley_Turbo_Skyliner_(DH-114)_at_Alice_Springs_Wheatley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Connellan_Airways" title="Connellan Airways">Connair</a> DH-114 at Alice Springs, early 1970s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 5 October 1921 the first aircraft landed at the original airport located in the Alice Springs township. <a href="/wiki/Connellan_Airways" title="Connellan Airways">Connellan Airways</a> (later to become Connair) was based there from 1939. The military buildup in the north of Australia in the late 1930s saw the need for an airport that could take larger and heavier aircraft. This led to the construction of Seven Mile Aerodrome and the diminished role of the Town Site Drome from 1946 until its eventual abandonment in 1968. It is now the site of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Australian_Aviation_Museum" title="Central Australian Aviation Museum">Central Australian Aviation Museum</a>.</p>
<p>Seven Mile Aerodrome was originally built in 1940 by the Australian <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Defence_(Australia)" title="Department of Defence (Australia)">Department of Defence</a> and was used primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>, to bring troops and supplies into the area. The airport became the main transit base for RAAF transport planes during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Several civilian aircraft were permitted at the airport, but during the war its primary purpose was military as a refuelling and staging facility, as the airport was strategically located near the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific Theater of Operations</a>. No. 57 Operational Base Unit (RAAF) ran and maintained the aerodrome.</p>
<p>In 1958 it officially became Alice Springs Airport. The main runway was extended to its present length of 2,438 m (7,999 ft) in 1961.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Units_based_at_Seven_Mile_Aerodrome">Units based at Seven Mile Aerodrome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_Springs_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Units based at Seven Mile Aerodrome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>No. 57 Operational Base Unit RAAF ran and maintained the aerodrome during World War II.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/No._87_Squadron_RAAF" title="No. 87 Squadron RAAF">No. 87 Squadron RAAF</a> was based at the aerodrome for a period of time to undertake aerial topographic survey work during World War II.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1972_hijacking">1972 hijacking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_Springs_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1972 hijacking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ansett_Airlines_Flight_232" title="Ansett Airlines Flight 232">Ansett Airlines Flight 232</a></div>
<p>Alice Springs Airport was the site of the resolution of Australia's first domestic <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_hijacking" title="Aircraft hijacking">aircraft hijacking</a>. On 15 November 1972, an <a href="/wiki/Ansett_Australia" title="Ansett Australia">Ansett</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fokker_F27" title="Fokker F27">Fokker F27 Friendship</a> was hijacked after taking off from <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Airport" title="Adelaide Airport">Adelaide Airport</a>. The hijacker, Miloslav Hrabinec, threatened the pilot with a rifle and demanded to be given a <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a> and flown to the desert. He was convinced to allow the plane to land at its intended destination of Alice Springs, where he engaged in a shoot-out with <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory_Police" title="Northern Territory Police">Northern Territory Police</a>, critically wounding a police officer before shooting himself in the head.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1977_suicide_pilot">1977 suicide pilot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_Springs_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1977 suicide pilot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Connellan_air_disaster" title="Connellan air disaster">Connellan air disaster</a></div>
<p>Tragedy struck the airport again on 5 January 1977, when a former employee of Connair, Colin Richard Forman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> flew a stolen aircraft into the Connair offices (formerly Connellan Airways) located at the airport, killing himself and three of the airline's employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A woman working in the offices suffered severe burns and died several days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privatisation">Privatisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_Springs_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Privatisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 1 April 1989 the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Airports_Corporation" title="Federal Airports Corporation">Federal Airports Corporation</a> (FAC) assumed control of the airport. On 10 June 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Australia" title="Government of Australia">Government of Australia</a> granted a 50-year lease plus a 49-year option to Northern Territory Airports Pty Ltd. Northern Territory Airports is 100% owned by Airport Developments Group (which also operates <a href="/wiki/Tennant_Creek_Airport" title="Tennant Creek Airport">Tennant Creek Airport</a>). Northern Territory Airports Pty Ltd has 100% ownership of Alice Springs Airport Pty Ltd (along with the <a href="/wiki/Darwin_International_Airport" title="Darwin International Airport">Darwin International Airport</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft_boneyard">Aircraft boneyard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_Springs_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aircraft boneyard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>On 27 May 2011 it was announced that Alice Springs Airport had been selected to be the first large-scale <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_boneyard" title="Aircraft boneyard">aircraft boneyard</a> outside the United States, with the first aircraft for storage to arrive early in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The facility, which commenced operation in June 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> is operated by Asia Pacific Aircraft Storage Ltd. APAS chose Alice Springs because its dry, arid climate is perfect for aircraft storage and preservation. The facility will store commercial aircraft not in use, as well as those planes that have been decommissioned from service and which will be stripped of parts to be recycled, such as engines, electronics and wiring.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_Springs_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On 15 November 1972, an <a href="/wiki/Ansett_Australia" title="Ansett Australia">Ansett</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fokker_F27" title="Fokker F27">Fokker F27 Friendship</a> operating as flight 232 was hijacked after taking off from <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Airport" title="Adelaide Airport">Adelaide Airport</a>. After being persuaded to allow the plane to land at Alice Springs, the hijacker claimed his own life after a brief shoot-out with police.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Connellan_air_disaster" title="Connellan air disaster">Connellan air disaster</a> occurred on 5 January 1977, claiming the lives of the suicidal pilot and four on the ground.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Pel-Air" title="Pel-Air">Pel-Air</a> <a href="/wiki/IAI_Westwind" title="IAI Westwind">IAI 1124 Westwind</a> operating a cargo flight from <a href="/wiki/RAAF_Base_Tindal" title="RAAF Base Tindal">Tindal Airport</a> crashed into the Ilparpa Range while attempting an <a href="/wiki/Instrument_approach" title="Instrument approach">instrument approach</a> for runway 12 on 27 April 1995, killing the two pilots and a company passenger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 16 December 2000, a mentally ill man broke into the hangar of the Alice Springs Aero Club, climbed into a <a href="/wiki/Piper_PA-28_Cherokee" title="Piper PA-28 Cherokee">Piper Warrior</a> aeroplane belonging to the club and was able to start the engine. Despite having no formal flight training, the man was able to taxi the aircraft to the runway and take off under the cover of darkness at around 5am. Shortly after becoming airborne, the aircraft impacted the ground approximately 2 kilometres (1.2 mi) east of the airport, fatally injuring the pilot. The accident was investigated by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory_Police" title="Northern Territory Police">Northern Territory Police</a> and evidence heard during the subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronial_inquiry" title="Coronial inquiry">coronial inquiry</a> revealed the offender had on a previous occasion been apprehended at <a href="/wiki/Darwin_International_Airport" title="Darwin International Airport">Darwin International Airport</a> while attempting to steal an aircraft after absconding from a mental health facility. He told authorities he wanted to steal a plane to "fly back to God". It is believed he acquired a key at this time which he later used to start the plane he successfully stole and crashed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
