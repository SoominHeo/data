<p><i><b>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</b></i> is the sixth studio album by <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Foo_Fighters" title="Foo Fighters">Foo Fighters</a>, released on September 25, 2007 by <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a>. The album is noted for a blend of regular rock and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustic_rock" title="Acoustic rock">acoustic rock</a> tracks with shifting dynamics, which emerged from the variety of styles employed on the <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">demos</a> the band produced. It also marks the second time the band worked with producer <a href="/wiki/Gil_Norton" title="Gil Norton">Gil Norton</a>, whom frontman <a href="/wiki/Dave_Grohl" title="Dave Grohl">Dave Grohl</a> brought to fully explore the potential of his compositions and have a record that sounded different from their previous work. Grohl tried to focus on songs with messages that resonated on the audience, writing reflective lyrics which drew inspiration from the birth of his daughter.</p>
<p>Critical reception to <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> was mostly positive, with praise to the sonic variety and songwriting, though some reviewers found the record inconsistent and uninspired. The album topped the charts in the United Kingdom, Australia, New Zealand and Austria, and had three successful singles, "<a href="/wiki/The_Pretender_(Foo_Fighters_song)" title="The Pretender (Foo Fighters song)">The Pretender</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Long_Road_to_Ruin" title="Long Road to Ruin">Long Road to Ruin</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Let_It_Die_(song)" title="Let It Die (song)">Let It Die</a>". <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> was nominated for five <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Awards</a>, winning <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rock_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rock Album">Best Rock Album</a>, and was also awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brit_Award" title="Brit Award">Brit Award</a> of Best International Album.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_recording">Background and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tour for the <a href="/wiki/Foo_Fighters" title="Foo Fighters">Foo Fighters</a>' fifth album, <i><a href="/wiki/In_Your_Honor" title="In Your Honor">In Your Honor</a></i>, had both acoustic and electric shows to fit the song variety in that record. Frontman <a href="/wiki/Dave_Grohl" title="Dave Grohl">Dave Grohl</a> discussed this with <a href="/wiki/RCA_Music_Group" title="RCA Music Group">RCA Music Group</a> president <a href="/wiki/Clive_Davis" title="Clive Davis">Clive Davis</a>, on how "it'd be so cool" if the Foo Fighters were the band that did those different shows that appealed to specific audiences "and they wouldn't necessarily have to go to both", to which Davis replied that "you can do both together". Grohl took this advice when composing his following album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Grohl added that "we didn't plan the new album to be half rock and half acoustic", picking the songs the band considered the best, with "demos which ranged from psycho fucking <a href="/wiki/Nomeansno" title="Nomeansno">Nomeansno</a> to sloppy, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Petty" title="Tom Petty">Tom Petty</a> country to fucking piano-driven songs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:27%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"We haven't been ready to write a record like this until now. I know that Dave wouldn't have been comfortable putting violins on a song before. But for whatever reasons, it just felt like the right time to explore those things now. <a href="/wiki/In_Your_Honor" title="In Your Honor">The last record</a>, obviously, was half heavy stuff, half acoustic songs. So it really was like two sides of the coin. It sounds obvious, but this time around we weren't afraid of incorporating everything into one song if it felt right."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"> —Taylor Hawkins regarding the album's sound<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhythm_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhythm-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Since Grohl felt the songs were different from the band's previous input and "had the potential to be something great", he considered that instead of doing something like the last three albums, the band had to go out of "our own comfort zone" and "needed someone to push us out of there". So Grohl decided to work again with <a href="/wiki/Gil_Norton" title="Gil Norton">Gil Norton</a>, who produced the band's second album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colour_and_the_Shape" title="The Colour and the Shape">The Colour and the Shape</a></i>, citing how Norton taught the band of the importance of pre-production and refining the composition, and claiming Norton's "unconventional" approach "seems to capture the best of this band", considering that with him "we're not going to do a straightforward <a href="/wiki/AC/DC" title="AC/DC">AC/DC</a> record, he's gonna make it different".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xfm_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-xfm-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Preparation was extensive: first Grohl had his usual start-off by developing demos with drummer <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Hawkins" title="Taylor Hawkins">Taylor Hawkins</a>, but for the first time Grohl tried to input vocals and lyrics in this early composition stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhythm_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-rhythm-5">[5]</a></sup> After rounding up composition with guitarist <a href="/wiki/Chris_Shiflett" title="Chris Shiflett">Chris Shiflett</a> and bassist <a href="/wiki/Nate_Mendel" title="Nate Mendel">Nate Mendel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drum_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-drum-7">[7]</a></sup> Grohl spent two weeks with Norton discussing "arrangements, harmony and melody" and reducing the song ideas, and then the band spent four weeks rehearsing, playing "a song a day, from noon to midnight".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-bill-8">[8]</a></sup> Hawkins stated that "we basically played each of these songs 100 different times, trying every little thing every different way" and that it was the first time since <i>The Colour and the Shape</i> "that Dave had to deal with someone in the room questioning all his ideas", given the more laid-back approach of previous producer <a href="/wiki/Nick_Raskulinecz" title="Nick Raskulinecz">Nick Raskulinecz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drum_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-drum-7">[7]</a></sup> Grohl claimed the choices were for the "most powerful, dramatic songs",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riff_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-riff-9">[9]</a></sup> and that there was an effort to "make everything sound as natural as possible - just like on the albums we grew up listening to", citing 1970s artists such as <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wings_(band)" title="Wings (band)">Wings</a> as a major influence. Shiflett added that for the first time he played <a href="/wiki/Lead_guitar" title="Lead guitar">lead guitar</a> in some tracks while Grohl "usually works out all the bits" of composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recording began on March 2007 at Studio 606 in Dave Grohl's Virginia home's basement(the studio has since been moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northridge,_California" title="Northridge, California">Northridge, California</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As the band took a ten-day break in April, Grohl thought that the record needed another uptempo song, so he spent his time developing an unfinished song that became "<a href="/wiki/The_Pretender_(Foo_Fighters_song)" title="The Pretender (Foo Fighters song)">The Pretender</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The sessions wrapped in mid-June,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and for the first time the band did not feel the need to rerecord any song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-4">[4]</a></sup> Grohl stated that while <i>In Your Honor</i> was a double album because he felt "schizophrenic" to alternate between loud and acoustic songs, Norton helped on sequencing the tracks into "an album that makes sense".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xfm_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-xfm-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg"><img alt="A bespectacled woman plays a guitar." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg/220px-Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg/330px-Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg/440px-Kaki_King_with_acoustic_guitar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The album features the Foo Fighters' first instrumental, "Ballad of the Beaconsfield Miners". It was written by Grohl after meeting with one of the miners involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaconsfield_mine_collapse" title="Beaconsfield mine collapse">Beaconsfield mine collapse</a> who requested an <a href="/wiki/IPod" title="IPod">iPod</a> with <i>In Your Honor</i> in it during the incident. As Grohl was moved by this action, he decided to "write something just to dedicate to him that night because he definitely seemed like a hero", and later promised to include the instrumental on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rip_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-rip-14">[14]</a></sup> The album version features <a href="/wiki/Kaki_King" title="Kaki King">Kaki King</a>, whom Grohl invited to record the song as she was visiting Studio 606. Grohl later said that "I showed it to her once, and she shredded 10 times better than I ever played it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Another guest was guitarist <a href="/wiki/Pat_Smear" title="Pat Smear">Pat Smear</a>, who had been a bandmember from 1995 to 1998, and a guest musician on the tour for <i>In Your Honor</i>. Smear, who has since been reinstated as a full-on member, described his participation as "the oddest recording experience I had with Foo Fighters" given he had no input in composition and was "going in and playing on a song that was already written".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> offers a mix of both electric and acoustic songs, which Grohl likened to the band growing older and "comfortable with all kinds of music" instead of just focusing on straight rock songs, saying that listening to the album he felt like "we've gotten over our insecurities, because it presents us in a way that we probably hid in the past."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup> Grohl also stated that "the idea now is to step up and make [<a href="/wiki/The_Zombies" title="The Zombies">The Zombies</a>'] <i><a href="/wiki/Odessey_and_Oracle" title="Odessey and Oracle">Odessey and Oracle</a></i>" - <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-bill-8">[8]</a></sup> the album he claimed to have listened the most during production -<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup> and that "it has always been my dream to mix <a href="/wiki/Steely_Dan" title="Steely Dan">Steely Dan</a> with Nomeansno."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rip_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-rip-14">[14]</a></sup> Amidst the amount of heavier tracks and themes Grohl decided to include the song "<a href="/wiki/Cheer_Up,_Boys_(Your_Make_Up_Is_Running)" title="Cheer Up, Boys (Your Make Up Is Running)">Cheer Up, Boys (Your Make Up Is Running)</a>", described as "the most light-hearted, melodic song of all" which "seemed like a little ray of hope in the middle of all this despair."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Grohl added that there was a bigger focus on melodies even in heavier tracks such as "The Pretender", "<a href="/wiki/Let_It_Die_(song)" title="Let It Die (song)">Let It Die</a>" and "Erase/Replace",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup> and that album closer "Home", a ballad featuring Grohl on the piano, was "the best song I've ever written".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-spin-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The songs of the album are noted for their changing dynamics - with "middle sections [that] turn into this mass orchestrated swarm and ridiculous time signatures" which include musical references to 1970s <a href="/wiki/Soft_rock" title="Soft rock">soft rock</a> bands such as Wings, <a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">Eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bread_(band)" title="Bread (band)">Bread</a> -<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rip_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-rip-14">[14]</a></sup> summed by Hawkins by saying the band "wanted to make sure that everything 'built' on this record, that each instrument started somewhere and went somewhere else in the course of a song".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhythm_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-rhythm-5">[5]</a></sup> The drummer attributed this to the acoustic tour leading the band to "shed some of the fear of incorporating mellower stuff with the heavy stuff",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drum_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-drum-7">[7]</a></sup> and Grohl added that "we wanted the stops to be pin-drop silent before exploding. If we had a beautiful melody, we'd throw a fucking string quartet in there. So we did everything we could to really magnify all those elements and that was fun. Usually you have a few parameters you're afraid to pass but, this time, there was no fear of going too far."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riff_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-riff-9">[9]</a></sup> Mix engineer <a href="/wiki/Rich_Costey" title="Rich Costey">Rich Costey</a> added that his work of "preserve what [the band] had done to a fairly large degree" with "balancing and rides to get the dynamics to happen" was difficult given the sonic variety of <i>Echoes</i>, which went from "[the Foo Fighters'] endless walls of guitar overdubs, almost like a swarm of bees" to string quartets: "The challenge of this type of mix is to retain the power of the track, yet define a space for everything."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sos_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-sos-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:27%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Most people think the world begins with their birth and ends with their death, but at some point your realise there's a much larger world out there that will continue existing long after you have made your exit. So I started to take in the big picture, and these realisations had an influence on the new album. There are songs about birth, death and life because my perception of these things has changed radically."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"> —Dave Grohl on the album's lyrics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>As the acoustic tour made Grohl realize "we were making music worth listening to, rather than music made for pummelling the person next to you" he decided to give more importance to the lyrics and "have a connection with the crowd in front of us", considering that among the many compositions the band made on pre-production "the ones that stand out are the ones that say something".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-change-20">[20]</a></sup> So for the first time the lyrics started being written before recording begun, with Grohl stating that he "sat in the back of the studio and just wrote every day for about 14 hours a day."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hot_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-hot-21">[21]</a></sup> Most of the lyrics of the album deal with themes of birth, death and life, which Grohl attributed to the birth of his daughter Violet, considering that having a child "changes your entire outlook on the world",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-total_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-total-10">[10]</a></sup> and that he was suddenly more emotional - "So when you're writing music with that in mind or that in your heart, everything just blooms into this fucking incredibly colourful, colourful feeling."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-4">[4]</a></sup> Helped by the extensive lyrical preparation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hot_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-hot-21">[21]</a></sup> the lyrics also tried to show more of Grohl's feelings, "those things that you've always wanted to do or always wanted to say",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clash_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-clash-22">[22]</a></sup> with Hawkins adding that he could not listen to "Stranger Things Have Happened" as "I'm one of his best friends, and the last thing I want to do is read a love letter to his wife or whoever it is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-bill-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging">Packaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Packaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cover art was made by <a href="/wiki/Invisible_Creature" title="Invisible Creature">Invisible Creature</a>, and features a combination between a <a href="/wiki/Aerial_bomb" title="Aerial bomb">aerial bomb</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Valve_audio_amplifier" title="Valve audio amplifier">guitar amplifier tube</a> to juxtapose the weapon "with another object that traditionally wasn’t associated with war or violence". The remainder of the album sleeve has similar juxtapositions of objects "that reflected the album's tone of life and mortality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album's title comes from a lyric on the album's final song, "Home". Grohl stated that as he struggled to think of a title given the musical variety of the album, even considering the title "The One With That Song On It", he looked at the song lyrics and after reading the ones from "Home" Grohl "thought it was nice because it's open to interpretation and it's a beautiful title and I think the album is beautiful in its diversity and its melody and its musicality – it goes from delicate acoustic moments to the heaviest shit we’ve ever done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hot_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-hot-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clash_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-clash-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album's first single, "The Pretender", had a forty-second preview released on a cross-promotional campaign with rock radio stations in July 2007, and eventually saw its debut on August 3, 2007 at <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a>'s broadcast of the <a href="/wiki/X_Games_XIII" title="X Games XIII">X Games XIII</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-bill-8">[8]</a></sup> It was released for <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">music download</a> and for radio play in August 2007, with a CD single coming out on September 17, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Long_Road_to_Ruin" title="Long Road to Ruin">Long Road to Ruin</a>" was released as the second single in December 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 2008, "<a href="/wiki/Cheer_Up,_Boys_(Your_Make_Up_Is_Running)" title="Cheer Up, Boys (Your Make Up Is Running)">Cheer Up, Boys (Your Make Up Is Running)</a>" was issued as a download single in the UK,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> with "Let It Die" being picked instead for the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> All three North American singles topped <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Rock_Tracks" title="Modern Rock Tracks">Modern Rock Tracks</a> charts, making it only the ninth album in history to spawn three number one hits on this chart,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and "The Pretender" set a record by spending eighteen weeks at the summit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> was released on September 24, 2007. Pre-orders through <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> were awarded with a free download of "The Pretender", advance tickets through <a href="/wiki/Ticketmaster" title="Ticketmaster">Ticketmaster</a>, and the bonus tracks "Seda" and "Once and For All".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The album was also issued as a double <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_play" title="Long play">vinyl record</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The promotional tour begun in September 2007, after some concerts in the United Kingdom during the summer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-bill-8">[8]</a></sup> and lasted until Fall 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> While the back-up band compiled for the <i>In Your Honor</i> tour - guitarist <a href="/wiki/Pat_Smear" title="Pat Smear">Pat Smear</a>, keyboardist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rami_Jaffe" title="Rami Jaffe">Rami Jaffe</a>, violinist <a href="/wiki/Jessy_Greene" title="Jessy Greene">Jessy Greene</a>, and percussionist <a href="/wiki/Drew_Hester" title="Drew Hester">Drew Hester</a> - remained to perform complex songs such as "Come Alive", a few tracks had more stripped-down arrangements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-bill-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-spin1-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>Initial critical reception to <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> was positive. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Standard_score" title="Standard score">normalized</a> rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album has received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">average</a> score of 71, based on 30 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacritic_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i>, Tom Sinclair considered that while the record was not ground-breaking, "the Foos have found a way to create their own archetype, with an instinctive feel for what constitutes a killer song", and praised "how damn near flawless the tone of the whole set feels".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-ew-36">[36]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/David_Fricke" title="David Fricke">David Fricke</a> praised the sonic variety, described by him as "an anthology of strong new songs by a great bunch of bands, all calling themselves Foo Fighters".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs-40">[40]</a></sup> Jessica Letkemann of <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> was acceptive of the "delicious sundown grooves" of the quieter piece and "the Foos' usual soft-louder-loudest 'radio friendly unit shifters'" - referencing a track of <a href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)" title="Nirvana (band)">Nirvana's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/In_Utero_(album)" title="In Utero (album)">In Utero</a></i> - while considering "Home" the only disappointing track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billrev_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-billrev-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> rated the album a B, describing it as a "candid attempt to recapitulate Nirvana Mark II's 10-year-old triumph, <i>The Colour and the Shape</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rc_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-rc-38">[38]</a></sup> Henrik Holmgren of <i><a href="/wiki/Melodic_(magazine)" title="Melodic (magazine)">Melodic</a></i> praised the production of the album and felt that Grohl singing "gets better and better with every record."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melodic_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-melodic-43">[43]</a></sup> Dave Simpson of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> called <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> the band's "most accomplished album", praising the composition and saying that "Gil Norton's stunning production can't disguise the raw humanity beneath the sheen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few critics considered the album not as inspired as the band's previous work. Kyle Anderson of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> wrote that "two-thirds of these tracks sound a lot like songs Grohl has done before", considering that album's strengths came from "the handful of songs that deviate from the wallop'n'wail template".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-spin-19">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> reviewer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> also felt the rock songs were not as remarkable "compared to the almost effortlessly engaging melodies of the softer songs", ultimately describing <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> as "just another Foo Fighters album instead of the consolidation of strengths that it was intended to be".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a>'s Adam Moerder considered that the album "feels like a retread" and that the band was "sounding less and less relatable", with innefective acoustic tracks and rock songs that "sound cold and detached compared to heart-wrenching Foo pop gems like '<a href="/wiki/Big_Me" title="Big Me">Big Me</a>' or '<a href="/wiki/Everlong" title="Everlong">Everlong</a>'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitch_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-pitch-37">[37]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Sputnikmusic" title="Sputnikmusic">Sputnikmusic</a> reviewer John Hanson was acceptive of the rock songs, where he felt "the boys are most comfortable", he considered that the songwriting "has just become stale" and ultimately described <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> as "extremely boring and uninspired".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sput_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-sput-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a>' Josh Timmermann was very critical of the overtly serious tone of the record compared to the lighthearted work the band had done before, joking that the title of "Cheer Up, Boys" "sounds like the suggestion of a concerned fan for a band he or she used to actually care about."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">Accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/50th_Grammy_Awards" title="50th Grammy Awards">50th Grammy Awards</a>, <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rock_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rock Album">Grammy Award for Best Rock Album</a>, and "The Pretender" was chosen for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Hard_Rock_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Hard Rock Performance">Best Hard Rock Performance</a>. The album was also nominated for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>, while "The Pretender" was also nominated for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Record_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Record of the Year">Record of the Year</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rock_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Rock Song">Best Rock Song</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The album also won Best International Album at the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Brit_Awards" title="Brit Awards">Brit Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> ranked it the 12th best album of 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> while <i>Rolling Stone</i> put the album at the 45th spot in a similar list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, <i>Echoes, Silence, Patience &amp; Grace</i> entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> album chart at number three, selling 168,000 copies in its first week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and has since been certified Gold by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RIAA_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-50">[50]</a></sup> As of April 14, 2011, it has sold 897,000 copies in US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The album debuted at the top of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_album_chart" title="UK album chart">UK album chart</a>, selling 135,685 albums in its first week;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Australia and New Zealand, being certified platinum in its first week in both countries;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rianz_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-rianz-54">[54]</a></sup> and Canada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cha_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-cha-55">[55]</a></sup> where the album went Platinum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRIA_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRIA-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel">Personnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_musicians">Additional musicians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echoes,_Silence,_Patience_%26_Grace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Additional musicians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
