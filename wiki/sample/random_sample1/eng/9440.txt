<p><b>Alexandar Zivojinovich</b>, <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">OC</a></span> (born 27 August 1953), better known by his stage name <b>Alex Lifeson</b>, is a Canadian musician, best known as the guitarist of the Canadian rock band <a href="/wiki/Rush_(band)" title="Rush (band)">Rush</a>. In 1968, Lifeson co-founded the band that would become Rush, with drummer <a href="/wiki/John_Rutsey" title="John Rutsey">John Rutsey</a> and bassist and singer <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Jones_(musician)" title="Jeff Jones (musician)">Jeff Jones</a>. Jones was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Geddy_Lee" title="Geddy Lee">Geddy Lee</a> a month later, and Rutsey was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Peart" title="Neil Peart">Neil Peart</a> in 1974; the band's line-up has remained the same ever since.</p>
<p>With Rush, Lifeson plays electric and acoustic guitars, as well as other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stringed_instrument" title="Stringed instrument">stringed instruments</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mandola" title="Mandola">mandola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bouzouki" title="Bouzouki">bouzouki</a>. He also performs backing vocals in live performances, and occasionally plays keyboards and bass pedal synthesizers. Like the other members of Rush, Lifeson performs real-time on-stage triggering of <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">sampled</a> instruments, concurrently with his guitar playing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onstagemag.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-onstagemag.com-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bulk of Lifeson's work in music has been with Rush, although Lifeson has contributed to a body of work outside of the band as well. Aside from music, Lifeson is part-owner of <a href="/wiki/The_Orbit_Room" title="The Orbit Room">The Orbit Room</a>, a bar and restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, a painter and a licensed aircraft <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vdtsgn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-vdtsgn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with his bandmates <a href="/wiki/Geddy_Lee" title="Geddy Lee">Geddy Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Peart" title="Neil Peart">Neil Peart</a>, Lifeson was made an Officer of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">Order of Canada</a> on 9 May 1996. The trio was the first rock band to be so honoured, as a group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lifeson was ranked 98th on <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a>'</i>s list of the 100 greatest guitarists of all time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStonePoll_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStonePoll-4">[4]</a></sup> and third (after <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Van_Halen" title="Eddie Van Halen">Eddie Van Halen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a>) in a <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_World" title="Guitar World">Guitar World</a></i> readers poll also listing the 100 greatest guitarists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuitarWorldPoll_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuitarWorldPoll-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lifeson was born as Alexandar Zivojinovich (Aleksandar Živojinović in Serbian) in <a href="/wiki/Fernie,_British_Columbia" title="Fernie, British Columbia">Fernie</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, to Serbian immigrants, Nenad and Melanija Živojinović, and raised in Toronto, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vdtsgn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-vdtsgn-2">[2]</a></sup> His stage name of "Lifeson" is a semi-literal translation of the surname <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDivojinovi%C4%87" title="Živojinović">Živojinović</a>, which means "son of life" in Serbian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His first exposure to formal music training came in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, which he renounced for the guitar at the age of 12. His first guitar was a Christmas gift from his father, a six-string Kent classical acoustic which was later upgraded to an electric Japanese model. During his adolescent years, he was influenced primarily by <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck" title="Jeff Beck">Jeff Beck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Steve_Hackett" title="Steve Hackett">Steve Hackett</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Allan_Holdsworth" title="Allan Holdsworth">Allan Holdsworth</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> he explained in 2011 that "Clapton's solos seemed a little easier and more approachable. I remember sitting at my record player and moving the needle back and forth to get the solo in 'Spoonful.' But there was nothing I could do with Hendrix."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1963 Lifeson met future Rush drummer <a href="/wiki/John_Rutsey" title="John Rutsey">John Rutsey</a> in school. Both interested in music, they decided to form a band. Lifeson was primarily a self-taught guitarist with the only formal instruction coming from a high school friend in 1971 who taught classical guitar lessons. This training lasted for roughly a year and a half.</p>
<p>Lifeson recalls what inspired him to play guitar in a 2008 interview:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>My brother-in-law played flamenco guitar. He lent his guitar to me and I grew to like it. When you're a kid, you don't want to play an accordion because it would be too boring. But your parents might want you to play one, especially if you're from a Yugoslavian family like me.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Lifeson's first girlfriend, Charlene, gave birth to their eldest son, Justin, in October 1970, and they married in 1975. Their second son, Adrian, was born two years later. Adrian is also involved in music, and performed on two tracks from Lifeson's 1996 solo project, <i>Victor</i>.</p>

<p>Lifeson's neighbour <a href="/wiki/John_Rutsey" title="John Rutsey">John Rutsey</a> began experimenting on a rented drum kit. In 1963, Lifeson and Rutsey formed The Projection, which eventually became Rush in August 1968 following the recruitment of original bassist and vocalist <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Jones_(musician)" title="Jeff Jones (musician)">Jeff Jones</a>. <a href="/wiki/Geddy_Lee" title="Geddy Lee">Geddy Lee</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> friend of Lifeson's, assumed this role soon after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instrumentally, Lifeson is renowned for his signature riffing, electronic effects and processing, unorthodox chord structures, and the copious arsenal of equipment he has used over the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rush was on hiatus for several years starting in 1997 owing to <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Rider:_Travels_on_the_Healing_Road" title="Ghost Rider: Travels on the Healing Road">personal tragedies in Neil Peart's life</a>, and Lifeson had not picked up a guitar for at least a year following those events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond-15">[15]</a></sup> However, after some work in his home studio and on various side projects, Lifeson returned to the studio with Rush to begin work on 2002's <i>Vapor Trails</i>. <i>Vapor Trails</i> is the first Rush album since the 1970s to lack keyboards—as such, Lifeson used over 50 different guitars in what <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shawn_Hammond" title="Shawn Hammond">Shawn Hammond</a> of <i>Guitar Player</i> called "his most rabid and experimental playing ever." Geddy Lee was amenable to leaving keyboards off the album due in part to Lifeson's ongoing concern about their use. Lifeson's approach to the guitar tracks for the album eschewed traditional guitar riffs and solos in favour of "tonality and harmonic quality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hammond-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During live performances, he is still responsible for cuing various guitar effects, the use of bass pedal synthesizers, and backing vocals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victor"><i>Victor</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Victor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the bulk of Lifeson's work in music has been with Rush, Lifeson's first major outside work was his solo project, <i><a href="/wiki/Victor_(album)" title="Victor (album)">Victor</a></i>, released in 1996. <i>Victor</i> was attributed as a self-titled work (i.e. <i>Victor</i> is attributed as the <i>artist</i> as well as the <i>album title</i>). This was done deliberately as an alternative to issuing the album explicitly under Lifeson's name. The title track is from the <a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a> poem, also entitled "Victor". Both son Adrian and wife Charlene also contributed to the album. A follow-up album, possibly including vocals by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_McLachlan" title="Sarah McLachlan">Sarah McLachlan</a>, was rumoured in the late 1990s, but was apparently shelved due to Atlantic Records' lack of support for the first album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Side_projects">Side projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Side projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lifeson has also contributed to a body of work outside of his involvement with the band in the form of instrumental contributions to other musical outfits. He made a guest appearance on the 1985 <a href="/wiki/Platinum_Blonde_(band)" title="Platinum Blonde (band)">Platinum Blonde</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_Shores" title="Alien Shores">Alien Shores</a></i> performing guitar solos on the songs "Crying Over You" and "Holy Water". Later, in 1990, he appeared on <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Gowan" title="Lawrence Gowan">Lawrence Gowan</a>'s album, <i>Lost Brotherhood</i> to play guitar. In 1995, he guested on two tracks on <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cochrane" title="Tom Cochrane">Tom Cochrane</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ragged_Ass_Road" title="Ragged Ass Road">Ragged Ass Road</a></i> album and then in 1996 on <a href="/wiki/I_Mother_Earth" title="I Mother Earth">I Mother Earth</a>'s "Like a Girl" from the <i><a href="/wiki/Scenery_and_Fish" title="Scenery and Fish">Scenery and Fish</a></i> album. In 1997 he appeared on the <i>Merry Axemas: A Guitar Christmas</i> album. Lifeson played "the Little Drummer Boy" which was released as track 9 on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merry_Axemas_at_Allmusic.2C_overview_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merry_Axemas_at_Allmusic.2C_overview-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2006, Lifeson founded The <a href="/wiki/Big_Dirty_Band" title="Big Dirty Band">Big Dirty Band</a>, which he created for the purpose of providing original soundtrack material for <i><a href="/wiki/Trailer_Park_Boys:_The_Movie" title="Trailer Park Boys: The Movie">Trailer Park Boys: The Movie</a></i>. Lifeson <a href="/wiki/Jam_session" title="Jam session">jammed</a> regularly with The Dexters (<a href="/wiki/The_Orbit_Room" title="The Orbit Room">The Orbit Room</a> house band from 1994 to 2004). Lifeson made a guest appearance on the 2007 album <i><a href="/wiki/Fear_of_a_Blank_Planet" title="Fear of a Blank Planet">Fear of a Blank Planet</a></i> by UK progressive rock band, <a href="/wiki/Porcupine_Tree" title="Porcupine Tree">Porcupine Tree</a>, contributing a solo during the song <i><a href="/wiki/Anesthetize" title="Anesthetize">Anesthetize</a></i>. He also appeared on the 2008 album <i><a href="/wiki/Tiles_(band)#About_.22Fly_Paper.22" title="Tiles (band)">Fly Paper</a></i> by Detroit progressive rockers <a href="/wiki/Tiles_(band)" title="Tiles (band)">Tiles</a>. He plays on the track "Sacred and Mundane". Outside of band related endeavours, Lifeson composed the theme for the first season of the science-fiction TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(TV_series)" title="Andromeda (TV series)">Andromeda</a></i>. He also produced 3 songs from the album <i><a href="/wiki/Away_from_the_Sun" title="Away from the Sun">Away from the Sun</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/3_Doors_Down" title="3 Doors Down">3 Doors Down</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_and_film_appearances">Television and film appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television and film appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lifeson made his film debut as himself under his birth name in the 1972 Canadian documentary film <i>Come on Children</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2003 episode of the Canadian <a href="/wiki/Mockumentary" title="Mockumentary">mockumentary</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Trailer_Park_Boys" title="Trailer Park Boys">Trailer Park Boys</a></i>, titled "<a href="/wiki/Closer_to_the_Heart" title="Closer to the Heart">Closer to the Heart</a>", Lifeson plays a partly fictional version of himself. In the story, he is kidnapped by <a href="/wiki/Robb_Wells" title="Robb Wells">Ricky</a> and held as punishment for his inability (or refusal) to provide the main characters with free tickets to a Rush concert. In the end of the episode, Alex reconciles with the characters, and performs a duet of "Closer to the Heart" with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Smith_(actor)" title="Mike Smith (actor)">Bubbles</a> at the trailer park. In 2006, Lifeson appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Trailer_Park_Boys:_The_Movie" title="Trailer Park Boys: The Movie">Trailer Park Boys: The Movie</a></i> as a traffic cop in the opening scene and in 2009 he appeared in their follow up movie, <i><a href="/wiki/Trailer_Park_Boys:_Countdown_to_Liquor_Day" title="Trailer Park Boys: Countdown to Liquor Day">Trailer Park Boys: Countdown to Liquor Day</a></i>, as an undercover vice cop in drag.</p>
<p>In 2008, Lifeson and the rest of Rush played "Tom Sawyer" at the end of an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colbert_Report" title="The Colbert Report">The Colbert Report</a></i>. According to Colbert, this was their first appearance on American television as a band in 33 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, he and the rest of the band appeared as themselves in the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/I_Love_You,_Man" title="I Love You, Man">I Love You, Man</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alex Lifeson appears as the border guard in the 2009 movie <i>Suck</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The role of Dr. Funtime in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Drunk_and_On_Drugs_Happy_Funtime_Hour" title="The Drunk and On Drugs Happy Funtime Hour">The Drunk and On Drugs Happy Funtime Hour</a></i> was originally written with Alex Lifeson in mind, but due to scheduling conflicts the role was given to <a href="/wiki/Maury_Chaykin" title="Maury Chaykin">Maury Chaykin</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Naples_incident">The Naples incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Naples incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve#United_States" title="New Year's Eve">New Year's Eve</a> 2003, Lifeson, his son, and his daughter-in-law were arrested at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ritz-Carlton" title="Ritz-Carlton">Ritz-Carlton</a> hotel in <a href="/wiki/Naples,_Florida" title="Naples, Florida">Naples, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Lifeson, after intervening in an altercation between his son and police, was accused of assaulting a sheriff's deputy in what was described as a drunken brawl. In addition to suffering a broken nose at the hands of the officers, Lifeson was <a href="/wiki/Taser" title="Taser">tased</a> six times. His son was also tased repeatedly.</p>
<p>On 21 April 2005, Lifeson and his son agreed to a plea deal with the local <a href="/wiki/Prosecutor" title="Prosecutor">prosecutor</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State%27s_Attorney" title="State's Attorney">State's Attorney</a> office to avoid jail time by pleading <a href="/wiki/Nolo_contendere" title="Nolo contendere">no contest</a> to a first-degree <a href="/wiki/Misdemeanor" title="Misdemeanor">misdemeanor</a> charge of resisting arrest without violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As part of the plea agreement Lifeson and his son were each sentenced to 12 months of probation with the adjudication of that probation suspended. Lifeson acknowledged his subsequent legal action against both the Ritz-Carlton and the Collier County Sheriff's Office for "their incredibly discourteous, arrogant and aggressive behaviour of which I had never experienced in thirty years of travel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Although both actions were initially dismissed in April 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> legal claims against the Ritz-Carlton were reinstated upon appeal, and ultimately settled out of court in August 2008, with Lifeson and his son agreeing to a confidential settlement from Ritz-Carlton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his journal-based book <i>Roadshow</i>, Peart relates the band's perspective on the events of that New Year's Eve.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Rush_.281970s.29">Early Rush (1970s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early Rush (1970s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Rush's early career, Lifeson used a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_ES-335" title="Gibson ES-335">Gibson ES-335</a> for the first tour, and in 1976 bought a 1974 <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Les_Paul" title="Gibson Les Paul">Gibson Les Paul</a>; he used those two guitars until the late 1970s. He had a <a href="/wiki/Fender_Stratocaster" title="Fender Stratocaster">Fender Stratocaster</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Bill_Lawrence_(guitar_maker)" title="Bill Lawrence (guitar maker)">Bill Lawrence</a> humbucker and <a href="/wiki/Floyd_Rose" title="Floyd Rose">Floyd Rose</a> vibrato bridge as backup "and for a different sound."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> For the <i><a href="/wiki/A_Farewell_to_Kings" title="A Farewell to Kings">A Farewell to Kings</a></i> sessions, Lifeson began using a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_EDS-1275" title="Gibson EDS-1275">Gibson EDS-1275</a> for songs like "<a href="/wiki/Xanadu_(Rush_song)" title="Xanadu (Rush song)">Xanadu</a>"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and his main guitar became a white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gibson_ES-355" title="Gibson ES-355">Gibson ES-355</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> During this period Lifeson used <a href="/wiki/Hiwatt" title="Hiwatt">Hiwatt</a> amplifiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He played a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_B_series#B-45" title="Gibson B series">twelve-string Gibson B-45</a> on songs like "<a href="/wiki/Closer_to_the_Heart" title="Closer to the Heart">Closer to the Heart</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s_and_1990s">1980s and 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1980s and 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1980 to 1986, Lifeson used four identically modified Stratocasters, all of them equipped with the Floyd Rose bridge. As a joke, he called these Hentor Sportscasters – a made-up name inspired by Peter Henderson's name, who was the producer of <i><a href="/wiki/Grace_Under_Pressure_(Rush_album)" title="Grace Under Pressure (Rush album)">Grace Under Pressure</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> He also played a Gibson Howard Roberts Fusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> By 1987, Lifeson switched to Signature guitars (Canadian-made) which, while "awful to play—very uncomfortable--...had a particular sound I liked."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> Lifeson primarily used <a href="/wiki/PRS_Guitars" title="PRS Guitars">PRS guitars</a> in the later-half of the 1990 Presto tour, and again during the recording of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roll_The_Bones" title="Roll The Bones">Roll The Bones</a></i> in 1990/1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He would continue to play PRS for the next sixteen years through the recording and touring of <i><a href="/wiki/Counterparts_(album)" title="Counterparts (album)">Counterparts</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Test_for_Echo" title="Test for Echo">Test for Echo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vapor_Trails" title="Vapor Trails">Vapor Trails</a></i> as well as the R30 tour.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s_onward:_Return_to_Gibson_guitars">2000s onward: Return to Gibson guitars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2000s onward: Return to Gibson guitars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Lifeson said that for the past few years he <i>"...used Gibson almost exclusively. There's nothing like having a low-slung Les Paul over my shoulder."</i></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gibson_.22Alex_Lifeson_Axcess.22">Gibson "Alex Lifeson Axcess"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title='Edit section: Gibson "Alex Lifeson Axcess"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early 2011, Gibson introduced the "Alex Lifeson Axcess", a guitar specially designed for him. These are custom made Les Pauls with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Floyd_Rose_tremolo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Floyd Rose tremolo (page does not exist)">Floyd Rose tremolo</a> systems and <a href="/wiki/Pickup_(music_technology)#Piezoelectric_pickups" title="Pickup (music technology)">piezoacoustic pick-ups</a>. He used these two custom Les Pauls on the <a href="/wiki/Time_Machine_Tour" title="Time Machine Tour">Time Machine Tour</a>. These guitars are also available through Gibson, in a viceroy Brown or Crimson colour. Lifeson used these two guitars heavily on the tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the 2012-2013 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwork_Angels_tour&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clockwork Angels tour (page does not exist)">Clockwork Angels tour</a>, Gibson built an Alex Lifeson Axcess model in black which became Lifeson's primary guitar for much of the show. For all acoustic work, he played one of his Axcess guitars using the piezo pick-ups; no acoustic guitars were used at all in the Clockwork Angels show.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Paul_Reed_Smith_acoustic_signature_guitar">Paul Reed Smith acoustic signature guitar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Paul Reed Smith acoustic signature guitar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For the 2015 R40 Tour, Lifeson used his signature acoustic guitar model by <a href="/wiki/PRS_Guitars" title="PRS Guitars">Paul Reed Smith</a>. The guitar is currently available for private stock order.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gibson_R40_Signature_Les_Paul_Axcess">Gibson R40 Signature Les Paul Axcess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gibson R40 Signature Les Paul Axcess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Gibson introduced an Alex Lifeson R40 Les Paul Axcess signature guitar in June 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This is a limited edition with 50 guitars signed and played by Lifeson, and another 250 available without the signature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amplification">Amplification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Amplification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Hughes &amp; Kettner introduced an Alex Lifeson signature series amplifier; Lifeson donates his royalties from the sale of these signature models to <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Lifeson abandoned his signature Triamps in favour for custom-built Lerxst Omega <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Amplification" title="Marshall Amplification">Silver Jubilee</a> clones, handmade by Mojotone in Burgaw, NC and <a href="/wiki/Mesa_Boogie_Mark_Series" title="Mesa Boogie Mark Series">Mesa/Boogie Mark V</a> heads. He still uses the Hughes &amp; Kettner Coreblades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Effects_unit" title="Effects unit">effects</a>, Lifeson is known to use <a href="/wiki/Chorus_effect" title="Chorus effect">chorus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_shifting" title="Phase shifting">phase shifting</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flanger" title="Flanger">flanging</a>. Throughout his career, he has used well-known pedals such as the <a href="/wiki/Electro-Harmonix" title="Electro-Harmonix">Electro-Harmonix Electric Mistress</a> flanger, the <a href="/wiki/Boss_Corporation" title="Boss Corporation">BOSS CE-1</a> chorus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunlop_crybaby" title="Dunlop crybaby">Dunlop crybaby</a> wah, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lifeson and his guitar technician <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Appleton_(guitar_technician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scott Appleton (guitar technician) (page does not exist)">Scott Appleton</a> have discussed in interviews Lifeson's use of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fractal_Audio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fractal Audio (page does not exist)">Fractal Audio</a>'s Axe-FX, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mainstage_(software)" title="Mainstage (software)">MainStage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Native_Instruments" title="Native Instruments">Native Instruments</a>' Guitar Rig.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_instruments_played">Other instruments played</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other instruments played">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stringed_instruments">Stringed instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Stringed instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to acoustic and electric guitars, Lifeson has also played <a href="/wiki/Mandola" title="Mandola">mandola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bouzouki" title="Bouzouki">bouzouki</a> on recent Rush studio albums, including <i><a href="/wiki/Test_for_Echo" title="Test for Echo">Test for Echo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vapor_Trails" title="Vapor Trails">Vapor Trails</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Snakes_%26_Arrows" title="Snakes &amp; Arrows">Snakes &amp; Arrows</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_instruments">Electronic instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Lifeson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Electronic instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During live Rush performances, Lifeson uses <a href="/wiki/MIDI_controller" title="MIDI controller">MIDI controllers</a> that enables him to use his free hands and feet to trigger sounds from <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">digital samplers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizers</a>, without taking his hands off his guitar. (Prior to this, Lifeson used <a href="/wiki/Moog_Taurus" title="Moog Taurus">Moog Taurus</a> Bass Pedals before they were replaced by Korg MIDI pedals in the 1980s.) Lifeson and his bandmates share a desire to accurately depict songs from their albums when playing live performances. Toward this goal, beginning in the late 1980s the band equipped their live performances with a capacious rack of samplers. The band members use these samplers in real-time to recreate the sounds of non-traditional instruments, <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompaniments</a>, vocal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmonies" title="Harmonies">harmonies</a>, and other sound "events" that are familiarly heard on the studio versions of the songs. In live performances, the band members share duties throughout most songs, with each member triggering certain sounds with his available limbs, while playing his primary instrument(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onstagemag.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-onstagemag.com-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
