<p><b>Alex Chola</b> (5 June 1956 – 27 April 1993) was a <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambian</a> footballer and coach. Voted Zambian Footballer of the Year in 1976, he is regarded as one of the greatest Zambian players in history and is the country’s second highest goal scorer after <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Chitalu" title="Godfrey Chitalu">Godfrey Chitalu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatest_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-greatest-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-records_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-records-3">[3]</a></sup> He made a mark at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mufulira_Blackpool_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mufulira Blackpool F.C. (page does not exist)">Mufulira Blackpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_Dynamos_F.C." title="Power Dynamos F.C.">Power Dynamos</a> before becoming Dynamos coach and winning the Coach of the Year award in 1992. Chola died in a plane crash off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a> on 27 April 1993.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Chola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chola was born in <a href="/wiki/Lubumbashi" title="Lubumbashi">Lubumbashi</a> where his father went to work and he reportedly played for local club Solbena F.C. before moving to <a href="/wiki/Ndola" title="Ndola">Ndola</a> as a teenager. After a brief stay, he left for <a href="/wiki/Mufulira" title="Mufulira">Mufulira</a> and signed with Division II side <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mufulira_Blackpool_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mufulira Blackpool F.C. (page does not exist)">Mufulira Blackpool</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mufulira_Blackpool">Mufulira Blackpool</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Chola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mufulira Blackpool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chola’s exceptional skills were evident from an early age and he forged a sharp frontline with giant striker John ‘Fuso’ Lengwe, Lee Mulenga and player-coach <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Kaushi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Simon Kaushi (page does not exist)">Simon “Kaodi” Kaushi</a>. Blackpool won promotion to the Zambian top league and in April 1975, Chola ended defending league champions <a href="/wiki/Green_Buffaloes_F.C." title="Green Buffaloes F.C.">Green Buffaloes</a> long unbeaten run with a clinical finish at Blackpool’s home ground Kamuchanga Stadium in Mufulira. It was the first league defeat in over a year for Buffaloes, who had won the title in 1974 without losing a single game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July of that same year, Blackpool captain John Botha was removed and suspended indefinitely for issuing damaging press statements against Kaushi, and Chola was named the new skipper at the age of 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chola was linked with several clubs during his time at Blackpool. <a href="/wiki/Mufulira_Wanderers_F.C." title="Mufulira Wanderers F.C.">Mufulira Wanderers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nkana_F.C." title="Nkana F.C.">Rokana United</a> and Buffaloes were all after his signature at some point but Blackpool managed to hold on to their prize asset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In Buffaloes' case, the player changed his mind when told he had to enlist with their sponsors Zambia Army, as he wanted to maintain his civilian status, and the move to Wanderers did not materialize as it was believed that such a move would have soured relationships between the two clubs.</p>
<p>In 1976, Blackpool won a hard-fought 4-3 <a href="/wiki/Zambian_Cup" title="Zambian Cup">Independence Cup</a> final win over town-mates <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Butondo_Western_Tigers_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Butondo Western Tigers F.C. (page does not exist)">Butondo Western Tigers</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dag_Hammarskj%C3%B6ld_Stadium" title="Dag Hammarskjöld Stadium">Dag Hammarskjöld Stadium</a> in Ndola, with Chola creating two goals and scoring the winner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It was Blackpool’s second and last trophy following their <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zambian_Heinrich_Chibuku_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zambian Heinrich Chibuku Cup (page does not exist)">Chibuku Cup</a> win of 1972. Despite a mid-table finish, Chola finished the season as Blackpool’s top scorer with 20 goals and was named Zambia’s Footballer of the year.</p>
<p>In 1978, Blackpool made a strong start going unbeaten in the first 8 games of the season and topping the table. They were still leading with 4 games to go but were pipped to the title by Wanderers, who finished on top by a single point, when Blackpool lost all three cases which were before the <a href="/wiki/Football_Association_of_Zambia" title="Football Association of Zambia">Football Association of Zambia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Football_Association_of_Zambia" title="Football Association of Zambia">FAZ</a>) disciplinary committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In the first one, they lost points to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ndola_United_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ndola United F.C. (page does not exist)">Ndola United</a> at Kamuchanga Stadium when a ball blew up and a replacement could not be found while a 2-2 draw with <a href="/wiki/Red_Arrows_F.C." title="Red Arrows F.C.">Red Arrows</a> which was abandoned when Blackpool walked off the pitch after Arrows were awarded a dubious penalty had its result upheld. The third case involved a former Blackpool player who featured for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kabwe_United_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kabwe United F.C. (page does not exist)">Kabwe United</a> while still on their books but the FAZ ruled against the Mufulira side.</p>
<p>The second-place finish was still an achievement but it was evident Chola had been carrying the team for a long time and fans wondered how much longer he would stay at Kamuchanga Stadium. Chola's amazing footwork, ball juggling skills and precise passes unsurprisginly led to the nicknames ‘Computer’ and ‘Master Dribbler.’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greatest_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-greatest-2">[2]</a></sup> He inspired a young <a href="/wiki/Kalusha_Bwalya" title="Kalusha Bwalya">Kalusha Bwalya</a> who later remarked that Chola "could do incredible things with the ball. It’s a pity there was no video at the time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After their second-place finish in 1978, Blackpool were expected to make the final step and capture their first ever league title, however, the team was caught in a relegation battle right from the start and finished second bottom but was saved from relegation when the FAZ increased the number of teams from 16 to 18.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_Dynamos">Power Dynamos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Chola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Power Dynamos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 1979, Chola and his family vanished from Mufulira and were reported to be in <a href="/wiki/Kitwe" title="Kitwe">Kitwe</a>, a move which upset Blackpool officials who felt betrayed and did not know which club had snagged their star as so many clubs were after him .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, Chola denied that he had deserted Blackpool and said he was merely on leave in Mufulira, despite the fact that a big truck was seen transporting his household goods from Mufulira. “I am a big boy and an international footballer. When I want to leave Blackpool, I will inform the public about my intentions to do so.’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>It soon emerged that Chola had been given a job by Copperbelt Power Company, the sponsors of Power Dynamos and this led to Blackpool banning the player indefinitely ‘for deserting the club,’ which ruled him out of international matches. This prompted Chola to write a letter of appeal to the FAZ who ruled in his favour and paved the way for his move to Arthur Davies Stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He made an instant impression, scoring twice on his debut, the Champion of Champions Cup semi-final on 3 February 1980 in a 2-0 win against Green Buffaloes. A week later, he scored the third goal in the final, a 3-0 triumph over Mufulira Wanderers in Ndola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ho collected more silverware later that year when Dynamos beat Buffaloes 2-0 to win the <a href="/wiki/Zambian_Cup" title="Zambian Cup">Independence Cup</a>, and ended the season as the club’s top scorer. The following year, Dynamos retained the Champions of Champions Cup and made their debut in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Africa_Cup_Winners_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Africa Cup Winners Cup (page does not exist)">Africa Cup Winners Cup</a> only to be eliminated by Sekondi Hasacaas of Ghana in the quarter-finals. In 1982, they won the Independence Cup for the third time in 4 years, but the league eluded them as they ended the season as runners-up to Kitwe rivals Nkana Red Devils. Dynamos however had a fine run in the Africa Cup Winners Cup, reaching the final only to lose 4-0 on aggregate to Egyptian club Arab Contractors. Chola ended the season as second highest goal-scorer in the Zambian league, runner-up to club-mate <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kaumba" title="Peter Kaumba">Peter Kaumba</a>.</p>
<p>Chola was in the Dynamos team that won the Rothmans International trophy in the 6 nations African Club soccer tournament in <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">Abidjan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a> in May 1983, after beating home team Stella Abidjan of Ivory Coast 2-1, FC 105 from Gabon 4-1 and Nigeria’s Bendel Insurance 2-1. In the final, Dynamos drew 0-0 with Camerounian giants Tonnere Yaonde FC and won 5-4 on post match penalties with goalkeeper Blackwell Chalwe saving the decisive spot-kick after a goalless 90 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 1983, new Zambia national team coach <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wieslaw_Grabowski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wieslaw Grabowski (page does not exist)">Wieslaw Grabowski</a> described Chola and his Dynamos team-mate Kaumba as world class players, with enough potential to play for any division I side in Europe. Grabowski joined the legion of fans who admired Chola’s bull juggling skills but he noted that his team-mates did not understand his crisp and timely passes. He further said that although Chola lacked speed, he was an intelligent player who could read the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> At the end of the season, he was runner-up once again to Ashlos Melu on the scorers’ chart. In December, Chola and Kaumba signed three-year professional contracts with Africa Sports of Ivory Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, the two returned home in July 1984 and it turned out that a major sponsor of Africa Sports had pulled out so this changed the fortunes of the club and they decided to return to Zambia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The duo arrived just in time to lend a hand to Dynamos final push for the league championship. Chola missed a penalty when Dynamos suffered their only league loss in October 1984, a 2-0 defeat to eventual runners-up Mufulira Wanderers but Dynamos avenged this a week later when they whipped Wanderers 4-1 in the reverse fixture at Arthur Davies Stadium. This helped Dynamos to the title for the first time in their history.</p>
<p>Dynamos were not finished as they crushed <a href="/wiki/Kabwe_Warriors_F.C." title="Kabwe Warriors F.C.">Kabwe Warriors</a> 4-2 in the semi-final of the Champion of Champions with Chola grabbing a hat-trick, and when they met Wanderers in the final, Dynamos prevailed 2-0.</p>
<p>In August, the following year, Dynamos lost a BP Challenge Cup first round match to Division I side City of Luska 3-0 which led to coach Freddie Mwila being sidelined amid reports of dissension from the players who were unhappy with his attitude towards them. He singled out Chola as a possible candidate for his job and Chola stepped up as player-coach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-new-20">[20]</a></sup> although Dynamos surrendered the title to bitter rivals Nkana by a single point when they failed to beat their cross-town rivals in their penultimate league game at home. They also lost the Champion of Champions trophy to Wanderers. The players though, said they were very happy with Chola’s training methods. Chola's younger brother Pascal Kunda played as a midfielder for Ndola United and joined Power Dynamos in 1985.</p>
<p>At the end of the season, Chola called time on his glorious career to focus on coaching. He however attempted a comeback in November 1987 in the Independence Cup final against Kabwe Warriors in Lusaka. He showed every bit of his exciting old self and had a hand in Dynamos’ first goal scored by Pearson Mwanza but tragedy struck when he was carried off the pitch with a fractured tibia of the left leg in the 28th minute after a late challenge by Warriors’ <a href="/wiki/Whiteson_Changwe" title="Whiteson Changwe">Whiteson Changwe</a>. Dynamos lost the final 3-2 which proved to be the last game he ever played. The match was also remembered for a 63-minute first half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Chola described the incident as unfortunate and not deliberate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_team">National team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Chola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chola first donned national colours in September 1974 when he featured for Zambia ‘B’ in a match against Brazilian club Operario in Lusaka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He marked his full international debut against Malawi on 14 April 1975 with a goal in a 3-3 draw during a CAN qualifier in <a href="/wiki/Lusaka" title="Lusaka">Lusaka</a>.</p>
<p>Chola starred in Zambia’s 1976 Olympic Games qualification run, scoring 6 times as Zambia qualified to Montreal 1976. However, Zambia withdrew for political reasons so Chola had to wait 4 more years to make his Olympics bow. In between, he featured for Zambia at <a href="/wiki/1978_African_Cup_of_Nations" title="1978 African Cup of Nations">CAN 78</a> where Zambia failed to progress from a group which featured <a href="/wiki/Ghana_national_football_team" title="Ghana national football team">Ghana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria_national_football_team" title="Nigeria national football team">Nigeria</a>. In 1980, Zambia were knocked out by <a href="/wiki/Egypt_national_football_team" title="Egypt national football team">Egypt</a> 6-2 on aggregate who subsequently withdrew in protest against the invasion of <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a> by <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> so Zambia took their place. He made two appearances at <a href="/wiki/1980_Summer_Olympics" title="1980 Summer Olympics">Moscow</a> 1980 as Zambia were eliminated at the group stage.</p>
<p>In November 1981, Chola was among 8 national team players who were injured when the bus in which they were travelling ploughed into a tree 15 km from Chingola, on their way from a practice match in Chililabombwe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Chola sustained shin and knee injuries and was out of action for a month but was fit enough for the <a href="/wiki/1982_African_Cup_of_Nations" title="1982 African Cup of Nations">CAN</a> in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>.</p>
<p>Chola made his second CAN appearance at Libya 1982 where Zambia reached the semi-finals only to lose to the hosts 2-1 after taking an early lead. The following year, Zambia played <a href="/wiki/Sudan_national_football_team" title="Sudan national football team">Sudan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a> in a CAN qualifier and lost 2-1, leaving them with the seemingly achievable aim of winning by one goal to qualify to the next tround. Despite sustained pressure in the second leg in Ndola, they could only manage a scoreless draw, with Chola missing a 55th-minute penalty, for which he came in for a lot of criticism from fans.</p>
<p>Chola put the disappointment behind him and later that year, starred as Zambia beat Egypt 1-0 in the first leg of an Olympics Games qualifier in Lusaka, with Peter Kaumba scoring a header off his free-kick. Zambia lost the second leg 2-0 and bowed out of the race, and this would be Chola’s last game for almost two years.</p>
<p>In August 1985, Zambian coach <a href="/wiki/Brightwell_Banda" title="Brightwell Banda">Brightwell Banda</a>, looking for some much needed creativity in midfield recalled Chola who was by that time player-coach at his club Power Dynamos for the two legged knockout qualifier against <a href="/wiki/Nigeria_national_football_team" title="Nigeria national football team">Nigeria</a>. He made his comeback when Zambia held the Eagles 0-0 in <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a> and the "Master-Dribbler" turned on the magic as he pulled the strings in midfield and Zambia ran out 1-0 winners to dump Nigeria out of the <a href="/wiki/1986_African_Cup_of_Nations#Qualifying_Tournament_Second_Round" title="1986 African Cup of Nations">1986 CAN</a> race.</p>
<p>After another appearance against in a friendly against <a href="/wiki/Botswana_national_football_team" title="Botswana national football team">Botswana</a>, Chola travelled with the Zambian team to Zimbabwe to defend their <a href="/wiki/CECAFA_Cup" title="CECAFA Cup">CECAFA Cup</a>. A 2-2 draw with the hosts and a 3-0 reverse to Uganda put paid to Zambia’s chances. Chola featured in both games but was substituted in both games and the coach revealed afterwards that he had been suffering from illness. This turned out to be his last game for Zambia. He made 100 appearances (including 12 Olympics related matches) and scored 43 goals, making him one of Zambia’s most capped players second only to <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Chitalu" title="Godfrey Chitalu">Godfrey Chitalu</a> in goalscoring.</p>
<p>In 2006, he was selected by <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_African_Football" title="Confederation of African Football">CAF</a> as one of the best 200 African football players of the last 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coaching_career">Coaching career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Chola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Coaching career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chola got involved in coaching as early as 1976, coaching youngsters in the Buseko Community Centre Club as trainer of the U-14 team which featured <a href="/wiki/Kalusha_Bwalya" title="Kalusha Bwalya">Kalusha Bwalya</a>.</p>
<p>In April 1983, Dynamos coach Freddie Mwila spoke of some ‘first-choice’ players coercing junior colleagues in a futile attempt to oust him from his position. ‘A star is only as good as his last game,’ he said. ‘Some of these senior players have been below par so far and (Technical Advisor) Bill McGarry and myself have been shouting at them about this. After all, I get paid for shouting at them because that is my job.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1985, Dynamos lost a BP Challenge Cup first round match to Division I side <a href="/wiki/City_of_Lusaka_F.C." title="City of Lusaka F.C.">City of Lusaka</a> 3-0 which upset Mwila who suggested a “change of coaches may bring some spark into the team because he may have been telling them the same old techniques.” He singled out Chola as a possible candidate for his job. There were reports of dissension from the players as well, unhappy with Mwila’s attitude towards them. He resigned and Chola took over,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-new-20">[20]</a></sup> although Dynamos surrendered the title to bitter rivals <a href="/wiki/Nkana_F.C." title="Nkana F.C.">Nkana Red Devils</a> and the Champion of Champions trophy to Wanderers. The players though, said they were very happy with Chola’s training methods.</p>
<p>Five years after leaving Dynamos, Mwila made a sensational return as coach amid reports of some players boycotting training upon hearing the news. Chola was relegated to the role of assistant coach with Scotsman Jim Bone as Technical Advisor. Rechristened ‘Power 90,’ Dynamos won 3 trophies in 1990, just missing out on the league to Nkana.</p>
<p>The following year, Dynamos bagged the big one when they overcame Nigeria’s Benue Cement Company Lions 5-4 on aggregate to scoop the Africa Cup Winners Cup, the only Zambian team to have achieved that feat to date. Dynamos had gone down 2-3 in the first leg in Nigeria but were able to win 3-1 at home despite being a goal down at half-time. Dynamos also added the league title for a historic double.</p>
<p>That same year, Chola attended a coaching course in Germany where he was awarded with an advanced diploma in coaching. The following season, he re-assumed coaching responsibilities after the departures of Bone and Mwila and Dynamos embarked on the defence of their Cup Winners Cup trophy. After negotiating their way past Mochudi Centre Chiefs of <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> in the second round, Dynamos players pressed for higher allowances and boycotted a trip to <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a> which led to Management suspending the entire team for the rest of the season save for goalkeeper Martin Mwamba, defender Webby Chilufya and midfielder Aggrey Chiyangi.</p>
<p>Chola therefore had to make do with a team full of youngsters from the reserve team, players like Winter Mumba, Kenan Simambe, Douglas Mwamba, Changa Chaiwa and Kellies Mwaba. To everyone’s surprise, Dynamos finished a credible 5th and only lost 8 league games. Chola was rewarded with the Coach of the Year award for the 1992 season.</p>
<p>Following Zambia’s surprise defeat to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_national_football_team" title="Madagascar national football team">Madagascar</a> in a <a href="/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1994 FIFA World Cup">World Cup Qualifier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antananarivo" title="Antananarivo">Antananarivo</a> in December 1992, coach <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Ndhlovu" title="Samuel Ndhlovu">Samuel Ndhlovu</a> was sacked and <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Chitalu" title="Godfrey Chitalu">Godfrey Chitalu</a> was appointed national team coach with Chola as his assistant. The two put together an exciting team which was tipped to make it all the way to the <a href="/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1994 FIFA World Cup">1994 World Cup</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> but on their way to <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> the team met its fate in the horrific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1993_Zambia_national_football_team_air_disaster" title="1993 Zambia national football team air disaster">air disaster</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alex_Chola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 27 April 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Zambia_national_football_team" title="Zambia national football team">Zambia national team</a> traveled to <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> for the first of their <a href="/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup_qualification" title="1994 FIFA World Cup qualification">1994 FIFA World Cup qualification</a> games in the group stage. The team's mode of transportation was a <a href="/wiki/Zambian_Air_Force" title="Zambian Air Force">Zambian Air Force</a> plane. After refueling in <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">Libreville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, the plane developed problems and plunged into the sea. Thirty people on board including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Mwape&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Mwape (page does not exist)">Michael Mwape</a>, the president of the <a href="/wiki/Football_Association_of_Zambia" title="Football Association of Zambia">FAZ</a>, Chola, Chitalu and eighteen players died in the accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chola was survived by a wife Monica and five children. Alex Chola road in Lusaka is named after him in his honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
