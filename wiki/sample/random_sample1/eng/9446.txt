<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inkosi" title="Inkosi">Inkosi</a> <b>Albert John Lutuli</b> (commonly spelled <b>Luthuli</b>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> c. 1898 – 21 July 1967), also known by his <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> name <b>Mvumbi</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> <a href="/wiki/Teacher" title="Teacher">teacher</a>, activist, <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> winner, and <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a>. Luthuli was elected president of the <a href="/wiki/African_National_Congress" title="African National Congress">African National Congress</a> (ANC), at the time an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbrella_organisation" title="Umbrella organisation">umbrella organisation</a> that led opposition to the white minority government in South Africa. He was awarded the 1960 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> for his role in the <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">non-violent</a> struggle against <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a>. He was the first African, and the first person from outside Europe and the Americas, to be awarded the Nobel Peace Prize.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The third son of <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist</a> missionary John Bunyan Lutuli and Mtonya Gumede, Albert Lutuli was born near <a href="/wiki/Bulawayo" title="Bulawayo">Bulawayo</a> in what was then called <a href="/wiki/Southern_Rhodesia" title="Southern Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a> (present day Zimbabwe), around 1898. His father died, and he and his mother returned to her ancestral home of <a href="/wiki/Groutville" title="Groutville">Groutville</a> in <a href="/wiki/KwaDukuza" title="KwaDukuza">KwaDukuza</a> (Stanger), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal_Province" title="KwaZulu-Natal Province">Natal</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. He stayed with his uncle, Martin Lutuli, who was at that time the elected chief of the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Zulu</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a> inhabiting the <a href="/wiki/Mission_(station)" title="Mission (station)">mission</a> reserve area now covered by the <a href="/wiki/Umzinyathi_District_Municipality" title="Umzinyathi District Municipality">Umzinyathi District Municipality</a>. Lutuli attended the <a href="/wiki/Adams_College" title="Adams College">Adams College</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-historic-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching">Teaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Teaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On completing a teaching course at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edendale,_South_Africa" title="Edendale, South Africa">Edendale</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Pietermaritzburg" title="Pietermaritzburg">Pietermaritzburg</a>, Lutuli accepted the post of principal and only teacher at a primary school in rural Blaauwbosch, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle,_KwaZulu-Natal" title="Newcastle, KwaZulu-Natal">Newcastle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KwaZulu_Natal" title="KwaZulu Natal">Natal</a>. Here Lutuli was confirmed in the Methodist Church and became a lay preacher. In 1920 he received a government bursary to attend a higher teachers' training course at Adams College, and subsequently joined the training college staff, teaching alongside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z._K._Mathews" title="Z. K. Mathews">Z. K. Mathews</a>, who was then head of the Adams College High School. To provide financial support for his mother, he declined a scholarship to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Fort_Hare" title="University of Fort Hare">University of Fort Hare</a>.</p>
<p>In 1928 he became secretary of the African Teacher's Association and in 1933 its president. He was also active in missionary work.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tribal_chief">Tribal chief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tribal chief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1933 the tribal elders asked Lutuli to become chief of the Zulu tribe in succession to his uncle. For two years he hesitated, but accepted the call in early 1936 and became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chieftain" title="Chieftain">chieftain</a>. He held this position until he was removed from his office by the Apartheid government in 1953. Their having done so notwithstanding, amongst his people he retained the use of the dignity "chief" as a pre-nominal style for the remainder of his life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-Apartheid_activist">Anti-Apartheid activist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anti-Apartheid activist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1936 the government disenfranchised the only black Africans who had voting rights at that time — those in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Province" title="Cape Province">Cape Province</a>. In 1948 the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_(South_Africa)" title="National Party (South Africa)">Nationalist Party</a>, which was in control of the government, adopted the policy of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> (apartness) and over the next decade the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dompas" title="Dompas">Pass Laws</a> were tightened.</p>
<p>In 1944 Lutuli joined the <a href="/wiki/African_National_Congress" title="African National Congress">African National Congress</a> (ANC). In 1945 he was elected to the Committee of the <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu" title="KwaZulu">KwaZulu</a> Provincial Division of ANC and in 1951 to the presidency of the Division. The next year he joined with other ANC leaders in organizing nonviolent campaigns to defy discriminatory laws.</p>
<p>The government, charging Lutuli with a conflict of interest, demanded that he withdraw his membership in ANC or forfeit his office as tribal chief. Refusing to do either, he was dismissed from his chieftainship.</p>
<p>A month later Lutuli was elected president-general of ANC, formally nominated by the future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_Africanist_Congress" title="Pan Africanist Congress">Pan Africanist Congress</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Potlako_Leballo" title="Potlako Leballo">Potlako Leballo</a>. Responding immediately, the government imposed two two-year bans on Lutuli's movement. When the second ban expired in 1956, he attended an ANC conference only to be arrested and <a href="/wiki/Treason_trial" title="Treason trial">charged with treason</a> a few months later, along with 155 others. In December 1957, after nearly a year in custody during the preliminary hearings, Lutuli was released and the charges against him and sixty-four of his compatriots were dropped.</p>
<p>He stood close to the <a href="/wiki/International_Fellowship_of_Reconciliation" title="International Fellowship of Reconciliation">International Fellowship of Reconciliation</a> that in 1957 opened a branch in Southern Rhodesia (now known as Zimbabwe.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrittain1964197_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrittain1964197-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bans">Bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Another five-year ban confined him to a 15-mile (24 km) radius of his home. The ban was temporarily lifted while he testified at the continuing treason trials. It was lifted again in March 1960, to permit his arrest for publicly burning his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dompas" title="Dompas">pass</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Sharpeville_massacre" title="Sharpeville massacre">Sharpeville massacre</a>. In the ensuing state of emergency he was arrested, found guilty, fined, given a suspended jail sentence, and finally returned to Groutville. One final time the ban was lifted, this time for ten days in early December 1961, to permit Lutuli and his wife to attend the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> ceremonies in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, an award described by <i>Die Transvaler</i> as "an inexplicable pathological phenomenon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ANC">ANC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: ANC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lutuli's leadership of the ANC covered the period of violent disputes between the party's "Africanist" and "Charterist" wings. Africanist critics claim Lutuli was peripherialized in Natal and the Transvaal ANC Provincial branch and its Communist Party (CPSA officially dissolved 1950 but secretly reconstituted 1953 as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SACP" title="SACP">SACP</a>) allies took advantage of this situation. Lutuli did not see the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Charter" title="Freedom Charter">Freedom Charter</a> before it was adopted by acclaim at <a href="/wiki/Kliptown" title="Kliptown">Kliptown</a> in 1955. After reading the document and realising the ANC, despite its numerical superiority, had been subordinated to one vote in a five-member multiracial and trade union "Congress Alliance", Lutuli rejected the Charter but then later accepted it partly to counter the more radical Africanist wing whom he likened to black nazis. In 1959 the Africanists split from the ANC over the issue of the Freedom Charter and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Tambo" title="Oliver Tambo">Oliver Tambo</a>'s 1958 rewriting of the ANC Constitution, founding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_Africanist_Congress" title="Pan Africanist Congress">Pan Africanist Congress</a> (PAC). The PAC posed a serious challenge to the ANC until its <a href="/wiki/Azanian_People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="Azanian People's Liberation Army">military wing</a> was destroyed at Itumbi camp, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chunya" title="Chunya">Chunya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> in March 1980.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Umkhonto_we_Sizwe">Umkhonto we Sizwe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lutuli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Umkhonto we Sizwe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 1961, without Lutuli's sanction, <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> of the Provincial ANC publicly launched <a href="/wiki/Umkhonto_we_Sizwe" title="Umkhonto we Sizwe">Umkhonto we Sizwe</a> at the All In Conference, where delegates from several movements had convened to discuss cooperation. Mandela's charisma and the global publicity surrounding his trial and imprisonment upstaged Lutuli, who grew increasingly despondent in isolation. (In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Walk_to_Freedom_(book)" title="Long Walk to Freedom (book)">Mandela's autobiography</a>, he claims that Lutuli was consulted and consented before the formation of Umkhonto we Sizwe.)</p>
<p>In 1962 he was elected <a href="/wiki/Rector_of_the_University_of_Glasgow" title="Rector of the University of Glasgow">Rector of the University of Glasgow</a> by the students, serving until 1965. Since he was banned from travelling to Glasgow the Luthuli Scholarship Fund was set up by the Student Representative Council to enable a black South African student to study at Glasgow University.</p>
<p>In 1962 he published an autobiography entitled <i>Let My People Go</i>.</p>
<p>A fourth ban, to run for five years confining Lutuli to the immediate vicinity of his home, was issued in May 1964, to run concurrently with the third ban.</p>
<p>In 1966, he was visited by <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>, who was visiting South Africa at the time. The two discussed the ANC's struggle. Senator Kennedy's visit to the country, and his meeting with Lutuli in particular, caused an increase of world awareness of the plight of black South Africans.</p>
<p>In July 1967, at the age of 69, Lutuli was fatally injured in an accident near his home in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanger" title="Stanger">Stanger</a>.</p>
