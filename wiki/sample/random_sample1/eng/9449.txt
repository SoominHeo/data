<p><b>Albert Hofmann</b> (11 January 1906 – 29 April 2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maps_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-maps-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraphobit_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraphobit-2">[2]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> scientist known best for being the first person to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">synthesize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">ingest</a>, and learn of the <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">psychedelic effects</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid_diethylamide" title="Lysergic acid diethylamide">lysergic acid diethylamide</a> (LSD). Hofmann was also the first person to isolate, synthesize, and name the principal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychedelic_mushroom" title="Psychedelic mushroom">psychedelic mushroom</a> compounds <a href="/wiki/Psilocybin" title="Psilocybin">psilocybin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psilocin" title="Psilocin">psilocin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He authored more than 100 scientific articles and numerous books, including <i>LSD: Mein Sorgenkind</i> (<i>LSD: My Problem Child</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraphobit_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-telegraphobit-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2007, he shared first place, alongside <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>, in a list of the 100 greatest living geniuses, published by <i>The Telegraph</i> newspaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hofmann was born in <a href="/wiki/Baden,_Switzerland" title="Baden, Switzerland">Baden, Switzerland</a>, the first of four children to factory toolmaker Adolf Hofmann and his wife Elisabeth (born Elisabeth Schenk). Owing to his father's low income, Albert's godfather paid for his education. When his father became ill, Hofmann obtained a position as a commercial apprentice in concurrence with his studies. At the age of twenty, Hofmann began his <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> degree at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Z%C3%BCrich" title="University of Zürich">University of Zürich</a>, finishing three years later, in 1929. His main interest was the chemistry of plants and animals, and he later conducted important research on the chemical structure of the common animal substance <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a>, for which he received his <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a>, with distinction, in the spring of 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding his decision to pursue a career as a chemist, Hofmann provided insight during a speech he delivered to the 1996 Worlds of Consciousness Conference in Heidelberg, Germany:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>One often asks oneself what roles planning and chance play in the realization of the most important events in our lives. [...] This [career] decision was not easy for me. I had already taken a Latin matricular exam, and therefore a career in the humanities stood out most prominently in the foreground. Moreover, an artistic career was tempting. In the end, however, it was a problem of theoretical knowledge which induced me to study chemistry, which was a great surprise to all who knew me. Mystical experiences in childhood, in which Nature was altered in magical ways, had provoked questions concerning the essence of the external, material world, and chemistry was the scientific field which might afford insights into this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_LSD">Discovery of LSD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery of LSD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_LSD#Discovery" title="History of LSD">Discovery of LSD</a></div>
<p>Hofmann became an employee of the pharmaceutical-chemical department of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandoz" title="Sandoz">Sandoz</a> Laboratories (now a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Novartis" title="Novartis">Novartis</a>), located in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a> as a co-worker with professor <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Stoll" title="Arthur Stoll">Arthur Stoll</a>, founder and director of the pharmaceutical department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashback.se_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-flashback.se-7">[7]</a></sup> He began studying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medicinal_plant" title="Medicinal plant">medicinal plant</a> <a href="/wiki/Drimia_maritima" title="Drimia maritima">squill</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a> <a href="/wiki/Ergot" title="Ergot">ergot</a> as part of a program to purify and synthesize active constituents for use as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" title="Pharmaceuticals">pharmaceuticals</a>. His main contribution was to elucidate the chemical structure of the common nucleus of the <i><a href="/wiki/Scilla" title="Scilla">Scilla</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Glycoside" title="Glycoside">glycosides</a> (an active principal of <a href="/wiki/Drimia_maritima" title="Drimia maritima">Mediterranean Squill</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashback.se_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-flashback.se-7">[7]</a></sup> While researching <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid" title="Lysergic acid">lysergic acid</a> derivatives, Hofmann first synthesized LSD on 16 November 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The main intention of the synthesis was to obtain a respiratory and circulatory stimulant (an <a href="/wiki/Analeptic" title="Analeptic">analeptic</a>) with no effects on the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> in analogy to <a href="/wiki/Nikethamide" title="Nikethamide">nikethamide</a> (which is also a diethylamide) by introducing this <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid" title="Lysergic acid">lysergic acid</a>. It was set aside for five years, until 16 April 1943, when Hofmann decided to reexamine it. While re-synthesizing LSD, he accidentally touched his hand to his mouth, nose or possibly eye, accidentally ingesting a small amount<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and fortuitously discovered its powerful effects. He described what he felt as being:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>... affected by a remarkable restlessness, combined with a slight dizziness. At home I lay down and sank into a not unpleasant intoxicated[-]like condition, characterized by an extremely stimulated imagination. In a dreamlike state, with eyes closed (I found the daylight to be unpleasantly glaring), I perceived an uninterrupted stream of fantastic pictures, extraordinary shapes with intense, kaleidoscopic play of colors. After some two hours this condition faded away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Three days later, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_LSD#.22Bicycle_Day.22" title="History of LSD">19 April 1943</a>, Hofmann intentionally ingested 250 micrograms of LSD. This day is now known as "Bicycle Day," because after starting to feel the effects of the drug as he rode home on a bike, and that became the first intentional acid trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_research">Further research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
<tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">

<td style="padding:0 10px;">It gave me an inner joy, an open mindedness, a gratefulness, open eyes and an internal sensitivity for the miracles of creation. [...] I think that in human evolution it has never been as necessary to have this substance LSD. It is just a tool to turn us into what we are supposed to be.</td>
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">”</td>
</tr>

<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right;"><cite>— Albert Hofmann, Speech on 100th birthday<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></cite></td>

</table>
<p>Hofmann, later, was to discover <a href="/wiki/4-Acetoxy-DET" title="4-Acetoxy-DET">4-Acetoxy-DET</a> (4-acetoxy-N,N-diethyltryptamine), also known as ethacetin, ethylacybin, or 4-AcO-DET, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallucinogenic" title="Hallucinogenic">hallucinogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Tryptamine" title="Tryptamine">tryptamine</a>. He first synthesized 4-AcO-DET in 1958 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandoz" title="Sandoz">Sandoz</a> lab. Hofmann became director of the natural products department at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandoz" title="Sandoz">Sandoz</a> and continued studying hallucinogenic substances found in Mexican <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushrooms</a> and other plants used by the aboriginal people there. This led to the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Psilocybin" title="Psilocybin">psilocybin</a>, the active agent of many "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychedelic_mushroom" title="Psychedelic mushroom">magic mushrooms</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bleidt_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bleidt-13">[13]</a></sup> Hofmann also became interested in the seeds of the Mexican <a href="/wiki/Morning_glory" title="Morning glory">morning glory</a> species <i><a href="/wiki/Turbina_corymbosa" title="Turbina corymbosa">Turbina corymbosa</a></i>, the seeds of which are called <i>ololiuhqui</i> by the natives. He was surprised to find the active compound of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ololiuhqui" title="Ololiuhqui">ololiuhqui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ergine" title="Ergine">ergine</a> (LSA, lysergic acid amide), to be closely related to LSD.</p>
<p>In 1962, he and his wife Anita Hofmann (née Guanella, sister of <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Guanella" title="Gustav Guanella">Gustav Guanella</a>, an important Swiss inventor) traveled to southern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> to search for the plant "Ska Maria Pastora" (Leaves of Mary the Shepherdess), later known as <i><a href="/wiki/Salvia_divinorum" title="Salvia divinorum">Salvia divinorum</a></i>. He was able to obtain samples of this plant, but never succeeded in identifying its active compound, which has since been identified as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diterpenoid" title="Diterpenoid">diterpenoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Salvinorin_A" title="Salvinorin A">salvinorin A</a>. In 1963, Hofmann attended the annual convention of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="World Academy of Arts and Sciences">World Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> (WAAS) in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Albert_Hofmann.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="297" data-file-width="400" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Albert_Hofmann.jpg/220px-Albert_Hofmann.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Albert_Hofmann.jpg/330px-Albert_Hofmann.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Albert_Hofmann.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Albert_Hofmann.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Albert Hofmann in 2006</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hofmann, interviewed shortly before his hundredth birthday, called LSD "medicine for the soul" and was frustrated by the worldwide prohibition of it. "It was used very successfully for ten years in psychoanalysis," he said, adding that the drug was misused by the <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">Counterculture of the 1960s</a>, and then criticized unfairly by the political establishment of the day. He conceded that it could be dangerous if misused, because a relatively high dose of 500 micrograms will have an extremely powerful psychoactive effect, especially if administered to a first-time user without adequate supervision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2007, Swiss medical authorities permitted psychotherapist Peter Gasser to perform psychotherapeutic experiments with patients who suffer from terminal-stage cancer and other deadly diseases. Completed in 2011, these experiments represent the first study of the therapeutic effects of LSD on humans in 35 years, as other studies have focused on the drug's effects on consciousness and body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Hofmann acclaimed the study, and continued to say he believed in the therapeutic benefits of LSD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2008, Hofmann wrote to <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a>, asking him to support this research; it is not known if Jobs responded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Multidisciplinary Association of Psychedelic Studies (MAPS) has supported research in the field of psychoanalysis using LSD, carrying on Hofmann's legacy and setting the groundwork for future studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hofmann was due to speak at the World Psychedelic Forum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> from 21 to 24 March 2008 but was forced to cancel because of bad health.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disposition_of_Hofmann.27s_papers">Disposition of Hofmann's papers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disposition of Hofmann's papers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After retiring from Sandoz in 1971, Hofmann was allowed to take his papers and research home. He gave his archives to the Albert Hofmann Foundation, a Los Angeles-based nonprofit, but the documents mostly sat in storage for years. The archives were sent to the San Francisco area in 2002 to be digitized, but that process was never completed. In 2013, the archives were sent to the Institute of Medical History in Bern, Switzerland, where they are currently being organized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hofmann died of a heart attack on 29 April 2008, surrounded by several grandchildren and great-grandchildren. He and his wife, Anita, who died in 2007, raised four children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_awards">Honors and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honors and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Swiss Federal Institute of Technology</i> (<a href="/wiki/ETH_Zurich" title="ETH Zurich">ETH Zurich</a>) honored him with the title D.Sc. (honoris causa) in 1969 together with <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Guanella" title="Gustav Guanella">Gustav Guanella</a>, his brother-in-law. In 1971 the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Confederation_of_Professional_Associations" title="Swedish Confederation of Professional Associations">Swedish Pharmaceutical Association</a> (<i>Sveriges Farmacevtförbund</i>) granted him the <a href="/wiki/Scheele_Award" title="Scheele Award">Scheele Award</a>, which commemorates the skills and achievements of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Pomerania" title="Swedish Pomerania">Swedish Pomeranian</a> chemist and pharmacist Carl Wilhelm Scheele.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
