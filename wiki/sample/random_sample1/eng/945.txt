<p><i><b>Eldorado</b></i> (subtitled <i><b>A Symphony by the Electric Light Orchestra</b></i>) is the fourth studio album by the <a href="/wiki/Electric_Light_Orchestra" title="Electric Light Orchestra">Electric Light Orchestra</a> (ELO), released in 1974.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eldorado_(Electric_Light_Orchestra_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eldorado</i> is the first complete ELO <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a>; bandleader Jeff Lynne conceived the storyline before he wrote any music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashback_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-flashback-1">[1]</a></sup> The plot follows a <a href="/wiki/Walter_Mitty" title="Walter Mitty">Walter Mitty</a>-like character who journeys into fantasy worlds via dreams, to escape the disillusionment of his mundane reality. Lynne began to write the album in response to criticisms from his father, a classical music lover, who said that Electric Light Orchestra's repertoire "had no tune".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The influence of the Beatles is prevalent, especially in the melody of the verse of "Mister Kingdom" which to some degree resembles the Beatles' "<a href="/wiki/Across_the_Universe" title="Across the Universe">Across the Universe</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eldorado_(Electric_Light_Orchestra_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eldorado</i> marks the first album on which <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Lynne" title="Jeff Lynne">Jeff Lynne</a> hired an orchestra; on previous albums, Lynne would overdub the strings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashback_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-flashback-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Louis_Clark" title="Louis Clark">Louis Clark</a> co-arranged (with Lynne and keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Tandy" title="Richard Tandy">Richard Tandy</a>) and conducted the strings, and would eventually go on to become a full member of the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashback_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-flashback-1">[1]</a></sup> The group's three resident string players continued to perform on recordings, however, and can be heard most prominently on the songs "Boy Blue" and "Laredo Tornado". <a href="/wiki/Mike_de_Albuquerque" title="Mike de Albuquerque">Mike de Albuquerque</a> departed early on in the recording process, as touring made him feel separated from his family. Lynne plays most of, if not all, the bass tracks and backing vocals for the album, even though de Albuquerque received credit. Nevertheless, de Albuquerque was involved in some of the released album, if not as prominently as previous albums. <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Groucutt" title="Kelly Groucutt">Kelly Groucutt</a> replaced de Albuquerque for the subsequent tour, when cellist <a href="/wiki/Melvyn_Gale" title="Melvyn Gale">Melvyn Gale</a> also joined. "Eldorado Finale" is heavily orchestrated much like "Eldorado Overture". Jeff Lynne said of the song, "I like the heavy chords and the slightly daft ending, where you hear the double bass players packing up their basses, because they wouldn't play another millisecond past the allotted moment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashback_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-flashback-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>"<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Get_It_Out_of_My_Head" title="Can't Get It Out of My Head">Can't Get It Out of My Head</a>" was released as a single (with "Illusions in G Major" as the flipside) and was a success in the US. An edited version of "<a href="/wiki/Boy_Blue_(Electric_Light_Orchestra_song)" title="Boy Blue (Electric Light Orchestra song)">Boy Blue</a>" was released as the album's second single, but failed to make any commercial impact. The album was certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Gold</a> in the United States soon after its release. The album and singles, however, failed to find a wide audience in the band's native United Kingdom.</p>
<p>In 1978, the filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Anger" title="Kenneth Anger">Kenneth Anger</a> re-released his 1954 film <i><a href="/wiki/Inauguration_of_the_Pleasure_Dome" title="Inauguration of the Pleasure Dome">Inauguration of the Pleasure Dome</a></i>, using <i>Eldorado</i> as the soundtrack. In July 2010, the album was named one of <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(magazine)" title="Classic Rock (magazine)"><i>Classic Rock</i> magazine</a>'</i>s "50 Albums That Built Prog Rock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
