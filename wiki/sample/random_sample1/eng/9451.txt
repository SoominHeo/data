<p>The <b>Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States federal government</a> complex located at 200 N.W. 5th Street in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Oklahoma_City" title="Downtown Oklahoma City">Downtown</a> <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The building was the target of the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_bombing" title="Oklahoma City bombing">Oklahoma City bombing</a> on April 19, 1995, which killed 168 people, 19 of whom were children under the age of six.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Binomial.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Binomial.com-1">[1]</a></sup> Half of the building collapsed seconds after the truck bomb detonated. The remains of the building were imploded a month after the attack, and the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_National_Memorial" title="Oklahoma City National Memorial">Oklahoma City National Memorial</a> was built on the site.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_and_use">Construction and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_P._Murrah_Federal_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The federal building was designed by architect Wendell Locke of Locke, Wright and Associates and constructed using <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> in 1977<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> at a cost of $14.5 million. The building, named for federal judge <a href="/wiki/Alfred_P._Murrah" title="Alfred P. Murrah">Alfred P. Murrah</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> native, opened on March 2, 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 1990s, the building contained regional offices for the <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_Administration" title="Social Security Administration">Social Security Administration</a>, the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">United States Secret Service</a>, the Department of Veterans Affairs vocational rehabilitation counseling center, the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> (D.E.A.), and the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms</a> (ATF). The building also contained recruiting offices for both the <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">Army</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Corps" title="Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a>. It housed approximately 550 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The building also housed a children's day care center.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_bombing_plots">Prior bombing plots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_P._Murrah_Federal_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prior bombing plots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1983, members of the white supremacist group <a href="/wiki/The_Covenant,_The_Sword,_and_the_Arm_of_the_Lord" title="The Covenant, The Sword, and the Arm of the Lord">The Covenant, The Sword, and the Arm of the Lord</a> (CSA), including founder <a href="/wiki/James_Ellison_(polygamist)" title="James Ellison (polygamist)">James Ellison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Snell_(criminal)" title="Richard Snell (criminal)">Richard Snell</a> plotted to park "a van or trailer in front of the Federal Building and blow it up with rockets detonated by a timer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> While the CSA was building a rocket launcher to attack the building, the ordnance accidentally detonated in one of the member's hands. The CSA took this as <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">divine intervention</a> and called off the planned attack. Convicted of murder in an unrelated case, Richard Snell was executed on April 19, 1995, the same day the bombing of the federal building was carried out, after <a href="/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States">Associate Supreme Court Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Thomas" title="Clarence Thomas">Clarence Thomas</a> declined to hear further appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Snell reportedly spent his last day watching news coverage of the bombing and laughing to himself.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bombing">Bombing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_P._Murrah_Federal_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bombing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_bombing" title="Oklahoma City bombing">Oklahoma City bombing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="792" data-file-width="1140" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG/220px-Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG/330px-Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG/440px-Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Murrah_Building_Before_Demolition.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Murrah Building during the recovery effort</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At 9:02 a.m. local time on April 19, 1995, a Ryder rental truck, containing approximately 7,000 pounds (3175 kg) of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> fertilizer, <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a> was detonated in front of the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building, destroying a third of the building and causing severe damage to several other buildings located nearby. As a result of the massive explosion, 168 people were killed, including 19 children, and over 800 others were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was the deadliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist_attack" title="Terrorist attack">terrorist attack</a>, with the most property damage, on American soil before the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>. It remains the deadliest act of <a href="/wiki/Domestic_terrorism" title="Domestic terrorism">domestic terrorism</a> in American history.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Timothy_McVeigh" title="Timothy McVeigh">Timothy McVeigh</a> was found guilty of the attack in a jury trial and was sentenced to death. He was executed in 2001. A co-conspirator, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Nichols" title="Terry Nichols">Terry Nichols</a>, is serving multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sentences" title="Life sentences">life sentences</a> in a federal prison. Third and fourth subjects, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_and_Lori_Fortier" title="Michael and Lori Fortier">Michael Fortier</a> and his wife Lori, assisted in the plot. They testified against both McVeigh and Nichols in exchange for a 12-year prison term for Michael Fortier and <a href="/wiki/Witness_immunity" title="Witness immunity">immunity</a> for Lori. Fortier was released into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witness_protection_program" title="Witness protection program">witness protection program</a> in January 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>McVeigh said that he bombed the Murrah building on the two-year anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Waco_siege" title="Waco siege">Waco siege</a> in 1993 to retaliate for U.S. government actions there and at the siege at <a href="/wiki/Ruby_Ridge" title="Ruby Ridge">Ruby Ridge</a>. Before his execution, McVeigh said that he did not know a day care center was in the building and that, had he known, "It might have given me pause to switch targets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Binomial.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Binomial.com-1">[1]</a></sup> The FBI said that McVeigh scouted the interior of the building in December 1994 and likely knew of the day-care center before the bombing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artwork_in_the_building">Artwork in the building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_P._Murrah_Federal_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Artwork in the building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Lost:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Sky Ribbons: An Oklahoma Tribute</i>, (1978) Fiber sculpture by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhardt_Knodel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gerhardt Knodel (page does not exist)">Gerhardt Knodel</a></li>
<li><i>Columbines at Cascade Canyon</i>, Photograph by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_D._Edgar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Albert D. Edgar (page does not exist)">Albert D. Edgar</a></li>
<li><i>Winter Scene</i>, Photography by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Curt_Clyne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Curt Clyne (page does not exist)">Curt Clyne</a></li>
<li><i>Morning Mist</i>, Photograph by <a href="/wiki/David_Halpern" title="David Halpern">David Halpern</a></li>
<li><i>Charon's Sentinels</i>, Photograph by David Halpern</li>
<li><i>Soaring Currents</i>, Sisal and rayon textile by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karen_Chapnick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karen Chapnick (page does not exist)">Karen Chapnick</a></li>
<li><i>Monolith</i>, Porcelain sculpture by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Simons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frank Simons (page does not exist)">Frank Simons</a></li>
<li><i>Through the Looking Glass</i>, Wool Textile by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Burgress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anna Burgress (page does not exist)">Anna Burgress</a></li>
<li><i>Palm Tree Coil</i>, Bronze sculpture by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jerry_McMillan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jerry McMillan (page does not exist)">Jerry McMillan</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>An untitled acrylic sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Eversley" title="Fred Eversley">Fred Eversley</a> was severely damaged, but survived the blast.</p>

</div>
<p>Rescue and recovery efforts were concluded at 11:50 pm on May 1, with the bodies of all but three victims recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITN_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITN-11">[11]</a></sup> For safety reasons, the building was to be demolished shortly afterward. However, McVeigh's attorney, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jones_(attorney)" title="Stephen Jones (attorney)">Stephen Jones</a>, called for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_(legal)" title="Motion (legal)">motion</a> to delay the demolition until the defense team could examine the site in preparation for <a href="#Timothy_McVeigh">the trial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unfinished_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unfinished-12">[12]</a></sup> More than a month after the bombing, at 7:01 am on May 23, the Murrah Federal building was demolished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITN_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ITN-11">[11]</a></sup> The final three bodies, those of two credit union employees and a customer, were recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNNI_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNNI-13">[13]</a></sup> For several days after the building's demolition, trucks hauled 800 tons of debris a day away from the site. Some of the debris was used as <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">evidence</a> in the conspirators' trials, incorporated into parts of memorials, donated to local schools, and sold to raise funds for relief efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unfinished_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Unfinished-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remnants_and_replacement">Remnants and replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_P._Murrah_Federal_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Remnants and replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several remnants of the Murrah Building stand on the site of the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_National_Memorial" title="Oklahoma City National Memorial">Oklahoma City National Memorial</a>. Its plaza (on what was once the south side of the building) has been incorporated into the memorial; the Murrah Building's original flagpole is still in use. The east wall of the Murrah Building (within the building's footprint) is intact, as well as portions of the south wall. The building's underground parking garage survived the blast and is used today, but it is guarded and closed to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/General_Services_Administration" title="General Services Administration">General Services Administration</a> immediately sought to replace the facility. The building site is a transition zone between the Central Business District and the North Downtown neighborhood. The new 185,000 square foot building was designed by <a href="/wiki/Ross_Barney_Architects" title="Ross Barney Architects">Ross Barney Architects</a> of Chicago, Illinois, with <a href="/wiki/Carol_Ross_Barney" title="Carol Ross Barney">Carol Ross Barney</a> as the lead designer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Constructed on a two city block site, one block north and west of the former site, the new building's design maximized sustainable design and workplace productivity initiatives. Security design was paramount to the Federal employees and its neighbors. Secure design was achieved based on the GSA's current standards for secure facilities including blast resistant glazing. Structural design resists <a href="/wiki/Progressive_collapse" title="Progressive collapse">progressive collapse</a>. Building mass, glazing inside the courtyard, and bollards help to maintain a sense of openness and security. The art in architecture component of the building incorporates a water feature that acts as an additional security barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
