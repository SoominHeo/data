<p><b>Alfred Newman</b> (March 17, 1900 – February 17, 1970)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup> was an American <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a>, arranger, and conductor of film music. From his start as a music prodigy, he came to be regarded as a respected figure in the history of film music. He won nine Academy Awards and was nominated forty-three times.</p>
<p>In a career spanning more than four decades, Newman composed the scores for over 200 motion pictures. Some of his most famous scores include <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights_(1939_film)" title="Wuthering Heights (1939 film)">Wuthering Heights</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunchback_of_Notre_Dame_(1939_film)" title="The Hunchback of Notre Dame (1939 film)">The Hunchback of Notre Dame</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mark_of_Zorro_(1940_film)" title="The Mark of Zorro (1940 film)">The Mark of Zorro</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/How_Green_Was_My_Valley_(film)" title="How Green Was My Valley (film)">How Green Was My Valley</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Bernadette_(film)" title="The Song of Bernadette (film)">The Song of Bernadette</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_from_Castile" title="Captain from Castile">Captain from Castile</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/All_About_Eve" title="All About Eve">All About Eve</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Is_a_Many-Splendored_Thing_(film)" title="Love Is a Many-Splendored Thing (film)">Love is a Many Splendored Thing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Anastasia_(1956_film)" title="Anastasia (1956 film)">Anastasia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Diary_of_Anne_Frank_(1959_film)" title="The Diary of Anne Frank (1959 film)">The Diary of Anne Frank</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_West_Was_Won_(film)" title="How the West Was Won (film)">How The West Was Won</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Story_Ever_Told" title="The Greatest Story Ever Told">The Greatest Story Ever Told</a></i>, and his final score, <i><a href="/wiki/Airport_(1970_film)" title="Airport (1970 film)">Airport</a></i>, all of which were nominated for or won Academy Awards.</p>
<p>Newman was also highly regarded as a conductor, and arranged and conducted many scores by other composers, including George Gershwin, Charlie Chaplin, and Irving Berlin. He also conducted the music for many film adaptations of Broadway musicals (having worked on Broadway for ten years before coming to Hollywood), as well as many original Hollywood musicals.</p>
<p>He was among the first musicians to compose and conduct original music during Hollywood's <a href="/wiki/Classical_Hollywood_cinema" title="Classical Hollywood cinema">Golden Age</a> of movies, later becoming a respected and powerful music director in the history of Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson2-2">[2]</a></sup> Newman and two of his fellow composers, <a href="/wiki/Max_Steiner" title="Max Steiner">Max Steiner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimitri_Tiomkin" title="Dimitri Tiomkin">Dimitri Tiomkin</a>, were considered the "three godfathers of film music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Newman was the eldest of ten children, born in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>, to Jewish parents who emigrated from Russia shortly before his birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:68</sup> Although many sources show a birth date of 1901, the musicologist Fred Steiner established that Alfred was actually born in 1900. His father, Michael Newman, was a produce dealer and his mother, Luba, took care of the family. Her father had been a cantor in Russia, which contributed to her love of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berg_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berg-6">[6]</a></sup> She sent Newman, her first born, to a local piano teacher to begin lessons when he was five. At one point, in order to take lessons, he walked a ten-mile round trip. And with barely enough to live on, his parents once had to sell their dog to make ends meet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berg_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Berg-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the age of eight he had become known locally as a piano prodigy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> His talent led virtuoso <a href="/wiki/Ignacy_Jan_Paderewski" title="Ignacy Jan Paderewski">Ignacy Jan Paderewski</a> to arrange a recital for him in New York,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigismund_Stojowski" title="Sigismund Stojowski">Sigismund Stojowski</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lambert" title="Alexander Lambert">Alexander Lambert</a>, at different periods, took him as a pupil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfiles_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mfiles-8">[8]</a></sup> To save Newman commuting cost, Stojowski convinced a ticket inspector to let young Newman sometimes travel free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup> Stojowski offered him a scholarship, after which Newman won a silver medal and a gold medal in a competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moviemusic_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-moviemusic-9">[9]</a></sup> He also studied harmony, counterpoint and composition with <a href="/wiki/Rubin_Goldmark" title="Rubin Goldmark">Rubin Goldmark</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Wedge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Wedge (page does not exist)">George Wedge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_jobs_as_pianist">Early jobs as pianist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early jobs as pianist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Newman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Newman at age 13</div>

<p>By the time Newman was twelve, however, his parents' meager income was not enough to support his large family, which led to him searching for ways to earn an income from music to help his family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> He then began playing in theaters and restaurants, including the <a href="/wiki/Strand_Theatre_(Brooklyn)" title="Strand Theatre (Brooklyn)">Strand theater</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Opera_House" title="Harlem Opera House">Harlem Opera House</a>, with a schedule that often had him playing five shows a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courrier_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Courrier-11">[11]</a></sup> During the shows, he typically accompanied singers as pianist. <a href="/wiki/Grace_La_Rue" title="Grace La Rue">Grace La Rue</a>, star of one the shows, was taken by Newman's talent and signed him on as her regular accompanist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newman, then 13, also attracted the attention of author <a href="/wiki/Ella_Wheeler_Wilcox" title="Ella Wheeler Wilcox">Ella Wheeler Wilcox</a>, who wanted to promote him to those who could further his music ambition. She greatly admired his ability to play Mendelssohn, Chopin, Liszt, Wagner and other composers, and with equal skill, in her opinion, as noted pianist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paderewski" title="Paderewski">Paderewski</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilcox_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilcox-12">[12]</a></sup> She said he "possessed most unusual moral qualities and characteristics":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He is a beautiful looking boy, modest, gentle, unassuming, and wholly unspoiled. I am not interested in him merely because he renders the great masters marvelously and even composes wonderfully, but rather because he has such a rare and interesting nature. His father is a poor Russian fruit dealer and Alfred is the oldest of eight children. The mother is a very beautiful woman, and both parents show good blood and breeding despite their humble position and lack of means. The family has made every possible sacrifice in order to educate this boy in music, and he has a most deep-seated sense of <i><a href="/wiki/Noblesse_oblige" title="Noblesse oblige">noblesse oblige</a></i>. His whole desire for success seems based upon his anxiety to make his parents happy and to repay them for what they have done for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilcox_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilcox-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[a]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He began traveling the <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">vaudeville</a> circuit with La Rue's show when he was 13, where she billed him as "The Marvelous Boy Pianist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup> While on tours, he was sometimes allowed to conduct the orchestras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup> This led to him making conducting his career goal, an ambition furthered by <a href="/wiki/William_Merrigan_Daly" title="William Merrigan Daly">William Merrigan Daly</a>, an experienced music director and composer who taught Newman the basics of conducting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup> By the time he was fifteen, he was regularly conducting performances for matinee shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Symphony" title="Cincinnati Symphony">Cincinnati Symphony</a> conductor <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Reiner" title="Fritz Reiner">Fritz Reiner</a> was so impressed by Newman, he invited him to be a guest conductor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full-time_Broadway_conductor">Full-time Broadway conductor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Full-time Broadway conductor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When he was nineteen, he began conducting full time in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the beginning of a ten-year career on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a> as the conductor of musicals by composers such as <a href="/wiki/George_Gershwin" title="George Gershwin">George Gershwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rodgers" title="Richard Rodgers">Richard Rodgers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Kern" title="Jerome Kern">Jerome Kern</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup> He conducted <i><a href="/wiki/George_White%27s_Scandals" title="George White's Scandals">George White's Scandals</a></i> in 1919, <i><a href="/wiki/Funny_Face_(musical)" title="Funny Face (musical)">Funny Face</a></i> in 1927 and <i><a href="/wiki/Treasure_Girl" title="Treasure Girl">Treasure Girl</a></i> in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Newman said he was always happiest as a conductor: "I studied music composition and counterpoint because I wanted to be a good conductor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courrier_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Courrier-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1930 songwriter and composer <a href="/wiki/Irving_Berlin" title="Irving Berlin">Irving Berlin</a> invited him to Hollywood to conduct his score for the film, <i><a href="/wiki/Reaching_for_the_Moon_(1930_film)" title="Reaching for the Moon (1930 film)">Reaching for the Moon</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courrier_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Courrier-11">[11]</a></sup> Although the musical film was originally planned to include songs written by Berlin, problems developed between him and director <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Goulding" title="Edmund Goulding">Edmund Goulding</a>, which led to most of his songs being taken out. Newman was kept on and received credit for directing the music, which became his Hollywood debut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Virginia_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Virginia-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_scoring_career">Film scoring career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Film scoring career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s">1930s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1930s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after Newman arrived in Hollywood in 1930 and finished directing the score for <i><a href="/wiki/Reaching_for_the_Moon_(1930_film)" title="Reaching for the Moon (1930 film)">Reaching for the Moon</a></i>, producer <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Goldwyn" title="Samuel Goldwyn">Samuel Goldwyn</a> offered him a contract to continue on as a movie composer. His first complete film score was for Goldwyn's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_Scene_(1931_film)" title="Street Scene (1931 film)">Street Scene</a></i> in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup> The score mirrored the busy and frantic sounds of everyday life in New York's <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a> in the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-16">[15]</a></sup> He later used that music theme in other films, such as <i><a href="/wiki/How_to_Marry_a_Millionaire" title="How to Marry a Millionaire">How to Marry a Millionaire</a></i> in 1953, which opens with him conducting an orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> The theme is also used in <i><a href="/wiki/Gentleman%27s_Agreement" title="Gentleman's Agreement">Gentleman's Agreement</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Wake_Up_Screaming" title="I Wake Up Screaming">I Wake Up Screaming</a></i>,<i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Corner" title="The Dark Corner">The Dark Corner</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cry_of_the_City" title="Cry of the City">Cry of the City</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kiss_of_Death_(1947_film)" title="Kiss of Death (1947 film)">Kiss of Death</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Where_the_Sidewalk_Ends_(film)" title="Where the Sidewalk Ends (film)">Where the Sidewalk Ends</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1931 <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a> had him to orchestrate his film, <i><a href="/wiki/City_Lights" title="City Lights">City Lights</a></i>, and used Newman again for <a href="/wiki/Modern_Times_(film)" title="Modern Times (film)"><i>Modern Times</i></a> in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaplin_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chaplin-19">[18]</a></sup> Hollywood reporter <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Skolsky" title="Sidney Skolsky">Sidney Skolsky</a> observed them working together as Newman conducted the 65-piece orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaplin_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chaplin-19">[18]</a></sup> He described Newman's ability to carefully synchronize the music to scenes, such as the factory sequence, where Chaplin throws the place into confusion. The music was timed to Chaplin's movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newman became Goldwyn's favorite composer, while his style evolved with each new film he scored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:74</sup> He scored numerous adventure stories and romances, historical pageants and swashbuckling epics, as did his contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Erich_Wolfgang_Korngold" title="Erich Wolfgang Korngold">Erich Wolfgang Korngold</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:75</sup> Newman also began taking lessons with <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>, who emigrated to the U.S. from Europe in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>He received his first Academy Award for <i><a href="/wiki/Alexander%27s_Ragtime_Band_(film)" title="Alexander's Ragtime Band (film)">Alexander's Ragtime Band</a></i> in 1938. In 1939, he wrote the music for Goldwyn's <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights_(1939_film)" title="Wuthering Heights (1939 film)">Wuthering Heights</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merle_Oberon" title="Merle Oberon">Merle Oberon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> His score was unique in the way it included different musical themes and created different motifs for the key actors, which helped frame the action. The theme for Cathy, for instance, consisted of a glowing pastoral with strings, while Heathcliff's theme, in contrast, produced a darker, more serious image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-16">[15]</a></sup> Also in 1939, he composed the music for <i><a href="/wiki/Gunga_Din_(film)" title="Gunga Din (film)">Gunga Din</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Beau_Geste_(1939_film)" title="Beau Geste (1939 film)">Beau Geste</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among Newman's specialties were films with a religious theme, although he himself was not known to be religious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:80</sup> Among the films were <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunchback_of_Notre_Dame_(1939_film)" title="The Hunchback of Notre Dame (1939 film)">The Hunchback of Notre Dame</a></i> (1939), starring <a href="/wiki/Charles_Laughton" title="Charles Laughton">Charles Laughton</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> and in subsequent years, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Bernadette_(film)" title="The Song of Bernadette (film)">The Song of Bernadette</a></i> (1943), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Robe_(film)" title="The Robe (film)">The Robe</a></i> (1953), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Story_Ever_Told" title="The Greatest Story Ever Told">The Greatest Story Ever Told</a></i> (1965).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1940s">1940s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1940s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1940 Newman began a 20-year career as music director with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20th_Century-Fox" title="20th Century-Fox">20th Century-Fox Studios</a>, composing over 200 film scores, nine of which won Academy Awards. He wore many hats at the studio depending on the need, acting as composer, arranger, music director and conductor for various films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup> However, he said that he preferred arranging and conducting over composing because the latter was lonely and demanding work. The demands of work contributed to his heavy smoking throughout his life, eventually leading to his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moviemusic_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-moviemusic-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was noted for developing what came to be known as the Newman System, a means of synchronizing the performance and recording of a musical score with the film, a system which is still in use today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson-26">[25]</a></sup> Newman's scores were developed around the overall mood of each film. He also tailored specific themes to accompany different characters as they appeared on screen, thereby enhancing each actor's role. The effects of this style of music created a forceful but less jarring score which connected the entire story, thereby keeping the film's theme more easily understood by viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>He composed the familiar fanfare which accompanies the studio logo at the beginning of Fox's productions, and still introduces Fox pictures today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fanfare_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-fanfare-27">[26]</a></sup> In 1953, Newman wrote the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinemascope" title="Cinemascope">CinemaScope</a> extension" for his fanfare. This fanfare was re-recorded in 1997 by his son <a href="/wiki/David_Newman_(composer)" title="David Newman (composer)">David</a>, also a composer, and it is this rendition that is used today.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Bernadette_(film)" title="The Song of Bernadette (film)">The Song of Bernadette</a></i> (1943) is said to be one of Newman's loveliest scores, recorded over a four-week period with an 80-piece orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green-28">[27]</a></sup> Newman used three different motifs to color different issues during the film. Among them was a brass chorale to represent Mother Church,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:80</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> while the theme representing Bernadette used strings to support her character's warmth and tenderness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:81</sup> Newman's interpretation added the sound of the wind and blowing leaves to give the music an ethereal quality that augmented Bernadette's visions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Green-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newman's score for <a href="/wiki/Wilson_(1944_film)" title="Wilson (1944 film)"><i>Wilson</i></a> (1944), a biopic about president <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, required he devote an unusual amount of time to research. The film was intended to be a tribute to Wilson by producer <a href="/wiki/Darryl_F._Zanuck" title="Darryl F. Zanuck">Darryl F. Zanuck</a>. Newman spent considerable time learning personal details about Wilson and his family, such as the songs they sang and played on their piano at home, the music they liked to dance and listen to, the songs they played during political rallies or political functions during his career. As a result, the film contained some forty realistic American-themed numbers intertwined throughout the film which gave it a strong sense of timeliness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:89</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1940s Newman scored a number of films related to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Among those were <i><a href="/wiki/A_Yank_in_the_R.A.F." title="A Yank in the R.A.F.">A Yank in the R.A.F.</a></i> (1941), <i><a href="/wiki/To_the_Shores_of_Tripoli" title="To the Shores of Tripoli">To the Shores of Tripoli</a></i> (1942) and <i><a href="/wiki/Twelve_O%27Clock_High" title="Twelve O'Clock High">Twelve O'Clock High</a></i> (1949), which one historian says is Newman's best dramatic opening theme for a movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:117</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Newman also composed or music directed the score to some of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Capra" title="Frank Capra">Frank Capra</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Why_We_Fight" title="Why We Fight">Why We Fight</a></i> series of films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Prelude_to_War" title="Prelude to War">Prelude to War</a></i> (1942) and <i><a href="/wiki/War_Comes_to_America" title="War Comes to America">War Comes to America</a></i> (1945). He created the music for <i>The All-Star Bond Rally</i> (1945), a documentary short film featuring Hollywood stars promoting the sales of War Bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> The previous year he scored another documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fighting_Lady" title="The Fighting Lady">The Fighting Lady</a></i> (1944).</p>
<p>He often studied period music and assimilated it into his scores. For films such as <i><a href="/wiki/How_Green_Was_My_Valley_(film)" title="How Green Was My Valley (film)">How Green Was My Valley</a></i> (1941), for example, he incorporated Welsh hymns. For <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_West_Was_Won_(film)" title="How the West Was Won (film)">How The West Was Won</a></i> (1962), he took folk tunes and transformed them into orchestral/choral works of tremendous power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> And for <a href="/wiki/The_Grapes_of_Wrath_(film)" title="The Grapes of Wrath (film)"><i>The Grapes of Wrath</i></a> (1940), he brought in the folk tune favorite "Red River Valley" throughout the score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-16">[15]</a></sup> His skill at incorporating familiar traditional music into modern scores was not limited to Western themes, however. During portions of the score for <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Is_a_Many-Splendored_Thing_(film)" title="Love Is a Many-Splendored Thing (film)">Love is a Many Splendored Thing</a></i>, for example, he created numbers with a distinctly Chinese sensibility, both with instruments and melodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Generally, however, he would create his own original melody and turn it into something haunting and memorable, as he did for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Robe_(film)" title="The Robe (film)">The Robe</a></i> (1953).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1947 he composed the music for <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_from_Castile" title="Captain from Castile">Captain from Castile</a></i>, which included the famous "Conquest march", an impassioned score for the Spanish conquistadors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:75</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> The march was adapted by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> (USC) as the official theme song for their sports teams, the <a href="/wiki/USC_Trojans" title="USC Trojans">USC Trojans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Newman also orchestrated and conducted the music for a biopic about the life of American composer <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Sousa" title="John Philip Sousa">John Philip Sousa</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stars_and_Stripes_Forever_(film)" title="Stars and Stripes Forever (film)">Stars and Stripes Forever</a></i> (1952), a film which includes numerous marches for which Sousa is best known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dramatic score for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Snake_Pit" title="The Snake Pit">The Snake Pit</a></i>, a 1948 film set in a lunatic asylum, was accentuated by Newman's careful use of effects to intensify the discomfort and fear portrayed by the actors, primarily its star <a href="/wiki/Olivia_de_Havilland" title="Olivia de Havilland">Olivia de Havilland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:79</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1952, <i><a href="/wiki/With_a_Song_in_My_Heart_(film)" title="With a Song in My Heart (film)">With a Song in My Heart</a></i> gave Newman his fifth Academy Award. It was presented to him by Walt Disney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Robe_(film)" title="The Robe (film)">The Robe</a></i> (1953), a <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> epic, was another of Newman's scores with a religious theme, with orchestration creating spaciousness, grandeur and simplicity. It was also the first picture to use stereo sound, which allowed Newman to experiment in developing the various moods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:85</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> The score was one of fellow composer <a href="/wiki/Franz_Waxman" title="Franz Waxman">Franz Waxman</a>'s favorites, and he re-worked part of it for the film's sequel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:85</sup></p>
<p>Newman received his eighth Oscar for <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_and_I_(1956_film)" title="The King and I (1956 film)">The King and I</a></i> in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> In 1959 Newman composed the score for <a href="/wiki/The_Diary_of_Anne_Frank_(1959_film)" title="The Diary of Anne Frank (1959 film)"><i>The Diary of Anne Frank</i></a>. Although based on the true-life tragic story of a young girl during World War II, Newman's score focuses on her optimistic personality, which as her diary attests, she continued to believe that people were good at heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:87</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> In contrast to Newman's use of uplifting violins and a hopeful old European sound for the girl, the score for the Nazis was an "oppressive march in half time" to create a fearsome effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-16">[15]</a></sup> Music historian <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Palmer" title="Christopher Palmer">Christopher Palmer</a> says that the score is one of Newman's finest, which because of its style, elegance and integrity, the emotions portrayed by the actors can be physically "felt" by the audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup> It was nominated for an Oscar.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newman's final musical score under his Fox contract was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Best_of_Everything_(film)" title="The Best of Everything (film)">The Best of Everything</a></i> (1959), and after leaving Fox in 1960, Newman freelanced for the remainder of his career, writing the scores for such films as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MGM" title="MGM">MGM</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_West_Was_Won_(film)" title="How the West Was Won (film)">How the West Was Won</a></i> (1962), which some consider his most familiar and best score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> It is listed on <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years_of_Film_Scores" title="AFI's 100 Years of Film Scores">AFI's 100 Years of Film Scores</a>. That film and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Story_Ever_Told" title="The Greatest Story Ever Told">The Greatest Story Ever Told</a></i> (1965), were nominated for an Oscar.</p>
<p>Newman remained active until the end of his life, scoring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Airport_(1970_film)" title="Airport (1970 film)">Airport</a></i> (1970) shortly before his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Newman died on February 17, 1970, at the age of 69, at his home in Hollywood, from complications of emphysema.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: LightCyan;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Arriving in Hollywood just as talking pictures were getting more technically sophisticated, he contributed to creating the musical sound of the era and was at the heart of the studio system at its peak...The passing of Newman was symbolic of the end of a golden age.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Thomas Hischak<br/>
<i>The Encyclopedia of Film Composers</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hischak_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hischak-16">[15]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>During his career, Newman was regarded as the most important, most influential and most respected figure in the history of film music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> He received 44 Oscar nominations and 9 Academy Awards, more than any other musical director or composer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:117</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarty_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCarty-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was considered the "most powerful music director in the history of Hollywood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Henderson2-2">[2]</a></sup> According to some music historians, his word about music was considered "the law" in Hollywood for nearly 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> With two of his fellow composers, <a href="/wiki/Max_Steiner" title="Max Steiner">Max Steiner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimitri_Tiomkin" title="Dimitri Tiomkin">Dimitri Tiomkin</a>, they were considered the "three godfathers of film music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:298</sup></p>
<p>He was awarded Oscars for <i>Alexander's Ragtime Band</i> (1938), <i>Tin Pan Alley</i> (1940), <i>The Song of Bernadette</i>(1943), <i>Mother Wore Tights</i> (1947), <i>With a Song in My Heart</i> (1952), <i>Call Me Madam</i> (1953), <i>Love Is a Many Splendored Thing</i> (1955), <i>The King and I</i> (1956) and <i>Camelot</i> (1967).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He composed the familiar fanfare which accompanies the studio logo at the beginning of Fox's productions, and still introduces Fox pictures today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fanfare_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-fanfare-27">[26]</a></sup> A segment of Newman's score for <a href="/wiki/David_O._Selznick" title="David O. Selznick">David O. Selznick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner_of_Zenda_(1937_film)" title="The Prisoner of Zenda (1937 film)">The Prisoner of Zenda</a></i> (1937) became the standard music which accompanied the Selznick International Logo when introducing his films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> At the University of Southern California, the 280-seat Allan Hancock Auditorium was renovated and re-dedicated as the Alfred Newman Recital Hall in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: LightCyan;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Newman was one of those rare Hollywood souls who generously nurtured the talents and careers of many other men who became legends in the field of film composition—including <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Herrmann" title="Bernard Herrmann">Bernard Herrmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Raksin" title="David Raksin">David Raksin</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a>.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Classic Themes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>While a composer, music director and conductor, he often contributed to the scores of others without credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarty_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCarty-46">[45]</a></sup> When he wasn't working on a particular movie, he was often approached by studio production heads needing advice, which he freely gave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogdanov_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov-7">[7]</a></sup> Other musicians were constantly exploring new ideas or perfecting older techniques, which required sharing their knowledge with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faulkner_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Faulkner-52">[51]</a></sup> Newman, during his years as a music director, sometimes went further: if one of his composers was stumped for a suitable melody, for instance, Newman would sometimes write a few bars on paper and hand it to the composer, suggesting he try it out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faulkner_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Faulkner-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a music director, it was Newman's job to find and select suitable composers for various films. When he saw a composer's potential, he also had the power to sign them to long term staff contracts. Music historian Robert R. Faulkner is of the opinion that had Newman not been music director at Twentieth Century Fox, composers such as <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Herrmann" title="Bernard Herrmann">Bernard Herrmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_North" title="Alex North">Alex North</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Raksin" title="David Raksin">David Raksin</a>, all of whose music was somewhat radical, might never have had such major careers in Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faulkner_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Faulkner-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The legacy of Alfred Newman and his influence on the language of music for the cinema is practically unmatched by anyone in Hollywood history. As an executive, he was hard but fair. As a mentor to his staff he was revered. The orchestras under his baton delighted in his abilities as a conductor. The music he himself composed, often under extreme emotional duress, is among the most gorgeous ever written. […] Not big in physical stature, he was a giant in character, a titan in the world he loved and dominated. He was a true musical force, and one that cannot in any sense be replaced.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— producer, Nick Redman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1999, the United States Postal Service issued a stamp in his honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Newman won nine Academy Awards, the third highest number of Oscars ever won by an individual (<a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> won twenty-six, <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Gibbons" title="Cedric Gibbons">Cedric Gibbons</a> won eleven) and was nominated for forty-five, making him the most nominated composer in Oscar history until 2006, when <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a> matched the record. Forty-three of Newman's nominations were for Best Original Score (making him the second most nominated in that category after John Williams) and two were for Original Song.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> ranked his score for <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_West_Was_Won_(film)" title="How the West Was Won (film)">How the West Was Won</a></i> as <abbr title="Number">No.</abbr> 25 on <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years_of_Film_Scores" title="AFI's 100 Years of Film Scores">their list of the 25 greatest film scores</a>. Ten of Newman's other scores were also nominated:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights_(1939_film)" title="Wuthering Heights (1939 film)">Wuthering Heights</a></i> (1939)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunchback_of_Notre_Dame_(1939_film)" title="The Hunchback of Notre Dame (1939 film)">The Hunchback of Notre Dame</a></i> (1939)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/How_Green_Was_My_Valley_(film)" title="How Green Was My Valley (film)">How Green Was My Valley</a></i> (1941)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Bernadette_(film)" title="The Song of Bernadette (film)">The Song of Bernadette</a></i> (1943)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Captain_from_Castile" title="Captain from Castile">Captain from Castile</a></i> (1947)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Newman_family">Newman family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Newman_(composer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Newman family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He married Martha Louise Montgomery (born December 5, 1920, Clarksdale, Mississippi - died May 9, 2005, Pacific Palisades, California), a former actress and Goldwyn Girl; they had five children.</p>
<p>He was the head of a family of major <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> film composers:</p>
<ul>
<li>His brother <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Newman" title="Lionel Newman">Lionel Newman</a> scored three dozen films and several TV series, adapting and conducting scores for hundreds of other films.</li>
<li>His brother <a href="/wiki/Emil_Newman" title="Emil Newman">Emil Newman</a> was music director for over eighty films.</li>
<li>His son <a href="/wiki/David_Newman_(composer)" title="David Newman (composer)">David Newman</a> has scored nearly one hundred films, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Roses_(film)" title="The War of the Roses (film)">The War of the Roses</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hoffa" title="Hoffa">Hoffa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nutty_Professor_(1996_film)" title="The Nutty Professor (1996 film)">The Nutty Professor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Anastasia_(1997_film)" title="Anastasia (1997 film)">Anastasia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Quest" title="Galaxy Quest">Galaxy Quest</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ice_Age_(2002_film)" title="Ice Age (2002 film)">Ice Age</a></i> and has received an Academy Award nomination.</li>
<li>His son <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newman" title="Thomas Newman">Thomas Newman</a> has scored over seventy-five films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Women_(1994_film)" title="Little Women (1994 film)">Little Women</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Unstrung_Heroes" title="Unstrung Heroes">Unstrung Heroes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Beauty_(1999_film)" title="American Beauty (1999 film)">American Beauty</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Road_to_Perdition" title="Road to Perdition">Road to Perdition</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Nemo" title="Finding Nemo">Finding Nemo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket%27s_A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events">Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_German" title="The Good German">The Good German</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/WALL-E" title="WALL-E">WALL-E</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Skyfall" title="Skyfall">Skyfall</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Saving_Mr._Banks" title="Saving Mr. Banks">Saving Mr. Banks</a></i> and has received thirteen Academy Award nominations.</li>
<li>His daughter <a href="/wiki/Maria_Newman" title="Maria Newman">Maria Newman</a> is an eminent musician and composer.</li>
<li>His nephew <a href="/wiki/Randy_Newman" title="Randy Newman">Randy Newman</a> is a two-time Academy Award winner, noted not only for his film work but also for a series of popular albums as a singer/songwriter.</li>
<li>His grandnephew <a href="/wiki/Joey_Newman" title="Joey Newman">Joey Newman</a> has scored many TV series, films, and video games.</li>
<li>His granddaughter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jaclyn_Newman_Dorn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jaclyn Newman Dorn (page does not exist)">Jaclyn Newman Dorn</a> is a music editor, and won a Golden Reel Award for <i><a href="/wiki/30_Days_of_Night:_Dark_Days" title="30 Days of Night: Dark Days">30 Days of Night: Dark Days</a></i>, and got another nomination for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burlesque_(2010_musical_film)" title="Burlesque (2010 musical film)">Burlesque</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
