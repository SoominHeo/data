<p><b>Alfred Day Hershey</b> (December 4, 1908 – May 22, 1997) was an American <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize</a>–winning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacteriologist" title="Bacteriologist">bacteriologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geneticist" title="Geneticist">geneticist</a>.</p>
<p>He was born in <a href="/wiki/Owosso,_Michigan" title="Owosso, Michigan">Owosso, Michigan</a> and received his B.S. in chemistry at <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a> in 1930 and his Ph.D. in <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriology</a> in 1934, taking a position shortly thereafter at the Department of Bacteriology at <a href="/wiki/Washington_University_in_St._Louis" title="Washington University in St. Louis">Washington University in St. Louis</a>.</p>
<p>He began performing experiments with <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophages</a> with Italian-American <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Luria" title="Salvador Luria">Salvador Luria</a>, German <a href="/wiki/Max_Delbr%C3%BCck" title="Max Delbrück">Max Delbrück</a>, Indian-Canadian Adam Hasnain, and Serbian Mila Huhtala in 1940, and observed that when two different strains of bacteriophage have infected the same bacteria, the two <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> may exchange <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_information" title="Genetic information">genetic information</a>.</p>
<p>He moved with his assistant <a href="/wiki/Martha_Chase" title="Martha Chase">Martha Chase</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cold_Spring_Harbor,_New_York" title="Cold Spring Harbor, New York">Cold Spring Harbor, New York</a>, in 1950 to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institution_of_Washington" title="Carnegie Institution of Washington">Carnegie Institution of Washington</a>'s Department of Genetics, where he and Chase performed the famous <a href="/wiki/Hershey%E2%80%93Chase_experiment" title="Hershey–Chase experiment">Hershey–Chase experiment</a> in 1952. This experiment provided additional evidence that DNA, not protein, was the genetic material of life.</p>
<p>He became director of the Carnegie Institution in 1962 and was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1969, shared with <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Luria" title="Salvador Luria">Salvador Luria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Delbr%C3%BCck" title="Max Delbrück">Max Delbrück</a> for their discovery on the <a href="/wiki/Viral_replication" title="Viral replication">replication of viruses</a> and their genetic structure.</p>
<p>In 1981, Hershey became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/World_Cultural_Council" title="World Cultural Council">World Cultural Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hershey had 1 child, Peter Manning Hershey (1956-1999) with his wife Harriet (often called Jill) (1918-2000). The family was active in the social network of Cold Spring Harbor Laboratories and regularly enjoyed the beach in season. Hershey was a <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Hershey died, another phage worker, <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Stahl" title="Franklin Stahl">Frank Stahl</a>, wrote: "The Phage Church, as we were sometimes called (see <a href="/wiki/Phage_group" title="Phage group">Phage group</a>), was led by the Trinity of Delbrück, Luria, and Hershey. Delbrück's status as founder and his <i><a href="/wiki/Papal_infallibility#Ex_cathedra" title="Papal infallibility">ex cathedra</a></i> manner made him the pope, of course, and Luria was the hard-working, socially sensitive priest-confessor. And Al (Hershey) was the saint."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
