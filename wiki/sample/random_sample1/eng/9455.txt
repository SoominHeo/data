<p><b>Ambrose Gwinnett Bierce</b> (June 24, 1842<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup> – circa 1914<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) was an American <a href="/wiki/Editorial" title="Editorial">editorialist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short story</a> writer, <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fabulist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirist</a>. He wrote the short story "<a href="/wiki/An_Occurrence_at_Owl_Creek_Bridge" title="An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge">An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge</a>" and compiled a satirical <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil%27s_Dictionary" title="The Devil's Dictionary">The Devil's Dictionary</a></i>. His vehemence as a <a href="/wiki/Critic" title="Critic">critic</a>, his motto "Nothing matters", and the sardonic view of <a href="/wiki/Human_nature" title="Human nature">human nature</a> that informed his work, all earned him the nickname "Bitter Bierce".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite his reputation as a searing critic, Bierce was known to encourage younger writers, including the poets <a href="/wiki/George_Sterling" title="George Sterling">George Sterling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_George_Scheffauer" title="Herman George Scheffauer">Herman George Scheffauer</a> and the fiction writer <a href="/wiki/W._C._Morrow" title="W. C. Morrow">W. C. Morrow</a>. Bierce employed a distinctive style of writing, especially in his stories. His style often embraces an <a href="/wiki/In_medias_res" title="In medias res">abrupt beginning</a>, dark imagery, vague references to time, limited descriptions, impossible events, and the theme of war.</p>
<p>In 1913, Bierce traveled to Mexico to gain first-hand experience of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">Mexican Revolution</a>. He was rumored to be traveling with rebel troops, and was not seen again.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bierce was born in a log cabin at Horse Cave Creek in <a href="/wiki/Meigs_County,_Ohio" title="Meigs County, Ohio">Meigs County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, on June 24, 1842, to Marcus Aurelius Bierce (1799–1876) and Laura Sherwood Bierce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup> His mother was a descendant of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Bradford_(Plymouth_governor)" title="William Bradford (Plymouth governor)">William Bradford</a>. He was the tenth of thirteen children whose father gave all names beginning with the letter "A": in order of birth, the Bierce siblings were Abigail, Amelia, Ann, Addison, Aurelius, Augustus, Almeda, Andrew, Albert, and Ambrose.</p>
<p>His parents were a poor but literary couple who instilled in him a deep love for books and writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup> Bierce grew up in <a href="/wiki/Kosciusko_County,_Indiana" title="Kosciusko County, Indiana">Kosciusko County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, attending <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> at the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw,_Indiana" title="Warsaw, Indiana">Warsaw</a>.</p>
<p>He left home at 15 to become a "<a href="/wiki/Printer%27s_devil" title="Printer's devil">printer's devil</a>" at a small Ohio newspaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_career">Military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the outset of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, Bierce enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army's</a> <a href="/wiki/9th_Indiana_Infantry_Regiment" title="9th Indiana Infantry Regiment">9th Indiana Infantry</a>. He participated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operations_in_Western_Virginia" title="Operations in Western Virginia">Operations in Western Virginia</a> campaign (1861), was present at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Philippi_(West_Virginia)" title="Battle of Philippi (West Virginia)">"first battle" at Philippi</a> and received newspaper attention for his daring rescue, under fire, of a gravely wounded comrade at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rich_Mountain" title="Battle of Rich Mountain">Battle of Rich Mountain</a>. In February 1862 he was commissioned a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Lieutenant#United_States" title="First Lieutenant">first lieutenant</a>, and served on the staff of General <a href="/wiki/William_Babcock_Hazen" title="William Babcock Hazen">William Babcock Hazen</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">topographical engineer</a>, making maps of likely battlefields.</p>
<p>Bierce fought at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shiloh" title="Battle of Shiloh">Battle of Shiloh</a> (April 1862), a terrifying experience that became a source for several later short stories and the <a href="/wiki/Memoir" title="Memoir">memoir</a> "What I Saw of Shiloh". In June 1864, he sustained a serious <a href="/wiki/Head_injury" title="Head injury">head wound</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kennesaw_Mountain" title="Battle of Kennesaw Mountain">Battle of Kennesaw Mountain</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and spent the rest of the summer on <a href="/wiki/Furlough" title="Furlough">furlough</a>, returning to active duty in September. He was discharged from the army in January 1865.</p>
<p>His military career resumed, however, when in mid-1866 he rejoined General Hazen as part of the latter's expedition to inspect military outposts across the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>. The expedition proceeded by horseback and wagon from <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a>, arriving toward year's end in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, California.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="469" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg/170px-Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg/255px-Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg/340px-Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ambrose_Bierce-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ambrose Bierce, by <a href="/wiki/Manchester_School_of_Painters#John_Herbert_Evelyn_Partington" title="Manchester School of Painters">J.H.E. Partington</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bierce married Mary Ellen "Mollie" Day on December 25, 1871. They had three children: sons Day (1872–1889)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919-6">[6]</a></sup> and Leigh (1874–1901)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919-6">[6]</a></sup> and daughter Helen (1875–1940). Both of Bierce's sons died before he did. Day committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> after a <a href="/wiki/Social_rejection" title="Social rejection">romantic rejection</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris1999206.E2.80.9308.2C_238_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris1999206.E2.80.9308.2C_238-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Leigh died of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919-6">[6]</a></sup> Bierce separated from his wife in 1888, after discovering compromising letters to her from an admirer. They divorced in 1904.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919-6">[6]</a></sup> Mollie Day Bierce died the following year.</p>
<p>Bierce was an avowed <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnostic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He suffered from lifelong <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919.E2.80.9320_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199919.E2.80.9320-10">[10]</a></sup> as well as complications from his war wounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online-literature.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-online-literature.com-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism">Journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, Bierce was awarded the rank of <a href="/wiki/Brevet_(military)" title="Brevet (military)">brevet</a> <a href="/wiki/Major_(United_States)" title="Major (United States)">major</a> before resigning from the Army. He remained in San Francisco for many years, eventually becoming famous as a contributor or editor of a number of local newspapers and periodicals, including <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_San_Francisco_News_Letter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The San Francisco News Letter (page does not exist)">The San Francisco News Letter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Argonaut" title="The Argonaut">The Argonaut</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Overland_Monthly" title="Overland Monthly">Overland Monthly</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Californian_(1880s_magazine)" title="The Californian (1880s magazine)">The Californian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wasp_(magazine)" title="The Wasp (magazine)">The Wasp</a></i>. A selection of his crime reporting from <i>The San Francisco News Letter</i> was included in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Library_of_America" title="The Library of America">The Library of America</a> anthology <i>True Crime</i>.</p>
<p>Bierce lived and wrote in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> from 1872 to 1875, contributing to <i><a href="/wiki/Fun_(magazine)" title="Fun (magazine)">Fun</a></i> magazine. His first book, <i>The Fiend's Delight</i>, a compilation of his articles, was published in London in 1873 by <a href="/wiki/John_Camden_Hotten" title="John Camden Hotten">John Camden Hotten</a> under the pseudonym "Dod Grile".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBierce20038_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBierce20038-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris1999143_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris1999143-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Returning to the United States, he again took up residence in San Francisco. From 1879 to 1880, he traveled to <a href="/wiki/Rockerville,_South_Dakota" title="Rockerville, South Dakota">Rockerville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deadwood,_South_Dakota" title="Deadwood, South Dakota">Deadwood</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dakota_Territory" title="Dakota Territory">Dakota Territory</a>, to try his hand as local manager for a New York <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> company. When the company failed he returned to San Francisco and resumed his career in <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>.</p>
<p>From January 1, 1881 until September 11, 1885 he was editor of <i>The Wasp</i> magazine, in which he began a column titled "Prattle". He also became one of the first regular columnists and editorialists on <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a>'s newspaper, <i><a href="/wiki/The_San_Francisco_Examiner" title="The San Francisco Examiner">The San Francisco Examiner</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup> eventually becoming one of the most prominent and influential writers and journalists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a>. He remained associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearst_Newspapers" title="Hearst Newspapers">Hearst Newspapers</a> until 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railroad_Refinancing_Bill">Railroad Refinancing Bill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Railroad Refinancing Bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Railroad" title="Central Pacific Railroad">Central Pacific</a> railroad companies had received large, low-interest loans from the U.S. government to build the <a href="/wiki/First_Transcontinental_Railroad" title="First Transcontinental Railroad">First Transcontinental Railroad</a>. Central Pacific executive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collis_P._Huntington" title="Collis P. Huntington">Collis P. Huntington</a> persuaded a friendly member of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> to introduce a bill excusing the companies from repaying the loans, amounting to $130 million (worth $3.74 billion today).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2315" data-file-width="2924" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg/220px-NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg/330px-NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg/440px-NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NE_corner_of_Logan_Circle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bierce's residence (right), 18 <a href="/wiki/Logan_Circle,_Washington,_D.C." title="Logan Circle, Washington, D.C.">Logan Circle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In January 1896 Hearst dispatched Bierce to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> to foil this attempt. The essence of the plot was secrecy; the railroads' advocates hoped to get the bill through Congress without any public notice or hearings. When the angered Huntington confronted Bierce on the steps of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">Capitol</a> and told Bierce to name his price, Bierce's answer ended up in newspapers nationwide:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>My price is one hundred thirty million dollars. If, when you are ready to pay, I happen to be out of town, you may hand it over to my friend, the Treasurer of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Bierce's coverage and diatribes on the subject aroused such public wrath that the bill was defeated. Bierce returned to California in November.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="McKinley_accusation">McKinley accusation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: McKinley accusation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of his penchant for biting social criticism and satire, Bierce's long newspaper career was often steeped in controversy. On several occasions his columns stirred up a storm of hostile reaction, which created difficulties for Hearst. One of the most notable of these incidents occurred following the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_William_McKinley" title="Assassination of William McKinley">assassination</a> of <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>, when Hearst's opponents turned a poem Bierce had written about the assassination of Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Goebel" title="William Goebel">William Goebel</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> in 1900 into a <i><a href="/wiki/Cause_c%C3%A9l%C3%A8bre" title="Cause célèbre">cause célèbre</a></i>.</p>
<p>Bierce meant his poem to express a national mood of dismay and fear, but after McKinley was shot in 1901, it seemed to foreshadow the crime:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p>The bullet that pierced Goebel's breast<br/>
Can not be found in all the West;<br/>
Good reason, it is speeding here<br/>
To stretch McKinley on his bier.</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>Hearst was thereby accused by rival newspapers—and by then-<a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Elihu_Root" title="Elihu Root">Elihu Root</a>—of having called for McKinley's assassination. Despite a national uproar that ended his ambitions for the presidency (and even his membership in the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Club" title="Bohemian Club">Bohemian Club</a>), Hearst kept employing Bierce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris1999236.E2.80.9337_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris1999236.E2.80.9337-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_works">Literary works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literary works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1222" data-file-width="905" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg/170px-Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg/255px-Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg/340px-Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ambrose_Bierce_1892-10-07.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bierce, 1892</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During his lifetime, Bierce was better known as a journalist than as a fiction writer. His most popular stories were written in rapid succession between 1888 and 1891, in what was characterized as "a tremendous burst of consummate art".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Bierce's works often highlight the inscrutability of the universe and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_humor" title="Black humor">absurdity of death</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ye_qi_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ye_qi-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bierce wrote realistically of the terrible things he had seen in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">war</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in such stories as "<a href="/wiki/An_Occurrence_at_Owl_Creek_Bridge" title="An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge">An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge</a>", "<a href="/wiki/A_Horseman_in_the_Sky" title="A Horseman in the Sky">A Horseman in the Sky</a>", "<a href="/wiki/One_of_the_Missing" title="One of the Missing">One of the Missing</a>", and "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Chickamauga" title="s:Chickamauga">Chickamauga</a>". His grimly realistic cycle of 25 war stories has been called "the greatest anti-war document in American literature".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Milton_Subotsky" title="Milton Subotsky">Milton Subotsky</a>, Bierce helped pioneer the <a href="/wiki/Psychological_horror" title="Psychological horror">psychological horror</a> story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In addition to his ghost and war stories, he also published several volumes of poetry. His <i>Fantastic Fables</i> anticipated the ironic style of <a href="/wiki/Grotesquerie" title="Grotesquerie">grotesquerie</a> that became a more common genre in the 20th century.</p>
<p>One of Bierce's most famous works is the much-quoted <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil%27s_Dictionary" title="The Devil's Dictionary">Devil's Dictionary</a></i>, originally an occasional newspaper item, first published in book form in 1906 as <i>The Cynic's Word Book.</i> It consists of satirical definitions of English words which lampoon <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">cant</a> and political double-talk.</p>
<p>Bierce's twelve-volume <i>Collected Works</i> were published in 1909, the seventh volume of which consists solely of <i>The Devil's Dictionary</i>, the title Bierce himself preferred to <i>The Cynic's Word Book</i>.</p>
<p>Bierce has been criticized by his contemporaries and later scholars for deliberately pursuing improbability and for his penchant toward "<a href="/wiki/Plot_twist" title="Plot twist">trick endings</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ye_qi_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ye_qi-17">[17]</a></sup> In his later stories, apparently under the influence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maupassant" title="Maupassant">Maupassant</a>, Bierce "dedicated himself to shocking the audience", as if his purpose was "to attack the reader's smug intellectual security".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bierce's bias towards <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(literature)" title="Naturalism (literature)">Naturalism</a> has also been noted:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> "The biting, deriding quality of his satire, unbalanced by any compassion for his targets, was often taken as petty meanness, showing contempt for humanity and an intolerance to the point of merciless cruelty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Crane" title="Stephen Crane">Stephen Crane</a> was of the minority of Bierce's contemporaries that valued Bierce's experimental short stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In his essay "<a href="/wiki/Supernatural_Horror_in_Literature" title="Supernatural Horror in Literature">Supernatural Horror in Literature</a>", <a href="/wiki/H._P._Lovecraft" title="H. P. Lovecraft">H. P. Lovecraft</a> characterized Bierce's fictional work as "grim and savage." Lovecraft goes on to say that nearly all of Bierce's stories are of the <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror genre</a> and some shine as great examples of <a href="/wiki/Weird_fiction" title="Weird fiction">weird fiction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance">Disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 1913, Bierce, then age 71, departed from <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> for a tour of his old <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> battlefields. By December he had passed through <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, crossing by way of <a href="/wiki/El_Paso,_Texas" title="El Paso, Texas">El Paso</a> into Mexico, which was in the throes of <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">revolution</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Ciudad Juárez">Ciudad Juárez</a> he joined <a href="/wiki/Pancho_Villa" title="Pancho Villa">Pancho Villa</a>'s army as an observer, and in that role he witnessed the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tierra_Blanca" title="Battle of Tierra Blanca">Battle of Tierra Blanca</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Texas-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bierce is known to have accompanied Villa's army as far as the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">Chihuahua</a>. His last known communication with the world was a letter he wrote there to <a href="/wiki/Blanche_Partington" title="Blanche Partington">Blanche Partington</a>, a close friend, dated December 26, 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBierce2003244f_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBierce2003244f-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> After closing this letter by saying, "As to me, I leave here tomorrow for an unknown destination," he vanished without a trace, <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_who_disappeared_mysteriously" title="List of people who disappeared mysteriously">his disappearance</a> becoming one of the most famous in American literary history. Skeptic <a href="/wiki/Joe_Nickell" title="Joe Nickell">Joe Nickell</a> argued that no letter had ever been found;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENickell1992_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENickell1992-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2012)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> all that existed was a notebook belonging to his secretary and companion, Carrie Christiansen, containing a rough summary of a purported letter and her statement that the originals had been destroyed.</p>
<p>There was an official investigation by U.S. consular officials of the disappearance of one of its citizens. Some of Villa's men were questioned at the time of his disappearance and afterwards, with contradictory accounts. Pancho Villa's representative in the U.S., <a href="/wiki/Felix_A._Sommerfeld" title="Felix A. Sommerfeld">Felix A. Sommerfeld</a>, was contacted by U.S. chief of staff <a href="/wiki/Hugh_L._Scott" title="Hugh L. Scott">Hugh L. Scott</a> and Sommerfeld investigated the disappearance. Bierce was said to have been last seen in the city of Chihuahua in January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oral tradition in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Mojada" title="Sierra Mojada">Sierra Mojada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a>, documented by a priest named James Lienert, states that Bierce was <a href="/wiki/Execution_by_firing_squad" title="Execution by firing squad">executed by firing squad</a> in the town cemetery there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, Nickell<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENickell1992_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENickell1992-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2012)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> finds this story to be incredible. He quotes Bierce's friend and biographer Walter Neale as saying that Bierce had not ridden for quite some time, was suffering from serious <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, and had been severely critical of Pancho Villa. Neale concludes that it would have been highly unlikely for Bierce to have gone to Mexico and joined Villa.</p>
<p>All investigations into his fate have proven fruitless, and Nickell concedes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENickell1992_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENickell1992-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2012)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that despite a lack of hard evidence that Bierce had gone to Mexico, there is also none that he had not. Therefore, despite an abundance of theories (including death by suicide), his end remains shrouded in mystery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_influence">Legacy and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambrose_Bierce&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:160px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambrose_Bierce_small.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="301" data-file-width="158" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Ambrose_Bierce_small.jpg" width="158"/></a>

<p>At least three films have been made of Bierce's story "<a href="/wiki/An_Occurrence_at_Owl_Creek_Bridge" title="An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge">An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge</a>". A silent film version, <i>The Bridge</i>, was made in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A French version called <i>La Rivière du Hibou</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Enrico" title="Robert Enrico">Robert Enrico</a>, was released in 1962;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> this black-and-white film faithfully recounts the original narrative using voiceover. It aired in 1964 on American television as one of the final episodes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">television series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)">The Twilight Zone</a></i>: "<a href="/wiki/An_Occurrence_at_Owl_Creek_Bridge_(film)" title="An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge (film)">An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Prior to <i>The Twilight Zone</i>, the story had been adapted as an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock_Presents" title="Alfred Hitchcock Presents">Alfred Hitchcock Presents</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Another version, directed by Brian James Egen, was released in 2005. It was also adapted for the CBS radio programs <i><a href="/wiki/Suspense_(radio_drama)" title="Suspense (radio drama)">Suspense</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_(radio_program)" title="Escape (radio program)">Escape</a></i>.</p>
<p>Actor James Lanphier (1920-1969) played Bierce, with <a href="/wiki/James_Hampton_(actor)" title="James Hampton (actor)">James Hampton</a> as <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a>, in the 1964 episode "The Paper Dynasty", of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_syndication" title="Television syndication">syndicated</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_(genre)" title="Western (genre)">western</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">television series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_Days" title="Death Valley Days">Death Valley Days</a></i>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Andrews" title="Stanley Andrews">Stanley Andrews</a>. In the story line, Hearst struggles to turn a profit despite increased circulation of <i>The San Francisco Examiner</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_O._Cornthwaite" title="Robert O. Cornthwaite">Robert O. Cornthwaite</a> appears as Sam Chamberlain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two adaptations were made of Bierce's story "Eyes of the Panther". One version was developed for <a href="/wiki/Shelley_Duvall" title="Shelley Duvall">Shelley Duvall</a>'s <i>Nightmare Classics</i> series and was released in 1990. It runs about 60 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A shorter version was released in 2007 by director Michael Barton and runs about 23 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/The_Damned_Thing_(short_story)" title="The Damned Thing (short story)">The Damned Thing</a>" was adapted into a <i><a href="/wiki/Masters_of_Horror" title="Masters of Horror">Masters of Horror</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Damned_Thing_(film)" title="The Damned Thing (film)">episode of the same title</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Tobe_Hooper" title="Tobe Hooper">Tobe Hooper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>American composer <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Waschka_II" title="Rodney Waschka II">Rodney Waschka II</a> composed an opera, <i>Saint Ambrose</i>, based on Bierce's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carlos_Fuentes" title="Carlos Fuentes">Carlos Fuentes</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Gringo" title="The Old Gringo">The Old Gringo</a></i> is a fictionalized account of Bierce's disappearance; it was later adapted into the film <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Gringo" title="Old Gringo">Old Gringo</a></i> (1989), starring <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Peck" title="Gregory Peck">Gregory Peck</a> in the title role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Bierce's disappearance and trip to Mexico provide the background for the <a href="/wiki/Vampire" title="Vampire">vampire</a> <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/From_Dusk_Till_Dawn_3:_The_Hangman%27s_Daughter" title="From Dusk Till Dawn 3: The Hangman's Daughter">From Dusk Till Dawn 3: The Hangman's Daughter</a></i> (2000), in which Bierce's character plays a central role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Bierce's fate is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Kersh" title="Gerald Kersh">Gerald Kersh</a>'s "The Oxoxoco Bottle" (aka “The Secret of the Bottle”), which appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Evening_Post" title="The Saturday Evening Post">The Saturday Evening Post</a></i> on December 7, 1957, and was reprinted in the anthology <i>Men Without Bones</i>. Bierce reappears in the future on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Shasta" title="Mount Shasta">Mount Shasta</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Heinlein" title="Robert Heinlein">Robert Heinlein</a>'s novella, "<a href="/wiki/Lost_Legacy" title="Lost Legacy">Lost Legacy</a>".</p>
<p>The short film "Ah! Silenciosa" (1999), starring <a href="/wiki/Jim_Beaver" title="Jim Beaver">Jim Beaver</a> as Bierce, weaves elements of "An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge" into a speculation on Bierce's disappearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the fall of 2001, <i>An Occurrence Remembered</i>, a theatrical retelling of Bierce's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An_Occurrence_At_Owl_Creek_Bridge" title="An Occurrence At Owl Creek Bridge">An Occurrence At Owl Creek Bridge</a></i> and <i>Chickamauga</i>, premiered off-Broadway in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> under the production and direction of <a href="/wiki/Lorin_Morgan-Richards" title="Lorin Morgan-Richards">Lorin Morgan-Richards</a> and lead choreographer Nicole Cavaliere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bierce was a major character in a series of mystery books written by <a href="/wiki/Oakley_Hall" title="Oakley Hall">Oakley Hall</a> and published between 1998 and 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bierce's short story "The Difficulty of Crossing a Field" is one of the first works of fiction to deal with an inexplicable occurrence. In this story, a man disappears from existence while crossing a field. The story precedes several stories in modern folklore and urban legends regarding mysteriously vanishing individuals.</p>
<p>Biographer Richard O'Conner argued that, "War was the making of Bierce as a man and a writer... [he became] truly capable of transferring the bloody, headless bodies and boar-eaten corpses of the battlefield onto paper."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Essayist <a href="/wiki/Clifton_Fadiman" title="Clifton Fadiman">Clifton Fadiman</a> wrote, "Bierce was never a great writer. He has painful faults of vulgarity and cheapness of imagination. But... his style, for one thing, will preserve him; and the purity of his <a href="/wiki/Misanthropy" title="Misanthropy">misanthropy</a>, too, will help to keep him alive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author Alan Gullette argues that Bierce's war tales may be the best writing on war, outranking his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Crane" title="Stephen Crane">Stephen Crane</a> (author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_Badge_of_Courage" title="The Red Badge of Courage">The Red Badge of Courage</a></i>) and even <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFloyd199918-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Vonnegut" title="Kurt Vonnegut">Kurt Vonnegut</a> once stated that he considered "An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge" the "greatest American short story" and a work of "flawless... American genius".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2012)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>American writer and researcher into anomalous phenomena <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fort" title="Charles Fort">Charles Fort</a> wrote about the unexplained disappearances of Ambrose Bierce and <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Small" title="Ambrose Small">Ambrose Small</a>, and asked, "Was somebody collecting Ambroses?" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ambrose Bierce features as a character in <a href="/wiki/Winston_Groom" title="Winston Groom">Winston Groom</a>'s 2016 novel <i>El Paso</i>. In the novel, Bierce is personally executed by Pancho Villa.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
