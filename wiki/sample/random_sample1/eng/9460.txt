<p><i><b>Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy</b></i> is a 2004 American <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Adam_McKay" title="Adam McKay">Adam McKay</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Judd_Apatow" title="Judd Apatow">Judd Apatow</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Will_Ferrell" title="Will Ferrell">Will Ferrell</a>, and written by McKay and Ferrell. The film is a <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">tongue-in-cheek</a> take on the culture of the 1970s, particularly the new <a href="/wiki/Action_News" title="Action News">Action News</a> format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etv_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-etv-3">[3]</a></sup> It portrays a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" title="San Diego, California">San Diego</a> TV station where Ferrell's title character clashes with his new female counterpart. This film is number 100 on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bravo_(US_TV_channel)" title="Bravo (US TV channel)">Bravo</a>'s 100 funniest movies, number 6 on TimeOut's top 100 comedy films of all time and 113 on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)#The_500_Greatest_Movies_of_All_Time" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire's 500 Greatest Movies of All Time</a></i>.</p>
<p>The film made $28.4 million in its opening weekend, and $90.6 million worldwide in its total theatrical run. A companion film assembled from <a href="/wiki/Outtake" title="Outtake">outtakes</a> and abandoned subplots, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Wake_Up,_Ron_Burgundy:_The_Lost_Movie" title="Wake Up, Ron Burgundy: The Lost Movie">Wake Up, Ron Burgundy: The Lost Movie</a></i>, was released <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">straight-to-DVD</a> in late 2004. A sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Anchorman_2:_The_Legend_Continues" title="Anchorman 2: The Legend Continues">Anchorman 2: The Legend Continues</a></i>, was released on December 18, 2013, with <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> replacing DreamWorks for distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-collider_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-collider-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huff_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-huff-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchorman:_The_Legend_of_Ron_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1974, Ron Burgundy (<a href="/wiki/Will_Ferrell" title="Will Ferrell">Will Ferrell</a>) is the famous <a href="/wiki/News_presenter" title="News presenter">anchorman</a> for a local <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> television station, fictional KVWN channel 4. He works alongside his friends, who he had known since childhood, on the news team; lead field reporter Brian Fantana (<a href="/wiki/Paul_Rudd" title="Paul Rudd">Paul Rudd</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sports_commentator" title="Sports commentator">sportscaster</a> Champion "Champ" Kind (<a href="/wiki/David_Koechner" title="David Koechner">David Koechner</a>), and intellectually disabled, but loyal and good-hearted <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">chief meteorologist</a> Brick Tamland (<a href="/wiki/Steve_Carell" title="Steve Carell">Steve Carell</a>). Station director Ed Harken (<a href="/wiki/Fred_Willard" title="Fred Willard">Fred Willard</a>) informs the team that they have maintained their long-held status as the highest-rated news program in San Diego, leading them to throw a wild party, where Ron unsuccessfully attempts to pick up a beautiful, blonde woman, Veronica Corningstone (<a href="/wiki/Christina_Applegate" title="Christina Applegate">Christina Applegate</a>). Ed later informs the team that they have been forced to hire Veronica Corningstone, the same woman whom Ron attempted to pick up. After a series of unsuccessful attempts by the team to seduce her, she finally relents and agrees to a "professional tour" of the city with Ron, culminating in a sexual relationship. Despite agreeing to keep the relationship discreet, Ron announces it on air as well as shouting in the office.</p>
<p>After a dispute with a motorcyclist (<a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a>) involving Ron's dog Baxter being punted off the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego%E2%80%93Coronado_Bridge" title="San Diego–Coronado Bridge">San Diego–Coronado Bridge</a>, Ron is late to work. Veronica fills in for him on-air, gets higher ratings than Burgundy normally gets, and the couple break up. Veronica is made co-anchor, to the disgust of the team, and both co-anchors become fierce rivals on and off the air. Depressed, the news team decides to buy new suits, but Brick, who was leading the way, gets them lost in a shady part of town. Confronted by main competitor Wes Mantooth (<a href="/wiki/Vince_Vaughn" title="Vince Vaughn">Vince Vaughn</a>) and his news team, Ron challenges them to a fight. However, when several other news teams converge onsite, a full-on melee ensues, only to be broken up by police sirens and causing them to flee. Realizing that having a woman as a co-anchor is straining their reputation, Ron gets in another heated argument with Veronica, and they get in a physical fight after she offends him about his hair, further increasing tensions.</p>
<p>One of Veronica's co-workers informs her that Ron will read anything written on the <a href="/wiki/Teleprompter" title="Teleprompter">teleprompter</a>, so she sneaks into the station and changes the text in revenge. The next day, Ron (unaware of what he is saying) concludes the broadcast with "Go fuck yourself, San Diego!", instead of his usual closing line, "You stay classy, San Diego!", subsequently triggering an angry mob outside the studio and prompting Ed to fire Ron. Knowing she went too far, Veronica attempts to apologize, but Burgundy angrily dismisses her. Burgundy becomes unemployed, friendless, and heavily antagonized by the public while Veronica enjoys fame, although her male co-workers hate her. Ed also informs Champ, Brick, and Brian that if they see Ron they may not speak with him or they will be fired.</p>
<p>Three months later, when a panda is about to give birth, every news team in San Diego rushes to the zoo to cover the story. In an attempt to sabotage her, a rival news anchor (<a href="/wiki/Tim_Robbins" title="Tim Robbins">Tim Robbins</a>) pushes Veronica into a Kodiak bear enclosure. When Ed could not locate Veronica, he recruits Ron. Once at the zoo, Ron jumps in the bear pen to save Veronica, as the public watches helplessly. The news team then jumps in to save Ron and Veronica. Just as a bear is about to attack, Baxter, who miraculously survived, appears and persuades the bear to spare them. As the group climbs out of the pit, Wes appears and holds the ladder over the bear pit, threatening to drop Ron in, and says that deep down he has always hated him, but then admits to Ron that he also respects him and pulls Ron out to safety.</p>
<p>After Ron and Veronica reconcile, it is shown that in years to come, Brian becomes the host of a <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a> reality show named <i>Intercourse Island</i>, Brick is married with 11 children and is a top political adviser to <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, Champ is a commentator for the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a> before getting fired after being accused by <a href="/wiki/Terry_Bradshaw" title="Terry Bradshaw">Terry Bradshaw</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_harassment" title="Sexual harassment">sexual harassment</a>, and Ron and Veronica are co-anchors for the <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>-esque <i>World News Center</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchorman:_The_Legend_of_Ron_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first draft of <i>Anchorman</i> written by Will Ferrell and Adam McKay was very strange and wildly different from the film's final form; Adam McKay stated that the script originally included "a musical number with sharks". Film director <a href="/wiki/Paul_Thomas_Anderson" title="Paul Thomas Anderson">Paul Thomas Anderson</a>, who used to be a producer of <i>Anchorman</i>, was taken aback by the script's bizarre nature, so much so that afterwards he left production, although in retrospect he mentioned lightheartedly, "I blew <i>that</i> [opportunity]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even though <i>Anchorman</i> is set in San Diego, the real San Diego is only seen in brief aerial shots—modern shots which include numerous buildings in downtown San Diego which had not yet been built or opened during the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmschoolrejects1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-filmschoolrejects1-8">[8]</a></sup> According to the official production notes and "making of" documentary (both included on the DVD), <i>Anchorman</i> was actually filmed in Los Angeles, <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_California" title="Glendale, California">Glendale</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a> on sets which were made to look like San Diego during the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmschoolrejects1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-filmschoolrejects1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film initially centered around the reporters tracking down a clumsy yet elusive group of hippie bank robbers known as "The Alarm Clock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digitalspy1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-digitalspy1-9">[9]</a></sup> This version of the film was met with poor test audience results, and a significant portion of the film was re-written and re-shot to replace the Alarm Clock with the panda story that appeared in the final version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digitalspy1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-digitalspy1-9">[9]</a></sup> The original Alarm Clock version was re-edited, along with alternate takes of existing scenes, into a separate direct-to-video film entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Wake_Up,_Ron_Burgundy:_The_Lost_Movie" title="Wake Up, Ron Burgundy: The Lost Movie">Wake Up, Ron Burgundy: The Lost Movie</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digitalspy1_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-digitalspy1-9">[9]</a></sup> It was released straight to DVD on December 28, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The physical appearance of the Ron Burgundy character was modeled after real-life news anchorman <a href="/wiki/Harold_Greene_(journalist)" title="Harold Greene (journalist)">Harold Greene</a> who worked at <a href="/wiki/KNSD" title="KNSD">KCST-TV</a> and <a href="/wiki/KGTV" title="KGTV">KGTV</a> in San Diego, CA during the mid-1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although he does not appear in the final film, <a href="/wiki/John_C._Reilly" title="John C. Reilly">John C. Reilly</a> attended an early read through and impressed Ferrell so much that a part in <i><a href="/wiki/Talladega_Nights:_The_Ballad_of_Ricky_Bobby" title="Talladega Nights: The Ballad of Ricky Bobby">Talladega Nights: The Ballad of Ricky Bobby</a></i> was developed for him; he would later appear in <a href="/wiki/Anchorman_2:_The_Legend_Continues" title="Anchorman 2: The Legend Continues">the 2013 sequel to <i>Anchorman</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gethard_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gethard-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchorman:_The_Legend_of_Ron_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anchorman:_Music_from_the_Motion_Picture" title="Anchorman: Music from the Motion Picture">Anchorman: Music from the Motion Picture</a></div>
<p>The film's musical score was composed by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Wurman" title="Alex Wurman">Alex Wurman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amazon1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-amazon1-13">[13]</a></sup> A soundtrack album compiling the songs used in the film was released on July 9, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amazon1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-amazon1-13">[13]</a></sup> The "<a href="/wiki/Jazz_flute" title="Jazz flute">jazz flute</a>" solo heard in the film is played by Katisse Buckingham, a Los Angeles-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_musician" title="Studio musician">studio musician</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kat_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kat-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yah_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-yah-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchorman:_The_Legend_of_Ron_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Release"></span><span id="Reception"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchorman:_The_Legend_of_Ron_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy</i> grossed $85,288,303 in North America, and $5,285,885 in other countries, for a worldwide total of $90,574,188.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxoffice_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-boxoffice-2">[2]</a></sup> In North America, the film opened at number two in its first weekend, with $28,416,365, behind <i><a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_2" title="Spider-Man 2">Spider-Man 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In its second weekend, the film dropped to number three in the United States, grossing an additional $13,849,313.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In its third weekend, the film dropped to number six in the United States, grossing $6,974,614.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In its fourth weekend, the film dropped to number nine in the United States, grossing $3,132,946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchorman:_The_Legend_of_Ron_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy</i> received generally positive reviews from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives the film a score of 66% based on 196 reviews, with an average rating of 6.3/10. The site's consensus reads: "Filled with inspired silliness and quotable lines, <i>Anchorman</i> isn't the most consistent comedy in the world, but Will Ferrell's buffoonish central performance helps keep this portrait of a clueless newsman from going off the rails."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Another review aggregation website <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Normalization_(statistics)" title="Normalization (statistics)">normalized</a> rating out of 100 top reviews from mainstream critics, calculated a score of 63 based on 38 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine ranked Ron Burgundy #26 in their "The 100 Greatest Movie Characters" poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <i>Empire</i> also ranked <i>Anchorman</i> at number 113 in their poll of the 500 Greatest Films Ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> ranked Burgundy #40 in their "The 100 Greatest Characters of the Last 20 Years" poll and Ferrell said, "He is my favorite character I've played, if I have to choose one ... Looking back, that makes it the most satisfying thing I've ever done".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He also stated later on that he had no idea that the film would take off like it did, saying "We had no idea that certain lines would be quoted the way they are and that the film would just take on this life after it left theatres, but thank god it did."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kirk Honeycutt of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> gave the film a positive review, saying "Proving that even infantile humor can be funny, <i>Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy</i> does make you laugh even if you hate yourself for doing so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Brian Lowry of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> gave the film a positive review, saying "Ferrell has seized on a clever concept rife with possibilities – namely, women breaking the glass ceiling in male-dominated TV news during the '70s – and smartly surrounded himself with a topnotch cast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Manohla_Dargis" title="Manohla Dargis">Manohla Dargis</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> gave the film a positive review, saying "Tightly directed by newcomer Adam McKay, a former head writer on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> who cooked up the screenplay with Ferrell, <i>Anchorman</i> never reaches the sublime heights of that modern comedy classic <i><a href="/wiki/There%27s_Something_About_Mary" title="There's Something About Mary">There's Something About Mary</a></i>. Big deal—it's a hoot nonetheless and the scaled-down aspirations seem smart".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dargis_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dargis-29">[29]</a></sup> Claudia Puig of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "That he can make his anchorman chauvinistic, deluded and ridiculous but still manage to give him some humanity is testimony to Ferrell's comic talents".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Puig_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Puig-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the film a C+, saying "Yet for a comedy set during the formative era of happy-talk news, <i>Anchorman</i> doesn't do enough to tweak the on-camera phoniness of dum-dum local journalism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gleiberman_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gleiberman-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "If you sense the presence of recycled jokes from <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_House" title="Animal House">Animal House</a></i> onward, you'd be right. But you'd be wrong to discount the comic rapport Ferrell has with his cohorts, notably the priceless Fred Willard as the harried station manager".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travers_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travers-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "Most of the time... <i>Anchorman</i> works, and a lot of the time it's very funny".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ReelViews" title="ReelViews">ReelViews</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "Ferrell carries the movie on his broad shoulders, nailing the character perfectly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Michael Wilmington of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> gave the film two and a half stars out of four, saying "It's a cute, silly, likable movie without much weight or intensity, but it's also pretty funny."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Ella Taylor of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L.A._Weekly" title="L.A. Weekly">L.A. Weekly</a></i> gave the film a negative review, saying "It's a long string of heavy-footed sight and sound gags that must have seemed a stitch at the drawing board, but made me squirm in my seat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Bill Muller of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Arizona_Republic" title="The Arizona Republic">The Arizona Republic</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "For every gag that works, there's one that doesn't, and the story is too thin to keep us engaged otherwise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Terry Lawson of the <i><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Free_Press" title="Detroit Free Press">Detroit Free Press</a></i> gave the film two out of four stars, saying "Little more than an extended <i>Saturday Night Live</i> skit with better wigs, real locations and a script that sputters as soon as its one-joke premise is exhausted."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Rene Rodriguez of the <i><a href="/wiki/Miami_Herald" title="Miami Herald">Miami Herald</a></i> gave the film two out of four stars, saying "It's a flat, dull picture marked by sporadic flashes of ridiculous brilliance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> A.O. Scott of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> gave the film three out of five stars, saying "It is not as maniacally uninhibited as <i>Old School</i> or as dementedly lovable as <i>Elf</i>, but its cheerful dumbness is hard to resist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Stephen Cole of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">The Globe and Mail</a></i> gave the film two and a half stars out of four, saying "Knights of Columbus! Wouldn't it be great if TV-based comedians weren't afraid of making movies that were funnier than they are?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Geoff Pevere of the <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> gave the film two out of five stars, saying "Like most of these sofa-spud comedies, <i>Anchorman</i> bears its attention-deficit disorder proudly, as it shifts tone and abruptly sidetracks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ty Burr of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> gave the film two and a half stars out of four, saying "Sloppy, crude, pursuing the most far-flung tangents in hopes of a laugh, <i>Anchorman</i> still gave me more stupid giggles than I'd care to admit if I weren't paid to."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Michael Booth of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Denver_Post" title="The Denver Post">The Denver Post</a></i> gave the film three and a half stars out of four, saying "Always affectionate toward its people, especially when they are at their most ridiculous, <i>Anchorman</i> meticulously builds America's dimmest news team. And we root for them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Jack Mathews of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "As much credit as Ferrell deserves for keeping this nonsense from slipping off the screen, Applegate deserves as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Megan Lehmann of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> gave the film two out of four stars, saying "Ferrell is funny spouting his weird stream-of-consciousness rants, but too often the plot leaves him hanging and he resorts to just Yelling Really Loud."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-8"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Jay Boyar of the <i><a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sentinel" title="Orlando Sentinel">Orlando Sentinel</a></i> gave the film two out of five stars, saying "Most of it isn't even as funny as those supposedly humorous features that local news broadcasts sometimes end with."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-9"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Joe Baltake of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sacramento_Bee" title="The Sacramento Bee">The Sacramento Bee</a></i> gave the film three out of four stars, saying "There isn't a second that goes by without Ferrell filling it with some form of comic shtick – a well-turned punch line, a goofy facial expression, a pratfall or some other kind of physical comedy business."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes1_27-10"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes1-27">[27]</a></sup> Moira MacDonald of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seattle_Times" title="The Seattle Times">The Seattle Times</a></i> gave the film two out of four stars, saying "While the film is sporadically funny, it was done much better on the small screen, decades ago."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Anna Smith of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(magazine)" title="Time Out (magazine)">Time Out</a></i> gave the film a positive review, saying "Takes a joke and runs with it – sometimes too far, but usually long enough to wear you down and force you to submit to its craziness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
