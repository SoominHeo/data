<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "curfew" comes from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> phrase "<i>couvre-feu</i>", which means "fire cover". It was later adopted into <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> as "curfeu", which later became the modern "curfew".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Its original meaning refers to a law made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_The_Conqueror" title="William The Conqueror">William The Conqueror</a> that all lights and fires should be covered at the ringing of an eight o'clock bell to prevent the spread of destructive fire within communities in timber buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol>
<li>An order issued by the public authorities or military forces requiring everyone or certain people to be indoors at certain times, often at night. It can be imposed to maintain public order (such as those after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_Blackout_of_2003" title="Northeast Blackout of 2003">Northeast Blackout of 2003</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_civil_unrest_in_France" title="2005 civil unrest in France">2005 civil unrest in France</a>, the <a href="/wiki/2010_Chile_earthquake" title="2010 Chile earthquake">2010 Chile earthquake</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Egyptian_revolution" title="2011 Egyptian revolution">2011 Egyptian revolution</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Ferguson_unrest" title="2014 Ferguson unrest">2014 Ferguson unrest</a>), or suppress targeted groups. Curfews have long been directed at certain groups in many cities or states, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese-American" title="Japanese-American">Japanese-American</a> university students on the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast of the United States</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-Americans</a> in many towns during the time of <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow_laws" title="Jim Crow laws">Jim Crow laws</a>, or people younger than a certain age (usually within a few years either side of 18) in many towns of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> since the 1980s; see below.</li>
<li>An order by the <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardian" title="Legal guardian">legal guardians</a> of a teenager to return home by a specific time, usually in the evening or night. This may apply daily, or vary with the day of the week, e.g., if the minor has to go to school the next day.</li>
<li>A daily requirement for guests to return to their <a href="/wiki/Hostel" title="Hostel">hostel</a> before a specified time, usually in the evening or night.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, a time after which a game must end, or play be suspended. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> the curfew rule for many years decreed that no <a href="/wiki/Innings" title="Innings">inning</a> could begin after 1 am local time (with the exception of international games).</li>
<li>In aeronautics, <a href="/wiki/Night_flying_restrictions" title="Night flying restrictions">night flying restrictions</a> may restrict aircraft operations over a defined period in the nighttime, to limit the disruption of <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_noise" title="Aircraft noise">aircraft noise</a> on the sleep of nearby residents. Notable examples are the <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> airports of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" title="London Heathrow Airport">Heathrow</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Gatwick_Airport" title="London Gatwick Airport">Gatwick</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted_Airport" title="London Stansted Airport">Stansted</a>, which operate under the <a href="/wiki/Quota_Count_system" title="Quota Count system">Quota Count system</a>.</li>
<li>In a few locations in the UK patrons of licensed premises may not enter after a "curfew" time. In <a href="/wiki/Inverclyde" title="Inverclyde">Inverclyde</a> for example this is currently set at 12:00 am.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ol>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 28 January 2011, and following the collapse of the police system, President <a href="/wiki/Hosni_Mubarak" title="Hosni Mubarak">Hosni Mubarak</a> declared a country-wide military enforced curfew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, it was ignored by demonstrators who continued their sit-in in Tahrir Square. Concerned residents formed neighborhood vigilante groups to defend their communities against looters and the newly escaped prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the second anniversary of the revolution, January 2013, a wave of demonstrations swept the country against President <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Morsi" title="Mohamed Morsi">Mohamed Morsi</a> who declared a curfew in <a href="/wiki/Port_Said" title="Port Said">Port Said</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isma%C3%AFlia" title="Ismaïlia">Ismaïlia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suez" title="Suez">Suez</a>, three cities where deadly street clashes had occurred. In defiance, the locals took to the streets during the curfew,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> organizing football tournaments and street festivals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> prohibiting police and military forces from enforcing the curfew.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iceland">Iceland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Iceland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland's</a> Child Protection Act (no. 80/2002 Art. 92), children aged 12 and under may not be outdoors after 20:00 (8:00 p.m.) unless accompanied by an adult. Children aged 13 to 16 may not be outdoors after 22:00 (10:00 p.m.), unless on their way home from a recognized event organized by a school, sports organization or youth club. During the period 1 May to 1 September, children may be outdoors for two hours longer.</p>
<p>Children and teenagers that break curfew are taken to the local police station and police officers inform their parents to get them. The age limits stated here shall be based upon year of birth, not date of birth. If a parent cannot be reached, the child or teenager is taken to a shelter.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom's</a> 2003 Anti-Social Behaviour Act created zones that allow police from 9 PM to 6 AM to hold and escort home unaccompanied minors under the age of 16, whether badly behaved or not. Although hailed as a success,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> ruled in one particular case that the law did not give the police a power of arrest, and officers could not force someone to come with them. On appeal the court of appeal held that the act gave police powers to escort minors home only if they are involved in, or at risk from, actual or imminently anticipated bad behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a few towns in the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Curfew_bell" title="Curfew bell">curfew bell</a> is still rung as a continuation of the medieval tradition where the bell used to be rung from the <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> to guide travelers safely towards a town or village as darkness fell, or when bad weather made it difficult to follow trackways and for the villagers to extinguish their lights and fires as a safety measure to combat accidental fires. Until 1100 it was against the law to burn any lights after the ringing of the curfew bell. In Morpeth, the curfew is rung each night at 8pm from <a href="/wiki/Morpeth_Clock_Tower" title="Morpeth Clock Tower">Morpeth Clock Tower</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Chertsey" title="Chertsey">Chertsey</a>, it is rung at 8pm, from <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas" title="Michaelmas">Michaelmas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lady_Day" title="Lady Day">Lady Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A short story concerning the Chertsey curfew, set in 1471, and entitled "<a href="/wiki/Blanche_Heriot" title="Blanche Heriot">Blanche Heriot</a>. A legend of old <a href="/wiki/Chertsey" title="Chertsey">Chertsey</a> Church" was published by Albert Richard Smith in 1843, and formed a basis for the poem "<a href="/wiki/Curfew_Must_Not_Ring_Tonight" title="Curfew Must Not Ring Tonight">Curfew Must Not Ring Tonight</a>". At Castleton in the Peak District, the curfew is rung from Michaelmas to Shrove Tuesday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At Wallingford in Oxfordshire, the curfew bell continues to be rung at 9pm rather than 8pm which is a one-hour extension granted by William The Conqueror as the Lord of the town was a Norman sympathiser. However, none of these curfew bells serve their original function.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_of_America">United States of America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States of America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Juvenile_curfews">Juvenile curfews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Juvenile curfews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Curfew law in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> is usually a matter of <a href="/wiki/Local_ordinance" title="Local ordinance">local ordinance</a> (mainly applied by a municipality or county), rather than federal law. However, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a> guarantees certain rights, which have been applied to the states through the 14th Amendment. Hence, any curfew law may be overruled and struck down if, for example, it violates a juvenile's <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">1st</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution">4th</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">5th</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">14th Amendment</a> rights. Nonetheless, curfews are set by state and local governments. They vary by state<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and even by county or municipality. In some cities there are curfews for persons under the age of 18. American military curfews are a tool used by commanders at various installations to shape the behavior of soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stated purpose of such laws is generally to deter disorderly behavior and crime, while others can include to protect youth from victimization and to strengthen parental responsibility<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> but their effectiveness is subject to debate. Generally, curfews attempt to address <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shooting" title="Shooting">shootings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Property_crime" title="Property crime">property crimes</a>, which are believed to happen mostly at night, but are less commonly used to address <a href="/wiki/Legal_drinking_age" title="Legal drinking age">underage drinking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">drunk driving</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teenage_pregnancy" title="Teenage pregnancy">teenage pregnancy</a>. Parents can be fined, <a href="/wiki/Criminal_charge" title="Criminal charge">charged</a> or ordered to take parenting classes for willingly, or through insufficient control or supervision, permitting the child to violate the curfew. Many local curfew laws were enacted in the 1950s and 1960s to attack the "juvenile delinquent" problem of youth gangs. Most curfew exceptions include:</p>
<ul>
<li>accompanied by a parent or an adult appointed by the parent;</li>
<li>going to or coming home from work, school, religious, or recreational activity;</li>
<li>engaging in a lawful employment activity or;</li>
<li>involved in an emergency;</li>
</ul>
<p>Some cities make it illegal for a business owner, operator, or any employee to knowingly allow a minor to remain in the establishment during curfew hours. A business owner, operator, or any employee may be also subject to fines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also concerns about <a href="/wiki/Racial_profiling" title="Racial profiling">racial profiling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2011 UC-Berkeley study looked at the 54 larger U.S. cities that enacted youth curfews between 1985 and 2002 and found that arrests of youths affected by curfew restrictions dropped almost 15% in the first year and approximately 10% in following years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In response to concerns about racial profiling, <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Maryland" title="Montgomery County, Maryland">Montgomery County, Maryland</a> passed a limited curfew, which would permit police officers to arrest juveniles in situations that appear threatening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mall_curfews">Mall curfews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mall curfews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many malls in the United States have policies that prohibit minors under a specified age from entering the mall after specified times,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> unless they are accompanied by a parent or another adult or are working at the mall during curfew times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Such policies are known as <i>mall curfews.</i> Malls that have policies prohibiting unaccompanied minors at any time are known as <i>parental escort policies.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Curfews_for_all">Curfews for all</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curfew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Curfews for all">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>States and municipalities in the United States have occasionally enacted curfews on the population at large, often as a result of severely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclement_weather" title="Inclement weather">inclement weather</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Political_unrest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Political unrest (page does not exist)">political unrest</a>. Some such curfews require all citizens simply to refrain from driving. Other require all citizens to remain inside, with exceptions granted to those in important positions, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elected_official" title="Elected official">elected officials</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement" title="Law enforcement">law enforcement</a> personnel, <a href="/wiki/First_responder" title="First responder">first responders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> workers, and the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>.</p>
<p>In 2015, the city of <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> enacted a curfew on all citizens that lasted for five days and prohibited all citizens from going outdoors from 10 pm to 5 am with the exception of those traveling to or from work and those with medical emergencies. This was in response to the <a href="/wiki/2015_Baltimore_protests" title="2015 Baltimore protests">2015 Baltimore protests</a>.</p>
