<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Yakyuken</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Yakyuken</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">野球拳</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is now widely known as a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Strip_game" title="Strip game">strip game</a> variation of <a href="/wiki/Rock%E2%80%93paper%E2%80%93scissors" title="Rock–paper–scissors">rock–paper–scissors</a> wherein the loser of each round removes an article of clothing, similar to <a href="/wiki/Strip_poker" title="Strip poker">strip poker</a>. Yakyuken gets its name from a <a href="/wiki/Shikoku" title="Shikoku">Shikoku</a> chant associated with the non-erotic version of the game which is still a local <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a> today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It became a part of culture in Japan due to mass media influence, and in other <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> countries after the influence of TV <a href="/wiki/Variety_show" title="Variety show">variety shows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soft_On_Demand" title="Soft On Demand">Soft On Demand</a>. Numerous <a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">hentai games</a> have been made featuring the Yakyuken gameplay, and several American studios, such as Lost Bets Productions, focus on the filming and marketing of Yakyuken and other related strip games.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakyuken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term initially originated from a Shikoku <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> game in October 1924, between the local teams of <a href="/wiki/Ehime_Prefecture" title="Ehime Prefecture">Ehime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kagawa_Prefecture" title="Kagawa Prefecture">Kagawa</a>. The Ehime team lost the game 0-6, and its manager, <a href="/wiki/Senry%C5%AB" title="Senryū">senryū</a> poet Goken Maeda (前田伍健), improvized a <a href="/wiki/Cheerleading" title="Cheerleading">cheerleading</a> dance from the tune of classical <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">kabuki</a> <i>Botan ni Chōougi no irodori</i> (牡丹蝶扇彩) to boost the morale of his humiliated team. This dance later became an iconic feature of the Ehime team.</p>
<p>In 1954, singers like Ichiro Wakahara (若原一郎) and Terukiku (照菊) from <a href="/wiki/King_Records_(Japan)" title="King Records (Japan)">King</a>, Yukie Satoshi (久保幸江) and Kubo Takakura (高倉敏) from <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Columbia" title="Nippon Columbia">Nippon Columbia</a>, and Aoki Harumi (青木はるみ) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Entertainment" title="Victor Entertainment">Victor Japan</a> each adapted the dance and its lyrics into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinyl_record" title="Vinyl record">record</a> <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a> named <i>Yakyuken</i> (lit. "baseball fist"), and the term quickly became known nationwide. In 1966, the city of <a href="/wiki/Matsuyama,_Ehime" title="Matsuyama, Ehime">Matsuyama</a>, where the cheerleading dance originated, introduced it as a representative <a href="/wiki/Taiko" title="Taiko">taiko</a> dance for Matsuyama in Shikoku's annual August banquet. In 1970, the banquet dance was transformed into the more popular <a href="/wiki/Sansukumi-ken" title="Sansukumi-ken">sansukumi-ken</a> <a href="/wiki/Parlour_game" title="Parlour game">parlour game</a> that continued to today, which the Matsuyama people regarded as <i><a href="/wiki/Honke" title="Honke">Honke</a> (lit. "senior branch" or "orthodox") Yakyuken</i>.</p>
<p>Strip sansukumi-ken, however, had been a popular activity in the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Red-light_district" title="Red-light district">red-light district</a> since late <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>. In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Nippon_TV" title="Nippon TV">Nippon TV</a> introduced a skit as part of its hugely popular <a href="/wiki/Owarai" title="Owarai">owarai</a> <a href="/wiki/Variety_show" title="Variety show">variety show</a> <i>Conte #55's Counterprogram Strikes!</i> (コント55号の裏番組をぶっとばせ!) by <a href="/wiki/Comedian" title="Comedian">comedians</a> <a href="/wiki/Kinichi_Hagimoto" title="Kinichi Hagimoto">Kinichi Hagimoto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jir%C5%8D_Sakagami" title="Jirō Sakagami">Jirō Sakagami</a>, where beautiful female guests were invited to play sansukumi-ken on stage, and the loser would undress and auction off her clothes to the <a href="/wiki/Studio_audience" title="Studio audience">studio audience</a> for <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">charity</a>. This skit was successful enough in terms of <a href="/wiki/Audience_measurement" title="Audience measurement">ratings</a> that later in the year it became its own separate show called <i>Conte #55's Yakyuken!!</i> (コント55号の野球ケン!!), named as Hagimoto was a keen baseball fan. The rating success of the show gave the audience a misconception that Yakyuken was related to strip games, which led to its more well-recognized modern misconnotation. Because of this, Hagimoto himself personally visited Matsuyama in 2005 and apologized to Sawada Tsuyoshitoshi (澤田剛年), the fourth-generation <a href="/wiki/Iemoto" title="Iemoto">iemoto</a> of <i>Honke Yakyuken</i>, for unintentionally distorting the definition of Yakyuken.</p>
<p>The erotic misconnotation of the word "Yakyuken" was further propagated by the prolific <a href="/wiki/Pornography_in_Japan" title="Pornography in Japan">Japanese pornographic industry</a>, as strip games and sexual intercourses involving the Yakyuken chant were frequently depicted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_video" title="Adult video">adult videos</a>. This led to the misconnotation becoming well-known in other countries where Japanese pornographic productions enjoy significant popularity. Yakyuken <a href="/wiki/Eroge" title="Eroge">eroges</a> are also popular in Japan and many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Asian" title="East Asian">East Asian</a> countries, with first Yakyuken video game created by Hudson Soft for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharp_MZ-80K" title="Sharp MZ-80K">Sharp MZ-80K</a> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (April 2013)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
