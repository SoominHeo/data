<p><b>Janosch</b> (born as <b>Horst Eckert</b>) (born March 11, 1931) is one of the best-known <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> children's book authors and illustrators. He was born in Hindenburg (now <a href="/wiki/Zabrze" title="Zabrze">Zabrze</a>, Poland) in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a> to a family of mixed German and Polish origin. He said in an interview that he is <a href="/wiki/Silesians" title="Silesians">Silesian</a> of nationality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the family fled to <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>. In the area of <a href="/wiki/Oldenburg" title="Oldenburg">Oldenburg</a>, Janosch worked in a textile mill. In 1953, he went to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, where he studied for some terms at the Academy of Arts, but broke off his studies eventually. Afterwards, he worked as a freelance artist. In 1960, his first book for children was published with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Lentz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Lentz (page does not exist)">George Lentz</a>, a friend of his, who also persuaded him to take on the penname <i>Janosch</i>. Within the next 10 years, numerous books for children followed with different publishing houses.</p>
<p>In 1976, he published his own rather different version of the fairy tale <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brave_Little_Tailor" title="The Brave Little Tailor">The Brave Little Tailor</a></i> by the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>, known as a fairy tale called, <i>Der Riese und der Schneider</i> (English version as "The Giant and the Tailor"), his own rather different version of the fairy tales <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Thumb" title="Tom Thumb">Tom Thumb</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Thumbling" title="Thumbling">Thumbling</a></i> by the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>, known as a fairy tale called, <i>Der Däumling</i> (English version as "Tom Thumb"), and a fairy tale called, <i>Die Prinzessin mit der Laus</i> (English version as "The Princess and the Louse"), three of which are part of the children's book <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neues_vom_Rumpelstilzchen_und_andere_Haus-M%C3%A4rchen_von_43_Autoren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neues vom Rumpelstilzchen und andere Haus-Märchen von 43 Autoren (page does not exist)">Update on Rumpelstiltskin and other Fairy Tales by 43 Authors</a></i>, which is compiled by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hans-Joachim_Gelberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hans-Joachim Gelberg (page does not exist)">Hans-Joachim Gelberg</a>, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Willi_Glasauer" title="Willi Glasauer">Willi Glasauer</a>, and published by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beltz_%26_Gelberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beltz &amp; Gelberg (page does not exist)">Beltz &amp; Gelberg</a>. He also published books for adults, in which he deals with his childhood experiences of growing up with an abusive father and a God-fearing mother, constantly recurring topics being friendship, family relationships, and the quest for the meaning of life.</p>
<p>Amongst his most famous figures is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigerente" title="Tigerente">Tigerente</a> that appears in <i>Oh, wie schön ist Panama</i> and the stories around the little tiger and the little bear.</p>
<p>By 1980, he had published over 100 books for children in nearly 30 languages. By then, he had left Germany, and now lives on the island of <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tigerente">Tigerente</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Janosch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tigerente">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Tigerente</b> (lit., tiger duck) is a children's book character created by the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> artist and author Janosch. It is a little wooden toy duck on wheels, striped black on yellow, that is pulled around on a string by various characters of Janosch's books. It never has a line of dialogue, but still has become by far the most popular figure ever created by the author. The Tigerente has since appeared on a wide range of products including posters, <a href="/wiki/Postcard" title="Postcard">postcards</a>, high school art projects, buttons, mugs, socks, umbrellas, cutlery and nearly every item of children's furniture imaginable, and has been the namesake and mascot of the German TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Tigerenten_Club" title="Tigerenten Club">Tigerenten Club</a></i>, all without ever uttering a single line. In the English translation of the book "The trip to Panama" the Tigerente is actually referred to as 'tiger duck'. The story has been made into a contemporary opera "Oh, the beautiful Panama" by <a href="/wiki/Lin_Wang_(composer)" title="Lin Wang (composer)">Lin Wang</a>. The opera was premiered at <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Oper_Berlin" title="Deutsche Oper Berlin">Deutsche Oper Berlin</a> in 2013 and was a sold-out event.</p>
