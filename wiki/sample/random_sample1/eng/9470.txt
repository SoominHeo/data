<p><b>Jarmila Kratochvílová</b> (<small>Czech pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Czech" title="Help:IPA for Czech">[ˈjarmɪla ˈkratoxviːlovaː]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Cs-Jarmila_Kratochvilova.ogg" title="File:Cs-Jarmila Kratochvilova.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/Cs-Jarmila_Kratochvilova.ogg" title="Cs-Jarmila Kratochvilova.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; born 26 January 1951, in <a href="/wiki/Gol%C4%8D%C5%AFv_Jen%C3%ADkov" title="Golčův Jeníkov">Golčův Jeníkov</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_people" title="Czech people">Czech</a> former <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> athlete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> She won the <a href="/wiki/400_metres" title="400 metres">400 metres</a> and <a href="/wiki/800_metres" title="800 metres">800 metres</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1983_World_Championships_in_Athletics" title="1983 World Championships in Athletics">1983 World Championships</a>, setting a <a href="/wiki/List_of_world_records_in_athletics" title="List of world records in athletics">world record</a> in the 400 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1983, she also set the world record for the 800 metres, which still stands and which is currently the longest-standing individual world record in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a>. Only one athlete, <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Jelimo" title="Pamela Jelimo">Pamela Jelimo</a> of Kenya, in 2008, has come within a second of Kratochvílová's mark since it was set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jarmila_Kratochv%C3%ADlov%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1983, Kratochvílová broke the 800 m world record with a time of 1:53.28. At the World Championships shortly afterwards, she won the 800 m and set a world record of 47.99 seconds to win the 400 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kratochvílová's 1983 400-metre world record of 47.99 seconds stood for only two years until it was broken by her great rival <a href="/wiki/Marita_Koch" title="Marita Koch">Marita Koch</a> in 1985. Koch's 400-metre world record of 47.60 seconds still stands as of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAAF_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAAF-6">[6]</a></sup> Koch and Kratochvílová are the only women who have broken the 48 second barrier in a 400-metre laned race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAAF_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAAF-6">[6]</a></sup> Her 800-metre world record is the longest standing track record in men or women's athletics, and was described by 1996 Olympic champion <a href="/wiki/Svetlana_Masterkova" title="Svetlana Masterkova">Svetlana Masterkova</a> as ".. very fast. It's impossible for women to run so fast. It will last for 100 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kratochvílová was a late developer, not breaking 53 seconds for the 400 metres until she was 27, and she was 32 when she set her world records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Her remarkably fast times, and her atypical muscular physique<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> spawned rumors of illegal drug use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wharton_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wharton-10">[10]</a></sup> Kratochvílová has maintained her innocence, and although in 2006 the Prague newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Mlad%C3%A1_fronta_DNES" title="Mladá fronta DNES">Mladá fronta DNES</a></i> claimed to have uncovered a doping program run by <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia's Communist government</a>, there was no link to Kratochvílová despite her being her country's highest profile athlete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since her retirement Kratochvílová has worked as an athletics coach and with the Czech national team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
