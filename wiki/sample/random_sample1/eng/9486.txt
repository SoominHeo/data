<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Yamaha YM2413</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<b>Yamaha YM2413</b></div>

</div>
<p>The <b>YM2413</b>, a.k.a. <b>OPLL</b>, is a cost-reduced <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation_synthesis" title="Frequency modulation synthesis">FM synthesis</a> <a href="/wiki/Sound_chip" title="Sound chip">sound chip</a> manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Corporation" title="Yamaha Corporation">Yamaha Corporation</a> and based on their <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_YM3812" title="Yamaha YM3812">YM3812</a> (OPL2). To make the chip cheaper to manufacture, many of the internal registers were removed. The result of this is that the YM2413 can only play one user-defined instrument at a time; the other 15 instrument settings are hard-coded and cannot be altered by the user. There were other <a href="/wiki/Cost_reduction" title="Cost reduction">cost-cutting</a> modifications: the number of <a href="/wiki/Waveform" title="Waveform">waveforms</a> was reduced to two, and the channels are not mixed using an <a href="/wiki/Adder_(electronics)" title="Adder (electronics)">adder</a>; instead, the chip's <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">DAC</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Time-division_multiplexing" title="Time-division multiplexing">time-division multiplexing</a> to play short segments of each channel in sequence, as also done in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YM2612" title="YM2612">YM2612</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_YM2413_Applications">List of YM2413 Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamaha_YM2413&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: List of YM2413 Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The YM2413 was used in the FM Sound Unit, an add-on for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Master_System" title="Sega Master System">Sega Mark III</a> sold exclusively in Japan that improved the sound quality of all compatible games. The Japanese model of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sega_Master_System" title="Sega Master System">Master System</a> came with this add-on built-in.</li>
<li>The YM2413 was used in an arcade board design produced by <a href="/wiki/SNK" title="SNK">SNK</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_Denshi" title="Alpha Denshi">Alpha Denshi</a> in the late 1980s for a number of their games. Games using the design include <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Soldiers" title="Time Soldiers">Time Soldiers</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Soldiers" title="Sky Soldiers">Sky Soldiers</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Gang_Wars_(video_game)" title="Gang Wars (video game)">Gang Wars</a></i>.</li>
<li>The YM2413 was used in the Yamaha PSS-170 and PSS-270 keyboards in 1986.</li>
<li>The YM2413 was used in the Yamaha PSS-140 in 1988.</li>
<li>The YM2413 was used in the Yamaha PSR-6 keyboard in 1994.</li>
<li>The YM2413 was used in several sound enhancement cartridges for <a href="/wiki/MSX" title="MSX">MSX</a> computers. It is also built into select MSX2 and MSX2+ systems, and all MSX Turbo R machines, as part of the MSX-Music standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_and_clones">Variants and clones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamaha_YM2413&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variants and clones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Konami's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-Memory_Controller#VRC7" title="Multi-Memory Controller">VRC7 MMC</a> contains a YM2413 derivative, used exclusively on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_Computer" title="Family Computer">Family Computer</a> game <a href="/wiki/Lagrange_Point_(video_game)" title="Lagrange Point (video game)">Lagrange Point</a>. It has 6 FM channels instead of 9 and lacks the rhythm channels. The hard-coded sound bank is also different.</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="https://sites.google.com/site/undocumentedsoundchips/yamaha/ymf281" rel="nofollow">Yamaha YMF281</a> is another YM2413 derivative. It has the same pinout and register set, but a different sound bank.</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="https://sites.google.com/site/undocumentedsoundchips/yamaha/ym2423" rel="nofollow">Yamaha YM2423</a> is yet another YM2413 derivative. Like the YMF281, it has the same pinout and register map, but a different sound bank.</li>
<li>UMC <b>U3567</b> is a 100% software compatible chip with a slightly different pinout, requiring a pin-to-pin adapter in order to be used on a YM2413 socket.</li>
<li>UMC <b>UM3567</b> is a 100% software compatible chip, with the same pinout as the U3567 but a slightly enhanced DAC output. It requires the same pin-to-pin adapter, but the different DAC output will also require the two pull-down resistors of the audio output pins to be removed from the motherboard.</li>
</ul>
